Abstract

Investigation into a hydrothermal reaction system with transition‐metal (TM) ions, 1,4‐bis(1,2,4‐triazol‐1‐lmethyl)benzene (BBTZ) and various charge‐tunable Keggin‐type polyoxometalates (POMs) led to the preparation of four new entangled coordination networks, [CoII(HBBTZ)(BBTZ)2.5][PMo12O40] (1), [CuI(BBTZ)]5[BW12O40]⋅H2O (2), [CuII(BBTZ)]3[AsWV3WVI9O40]⋅10 H2O (3), and [CuII5(BBTZ)7(H2O)6][P2W22Cu2O77(OH)2]⋅6 H2O (4). All compounds were characterized by using elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analysis, powder X‐ray diffraction, and single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction. The mixed valence of W centers in compound 3 was further confirmed by using XPS spectroscopy and bond‐valence sum calculations. In the structural analysis, the entangled networks of 1–4 demonstrate zipper‐closing packing, 3D polythreading, 3D polycatenation, and 3D self‐penetration, respectively. Moreover, with the enhancement of POM negative charges and the use of different TM types, the number of nodes in the coordination networks of 1–4 increased and the basic metal–organic building motifs changed from a 1D zipper‐type chain (in 1) to a 2D pseudorotaxane layer (in 2) to a 3D diamond‐like framework (in 3) and finally to a 3D self‐penetrating framework (in 4). The photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–4 for the degradation of methylene blue under UV light were also investigated; all compounds showed good catalytic activity and the photocatalytic activity order of Keggin‐type species was initially found to be {XMo12O40}>{XW12O40}>{XW12−nTMnO40}.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of polyoxometalate (POM)‐based coordination networks have been paid much attention in recent years. The combination of POMs and metal–organic coordination moieties has not only led to more complicated and fascinating structural topologies, but also exerted synergetic effects and merged the merits of both aspects, which reveal potential applications in heterogeneous catalysis, selective absorption, and photonic, electronic, and magnetic functional materials.1–7 In this research field, entangled coordination networks represent an important and interesting subfamily. The introduction of POM units has dramatically promoted the development of this area in constructing novel entangled networks. In the last two decades, a series of new interpenetrating, self‐penetrating polyrotaxane, polycatenane, polythreading, and polyknotting frameworks have been explored.8–13 Of these syntheses, one simple and representative synthetic system is composed of transition‐metal (TM) ions, neutral N‐donor bridging ligands (L), and POMs. In this reaction system, TM and L usually act as the node and linker, respectively, whereas the POMs can play three kinds of roles, that is, the template, pillar (or linker), or node.6–13 Moreover, the use of TM ions with different coordination geometries can sometimes modulate the linking modes of nodes in the coordination networks and influence the final structural dimensions and topologies. However, one of the most important driving forces for exploring new POM‐based coordination networks originates from the design and choice of organic N‐donor bridging ligands. Initially, pyridine‐based bridging ligands, such as the rigid 4,4′‐bipyridine ligand and the flexible 1,3‐bis(pyridyl)propane ligand, were widely explored in the construction of POM‐based networks.14–18 Subsequently, imidazole‐based rigid and flexible linking ligands were also employed in these reaction systems, which further enlarged the number and sort of POM‐based network species.19–24 Based on these reported compounds, it has been found that rigid bridging ligands usually possess advantages in constructing open frameworks with potential cavities or channels, but the frameworks thus formed lack structural variety. In contrast, flexible bridging ligands endow the hybrid networks with variable structural topologies, but stable open frameworks cannot be easily obtained with flexible ligands. Based on the above problems, exploring new active N‐donor terminal groups (except pyridine and imidazole) and bridging ligands that can combine the advantages of rigid and flexible ligands has become an important project in this area.

It is worth noting that multidentate ligands, such as triazole and tetrazole ligands, and the bridging ligands based on such N‐donor groups have recently been employed to construct POM‐based coordination networks due to their relatively high coordination activity with TM ions and various coordination modes.12, 25–31 If one rigid phenyl center is used to link two triazole rings through the flexible methylene sp3 carbon atoms to form 1,4‐bis‐(1,2,4‐triazol‐1‐ylmethyl)benzene (BBTZ; Scheme 1),6, 32–35 this new bridging ligand will contain both flexibility and appropriate rigidity, and represents a new type of N‐donor ligand for POM‐based network assemblies. With the use of the BBTZ ligand, a series of new composite compounds based on isopolymolybdates have been successfully prepared.6, 32 However, hybrid compounds based on Keggin‐type POM units have rarely been reported.32–34 Considering that Keggin‐type POMs are one of the best chemically tunable clusters in POM chemistry, with a variety of compositions, properties, and negative charges, it is possible to build an ideal synthetic system, for example, {TM/BBTZ/Keggin POM}, and chemically modulate the different precursors (TM, L, and POM) to investigate the inherent relationships between the final structural topologies and the precursor features. It is also worth mentioning that Keggin‐type POMs are usually the ideal catalysts in POM chemistry and their acidic and redox properties can be also chemically tuned.1–6 Therefore, the introduction of different Keggin‐type POM clusters into the TM‐BBTZ systems may result in new types of multifunctional composite materials in heterogeneous catalysis, photo‐, and electrocatalysis.

Based on the above considerations, we carried out systematic research into the hydrothermal reaction system {TM/BBTZ/Keggin POM} and have initially explored the effect of POM negative charges on the number of {CuI‐BBTZ} secondary building units.35 Herein, four new POM‐based entangled coordination networks were prepared by using this reaction system: [CoII(HBBTZ)(BBTZ)2.5][PMo12O40] (1), [CuI(BBTZ)]5[BW12O40]⋅H2O (2), [CuII(BBTZ)]3[AsWV3WVI9O40]⋅10 H2O (3), and [CuII5(BBTZ)7(H2O)6][P2W22Cu2O77(OH)2]⋅6 H2O (4). In compounds 1–4, the entangled assemblies show a variety of morphologies: 2D zipper‐closing packing (compound 1), 3D polythreading (compound 2), 3D polycatenation (compound 3), and 3D self‐penetration (compound 4). With an increase in negative charge on the Keggin‐type POMs and the introduction of different TM cations, the basic TM‐BBTZ building motifs change from a 1D zipper‐type chain to a 2D polypseudorotaxane layer to a 3D diamond‐like framework to a 3D self‐penetrating framework. To our knowledge, the 2D polypseudorotaxane layer and 3D polythreading framework in compound 2 and the (3,6)‐connected 3D self‐penetrating framework in compound 4 represent unprecedented entangled topologies in entangled systems. The photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–4 were also investigated by using the catalytic model of methylene blue (MB) degradation under UV light.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis

Compounds 1–4 contain Keggin‐type polyoxoanions, but the POM units in the four compounds have different negative charges due to the different compositions in this type of cluster. Generally, three main routes have been explored to chemically modulate the negative charge of Keggin‐type polyoxoanions: 1) exchanging the central heteroatoms of the POM cluster with elements in different groups of the periodic table; 2) reducing the POMs to heteropoly blue species by using relatively strong reductants; and 3) introducing different numbers of low‐valent metal centers into the POM clusters. In this case, all above three methods were used during the synthesis process. In compounds 1 and 2, Keggin‐type polyoxoanions [PMo12O40]3− and [BW12O40]5− were synthesized according to the literature methods36 and directly used as precursors for the structural assemblies. The polyoxoanions in compounds 3 and 4 were prepared in situ during the reaction process. In compound 3, the Keggin‐type POM cluster exhibits a three‐electron‐reduced heteropoly blue [AsWV3WVI9O40]6−, which was assembled by a reaction between the [AsW9O34]9− precursor and the reducing agent hexamethylenetetramine (hmta) under acidic conditions (pH 2.5) in a hydrothermal environment. In compound 4, the [PW10Cu2(H2O)2O38]7− precursor37 may undergo a disassembly and reassembly process in a hydrothermal environment to form a new double‐Keggin‐type POM unit [P2W22Cu2O77(OH)2]10−. The synthesis of compounds 1–4 realized the modulation of Keggin‐type POM negative charges by virtue of various synthetic strategies and provided a practical route for investigating the effect of POM charges on the structures of assemblies.

Furthermore, the CuI ions in compound 2 were obtained in situ by the reduction of CuII ions with N‐donor ligands (BBTZ) in a hydrothermal environment. However, the CuII ions in compounds 3 and 4 are not reduced by the N‐donor ligands. It is presumed that the use of different copper salts (CuCl2⋅3 H2O for compound 2 and Cu(NO3)2⋅6 H2O for 3 and 4) may induce these different results. The nitrate anion always acts as a strong oxidizing agent in acidic environments, thus the CuII ions may be protected by nitrate without being converted into CuI ions during the synthesis.35

Crystal Structures of 1–4

Compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P, and the basic structural unit consists of chainlike [Co(HBBTZ)(BBTZ)2.5]3+ fragments and POM [PMo12O40]3− moieties (Figure 1 and Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). The polyoxoanion unit [PMo12O40]3− exhibits typical α‐Keggin‐type structural features. In metal–organic fragments, there is only one type of crystallographically independent Co2+ center. Each Co center adopts a six‐coordinated environment with five nitrogen atoms from the triazole groups of the BBTZ ligands and one terminal oxygen atom from the [PMo12O40]3− polyoxoanion (Figure 1). The bond lengths of CoN range from 2.107(4) to 2.181(4) Å and the N‐Co‐N bond angles vary from 84.4(2) to 166.58(2)°. The bond length of CoO17 is 2.504(4) Å, which can be viewed as a weak coordination bond between Co2+ center and the surface O atom of [PMo12O40]3−. It is worth noting that the BBTZ ligands in this metal–organic chainlike moiety can be separated into three groups: The first group includes two BBTZ ligands that link two Co centers into a binuclear loop; the second group contains one BBTZ ligand that connects the loops into 1D chains; the third group contains two BBTZ ligands that act as terminal ligands to coordinate a Co2+ center with one triazole group, whereas the second triazole group is protonated and hydrogen bonded to the triazole group of adjacent BBTZ ligands (Figure 2 and Figure S2 in the Supporting Information). The typical hydrogen bond is N12H⋅⋅⋅N18 with a length of 2.64(2) Å. Based on these connection modes, the 1D metal–organic chains along the b axis are further hydrogen bonded together along the a axis to form a 2D supramolecular layer on the ab plane (Figure 2 and Figure S2 in the Supporting Information). The layer contains 1D channels with a window size of approximately 11.27(5)×14.37(3) Å. The Keggin‐type POM units reside in the channels (Figure 3). Note that the surface of the supramolecular layer is not flat but possesses a zipper‐like structural feature. Thus, the adjacent 2D layers are packed together through a zipper‐closing mode (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information). Obvious π⋅⋅⋅π interactions exist between the benzene rings of adjacent layers with a separation of about 3.65 Å (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information).

Compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The basic building blocks of 2 include one Keggin‐type polyoxoanion [BW12O40]5−, one loop‐containing metal–organic chain unit, and four types of Cu‐BBTZ chainlike moieties (Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). In compound 2, the polyoxoanion [BW12O40]5− exhibits typical α‐Keggin‐type POM features. The cationic Cu‐BBTZ moieties contain six crystallographically independent Cu centers and all exhibit a +1 oxidation state based on bond‐valence sum (BVS) calculations,38 single‐crystal coloration, and their coordination environments.

As shown in Figure 4a and Figure S6 in the Supporting Information, two wavelike 1D chains based on Cu5 ions and BBTZ linkers are further connected by the surface O atoms (O33 and O33′) of the POM units, which results in loop‐containing 1D chains. The bond lengths of Cu5N are in the range of 1.896(2) to 1.924(2) Å and the bond lengths of Cu5O range from 2.447(2) to 2.518(2) Å. Interestingly, another 1D wavelike thread unit based on Cu6 centers, Cu4 centers, and BBTZ ligands passes through the loops of Cu5BBTZ chains to form a 2D polypseudorotaxane layer (Figure 4a). Furthermore, these 2D layers are parallel with each other and stacked together along the b axis to form a 3D supramolecular framework. The POM units reside in the interspaces between two adjacent layers (Figure 4b). Moreover, this 3D supramolecular framework contains large channels along the b axis with dimensions of 35.93(2)×13.98(2) Å, in which five 1D Cu‐BBTZ chains are encapsulated (Figure 5 and Figure S7 in the Supporting Information). Based on this structural motif, the whole framework can be described as a 3D polythreading network that consists of 2D pseudorotaxane layers and 1D Cu‐BBTZ chains (Figure 6). It is obvious that the polyoxoanions play an important role in stabilizing the whole cationic metal–organic framework; otherwise each individual thread moiety can easily slip off the skeleton. By carefully checking the interactions between the POM units and the coordination network, we find that most Cu ions in the network are coordinated with the surface O atoms of the POM units (Figure S8 in the Supporting Information). Typical coordination bonds between Cu centers and the O atoms of the POM are as follows: Cu1O20 2.66(7) Å, Cu1′O36 2.44(4) Å, Cu4O21 2.84(6) Å, Cu5O33 2.518(2) Å, Cu5O33′ 2.447(2) Å.

Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. Its basic structural units contain the Keggin‐type polyoxoanion [AsW12O40]6−, the 2D CuII‐BBTZ network, and a 3D Cu‐BBTZ open framework (Figure 7 and Figure S9 in the Supporting Information). In compound 3, the Keggin‐type polyoxoanion [AsWV3WVI9O40]6− represents a three‐electron‐reduced heteropoly blue, which was confirmed by using XPS spectroscopy and the BVS calculations38 (see Figure S20 and Table S5 in the Supporting Information). The overall cationic coordination network of 3 contains two types of motifs, that is, the 2D network and the 3D open framework. The 2D network possesses one crystallographically independent Cu1 center, which is hexacoordinated with four N‐donor ligands from BBTZ and two O‐donor ligands from the POM units (Figure 7a, b and Figure S9a in the Supporting Information). The bond lengths of Cu1N are in the range of 2.031(2) to 2.079(2) Å and the bond length of Cu1O is 2.434(1) Å. All BBTZ ligands possess similar structural configurations and link the Cu1 centers into the 2D network with (44) sqp topology. Each pore is large, with dimensions of 15.46(3)×25.05(1) Å. The 3D open framework motif also contains one crystallographically independent Cu2 center that is six‐coordinated with four N‐donor ligands from BBTZ and two O donor ligands from the POM clusters (Figure 7c, d, and Figure S9c in the Supporting Information). The bond lengths of Cu2N and Cu2O range from 1.98(2) to 2.06(2) Å and 2.57(1) to 2.62(1) Å, respectively. In this 3D framework, the BBTZ ligands exhibit two types of structural configuration (Figure S10 in the Supporting Information) and connect the Cu2 centers into a 3D open framework with (66) dia topology (Figure 7c and d). It is interesting that the 2D sqp motifs and the 3D dia framework are further entangled with each other to form an overall 3D(2D/3D) polycatenation framework (Figure 7e, f, Figures S11 and S12 in the Supporting Information).

This type of polycatenation framework still has quite large interspaces and encapsulates the Keggin‐type polyoxoanions and lattice water molecules (Figure 8). It is worth mentioning that the CuII ions on the cationic MOFs and the polyoxoanions possess weak coordination interactions, and each POM unit connects with six Cu centers in 3 (Table S3 and Figure S13a, b in the Supporting Information). If this connection is considered further, the above 3D(2D/3D) polycatenation framework can transfer into a more complicated framework with three different 6‐nodes (Figure S13c in the Supporting Information). This 3‐nodal net can be represented as a 6,6,6‐c net with stoichiometry (6‐c)(6‐c)2(6‐c) and the point symbol is {32.42.510.6}{32.43.55.65}2{44.59.62}.39

Compound 4 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The basic structural building blocks include a 2D wavelike Cu‐BBTZ network, a double‐Keggin‐type polyoxoanion [P2W22Cu2O77(OH)2]10−, a fishbone‐like 1D chain motif, and six lattice water molecules (Figure 9 and Figures S14–S16 in the Supporting Information). The double‐Keggin‐type polyoxoanion can be viewed as two [PW11CuO38.5(OH)] fragments linked together through a {Cu‐OH‐W} bridge. The OH groups were identified by using bond valence sum (BVS) calculations. BVS values of around 1.15 to 1.20 were assigned for the monoprotonated O atoms (hydroxo groups), which are similar to reported POMs with OH groups.40–44 In this polyoxoanion, there are three site‐occupancy disordered positions shared by W and Cu centers, that is, W1/Cu5 (65 %/35 %), W2/Cu6 (85 %/15 %), and W3/Cu4 (50 %/50 %; Figure S15 in the Supporting Information).

It is known that the TM‐substituted Keggin‐type polyoxotungstates can sometimes be linked together through{TM‐OH‐W} bonds into trimers,40 1D chains,41 and 3D frameworks.42 Furthermore, discrete double‐Keggin‐type and tetra‐Keggin‐type clusters linked through {TM‐OH‐TM} linkers (TM=Fe3+ and Ti4+) have also been reported.43, 44 However, the double‐Keggin POM cluster linked by a single {TM‐OH‐W} bridge in compound 4 is observed here for the first time. The 2D Cu‐BBTZ network contains one crystallographically independent Cu1 center, which is hexacoordinated with four N‐donor ligands from BBTZ and two O‐donor ligands from the POM units (Figure 9a and Figure S14 in the Supporting Information). The bond lengths of Cu1N are in the range of 1.96(2) to 2.07(2) Å and the bond length of Cu1O is 2.39(2) Å. In the 2D layer motif, all the BBTZ ligands exhibit similar structural configurations and act as linkers to join the Cu1 centers together. Such a 2D undulated network possesses two kinds of channel (A and B) with the sizes of 14.27(4)×14.92(8) and 13.51(6)×14.92(8) Å, respectively.

Note that each double‐Keggin POM unit passes through one channel B of the 2D wavelike network in 4 and further acts as a pillar to link the adjacent parallel 2D networks into a 3D open framework (Figure 9b, c and Figure S17 in the Supporting Information). The structural topology analysis suggests that this 3D open framework exhibits a (3,6)‐connected self‐penetrating net (Figure S18 in the Supporting Information). Each Keggin unit acts as a 3‐node whereas each Cu center acts as a 6‐node. This 3,6‐c net can be represented by the topological point symbol {44.52.69}{5.62}.39

Furthermore, compound 4 also contains a disordered 1D fishbone‐like Cu‐BBTZ chain motif with two possible positions, with each displaying a site occupancy of 50 % (Figure S16 in the Supporting Information). Based on the above situation, the 1D fishbone‐like chains display two kinds of structural configurations and act as guests that reside in the 1D channels of the 3D self‐penetrating framework (Figure 10 and Figure S19 in the Supporting Information).

Effect of the POM Charge on the Structural Dimensions

The rational design and synthesis of exact desired POM‐based coordination networks is still a challenge, especially considering the black‐box hydrothermal environment. Nevertheless, continuously emerging new compounds in this research field can enlighten us to draw conclusions and discover new trends and relationships between the final structures and the initial building blocks, which may provide further guidance or new strategies for ongoing synthetic work. In this case, the modulation of the negative charge of the Keggin‐type POM units strongly influences the structural assemblies in the {TM/BBTZ/Keggin POM} reaction system (Table 1 and Scheme 2).

First, increasing the charge number of POM units led to an increase in TM centers in the final crystal structures (Table 1). It is known that TM ions are usually important nodes for the construction of coordination networks. The increase in node number may not only introduce more organic linkers into the structural assemblies, but also induce more complicated and versatile structural assemblies. A clear trend is observed, in which the increase in POM negative charge is accompanied by a promotion of the structural dimension of the basic motifs in compounds 1–4 (Scheme 2). It is further confirmed that the increase in node number may also improve the sorts and numbers of secondary building blocks in compounds 1–4, which leads to final structural assemblies with higher dimensions and more variable entanglements.

Secondly, with the increase in the negative charge on the POMs, the surface O atoms of the POMs exhibit a stronger tendency to coordinate with TM ions on the metal–organic building blocks as shown in compounds 3–4. Thus, the roles of the POMs will change from simple anionic templates into pillar units or even new nodes that can be employed in constructing high‐dimensional and complicated entangled frameworks (Table 1).

Effect of the TM Coordination Mode on the Structural Dimension

From the structural topologies of compounds 1–4, it can be seen that only increasing the node number may not always promote the structural dimensions of the final POM‐based metal–organic assemblies. As shown in compound 2, there are six CuI cations as nodes in the metal–organic moieties, but each CuI center acts as a 2‐node that only forms the 1D‐loop‐containing chains and 1D threads. These two‐ or four‐coordinated CuI nodes are not good at constructing high‐dimensional frameworks. When the hexacoordinated CuII centers were introduced into the reaction systems, for example, in compounds 3 and 4, they can act as 4‐nodes and even 6‐nodes to extend the structural topology into three dimensions.

Based on above experimental results and discussion, it was initially concluded that the use of Keggin POM units with high negative charges and hexacoordinated TM cations in the {TM/BBTZ/Keggin POM} reaction system tends to form new high‐dimensional entangled POM‐based coordination networks or even metal–organic frameworks.

Photocatalysis Properties

It is known that a wide range of POMs possess photocatalytic activities in the degradation of organic dyes under UV irradiation.45 Therefore, the photocatalytic properties of POMs have a potential application in the purification of polluted water. Herein, methylene blue (MB), a typical difficult‐to‐degrade dye contaminant model in waste water, was used to estimate the photocatalytic effectiveness of compounds 1–4. It is worth mentioning that compounds 1–4 cannot be dissolved in aqueous solution, thus they are used as the heterogenous catalysts. In a typical process, aqueous MB (300 mL, 10.0 mg L−1) was combined with the catalyst (compounds 1–4; 50 mg) and exposed to UV light (125 W). The photodegradation process of MB without any photocatalyst was also performed for comparison.

As shown in Figure 11, the absorption peaks of aqueous MB in the presence of compounds 1–4 dramatically decreased within 90 min under UV irradiation. Furthermore, based on the plots of Ct/C0 versus reaction time (Figure 12), it can be observed that the photocatalytic activities obviously increase from 33.3 % (no catalyst) to 93.6, 90.9, 90.8, and 80.2 % for compounds 1–4, respectively, after 90 min of irradiation. These results demonstrate that compounds 1–4 exhibit excellent photocatalytic properties for the degradation of MB. The degradation efficiency of the Keggin species follows the order {PMoO12O40}>{BW12O40}≈{AsWV3WVI9O40}>{PW11CuO38.5(OH)}2. More species in this series need to be prepared to establish a full view of the degradation efficiency order of Keggin species. Moreover, because compound 1 exhibits the highest catalytic activity in the degradation of MB under UV, it was chosen as a representative catalyst to investigate catalyst lifetime. In our initial experiment, the catalytic activity of compound 1 was maintained when it was recycled five times (Figure S21 in the Supporting Information).

Conclusion

Research into a hydrothermal synthetic system that contained TM ions, BBTZ, and various charge‐tunable Keggin‐type POMs has led to the successful preparation of four new POM‐based entangled coordination networks. In this reaction system, modulation of the negative charges on the Keggin‐type POMs has not only resulted in new types of entangled structural topology, such as the polypseudorotaxane layer and polythreading framework in compound 2 and the (3,6)‐connected 3D self‐penetrating framework in compound 4, but has also provided an interesting research strategy that can reveal the inherent relationships between the final structural features and the TM cations, BBTZ ligands, and various Keggin‐POM units. In this case, with increased negative charges on the Keggin‐type POM and modulation of the TM types, the basic TM‐BBTZ building motifs of compounds 1–4 form a 1D zipper‐type chain, a 2D polypseudorotaxane layer, a 3D diamond‐like framework, and a 3D self‐penetrating framework, respectively. This trend may be important for the design and synthesis of new POM‐based coordination networks or even metal–organic frameworks in the {TM/BBTZ/POM} reaction system and encourage the exploration of new types of active photocatalyst for the UV‐irradiated degradation of pollutant dyes in water.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods

All chemicals and organic solvents used for the syntheses were of reagent grade and were used without further purification. The 1,4‐bis‐(triazole‐1‐ylmethyl)benzene (BBTZ) ligand was synthesized according to a literature procedure.46 H3PMo12O40⋅n H2O, K5BW12O40⋅n H2O, and K5.5Na1.5[PW10Cu2(H2O)2O38]⋅13 H2O were prepared according to literature methods.36, 37 Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed by using a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. The FT‐IR spectra were analyzed as KBr pellets by using a Mattson Alpha‐Centauri spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. TGA measurements were carried out under a N2 flow by using a Pyris Diamond TG instrument at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X‐ray diffraction studies were performed by using a Rigaku D/max‐IIB X‐ray diffractometer at a scanning rate of 1° min−1 with 2θ=5–50° and with CuKα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å). X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy of compound 3 was performed by using a VG ESCALAB MK II spectrometer with a MgKα (1253.6 eV) achromatic X‐ray source. Photocatalytic experiments in aqueous solutions were accomplished in a 500 mL water‐cooled cylindrical quartz container. The reaction mixture in the container was kept at RT by a continuous flow of water through an external cooling coil. A high‐pressure 125 W mercury lamp was used as the UV light source. A suspension of powdered catalyst (50 mg) in a fresh aqueous solution of methylene blue (MB; 300 mL, 10.0 mg L−1) was magnetically stirred in the dark for 30 min before irradiation to establish an adsorption/desorption equilibrium of MB on the sample surface. At given intervals (15 min) during the irradiation period, aliquots of 3 mL were removed from the beaker and analyzed by using UV/Vis spectroscopy. The organic dye concentration was estimated from the absorbance at λ=665 nm.

X‐ray Crystallography

Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction data for compounds 1, 2, and 4 were collected by using an Oxford Diffraction Gemini R Ultra diffractometer at a temperature of 296(2) K by using an ω scan technique with MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å). Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction data for compound 3 were recorded by using a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) at a temperature of 296(2) K. All structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by using full‐matrix least‐squares methods in the SHELXTL package.47 During the refinement of compounds 1–4, nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, except for the lattice water molecules. The hydrogen atoms on organic carbon centers were fixed in calculated positions. Hydrogen atoms on water molecules cannot be assigned from the weak reflection peaks but were directly included into the final molecular formula. During the refinement of compound 2, the heteroatom boron center of the POM unit was only isotropically refined due to its very weak reflection in contrast to the W and O atoms. Note that the restraint commands “ISOR”, “AFIX”, “DFIX”, and “DELU” were introduced in the refinement of compounds 3 and 4 to solve the obvious NPD and ADP problems on many O, N, and C atoms and the unreasonable structural features of the organic ligands. These refinements led to relatively high restraint values of 452 and 711 in compounds 3 and 4, respectively. During the final refinement of compound 3, only five lattice water molecules could be assigned from the weak residual peaks. However, the structural feature suggests that there is still a solvent‐accessible void in the compound. Thus, the SQUEEZE program was used to remove the contributions of weak reflections from the crystal data and a new calculation result of 3*.hkl was further used to refine the whole crystal structure. Based on the SQUEEZE calculation results, the elemental analysis, and the TGA, another five lattice water molecules were directly added to the final molecular formula of compound 3. During the refinement of compound 4, the double‐Keggin‐type POM unit had three site‐occupancy‐disordered metal centers, that is, W1/Cu5 (65 %/35 %), W2/Cu6 (85 %/15 %), and W3/Cu4 (50 %/50 %). Furthermore, one of the BBTZ ligands on the 1D fishbone‐like chain in compound 4 also has two possible positions with 50 % site occupancy for each.
