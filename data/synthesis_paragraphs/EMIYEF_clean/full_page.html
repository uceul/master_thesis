Unprecedented cyclic [Mo6O19]2−cluster and five organic–inorganic hybrids based on polyoxomolybdates and 4-amino-3,5-bis(pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole†

Ming-Xing Li *ac, Hui-Ling Chen a, Jin-Peng Geng a, Xiang He a, Min Shao b, Shou-Rong Zhu a and Zhao-Xi Wang *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, College of Science, Shanghai University, Shanghai, 200444, China. E-mail: mx_li@mail.shu.edu.cn; zxwang@shu.edu.cn
bInstrumental Analysis and Research Center, Shanghai University, Shanghai, 200444, China
cState Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, Nanjing University, Nanjing, 210093, China

Received 1st February 2010 , Accepted 5th November 2010

First published on 2nd December 2010

Five novel hybrid compounds, [Mo8O26Cu2(4-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (1), (4-H2abpt)2[Mo8O26] (2), [Mo8O26Cu2(3-abpt)2(H2O)2]n (3), [MoO4Cu(3-abpt)(H2O)]n (4), and [Mo6O19Co(3-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (5) were prepared by reacting MoO3/(NH4)6Mo7O24 with 4-amino-3,5-bis(3/4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (3/4-abpt) and corresponding metal salts. Compound 1 is a 2D coordination polymer containing extended γ-[Mo8O26]4− and [Cu2(4-abpt)2]4+ ribbons. Compound 2 is a supramolecular hybrid aggregate composed of H-bond linking β-[Mo8O26]4− and 4-H2abpt2+. Compound 3 exhibits a 2D rectangular network constructed by isolated β-[Mo8O26]4−clusters and [Cu2(3-abpt)2]4+. In compound 4, each MoO42− anion links two Cu(II) ions to form a S-type [CuMoO4]n chain, which is further linked by bidentate 3-abpt to form a 2D wave-like network. In complex 5, unprecedented cyclic [Mo6O19]2−cluster links two neighbouring hexamolybdates and two Co(II) ions to form a 2D [CoMo6O19]n network, which is further pillared by tetradentate 3-abpt to generate a 3D metal–organic framework. These compounds are thermally stable under 250 °C. The composites of the final residues were analyzed by PXRD.

Introduction

Organic–inorganic hybrid materials formed by the combination of inorganic and organic building blocks are attractive for the purpose of creating functional polymeric materials that endow both advantages of inorganic and organic materials.1,2 The design and assembly of organic–inorganic hybrid materials is of great interest due to their structural diversity and potential applications for catalysis, porous sorption, luminescence, conductivity and magnetism.3–5 Many research efforts have been focused on the assembly of polyoxometalates with large organic ligands and transition metal ions to form extended metal–organic hybrid frameworks.6–8 Polyoxometalates are bulky anions that can induce novel architecture in the organic sublattice of these hybrid materials.9 The cationic metal complexes can serve as charge compensating species which may bond directly to the polyoxometalate skeleton to play space-pillaring and structure-directing roles.10–13 Polyoxometalates possess a large number of terminal and bridge oxygen atoms as potential active coordination sites to combine with transition metals. The backbones of polyoxometalates are highly influenced by reaction conditions such as pH value, solvent and temperature.

A number of N-heterocyclic ligands such as 4,4′-bipyridine, 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane and 2,4,6-tris(pyridyl)-1,3,5-triazine are frequently employed in constructing hybrid complexes with polyoxometalates.14–16 However, it is still a challenging task to explore successfully synthetic strategies for the preparation of hybrid coordination polymers with predictable structures and properties. The angular dipyridyl derivative 4-amino-3,5-bis(pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (abpt) has five potential nitrogen donors, which can be introduced as ligands covalently linked to the inorganic backbone of polyoxometalate and hetero metal to form hybrid coordination polymers.17–19 This N-containing ligand shows a bent backbone and diverse coordination modes via pyridyl and triazole N-donors.20 The amino group can provide potential coordination or hydrogen bonding sites that will influence the final coordination architectures. In our course to investigate metal–organic frameworks based on the abpt ligand,21–23 we combined polyoxomolybdates with 3/4-abpt ligands and transition metal ions. Five novel abpt organic–inorganic hybrids have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions. Herein, we report their synthesis, crystal structures and thermal stabilities.

Experimental

Materials and methods

4-Amino-3,5-bis(3/4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazoles were prepared according to the literature method.24 Other chemicals were of reagent grade and used as purchased without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Nicolet A370 FT-IR spectrometer by KBr pellets in the range of 400–4000 cm−1. Powder X-ray diffraction data were recorded on a Rigaku DLMAX-2550 diffractometer. Thermal analyses were completed on a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA85e thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in N2 atmosphere.

Synthesis of [Mo8O26Cu2(4-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (1)

A mixture of MoO3 (0.058 g, 0.40 mmol), 4-abpt (0.024 g, 0.10 mmol), Cu(ClO4)2·H2O (0.059 g, 0.15 mmol) and water (8 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 180 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Orange rod-like crystals of 1 were collected in 45% yield based on 4-abpt. Anal. calcd for C12H14CuMo4N6O15 (%): C 15.51, H 1.52, N 9.04. Found: C 14.85, H 1.52, N 8.38. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3487 m, 3337 m, 3079 w, 1632 s, 1597 w, 1482 s, 1205 w, 993 m, 949 s, 860 s, 838 s, 788 m, 708 m, 631 m, 527 m.

Synthesis of (4-H2abpt)2[Mo8O26] (2)

A mixture of (NH4)6Mo7O24·4H2O (0.075 g, 0.06 mmol), 4-abpt (0.024 g, 0.10 mmol), MnSO4·H2O (0.034 g, 0.20 mmol) and water (8 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 180 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Colourless prismatic crystals of 2 were collected and washed with methanol (35% yield based on 4-abpt). Anal. calcd for C24H24Mo8N12O26 (%): C 17.32, H 1.45, N 10.10. Found: C 16.53, H 1.47, N 9.87. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3360 w, 3218 w, 3094 w, 1632 s, 1597 s, 1460 m, 1202 m, 946 s, 916 s, 845 s, 794 s, 699 m, 659 m, 526 m.

Synthesis of [Mo8O26Cu2(3-abpt)2(H2O)2]n (3)

A mixture of (NH4)6Mo7O24·4H2O (0.075 g, 0.06 mmol), 3-abpt (0.024 g, 0.10 mmol), CuSO4·5H2O (0.025 g, 0.10 mmol) and water (8 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 140 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Blue prismatic crystals of 3 were collected in 51% yield based on 3-abpt. Anal. calcd for C12H12CuMo4N6O14 (%): C 15.88, H 1.33, N 9.22. Found: C 16.00, H 1.54, N 9.57. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3324 m, 3090 w, 1615 s, 1578 m, 1446 m, 1199 s, 955 s, 889 s, 846 s, 817 m, 693 m, 652 m, 522 m.

Synthesis of [MoO4Cu(3-abpt)(H2O)]n (4)

A mixture of MoO3 (0.029 g, 0.20 mmol), 3-abpt (0.024 g, 0.10 mmol), Cu(CH3COO)2·H2O (0.040 g, 0.20 mmol) and water (8 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 140 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Blue cubic crystals of 4 were collected in 43% yield based on 3-abpt. Anal. calcd for C12H12CuMoN6O5 (%): C 30.04, H 2.52, N 17.52. Found: C 31.98, H 2.56, N 18.83. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3444 m, 3362 w, 3064 m, 1611 s, 1586 m, 1460 m, 1201 m, 906 m, 869 m, 839 s, 822 s, 804 m, 696 m, 652 w, 512 w.

Synthesis of [Mo6O19Co(3-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (5)

A mixture of MoO3 (0.086 g, 0.60 mmol), 3-abpt (0.047 g, 0.20 mmol), Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.029 g, 0.10 mmol) and water (8 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 140 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1. Pink cubic crystals of 5 were collected in 50% yield based on 3-abpt. Anal. calcd for C24H28CoMo6N12O23 (%): C 19.38, H 1.90, N 11.30. Found: C 18.89, H 1.89, N 11.26. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3425 w, 3240 w, 3143 w, 1612 s, 1584 m, 1485 m, 1202 m, 934 s, 907 s, 854 s, 805 s, 682 m, 588 m, 523 m.

Crystal structure determination

The well-shaped single crystals of 1–5 were selected for X-ray diffraction study. Data collections were performed with graphite-monochromatic Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) on a Bruker Smart Apex-II CCD diffractometer at room temperature. Determinations of the crystal system, orientation matrix and cell dimensions were performed according to the established procedures. Lorentz polarization and absorption correction were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with SHELXTL program.25 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and hydrogen atoms were located and included at their calculated position. The crystal data and refinement results are summarized in Table S1 (ESI).† The selected bond distances and angles are listed in Table S2. The measured and simulated powder X-ray diffraction patterns of 1–5 are given in Fig. S2 (ESI).†

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of [Mo8O26Cu2(4-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (1)

Compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space groupP. It is a 2D coordination polymer constructed by extended γ-[Mo8O26]4− and [Cu2(4-abpt)2]4+ ribbons. As shown in Fig. 1a, the [Mo8O26]4− anion is centro-symmetric and has four crystallographically unique Mo(VI) atoms. Each Mo(VI) atom is in a distorted octahedral geometry, coordinated by six oxygen atoms. The [Mo8O26]4− anion consists of eight {MoO6} octahedra with four kinds of oxygen atoms (six μ2-O, four μ3-O, two μ4-O, and fourteen terminal Ot). This is a typical γ-structure octamolybdate.26 The Mo–O distances vary from 1.680(4) to 2.420(3) Å. Interestingly, the γ-[Mo8O26]4− anion further connects to two neighbouring octamolybdate clusters by sharing O(2) atom. This leads to the formation of a 1D γ-[Mo8O26]4− polymeric ribbon.
Fig. 1 Views of (a) 1D γ-[Mo8O26]4− polymeric ribbon, (b) 1D [Cu2(4-abpt)2]4+ polymeric ribbon, (c) 2D organic–inorganic hybrid framework of 1.

Each Cu(II) ion is in a trigonal-pyramidal geometry, coordinated by three N atoms from three 4-abpt ligands and O(12) atom, which is a terminal oxygen atom of γ-[Mo8O26]4− (Fig. 1b). Average Cu–N bond distance is 1.984 Å. O(12) occupies the apical position with a longer Cu–O(12) distance of 2.507(4) Å. 4-Abpt acts as a tridentate ligand coordinates to three Cu(II) ions viatriazole N(2), N(3) and pyridyl N(1) donors. Pyridyl N(6) and amino group N(5) are uncoordinated. The 4-abpt ligands connect Cu(II) ions to form a 1D [Cu2(4-abpt)2]4+ polymeric ribbon. The [Cu2(4-abpt)2]4+ and γ-[Mo8O26]4− polymeric ribbons are stacked alternatively and links through weak Cu–O(12) coordination bond. This leads to the formation of a 2D organic–inorganic hybrid framework as shown in Fig. 1c.

Crystal structure of (4-H2abpt)2[Mo8O26] (2)

Compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. It is a supramolecular compound composed of two 4-H2abpt2+ organic cations and one [Mo8O26]4− anion. As shown in Fig. 2, the [Mo8O26]4− anion is built up from eight edge-sharing {MoO6} octahedra with four kinds of oxygen atoms (six μ2-O, four μ3-O, two μ5-O, and fourteen terminal Ot). This is a typical β-structure octamolybdate.26 The Mo–O distances vary from 1.691(3) to 2.394(2) Å.
Fig. 2 Asymmetric structural unit and H-bonding network of 2.

4-Abpt is protonated on both pyridyl nitrogen atoms, which is uncoordinated, but bound to neighbouring four β-[Mo8O26]4−clusters by hydrogen bonds. The H-bonding distances of N(5)–H(5B)⋯O(5), N(5)–H(5A)⋯O(2)#3, N(6)–H(6)⋯O(13)#4, and N(1)–H(1A)⋯O(9)#5 are at 2.834(4), 3.016(4), 2.922(5), and 2.821(5) Å, respectively. This leads to the formation of a supramolecular organic–inorganic hybrid aggregate.

Crystal structure of [Mo8O26Cu2(3-abpt)2(H2O)2]n (3)

Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. It is a 2D coordination polymer constructing by [Mo8O26]4−cluster and [Cu2(3-abpt)2]4+ fragment. Obviously different from structure of 1, the octamolybdate adopts β-[Mo8O26]4− structural mode, which contains four crystallographically unique Mo(VI) atoms (Fig. 3). The Mo–O distances vary from 1.687(2) to 2.420(2) Å. Unlike the 1D γ-[Mo8O26]4− polymeric ribbon in 1, the β-[Mo8O26]4−cluster in 3 is isolated and bound by [Cu2(3-abpt)2]4+ subunit.
Fig. 3 Asymmetric structural unit and 2D rectangle polymeric network of 3.

Each Cu(II) ion is in a distorted square-pyramidal geometry, coordinated by two pyridyl N atoms, two terminal O atoms of octamolybdate and one H2O molecule. N(1)–Cu(1)–O(14) and N(6)–Cu(1)–O(10) bond angles are 171.00(12), 167.52(11)°, respectively. Average Cu–N bond distance is 1.993 Å, and Cu–O distance is 1.953 Å. O(12) occupies the apical position with a longer Cu(1)–O(12) bond distance of 2.519(2) Å. 3-Abpt acts as a bidentate ligand coordinates to two Cu(II) ions via two pyridyl N donors. Triazole N atoms and amino group are uncoordinated. Every Cu(II) ion is coordinated by one octamolybdate and two 3-abpt ligands in T-type. 3-Abpt and octamolybdate act as bidentate spacers, link Cu(II) ions to form a 2D rectangle polymeric network. The rectangle dimensions are 10.2 Å × 24.7 Å.

Crystal structure of [MoO4Cu(3-abpt)(H2O)]n (4)

Compound 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic space groupP21/c. It is a 2D coordination polymer constructed by MoO42− tetrahedron and [Cu(3-abpt)(H2O)]2+ subunit. The asymmetric unit contains MoO42− anion, Cu(II) ion, 3-abpt ligand and a coordinated H2O molecule (Fig. 4). Mo(VI) atom is in a typical tetrahedral geometry, coordinated by four O atoms. Mo–O bond distances are between 1.738(4) and 1.785(4) Å. O–Mo–O bond angles are in the range of 108.1(2)–110.9(2)°.
Fig. 4 Asymmetric structural unit and 2D polymeric network of 4.

Cu(II) ion is in a square-pyramidal geometry, coordinated by two pyridyl N atoms, two O atoms from different MoO42− anions, and water O(1). O(1)–Cu(1)–O(4) and N(1)–Cu(1)–N(6) bond angles are 171.63(19), 162.2(2)°, respectively. O(2) occupies the apical position with a Cu(1)–O(2) distance of 2.163(4) Å, which is obviously shorter than the apical Cu–O bond distances in 1 and 3. Cu(1)–O(4) bond distance is 1.920(4) Å, and Cu(1)–O(1) is 1.964(5) Å. Average Cu–N distance is 2.021 Å.

Each MoO42− links two Cu(II) ions to form a S-type [CuMoO4]n polymeric chain. 3-Abpt acts as a bidentate ligand coordinates to two Cu(II) ions via both pyridyl N donors. This leads to the formation of a 2D wave-like polymeric network. In this organic–inorganic hybrid framework, the S-type [CuMoO4]n chains are pillared by bidentate 3-abpt ligands. Triazole N atoms and amino group are uncoordinated. The amino group are bound to O(5)#3 and O(5)#5 with hydrogen bonds [N(4)–H(4B)⋯O(5)#3 and N(4)–H(4B)⋯O(5)#5 at 2.876(7) and 2.914(7) Å, respectively]. The coordinated water links N(5)#4 and O(3)#4 with strong hydrogen bond N(4)–H(4B)⋯O(5)#3 of 2.746(7) Å and N(4)–H(4B)⋯O(5)#5 of 2.622(6) Å.

Crystal structure of [Mo6O19Co(3-abpt)2]n·4nH2O (5)

Compound 5 crystallizes in the triclinic space groupP. It is a 3D coordination polymer constructed by [Mo6O19]2−clusters and [Co(3-abpt)2]2+ subunits. As shown in Fig. 5a, [Mo6O19]2−cluster is centro-symmetric, and built up from four distorted {MoO5N} octahedra and two {MoO6} octahedra with three kinds of oxygen atoms (two μ2-O, four μ3-O, and thirteen terminal Ot). O(10) is a water molecule. The Mo–O distances vary from 1.690(3) to 2.359(3) Å. Unlike the well-known Linquist [Mo6O19]2−cluster, which is highly spherical-symmetrical and contains one μ6-O atom at spherical centre,27 complex 5 contains an unprecedented cyclic [Mo6O19]2−cluster. The six-membered metallocycle exhibits chair-like conformation.
Fig. 5 Views of (a) [Mo6O19]2−cluster and Co(II) coordinate environment, (b) 2D [CoMo6O19]n polymeric network (green = Co octahedra, purple = Mo octahedra), (c) 3D organic–inorganic hybrid framework of 5.

Co(II) ion adopts a symmetric octahedral geometry, coordinated by four pyridyl nitrogen atoms and two hexamolybdate oxygen atoms in trans conformation. Average Co–N bond distance is 2.180(3) Å, and Co–O distance is 2.083(3) Å. The 3-abpt ligand is tetradentate, coordinates to two Co(II) atoms viapyridyl N donors, and two Mo(VI) atoms viatriazole N donors. Mo(1)–N(2) and Mo(2)–N(1) bond distances are 2.359(3), 2.582 Å, respectively. [Mo6O19]2−cluster links two neighbouring hexamolybdatevia sharing O(9) and O(9A). Meanwhile, the [Mo6O19]2−cluster yet links to two Co(II) atoms through O(4) and O(4A). These coordination behaviours lead to the formation of a 2D [CoMo6O19]n polymeric network (Fig. 5b). Such inorganic polymeric networks are pillared by tetradentate 3-abpt organic ligands to generate a 3D organic–inorganic hybrid framework as shown in packing diagram (Fig. 5c).

Synthesis and infrared spectra

The hydrothermal reaction of Cu(ClO4)2, MoO3 and 4-abpt afforded the complex 1, which is a 2D coordination polymer containing γ-[Mo8O26]4− polymeric ribbon. In an attempt to prepare similar 2D bimetallic Mn–Mo8 coordination polymer, a supramolecular compound of 2 containing β-[Mo8O26]4−cluster was prepared, which is absent of a hetero metal ion in the structure. Complex 3 was synthesized with a similar procedure of 1, except (NH4)6Mo7O24 and 3-abpt were used instead of MoO3 and 4-abpt. Interestingly, the resultant complex 3 exhibits a 2D rectangular polymeric network, which contains isolated β-[Mo8O26]4−cluster. Its structure is obviously different with that of 1. Further, the hydrothermal reaction of Cu(CH3COO)2, MoO3 and 3-abpt afforded complex 4, which is a 2D coordination polymer containing S-type [CuMoO4]n polymeric chain. Try to prepare similar cobalt complex, we obtained a novel 3D coordination polymer 5 constructing from 2D [CoMo6O19]n inorganic network and tetradentate 3-abpt ligand. This complex contains unprecedented cyclic chair-like [Mo6O19]2−cluster. The ratios of reacting reagents are rational according to the formulas of 1–5. Their formulas have been confirmed by elemental analysis data and single-crystal X-ray diffraction, although from the PXRD results it can be seen that some impurities appear to remain in compounds 2 and 4.

The infrared spectra of 1–5 are given in Fig. S1.† The IR spectra of 1–3 exhibit two groups of strong bands near 910 and 690 cm−1, attributed to stretching vibrations of terminal MoO and Mo–O–Mo in octamolybdate.28 In complex 4, the vibration absorption of ν (Mo–O) occurs at 840 cm−1 for [MoO4]2−. In complex 5, the cyclic [Mo6O19] cluster exhibits two strong absorption bands near 910 and 590 cm−1. The IR spectra of 1–5 also exhibit characteristic absorption bands of 3/4-abpt ligands. The bands occur near 3300, 3060, 1610 and 1580 cm−1 are assigned, respectively, to the stretching vibrations of N–H, C–H, CC and CN bonds. The bands in the range of 3500–3350 cm−1 were associated with the coordinated and lattice water molecules.

Thermal analysis

Thermogravimetric analyses of complexes 1–5 were performed in the temperature range of 20–800 °C in N2 atmosphere. The composites of the final residues were analyzed by powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S4).† As shown in Fig. 6, complex 1 lost four lattice water molecules in 40–140 °C (found: 2.8%; calcd: 3.9%). The anhydrous compound is thermally stable up to 300 °C. Over the range of 300–650 °C, successive weight-loss process corresponds to the decomposition of 4-abpt and octamolybdate. The final 57.6% residue was a mixture of MoO2 and Cu (calcd: 61.8%), which was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction data compared with the standard PXRD cards of MoO2 and Cu (Fig. S4).† Usually, the thermal decomposition of polyoxomolybdates under an oxygen atmosphere would lead to a final MoO3 residue. However, lower valence metal oxide residue would be obtained under a reducing atmosphere.29 The thermal decomposition of organic 4-abpt need take oxygen atoms from [Cu2Mo8O26] cluster under an N2 atmosphere. This process would yield a residue containing MoO2 and Cu. Complex 2 is thermally stable up to 250 °C. Over the range of 250–730 °C, successive weight-loss process corresponds to the decomposition of 4-abpt and octamolybdate. This leads to final MoO2 residue (found: 60.0%; calcd: 61.5%). Complex 3 lost two coordinated water molecules in 260–320 °C (found: 1.9%; calcd: 2.0%). Over the range of 320–650 °C, successive weight-loss process corresponds to the decomposition of 3-abpt and octamolybdate. The final residue was a mixture of Mo2C and Cu (found: 52.0%; calcd: 51.7%), which was confirmed by PXRD. Complex 4 lost coordinated water in 270–310 °C (found: 3.3%; calcd: 3.7%). 3-Abpt was released in 310–650 °C with 48.3% weight-loss (calcd: 49.6%). The resulting CuMoO4 residue is stable up to 800 °C (found: 48.4%; calcd: 46.8%). Complex 5 lost four lattice water molecules in 50–200 °C (found: 4.9%; calcd: 4.8%). In 250–610 °C, the successive weight-loss process corresponds to the decomposition of 3-abpt and hexamolybdate. The final 47.5% residue was a mixture of Mo2C and Co3Mo (calcd: 46.4%).
Fig. 6 The thermogravimetric curves of 1–5.

Conclusions

Organic–inorganic hybrid compounds may adopt varied structures depending on organic ligands, charges, and presence of heterometallic sites. By reacting MoO3/(NH4)6Mo7O24 with 3/4-abpt and metal salts, five novel hybrid complexes were prepared. Amongst them, polyoxomolybdates exhibit γ-[Mo8O26]4−, β-[Mo8O26]4−, [MoO4]2− and unprecedented cyclic [Mo6O19]2− backbones. 3/4-Abpt acts as bi-, tri- and tetradentate ligands using pyridyl and triazole N donors. The hybrid complexes exhibit 2D and 3D metal–organic framework except one supramolecular aggregate. The angular 4-amino-3,5-bis(pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole is a good organic building block, which can be introduced as ligand covalently linked to the inorganic backbone of polyoxometalate and hetero metal to form hybrid coordination polymer.
