Design and synthesis of luminescent porous coordination polymers for chromaticity modulation, sensing of nitrobenzene and iodine encapsulation†

Haijun Wang a, Fujun Cheng a, Changchang Zou a, Qianqian Li a, Yinying Hua a, Jingui Duan *abc and Wanqin Jin *a
aState Key Laboratory of Materials-Oriented Chemical Engineering, College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing Tech University, Nanjing, 210009, China. E-mail: duanjingui@njtech.edu.cn; wqjin@njtech.edu.cn
bState Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, Nanjing University, Nanjing, 210023, China
cJiangsu National Synergetic Innovation Center for Advanced Materials, Nanjing Tech University, Nanjing, 210009, China

Received 26th April 2016 , Accepted 2nd June 2016

First published on 3rd June 2016

We design and prepare a family of luminescent porous coordination polymers (PCPs) constructed from three C2v symmetry ligands with increased conjugation moieties. Single crystal X-ray analyses revealed that their pore types/sizes and volumes were systematically tuned. Eu-based NTU-5 and NTU-8 emit red light. However, in contrast to the typical green emission of Tb-based PCPs, the increased conjugation moieties in NTU-6 and NTU-9 with a Tb center resulted in a very unusual blue emission. These unique emissions from these iso-structures demonstrated varied pathways of electronic transfer, which were further confirmed by lifetime experiments. Encouraged by these observations, the chromaticity modulation from pink to blue has been well realized by a facile method of self-assembly of the Te-H3L ligand and varied molar ratios of Eu3+/Tb3+ ions. Additionally, NTU-6 and NTU-9 showed high potential for nitrobenzene sensing (quenching effect coefficients: 589.5 and 445.6 M−1) and encapsulation of I2 molecules.

Introduction

Porous coordination polymers (PCPs), formed from organic linkers that are bridged together by metal ions or clusters, have witnessed rapid developments of applications in various disciplines, such as gas storage and separation, stereo-selectivity catalysis, proton conductivity and chemical sensing.1 In contrast to zeolite and activated carbon,2 deliberate selection of inorganic clusters and organic linkers will bring more significant opportunities for constructing structures with high surface area, high porosity and tuneable pore size/type.3

With unique advantages of sharp, nonoverlapping, and easily identifiable emission bands of intra-4f transitions, lanthanide-based PCPs (Ln-PCPs) offer a unique platform for the development of solid state luminescent materials in crystalline form.4 However, the defined electronic energy levels of lanthanide ions, shielded by 4f orbitals, offer less sensitivity to the chemical environments. Those unique properties enable them unlike some organic fluorophores that may change emission performance on the basis of conditions of different guests or solvents. According to some recent studies, efficient energy transferring via the antenna effect, wherein the excitation energy is transferred from an adjacent strongly absorbing chromophore to the Ln3+ centers, could circumvent this problem.5 Therefore, the selection of a suitable sensitizer is very important. Till now, several strategies, such as encapsulating a luminescent moiety within pores,6 doping different lanthanide centers7 and changing of the temperature,8 have been employed to tune the emission of Ln-PCPs. However, the systematic design and preparation of Ln-PCPs with gradually increased conjugation moieties have not been reported before.

In our previous work, we investigated the structures and properties of some highly porous frameworks with low symmetry ligands (C2v).3b,9 The related PCPs with a functional amide moiety and finely tuned pore systems demonstrated high storage/selectivity toward CO2, as well as good selectivity for Cu2+ ion sensing. Here, in order to continue our work, we designed and prepared another two rigid C2v ligands (Te-H3L and Qu-H3L), featuring increased conjugation moieties, for preparation of highly porous and functional PCPs (Scheme 1). This is because the increased conjugation and uniform distribution of conjugation groups with different color zones when complexed with Ln3+ ions play an important role in the assembly of porous structures, and also facilitate energy, charge and electron transfer for potential chromaticity modulation.10 Based on this idea and combined with one reported ligand (biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylic acid: Bi-H3L), seven Ln-based PCPs, [Ln(L)(H2O)2]·guest, named Ln(Bi-L) and NTU-5 to NTU-10, (Ln3+: Eu3+, Tb3+ and La3+; H3L: [1,1′:4′,1′′-terphenyl]-3,4′′,5-tricarboxylic acid (Te-H3L) and [1,1′;4′,1′′;4′′,1′′′]-quaterphenyl-3,4′′′,5-tricarboxylic acid (Qu-H3L); NTU: Nanjing Tech University) were prepared. NTU-5 and NTU-8 with a Eu3+ center emit red light. However, an unusual blue emission, different from the typical green emission of Tb-based PCPs, was found in NTU-6 and NTU-9. Thus, fine chromaticity modulations from blue to pink were successfully achieved by the facile method of self-assembly of the Te-H3L ligand and varied molar ratios of Eu3+/Tb3+ ions. Additionally, in contrast to other benzene derivatives, NTU-6 and NTU-9 featuring quenching effect coefficients (589.5 M−1 and 445.6 M−1) are highly sensitive for nitrobenzene detection only. Further, their porous natures were explored by the effective capture and release of iodine.

Synthesis of Ln(Bi-L) (Ln3+ = Eu3+, Tb3+ and La3+)

Results and discussion

Solvothermal reactions of Ln(NO3)3·6H2O (Ln: Eu3+, Tb3+ and La3+) and three C2v ligands (Bi-H3L, Te-H3L and Qu-H3L) afforded seven block crystals: Ln(Bi-L) and NTU-5 to NTU-10. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses revealed that this series of PCPs can be formulated as [Ln(L)(H2O)2]·guest. Six of them crystallize in the monoclinic C2/c space group and the other in the I2/a space group (Table 1). All bond lengths and bond angles, especially for Ln–O and O–Ln–O, are within their rational range. Their asymmetric units include one lanthanide atom, two coordinated H2O molecules and one ligand. Each Ln3+ ion is coordinated in a dodecahedral geometry with four O atoms from two chelate ligands, three O atoms from bridged ligands and two water molecules. The ligands act as a μ4-bridge link to four Ln3+ ions (Fig. S4†); the Ln–Ln clusters are bridged together by six carboxylate groups to form a three-dimensional (3D) open framework with quadrilateral channels (Fig. 1). Due to the systemically tuned length of the involved ligands at the 4′ position, the generated cages in this series of PCPs increased gradually from 15 Å to 21 Å (Fig. 1d–f). However, the isophthalic acid moiety restricted the pore sizes to about 6.5 × 6.5 Å2 along the related direction of this series of compounds (Fig. 1g). Further, the increased conjugation systems in Te-H3L and Qu-H3L offer significant chance for π⋯π interaction of adjacent ligands in NTU-5 to NTU-10 (Fig. 2), indicating potential for different electronic transitions.11 In addition, the comprehension of the underlying network topology is helpful in understanding the features of whole structures. Here, Ln2(ArCOO)4 clusters were simplified as 6-connected nodes, where, L3− ligands are 3-connected linkers. Thus, all of them show 3-dimensional 3,6-connected nets (Fig. 3) with a mono-tile inside, as well as the same Schläfli symbol of {42·6}2{44·62·87·102}, which is rare in popular 3,6-c frameworks.12 The calculated solvent-accessible volumes of NTU-5 to NTU-10, using the PLATON program, reached 50.9% to 54.1%, highlighting their porous nature. Their phase purities were confirmed from their powder X-ray diffraction patterns (Fig. S11, 14–16 and 19–21†). The thermal gravimetric analyses showed that thermal decomposition of this series of structures started around 450 °C under a N2 atmosphere. (Fig. S10, 13 and 18†).

To determine whether the ligands provide efficient sensitization for the lanthanide ions, the solid state luminescence spectra of this series of PCPs are recorded at room temperature (Fig. S23 and 24†). The excitation spectra were obtained by monitoring the emission of Eu-complexes at 619 nm, and the results showed that the optimum excitation wavelengths for Eu(Bi-L) and NTU-5 are 396 nm, as well as a blue shifted wavelength for NTU-8 (360 nm). Their emission spectra revealed well-resolved magnified luminescence of f–f transitions, attributed to the energy transfer from the ligands to Eu3+. Characteristic transitions of the Eu3+ ion are also evident, at 594, 619, 651 and 702 nm, which are ascribed to the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 1, 2, 3, and 4) transitions, respectively. In contrast, Tb-based PCPs exhibit ligand related excitation spectra. The optimal excitation wavelengths gradually increased from 351 nm (Tb(Bi-L)) to 356 nm (NTU-6) and 368 nm (NTU-9). More surprisingly, NTU-6 and NTU-9 demonstrate a significant blue shifted emission around 400 nm, but not the typical characteristic emission of Tb3+, such as the emission of Tb(Bi-L) at 490 nm, 546 nm, 586 nm and 623 nm. To understand these special emissions, we investigated the lifetimes of their as-synthesized phase at room temperature (Fig. 4). The luminescence lifetimes of the three Eu-based PCPs and Tb(Bi-L) were determined to be 600–1100 μs, which are similar to the performance of Eu- and Tb-based PCPs with a typical metal-centered emission (antenna effect).9c However, as expected, NTU-6 (764 ps) and NTU-9 (1441 ps) displayed a significant short lifetime, which arises from direct organic ligand excitation.4,13

With PCPs bearing the same unique emissions and iso-structures as NTU-5 and NTU-6, we believed that the chromaticity modulation from pink to blue would be possible. Thus, we prepared a family of Eux/Tb1−x(Te-L) in which mixed Eu3+/Tb3+ ions with varied molar ratios can be systematically incorporated into the frameworks (mole ratio of Tb3+: 80–100%). The PXRD patterns indicate that this series of samples features the same structure as NTU-5 and 6 (Fig. S25†). With the increased ratio of Eu3+ ion, their luminescence spectra showed that the peaks around 592 nm and 616 nm gradually increased. Meanwhile, a strong and broad peak around 400 nm was found in all of them (Fig. 5). The systematic chromaticity modulation from pink to blue has been realized, which was confirmed from the photograph of this series of samples under UV light (365 nm). Thus, the fine-tuned emission would make the compounds of Eux/Tb1−x(Te-L) as promising materials for desired light devices.


Fig. 5 Luminescence spectra of Eu/Tb(Te-L) and their photographs under UV light of 365 nm.

Recently, luminescence detection of small organic molecules has received a great deal of attention, and thus a large number of luminescent PCPs have been reported.14 Although these luminescent PCPs display good sensing properties, the detection of benzene derivatives needs to be further improved. Considering the unique blue emission and highly conjugated groups of Tb-based PCPs, it is necessary to explore their performance for sensing of benzene derivatives. Hexane exchanged NTU-6 and NTU-9 were immersed in benzene, toluene, m-xylene, chlorobenzene and nitrobenzene to form aromatic molecule incorporated materials (A@NTU-6 and A@NTU-9) for their luminescence studies. As shown in Fig. 6, the luminescence spectra are largely dependent on the guest molecules, particularly in the case of nitrobenzene, which exhibits the most significant quenching effects in NTU-6 and also in NTU-9. The mechanism of molecule reorganization should be explained as a function of the strongly electron-withdrawing –NO2 group that offers electron transition between the framework and nitrobenzene molecules.15 To further understand these observations, the quenching effect coefficients of these two PCPs were calculated by using the Stern–Volmer equation (I0/I = 1 + Ksv·[M]). By comparing the quenching effect coefficients (Ksv) in Table 2, we found that the Ksv for nitrobenzene in NTU-6 and NTU-9 reached 589.6 and 445.5 M−1, revealing very high sensitivity for nitrobenzene detection. Thus, these results imply that both NTU-6 and NTU-9 might be utilized as novel functional sensing materials for benzene derivatives.

Generally, iodine is primarily used in human nutrition and in industry for production of acetic acid and certain polymers. However, the natural source of iodine is scarce, and moreover, iodine is a bluish-black solid that visibly sublimates into a noxious gas. Thus, it is necessary to find a material with highly efficient enrichment and release of iodine.16 Due to the high porosity of this series of PCPs, its iodine (I2) accommodation properties were investigated (Fig. 7 and S34–37†). Here, we discuss the results of NTU-6 and NTU-9 in detail.


Fig. 7 UV spectra of I2 released from I2@NTU-6 (a: 20 mg in 2 ml of EtOH) and I2@NTU-9 (c: 14 mg in 2.5 ml of EtOH); the time course of I2 release of I2@NTU-6 (b) and I2@NTU-9 (d), generated from the peak intensity at 290 nm.

After soaking the hexane-exchanged PCPs (∼30 mg) in a highly concentrated I2 solution (hexane) for 24 h, we found that the color of PCPs changed from white to deep yellow, while the luminescence was quenched (Fig. S26–28†). This is because iodine as a heavy atom is well known for quenching fluorescence emission, reflecting successful iodine absorption. To determine the possible interactions of the host and iodine species, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analyses of NTU-6, NTU-9, and their I2 encapsulated phases were performed. Comparing the binding energies, no special I2⋯O and I2⋯Tb interactions were found (Fig. S30†). However, considering their structures, the encapsulation should be assigned as the I2⋯π interaction from the introduced conjugation moieties.17 Additionally, the XPS results of the I 3d scan showed that the valence of encapsulated iodine species is zero,16b which indicates that only physical absorption occurs (Fig. S31†).

TG experiments showed that ∼14% weight loss in the temperature range of 65–200 °C was found for air dried I2@NTU-6 and I2@NTU-9. These values indicate that each unit cell of NTU-6 and NTU-9 can accommodate about 2.8 and 3.2 I2 molecules, respectively (Fig. S32 and 33†). In order to confirm this behavior, another portion of air dried I2@NTU-6 was soaked into dry ethanol, with its color changed gradually from deep yellow to colorless. Meanwhile, the colorless ethanol solution gradually changed to yellow. To further study the process of I2 release, the in situ UV-spectra were collected at room temperature. The intensity of the absorbance increased with time, especially for the peaks at 290 and 360 nm. Compared with the standard curve of the I2 solution at 290 nm, we found that the concentration of the I2 solution reached 1455 mg L−1 and 779 mg L−1 for I2@NTU-6 (20 mg in 2 mL of EtOH) and I2@NTU-9 (14 mg in 2.5 mL of EtOH) release. Further calculation shows that 2.95 and 3.21 I2 molecules were released for each corresponding unit cell, reflecting complete release. Therefore, the I2 sorption process is reversible in NTU-6 and NTU-9, which was clearly confirmed from their luminescence spectra and photographs (Fig. S28†). In addition, the I2 accommodation behaviors of the other four PCPs have also been examined. Thus, the porosity of these PCPs has been identified, as well as their potential for efficient enrichment and release of I2 molecules.

Conclusions

In summary, by using rationally designed C2v symmetry ligands, we have constructed seven new luminescent PCPs with systematically tuned pore parameters. The luminescence experiments showed that the highly conjugated moieties resulted in an unusual blue emission from Tb-based NTU-6 and NTU-9, while typical red light was observed from Eu-based NTU-5 and NTU-8. These unique emissions from these iso-structures demonstrated the varied pathways of electronic transfer, which were further confirmed by lifetime experiments. With PCPs bearing the same unique emissions and iso-structures as NTU-5 and NTU-6, the fine-tuning of chromaticity from pink to blue has been realized by a facile method of self-assembly of the Te-H3L ligand and varied molar ratios of Eu3+/Tb3+ ions. Further, the sensing experiments and quenching effect coefficients (589.6 and 445.5 M−1) showed that both NTU-6 and NTU-9 are ideal luminescence sensors for selective probing of nitrobenzene. Meanwhile, their porous natures have been identified, as well as their potential for efficient enrichment and release of I2. Thus, our PCP tuning strategy will offer a significant possibility to create new luminescent systems and devices.
