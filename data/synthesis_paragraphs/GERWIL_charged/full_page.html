    Abstract

    By using a rigid octadentenate carboxylate linker 5,5′,5″,5″′-silanetetrayltetraisophthalic acid (H8L), three new microporous coordination polymers, namely, [Cu4L(H2O)4]·2DMF·10H2O (1), [Zn5L2(H2O)4][NH2(CH3)2]6·2DMF·4H2O (2), and [Cd3(H2L)2][NH2(CH3)2]6·4DMF·14H2O (3), were solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. Compound 1 is a 3D framework built from a square paddlewheel [Cu2(O2CR)4] and a 8-connected cube L ligand to give a (4,8)-connected scu structure. Compound 2 is a 3D 4-nodal (4,4,4,8)-connected network with small 1D channels built on three tetrahedral Zn units and a 8-connecting ligand L. Compound 3 possesses a 3D structure with a (4,4,6)-connected net built on two tetrahedral Cd units and a 6-connecting ligand H2L6–. All the three compounds consist of large solvent accessible voids, but only compound 1 possesses permanent porosity as confirmed by N2, H2, O2, CO2 and CH4 gas adsorption measurements. Compounds 2 and 3 exhibited strong blue fluorescent emissions with a peak at 414 and 412 nm, respectively, at RT upon excitation owing to a ligand-centered excited state.

    Synopsis

    Three new microporous coordination polymers were solvothermally synthesized by using a rigid octadentenate carboxylate linker, which exhibits different structure topologies.

    The design and synthesis of porous coordination polymers (or metal–organic frameworks, MOFs) have become an attractive prospect not only due to their intriguing variety of structures but also to their potential applications in many fields such as luminescence,(1) chemical sensors,(2) separation,(3) and catalysis.(4) To fully realize their potential, it is necessary to construct various MOFs with specific structure and functionality by the deliberate design and selection of organic linkers and metal-containing units. Among various organic bridging ligands, those of multiple carboxylate groups have emerged as ideal linkers for building MOFs because of their abundant coordination modes to metal ions.(5) A number of multidentate aromatic carboxylic acid ligands,(5b, 6) e.g., terephthalic acid, 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid, biphenyl-3,4,3′,4′-tetracarboxylic acid, 3,3′5,5′-biphenyltetracarboxylic acid, etc., have been used to construct a series of robust MOFs. On the other hand, a large number of porous MOFs reported so far belong to the class of 4-connected net topology,(7) particularly the tetrahedron-based ones. It is understood that, from both zeolite chemistry and crystal engineering points of view, the use of tetrahedral structure building units may readily result in 3D porous frameworks with a low framework density. With these in mind, a series of tetrapodal ligands such as 4,4′,4″,4″′-methanetetrayltetrabenzoic acid (L1),(8) 4′,4″,4″′,4″″-methanetetrayltetrabiphenyl-4-carboxylic acid (L2),(9) 4,4′,4″,4″′-(4,4′,4″,4″′-methanetetrayltetrakis(4,1-phenylene)tetrakis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))tetrabenzoic acid (L3),(10) and tetrakis[4-(carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]methane acid (L4)(11) were prepared and successfully used in the construction of porous MOFs (Scheme 1). Further development on porous MOFs has shown that m-benzenedicarboylate moieties are very powerful organic groups to stabilize the frameworks by the formation of paddlewheel M2(O2C−)4 clusters and the corresponding cages of moderate sizes.(6b, 12) On the basis of these strategies, we(13) (and others(14)) recently reported the synthesis of a new flexible organic linker tetrakis[(3,5-dicarboxyphenoxy)methyl] methane (L5) with the combination of m-benzenedicarboylate moieties and a tetrahedral geometry and constructed a robust MOF with the rare (4,8)-connected scu topology for acetylene storage at ambient temperature. As an extension of our studies, we herein report the synthesis of a new rigid tetrapodal octadentenate ligand 5,5′,5″,5″′-silanetetrayltetraisophthalic acid (H8L, Scheme 1) and its resulting three MOFs [Cu4L(H2O)4]·2DMF·10H2O (1), [Zn5L2(H2O)4][NH2(CH3)2]6·2DMF·4H2O (2) and [Cd3(H2L)2][NH2(CH3)2]6·4DMF·14H2O (3). Their crystal structures, thermal stability, gas adsorption, and photoluminescent properties were studied in detail.

    Materials and General Methods

    All the chemicals except the ligand H8L were commercially purchased and used without further purification. Elemental analyses of C, H, N were performed on an Elementar Vario MICRO elemental analyzer at the Analysis Center of Nanjing University. Fourier transformed infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained on a Bruker Vector 22 FT-IR spectrophotometer by using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetrical analyses (TGA) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer thermal analyzer under nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C/min. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected in the 2θ = 5–50 deg range with a scan speed of 0.1 s/deg on a Bruker D8 Advance instrument using a Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) at room temperature. Photoluminescence analyses for the solid samples were performed on a Hitachi 850 fluorescence spectrophotometer. A Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area porosimetry system was used to measure gas adsorption. In order to remove guest solvent molecules in the framework, the samples were treated with supercritical carbon dioxide (SCD). The SCD processing was performed with a SFT-10 Pump linkup pressure vessel and a reactor temperature control system. The freshly prepared sample was immersed in CH2Cl2 for 48 h with fresh CH2Cl2 added every 8 h. After the solvent exchange, the samples were placed inside the pressure vessel. The CO2 was injected into the pressure vessel with a pressure of 1300 psi at a temperature of 50 °C. After the samples were kept in SCD for 8 h, the CO2 was slowly released in ca. 0.5 h. The above process was repeated four times to afford the SCD-activated samples. The samples were placed in a sealed container and stored in a glovebox or tested for gas adsorption immediately. The sorption measurements for N2, H2 and O2 were carried out at 77 K with liquid nitrogen, while those for CH4 and CO2 sorption were performed at 273 K with a water bath.

    Preparation of H8L

    The H8L ligand was synthesized by a two-step procedure. First, a mixture of 3,5-dibromotoluene (200 mmol) and lithium ribbon (550 mmol) in diethyl ether was stirred at 0 °C under Ar for 2 h. Then, SiCl4 (39 mmol) was added and the mixture was stirred at room temperature for 6 h. The mixture was quenched by water (100 mL) at 0 °C. A yellow solid (E1) was obtained by filter, washed with water, and dried under a vacuum at 50 °C. Yield 62%. Second, 6.2 mmol of E1 was dissolved in 150 mL of glacial acetic acid/acetic anhydride/sulphuric (10:4:1 in volume). The reaction mixture was stirred for 12 h at 0 °C. The product was decomposed with water (200 mL) and filtered. The green solid was dissolved in aqueous NaOH, and the solution was acidified with 6 M aqueous HCl at 0 °C. The precipitate was collected, washed with water, and dried under a vacuum at 50 °C. Yield: 28%. 1H NMR (DMSO-d6): 8.25 (8H, s), 8.64 (4H, s), 13.49 (8H, s). Elemental analysis, calculated for SiC32H20O16: C, 55.82; H, 2.99. Found: C, 55.91; H, 2.99.     Single-Crystal X-ray Crystallography

    The suitable crystals of compounds 1–3 were selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The data collections were carried out on a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD diffractometer at 296 K, using graphite-monochromated Mo–Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reductions and absorption corrections were performed using the SAINT and SADABS programs,(15) respectively. The structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS-97 program and refined with full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXL-97 program.(16) All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. The hydrogen atoms of water molecules are not located for compound 1. In addition, the hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecules could not be located for compound 2. In compound 3, the nitrogen atom of the guest NH2(CH3)2+ counterion was split over two sites (each having half-occupancy). The hydrogen atoms on the disordered atoms were not included in the model. Because of the highly disordered solvent molecules in compounds 1–3, the PLATON/SQUEEZE route was employed to calculate the diffraction contribution from the solvent molecules, and thereby to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities.(17) The final formula were derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analyses data. Details of the crystal parameters, data collection and refinements for complexes 1–3 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles are shown in Table S1 (Supporting Information).     Structure Description

    Compound 1 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group P4/nmm with two asymmetric units in one cell. Each asymmetric unit contains one-eighth of an L ligand, one Cu atom, and a coordinating water molecule. As shown in Figure 1, each Cu atom coordinates to four carboxylate groups from four different L8– ligands and a water molecule in a square-pyramidal geometry. The two adjacent CuII ions are linked together by four carboxylate groups to form a paddlewheel dinuclear cluster [Cu2(O2CR)4]. The Cu–O, Cu–Ow and Cu···Cu distances are 1.955(3)–1.969(3), 2.153(5), and 2.659(2) Å, respectively, which are consistent with those found in other [Cu2(O2CR)4] paddlewheel-based MOFs.(18, 11b) The ligand H8L, with its center Si atom sitting on the S4-symmetry tetrahedron center, is fully deprotonated and coordinates via its eight carboxylate groups to eight dinuclear cluster [Cu2(O2CR)4] in a bidentate fashion. This gives rise to a cube coordination environment of L.
    As shown in Figure 1, the framework of 1 is constructed from the 4-connected square planar [Cu2(O2CR)4] cluster and 8-connected cube ligand L. The connections between one L and eight paddlewheel [Cu2(O2CR)4] result in a cube-shaped building unit, which stacks over each other along the a-axis through sharing top and bottom faces to form a 1D square column. Each column is then linked with four neighboring ones via sharing common edges (i.e., [Cu2(O2CR)4] units) to generate a 3D framework. This leads to a highly open framework of 1. Viewing along the a-, b- and c-axes, there exist open channels of approximately 4.7 × 6.3, 4.7 × 6.3, and 7.2 × 7.2 Å2, respectively, in which disordered guest solvent molecules locate. PLATON(17) calculations show that the guest accessible void (1248.7 Å3 per unit cell) comprises 44.8% of the unit cell volume. From the topological viewpoint, 1 can be considered as a (4,8)-connected scu net with a point symbol of {4462}2{416612} (Figure 2). The paddlewheel [Cu2(O2CR)4] cluster and ligand L are considered as a 4-connected square planar and 8-connected cube node, respectively.
    Compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with four asymmetric units in one cell. In 2, there are three crystallographically independent Zn atoms (fully occupied Zn1 and Zn3, half-occupied Zn2), one L ligand, a DMF solvent molecules, and three NH2(CH3)2+ counterions. The NH2(CH3)2+ was likely generated via either hydrolysis or decarbonylation of DMF under solvothermal conditions, which is not without precedent.(19) As shown in Figure 3a, both Zn1 and Zn2 atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by four O atoms from four different L ligands. The Zn3 atom is four-coordinated by three O atoms from three L ligands and a water molecule, which is symmetry-related to another Zn3A to form a dinuclear SBU binded by two carboxylates. The Zn–O distances range from 1.923(2) to 2.004(4) Å, which are in normal ranges. The H8L ligand is fully deprotonated and links to four Zn1 atoms, two Zn2 atoms and two Zn3 atoms by monodentate and bidentate coordination modes (Figure 3a), forming a complicated 3D open framework structure. Considering the Zn1, Zn2, and dinuclear (Zn3)2 as respective 4-connected nodes and L ligand as a 8-connected node, compound 2 features a 4-nodal (4,4,4,8)-connected net with a point (Schläfli) symbol of {413615}2{4363}{4462}{456}2 (Figure 3b). The structure shows small 1D channels with a window size of 5.3 × 5.3 Å2 along the a axis, in which the disordered solvent molecules reside (Figure S4). The solvent accessible volume is estimated by using the PLATON program(17) to be about 27.4% of the total crystal volume.
    Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The asymmetric unit consists of a half-occupied Cd1 and a full-occupied Cd2 atom, one H2L ligand, four NH2(CH3)2+ counterions. As shown in Figure 4a, both Cd1 and Cd2 atoms are six-coordinated by four O atoms from two chelating carboxylate groups and two O atoms from two monodentate carboxylate groups to form a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd–O distances range from 2.2437(12) to 2.6019(11) Å, which are similar to those found in other Cd complexes.(20) The ligand H8L, partly deprotonated as H2L6–, connects to six Cd atoms in a monodentate and chelating coordination mode, respectively, with the rest two carboxylate groups uncoordinated (Figure 4a). The two crystalographically independent Cd atoms connect with four ligands, respectively, behaving as two different tetrahedral nodes, while the H2L6– ligand acts as a 6-connected node, linking to six Cd metals. This gives rise to a 3D framework of a 3-nodal (4,4,6) connected net with Schläfli symbol of {4363}{4462}2{47678}2 (Figure 4b). There are small 1D channels with a window size of 5.2 × 4.9 Å2 along the a axis, which are occupied by the disordered solvent molecules. The void volume is calculated by PLATON to be 30.4% of the total crystal volume (Figure S5).
    Compounds 1–3 exhibit different structure topologies, showing the diverse coordination modes of the ligand L. Though H8L is a rigid ligand, its phenyl rings can rotate with each other to form different coordination environments. In 1, H8L was fully deprotonated and cubically coordinated to eight paddlewheel clusters [Cu2(O2CR)4] in a chelating fashion. In 2, it was also fully deprotonated and highly distorted, 8-connecting with Zn atoms in monodentate and bidentate coordination modes. In 3, however, it was partly deprotonated, and acted as a 6-connecting linker to six Cd atoms in monodentate and chelating coordination modes. Furthermore, the inorganic structure building units (i.e., metal sites) in compounds 1–3 are also diversified. Compound 1 is highly symmetric, built on a square planar paddlewheel cluster. On the other hand, 2 and 3 possess a low symmetry, built on three and two kinds of distorted tetrahedron nodes with mono- and dinuclear metal units, respectively.
    It is noted that compounds 1 and 2 possess different structures with 8-connecting nodes. In general, seven-, eight-, and higher connected MOFs are relatively scarce and usually found in those with multinuclear metal clusters. The recent use of octadentate carboxylate ligands have witnessed a rapid growth of MOFs with 8-connecting node,(9, 13, 14) such as (4,8)-flu based on tetrahedral and cubical nodes, (4,8)-scu based on square-planar and cubical nodes. It can be expected that more MOFs with interesting structure topology could be obtained by using the octadentenate carboxylic acid H8L.

    Thermal Properties

    To investigate the thermal stability of compounds 1–3, the TG analyses were carried out (Figure 5). Compound 1 displayed a weight loss of 18.57% at 25–150 °C, corresponding to a loss of water molecules (calcd 18.92%). PXRD studies showed the framework of 1 remained intact after removal of water molecules (Figure S1). The weight losses from 250 to 400 °C likely contributed to the departure of the DMF molecules and the decomposition of the structure. Compounds 2 and 3 exhibited a similar TGA profile. The first weight loss occurred at 25–160 °C with weight losses of 6.59% for 2 (calcd 6.39%), and 9.34% (calcd 9.99%) for 3, respectively. The framework began to decompose with the slow release of guest molecules started from 160 to 400 °C, accompanied by the loss of the framework crystallinity. The abrupt weight loss at 400–500 °C could be attributed to the loss of the ligand.

    Adsorption Properties

    The X-ray structure analyses showed that compounds 1–3 consist of solvent accessible voids of 44.8, 27.4, and 30.4% of their respective total crystal volume. We thus carried out gas adsorption measurements over three compounds. The samples were treated with supercritical carbon dioxide for four times in order to remove guest solvent molecules in the framework. The results showed that parts of guest solvent molecules in compound 1 were removed (Figure S6), giving rise to permanent porosity, while compounds 2 and 3 were not stable after removal of guest molecules, which are consistent with the thermal stability studies. As shown in Figure 6, N2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K for 1 reveals a type-I behavior, indicating the microporous nature.     Photoluminescence Properties

    Recently, inorganic–organic coordination complexes, especially with d10 closed-shell metal centers, have been investigated for fluorescence properties owing to their potential applications as luminescent materials.(25) The luminescence properties of H8L, 2, and 3 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. As shown in Figure 8, the free H8L ligand shows a strong blue fluorescent emission peak at 444 nm upon excited at 365 nm, which is probably attributable to the π* → π transition.(26) Compounds 2 and 3 also exhibit a strong blue fluorescent emission peak with a maximum at 414 and 412 nm (excitation at 310 nm), respectively. It is well-known that both Zn2+ and Cd2+ ions are difficult to be oxidized or reduced, owing to their stable d10 configurations. As a result, the observed emissions of 2 and 3 can probably be attributed to a ligand-centered excited state with possible involvement of metal-to-ligand charge transfers.(26, 27) In comparison with the free ligand, the emissions in compounds 2 and 3 are blue-shifted, which could be probably due to the increased energy gaps between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the ligands when coordinating to the metal ions.

    Three new metal–organic coordination polymers were solvothermally synthesized by using a rigid octadentenate carboxylic acid. With the change of coordination of ligand to metal, these compounds possess different and interesting topological structures. Compound 1 exhibits permanent porosity and shows high H2 storage capacities under 1 atm at 77 K, and selective gas adsorption properties for CO2 over CH4. The preparations of 1–3 further demonstrate the promising route of judiciously choosing well-devised inorganic building blocks and organic ligands of certain geometry and functionality to build new MOFs with interesting structure and properties.
