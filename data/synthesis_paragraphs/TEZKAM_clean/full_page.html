Abstract

We report two new 3D structures, [Zn3(bpdc)3(2,2′‐dmbpy)] (DMF)x(H2O)y (1) and [Zn3(bpdc)3(3,3′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)4(H2O)0.5 (2), by methyl functionalization of the pillar ligand in [Zn3(bpdc)3(bpy)] (DMF)4⋅(H2O) (3) (bpdc=biphenyl‐4,4′‐dicarboxylic acid; z,z′‐dmbpy=z,z′‐dimethyl‐4,4′‐bipyridine; bpy=4,4′‐bipyridine). Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis indicates that 2 is isostructural to 3, and the power X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) study shows a very similar framework of 1 to 2 and 3. Both 1 and 2 are 3D porous structures made of Zn3(COO)6 secondary building units (SBUs) and 2,2′‐ or 3,3′‐dmbpy as pillar ligand. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and PXRD studies reveal high thermal and water stability for both compounds. Gas‐adsorption studies show that the reduction of surface area and pore volume by introducing a methyl group to the bpy ligand leads to a decrease in H2 uptake for both compounds. However, CO2 adsorption experiments with 1′ (guest‐free 1) indicate significant enhancement in CO2 uptake, whereas for 2′ (guest‐free 2) the adsorbed amount is decreased. These results suggest that there are two opposing and competitive effects brought on by methyl functionalization: the enhancement due to increased isosteric heats of CO2 adsorption (Qst), and the detraction due to the reduction of surface area and pore volume. For 1′, the enhancement effect dominates, which leads to a significantly higher uptake of CO2 than its parent compound 3′ (guest‐free 3). For 2′, the detraction effect predominates, thereby resulting in reduced CO2 uptake relative to its parent structure 3′. IR and Raman spectroscopic studies also present evidence for strong interaction between CO2 and methyl‐functionalized π moieties. Furthermore, all compounds exhibit high separation capability for CO2 over other small gases including CH4, CO, N2, and O2.

Introduction

Microporous metal–organic frameworks (MMOFs) have received substantial attention over the past decade because of their attractive properties and potential in various applications.1 Current and intensive research and development on MMOF materials have revealed the interesting, unique, and useful porosity‐related functionality of these crystalline solids, thus making them highly promising for utilization in various areas, including, but not limited to, storage and separation of small gases and hydrocarbons,2 catalysis,3 guest‐ and/or pore‐induced magnetization,4 and sensing and detection.5

Today, effective carbon dioxide capture and sequestration (CCS) is becoming one of the most critical and urgent missions for the entire human society to slow down global warming, a direct consequence of rapid CO2 accumulation in the atmosphere.6 Considerable effort has been made to develop effective methods and materials capable of capturing and sequestering CO2.7 Recent work on MMOFs has demonstrated their potential for use as adsorbent materials in adsorption‐based separation.2c, 8 Among continuous efforts to improve their performance,8g, 8h, 9 the modification of the MMOF framework with a chemical functional group has been considered the most effective way to enhance their CO2 affinity, and consequently improve their CO2 uptake and separation capacity.10 However, unlike the amino11 and hydroxy12 groups, which have been extensively used to functionalize MMOFs, the methyl group has seldom been considered a real functional group and intensively studied. This is mostly due to its relatively “inert” chemical nature. However, our recent studies reveal the two opposing and competitive effects that methyl functionalization of the MMOFs can have on CO2 adsorption: a positive effect in terms of the enhancement of CO2‐binding affinity, and a negative effect in terms of the reduction of pore space.13 The study is in agreement with the results obtained from a previous theoretical work, which stated that the interaction between an aromatic moiety and CO2 can be strengthened by the introduction of an electron‐donating group such as CH3, and the CO2‐loaded complex can be stabilized by the hydrogen‐bond‐like interactions with methyl hydrogen atoms.14 The enhanced interaction between a methyl group and CO2 might override the reduction in pore size caused by the addition of CH3 to the ligand, and thus facilitate the adsorption of CO2. In addition, introducing a CH3 functional group might bring about two additional advantageous features: the potential to prevent the occurrence of high‐degree framework interpenetration and/or severe structure distortion upon guest removal, and the enhancement of water/moisture stability of the frameworks due to the introduction of hydrophobic CH3 groups.15

To further confirm and better understand the effect of introducing a methyl group on the CO2 adsorption and other functions of MMOFs, we have prepared two methyl‐functionalized structures, [Zn3(bpdc)3(2,2′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)x(H2O)y (1) and [Zn3(bpdc)3(3,3′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)4(H2O)0.5 (2), and their parent compound [Zn3(bpdc)3(bpy)]⋅(DMF)4⋅(H2O) (3)16 (bpdc=biphenyl‐4,4′‐dicarboxylic acid; z,z′‐dmbpy=z,z′‐dimethyl‐4,4′‐bipyridine; bpy=4,4′‐bipyridine) and systemically investigated their gas‐adsorption and separation properties. The effects of methyl functional groups in these structures are analyzed and compared.

Results and Discussion

All three compounds were synthesized by solvothermal reactions. Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis revealed that the methyl‐functionalized compound 2 is isostructural to compound 3. As in 3, compound 2 is also constructed of trimetallic Zn clusters as secondary building units, or SBUs (Figure 1), in which two crystallographically independent Zn atoms are found: one octahedrally coordinated central Zn atom (Zn1), and two tetrahedrally coordinated outer Zn atoms (Zn2). Each cluster unit is connected with six neighboring units through six bpdc ligands to form a 2D layer. The 3D framework is established through the coordination of 3,3′‐dmbpy pillar ligands to the outer Zn2 atoms that connect two adjacent layers. Two such identical 3D motifs interpenetrate to complete the overall framework. As methyl groups take up additional space in the pores, the solvent‐accessible volume of 2, calculated by PLATON as 39 %, is slightly lower than that of 3 (41 %). Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) study shows a very similar framework of compound 1 to those of 2 and 3.

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) reveals high thermal stability of all three compounds (see Figures S7–S9 in the Supporting Information). The first continuous weight losses (18.81 % for 1, 20.47 % for 2, and 21.46 % for 3) up to approximately 200 °C can be assigned to the loss of solvent molecules. Upon removal of all guest molecules, the guest‐free frameworks of 1, 2, and 3 (designated 1′, 2′, and 3′, respectively) remain stable up to approximately 375 °C. The experimental mass losses of compounds 2 and 3 are in good agreement with the calculated values from single‐crystal data: 21.46 % for 2, and 22.50 % for 3. The purity of the three compounds was examined by their PXRD patterns (Figure 2). The PXRD pattern of guest‐free framework 3′ confirms that the framework remains highly crystalline after outgassing. Slight peak shifts or splitting are observed after outgassing compounds 1′ and 2′. We speculate that the differences in PXRD might be due to a slight structure distortion upon guest removal. To investigate the effect of CH3 on the water stability, compounds 1 and 3 were tested in a water‐vapor (323 K) environment. Figures S4 and S5 in the Supporting Information show that the framework of 1 remains unchanged after exposure to water vapor for up to 10 days, whereas its parent structure 3 (without CH3 functionalization) shows significant degradation after exposure over the same length of time. These results show that functionalizing the framework with hydrophobic groups such as a CH3 might effectively enhance the stability of MOF structures under moisture.13

Owing to their very slow diffusion rate, the porosity of compounds 1 and 2 could not be characterized by N2‐adsorption measurements. As an alternative, CO2‐adsorption experiments at 273 K were carried out to evaluate the pore characteristics of all three compounds 1, 2, and 3 (Table 1). Although the results (e.g., surface‐area values) obtained from CO2‐adsorption data are typically lower than those from N2‐ or Ar‐adsorption measurements conducted at 77 and 87 K, respectively, they provide qualitative information on the porosity of the structures and are helpful for a comparison of three related compounds. Here, the calculated pore diameters, determined by the Horvath–Kawazoe (HK) method,17 are 7.58, 7.67, and 7.58 Å for 1, 2, and 3 respectively, which indicates that the overall pore diameters are not significantly affected upon methyl functionalization.18 The results are in excellent agreement with the values calculated from the structure data (an effective maximum dimension of approximately 8 Å for the window opening). The pore volumes of 1′, 2′, and 3′ were calculated by the HK method as 0.18, 0.13, and 0.21 cm3 g−1 for 1′, 2′, and 3′, respectively (AS1win 2.01, Quantachrome, Inc.). The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and Langmuir surface areas are estimated to be 308/466, 195/378, and 457/804 m2 g−1 for compounds 1′, 2′, and 3′, respectively. Compared to the parent structure 3′, the significant reduction of both surface area and pore volume in the functionalized compounds 1′ and 2′ is a direct consequence of space occupation by the methyl groups in the voids. It is worth noting that the surface area and pore volume of 2′ are appreciably smaller than those of 1′. One possible reason is that in the structure of 2′, the two methyl groups at the 3‐position on the pyridine rings partially point to the channel due to the steric repulsion, thus severely reducing the pore diameters and lowering the surface area and pore volume. According to the crystal data, the dihedral angles between two pyridine rings are 66.5° for 2′ and 22.9° for 3′, which indicates the existence of repulsion between the two methyl groups.

The hydrogen‐adsorption and ‐desorption isotherms of compounds 1′, 2′, and 3′ were measured at 77 K and plotted in Figure 3. The uptake of hydrogen corresponds to 1.5, 1.3, and 1.7 wt % at 1 atm for 1′, 2′, and 3′, respectively. Clearly, the parent structure 3′ has higher hydrogen‐adsorption capacity over the two methyl‐group‐functionalized compounds 1′ and 2′. Apparently, the uptake amount of H2 has the same trend with the surface areas and pore volumes of 1′, 2′, and 3′. It is again consistent with our previous findings that the pore properties (pore volume and surface area) play the dominant role in the H2 uptake when the adsorbate–adsorbent interactions are similar.

The CO2‐adsorption isotherms for evacuated compounds 1′, 2′, and 3′ were collected at 273, 288, and 298 K. Figure 4 shows the data recorded at room temperature. As seen from the isotherm data, despite the significantly reduced surface area and pore volume with respect to the parent structure 3′, methyl‐functionalized compound 1′ shows the highest CO2‐capture ability among the three compounds, with a maximum uptake of 11.5 wt % at 298 K and 1 atm. For the other methyl‐functionalized compound 2′, which has the lowest surface area and pore volume, the CO2 uptake is 8.5 wt % under the same conditions. The CO2 uptake is 10.2 wt % for compound 3′ at 298 K and 1 atm. This result clearly shows that the pore properties (pore volume and surface area) have contributing but not dominating roles in the CO2 uptake.

To further evaluate and understand the role of methyl groups, we analyzed the interactions between CO2 and methyl‐functionalized frameworks by calculating the isosteric heats (Qst) of CO2 adsorption on the basis of the adsorption isotherms collected at 273, 288, and 298 K for 1′, 2′, and 3′ (Figure 5). As shown in Figure 5, the Qst values are 29.1–31.6, 25.2–30.8, and 28.7–30.5 kJ mol−1 for 1′, 2′, and 3′ over the entire loading range for each compound. It is worth noting that the Qst values at zero loading for both methyl‐functionalized compounds (31.6 kJ mol−1 for 1′ and 30.8 kJ mol−1 for 2′) are higher than that of the parent structure 3′ (29.0 kJ mol−1), which is indicative of stronger interactions between CO2 and the methyl‐modified frameworks. Both the experimental isotherm data and the calculated Qst values can be considered solid evidence in support of the theoretical results:14 The introduction of the methyl group directly attached to the aromatic ring (although with a reduction in pore space) can significantly enhance the quadrupole–π‐electron interaction due to the smaller pore diameter and electron donation of methyl group to the aromatic system, and as a result it leads to enhanced CO2 uptake. Relative to parent compound 3′, data obtained for the two methyl‐functionalized frameworks 1′ and 2′ also reveal two competitive factors that affect the CO2 uptake: the positive effect in terms of the enhancement of CO2 affinity through functionalization, and the negative effect in terms of pore‐space reduction due to the addition of the methyl group. The two contrary effects are coinstantaneous and competitive during CO2 adsorption. When the positive effect dominates and overrides the space‐loss effect during CO2 adsorption, the strong increase in CO2 affinity is achieved, and accordingly, the CO2 uptake capacity will be enhanced considerably. As in the case of compound 1′, the amount of CO2 uptake is enhanced by the methyl functionalization regardless of the reduction in surface area and pore volume of 1′ relative to those of 3′, thus exemplifying the positive effect of ligand functionalization on CO2 adsorption. On the other hand, if the increase in the CO2 affinity is not high enough to compensate the loss of pore volume and surface area, the CO2‐adsorption capacity will be decreased. For compound 2′, despite the higher Qst value observed at the lower CO2‐loading range (<1.7 wt %), the dramatically reduced surface area and pore volume of 2′ make the negative effect dominant in the competition, which gives rise to the decrease in CO2 uptake relative to 3′.

We also carried out adsorption studies with other small gases, including CH4, N2, CO, and O2, to assess the effects of methyl functionalization on the CO2‐adsorption selectivity of 1′ and 2′ relative to 3′. Figures S9–S11 in the Supporting Information show the adsorption isotherms of all small gases of 1′, 2′, and 3′, respectively. The single‐component separation ratio of CO2/CH4, CO2/N2, CO2/CO, and CO2/O2, defined as q1/q2 at a specified pressure (for which qi is the adsorbed amount of a pure gas), were 3.7, 12.2, 8.4, and 13.1 for compound 1′; 2.4, 8.8, 5.8, and 8.4 for compound 2′; and 2.4, 10.1, 6.9, and 10.8 for compound 3′ at 298 K and 1 atm (Table 2). The enhancement of selectivity is observed for compound 1′ over 3′, whereas the separation ratios are lower for 2′ than 3′, which is consistent with the trend in their single‐gas adsorption. Overall, these values are lower than RPM3‐Zn and RPM4‐Zn structures,17 which show exceptionally high CO2‐adsorption selectivity over other small gases. A possible reason is the relatively large pore diameters of the three title compounds relative to RPM3‐Zn and RPM4‐Zn.

To characterize the nature of the chemical interactions of H2 and CO2 in these frameworks, we performed IR absorption spectroscopy measurements on 2′. Figure 6 shows the IR absorption spectrum of H2 at room temperature and 55 bar referenced to activated compound 2′ under vacuum. After subtraction of the H2 gas phase as described earlier,19 an approximately −31 cm−1 redshift of the H2 vibrational stretch mode relative to the unperturbed ν(ortho‐H2) at 4155 cm−1 is observed; these shifts are similar to those observed in 1D pore structure MOFs with aliphatic linkers. IR frequency shifts of H2 interacting with the aromatic linkers was found to be slightly larger than those observed in this study.19 This result is a strong indicator of an interaction of the H2 with the CH3 groups at these conditions. IR absorption spectroscopy measurements were also performed for CO2 adsorption in 2. Figure 7 shows the spectrum of adsorbed CO2 when loaded at 1 torr and room temperature referenced to MOF under vacuum with a subtraction of the gas phase. An approximately −12 cm−1 redshift of the (CO) asymmetric stretch mode of CO2 is observed. This shift suggests an enhanced interaction of the CO2, mediated with its carbon, as an electron acceptor that interacts with the CH3‐modified π‐conjugated species dmbpy with a stronger electron‐donating effect than bpy.20

Raman spectroscopy measurements are complimentary to IR spectroscopy and give information about both the symmetric CO2 stretching mode and structural changes of the framework itself. Raman spectra of CO2 adsorption as a function of temperature are shown in Figure 8. An atmosphere of CO2 was introduced at room temperature and kept up to −60 °C. At room temperature, an approximately −6 cm−1 redshift in the symmetric CO2 stretching mode is observed, thus indicating an interaction of the CO2 as an electron acceptor. As the temperature is decreased, an additional 2 cm−1 redshift was observed, thus indicating the occupation of additional sites at the higher loadings or to CO2–CO2 interactions.

No changes in the Raman modes of the framework are observed as CO2 is adsorbed, which is different than what was observed in [Zn2(bpdc)2(bpee)] (bpee=1,2‐bipyridylethene).21 This is consistent with the connectivity of the Zn metal center with the bpdc linker in a different way than that in [Zn2(bpdc)2(bpee)].

Conclusion

In summary, we have synthesized and characterized two new 3D frameworks, [Zn3(bpdc)3(2,2′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)x(H2O)y and [Zn3(bpdc)3(3,3′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)4(H2O)0.5, by modifying their parent structure [Zn3(bpdc)3(bpy)]⋅(DMF)4⋅(H2O) with methyl‐functionalized pillar ligands. A gas‐adsorption study shows the decrease of H2‐uptake capacity upon methyl functionalization, due to the reduction in surface area and pore volume as a result of methyl occupation in the pore space. However, for CO2 adsorption, very different results are found in the two methyl‐functionalized compounds, thus indicating the two opposing and competitive effects brought by methyl functionalization: enhanced CO2 affinity and reduced porosity. When the positive effect is dominant in the competition, enhanced CO2 affinity overrides the loss induced by pore and surface‐area reduction, and thereby facilitates the CO2‐uptake capacity of the framework. On the other hand, if the enhanced CO2 affinity cannot compensate for the loss of porosity, the negative effect will result in the decrease of CO2‐adsorption ability. IR and Raman spectroscopy measurements support the enhanced CO2–dmbpy interaction as a result of the stronger electron‐donating feature of dmbpy toward electron‐accepting CO2, which is mediated by the presence of the methyl group in 2′.

Experimental Section

Materials

All chemicals were purchased from commercial sources (Alfa Aesar, Acros, Aldrich or TCI America) and used as received. The ligands 2,2′‐dmbpy and 3,3′‐dmbpy were synthesized according to the literature with modifications.22

2,2′‐Dimethyl‐4,4′‐bipyridine (2,2′‐dmbpy)

Sodium (1 g, 44 mmol) was added to dry 2‐methylpyridine (80 mL) in very small pieces under stirring and purged with N2. The reaction was kept at room temperature for 48 h to dissolve the sodium, and the solution turned deep blue. Digestion of the sodium was completed by further heating at 90 °C for 9 h. Oxygen was then carefully bubbled into the solution for half an hour and a pale yellow solution was formed. After cooling to room temperature, the excess amount of 2‐methylpyridine was removed by high vacuum. The residue was subjected to chromatography on a silica column with EtOAc to yield a yellow product, which was further purified by the recrystallization from n‐hexane/petroleum ether (10:2). Yield: 22.5 %; m.p. 69–71 °C; 1H NMR (400 MHz, CDCl3): δ=8.60 (d, J=5.01 Hz, 2 H; ArH), 7.38 (s, 2 H; ArH), 7.32 (d, J=4.90 Hz, 2 H; ArH), 2.64 ppm (s, 6 H; CH3); 13C NMR (100 MHz, CDCl3): δ=24.56, 118.61, 121.00, 146.18, 149.91, 159.37 ppm; IR (KBr): =3023, 2968, 2922, 1595, 1535, 1443, 1372, 1038, 827, 625 cm−1.

3,3′‐Dimethyl‐4,4′‐bipyridine (3,3′‐dmbpy)

3,3′‐dmbpy was synthesized from the 3‐methylpyridine and Na by using the same procedure described above for 2,2′‐dmbpy. Yield: 35 %, m.p. 75–76 °C; 1H NMR (400 MHz, CDCl3): δ=2.07 (s, 6 H; CH3), 7.01 (d, J=4.91 Hz, 2 H; ArH), 8.51 (d, J=4.92 Hz, 2 H; ArH), 8.57 ppm (s, 2 H; ArH); 13C NMR (100 MHz, CDCl3): δ=16.49, 122.84, 130.44, 146.40, 147.48, 151.29 ppm; IR (KBr): =3033.8, 2966.3, 2925.8, 1596.6, 1568.0, 1537.2,1473.5, 1379.0, 1037.6, 896.8, 634.5 cm−1.

Compound 1

[Zn3(bpdc)3(2,2′‐dmbpy)]⋅(DMF)x(H2O)y was synthesized by the reaction of a mixture of Zn(NO3)⋅6 H2O (0.089 g, 0.3 mmol), 2,2′‐dmbpy (0.019 g, 0.1 mmol), and bpdc (0.072 g, 0.3 mmol) in DMF (10 mL) in a Teflon‐lined autoclave at 85 °C for 4 days. Yellow block‐shaped crystals of 1 were isolated by filtration and washed with DMF.

Compound 2

Thermogravimetric Analysis

The thermal properties of the compound were evaluated with a TA Instruments Q50 TG instrument. The samples (≈10 mg) were loaded onto a sample pan and heated from room temperature to 600 °C at a rate of 10 °C min−1 under N2 (flow and purge rate at 40 and 60 mL min−1, respectively).

Gas‐Adsorption Experiments

All gas‐sorption experiments were performed on a volumetric gas‐sorption analyzer (Autosorb‐1‐MP, Quantachrome Instruments). Liquid nitrogen was used as a coolant to achieve cryogenic temperatures (77 K). Ultra‐high‐purity H2, N2, CO, CO2, and CH4 (99.999 %) were used. The initial outgassing process for each sample was carried out at 373 K for all three compounds under vacuum overnight. Outgassed samples in the amount of approximately 85–90 mg were used for gas‐sorption measurements, and the weight of each sample was recorded before and after outgassing to confirm the removal of guest molecules and to ensure the framework integrity. The outgassing procedure was repeated on the same sample between experiments for 0.5–1 h. Pore properties (e.g., pore volume, pore size, and surface area) were analyzed with Autosorb v1.50 software. Temperature‐dependent adsorption–desorption isotherms of CO2 (278, 288, and 298 K) on 1′, 2′, and 3′ were collected from 10−3 to 1 atm and are depicted in Figures S7–S9 in the Supporting Information. Plots in Figures S10–S12 in the Supporting Information are isotherms of CO2 in 1′, 2′, and 3′ compared with those of other selected small gases (298 K and up to 1 atm).

IR and Raman Experiments

IR absorption spectroscopy measurements were performed in transmission at room temperature with a liquid N2 cooled InSb detector. Approximately 12 mg of the samples was pressed onto a KBr support and mounted into a high‐temperature, high‐pressure cell (Specac P/NH 5850c). Raman spectroscopy measurements were performed using a 532 nm solid‐state laser. The activated sample was loaded into a Linkam FTIR600 cooling/heating stage. A laser power of approximately 10 % (1.23 mW) was used to avoid degradation of the sample under the beam.
