Auxiliary ligand-directed and counter anion-templated effects on coordination networks based on semirigid 2-aminodiacetic terephthalic acid ligand†

Jie Ma ab, Lian Chen a, Ming-Yan Wu a, Shu-Quan Zhang ab, Ke-Cai Xiong ab, Dong Han ab, Fei-Long Jiang *a and Mao-Chun Hong *a

E-mail: hmc@fjirsm.ac.cn; fjiang@fjirsm.ac.cn; Fax: +86-591-83794946; Tel: +86-591-83792460

Reactions of the semirigid polycarboxylate ligand, 2-aminodiacetic terephthalic acid (H4adtp), with Zn(II) and Cd(II) salts resulted in {[Zn2(adtp)(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1) and [Cd2(adtp)(μ2-H2O)(H2O)3]n (3), respectively. By introducing auxiliary ligand 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy), two 3D regularly arranged pillared-layered frameworks {[Zn3(adtp)(bpy)1.5(μ3-OH)(H2O)]·NO3·3H2O}n (2a) and {[Cd4(adtp)2(bpy)(H2O)4]·2.5H2O}n (4) were obtained. Complex 1 is characterized as a two-dimensional (2D) layer structure, whereas complex 3 features a three-dimensional (3D) framework with two types of alternately arrayed channels. In complex 2a, the one-dimensional (1D) zinc chains built up of alternately interlinked tetranuclear and dinuclear units are cross-linked into 2D layers, which are further pillared by bpy into a 3D cationic framework with NO3− as counterions. As for 4, a 3D pillared-layer framework is observed and comprises carboxylic μ2-O bridged –M–O–M– chains. The auxiliary ligand bpy exerts a structure-directing effect on the resultant 3D pillared-layer architectures in 2a and 4, wherein μ3-OH bridged tetranuclear metal units or carboxylic μ2-O bridged –M–O–M– chains appear. Additionally, the cationic framework in 2a is strongly dependent on counter anions and the concentration of bpy ligand. The isomorphic {[Zn3(adtp)(bpy)1.5(μ3-OH)(H2O)]·ClO4·3H2O}n (2b) with ClO4− as template and counterions is also obtained. The luminescent properties of 1, 2a, 3 and 4 are investigated in detail. Compared with free H4adtp, complex 3 exhibits a larger blue-shifted emission band. Complex 2a displays tunable photoluminescence by variation of excitation light and temperature due to multi-centers emissions.

Introduction

Versatile architectures and intriguing topologies of coordination networks not only aesthetically attract structural chemists but also have intimate associations with intrinsic physicochemical properties involving the potential and practical applications in gas storage, magnetic materials, luminescent probes and sensors, etc.1–3 An ever increasing scientific effort has been devoted to the construction of extended coordination networks expecting successful prediction and rational design of default structures. The disciplines of reticular chemistry as a useful tool are utilized to the syntheses of predetermined ordered frameworks constructed from well-defined rigid molecular building blocks.4 For reticular chemistry, symmetrical and rigid-shaped multimetallic cluster with organic spacers is the critical condition. However, the semirigid and flexible polycarboxylate ligands adopt various binding modes so that the metal centers inevitably form diverse polynuclear metal units and chains, which make the rational design of default structures very tricky and confusing.5 Therefore, new synthesis strategies are instructive to the rational construction of specific architectures such as pillared-layer frameworks. Metal cluster units or chains containing different terminal sites can be substituted by rigid multidentate N-donor ligands resulting in higher-dimensional frameworks. An effective and controllable route is to introduce rigid multidentate N-donor ligands as struts to connect well-defined ready two-dimensional (2D) layers to construct three-dimensional (3D) pillared-layer frameworks.6 On the other hand, the introduction of rigid multidentate N-donor ligands may result in pillared-layer regular arrangement of coordination networks with layers that cannot exist independently.7

Besides the coordination geometries of metal ions and the organic ligands, the construction of coordination networks is affected by unpredictable roles of various synthesis conditions such as solvent, temperature, concentration, pH value, template, counterion, etc.8 The application of templates as a well-established approach are most successfully used in the rational synthesis of coordination networks and supramolecular assemblies.9 Templates not only facilitate the crystallization of well-defined molecular architectures such as macrocycles and cages, interlocked species, helical assemblies and other metal–organic frameworks, but also trigger crystal-to-crystal transformation.10 Anions as templates play crucial roles in framework support and charge balance to their hosts. Whether anions are valid templates or not is profoundly influenced by the intrinsic properties of anions such as characteristic size, charge, shape, hydrogen bond, and even the anion–π interaction.11

Our efforts focus on the construction of pillared-layer structures and roles of the synthetic conditions in these processes. In this context, the bpy ligand as strut was introduced to facilitate the construction of the pillared-layer coordination networks based on the semirigid polycarboxylate H4adtp ligand with layers that cannot exist independently. Bpy ligand as a good candidate is commonly engaged in the construction of porous pillared-layer frameworks.12 We have previous employed bpy as spacers to pillar adjacent 2D layers in {[Ni4(adip)2(H2O)10]·6H2O}n (H4adip = 5-aminodiacetic isophthalic acid) by substituting the coordination waters in layers.13 This success promotes us to continue exploiting the further functions of the bpy ligand in similar systems. The semirigid H4adtp, an isomer of H4adip, is chosen as the polycarboxylate ligand.14 The flexible aminodiacetic moiety and the ortho-carboxylate group of the H4adtp ligand can be regarded as a tripod with two acetic functionalities and one ortho-phenyl carboxylate group acting as arms attached to one head imino nitrogen atom. The three arms in possession of three carboxylate groups can provide six potential binding points to chelate and/or bridge metal centers to polynuclear metal units or chains. The residual phenyl carboxylate group can extend in another direction. The abundant coordination modes of H4adtp provide us with an excellent platform to investigate the functions of the bpy spacer on the construction of pillared-layer regular arranged coordination networks. In absence of bpy ligand, {[Zn2(adtp)(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1) and [Cd2(adtp)(μ2-H2O)(H2O)3]n (3) are first synthesized by a solvothermal method. The introduction of the bpy ligand results in {[Zn3(adtp)(bpy)1.5(μ3-OH)(H2O)]·X·3H2O}n (X = NO3− (2a), ClO4− (2b)) and {[Cd4(adtp)2(bpy)(H2O)4]·2.5H2O}n (4). Meanwhile, the effects of anionic templates and concentration of bpy ligand on the frameworks of 2a and 2b are investigated systematically. Furthermore, luminescence properties of the complexes are discussed in detail.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

H4adtp was prepared according to the literature.15 The other chemicals were commercially purchased and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H, N were performed on a German Elementary Vario EL III instrument. The FT-IR spectra were obtained on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses were recorded on a NETZSCH STA 449C unit at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. The power X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were collected by a Rigaku DMAX2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.154 nm). Emission and excitation spectra and transient decays were measured on an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920 spectrofluorimeter equipped with both continuous and pulse xenon lamps (450 W). The absolute quantum yield (QY) of ZrO2:Eu3+ NPs was recorded at room temperature by employing a barium sulfate coated integrating sphere (Edinburgh) as the sample chamber that was mounted on the fluorimeter (FLS920) with the entry and output port of the sphere located at a 90° geometry from each other in the plane of the spectrometer. For low temperature measurements, samples were mounted on a closed cycle cryostat (10–350 K, DE202, Advanced Research Systems).

X-ray crystallography

All the structures data of complexes 1–4 were collected on a Rigaku Mercury CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. All of the structures were resolved by the direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 by SHELX-97.16 All nonhydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. The hydrogen atoms of water molecules were found in the electron density map and refined by riding except O11, O12 atoms in 1, hydroxyl-O9, O10 atoms in 2a, O9 atom in 3, and O17, O18, O19, O20, O21, O22, O23 atoms in 4, which cannot be found. The other hydrogen atoms were located at geometrically calculated positions and refined by riding. O21 in 4 possesses the occupancy of 0.5. In complex 1, O11 and O12 were restrained using the ISOR command on SHELXL-97 due to their large anisotropical thermal displacement parameters. For complex 2a, due to the disorder of NO3− group and lattice water molecules, suitable models cannot be found and the Platon SQUEEZE program was employed to extract the electron density. The removed electron density of ∼277.7 e− per unit cell agrees with the chemically determined {[Zn3(adtp)(bpy)1.5(μ3-OH)(H2O)]·NO3·3H2O}n formula, and Z = 4 (248 e−). The structure of 2b is an isomorph of 2a, which was demonstrated by the power X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns. Result and discussion

The coordination modes of H4adtp ligand are showed in Scheme 1. The following structure discussion is based on the CIF files of complexes 1–4 and the final formulae of complexes 1–4 are further determined by elemental analyses and thermogravimetric studies.
Scheme 1 The coordination modes of H4adtp in complexes 1–4.

Description of {[Zn2(adtp)(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1)

Complex 1 crystallizes in a triclinic P space group. The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically distinct Zn(II) ions, one adtp4− ligand, two terminal water ligands and two free aqua molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, Zn1 and Zn2 are located in a distorted octahedral geometry and trigonal bipyramid environment, respectively. As illustrated in Scheme 1A, four carboxylate groups of the adtp4− ligand adopt monodentate, syn–anti bidentate bridging and μ2-η2:η1 bridging coordination modes to link five different metal centers. Ligand adip4− chelates three basal and one apical sites of Zn2 using the imino nitrogen atom (N1) and carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2, O3 and O5) of its three arms. The residual basal site is occupied by one phenyl carboxylate oxygen atom (O7C) from a second adtp4− ligand. Zn2 is bridged to Zn1 via one syn–anti coordinating carboxylate group of one short arm with a Zn1⋯Zn2 distance of 5.2252(10) Å. One axial and one equatorial sites of Zn1 are ligated by two terminal water ligands (O9 and O10). The residual three face-related sites are afforded to one chelating bidentate carboxylate group (O1A and O2A) and one syn–anti carboxylate bridge (O4B).

(b) Tetranuclear zinc unit. The dashed lines represent the weak interaction. Symmetry codes: A: −x, 1 − y, 2 − z; B: 1 − x, 1 − y, 2 − z; C: −1 + x, y, z. (c) 2D layer. (d) The stacking motif of the layers. Green dashed lines represent hydrogen bonds.

As visualized in Fig. 1b, the asymmetric unit generates its symmetry equivalent by an inverse operation. The inverse center is situated in the midpoint of two symmetry equivalent zinc atoms. Zinc atoms from different asymmetric unit are bridged into a dinuclear unit by μ2-bridging carboxylate oxygen atom (O2) of the long arm. The closest metal–metal distance (Zn1⋯Zn2A or Zn2⋯Zn1A) in the dinuclear unit is 3.7731(9) Å. The carboxylate O3 atoms (O3B and O3C) from the short arms present weak interactions to the closest Zn2 atoms (Zn2 and Zn2A) with the distance of 2.6349(34) Å. All the adtp4− ligands are parallel to bind metal centers with an alternately reversed arrangement (Fig. 1b and (c). 1D zinc chains are formed and cross-linked by adtp4− ligands into a 2D layer (Fig. 1c). Two different types of intermolecular hydrogen bonds are observed between adjacent layers (Fig. S3, ESI†). As depicted in Fig. S3, ESI,† the free carboxylate oxygen atoms O8 are hydrogen bonded to terminal water ligands O10, resulting in an O8B⋯O10A distance of 2.6866(60) Å. Additionally, the equivalent terminal water ligands (O9) form double hydrogen bonds with O9⋯O9A length of 2.8894(61) Å. Finally, the 2D layers are assembled into a 3D supramolecular structure through intermolecular hydrogen bonds (Fig. 1d).

Description of {[Zn3(adtp)(bpy)1.5(μ3-OH)(H2O)]·X·3H2O}n (X = NO3− (2a), ClO4− (2b))

Power XRD analyses of 2a and 2b reveal that they are isostructural, and herein only 2a is described in detail as a representative example. The complex 2a belongs to a triclinic P space group. The asymmetric unit consists of three crystallographically unique Zn(II) ions, one adtp4− anion, one and a half bpy ligands, one μ3-OH group and one terminal water ligand, as well as three lattice water molecules and one guest NO3− ion. The adip4− ligand adopts coordination mode B to connect six metal centers via one monodentate carboxylate μ2-oxygen bridge, two syn–anti and one syn–syn bidentate bridging carboxylate groups (Scheme 1B). As illustrated in Fig. 2a, Zn1 lies in a tetrahedron coordination geometry with four vertices occupied by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O4A and O7B), one μ3-hydroxy oxygen atom (O9) and one bipyridyl nitrogen atom (N4). The other two metal centers Zn2 and Zn3 are located in six-coordinated octahedral environments. Two symmetry related μ3-hydroxy oxygen atoms O9 and O9D bind to Zn2 at equatorial and axial sites, respectively. The other three equatorial sites are afforded to two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O6 and O8C) and one terminal aqua ligand (O10), while the other axial site is occupied by one bipyridyl nitrogen atom (N3). Zn3 is chelated by an adtp4− anion via its imino nitrogen atom (N1) and carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O3 and O5) of its three arms. The remaining two cis-related coordination sites are occupied by one bipyridyl nitrogen atom (N2E) and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O1A).

Symmetry codes: A: 1 − x, 2 − y, −z; B: 1½ + x, −1 + y, ½ − z; C: x, −1 + y, z, D: 1½ − x, y, ½ − z, E: 2 − x, 2 − y, −z. (b) Tetranuclear and dinuclear zinc units. Symmetry codes: A: 1½ − x, y, ½ − z; B: 1 − x, 2 − y, −z; C: ½ + x, 2 − y, ½ + z. (c) 1D zinc chain. (d) 2D layer. (e) 3D pillared-layer architecture. Polyhedrons: violet for Zn1 and Zn2 and green for Zn3.

The intriguing tetranuclear and dinuclear metal units appear simultaneously in the structure (Fig. 2b). Two μ3-OH groups and two syn–syn bidentate bridging carboxylate groups interlink four Zn atoms (Zn1 and Zn1A; Zn2 and Zn2A) to form a boat-like tetranuclear zinc cluster with non-bonding Zn⋯Zn distances of 3.2529(5)–3.5126(5) Å. Two symmetry related Zn3 atoms are doubly bridged to a dinuclear zinc cluster by two equivalent carboxylate μ2-O1 atoms from short arms. Each tetranuclear unit is connected to two dinuclear units. The alternately arranged tetranuclear and dinuclear units are interlinked into a zigzag chain via syn–anti bidentate bridging carboxylate groups (Fig. 2b and c). The single chains are parallel and cross-linked into a 2D layer with the spacer phenyl ring between two adjacent chains (Fig. 2d). The 2D layers are pillared by bpy ligands into a 3D architecture (Fig. 2d). The solvent-accessible volume represents 26.0% of the cell volume.

Description of [Cd2(adtp)(μ2-H2O)(H2O)3]n (3)

Complex 3 also crystallizes in a triclinic P space group. The asymmetric unit is comprised of two crystallographically distinct Cd (II) ions, one adtp4− anion, one μ2-H2O ligand and three terminal water ligands. The carboxylate groups of the adip4− ligand adopt coordination mode C to link five metal centers by monodentate, monodentate μ2-bridging, syn–anti and anti–anti bidentate bridging modes (Scheme 1C). As visualized in Fig. 3a, Cd1 and Cd2 are seven-coordinated and six-coordinated, respectively. The adtp4− ligand chelates Cd1 using its imino nitrogen atom (N1) and carboxylate oxygen atom (O2, O4 and O5) of its three arms, leaving the residual three coordination sites occupied by two terminal water ligands and one symmetry generated carboxylate oxygen atom (O5B) from a second adtp4− ligand. Two symmetry related O5 and O5B doubly bridge Cd1 and its symmetry equivalent Cd1A to form a dinuclear unit (Fig. 3b). Three meridian-related coordination sites of Cd2 are occupied by carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O3A and O8C) from three adtp4− ligands, while one terminal aqua ligand (O10) and two symmetry related μ2-bridging water molecules (O9 and O9D) bind to the residual three meridional sites. Accordingly, the symmetry related Cd2 and Cd2A are doubly bridged into a dinuclear unit by two μ2-H2O ligands. Cd1 atom is linked to three symmetry related Cd2 atoms via syn–anti, anti–anti carboxylate bridges and phenyl carboxylate linker, respectively. Therefore, each Cd1 dinuclear unit connects six Cd2 dinuclears, and vice versa (Fig. 3b). The adtp4− ligands link Cd2 dinuclear units to form 1D parallelogram A tunnels along the a axis (Fig. 3c). In the same direction, the mixed Cd1 and Cd2 atoms are joined into similar B tunnels by adtp4− ligands (Fig. 3c). Each type of tunnel is arranged into 2D double layers by sharing one pair of parallel edges along the ab plane, and the two types of double layers are alternately stacked into a 3D architecture by sharing the other pair of parallel edges (Fig. 3e).

Description of {[Cd4(adtp)2(bpy)(H2O)4]·2.5H2O}n (4)

Complex 4 belongs to a monoclinic P2/c space group. The asymmetric unit is composed of four crystallographically unique Cd(II) ions, two types of adtp4− ligands (entitled as Ld and Le), four terminal aqua ligands and two and a half free water molecules. Ld and Le adopt two different coordination modes D and E, respectively (Scheme 1). As illustrated in Fig. 4a, Cd1, Cd3, and Cd4 are seven-coordinated, and Cd2 is six-coordinated. Ld and Le chelate Cd2 and Cd4 using their imino nitrogen atoms and carboxylate oxygen atoms of their three arms, respectively. The carboxylate groups from three arms of Ld bridge Cd2 to Cd1, Cd3 and Cd4 by a μ2-η2:η1 bridging coordination mode, while one short and one long arms of Le bridge Cd4 to Cd2 and Cd3 in the same way. The residual phenyl carboxylate groups of Ld and Le bind to the Cd1 atom in bidentate chelating and monodentate fashion, respectively. The bpy ligand spaces Cd1 and Cd4 in different asymmetric units. The terminal water ligands occupy the residual coordination sites of Cd1, Cd2, and Cd3. The arms of two types of adtp4− ligands bridge four Cd atoms in one asymmetric unit into a linear tetranuclear unit. Eight Cd (II) ions from two adjacent asymmetry units are bridged by carboxylate μ2-O atoms into an octanuclear unit which comprises a Cd6O6 twelve-member ring with another two metal ions (Cd1 and Cd1C) appended to it (Fig. 4b and d). The octanuclear units are doubly joined by μ2-O6 atoms into a 1D metal chain (Fig. 4c and e). The whole metal chains are bridged by carboxylate μ2-O atoms with bridged Cd⋯Cd distances of 3.8995(5)–4.4064(6) Å. The 1D chains are cross-linked to a 2D thick layer through the residual phenyl carboxylate groups of adtp4− ligands (Fig. 4f). Finally, the layers are pillared by bpy ligands into a 3D architecture (Fig. 4g).

In complexes 1, 2 and 3, μ2-O atoms bridged dinuclear metal units and syn–anti/syn–syn carboxylic groups bridged metal chains are commonly observed, whereas carboxylate μ2-O atoms bridged metal chains are absent, which are present in complex 4. Compared with coordination modes in other complexes, the Ld ligands in 4 furnish more μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate groups to bridge the metal centers and three arms of the imino nitrogen atom connect one metal center to other three ones using carboxylate μ2-O bridges. The Ld ligand accommodates the other one μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate group. Twelve arms of four adtp4− ligands from adjacent asymmetry units encase eight metal centers up and down. The three arms of imino nitrogen atom in 4 display unprecedented aggregation for metal atoms.

Effect of bpy ligand and anionic templates on the framework

The structure-directing effect of bpy ligand on the Zn(II) and Cd(II) coordination networks has been clearly demonstrated in pillared-layer frameworks of 2a, 2b and 4. In the presence of bpy ligand, the metal centers in 2a, 2b and 4 prefer to form metal chains which are further interlinked into 2D layers by adtp4−. The bpy ligands as struts space the adjacent layers and extend the dimensions of coordination networks to 3D regular pillared-layer frameworks.

It is interesting that the cationic framework in 2a and 2b displays strong dependence on counter anions and the concentration of bpy ligand. When the zinc salts of anions F−, Cl−, Br−, I−, AcO− and SO42− replace the Zn(NO3)2 or Zn(ClO4)2, only complex 1 is obtained. The anions NO3− and ClO4− with suitable size, charge, and shape favor the formation of 2a and 2b as the valid templates. The bpy ligand as the composition of the framework controls the results of syntheses via different molar ratios to H4adtp. The stoichiometric mixture of bpy and H4adtp yields the mixture of 1 and 2a (2b), and excessive bpy is crucial to obtain pure 2a (2b). It can be presumed that the reactions of 2a and 2b are thermodynamically favorable. In light of this, the auxiliary ligand-directed effect of bpy ligand on coordination networks has an intimate association with the concentration.

Thermal analyses

To investigate the thermal stability of the complexes, the thermal gravimetric analyses are examined in the range 30–1000 °C. The TGA curves are shown in Fig. S2, ESI.† The curve of complex 1 exhibits a plateau from 100 to 400 °C after a rapid decrease in the weight. After the loss of two free water molecules at 449 °C (calculated: 7.26%, found: 7.33%), the complex begins to decompose rapidly. The curves of complexes 2a and 2b undergo a slow decrease of the weight corresponding to their three lattice water molecules in the range of 30–210 °C (calculated: 6.18%, found: 6.50%, for 2a; calculated: 5.91%, found: 5.73% for 2b). The rapid decomposition occurs at 270 °C and 340 °C for 2a and 2b, respectively. The curve of complex 3 exhibits a plateau from 30 to 120 °C owing to no lattice water molecules and begins to decompose after a smooth departure of four coordination water molecules from 120 to 320 °C (calculated: 12.20%, found: 12.26%). For complex 4, the loss of all the water molecules occurs from 30 to 290 °C (calculated: 8.93%, found: 8.75%) and then the framework undergoes decomposition.

Luminescent properties

The solid-state photoluminescent spectra of H4adtp and complexes 1, 3 and 4 at room temperature are depicted in Fig. 5. H4adtp, 1, 3 and 4 exhibit intense blue radiation emission with maxima at ca. 456, 456, 416, and 436 nm upon excitation at 330, 330, 365 and 365 nm, respectively. Compared with the emission band of H4adtp, blue-shifted emission bands with different extent for 3 and 4 are observed. The blue-shift of 3 and 4 may be assigned to the enlarged HOMO–LUMO gap due to the coordination of H4adtp.17 Compared with the bpy emission spectrum, another possibility of the emission for complex 4 that should be considered is that the blue-shift may be ascribed to the intense emission of bpy covering the emission of H4adtp.18

Conclusion

In summary, various Zn(II) and Cd(II) coordination architectures based semirigid polycarboxylate H4adtp ligands have been constructed in the absence or presence of bpy ligands. The function of bpy ligand as a structure-directing agent has been demonstrated to be a good strategy for the construction of pillared-layer structures. Meanwhile, the introduction of bpy ligand can facilitate the formation of novel metal aggregations such as μ3-bridged tetranuclear units and carboxylate μ2-O bridged metal chains. In the assembly of pillared-layer architecture, the concentration of bpy ligand and the counter anions as template with suitable charge, shape and size are crucial for obtaining the pure desirable product. This suggests to us to pay more attention to unpredictable roles of various synthetic conditions in the modulation of N-donor ligand on the structure. The luminescent behaviors of complex 2a display luminescent tunable properties upon varying the excitation light and temperature, which provide us with a new route for the preparation of the tunable luminescent materials.
