A series of s-block (Ca, Sr and Ba) metal–organic frameworks: synthesis and structure–property correlation†

K. S. Asha a, Madhuri Makkitaya a, Anshu Sirohi b, Lalit Yadav b, Goutam Sheet b and Sukhendu Mandal *a
aSchool of Chemistry, Indian Institute of Science Education and Research Thiruvananthapuram, Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala 695016, India. E-mail: sukhendu@iisertvm.ac.in
bDepartment of Physical Science, Indian Institute of Science Education and Research Mohali, Mohali, Punjab 140306, India

Received 12th October 2015 , Accepted 1st January 2016

First published on 6th January 2016

A series of metal–organic frameworks based on alkaline earth metal ions (Ca, Sr and Ba) and 1,3,5-benzenetribenzoic acid (BTB) have been synthesized and characterized. These are [H2N(CH3)2][Ca7(BTB)5(H2O)8(DMF)4]·4H2O (1), [H2N(CH3)2]2[Sr5(H2O)6(BTB)4] (2) and [H2N(CH3)2][Ba(H2O)(BTB)] (3). All the structures are three-dimensional in nature with different secondary building units. Compound 1 contains one-dimensional Ca–O–Ca zigzag chains while compound 2 contains Sr5O28 pentameric clusters and compound 3 contains one-dimensional Ba–O–Ba chains. Both compounds 1 and 3 form (3,6)-net connectivity while compound 2 forms (3,12)-net connectivity. Optical band gap energy measurements show that compound 1 (2.65 eV) has low band gap energy compared to 2 (3.22 eV) and 3 (3.32 eV). This variation in band gap energy may be due to the difference in structural arrangement. Compound 3 crystallizes in a non-centrosymmetric space group (Pna21), which belongs to the polar point group C2v. This compound displays a strong SHG response and good ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs)1,2 are crystalline materials composed of metal ions, connected by functionalized organic linkers, forming a hierarchy of structures. A wide variety of metal centres and functionalized organic linkers are used to form MOFs under different synthetic conditions, giving rise to unprecedented structural diversity tailored for specific applications.3–5 The majority of MOFs consist of transition metals and, more recently, rare earth metal cations; however, s-blocks metals have received relatively less attention,6 probably because, as it happened with lanthanide elements, they are usually regarded as unsuitable metal centres to form such polymers. Due to the large electronegativity difference, the bonding interaction between s-block metal centres and carboxylate oxygen atoms is ionic in nature. This provides little room for prediction and control over the coordination geometry. The coordination nature of the metal centre within the network is strongly correlated with the mutual orientation of the functional groups. The unpredicted coordination behaviour and the dominance of steric considerations are challenges in carrying out systematic studies of MOFs based on s-block metal ions.

Due to the large variation in coordination numbers of metal ions and diversity of bridging modes of ligands, prediction of the framework structure constructed from a particular building component is a challenging task.7–11 However, by selecting metal ions with specific coordination numbers and geometry and rigid ligands, we could synthesize predictable network structures. 1,3,5-Benzenetribenzoic acid (H3BTB) is one of the ligands most widely used as a rigid nonplanar C3-symmetric 3-connected organic node.12–14 The reaction of BTB with different types of metal ions produces a large number of framework structures with hierarchical net topology. Although research on MOFs based on the BTB ligand have recently gained much interest, structures based on alkaline earth metal ions are rare.15 BTB ligands with different metal ions produce framework structures with hierarchical architectures, which allow us to study the role of metal ions and architectures in the electronic and optical properties.16–20 Investigations of key factors to control the optical band gap energy of these framework structures will shed light on the semiconducting behaviour of these materials.

We report here three novel three-dimensional alkaline-earth polymeric frameworks belonging to three different structural types, in which the metal centres are coordinated to BTB ligands. The crystal structures and topological analyses were described, and all the compounds were well characterized. We have measured the optical band gap energy of these structures, and the results underscore the effect of the structural architecture on the band gap energy. We have also measured the physical properties associated with the acentric structure of compound 3 such as second harmonic generation (SHG) and piezoelectric as well as ferroelectric properties. Reports on MOFs with ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties are very rare and, to the best our knowledge, this is the first report of thermally stable alkaline earth metal-based MOFs with ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties.

Results and discussion

Structural description

Compound [H2N(CH3)2][Ca7(BTB)5(H2O)8(DMF)4]·4H2O, 1. Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in a monoclinic system with the space group C2/c (Table S1, see the ESI†). Its asymmetric unit contains three and a half Ca ions, two and a half molecules of BTB anions, four coordinated water and two coordinated dimethylformamide molecules and a half molecule of a dimethylammonium cation (Fig. S1, see the ESI†). The Ca ions in 1 have different coordination numbers. The Ca(1) ion connected to seven oxygen atoms to form Ca(1)O7 polyhedra, the Ca(2) ion linked with nine oxygen atoms to form Ca(2)O9 polyhedra, and Ca(3) and Ca(4) connected to six oxygen atoms to adopt a distorted octahedral geometry. The Ca–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.309(4)–3.125(4) Å (Av. Ca–O = 2.4330 Å) (Table S2, see the ESI†). The bond distances and angles associated with the BTB anions are in the range expected for this type of bonding. There are three types of BTB anions: acid-1 is connected to all the Ca ions and is also bridged to the Ca(2) ion through carboxylate oxygen atoms and the bonding connectivity allows one of the oxygen atoms to be free (Fig. S1, see the ESI†); acid-2 is connected to all the Ca ions and is also bridged to Ca(1) and Ca(2) ions (Fig. S2, see the ESI†); acid-3 is connected to Ca(1) and Ca(3) ions and the bonding connectivity allows two of the oxygen atoms to be free (Fig. S3, see the ESI†). The connectivity in 1 between the Ca2+ ions and BTB anions gives rise to a three-dimensional structure. The Ca ions are linked through oxygen atoms (edge and corner-wise) to form one-dimensional zigzag chains with infinite Ca–O–Ca bonds (Fig. 1a). Each one-dimensional chain is connected to six other chains through BTB anions to form a three-dimensional structure with (3,6)-connectivity (Fig. 1b). Topological analysis using TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a 4 nodal net, 12,18,20,27-c net, which can be presented as the Schläfli symbol {336·430}{355·477·521}{367·4112·511}{391·4218·542} (td10 = 15891) (see the ESI†).23

Compound [H2N(CH3)2]2[Sr5(H2O)6(BTB)4], 2. Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compound 2 crystallizes in a monoclinic system with the space group C2/c (Table S1, see the ESI†). Its asymmetric unit contains two and a half Sr ions, two molecules of BTB, three coordinated water molecules and one molecule of a dimethylammonium cation. The Sr ions in 2 adopt different coordination numbers. Sr(1) and Sr(3) coordinated to eight oxygen atoms to form SrO8 polyhedral units while Sr(2) connected to six oxygen atoms to form a SrO6 polyhedral unit. These polyhedral units are linked through edges to form the Sr5O28 pentameric cluster (Fig. 2a). The Sr–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.799(5)–2.422(5) Å (Av. 2.5598 Å) (Table S3, see the ESI†). There are two BTB anions present in the structure, and both acids have the same mode of connectivity to the Sr metal ions (Fig. S4, see the ESI†). The BTB anion is connected to all the Sr ions and is also bridged to the Sr(1) ion through carboxylate oxygen atoms (Fig. S4, see the ESI†). The connectivity in 2 between the Sr2+ ions and BTB anions gives rise to a three-dimensional structure. Each pentamer cluster can be viewed as a 12-connected node capped by twelve 3-connected tritopic BTB ligands to give an overall (3,12) network of {348·482·56}2{363·4132·515}2{378·4198·549} topology (Fig. 2b). Examples of the (3,12)-connected 3D framework via using covalent linkage are rare and this compound is one of the few examples.21 Each pentameric unit is linked through carboxylate anions to eight other pentameric clusters to form a layered architecture (Fig. 2c). This layered architecture, in turn, connected in other directions to form a three-dimensional architecture (Fig. 2d). Topological analysis using TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with 3-nodal net, 17,21,26-c net, which can be presented as the Schläfli symbol {348·482·56}2{363·4132·515}2{378·4198·549} (td10 = 16620) (see the ESI†).23

Compound [H2N(CH3)2][Ba(H2O)(BTB)], 3. Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compound 3 crystallizes in an orthorhombic system with the polar space group Pna21 (Table S1†). Its asymmetric unit contains one Ba ion, one molecule of BTB, one coordinated water molecule and one molecule of a dimethylammonium cation. The Ba(1) ion connected to nine oxygen atoms to form the BaO9 polyhedral unit. The Ba–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.753(7)–2.959(7) Å (Av. 2.8174 Å) (Table S4, see the ESI†). Only one BTB anion is present in the structure, and the acid connected and bridged to the Ba ion; the bonding connectivity allows one of the oxygen atoms to be free (Fig. S5, see the ESI†). The connectivity in 3 between the Ba2+ ions and BTB anions gives rise to an anionic three-dimensional structure. The anionic charge of the framework is balanced by the dimethylformamide cation. The Ba ions are linked through oxygen atoms edge-wise to form a one-dimensional chain with infinite Ba–O–Ba bonds along the a-axis (Fig. 3a). One-dimensional chains are connected to six other chains through BTB anions forming a three-dimensional architecture with (3,6)-net connectivity (Fig. 3b). This connectivity creates one-dimensional channels along the a-axis. The BTB ligands lie on the bc plane and stack in an ABAB fashion. Each BTB plane is offset and interacts with each other through weak π–π interactions (the adjacent distance is around 3.54 Å). Due to the C3 symmetry and mutual stacking of the BTB ligands, the one-dimensional channels take the shape of distorted honeycombs. The dimensions of the channels are approximately 10 × 15 Å2, and the channels are occupied by distorted dimethylammonium cations. The coordinated water molecules are directed toward the pore interior. The guest-accessible void volume, as calculated by PLATON software, is 31%. The closely related structure has been reported in the literature.22 Topological analysis using TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a uninodal 20-c net, which can be presented as the Schläfli symbol {375·4106·59} (td10 = 7989) (see the ESI†).23

Powder X-ray diffraction and thermal studies

Powder X-ray diffraction of the bulk materials was carried out for all these three compounds to confirm their phase purity. The experimental PXRD patterns almost matched with the simulated XRD patterns generated based on the structures determined using single crystal XRD, indicating their crystalline phase purity (Fig. S6–S8, see the ESI†). The structural rigidity and thermal stability of all three compounds were well reflected from their variable temperature powder X-ray diffraction data (VTPXRD) (Fig. S9–S11, see the ESI†). The VTPXRD patterns reveal that there was no structural transformation and the frameworks were stable up to 400 °C for all the compounds. The TGA trace for the as-synthesized compounds showed a continuous weight loss up to 400 °C which may be due to lattice water, coordinated water, coordinated DMF molecules and dimethylammonium cation molecules (Fig. S12–S14, see the ESI†). After 400 °C the weight loss is due to the decomposition of the framework. The IR spectra also proved that the frameworks were stable up to 400 °C (Fig. S15–S17, see the ESI†). The high bond energy between the alkaline earth metal and the carboxylate moiety is primarily responsible for this high thermal stability. The single crystal structure showed that compound 3 is microporous in nature, which prompted us to measure the porosity of the compound. CO2 adsorption at 195 K showed a type I isotherm and confirmed the robust porosity via the reversible isotherm (Fig. S18†). N2 adsorption studies were also carried out for compound 3 and the BET surface area was calculated to be 245.46 m2 g−1 (Fig. S19†). The compound retains its crystallinity after the adsorption–desorption experiment (Fig. S20†).

Band gap energy

The optical absorption associated with Ebg could be assessed at 2.65 eV, 3.22 eV and 3.32 eV for 1–3, respectively (Fig. S21† and Table 1). The single crystal structures of 1–3 reveal that the metal nodes are well isolated and connected by organic molecular linkers. This well-isolated structural arrangement generates molecular-like electronic structures. MOF-based structures exhibit a flat valence band (VB) and a conduction band (CB) with very small dispersion.18c We have measured the indirect band gap energy for this case. Though all the structures are three-dimensional in nature, the coordination modes of the metal ions are largely different from compounds 1 to 3, respectively. In the case of 1, the Ca ions adopt coordination numbers ranging from six to nine while in 2 and 3, the Sr ions and the Ba ions adopt coordination numbers of six and eight, and only nine, respectively. The Ca ions are connected through corners and edges to form the one-dimensional Ca–O–Ca zigzag chains in 1, while the Sr ions are connected through edges to form the Sr5O28 pentamer cluster in 2 and the Ba ions are connected through edges to form the Ba–O–Ba chains in 3. These differences in metal coordination and structural arrangement may be responsible for the variation in band gap energy of compounds 1–3.

Electric measurements of compound 3

Compound 3 crystallizes in the space group Pna21 which belong to the point group C2v. To explore the local ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties of compound 3, we have made use of piezo-response force microscopy (PFM) (Fig. 4). The PFM phase responses reveal a clear hysteresis in the phase vs. dc bias (Vdc) diagram (Fig. 4b). The phase switches sharply at Vdc = 20 V by ~180° with coercive voltages of ±4 V.24 Notably, the phase of the PFM response signal is directly related to the direction of the electric polarization of the microscopic region of the surface monitored under the tip. Thus, the switching of the phase of the PFM response signal hysteretically by 180° in response to a sweeping DC voltage is attributed to the switching of the direction of polarization of the dipoles of compound 3 along the direction of the electric field. This implies that the hysteretic 180° phase switching observed in the PFM response is evidence of the local ferroelectricity of compound 3. The amplitude of the response signal in PFM is directly related to the local strain in compound 3 experienced by the cantilever. The amplitude vs. Vdc curves are also hysteretic and the shape of the loops strongly resembles the “butterfly loop” normally observed in piezoelectrics (Fig. 4c).25 The observations of hysteretic phase switching and butterfly loops indicate that compound 3 possesses ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties. Notably, hysteretic effects in the PFM phase and amplitude may also arise from electrostatic and electrochemical effects. To minimize the role of the electrostatic effects, all the measurements were carried out by using the protocol developed by Sekhon et al. popularly known as SS-PFM.25 In this method, Vdc is applied in a sequence of pulses instead of sweeping Vdc continuously while the amplitude and phase measurements are conducted in the “off-states” of the pulses.
Fig. 4 (a) Tuning curves for point 1 (red curve), point 2 (blue curve) and point 3 (black curve); (b) phase–voltage hysteresis loops for point 1 (red curve), point 2 (blue curve) and point 3 (black curve); (c) corresponding “butterfly” loops for point 1 (red curve), point 2 (blue curve) and point 3 (black curve).

A significant difference in the “off-state” results in comparison to the “on-state” measurements was observed. This observation indicates that the electrostatic effects have been minimized in the “off-state” measurements. Hence, we rule out the electrostatic effect on the hysteretic effects exhibited by compound 3.

Normally, ferroelectric compounds should be piezoelectrically active while piezoelectric compounds are less restricted and only need to satisfy non-centrosymmetric requirements. Here, compound 3 crystallized in a non-centrosymmetric space group, which is responsible for its piezoelectric behaviour. The motion of the extra-framework cation (dimethylammonium cation) in the channels of the porous structure at room temperature might lead to the switching of phase under electric field. Thus, the motion of the dimethylammonium cation may be responsible for the ferroelectric nature of this compound.

Second harmonic generation measurement

We also measured the second harmonic generation (SHG) of compound 3 and it was found that it exhibits an SHG efficiency ~1.4 times that of potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KDP). To the best of our knowledge, compound 3 is the first report of a thermally stable alkaline earth metal-based NLO-active MOF with ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties.

Conclusions

We have synthesized a series of three-dimensional alkaline earth metal-based metal–organic frameworks using solvothermal methods. Interestingly in compound 1, Ca ions are connected through corners and edges to form the one-dimensional zigzag chain with Ca–O–Ca bonds. Sr ions are connected through edges to form the Sr5O28 pentameric cluster in compound 2. In compound 3, BaO9 polyhedral units are linked through edges to form the one-dimensional chain with Ba–O–Ba bonds. The formation of the (3,12)-net in compound 2 is noteworthy. Compound 3 is porous as demonstrated by gas adsorption measurements. We have measured the optical band gap energy of all the compounds and it was exhibited that compound 1 (2.65 eV) has a lower band gap energy compared to 2 (3.22 eV) and 3 (3.32 eV). The differences in metal coordination and structural arrangement may be responsible for the variation in band gap energy. Due to the polar characteristics of compound 3, it exhibits interesting ferroelectric, piezoelectric and NLO properties. To the best of our knowledge, compound 3 is the first report of a thermally stable porous alkaline earth metal-based NLO-active MOF with ferroelectric and piezoelectric properties. Thus the compound 3 may exploit facile entry for the incorporation of multi- functionalities into porous MOFs and for the preconceived needs in materials design.

Experimental section

Materials

All chemicals were of reagent-grade quality, obtained from Sigma-Aldrich and used as-received without further purification. Elemental analysis (C, H and N) was carried out on a vario MICRO cube elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectrum was recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Prestige-21 SHIMADZU FT-IR spectrometer. TGA was performed on an SDT Q600 (Shimadzu) analyzer under flowing nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C per minute. Powder X-ray diffraction data were collected using an X'Pert PRO (PANalytical) powder diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα radiation (1.540598 Å).

Synthesis

Compound 1. 0.047 g (0.2 mmol) of calcium nitrate [Ca(NO3)2·4H2O] was weighed in a clean glass vial and dissolved in 5 mL of H2O, and 0.087 g (0.2 mmol) of BTB was dissolved in 5 mL of DMF and added to the inorganic precursor. Then, the mixture was sonicated for 5 minutes. The pH of the reaction was maintained at 8–9 by adding diluted NaOH solution. Afterwards, it was kept in an oven at 100 °C for 120 h. Colorless crystals were collected (in 70% yield with respect to the ligand) and washed many times to remove the unreacted crystallized ligand molecules.
Compound 2. 0.042 g (0.2 mmol) of strontium nitrate [Sr(NO3)2] was weighed in a clean glass vial and dissolved in 5 mL of H2O, and 0.087 g (0.2 mmol) of BTB was dissolved in 5 mL of DMF and added to the reaction mixture. The mixture was sonicated and then kept in an oven at 100 °C for 120 h. Colorless crystals were collected (in a yield of 75% with respect to the ligand) and washed.
Compound 3. 0.052 g (0.2 mmol) of barium nitrate [Ba(NO3)2] and 0.087 g (0.2 mmol) of BTB were weighed in a clean glass vial and dissolved in 10 mL of DMF. The mixture was sonicated and then kept in an oven at 100 °C for 120 h. Colorless crystals were collected (in a yield of 90% with respect to the ligand) and washed.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction

For single crystal X-ray diffraction measurement, suitable crystals of all the compounds were carefully selected under a polarizing microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber. The data were collected on a Bruker AXS Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at 298 K. The X-ray generator was operated at 50 kV and 35 mA using MoKα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. Data were collected with an ω scan width of 0.3°. A total of 606 frames were collected in three different settings of φ (0°, 90°, 180°) keeping the sample-to-detector distance fixed at 6.03 cm and the detector position (2θ) fixed at −25°.

The data were reduced using SAINTPLUS26 and an empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.27 The crystal structure was determined by direct methods using SHELXS97 and refined using SHELXL97 present in the SHELXTL V6.14 (ref. 28) package. All hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions during the final step of the refinement process. For the final refinement, the hydrogen atoms were placed in geometrically ideal positions and refined using the riding model. The last cycles of the refinement included the atomic positions, anisotropic thermal parameters for all the non-hydrogen atoms, and isotropic thermal parameters for all the hydrogen atoms. Full-matrix least-squares structure refinement against F2 was carried out using the WINGX29 package of programs. The crystallographic parameters for all compounds 1–3 are provided in Table S1.† CCDC 1055172–1055174 contain the crystallographic data for 1–3, respectively.

Characterization

Initial characterization was carried out through powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and IR spectroscopic studies. IR spectroscopic studies were carried out in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 using the KBr pellet method. The IR spectrum exhibited typical peaks corresponding to the lattice and coordinated water molecules, dimethylformamide, dimethylammonium cation and carboxylate moieties in their respective bands (Fig. S15–17, see the ESI†).

Gas adsorption measurements

CO2 adsorption measurements were performed using a BELSORP-max (Bel, Japan). The gas used was of 99.999% purity. Prior to the measurement, the guest free sample was preheated at 120 °C under vacuum for 5 h using a BelPrepvacII and purged with N2 on cooling. The outgassing procedure was repeated during measurement and the CO2 adsorption isotherm was collected at 195 K.

Optical band gap measurements

The optical absorption spectra of compounds 1–3 were collected on a Shimadzu UV/Vis/NIR spectrophotometer. The room-temperature diffuse reflectance spectra were measured using BaSO4 as a reference material and converted to a Kubelka–Munk function, from which the band gap of the compound was estimated.30,31 This two-flux model, which considers only diffuse light, is used to determine the absorption coefficient from a surface that both scatters and absorbs the incident radiation. Second harmonic generation measurements

For SHG experiments, powder samples were illuminated by a Q-switched Nd:YAG laser beam with a fundamental wavelength of 1064 nm (Spectra Physics, PROLAB 170, pulse width 10 ns, repetition rate 10 Hz). The beam was passed through a couple of high-energy laser mirrors and a long-pass colored glass filter before being focused onto a glass capillary using a converging lens of 200 mm focal length. The incoherently scattered SH photons were collected in the transverse direction using a combination of a monochromator and a photomultiplier tube. The second-harmonic signal was then sampled, averaged over 512 shots and recorded using a digital storage oscilloscope. KDP powder was used as the reference material.

Piezo-response force microscopy (PFM)

We performed the piezo-response force microscopy measurements using an Asylum Research AFM (MFP-3D) with an additional high-voltage amplifier. We used a conducting AFM cantilever (free air resonance frequency = 70 kHz, spring constant = 2 N m−1), which had a Pt–Ir coated tip. The cantilever was brought into contact with the sample (powder sample in pellet form). An AC excitation of 5 V riding on a dc bias voltage (Vdc) was applied between the tip and the amplifier ground. The reaction of the sample to the applied modulating field was measured using the reflection of a laser beam from the cantilever. This reflection was recorded by using a position-sensitive photodiode.32
