Dependence of the SBU length on the size of metal ions in alkaline earth MOFs derived from a flexible C3-symmetric tricarboxylic acid†

aDepartment of Chemistry, Indian Institute of Technology Bombay, Mumbai, 400 076 India. E-mail: rmv@chem.iitb.ac.in; Tel: +91 22 2576 7163
bCotton College, Guwahati, 781001 India

First published on 31st October 2016

Four new alkaline-earth metal-based metal–organic frameworks, [Mg4(TCMTB)2(OAc)2(DMA)2(H2O)3]n (Mg-TCMTB), {[Ca4(TCMTB)2(OH)(DMF)2(H2O)5]·Cl}n (Ca-TCMTB), [Sr4(TCMTB)2(OH)(OAc)(DMA)6(H2O)]n (Sr-TCMTB) and [Ba9(TCMTB)4(NO3)6(DMA)14]n (Ba-TCMTB) (H3TCMTB = 2,4,6-tris[(4′-carboxyphenoxy)methyl]-1,3,5-trimethylbenzene), have been synthesized and structurally characterized. Structural analysis of the MOFs reveals the presence of diverse structures and topologies in these systems due to the conformational flexibility and multiple coordination sites in H3TCMTB. Coordination polymers Mg-TCMTB, Ca-TCMTB and Ba-TCMTB MOFs are three-dimensional frameworks exhibiting 2-fold interpenetration and one-dimensional hexagonal channels, while Sr-TCMTB is a 2-fold interpenetrated layered MOF. The 2D layers in Sr-TCMTB are interconnected through H–O⋯H hydrogen bonds. Increasing ionic radii and coordination number on moving down the group results in the formation of bi-, tetra- and nona-nuclear M–O–M connected inorganic building units. Owing to its smaller size and lower coordination number, framework Mg-TCMTB gives rise to a moderate surface area of 33.0 m2 g−1 (SABET) and 93.8 m2 g−1 (SALang) which is the highest observed among all the four MOFs. Emission studies of the new MOFs reveal the presence of strong photoluminescence at 380 nm.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are crystalline porous solids, comprising inorganic secondary building units (SBUs) linked by polydentate organic linkers. Transition metal-based MOFs have witnessed tremendous growth in the past two decades due to their controllable synthesis and potential applications in gas storage,1–5 magnetism,6 chemical sensing,7–10 proton conductivity,11–13 catalysis,14,15 and detoxification.16–18 In recent years, there has been widespread interest in alkaline-earth metal ion-based MOFs due to their many advantages over others such as low density, non-toxicity, cost-effectiveness and high affinity for small gas molecules such as hydrogen.19–23 Unlike transition metal MOFs, investigations on alkaline-earth metal MOFs are limited due to several challenges in their controllable synthesis.19–25 The main reason is that alkaline-earth metals show variable coordination numbers (the most preferred coordination numbers are 6 for magnesium, 6 to 8 for calcium, and 6 to 12 for strontium and barium) which renders uncontrolled coordination geometries around the metal centre.26–29

Besides, alkaline-earth metal ions have high affinity for oxygen and therefore polycarboxylate ligands are a better choice for design and synthesis of new alkaline-earth metal MOFs. Also, carboxylates exhibit a range of bridging modes.30 Typically, the synthesis of alkaline-earth metal MOFs is achieved by reacting polycarboxylate ligands with suitable metal precursors at high temperature. Ever since the initial report on Mg-MOF [Mg3(2,6-ndc)3(DEF)4] (H2ndc = 2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid) by Long et al.,31 several ditopic carboxylate ligands have been realized as organic building blocks to construct a variety of alkaline-earth metal MOFs.28–40 A few tricarboxylate ligand-based group 2 MOFs have also been reported. For example, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxyl acid (H3BTC),22,24,41–43 and 1,3,5-tris(4-carboxyphenyl)benzene (H3BTB) have been particularly investigated as spacers.44–47 It is obvious from the literature examples that the realization of new alkaline-earth metal-based MOFs has mainly focused on rigid ligands. Barring a few examples, reports on flexible tricarboxylate ligands are scarce.48 These flexible ligands adopt different conformations and symmetries during the self-assembly process, leading to difficulties in isolation of the MOFs as single crystals.49 Nevertheless, flexible ligands allow access to the framework with higher dimensionalities by an increased tendency of the formation of M–O–M bridges.39,50

In the course of our investigations on flexible tricarboxylate ligand-based MOFs, earlier we reported on three-dimensional helical (with Zn2+ metal) and two-dimensional sheet-like (with Cd2+ and In3+ metals) MOFs by employing H3TCMTB (2,4,6-tris[(4′-carboxyphenoxy)methyl]-1,3,5-trimethylbenzene) as the organic linker.51,52 The H3TCMTB ligand is a conformationally flexible linker, exhibiting three arms and six carboxylate oxygen sites from three well separated carboxylate groups. The choice of H3TCMTB is also intentional to investigate the effect of the size of alkali metal ions on the inorganic connectivity (SBUs) of the MOFs. It has been reported that upon progressing down the alkaline-earth metal group, the inorganic connectivity increases.53 Keeping these facts in mind, we have now carried out the reaction of H3TCMTB with Mg2+, Ca2+, Sr2+ and Ba2+ metal ions, resulting in the isolation of four new alkaline-earth metal MOFs. The details of these studies are presented herein.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All the reagents and solvents were procured from commercial sources and purified by standard procedures. H3TCMTB was synthesized by following an earlier reported protocol.51,52 Elemental analyses were performed on a Thermo Finnigan (FLASH EA 1112) microanalyzer. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Perkin Elmer Spectrum One infrared spectrometer as KBr diluted disks. Thermogravimetric analysis was performed on a Perkin Elmer Pyris Diamond TGA instrument. PXRD data were obtained on a PANalytical X'pert Pro diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å). Single crystal X-ray diffraction experiments were carried out on a Rigaku Saturn 724+ CCD diffractometer equipped with a microfocus Mo-Kα radiation source (λ = 0.71075 Å). Gas sorption experiments were performed isothermally at 77 K on a Quantachrome Autosorb-1 adsorption analyzer. Absorption and photoluminescence spectra were recorded on Varian Cary Bio 100 UV-vis and Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometers, respectively.

X-ray crystallography. Single crystals of Mg-TCMTB, Ca-TCMTB, Sr-TCMTB and Ba-TCMTB of suitable size were chosen for the X-ray diffraction studies. The measurements were conducted at 150 K under continuous flow of cooled nitrogen gas. Data integration and indexing were performed using CrystalClear software and a multi-scan method was employed to correct for absorption. The structures were solved by direct methods using SIR-92,54 and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 using SHELXL-97.55 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The C–H protons were placed in their geometrically idealized positions and refined isotopically as riding atoms. The H atoms attached to oxygen were located in the difference Fourier maps and refined as rigid atoms in their as-found positions. The Fourier maps also indicated residual electron densities corresponding to severely disordered solvent molecules in the voids, which could not be modelled to any definite guest molecules. To account for such electron densities, the final refinement was performed with modification of the structure factors using the SQUEEZE subroutine of PLATON.56aSQUEEZE improved the data quality of Mg-TCMTB, Sr-TCMTB and Ba-TCMTB to a sizeable extent. However, a relatively high R factor was obtained for Ca-TCMTB due to very high atomic displacement parameters and partial occupancy of the disordered guest molecules. Attempts made to resolve disorder by dividing the atoms with high displacement parameters into two parts were unsuccessful. Further, several attempts to recollect the intensity data of freshly prepared Ca-TCMTB crystals also did not yield desired results. All the structures were analysed by using the ADDSYM subroutine of PLATON to ensure that additional symmetries were not present. The solvent accessible volumes were calculated using the CALC SOLV utility of PLATON. Results and discussion

Synthesis

Solvothermal synthesis has been employed for the preparation of all the four frameworks described in the present study since this synthetic protocol not only increases the solubility of the as-synthesized complexes, but also favours often an ordered molecular self-assembly. Thus, the polymers Mg-TCMTB, Ca-TCMTB, Sr-TCMTB and Ba-TCMTB have been obtained from the reaction of H3TCMTB with Mg(NO3)2·6H2O, CaCl2, Sr(NO3)2 and Ba(NO3)2, respectively, either in aqueous N,N′-dimethylacetamide or aqueous N,N′-dimethylformamide (Scheme 1). The new compounds were washed thoroughly with the solvent used for the reaction and dried under vacuum. All the compounds have been characterized by elemental analysis, FT-IR spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analysis and powder XRD studies.

Crystal structures

In order to assign definitive structures for these group 2 frameworks formed by TCMTB, single crystal X-ray diffraction studies have been carried out for each compound of the series. A brief structural description of the individual compounds is initially provided below, which is followed by a discussion of the structural trends in the series.

Mg-TCMTB

Mg-TCMTB crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/c space group which possesses a two-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional network with one-dimensional hexagonal channels in each net. The asymmetric part of the unit cell consists of four crystallographically unique Mg2+ ions coordinated to two TCMTB3− ligands, two DMA molecules, three terminal aqua ligands and two bridged acetate ligands. Each TCMTB3− ligand binds with six different Mg2+ ions through two different coordination modes, which can be described as (η1-η1)-(η1-η1)-(η1-η2)-μ6 (see Fig. 1 and S1†). The Mg2+ ions exhibit a slightly distorted octahedral geometry around Mg(1), Mg(2) and Mg(3) ions and a distorted square pyramidal geometry around the Mg(4) ion. The Mg(1) ion is coordinated to three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers (Mg–O = 2.032(4)–2.094(3) Å), one oxygen atom from DMA (Mg–O = 2.116(4) Å), one oxygen atom from a terminal aqua ligand (Mg–O = 2.105(3) Å) and one oxygen atom from the bridged acetate ion (Mg–O = 2.058(3) Å). The Mg(2) ion is coordinated to four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− linkers (Mg–O = 1.995(3)–2.197(3) Å), one oxygen atom from a terminal aqua ligand (Mg–O = 2.112(4) Å) and one oxygen atom from the acetate ion (Mg–O = 2.004(3) Å). The Mg(3) ion is surrounded by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers (Mg–O = 2.030(4)–2.069(3) Å), one oxygen atom from DMA (Mg–O = 2.085(4) Å), one oxygen atom from a terminal aqua ligand (Mg–O = 2.073(3) Å) and one oxygen atom from the bridged acetate ion (Mg–O = 2.077(4) Å). The Mg(4) ion is coordinated to four carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers (Mg–O = 1.934(5)–2.217(4) Å) and one oxygen atom from the bridging acetate ion (Mg–O = 1.955(4) Å). The longer distances correspond to Mg(2)–O(8) and Mg(4)–O(18) bonds, wherein O(8) and O(18) are the corner-sharing atoms furnished by TCMTB3− linkers through the η1-η2 mode of binding.
Fig. 1 Crystal structure of Mg-TCMTB. (a) Polyhedral representation of the coordination environment around Mg2+ ions, (b) connectivity between Mg2+ ions inside the channels and (c) a 3D network with hexagonal channels. Only relevant atoms have been shown.

The four Mg2+ ions are assembled into two distinguishable dinuclear clusters, Mg(1)⋯Mg(2) and Mg(3)⋯Mg(4), which are bridged by acetate ions along the a-axis as depicted in Fig. 1b. The acetate ions are produced from the decomposition of the DMA solvent used in the reaction. The distances between Mg2+ ions in Mg(1)⋯Mg(2) and Mg(3)⋯Mg(4) clusters are 3.531(2) Å and 3.543(2) Å, respectively. The –Mg–O–Mg–OAc–Mg–O–Mg– infinite chains are interconnected by TCMTB3− linkers in the bc-plane which leads to the formation of the three-dimensional network with one-dimensional hexagonal pores along the a-direction (Fig. 1c). The terminally coordinated aqua and DMA molecules are directed towards the pore interior thus blocking the pores to some extent. Owing to the large window between TCMTB3− linkers in the same network (ca. 8.46 Å), it is not surprising that the interpenetration of the similar nets appears to stabilize the lattice packing. The central phenyl rings of the TCMTB3− linkers show face-to-face π–π interactions with those present in the adjacent interpenetrated nets (shortest inter-net C⋯C distances: 4.80 and 4.96 Å). The total solvent accessible volume calculated by PLATON is 3600 Å3 which corresponds to 34% of the unit cell volume. Topological analysis by TOPOS indicates that Mg-TCMTB has a uninodal network, where the Mg and O centres share the same point symbol {342·442·57}.

Ca-TCMTB

Ca-TCMTB crystallizes in the triclinic space group P with four Ca2+ ions, two TCMTB3− linkers, two DMF molecules, five terminal aqua ligands and one hydroxyl ligand in the asymmetric unit. The whole framework is positively charged which is balanced by the chloride ions present in the channels. The TCMTB3− ligand adopts two different types of connectivity, heptadentate and octadentate (Fig. S2†). The Ca(1) ion exhibits a seven coordinated environment through six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− linkers (Ca–O = 2.301(5)–2.679(6) Å) and one oxygen atom from an aqua ligand (Ca–O = 2.370(7)) Å. The Ca(2) ion is hexacoordinated with three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers (Ca–O = 2.310(7)–2.376(6) Å) and three oxygen atoms from aqua ligands (Ca–O = 2.375(8)–2.448(8) Å). The Ca(3) ion is octacoordinated through binding with six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− linkers (Ca–O = 2343(6)–2.659(6) Å) and two oxygen atoms from DMA ligands (Ca–O = 2.363(6)–2.380(0) Å). The Ca(4) ion is hexacoordinated and is surrounded by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers (Ca–O = 2.416(6)–2.628(8) Å), one oxygen atom from a terminal aqua ligand (Ca–O = 2.942(1) Å) and one oxygen atom from the terminal hydroxyl group (Ca–O = 2.359(1) Å). The Ca–Ocarboxylate distances are comparable to those reported for other Ca2+-carboxylate complexes.20,57

The carboxylate groups of the TCMTB3− ligand bridge the Ca2+ ions along the a-axis, leading to the formation of tetrametallic Ca4O20 clusters (Fig. 2). The Ca⋯Ca distances in the Ca4O20 units range from 3.545(3) to 4.145(3) Å. It is worthwhile to note that the Ca⋯Ca distances between the face-sharing Ca2+ ions are well within the sum of van der Waals radii (the sum of Ca⋯Ca van der Waals radii is 3.58 Å),58 while that between corner-sharing Ca2+ ions is marginally longer. Each Ca4O20 cluster simultaneously crosslinks three TCMTB3− ligands to generate a three-dimensional framework with one-dimensional hexagonal channels running along the a-axis (Fig. S3†). The TCMTB3− ligand exhibits a syn–anti–anti conformation. The overall structure is a pair of identical networks that are mutually interpenetrated into a 2-fold interpenetrated framework with weak π–π interactions (4.17 Å and 4.95 Å between the centroids of neighbouring TCMTB3− linkers). Disordered solvent molecules are present in the channels. PLATON analysis indicates that the total solvent accessible volume is 1851.6 Å3 which corresponds to 34.7% of the unit cell volume. Topology analysis indicates that this framework is a 3-nodal net (see the ESI† for other details).


Fig. 2 A view of the framework of Ca-TCMTB (down the b-axis) (H-atoms and solvent molecules are omitted for clarity).

Sr-TCMTB

Sr-TCMTB is a two-dimensional layered structure which crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The asymmetric unit of the unit cell consists of four Sr2+ ions, two TCMTB3− linkers, six metal-bound DMA molecules, one terminally coordinated water molecule, one hydroxyl anion and a bridged acetate anion. The TCMTB3− exhibits hexadentate and octadentate connectivity towards Sr2+ ions through terminal, chelating and bridging coordination modes. Sr(1), Sr(2) and Sr(3) are ligated by eight oxygen atoms, while the Sr(4) ion is heptacoordinated, again surrounded by only oxygen atoms. The Sr(1) ion is coordinated to six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− ligands, one DMA and one aqua molecule. Sr(2) is coordinated to six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− and two DMA molecules. Sr(3) is coordinated to six carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3−, oxygen atom from the acetate anion and one oxygen atom from a DMA molecule. Sr(4) is coordinated to two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two different TCMTB3−, two oxygen atoms from the η2-bridged acetate ion, one oxygen atom from the terminal aqua ligand, one oxygen atom from DMA and a terminal hydroxide. Thus, a tetranuclear Sr4O23 inorganic SBU is formed which runs along the a-axis (connected through –Sr–O–Sr– linkages) in which the four Sr2+ polyhedra are assembled through face-sharing (see Fig. S5†). The Sr–O bond distances lie in the range of 2.481(8)–2.771(8) Å, which are consistent with the values observed for other Sr-MOFs.57–61 The –Sr–O–Sr– connectivity is confined to the Sr4O23 tetramer only and hence the structure is regarded as a two-dimensional layered polymer. The 2D layers contain dangling hydroxyl and carboxylate groups on the basal planes which form hydrogen bonds (O⋯O; 2.826 Å), resulting in a three-dimensional H-bonded supramolecular framework. Fig. 3 depicts the hydrogen bonds formed between the adjacent layers. The two-dimensional sheets are interpenetrated with each other, forming a 2-fold interpenetrated network with a total solvent accessible volume of 797.8 Å3 (14.4%). Sr-TCMTB is a doubly interpenetrated layered 4-nodel net (see the ESI† for other details).
Fig. 3 Crystal structure of Sr-TCMTB exhibiting two 2D sheets connected by H-bonds (green dashed lines).

Ba-TCMTB

Ba-TCMTB crystallizes in the triclinic centrosymmetric space group with nine crystallographically unique Ba2+ ions, four TCMTB3− ligands, six coordinated nitrate anions and fourteen coordinated DMA molecules in the asymmetric unit of the unit cell. The TCMTB3− linkers adopt two different types of overall connectivity (Fig. S6†). The nitrate anions act as bridges between the Ba2+ ions. As shown in Fig. 4, Ba2+ ions in Ba-TCMTB exhibit two types of coordination environments: octacoordinated at Ba(2), Ba(4), Ba(6) and Ba(8) ions and nonacoordinated at Ba(1), Ba(3), Ba(5), Ba(7) and Ba(9) ions. Ba(2), Ba(4) and Ba(6) are coordinated with two η2-carboxylate oxygen atoms which also bridge the neighbouring barium atoms, three η1-bridging oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− ligands, one oxygen atom from a nitrate anion and two oxygen atoms from DMA molecules. The Ba(8) ion is coordinated with two η2-carboxylate oxygen atoms, three η1-bridging carboxylate oxygen atoms, two chelating oxygen atoms from a nitrate ion and one oxygen atom from a DMA molecule. Each nonacoordinated Ba2+ ion possesses a unique coordination environment. Ba(1) is coordinated to six carboxylate oxygen from four different TCMTB3− ligands, two oxygen from a nitrate anion and one oxygen atom from a DMA molecule. Ba(3) is coordinated to four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− ligands, four oxygen atoms from two chelating nitrate anions and one oxygen atom from a DMA molecule. Ba(5) is coordinated to eight carboxylate oxygen atoms from five different TCMTB3− ligands and one oxygen atom from a DMA molecule. Ba(7) is coordinated to four carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different TCMTB3− linkers, three oxygen atoms from two nitrate anions and two oxygen atoms from two DMA molecules. The Ba(9) ion is coordinated to five carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different TCMTB3− ligands, two oxygen atoms from one nitrate anion and two oxygen from DMA molecules.
Fig. 4 (a) Coordination environment of Ba2+ ions in Ba-TCMTB; (b) ligand-to-metal connectivity.

All the nine Ba2+ ions are interconnected through μ2-O bridges furnished by nitrate and carboxylate groups along the c-axis to form one-dimensional Ba–O–Ba infinite chains as the inorganic SBUs. The Ba–O bond distances are found to be in the range of 2.628(4)–2.982(5) Å. The Ba⋯Ba distances are in the range of 4.1254(8)–4.3627(1) Å (the sum of van der Waals radii of Ba is 4.28 Å) (Table S4†).59,60 The adjacent Ba–O–Ba chains are bridged by TCMTB3− linkers in the ab-plane to form the final 3-D network. The overall structure of the Ba-TCMTB MOF is a 2-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional network with a weak π–π interaction of 3.97 and 3.99 Å between the ligand centroids.

Structural trends

Fig. 5a illustrates the M–O–M connectivity in the alkaline-earth metal MOFs through the polyhedra connected to each other. The ligand denticity observed in all these complexes is shown in Chart 1. As would be expected, both the coordination number of the metal ions and the lengths of the inorganic building units increase upon moving down the group (Fig. 5a and Table 2). The Mg2+ ions show binuclear, Ca2+ and Sr2+ ions show tetranuclear and Ba2+ ions show nonanuclear M–O–M connectivity. In addition, the nature of the polyhedral connectivity changes from corner-sharing to purely face-sharing in Ba-TCMTB. Isolated Mg octahedra in Mg-TCMTB are connected through carboxylate oxygen termini, while three face-sharing polyhedra and one corner-sharing polyhedron occur in Ca-TCMTB. Sr-TCMTB and Ba-TCMTB MOFs show only face-sharing polyhedral connectivity. The present studies have also shown that the ligand denticity increases as a function of the cation size and coordination number requirements. Ba2+ being the largest stable metal of group 2 generates infinite Ba–O–Ba connectivity. Though the size of metal ions increases consistently upon going down the group, the mode of interpenetration remains constant, wherein all the four MOFs show very similar 2-fold interpenetration (Fig. 5b).

Thermogravimetric analysis

Thermogravimetric analysis has been performed at a heating rate of 10 °C per minute in the temperature range of 25 to 1000 °C under continuous flow of nitrogen gas. All the complexes show nearly similar thermal decomposition behaviour with continuous weight loss (Fig. S11, ESI†). The weight loss observed up to ∼280 °C is attributed to the loss of solvent molecules (both coordinated and uncoordinated lattice solvent molecules). After removal of the solvent molecules, a gradual decomposition of organic ligands occurs. The PXRD patterns of the TGA analysed samples are significantly different from the corresponding patterns of the as-synthesized samples (Fig. S13–16, ESI†). The differences in PXRD patterns clearly indicate the decomposition of the frameworks upon heating.

Surface area measurements

Porosity measurements have been carried out by measuring the N2 adsorption/desorption isotherms on freshly activated samples at 77 K (Fig. S17, ESI†). Mg-TCMTB is microporous in nature with a physisorption isotherm of type I which is characterized by a sharp uptake at low pressure (P/P0 < 0.10). The isotherm of Mg-TCMTB also reflects the irreversibility of the physisorption phenomenon by a small hysteresis. The other MOFs follow the type IV isotherm and are mainly non-porous. Table S5† summarizes the BET and Langmuir surface areas obtained from the N2 adsorption isotherms.

Photoluminescence studies

The photoluminescence (PL) properties of the MOFs have been investigated as their DMSO dispersion, owing to their poor solubility in most solvents. As shown in Fig. 6, the free ligand exhibits an intense emission maximum at 340 nm and a shoulder at 425 nm upon excitation at 270 nm. The MOFs exhibit broad emission bands with emission maxima centered at around 380 nm upon excitation at 300 nm. Conclusions

Four new alkaline-earth metal MOFs have been synthesized and structurally characterized. In view of the difficulty in constructing MOFs of alkaline earth metals, a flexible tricarboxylic acid has been chosen which can serve as a better building block for targeting 3-D structures as it exhibits multiple binding sites and rotational freedom. This work also demonstrates that different alkaline earth metal cations, Mg2+, Ca2+, Sr2+ and Ba2+, can form inorganic SBUs of different lengths as the ionic radii, polarizability and coordination number increase gradually upon moving down the group. Given these differences, the detailed crystallographic analyses have shown that the overall structures of all the four MOFs are 2-fold interpenetrated, resulting from mutual interweaving of two similar nets. The frameworks comprise parallel –M–O–M– chains bridged by the uncharged aromatic core of the ligand forming 1-D hexagonal channels. The N2 adsorption/desorption measurements performed on these complexes agree with the fact that Mg-TCMTB is a microporous, whereas the Ca-, Sr- and Ba-TCMTB MOFs adsorb very little amounts of N2 which is indicative of either mesoporosity or framework collapse upon removal of the solvent molecules.
