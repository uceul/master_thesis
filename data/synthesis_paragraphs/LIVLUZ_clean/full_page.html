Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 39, January 2014, Pages 70-74

Metal cation-dependent construction of two 3-D interpenetrating networks based on the ligand 1-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole

Author links open overlay panelTengLiaXiangLiuaZhuang-PengHuangaQiangLinaChu-LingLinaQing-GuangZhanaXiang-DongXubYue-PengCaia
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2013.10.036Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Two metal cation-dependent 3-D d10 metal-organic frameworks 1-2 were constructed.

•

1 and 2 present 5-/4-fold interpenetrating diamondoid networks, respectively.

•

1-D channels in MOF 2 makes it show a selective adsorption of CO2 over N2/H2.

Abstract

Two d10 metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with 1-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt), namely [Zn(cpt)2]n(1) and {[Cd(cpt)2] · 2DMF}n (2), have been solvothermally synthesized by fine control over synthetic conditions, and structurally characterized. The results reveal that compound 1, once reported in literature, crystallizes in the acentric Cc space groups, in which the tetrahedral Zn2 + center in 1 coordinates to two triazole nitrogen atoms and two carboxylate oxygen atoms from four μ2 − η1:η1 bridging cpt− ligands, leading to a noncentrosymmetric 5-fold interpenetrating diamondoid 3-D network. On the other hand, new compound 2 has orthorhombic and space group Pbc21, in which distorted octahedral Cd2 + center in 2 coordinates to two triazole nitrogen atoms and two carboxylate oxygen atoms from four μ2 − η1:η1:η1 bridging cpt− ligands, resulting in an interesting 4-fold interpenetrating diamondoid 3-D network structures containing 1-D channels with a cross section of 9.5 × 9.3 Å. Considering their different topological structures, the gas adsorption experiments of 2 were carried out, and the results show that 2 presents a selective adsorption of CO2 over N2/H2. Moreover, the fluorescent properties of compounds 1 and 2 were also studied.

Graphical abstract

Two 3-D d10 MOFs 1-2 were metal cation-dependent, in which 1 is a 5-fold interpenetrating diamondoid network, while 2 is an interesting 4-fold interpenetrating diamondoid 3-D network structures containing 1-D channels presenting a selective adsorption of CO2 over N2/H2.

The construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has grown rapidly in chemistry and material science fields over the past two decades; this interest stems from their intriguing variety of topologies and structural diversity as well as from their potential applications as functional materials, such as gas storage, ion-exchange, catalysis, magnetism, and molecular sensing [1], [2], [3]. Therefore, rational design and construction of new materials of MOFs with specific networks have become a particularly important and topical subject. To date, although people have been making dramatic progress in the research of the optimum strategy and manipulation in the self-assembly of MOFs [4], [5], [6], since these desired and specific properties are markedly dependent on the coordination network topology, it is still a big challenge to achieve target MOFs which are designed in advance. Many efforts have been devoted to fundamental structural aspects to understand and control the several factors that affect the self-assembly of MOF architectures. These factors include the coordination geometry of metal cations, the structure of organic ligands, the solvent system, pH value, temperature, the counter-anion, and the ligand-to-metal ratio [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13]. Accordingly, an investigation for understanding the relationships between the structures of MOFs and those factors is the key of controlling MOF assembly. Among them, the central metal ions play an extremely important role in directing the extended structure of the resulting complexes mainly through their coordination configuration and oxidation state significant effecting on coordination modes of organic ligands. Thus, considerable efforts have been devoted to design or choose the central metal cations [14], [15], [16], [17].

As a part of our work towards rational design and preparation of functional coordination polymers with intriguing structures and potential applications in gas adsorption, we have recently carried out a systematic study of metal–organic complexes from the multifunctional aromatic polycarboxylate ligands such as isonicotinate [14], and 4, 5-imidazoledicarboxylate [18], [19], [20], 2,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate [21], and 2,6-dimethylpyridine-3,5-dicarboxylate [22]. Importantly, the assembly process of these ligands to metal cations has been highly influenced by temperature, organic ligands, central metal ions and metal to ligand ratio, etc. In some cases, a small change in any of these factors can bring about new complexes with different structural topologies and different functions. Through controlling and tuning these effect factors, we have prepared a series of novel coordination polymers [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22], but metal–organic complexes from various metal ions and 4-(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (Hcpt) with aromatic carboxylic acid and triazolyl groups have seldom been reported.

As an extension of our previous work, we herein report the construction of zinc(II) and cadmium(II) complexes with 1-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt), namely, [Zn(cpt)2]n (1) and {[Cd(cpt)2]2 · 2DMF}n (2) (Scheme 1). Controlled syntheses and structural analyses of two compounds have been discussed in detail. Moreover, the luminescence of 1–2 in the solid state at room temperature and gas (N2, H2, CO2)-adsorption properties of 2 at different temperatures have also been investigated.

A slightly revised experimental procedure was employed to synthesize ligand Hcpt [23]. And the compounds 1 and 2 were synthesized solvothermally by treating MIICl2 (M = Zn(1), Cd(2)), Hcpt and DMF at 150 °C for three days [24]. The molecular formula of two compounds was identified by elemental analysis, and they were also characterized by IR and X-ray diffraction. In the IR spectrum of compounds 1 and 2, the strong bands at ca. 1687, 1608 and 1384 cm− 1 or so, respectively, correspond to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching bands of the carboxylate groups, and the strong peaks appear at 1382 and 1605 cm− 1 diagnostic of coordinated carboxylate, whereas the peak due to DMF appears at 1650 cm− 1 in compound 2. Moreover, the absence of strong bands ranging from 1693 to 1730 cm− 1 for compounds 1–2 indicates that the ligand Hcpt in two compounds are deprotonated [25].

Single-crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compound [Zn(cpt)2]n (1) is crystallized in the non-centrosymmetric monoclinic space group Cc, and once reported by Wang et al. in 2012 [26], showing 5-fold interpenetrating 3-D diamondoid network (Scheme 1 and Fig. S1–2). Compound 2 with the formula of {[Cd(cpt)2] · 2DMF}n was colorless block-shaped crystals and presents orthorhombic space group Pbc21 [27]. Once isolated, it is stable in air and insoluble in common organic solvents. Its solid-state structure reveals that the asymmetric unit consists of one Cd(II), two coordinated cpt− ligands and two free DMF molecules. Each metal Cd2 + ion shows distorted octahedral geometry with equatorial coordination from one nitrogen atom (N4) of 1,2,4-triazole group and three oxygen atoms (O2a, O3b, O4b) of two bidentate carboxylates, while one 1,2,4-triazole N atom (N1) and one oxygen atom (O1a) of one bidentate carboxylate occupy the axial sites. This way, each metal Cd2 + ion is bonded to four different cpt− ligand units (Fig. 1). The CdO as well as CdN bond distances (see Table S1 in the Supporting Information) are comparable to those observed in other octahedral complexes of the metal ion [16]. Similar to 1, ligand Hcpt in compound 2 was also completely deprotonated, and each cpt− ligand bridges two Cd2 + ions and adopts μ2 − η1:η1:η1 coordination mode (mode II) (Scheme 2), in which the 1,2,4-triazole and the benzene moieties are not coplanar but slightly twisted with respect to each other (dihedral angles of 34.98° and 42.75°, respectively). This binding mode leads to the formation of large adamantane-like cages with a common Cd⋯Cd distance of 12.4 Å (Fig. 2a–b). Because of the spacious nature of a single network, it allows another three identical diamondoid networks to penetrate it leading to the formation of 4-fold interpenetrated 3-D metal–organic framework containing 1-D channels with cross section of ca. 9.5×9.3 Å (the channel size is measured by considering the van der Waals radii of the constituting atoms) extending along the crystallographic a axis (Fig. 2c–d). The guest DMF molecules are accommodated inside 1-D channels, which account for 41.5% solvent-accessible voids calculated by PLATON [28] and is stabilized by weak hydrogen bonding CH⋯O/π interactions between 1,2,4-triazole and DMF molecules (Table S2).

The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of 2 was investigated. The simulated and experimental PXRD patterns of 2 are shown in Fig. S3 indicating the phase purity of the products. The most peak positions of simulated and experimental patterns are in good agreement with each other, and the differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the powder samples [29].

Compound 1 was once reported in literature [26], so compound 2 was selected to examine their thermal stability. The TGA curve of compound 2 shows that the weight loss of 22.99% from 38 to 181 °C is due to the removal of two uncoordinated DMF molecules. There is no obvious weight loss before 265 C in the TGA curve of compound 2. Beyond this temperature, a rapid weight loss is observed, resulting in the formation of CdO as the main residue justified by XPRD (Figs. S3–4).

The solid-state emission spectra of compounds 1–2 together with the ligand Hcpt in this work have been investigated at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 3, the intense emission peaks appear at 413 nm (λex = 335 nm) for 1 and 392 nm (λex = 305 nm) for 2. As for the free organic ligand Hcpt, the weak emission is observed at 475 nm (λex = 343 nm). Presumably, the emissions for compounds 1–2 should be ascribed to the metal-perturbed intraligand charge transfers, whereas the blue-shift of these bands compared to the carboxylate cpt− ligand may be the result of the metal–ligand coordinative interactions. The differences in emissions for 1 and 2 are probably due to the dissimilar coordination environments around the central metal ions and differences of guest DMF molecules in the channels of MOF [30].

Because compounds 1 and 2 account for 1.5% and 41.5% solvent-accessible voids calculated by PLATON [28], the host framework of 2 was subjected to gas sorptions using CO2, H2 and N2. Before isotherm measurements, the bulk sample of 2 was dried under high vacuum at 200 C for 1 h to remove DMF molecules. The frameworks display selective sorption of CO2 over N2 and H2. As depicted in Fig. 4, the sorption isotherms of N2 and H2 for 2 at 77 K revealed only surface adsorption, thus indicating that neither N2 nor H2 molecules can diffuse into the channels. At relatively high pressure, slight loading of N2 occurred, which may arise from capillary condensation. On the contrary, the adsorption isotherms of CO2 measured at 195 K for 2 exhibited satisfactory capacity for CO2 storage of type-I profiles.

Comparison of the present two structures may provide clues to the design of new solid materials. From the above analysis and discussion, based on the same ligand Hcpt and the same reaction conditions except different metal cations, two compounds 1–2 display different assemblies and topological structures (Scheme 1). Obviously, the coordination-induced effect of metal cations with different geometrical configurations plays a very important role in the different assembling process of compounds 1 and 2 under the employed reaction conditions. Because of small atomic radius and low coordination number (CN = 4), Zn2 + ion in compound 1 was four-coordinated to four μ2 − η1:η1 bridging cpt− ligands, in which only one carboxylatic oxygen atom of each cpt− ligand participated in coordination of Zn2 + ion and the rest one oxygen atom involved in hydrogen bonding CH⋯O interactions from adjacent 3-D diamondoid network (Table S2), resulting in closed 5-fold interpenetration network (Fig. S2) without any enwrapped solvent DMF molecule. By comparison, because of big atomic radius and high coordination number (CN = 6), though octahedral coordination sphere of Cd2 + ion in compound 2 has also surrounded by four cpt− ligands similar to that of Zn2 + ion in compound 1, each cpt− ligand presents μ2 – η1:η1:η1 coordination mannar (mode II), resulting in the dihedral angle between the 1,2,4-triazole and the benzene moieties in the cpt− ligand which further increases from 29.04° to 34.98° for 1 and 38.16° to 42.75° for 2. Based on the above-mentioned connection modes between metal ion and cpt− ligand, 4-fold interpenetration diamondoid 3-D network containing 1-D channels in 2 was formed as depicted in Fig. 2. Therefore, study of MOFs' assembling strategies not only adds to structural diversity of coordination polymers but also provides new insights into the relationship between structure and properties of these materials.

As shown in Scheme 1, by using different d10 transition metal chlorides of ZnCl2 and CdCl2, two different topological MOFs of 1 and 2, namely 5-fold interpenetration diamondoid network for 1 and 4-fold interpenetration diamondoid network with 1-D channels for 2, were constructed under the same reaction conditions, in which the coordination modes of the cpt− ligand in complexes 1 and 2 vary from a mode I of μ2:η1:η1 to mode II of μ2:η1:η1:η1. Close comparison of the structures of 1 and 2 reveals that significant differences of their topologies mainly stem from different coordination geometry of metal cations, and then further affect the properties of two complexes. More work needs to be done to understand better the controlling effect of metal cations which is now becoming an interesting topic in crystal engineering and coordination chemistry.
