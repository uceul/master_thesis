Abstract

A fluorine‐functionalized HKUST‐1 (HKUST‐1‐F) has been synthesized by a solvothermal reaction between copper nitrate and a potassium salt of 2‐fluoro‐1,3,5‐benzenetricarboxylic acid (K[H2BTC‐F]). HKUST‐1‐F shows nearly the same crystal structure as that of UHM‐31, a known fluorinated HKUST‐1. Because of its reduced surface area, HKUST‐1‐F takes up smaller amounts of gases (N2 , H2 , CO2 ) than UHM‐31 at 1 bar. After a water vapor adsorption measurement at 298 K, HKUST‐1‐F turned into an unknown structure, based on powder X‐ray diffraction analyses. HKUST‐1‐F could be further functionalized with benzylamine in a postsynthetic modification; the nucleophilic replacement of the F atom in BTC‐F with benzylamine in methylene chloride at room temperature took place in a conversion yield of ~38% based on 1H NMR analyses.

Introduction

A unique nature of crystalline metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) as a porous material is that the assembly of simple building blocks produces a variety of network structures which in turn can be modified to execute various functions. As representative examples, a simple ligand, benzene‐1,3,5‐tricarboxylic acid (H3BTC), reacts with Fe(III), Tb(III), or Cu(II) ions to form catalytic MIL‐100(Fe),1 luminescent and ion‐sensing Tb‐MOF‐76,2 or gas‐separating HKUST‐1,3 respectively. Furthermore, the organic linkers in MOFs can be chemically modified in the solid state, leading to functionalized frameworks and pores. This strategy, the so‐called postsynthetic modification (PSM), has been widely applied to change the framework's surface functionality.4

Wang and Cohen first demonstrated that the —NH2 group of 2‐amino‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate (BDC‐NH2) in IRMOF‐3 can be reacted with various anhydrides in the solid state.5 The initial success of the PSM approach has made it a general method for the functionalization of various MOFs if the organic linkers contain potentially reactive functional groups. The most sought‐after functionality is —NH2 which is usually present in the organic ligand or can be generated postsynthetically, as demonstrated in MIL‐101.6 However, the introduction of —NH2 to the BDC linkers of MIL‐101 is carried out by the reduction of the —NO2 groups which are directly generated by a nitration reaction in a very acidic condition, which may not be applicable to most MOFs.

Fröba and coworkers synthesized 2‐amino‐1,3,5‐benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTC‐NH2) from 2‐fluoro‐1,3,5‐trimethylbenzene in a multistep organic reaction, and prepared UHM‐30, an amino‐functionalized HKUST‐1 or HKUST‐1‐NH2 via a solvothermal reaction with Cu(II) ion.7 Indeed, UHM‐30 could be functionalized through the coupling reaction with acetic anhydride. Since UHM‐30 has nearly the same structure as HKUST‐1 except for the introduced —NH2 groups, it can be anticipated as one of the versatile platform MOFs that will be able to exhibit improved chemical and/or physical properties compared to unfunctionalized HKUST‐1.

HKUST‐1 (aka CuBTC) formulated as Cu3BTC2(H2O)3 is one of the most investigated MOFs thus far and the prototype structure of various MOFs exhibiting the tbo topology.8 Since HKUST‐1 also shows a large CO2 affinity,9 it has been coated on inorganic supports as thin films useful for the membrane applications.10, 11 Very recently, HKUST‐1 has been in the focus again due to its large methane delivery capacity; its CH4 storage capacity is the largest among the known MOFs.12, 13 Recently, Fröba and coworkers reported the preparation of fluoro‐functionalized HKUST‐1 (UHM‐31) from copper nitrate and 2‐fluoro‐1,3,5‐benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTC‐F).14 It has an improved CO2 uptake capacity at 298 K and 1 bar, whereas its H2 and CH4 adsorption capacities remain almost the same. Here, we present the water vapor adsorption and PSMs of UHM‐31 (HKUST‐1‐F in this work). We also report the crystal structure of a potassium salt of H3BTC‐F or K[H2BTC‐F] that has been used for the preparation of HKUST‐1‐F (Figure 1).

Experimental

General Methods

All chemicals and solvents used in the syntheses were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku MiniFlex diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54 Å). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out using a Scinco TGA‐S1000 thermal analysis system. Fourier transform infrared (FT‐IR) spectra of the samples prepared as KBr pellets were measured using a JASCO FT/IR‐4000 spectrophotometer. 1H NMR spectra were obtained on a Bruker 400 MHz NMR spectrometer. Elemental analyses were carried out on a Perkin‐Elmer 2400 Series II CHN analyzer. Adsorption isotherms of ultrahigh‐purity‐grade N2, H2, and CO2 gases at pressures up to 1 bar were measured by standard volumetric procedures on a BELSORP‐mini instrument (BEL‐Japan, INC.). The N2 and H2 isotherms were measured using a liquid nitrogen bath (77 K), and CO2 isotherms were measured using a circulator (253, 273, and 298 K).

Synthesis of Compounds. K[H2BTCF]

A potassium salt of H3BTC‐F was obtained by slightly modifying the published synthetic procedure for H3BTC‐F.7 2‐Flouro‐1,3,5‐trimethylbenzene (10.0 g, 72.4 mmol) was stirred in boiling water (530 mL) for 30 min, and into the mixture potassium permanganate (53.2 g, 337 mmol) and magnesium sulfate heptahydrate (42.6 g, 173 mmol) were added and stirred for 1 h. The produced solid was filtered and washed with hot water (100 mL). The aqueous reaction mixture and filtrate were combined and concentrated under reduced pressure to ~300 mL and stored at a refrigerator for 20 h to give a precipitate. After removing the precipitate, the filtrate was concentrated under reduced pressure to ~150 mL. The solution was then acidified with sulfuric acid (50%) up to pH ~6 to give a white powder, which was washed with EtOH and dried under reduced pressure (4.48 g, 19.6 mmol; yield: 27%). HKUST‐1‐F

Cu(NO3)2·2.5 H2O (40 mg, 0.17 mmol) and K[H2BTC‐F] (21 mg, 0.09 mmol) were dissolved in a mixture of N,N‐dimethylformamide (DMF; 5.0 mL) and H2O (5.0 mL). X‐ray Crystallography

The XRD data for K[H2BTC‐F] and HKUST‐1‐F were collected at 100 K on an ADSC Quantum 210 CCD diffractometer with synchrotron radiation (λ = 0.70000 Å) at 2D SMC with a silicon (111) double‐crystal monochromator at the Pohang Accelerator Laboratory, Korea. The ADSC Quantum‐210 ADX program15 was used for data collection. The program HKL3000sm16 was used for the cell refinement, reduction, and absorption correction. Crystal structure was solved by direct methods and subsequent least‐squares full‐matrix refinements using the SHELX‐TL software package.17 Non‐H atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters, and hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined by applying a riding model. In the case of HKUST‐1‐F, the large volume fraction of the disordered solvent was calculated by the PALTON SOLV CALC18 to 10 725 Å 3, which corresponds to 58.8% of the unit cell volume. The PALTON SQUEEZE18 routine was applied to produce a modified reflection data excluding the solvent contribution (3407 e− per unit cell). The F atom was disordered over three sites. The unit cell diagrams of the structures are displayed in Figures S3 and S4. The crystal and refinement data are listed in Tables S1 and S2. The selected bond distances are listed in Tables S3–S5. Crystallographic data for the structure reported here has been deposited with CCDC 1020923 (K[H2BTC‐F]) and 1020922 (HKUST‐1‐F). These data can be obtained free of charge via http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/conts/retrieving.html or from CCDC, Cambridge.

Water Sorption Measurements

The adsorption isotherms of HKUST‐1 and HKUST‐1‐F were measured at 298 K using the standard volumetric procedure on a BELSORP‐max instrument (BEL‐Japan, INC.). The samples were filtered and dried under low vacuum (~10−3 Torr) in the Schlenk line before they were loaded into the sample tube of the gas adsorption analyzer. The dried samples (~100 mg) were further evacuated at 170 °C (<1.0 × 10−3 Torr) for 10 h just before the measurement. The dead volume of the sample cell was automatically measured using helium gas. Pressure equilibrium points were also collected automatically by the equipment. After each measurement, weight of the samples was measured by a microbalance. An adsorption/desorption cycle took about 90 h.

Results and Discussion

As the unfunctionalized H3BTC (benzene‐1,3,5‐tricarboxlyic acid) has been well known for its interesting hydrogen‐bonding network and supramolecular chemistry,19, 20 we were interested in the crystal structure of H3BTC‐F. Thus, its crystals suitable for single‐crystal XRD analyses were grown in water when a saturated aqueous solution in an open vial was standing on a bench at room temperature for 3 days. While H3BTC is almost insoluble in water, the ligand was readily dissolved in water, which was curious until its crystal structure was elucidated. The crystal structure revealed that the ligand was not H3BTC‐F but a potassium salt, K[H2BTC‐F], exhibiting an extended structure (Figure 2 and Figure S3). Based on the bond distances between C and O atoms, it was possible to identify the C═O double and C—O single bonds of two —COOH groups: 1.304(6) and 1.218(6) Å for C8—O3 and C8—O4; and 1.325(6) and 1.215(6) Å for C9—O5 and C9—O6, respectively. The deprotonated —COO− group shows similar C–O bond distances: 1.259(6) and 1.271(6) Å for C7—O1 and C7—O2. The COOH groups form hydrogen bonds (Figure S3 and Table S3). The K+ ion is coordinated by seven O atoms and two F atoms. The five K—O bond distances ranging from 2.717(4) to 2.909(4) Å are similar to those in K(18‐crown‐6)]+ (2.762(5)–3.050(4) Å)21 and a potassium complex of magneson (2.688(8)–3.116(6) Å),22 but longer than those (2.305(1)–2.597(1) Å) in potassium hydrogen phthalate monohydrate.23 One distance (K1—O1 = 3.275(4) Å) is significantly longer than the others, and thus the K1—O1 bond strength is weak. It is notable that an F atom bridges two K+ ions with bond distances of 2.870(3) and 2.950(3) Å, which is slightly longer than those (2.691(2), 2.861(2) Å) in K{HB(C6F5)3}TMEDA2.24 This crystal structure is also supported by the elemental analysis and TGA results. In the TGA curve obtained under air atmosphere, the organic part decomposes near 300 °C: the inorganic residue was considered as KO2 (27.0 wt%, 325–540 °C) and KO (21.0 wt%, >540 °C) (Figure S5). These values match well with the calculated values based on the empirical formula. It is assumed that during the final synthetic step of acidifying the reaction mixture the potassium salt precipitated before the full acidification of the ligand.

HKUST‐1‐F was prepared by a solvothermal reaction of K[H2BTC‐F] and Cu(NO3)2·2.5 H2O in a mixture of DMF/H2O (5.0/5.0 mL) at 85 °C for 1 day. This reaction condition was different from that employed for UHM‐31, where the solvent was N,N‐dimethylacetamide (DMA).14 In the IR spectrum, the as‐prepared sample showed an Ar–F band at 1230 cm−1, which was not present in the spectrum of HKUST‐1 (Figure S1). Single‐crystal XRD analysis showed that HKUST‐1‐F had the same structure as UHM‐31 with slightly different unit cell parameters (Table S4). The measured PXRD pattern of the as‐synthesized HKUST‐1‐F was coincident with that of the unfunctionalized HKUST‐1, indicating that both MOFs had almost same framework structures (Figure 3).

The occluded guest species, which could not be identified with the single‐crystal X‐ray data owing to severe disorder, were identified as DMF based on the 1H NMR spectrum of the as‐prepared crystals dissolved in DCl/DMSO‐d6 (Figure S2). The TGA curves measured under ambient atmosphere showed a gradual weight loss of 34.8% due to the removal of the occluded DMF guest molecules until 230 °C, at which point the framework decomposed abruptly without involving a plateau (Figure S6). The thermal stability of HKUST‐1‐F was lower than that of HKUST‐1, which was stable until 300 °C at the same measurement condition. For more facile removal of the guest DMF, the as‐prepared crystals were soaked in EtOH for 1 day. As expected, the EtOH in the guest‐exchanged solid could be removed until 150 °C, which is much lower than the temperature (230 °C) for the removal of the DMF in the as‐prepared sample (Figure S7).

When the activated sample prepared by heating the solvent‐exchanged sample with EtOH under vacuum at 160 °C for 6 h was subjected to the TGA analysis, it quickly adsorbed water from air to give the small bump at 50 °C in the TGA trace (Figure S8). This indicates that during the activation process, the coordinated water molecules were detached from the copper centers. A nitrogen adsorption isotherm for the activated sample resulted in a Type‐I adsorption isotherm (Figure S9). The BET surface area was calculated to be 1404 m2/g, which is smaller than that (1507 m2/g) of HKUST‐125 and those of the functionalized HKUST‐1s: 1834 m2/g for UHM‐30 (HKUST‐1‐NH2)7 and 1802 m2/g for UHM‐31.14 In spite of the smaller surface area, HKUST‐1‐F adsorbed a significant amount of H2 at 77 K and 1 bar; the amount (2.1 wt%) is slightly smaller than that of HKUST‐1 (2.47 wt%),25 UHM‐30 (2.28 wt%),7 and UHM‐31 (2.32 wt%)14 (Figure S10). The CO2 gas adsorption isotherms were measured at 253, 273, and 298 K (Figure S11). The CO2 adsorption capacity of HKUST‐1‐F is 4.20 mmol/g at 298 K and 1 bar, which is slightly smaller than (4.69 mmol/g) that for HKUST‐126; however, it is much smaller than (5.26 mmol/g) that for UHM‐307 and (6.56 mmol/g) for UHM‐31.14 The calculated isosteric heat of CO2 adsorption for HKUST‐1‐F based on the three isotherms at 253, 273, and 298 K is 27.9 kJ/mol (Figure S11). In comparison with UHM‐31, HKUST‐1‐F shows reduced gas adsorption properties, presumably due to the smaller BET surface area.

We also conducted water vapor adsorption measurement. HKUST‐1‐F exhibited a same water uptake behavior at low pressure (P/P0 < 0.1) as HKUST‐1, but was saturated with water at a lower pressure (P/P0 ~ 0.1) than HKUST‐1 (P/P0 ~ 0.2) (Figure 4). HKUST‐1 has a desorption trace that almost overlaps with the adsorption branch at a relatively low pressure, which is a known result.26 In contrast, HKUST‐1‐F shows a large discrepancy between the adsorption and desorption traces, implying that water is chemisorbed. HKUST‐1 is known to be unstable in water, leading to a reduced surface area from 1340 to 647 m2/g after a water adsorption cycle and a changed PXRD pattern.26 HKUST‐1‐F was also unstable in water. The PXRD pattern of the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F was different from that for the as‐prepared sample (Figure 4(d)); it was not same as that for the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1 either.26 Moreover, the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F became nonporous based on a N2 adsorption measurement (Figure S10).

An optical microscope examination showed that the morphology of the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F was also different from that of the HKUST‐1‐F; block crystals became tiny needle crystals (Figure S13). We have traced the transformation process by measuring the PXRD patterns of the HKUST‐1‐F sample with water vapor exposure for 24 h. Interestingly, the process involved an amorphous phase that turned to a new crystalline phase corresponding to the needle crystals of the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F (Figure 4(d) and Figure S14). The nonporosity and observed X‐ray intensities at higher diffraction angles of the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F suggest that the water‐sorbed HKUST‐1‐F would have a condensed structure built from the framework destruction and reorganization in the presence of water.

Fluorobenzene undergoes more readily nucleophilic aromatic substitution reactions than other halogenated benzenes, and the reaction is facilitated if the electron‐withdrawing groups are located at ortho and/or para positions as in the BTC‐F linker in HKUST‐1‐F. Even though a significant number of studies have been performed on the PSM of MOFs, those on MOFs containing aryl halides are still rare.4, 27 Furthermore, the aromatic nucleophilic substitution has been reported just only once for the postmodification of UiO‐66‐Br to UiO‐66‐CN at high temperatures (>140 °C).28 We noted that the H3BTC‐F was susceptible to a nucleophilic attack and used as a precursor for the synthesis of H3BTC‐NH2 under very mild condition.7 Thus, a similar reaction was anticipated to take place in HKUST‐1‐F (Scheme 1).

HKUST‐1‐F crystals (100 mg) were reacted with benzylamine (0.10 mL) in 10 mL of methylene chloride (MC) at room temperature for 12 h (Figure 5). The measured PXRD pattern of the product (HKUST‐1‐bam) was same as that of the pristine HKUST‐1‐F with an increased intensity at 2θ = 11.8° (Figure 3). Unfortunately, the activated HKUST‐1‐bam did not take up nitrogen gas at 77 K. The 1H NMR spectrum of HKUST‐1‐bam dissolved in DCl/DMSO‐d6 gave the H3 BTC‐NHCH2Ph signals along with those of the unreacted H3BTC‐F (Figure 5). Based on the integration of the signals, the conversion yield of BTC‐F to BTC‐NHCH2Ph was ~38% (Figure S9). It was also observed that MC, DMF, and unreacted benzylamine were present in the postmodified sample. The TGA trace of HKUST‐1‐bam showed a large increase in the weight of the organic part. When the weight losses above 200 °C, where the thermal decomposition of the organic linkers takes place, are considered, the weight loss ratios between the linkers to inorganic residue (CuO) increased from 57.3/28.7 = 2.0 for HKUST‐1‐F to 57.5/22.9 = 2.5 for HKUST‐1‐bam, which is due to the introduction of the benzylamine groups. However, we could see 19.8 wt % of weight loss below 200 °C, probably due to the removal of the trapped solvent molecules and benzylamine. Therefore, it is presumed that the bulky benzylamine groups might have blocked the pores and thus the DMF molecules could not be replaced with methylene chloride. Another possible reason is that the generated HF reacts quickly with benzylamine to form benzylammonium fluoride salt. We have tried to conduct the nucleophilic substitutions with other amines such as propylamine, ethylenediamine, maleic anhydride, and p‐xylylenediamine in acetone, chloroform, or methylene chloride. However, the alkylamines and maleic anhydride made HKUST‐1‐F crystals amorphous, and the bulky p‐xylylenediamine did not react with HKUST‐1‐F.

Conclusion

We have investigated the gas adsorption properties and PSMs of a fluorinated HKUST‐1 (HKUST‐1‐F), which was prepared by a solvothermal reaction of Cu(II) and K[H2BTC‐F] in DMF/H2O. We determined the crystal structures of K[H2BTC‐F] and HKSUT‐1‐F. HKUST‐1‐F has nearly the same structure as UHM‐31 which was prepared using H3BTC‐F by Fröba and coworkers. HKUST‐1‐F had noticeably reduced H2 and CO2 uptake at 298 K and 1 bar than HKUST‐1 and UHM‐31. The framework of HKUST‐1‐F transformed into an unknown structure during a water vapor adsorption measurement. We demonstrated that HKUST‐1‐F could react with benzylamine under a mild reaction condition, which is the first example of a PSM via an aromatic substitution reaction in HKUST‐1. It is anticipated that HKUST‐1 may be able to produce porous post‐modified structures if the reaction conditions are appropriately chosen under anhydrous conditions.
