Construction of POMOFs with different degrees of interpenetration and the same topology†

Liang Li ab, Jing-Wen Sun a, Jing-Quan Sha *ab, Guang-Ming Li a, Peng-Fei Yan *a and Cheng Wang a
aKey Laboratory of Functional Inorganic Material Chemistry (MOE), School of Chemistry and Materials Science, Heilongjiang University, Harbin 150080, PR China. E-mail: shajq2002@126.com; Yanpf@vip.sina.com
bThe Key Laboratory of Biological Medicine Formulation, Heilongjiang Provincial, School of Pharmacy, Jiamusi University, Jiamusi, 154007, PR China

Received 28th August 2014 , Accepted 13th October 2014

First published on 14th October 2014

Three metal–organic networks with different degrees of interpenetration based on Keggin-type polyoxometalates have been hydrothermally synthesized by a simple change in the organic ligand and structurally characterized. By changing the length and the coordination mode of the ligand, we obtained compound 1 which represents the first example of a three-fold interpenetrated POMOF architecture with pcu topology, and POMs occupy the vertex of the pcu lattice. Compound 2 exhibits a POM-pillared metal–organic framework, which can be described overall as a two-fold interpenetrated pcu framework, and POMs occupy the arris of the pcu lattice. Different from 1 and 2, compound 3 can be described as a single pcu network and POMs occupy the face of the pcu lattice. The influence of the organic ligand on the degree of interpenetration is also discussed. Furthermore, the results indicate that the three compounds present good photocatalytic activities.

Introduction

Self-assembled combinations of well-designed molecular building blocks (MBBs) and organic ligands have attracted extensive attention from materials chemists and have afforded a wide range of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) because of their intriguing structures and fascinating properties like gas absorption and catalysis.1 Because such properties are primarily bound to the structure, the control over the structure is crucial.2 From the synthetic point of view, polyoxometalates (POMs), as primarily soluble anionic metal oxide clusters, are suitable inorganic MBBs to combine with different transition metal organic subunits due to their discrete structures as well as their biological, magnetic, materials science and catalytic properties.3 Therefore, grafting POMs into MOFs can integrate both the properties of the MOFs and POMs in the same system, which can open up a new avenue for the creation of a variety of novel topologies and multifunctional POM-based metal–organic frameworks (POMOFs).4

Interpenetrated systems, which are one of the common characteristics of organic–inorganic hybrid compounds, have become more and more attractive because of their fascinating structures and special applications,5 and many spectacular interpenetrated topologies have been observed.6 The targeted synthesis of many compounds with interpenetrated features has been performed by changing the temperature or the concentration of the reactants,7 the types of anions in salts,8 the organic ligands9 and the template during the synthesis.10 However, only a few examples of POMOFs with interpenetrated structures were reported,11 which may be due to the bigger size and the uncontrollable coordination geometry of POMs. Therefore, the study of interpenetrated POMOFs remains greatly challenging. Note that recent studies show that interpenetration is not quite so bad in many aspects. For example, theoretical calculations have predicted that non-interpenetrated structures would have better gas-sorption capacities than interpenetrated structures,12 but Forster's group reported that interpenetrated structures showed higher gas-sorption capacities for N2 and H2 gases at 77 K under both low and high pressures and at 298 K under high pressure in 2008.10a In 2012, Suh and coworkers also reported that the interpenetrated structure exhibits generally much higher gas adsorption capacities than the non-interpenetrated structure at low pressures.10d Despite these contradictory arguments, it is difficult to prove which one is the more general case because the study should compare the properties of frameworks with similar structures that only differ in their degree of interpenetration, namely, there is a lack of a systematic study on how to control the interpenetration.

Based on the previous work and comprehensive consideration as well as to obtain and control the degree of interpenetration of POMOF networks, we relied on the suitably designed structure and coordination mode of organic molecules as a general strategy for the preparation of target compounds. In this work, we selected 4,4′-bipyridine (hereafter denoted as bipy), 1,4-bis(pyrid-4-yl)benzene (hereafter denoted as bpyp) and 1,4-bis(1-imidazolyl)benzene (hereafter denoted as bib) (Scheme 1) to connect with POM clusters and copper atoms in order to construct POMOF compounds with different degrees of interpenetration; more specifically, compounds 1, 2 and 3 exhibit three-fold, two-fold and non-interpenetrated frameworks, respectively (Scheme 1). Furthermore, we investigated their photocatalytic activities for the decomposition of Rhodamine B.

Experimental section

General methods and materials

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Elemental analysis (C, H, N and Cu) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer and on a Leaman inductively coupled plasma (ICP) spectrometer. The IR spectra were obtained on an Alpha Centauri FT/IR spectrometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. The XPRD patterns were obtained using a Rigaku D/Max 2500V PC diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation; the scanning rate was 4° s−1, with 2θ ranging from 5–40°. UV-Vis absorption spectra were recorded on a 756 CRT UV-Vis spectrophotometer.
Synthesis of [Cu2(bipy)2][H2SiW12O40]·(bipy) (1). A mixture of H4[SiW12O40] (300 mg), bipy (80 mg), Cu(NO3)2 (160 mg) and (CH3)4NOH (4 mmol) was dissolved in 10 ml of distilled water at room temperature. The pH value was adjusted to about 2.4 with 2.0 M HCl and then the solution was kept at 170 °C for 4 days. The reactor was then slowly cooled to room temperature over a period of 13 h. Dark red block crystals of 1 were obtained (32% yield based on Cu). Elemental analysis found: C 10.34, H 0.81, N 2.38, Cu 3.68%; calc. for 1, C30H26N6Cu2SiW12O40 (3471.82): C 10.37, H 0.75, N 2.42, Cu 3.69%.
Synthesis of [Cu(bpyb)2(H2O)2][H2SiW12O40]·(bpyb) (2). A mixture of H4[SiW12O40] (300 mg), bpyb (80 mg), Cu(NO3)2 (160 mg) and (CH3)4NOH (4 mmol) was dissolved in 10 ml of distilled water at room temperature. The pH value was adjusted to about 2.9 with 2.0 M HCl and then the solution was kept at 170 °C for 4 days. The reactor was then slowly cooled to room temperature over a period of 13 h. Blue block crystals of 2 were obtained (42% yield based on Cu). Elemental analysis found: C 15.66, H 1.23, N 2.27, Cu 1.72%; calc. for 1, C48H42N6CuSiW12O42 (3672.42): C 15.68, H 1.14, N 2.29, Cu 1.74%.
Synthesis of [Cu(bib)][H2SiW12O40]·(bib)·2H2O (3). A mixture of H4[SiW12O40] (300 mg), bib (80 mg), Cu(NO3)2 (160 mg) and (CH3)4NOH (4 mmol) was dissolved in 10 ml of distilled water at room temperature. The pH value was adjusted to about 2.8 with 2.0 M HCl and then the solution was kept at 170 °C for 4 days. The reactor was then slowly cooled to room temperature over a period of 13 h. Green block crystals of 3 were obtained (28% yield based on Cu). Elemental analysis found: C 8.46, H 0.81, N 3.27, Cu 1.87%; calc. for 1, C24H26N8CuSiW12O42 (3396.20): C 8.48, H 0.76, N 3.30, Cu 1.88%.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement

X-ray single crystal diffraction data for compounds 1 to 3 were collected on a Bruker Apex-II CCD detector using graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Routine Lorentz and polarization corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least squares using the SHELXTL-97 program package on a Legend computer.13 All of the non-hydrogen atoms except some disordered atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal displacement coefficients. The positions of hydrogen atoms on carbon atoms were calculated theoretically. During the refinement, the command “isor” was used to restrain the non-H atoms with ADP and NPD problems, which led to relatively high restraint values: 178 for 1. The command “ISOR” was used to refine atoms C1, C3, C5, C12, C14, C15, N2, N3, O1, O2, O3, O4, O5, O7, O8, O15, O16, and O17. Additionally, the restraint commands “DFIX” and “DANG” were used to amend the pyridine. The detailed crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond distances for compounds 1 to 3 are listed in Table S1 to S3.† The structures of the three compounds reported in this paper have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center with CCDC numbers 1011203 for 1, 1011204 for 2, and 1011205 for 3.

Results and discussion

As described in the experimental section, during the syntheses of the title compounds, the use of the (CH3)4NOH species was necessary for the isolation of the compounds. It may be explained that (CH3)4NOH plays a role in mineralization. Bond valence sum calculations14 for the compounds show that all tungsten atoms are in the +VI oxidation state, and the copper atoms are in the +I oxidation state for 1 and +II for 2 and 3, which was confirmed by crystal color, charge neutrality and coordination environments' bond valence sum calculations. Compounds 1, 2, and 3 crystallize in the monoclinic space groups P21/c, C2/m, and C2/c, respectively.

Structural description

Structure of [Cu2(bipy)2][H2SiW12O40]·(bipy) (1). A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study performed on compound 1 reveals the formation of a 2D six-connected network that crystallizes in the space group P21/c. As shown in Fig. 1a, the asymmetric unit comprises one and a half bipy molecules, half of a {SiW12} cluster and one Cu atom. Each Cu(I) atom is coordinated by two N atoms from two bipy molecules and one O atom from the {SiW12} cluster to furnish a T-shaped coordination geometry (Fig. S1a†). The bond distances are 1.936 Å for Cu1–N1 and 1.939 Å for Cu1–N2. Each {SiW12} cluster acts as a bidentate inorganic ligand coordinating with two Cu centers (Fig. S1b†). Additionally, there are two kinds of crystallographically independent bipy ligands: the one linking with Cu atoms is named L1 (Fig. S1c†) and the other is isolated as L2.
Fig. 1 a) Ball/stick representation of the unit cell of compound 1; the subunit of b) the 2D parallelogram-like fragment and c) the pcu topology; d) the large pore in the single net of 1 viewed from different directions; and e) the topology of the 3-fold interpenetrated structure.

The structure of compound 1 can be described overall as a three-fold interpenetrated POMOF based on the pcu topology. Each pcu framework is constructed from {SiW12} clusters as nodes and Cu atoms and bipy ligands as connectors. More specifically, the Cu centers and L1 molecules are connected to each other forming the 1D chains, which are further connected by {SiW12} clusters forming a 2D parallelogram-like fragment (Fig.1b and S2†). The distance is 15.504 Å for Si–Si and the angle is 88.49 ° for Si–Si–Si. Two parallelogram-like fragments are then linked with each other by the connectors (L2) via the N3–O20 hydrogen bond (2.826 Å) to give a perfectly cubic-like framework with pcu topology (Fig. 1c and S3†). Interestingly, due to the large pore of the whole net (Fig. 1d) and to support the stability of the structure, three identical pcu frameworks interpenetrate one another to achieve a three-fold interpenetrated structure architecture (Fig. 1e and S4†). To our knowledge, this is the first example of a three-fold interpenetrated feature constructed by a POM-based metal–organic hybrid with pcu topology.

Structure of [Cu(bpyb)2(H2O)2][H2SiW12O40]·(bpyb) (2). A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study performed on compound 2 reveals the formation of a 2D six-connected network that crystallizes in the space group C2/m. As shown in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit comprises one bpyb molecule, one-third of a {SiW12} cluster, one-third of a Cu atom and crystal water. Each Cu(II) ion exhibits an octahedral coordination geometry defined by four N atoms from four bpyb molecules and two water molecules (Fig. S5†). The bond distances are 2.038 Å for Cu–N and 2.327 Å for Cu–O; all of the distances are in the normal range. Furthermore, there are two crystallographically independent bpyb molecules: L1 is a bidentate ligand with a linear geometry coordinated by two Cu1 atoms (Fig. S5†) and L2 is isolated. Different from compound 1, the {SiW12} clusters in 2 are isolated.
Fig. 2 a) Ball/stick representation of the unit cell of compound 2; b) the stack of the nearly square MOFs; c) the subunit of 2 with the pcu topology; d) the large pore in the single net of 2 viewed from different directions; and e) the topology of the 2-fold interpenetrated structure.

The structure of compound 2 comprises POM-pillared MOFs and can be described overall as a two-fold interpenetrated framework with the pcu topology. Each pcu framework is constructed from Cu centers as nodes and {SiW12} clusters and bpyb molecules as connectors. First, each Cu(II) cation links with four bpyb molecules and two water molecules forming 6-connected octahedral nodes, and each bpyb ligand as a bidentate model links with two Cu atoms, forming the nearly square MOFs having very large cavities with dimensions of ca. 22.242 × 22.242 Å via Cu and Cu ions (Fig. 2b and S6†). Moreover, POMs play an important role in this compound and conduct the formation of this whole pcu net. Namely, each POM connects two Cu–organic polymers via the hydrogen bond between O9 and O1W (2.835 Å) (Fig. 2c and S7†) resulting in the formation of the pcu framework (Fig. 2d and S8†). Interestingly, because the void space in the single net is so large, two identical pcu nets interpenetrate each other to achieve a two-fold interpenetrated structure architecture (Fig. 2e). Note that there are pores between the two interpenetrated frameworks which are occupied by isolated bpyb molecules (Fig. S9†).

Structure of [Cu(bib)][H2SiW12O40]·(bib)·2H2O (3). A single-crystal X-ray diffraction study performed on compound 3 reveals the formation of a 3D six-connected network that crystallizes in the space group C2/c. As shown in Fig. 3a, the asymmetric unit comprises one bib molecule, half of a {SiW12} cluster, half of a Cu atom and one crystal water molecule. Each Cu(II) cation exhibits an octahedral coordination geometry defined by four O atoms from four {SiW12} clusters and two N atoms from two bib molecules. The bond distances are 2.121–2.314 Å for Cu–O and 1.943 Å for Cu–N. All of the distances are in the normal range. Furthermore, there are two crystallographically independent bib molecules: L1 links with two Cu centers in the trans-mode and L2 is isolated in this compound. Additionally, each {SiW12} cluster as a connector links with four Cu centers with Cu1–O20 and Cu1–O3 bonds (Fig. S10†).
Fig. 3 a) Ball/stick representation of the unit cell of compound 3; b) the ball/stick representation of the inorganic mesh-like nets; c) the pcu topology of 3; d) the large pore in the single net of 3 viewed from different directions; and e) the non-interpenetrated structure.

Different from compounds 1 and 2, the structure of compound 3 can be described as a single pcu network. First, the adjacent Cu centers are held together via the POMs to construct the inorganic mesh-like nets (Fig. 3b and S11†). Furthermore, each L1 molecule links two Cu atoms from different nets forming the whole organic–inorganic hybrid compound in the trans-mode (Fig.3c, S12 and S13†), which indicates that the trans-coordination mode can improve the stability of the overall structure. Note that the whole 3D pcu structure constructed by the inorganic mesh-like nets and the Cu–L1 chains possesses a large pore (Fig. 3d & e) which is smaller than those of compound 1 and 2; compound 3 exhibits a non-interpenetrated pcu network, maybe because the POMs occupy the face of the nets, which prevents the formation of the interpenetration features.

Influence of the organic molecule on the structure of the compounds

It is obvious that the type of organic molecule plays a significant role in determining the structure of the resultant compounds shown in Fig. 4. Compound 1 was synthesized by using the bipy ligand and 2 and 3 by using bpyb and bib ligands, respectively. During the investigation of the networks, we found that the length and the coordination angle of the ligand play an important role in the construction of the final structure. In compound 1, the bipy molecule (7.043 Å) is shorter than the {SiW12} cluster (10 Å), which leads to the POMs arranging two sides of the Cu–bipy chains, and the pillared L2 molecules can only connect to POMs clusters via the hydrogen bonds. The above reasons cause the {SiW12} clusters to act as the vertexes to form the pcu lattice and the large cavity (16 Å × 31 Å), which leads to a three-fold interpenetrated structure. When the length of the ligand is increased, i.e. the bpyb molecule (11.331 Å) is longer than the {SiW12} cluster, a 2D metal–organic polymer is obtained wherein the {SiW12} clusters are located exactly. Furthermore, the {SiW12} clusters as pillars link to the Cu centers forming the pcu lattice with Cu centers as vertexes and giving the large cavity (11 Å × 11 Å). As a result, the two-fold interpenetrated structure is formed. When the bib molecule replaces the bpyb molecule in compound 3, only one single pcu net remains, which may be explained as the guiding function of the bib ligands. Firstly, the bib molecule coordinates with the Cu centers in the trans-mode with an angle of 160 °, which is different from that of the bipy and bpyb molecules (180°). Secondly, the length of the bib molecule (9.717 Å) is a little shorter than the diameter of the {SiW12} cluster, which prevents the formation of the Cu–bib layers like in compound 2, forming only Cu–bib chains. As a result, the {SiW12} clusters link with the Cu–bib chains forming the pcu lattice, and the {SiW12} clusters occupy the face of the nets. Due to these reasons, a single pcu net is formed. This result indicates that the controllable and designable length and coordination modes of organic molecules can greatly control the degree of interpenetration in POM-based metal–organic frameworks.

XRPD patterns and IR spectra

The XPRD patterns of compounds 1–3 are presented in Fig. S14.† The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match well, thus indicating that the phase purity of the compounds is good. The difference in the reflection intensities between the simulated and the experimental patterns is due to the different orientations of the crystals in the powder samples. The IR spectra of compounds 1–3 are shown in Fig. S15.† The characteristic bands at 958, 906, and 786 cm−1 for 1; 971, 913 and 783 for 2; and 978, 920 and 798 for 3 are attributed to ν(WO), ν(Si–O), and ν(W–O–W) vibrations, respectively. The bands in the region of 1620– 1205 cm−1 are attributed to the organic ligands.

Photocatalytic properties

The use of POMs as photocatalysts to decompose waste organic molecules so as to purify water resources has attracted great attention in recent years. The introduction of transition metal complexes as functional groups into POMs can enrich their potential applications. Herein, to investigate the photocatalytic activities of compounds 1–3 as catalysts, the photodecomposition of Rhodamine-B (RhB) was evaluated under UV light irradiation through a typical process (see the ESI†). The photodegradation of RhB assisted by compounds 1–3 and their matrix ((NBu4)4[SiW12O40]) is shown in Fig. 5. RhB has a major absorption peak at 554 nm, which decreased from 1.13 to 0.55 for 1, from 0.964 to 0.292 for 2, from 0.968 to 0.523 for 3, and from 0.774 to 0.508 for (NBu4)4[SiW12O40]. After irradiation of compounds 1–3 for 360 min, the photocatalytic decomposition rate, defined as 1 − C/C0, was 51.3% for 1,69.7% for 2 and 46.2% for 3. On the other hand, the photocatalytic decomposition rate using (NBu4)4[SiW12O40] as the catalyst was 35.04% after UV light irradiation for 360 min.
Fig. 5 UV-vis absorption changes observed for RhB solutions as a function of UV light irradiation time in the presence of compounds 1–3 and (NBu4)4[SiW12O40] as catalysts.

The photocatalytic mechanisms may be deduced as follows: during photocatalytic reaction, (*{SiW12}) abstracts electrons from H2O molecules and holds the electrons (reactions (1) and (2)). The reduced POM ({SiW12}−) is quite stable but is rapidly reoxidized in the presence of O2 through reaction (3); the main function of O2 in POM reactions seems to be the reoxidation (regeneration) of the catalyst.15 The reoxidation (reaction (3)) accompanies the generation of superoxides. These cycles occur continuously while the system is exposed to UV light.16 Furthermore, the RhB dye is also excited by UV light to generate *RhB molecules as in reaction (4). Finally, after several photo-oxidation reactions, the degradation of the RhB dye by the hydroxyl radicals and the superoxides occurs17 (reaction (5)).

Conclusions

In summary, we have prepared and characterized three new POMOFs which contain three-fold interpenetrated, two-fold interpenetrated, and non-interpenetrated nets: compounds 1, 2, and 3, respectively. It is interesting to note that the three POMOFs possess the same pcu topology with the POMs occupying the vertex, arris and face of the pcu lattice. It can be concluded that the type of organic molecule plays an important role in forming the final products. This work represents the first example of ligand molecules controlling the degree of interpenetration in POMOFs. So the work provides not only novel examples of POMOFs with different fold interpenetration, but also a new strategy for controlling interpenetrated systems.
