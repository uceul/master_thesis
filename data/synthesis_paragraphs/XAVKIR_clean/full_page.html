A mesoporous (3,36)-connected txt-type metal–organic framework constructed by using a naphthyl-embedded ligand exhibiting high CO2 storage and selectivity†

Zhiyong Lu a, Jianfeng Zhang *a, Haiyan He a, Liting Du *b and Cheng Hang c
aCollege of Mechanics and Materials, Hohai University, Nanjing 210098, China. E-mail: jfzhang_sic@163.com
bAdvanced Analysis and Testing Center, Nanjing Forestry University, Nanjing 210037, China. E-mail: duliting1107@gmail.com
cState Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, Nanjing University, Nanjing 210093, China

Received 31st December 2016 , Accepted 24th February 2017

First published on 24th February 2017

A highly mesoporous (3,36)-connected txt-type metal–organic framework, termed HHU-1, with a naphthalene-embedded ligand was successfully synthesized. HHU-1 exhibits a BET surface area of 2290 m2 g−1 and a high pore volume of 1.33 cm3 g−1. Due to the conjugation effect induced by the naphthyl of ligands, acylamide groups stretching into pores contribute to a comparably higher CO2 adsorption enthalpy (27.7 kJ mol−1), which endows HHU-1 with a high CO2 storage capacity (1071 mg g−1 at 40 bar) as well as high selectivity (CO2:N2 = 140).

Introduction

Carbon dioxide is considered to be the main anthropogenic contributor to the greenhouse gas effect, as it is allegedly responsible for 60% increase in atmospheric temperature, commonly referred to as “global warming”.1–3 In the past decade, great efforts have been dedicated to develop versatile CO2 capture materials and a number of porous solids such as zeolites, and mesoporous silicas have been intensively tested. As a new kind of porous crystalline material, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) exhibit promising properties with respect to CO2 storage and separation.4–9

It has always been a challenge to obtain high CO2 selectivity in MOFs with large pores. Theoretically, for seeking MOFs with high CO2 selectivity as well as high CO2 uptake capacity, large pores and polar functional groups are both preferred.1 As is widely acknowledged, a high surface area and large pores, mesopores for instance, always suggest promising gas storage capacity.10–12 Routine strategies toward high porosity are always focused on ligand elongation, with CO2-non-preferred groups like alkynyl and phenyl as basic units.13–19 As a result, however, high CO2 storage capacity was guaranteed but a decline in CO2 selectivity obeying to the drop of density of CO2 binding sites. Besides the molecule-sieving effect, directly incorporating polar functional groups is most effective in realizing CO2 selectivity especially in MOFs with a high surface area.20–22 Herein, we present a combination of the two strategies by introducing polar functional acylamide groups as well as naphthyl in ligand-elongation towards mesoporous MOFs’ construction (Scheme 1). By this method, a (3,36)-connected txt-type MOF with mesopores was obtained, with a high selectivity (CO2:N2 = 140) as well as high storage capacity of CO2 (1071 mg g−1 at 40 bar). Compared with other txt-type MOFs,23–25 the naphthyl moiety is capable of compelling acylamide groups to point into the pores through the conjugation effect, and this contributes to a comparably higher CO2 adsorption enthalpy (27.7 kJ mol−1).

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemical reagents were obtained from commercial sources and, unless otherwise noted, were used as received without further purification. 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker DRX-500 spectrometer with tetramethylsilane as an internal reference. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a PerkinElmer 240 analyzer. IR spectra were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a VECTOR TM 22 spectrometer using KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray diffractometer at 40 kV, 40 mA with Cu Kα radiation. Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed under a N2 atmosphere (100 ml min−1) with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 using a 2960 SDT thermogravimetric analyzer. MOF supercritical CO2 drying was performed using a Tousimis™ Samdri® PVT-30 critical point dryer (Tousimis, Rockville, MD, USA).

Synthesis of H4PANAD

Details of the synthesis of H4PANAD are given in the ESI.†

Synthesis of [Cu2(PANAD)(H2O)]n (HHU-1)

A solution of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (19.3 mg, 0.08 mmol), H4PANAD (9.98 mg, 0.012 mmol) and 0.05 ml of concentrated HCl was mixed in a solvent of DMF/H2O (v/v = 5/1) (2 ml). The mixture was sealed in a Pyrex tube and heated to 55 °C for 5 days. The green octahedral crystals obtained were filtered and washed with DMF. The yield of the reaction based upon the weight of the solvent-free material is ∼50.6% based upon H4PANAD. Crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray crystallographic data of HHU-1 were collected on a Bruker single-crystal Apex Duo CCD diffractometer with Mo/Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 173 K. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined with full-matrix least squares on F2 using the SHELXTL package. The unit cell includes a large region of disordered solvent molecules, which could not be modeled as discrete atomic sites. Contributions to scattering due to these solvent molecules were removed using the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON; the structure was then refined again using the data generated. Gas adsorption measurements

Low pressure gas sorption measurements were conducted using a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 gas adsorption analyzer. N2 and Ar sorption isotherms were obtained at 77 K in a liquid nitrogen bath and at 87 K in a liquid argon bath, respectively. Pore size distribution (PSD) was calculated from analysis of the Ar isotherm (adsorption branch) at 87 K using non-local density functional theory (NLDFT) implementing a hybrid kernel based on a zeolite/silica model containing cylindrical pores in the software package of Quantachrome AUTOSORB-1 automatic volumetric instrument. High pressure gravimetric CO2 and CH4 adsorption measurements were performed on an ISOSORP HyGpra+V adsorption analyzer (Rubotherm, Germany) at 298 K.

Solvent-exchanged samples were prepared by immersing crystals of HHU-1 in methanol for three days while refreshing the methanol solvent three times every 8 hours. Then we conducted supercritical CO2 drying upon the methanol-exchanged sample, and then successfully obtained the activated sample.

Results and discussion

Structural analysis of HHU-1

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis revealed that HHU-1 crystallizes in the cubic space group Imm, and the symmetric unit consists of one quarter PANAD ligand, two crystallographically quarter Cu2+ ions and one quarter coordinated water molecule (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). All copper clusters are presented in paddle-wheel style, of which four equatorial sites are occupied by different bdc (bdc for benzene-1,3-dicarboxylate) moieties. One of the axial sites of the paddle-wheel is coordinated to the pyridyl nitrogen atom of the ligand, making the paddle-wheel an extending site. Therefore, the small rhombihexahedron cage, composed of 12 cupric paddle-wheel clusters and 24 bdc moieties, totally holds 36 points of extension (Fig. 1a). By regarding ligands as 3-connected nodes and the small rhombihexahedron cages as 36-connected nodes, the structure of HHU-1 can be simplified as a (3,36)-connected txt-type net. Such high connectivity (>24) is quite rare among the ever reported MOFs. The overall structure consists of three types of cages, cages A, B, and C, as shown in Fig. 1c. Cage A is constructed by four PANAD ligands and eight paddle-wheel clusters, with the diameter of 2.1 nm. Cage B, the small rhombihexahedron cage previously mentioned, is with a diameter of 1.2 nm. Although it was illustrated as a sphere, cage C is actually shaped like a cylinder surrounded by six half-ligands and six paddlewheel clusters, with a size of 0.8 nm2 × 1.7 nm. The inner cavity of HHU-1 endows it with high porosity, as calculated using the PLATON routine, with the solvent accessible volume of 74.1% in the dehydrated structure of HHU-1. Notably, the acylamide groups connected with naphthyl show conjugation effects with naphthyl, and this makes these amide groups point into the pores. This effect has never been observed in other txt-type MOFs without the naphthalene moiety.23–25

Adsorption measurements

The permanent porosity of HHU-1 was analyzed based on N2 sorption at 77 K. As shown in Fig. 2, the N2 adsorption for HHU-1 exhibits a reversible pseudo-type I isotherm with a slight step before the plateau appears. This type of isotherm is typical in MOFs with both micro- and mesopores. Similar behavior was also observed in the Ar isotherm of HHU-1 at 87 K, as shown in Fig. 2. The saturated nitrogen uptake at 77 K in HHU-1 is 864.8 cm3 g−1. By applying the Brunauer–Emmer–Teller (BET) model, the apparent surface area of HHU-1 was calculated to be 2290 m2 g−1. The total pore volume of HHU-1 obtained from N2 isotherms (P/P0 = 0.975) is 1.33 cm3 g−1. Based on the Ar adsorption isotherm of HHU-1 at 87 K (Fig. 2), a pore size distribution analysis by the NLDFT method shows that there are distributions of micropores at 0.6–1.0 nm and 1.5–1.7 nm, and mesopores at 2.0–2.5 nm (Fig. 2-insert), which are in coincidence with the three types of cages observed in the crystal structure of HHU-1.
Fig. 2 N2 and Ar adsorption isotherms of HHU-1 (N2 at 77 K and Ar at 87 K, insert is the pore-distribution of HHU-1).

The gas separation capacity of HHU-1 was evaluated by using the CO2 and N2 sorption isotherms. As depicted in Fig. 3, HHU-1 can adsorb 114.6 cm3 g−1 [18.4 wt%, wt% = 100(mass of adsorbed gas)/(mass of MOF + mass of adsorbed gas)] of CO2 under the condition of 273 K and 1 bar. At 298 K and 1 bar, the CO2 uptake amount of HHU-1 is 66.8 cm3 g−1 (11.6 wt%). Based on the adsorption isotherms obtained at 273 K and 298 K, the zero coverage CO2 adsorption enthalpy of HHU-1 is calculated to be 27.7 kJ mol−1 (Fig. 3b), which is higher than the values of some mesoporous MOFs with the same small-rhombihexahedron-cages, such as PCN-6626 (26.2 kJ mol−1), PCN-6826 (21.2 kJ mol−1), Cu-BTB27 (24.1 kJ mol−1), and NU-12528 (23.8 kJ mol−1). Commonly, isoreticular pore expansion leads to a decrease in CO2 adsorption enthalpy. The previous case of minimizing the decrease of CO2 adsorption enthalpy against pore expansion by introducing an amide spacer as a basic unit for ligand-elongation, although was proved to be effective, however, is still unable to avoid the tendency of decline (NJU-Bai21, 25.9 kJ mol−1; NJU-Bai22, 25.6 kJ mol−1; NJU-Bai23, 25.1 kJ mol−1).25 In this case, the conjugation effect caused by the naphthyl part in the ligand makes the amide groups point into the pores. By this configuration, amide groups are more accessible to CO2 molecules, and therefore, a comparably higher CO2 adsorption enthalpy was observed.


Fig. 3 (a) CO2 adsorption isotherms of HHU-1 at 273 K and 298 K; (b) the CO2 adsorption enthalpy of HHU-1.

According to the N2 adsorption isotherms obtained at 273 K (Fig. S7†), the N2 uptake amount of HHU-1 at 1 bar is 5.04 cm3 g−1. Compared with the CO2 uptake (114.6 cm3 g−1) of HHU-1, the discrepancy between CO2 adsorption and N2 indicates selective adsorption of CO2. The adsorptive selectivity factor (S) of CO2 is defined as a molar ratio of the adsorbed amount of CO2 at 0.15 bar to the amount of N2 at 0.75 bar, which is normalized by S = (q1/q2)/(p1/p2), where qi represents the adsorbed quantity of component i, and pi represents the partial pressure of component i. At the partial pressure of 0.15 bar, the CO2 uptake of HHU-1 is 28.1 cm3 g−1, while at the partial pressure of 0.75 bar, the N2 uptake is 4.3 cm3 g−1. Thus, the CO2 adsorption selectivity is calculated to be 32.7. Also, by using the initial slope ratio estimated from Henry's law constants of single-component gas adsorption isotherms, the CO2/N2 separation ratio is calculated to be 32 (Fig. S7†), which is quite consistent with that obtained from the uptake ratio. Meanwhile, this value is comparably higher than that of MOF-17729 (17.5), PCN-8830 (29.8), MIL-10130 (11.4) and most other MOF materials30 (mainly in the range of 10–50). Moreover, by applying the Ideal Adsorption Solution Theory (IAST), HHU-1 shows a high CO2/N2 selectivity of 140 in a 15:85 molar ratio of CO2 and N2 mixtures at 273 K and 1 bar (Fig. S8†), indicating that this material may be a promising candidate for post-combustion CO2 capture application.

We further measured the CO2 storage capacity of HHU-1 at 298 K under high pressure. As shown in Fig. 4a, HHU-1 shows a high excess CO2 uptake of 947.4 mg g−1 (48.7 wt%) at 40 bar. If the gaseous CO2 compressed within the void pore is taken into consideration, the total CO2 uptake is 1071 mg g−1 (51.7 wt%) at 40 bar. This value is larger than that of MIL-100(Cr)31 (792 mg g−1, SBET = 1900 m2 g−1), HKUST-132 (40.1 wt%, SBET = 2211 m2 g−1), Cu-TDPAT33 (892 mg g−1, SBET = 1938 m2 g−1), IRMOF-632 (858 mg g−1, SBET = 2516 m2 g−1), and IRMOF-1132 (647 mg g−1, SBET = 2096 m2 g−1) with a comparative surface area with HHU-1. Interestingly, the CO2 adsorption isotherm of HHU-1 shows a slight steep at P = 13 bar in its CO2 adsorption isotherm. This is correlated with the flexibility induced by amide groups. When CO2 molecules are filled in the pores under high pressure, the naphthalene moieties begin to rotate to adapt to the congestion environment caused by CO2 molecules.


Fig. 4 (a) CO2 adsorption isotherm of HHU-1 in the high pressure range (0–40 bar); (b) CH4 adsorption isotherm of HHU-1 in the high pressure range (0–120 bar).

The potential of HHU-1 for methane storage was also examined. As shown in Fig. 4b, the excess uptake reaches saturation when the pressure is up to 80 bar, with an amount of about 226 cm3 g−1. When the gaseous CH4 within the pores is considered, the total CH4 uptake of HHU-1 at 65 bar and 120 bar is about 293 cm3 g−1 (0.209 g g−1) and 398 cm3 g−1 (0.284 g g−1), respectively. When compared with the compressed natural gas (CNG), HHU-1 achieves methane uptake at 120 bar and 298 K corresponding to 56.8% of the CNG pressurized to 250 bar. Taking 5 bar as a specific lower pressure limit and 65 bar as the upper limit, the methane delivery amount of HHU-1 is 130 cm3 (STP) cm−3, that is a tank filled with HHU-1 can deliver 49.4% as much fuel as the CNG tank operating at the same lower pressure limit and at 250 bar as the upper limit. Although this value is lower compared to the best performing MOFs,34 it is still quite comparable to CoMOF-7435 (136 cm3 cm−3), NOTT-10036 (139 cm3 cm−3), NOTT-11937 (134 cm3 cm−3), and MOF-21038 (131 cm3 cm−3), and higher than DUT-439 (124 cm3 cm−3), ZJU-3240 (120 cm3 cm−3), and MOF-20038 (106 cm3 cm−3).

Conclusions

By introducing polar functional acylamide groups as well as the naphthyl moiety in ligand-elongation, we successfully obtained a mesoporous MOF with txt-topology, HHU-1. The activated sample of HHU-1 exhibits a BET surface area and pore volume of 2290 m2 g−1 and 1.33 cm3 g−1, respectively. The conjugation effect caused by the naphthyl part in the ligand compel amide groups to point into the pores, which makes amide groups more accessible to CO2 molecules and therefore contributes to a comparably higher CO2 adsorption enthalpy. Accordingly, it exhibits high CO2 selectivity and can store quite large amounts of carbon dioxide and methane under high pressure and at room temperature. This work is expected to motivate more extensive studies on the design and synthesis of new organic linkers through the ligand-elongation approach.
