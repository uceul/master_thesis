    Abstract

    By using a presynthetically cross-linked octacarboxylate ligand, a chemically cross-linked version of the NbO-type metal–organic framework (MOF) NOTT-101 (ZJNU-80) was prepared. Single-crystal X-ray structure analysis showed that ZJNU-80 adopts the same topology as the parent compound NOTT-101, and the tethering groups take part in the window partition, not the cage partition. The gas adsorption studies showed that, despite the lower porosity, ZJNU-80a exhibits low-pressure gas adsorption behavior similar to that of the parent MOF NOTT-101a toward CO2, CH4, and N2 at ambient temperature because of the fact that the window partition as a result of chemical cross-linking does not almost alter the pore-size distributions. However, different adsorption behaviors toward 1-butene, a molecule with even larger kinetic diameter than that of the aforementioned adsorbates, were observed because the window partition alters the efficiency with which 1-butene molecules pack within ZJNU-80a and NOTT-101a at conditions close to saturation. This work provides a fundamental understanding on the effect of chemical cross-linking on the MOF’s structure and gas adsorption properties.

    Synopsis

    This work presents a chemically cross-linked version of the prototypical NbO-type metal−organic framework (MOF) and provides a fundamental understanding of the effect of chemcial cross-linking on the MOF’s structure and gas adsorption properties.

    Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs),(1) also called porous coordination polymers (PCPs),(2) are a relatively new but increasingly important class of porous materials that are readily built via the self-assembly of metal ions/metal-containing clusters and organic bridging units. Because of the richness and diversity of metal ions/metal-containing clusters and organic bridging units, the pore sizes of the MOFs can be tuned by their intricate interplay, and the pore environment can be modified by the immobilization of chemical functionalities. The ability to systematically modulate the pore size and readily functionalize the pore surface led to their potential applications in a wide variety of areas including gas adsorption, separation, catalysis, and other technologies.(3)
    Recently, several groups, including us, have become interested in utilizing diisophthalate ligands to prepare porous NbO-type MOFs for gas separation and storage because NbO-type MOFs afford an ideal MOF platform for investigating the impact of functional groups on gas adsorption.(4) Virtually, some studies have revealed that the introduction of specific functional groups into the NbO-type frameworks can enhance the gas binding energy and thus improve the gas uptake.(4a, 4f-4h, 4k, 4p) For example, we investigated the CO2 adsorption properties of three isoreticular NbO-type MOFs in which the organic ligands were attached with distinct highly polarized heterocyclic moieties and demonstrated that the immobilization of highly polarized heterocyclic functional groups into the framework represents an effective approach to improving the CO2 adsorption performance of porous MOF materials.(4a) Besides, it is well-known that the gas adsorption properties of the MOFs also highly depend on the pore size. In general, the large pore size did not necessarily result in high gas uptake capacities for small-sized molecules such as CO2 under ambient conditions. Therefore, it is very important to control the pore size during the MOF synthesis. Although some strategies such as framework interpenetration(5) and ion exchange(6) have been put forward to regulate the pore size,(7) the development of new synthetic strategies is still desired. Because the NbO-type MOF NOTT-101 constructed from copper-based paddlewheel secondary building units (SBUs) and terphenyl-3,3″,5,5″-tetracarboxylic acid (TPTC) contains two different types of pore cages as well as two different types of windows,(4r) which might not be optimal for the specified gas separation and storage, and also it is very difficult for NOTT-101 to realize the pore size tuning using the above-mentioned methods, we therefore plan to develop other synthetic strategies to partition the pore space of NOTT-101.
    Chemical cross-linking is a well-established technique to change the properties of materials such as protein crystals and polymers, but the application of cross-linking to MOFs has been somewhat limited.(8) For example, Lee et al. employed the postsynthetical modification approach to synthesize cross-linked coordination solids showing enhanced framework stabilities compared to the parent material.(9) Cohen et al. used the presynthetical modification approach to construct the cross-linked version of isoreticular MOFs, but the relative positions of the tethering groups are still unclear.(10) From these pioneering seminal works, our initial idea to realize a pore space partition in NOTT-101 was to presynthetically cross-link the diisophthalate ligands TPTC.(4p, 4r) For this purpose, we used an alkyl chain to connect two TPTC ligands, prepared a pre-cross-linked TPTC ligand, 1,3-bis(2,5-diisophthalatophenoxy)propane (H8L), shown in Scheme 1, and used it to construct a copper-based MOF. Herein, we reported a cross-linked NbO-type MOF, with the aim of investigating how the tethering groups dissect the pore space, namely, cage or window partition, together with the influence of the corresponding pore partition on the gas adsorption properties.

    Materials and Measurements

    All solvents and reagents were commercially available and are not further purified as received unless otherwise noted. 2,5-Dibromophenol(11) and dimethyl 5-(pinacolboryl)isophthalate(4b) were synthesized according to the reported literature. A Bruke Avance 400 or 600 spectrometer was used to acquire 1H and 13C NMR spectra. Chemical shifts are given in parts per million (ppm) with reference to the peaks of the residual deuterated solvents [CDCl3 or dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)-d6]. A Nicolet 5DX Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectrometer was used to record FTIR spectra of the samples prepared as KBr pellets between 4000 and 400 cm–1. Elemental analyses for carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen were carried out on a PerkinElmer 240 CHN analyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was conducted on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer in an atmosphere of flowing N2 at a heating rate of 5 K min–1 up to 1073 K. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were performed on a Philips PW3040/60 automated powder diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation source (λ = 1.54056 Å), in which the voltage and current of the X-ray tube are 40 kV and 40 mA, respectively. Gas adsorption isotherm data under a pressure up to 1.1 bar were collected on a Micrometrics ASAP 2020 HD88 surface-area-and-porosity analyzer. The purity (volume percentage) of all of the gases used was as follows: N2, 99.9999%; CO2, 99.999%; CH4, 99.9%; n-C4H10, 99.5%; iso-C4H10, 99.5%; 1-C4H8, 99.5%; iso-C4H8, 99.7%; cis-2-C4H8, 99.5%; trans-2-C4H8, 99.5%. A temperature of 77 K was achieved by using liquid N2, and other specified temperatures (278, 288, and 298 K) were achieved by using a circulating water bath (Julabo F12).

    Crystal Structure Determination

    Crystallographic data were collected on a Bruker SMART APEX II CCD area-detector diffractometer with Cu Ka radiation source (λ = 1.54178 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by a full-matrix-least-squares method using the SHELXTL software package. There are a large number of guest molecules in the unit cell that could not be modeled as specific atom positions. PLATON/SQUEEZE(12) was employed to treat them as diffuse contributions, thus giving a solvent-free hkl file, which was refined again using the SHELXTL software package. The details of the crystal parameters, data collection, and refinements are summarized in Table S1. CCDC 1446515 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper.

    Synthesis and Characterization of the Organic Linker

    1,3-Bis(2,5-dibromophenoxy)propane

    A mixture of 2,5-dibromophenol (2.00 g, 7.94 mmol), 1,3-dibromopropane (0.80 g, 3.97 mmol), and K2CO3 (3.29 g, 23.82 mmol) in anhydrous acetone (100 mL) was heated to reflux and stirred for 48 h under N2. After the mixture was cooled to room temperature, the solvent was rotoevaporated, and the resulting residue was extracted with CH2Cl2. The organic phase was washed with brine, dried over anhydrous MgSO4, and concentrated under reduced pressure. Purification of the resulting solid was achieved by silica gel column chromatography using petroleum ether/CH2Cl2 (8:1, v/v) as the eluent, affording 1,3-bis(2,5-dibromophenoxy)propane as a white solid (1.30 g, 2.39 mmol, 60.2% yield). 1H NMR (CDCl3, 400.0 MHz): δ 7.394 (d, J = 8.4 Hz, 2H), 7.080 (d, J = 2.4 Hz, 2H), 6.990 (dd, J = 8.4 and 2.4 Hz, 2H), 4.277 (t, J = 6.0 Hz, 4H), 2.380 (m, J = 6.0 Hz, 2H).

    1,3-Bis[2,5-bis[3,5-bis(methoxycarbonyl)phenyl]phenoxy]propane

    To a mixture of 1,3-bis(2,5-dibromophenoxy)propane (0.50 g, 0.92 mmol), dimethyl 5-(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolan-2-yl)isophthalate (1.47 g, 4.60 mmol), K3PO4 (2.34 g, 11.03 mmol), and Pd(PPh3)4 (0.21 g, 0.18 mmol) was added dry dioxane (50 mL). The reaction mixture was heated at reflux with stirring for 3 days under N2. After the mixture was cooled, the solvent was rotoevaporated. The residue was extracted with CH2Cl2, and the organic layer was washed with brine, dried over anhydrous MgSO4, and concentrated via rotary evaporation. Purification of the product was achieved by silica gel column chromatography using petroleum ether/CH2Cl2/ethyl acetate (10:10:1, v/v/v) as the eluent. The methyl ester intermediate was obtained as a white solid (0.27 g, 0.27 mmol, 30.0% yield). 1H NMR (CDCl3, 400.0 MHz): δ 8.705 (s, 2H), 8.475–8.523 (m, 10H), 7.487 (d, J = 8.0 Hz, 2H), 7.284–7.355 (m, 4H), 4.315 (t, J = 6.0 Hz, 4H), 4.000 (s, 12H), 3.836 (s, 12H), 2.257 (m, 2H).

    1,3-Bis(2,5-diisophthalatophenoxy)propane (H8L)

    The methyl ester intermediate (0.27 g, 0.27 mmol) was dissolved in a mixture of CH3OH (20 mL) and tetrahydrofuran (20 mL), and then 6 M NaOH (20 mL) was added to the solution. The solution was heated to reflux and stirred overnight and then was allowed to cool. After the solvent was removed by rotary evaporation, the residue was dissolved in water and acidified with concentrated HCl at 273 K. The resulting precipitation was filtered off, washed with water, and dried under reduced pressure at 343 K. The target compound was obtained as an off-white solid in almost quantitative yield. 1H NMR (DMSO-d6, 400.1 MHz): δ 8.473 (s, 2H), 8.414 (d, J = 1.2 Hz, 4H), 8.367 (d, J = 1.6 Hz, 4H), 8.298 (s, 2H), 7.526 (d, J = 7.6 Hz, 2H), 7.382–7.411 (m, 4H), 4.356 (t, J = 6.0 Hz, 4H), 2.139 (m, 2H). 13C NMR (DMSO-d6, 150.9 MHz): δ 167.192, 167.058, 156.277, 140.985, 140.554, 138.322, 134.310, 132.711, 131.836, 131.572, 131.326, 129.587, 128.906, 128.025, 120.384, 111.938, 65.501, 28.986. Selected FTIR (KBr, cm–1): 1718, 1603, 1458, 1394, 1213, 758, 669.

    Synthesis and Characterization of the MOF ZJNU-80

    A mixture of H8L (5.0 mg, 5.65 μmol) and CuCl2·2H2O (5.0 mg, 29.32 μmol) was placed in a 20 mL scintillation vial, and a mixed solvent of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF; 1.5 mL), ethanol (EtOH, 0.5 mL), and water (H2O; 0.1 mL) was added. After the addition of 6 M HCl (100 μL), the vial was capped and heated to 363 K for 5 days and then allowed to cool to room temperature. The small block-shaped crystals formed were filtered off and air-dried. The formula of ZJNU-80 can be best determined to be [Cu4L(H2O)4]·6DMF·2EtOH·H2O on the basis of single-crystal X-ray crystallography, TGA, and microanalysis. Elem anal.     The cross-linked ligand H8L was readily synthesized by a three-step reaction. Briefly, Williamson etherification of 2,5-dibromophenol with 1,3-dibromopropane to give the tetrabromo intermediate, which was cross-coupled with dimethyl 5-(4,4,5,5-tetramethyl-1,3,2-dioxaborolan-2-yl)isophthalate followed by hydrolysis and acidification to generate the target compound. All intermediates and the target compound were characterized by NMR spectroscopy. With the ligand in hand, the direct solvothermal synthesis of copper-based MOFs was pursued. The combination of the ligand and CuCl2·2H2O in a mixed solvent consisting of DMF, EtOH, and H2O in acidic conditions at 363 K for 5 days led to the formation of blue block-shaped crystals (which we termed ZJNU-80), which are insoluble in common organic solvents. FTIR spectra showed that no characteristic vibrational band arising from the carboxylate group was observed in the range of 1680–1760 cm–1, indicating complete deprotonation of the organic ligand (Figure S1).
    TGA was performed to evaluate the thermal stability. As shown in Figure 1a, ZJNU-80 displayed thermal stability comparable to that of the parent MOF NOTT-101, with framework degradation occurring at ca. 603 K. This is different with the earlier studies by Lee et al., who have described that chemical cross-linking makes the fragile coordination polymers more thermally stable.(9) We speculated that no enhancement in the thermal stability is presumably attributable to the thermal lability of the alkyl chains. For compound ZJNU-80, the weight loss of 35.0% from 298 to 583 K corresponds to the release of free solvent molecules and terminal H2O molecules (6DMF + 2EtOH + 5H2O; calcd 35.4%).
    To characterize the structure, the PXRD pattern of the as-synthesized ZJNU-80 was measured. Notably, the PXRD pattern of ZJNU-80 well-matched the one simulated from the single-crystal structure of NOTT-101 (Figure 1b), indicating that chemical cross-linking did not interfere with formation of the isoreticular NbO-type topology architecture. To rule out the possibility that the cross-linked ligands decomposed during the solvothermal reaction, we performed 1H NMR analysis of the acid-digested sample, showing that the ligand remains intact (Figure S2). Obviously, there exist two possibilities regarding the MOF’s structure: (1) one of two diisophthalate moieties in the organic ligand was used structurally in the MOF formation, and the other part was dangling within the MOF’s pore space; (2) the two diisophthalate moieties in the organic ligand are simultaneously used in the MOF construction. In the latter case, there are several possible ways in which the tethering groups can be arranged across the MOF’s pore. For example, it is possible that the tethering groups might dissect either windows or cages.
    To identify which above-mentioned case occurred, the single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of ZJNU-80 was performed, showing that the compound crystallized in the space group of R3̅m, the same as those of the most reported NbO-type MOFs. Besides guest molecules, the asymmetric unit consists of half of copper ion, one-eighth of fully deprotonated ligand, and half of coordinating H2O molecule. Like other NbO-type MOFs, ZJNU-80 consists of dicopper paddlewheel SBUs connected by the organic linkers to form a three-dimensional noninterpenetrated network structure containing two different types of polyhedral nanocages shown in Figure 2a. The spherical cage is formed by 6 dicopper paddlewheels, while the shuttle-shaped cage is formed by 12 dicopper paddlewheels. Furthermore, close examination of the structure revealed that the tethering groups play a role in dissecting the window, not the cage, thus confirming that the second case occurs. This is different from that occurring in most functionalized NbO-type MOFs where the functional groups point to the pore cage and thus play a role in dissecting the cage.(3c, 4p) As a consequence, there are three different types of windows at the interconnections of these two cages. The first type of window contains three dicopper paddlewheels bridged by three isophthalate moieties and can be described as an equilateral triangle by taking into account the connections between the centers of dicopper paddlewheels (Figure 2b). The second type of window is sustained by three dicopper paddlewheels, which are interconnected by one isophthalate moiety and two organic ligands, and can be described as an isosceles triangle (Figure 2d). The third type of window is similar to the second one in shape but is dissected into two small parts by the tethering groups (Figure 2c). Apparently, compared to MOF NOTT-101a, access to the cages of ZJNU-80a is somewhat limited because of the window partition as a result of chemical cross-linking. PLATON analysis indicates that the framework has a potentially solvent-accessible pore volume of 57.3% (6671.0 Å3 out of 11641.5 Å3per unit cell volume) when the solvent molecules and terminal H2O molecules are excluded, which is smaller than that of NOTT-101 (67.4%).
    To investigate the effect of the window partition on the pore textural properties, we performed N2 gas adsorption studies at 77 K on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 surface area and pore size analyzer. Prior to gas measurement, the as-synthesized ZJNU-80 was solvent-exchanged with low-boiling-point acetone followed by heating at 373 K under a dynamic vacuum, generating the activated ZJNU-80a (hereafter, a indicates the activated MOF materials). The integrity of the framework was confirmed by PXRD (Figure S3). As depicted in Figure 3a, ZJNU-80a exhibited the reversible type I adsorption isotherm, revealing the microporous nature for this MOF. On the basis of the N2 isotherms, the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and Langmuir surface areas of ZJNU-80a were calculated to be 2279 and 2516 m2 g–1, respectively (Figure S4). The pore volume determined from the maximum adsorbed amount of N2 is 0.8942 cm3 g–1, which is somewhat higher than the value of 0.789 cm3 g–1 calculated using PLATON software. The result is not unexpected because the gas-adsorption-derived porosities frequently overestimated the crystallographically derived porosities by up to 20%. For example, even for the well investigated MOF HKUST-1, the experimentally measured pore volume is ca. 20% higher than the theoretical values.(13) These values are lower than those of NOTT-101a (2755 and 2961 m2 g–1 and 1.0579 cm3 g–1 for the BET and Langmuir surface areas and pore volume, respectively), which is expected because of the presence of the tethering groups in ZJNU-80a, which occlude the MOF’s pore space. It should be mentioned that the BET surface area and pore volume of ZJNU-80a are higher than those of the dimethyl-functionalized analogue NOTT-106a,(4p) presumably because of the grafting methyl groups on the central benzene spacer of the linkers in the latter compound projecting into the pore cage. However, the pore-size distribution (PSD) of ZJNU-80a determined by applying a nonlocal density functional theory (NLDFT) model to the adsorption data is very similar to that of NOTT-101a (Figure 3a, inset). The experimentally observed results on the pore textural properties also indirectly reflect that the tethering groups take part in the window partition, not the cage partition.
    The adsorption behaviors of ZJNU-80a toward CO2, CH4, and N2 were examined at 278, 288, and 298 K at a pressure of up to 1.1 bar. As shown in Figure 3b, all isotherms exhibit fairly good reversibility without any hysteresis, indicating that all gas molecules adsorbed can be easily removed under vacuum during the desorption process. There is no loss in the adsorbed quantity after four cycles without the reactivation process between each cycle, indicating that the adsorption isotherms are repeatable (Figure S5). At 298 K and 1 atm, the CO2, CH4, and N2 uptakes are 86.4, 20.9, and 6.7 cm3 (STP) g–1, respectively. When the temperature decreased to 278 K, the adsorption amounts increased to 141.7, 31.4, and 9.6 cm3 (STP) g–1, respectively. The CO2 uptake is comparable to that of other NbO-type MOFs such as ZJNU-40(4a) and SNU-50.(4n) Preferable adsorption of CO2 over CH4 and N2 implied possible application in selective adsorption and separation. The CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 Henry’s law selectivities are estimated to be 5.1 and 15.8 at 298 K, respectively. On the basis of adsorption isotherms measured at 298, 288, and 278 K (Figure S6), the isosteric heats of CO2, CH4, and N2 adsorption at low coverage were calculated to be 23.7, 17.5, and 13.0 kJ mol–1 (Figure S7). Surprisingly, compared to the parent MOF NOTT-101, no substantial difference regarding these gas adsorptions was observed (Figures 3b and S6), which means that the tethering groups do not impact the gas uptake capacities, despite the reduced pore volume. The similar gas adsorption properties might be interpreted as follows. At the conditions under which pore saturation is not reached, namely, the relatively low pressure, the gas uptake be mainly controlled by the gas affinity toward the framework. Because the tethering groups partitioning the windows did not virtually change the PSD, the result is reasonably expected.
    The sorption behavior was further investigated using 1-butene as the adsorbate, a molecule with a larger kinetic diameter (4.5 Å) than the above-mentioned ones (CO2, 3.30 Å; CH4, 3.8 Å; N2, 3.64 Å),(3k) at 298 K and a pressure of up to 1.1 bar. In drastic contrast with the above results, the two compounds exhibit different adsorption behavior toward 1-butene. As shown in Figure 3c, ZJNU-80a showed stronger interactions with 1-butene than NOTT-101a at low-pressure regions. At 298 K and 1 atm, 1-butene adsorption of the two compounds nearly reached saturation. Such distinct adsorption behavior can be attributed to the window partition, making the efficiency with which 1-butene molecules pack within ZJNU-80a and NOTT-101a at conditions close to saturation different.(14) As a result, the two compounds exhibit different adsorption capacities toward 1-butene. At 298 K and 1 atm, adsorption uptakes of 1-butene are 187.2 and 208.7 cm3 (STP) g–1 for ZJNU-80a and NOTT-101a, respectively. For other examined C4 hydrocarbon adsorbates including n-butane, isobutane, isobutene, cis-2-butene, and trans-2-butene, ZJNU-80a also shows much lower uptakes than NOTT-101a at 298 K and 1 atm (Figure S8). Therefore, the results perhaps point to a new direction for the design of adsorbents for mixture separations by utilizing a chemical cross-linking strategy to tailor the pore space and thus adjust the molecule packing effect.

    In summary, we successfully achieved a chemical cross-linked version of the prototypical NbO-type MOF NOTT-101 by the direct combination of a tethered octacarboxylate ligand with copper ions under solvothermal conditions. Although the ligand constituting NOTT-101 is presynthetically cross-linked, the resulting MOF ZJNU-80 remained the same topology as that of the parent MOF NOTT-101. Furthermore, compared to the parent MOF NOTT-101, the cross-linked MOF exhibited similar thermal stability as well as low-pressure gas adsorption properties with regard to CO2, CH4, and N2, despite the lower porosity. This is presumably due to the tethering groups participating in the window partition, not the cage partition. When the adsorbate becomes bigger, the tethering groups can tailor the molecule packing efficiency, thus leading to different adsorption behaviors. This work provides a fundamental understanding of the effect of chemical cross-linking on the MOF’s structure and gas adsorption properties. Further studies will focus on the covalent attachment of appropriate organic functionalities to the tethering groups to develop multifunctional MOFs for diverse applications.

        Separating mixtures by exploiting molecular packing effects in microporous materials
        Krishna, Rajamani
        Physical Chemistry Chemical Physics (2015), 17 (1), 39-59CODEN: PPCPFQ; ISSN:1463-9076. (Royal Society of Chemistry)
        A review. We examine mixt. sepns. with microporous adsorbents such as zeolites, metal-org. frameworks (MOFs) and zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs), operating under conditions close to pore satn. Pore satn. is realized, for example, when sepg. bulk liq. phase mixts. of polar compds. such as water, alcs. and ketones. For the operating conditions used in industrial practice, pore satn. is also attained in sepns. of hydrocarbon mixts. such as xylene isomers and hexane isomers. Sepns. under pore satn. conditions are strongly influenced by differences in the satn. capacities of the constituent species; the adsorption is often in favor of the component with the higher satn. capacity. Effective sepns. are achieved by exploiting differences in the efficiency with which mols. pack within the ordered cryst. porous materials. For mixts. of chain alcs., the shorter alc. can be preferentially adsorbed because of its higher satn. capacity. With hydrophilic adsorbents, water can be selectively adsorbed from water-alc. mixts. For sepns. of o-xylene-m-xylene-p-xylene mixts., the pore dimensions of MOFs can be tailored in such a manner as to allow optimal packing of the isomer that needs to be adsorbed preferentially. Subtle configurational differences between linear and branched alkane isomers result in significantly different packing efficiencies within the pore topol. of MFI, AFI, ATS, and CFI zeolites. A common characteristic feature of most sepns. that are reliant on mol. packing effects is that adsorption and intra-cryst. diffusion are synergistic; this enhances the sepn. efficiencies in fixed bed adsorbers.
