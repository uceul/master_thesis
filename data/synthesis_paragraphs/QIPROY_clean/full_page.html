Construction of Cu(II) coordination polymers based on semi-rigid tetrahedral pyridine ligands†

Weiting Yang a, Fei-Yan Yi a, Xiao-Dong Li b, Lei Wang a, Song Dang a and Zhong-Ming Sun *a
aState Key Laboratory of Rare Earth Resource Utilization, Changchun Institute of Applied Chemistry, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Changchun, 130022, China. E-mail: szm@ciac.ac.cn
bJilin Jianzhu Univ., Minist. Educ., Key Lab Songliao Aquat. Environm., 5088 Xincheng St, Changchun 130118, Jilin Province, China

Received 14th August 2013 , Accepted 25th September 2013

First published on 26th September 2013

The assembly of a Cu(II) ion and the semi-rigid tetrahedral pyridine ligand, tetrakis(3-pyridyloxymethylene)methane (L1), yields three coordination polymers (1–3) by synergy between different solvent mixtures. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that Cu4(L1)(OAc)8 (1) features a two-dimensional sql network, and comprises dimeric five-coordinated Cu-centered square pyramids as the inorganic building units; Cu2(L1)(C7H5O2)4 (2) is also a layered assembly but with a 3,4-connected 3,4L13 net. In this complex, mononuclear CuO2N2 square planar structures and dinuclear CuO3N2 square pyramids both serve as the inorganic building units. Cu(H2O)2(L1)(OAc)2 (3) is a three-dimensional framework structure based on six-coordinated CuO2N4 square bipyramidal inorganic nodes and tetrahedral semi-rigid pyridine ligands, and dia topology is simplified for 3.

Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a class of crystalline hybrid materials constructed from the limitless choice of metal ion connectors and polydentate organic bridging ligands. Their high surface area, low framework density and well-defined porosity make them good candidates for capturing energy-related gas, such as H2, CH4 and CO2.1 Alongside the major interest in gas storage, other extensive applications of MOFs have gradually begun to flourish in a wide range of fields, including molecular separation,2 heterogeneous catalysis,3 nonlinear optics,4 drug delivery5 and chemical sensing.6 The development of MOFs for this increasing number of applications is heavily reliant on their fantastic chemical and physical properties which are imparted by the sophisticated and nuanced modulation and functionality of the inorganic vertices and the organic connectors.

Among the various organic ligands for construction of CPs, oxygen- and nitrogen-donor bridging species are dominant, such as carboxylic acids,7 pyridine derivatives8 and so on. In addition, most of the organic linkers reported so far are rigid. In contrast, CPs constructed with flexible ligands have been less documented partially due to their inability to maintain their structural geometries during the self-assembly process. However, flexibility of the ligands is essential to form certain structures with particular properties as the dynamic framework in response to external stimuli, such as gate-opening behaviour.9 Our recent efforts in preparing CPs have been devoted to isolating novel frameworks based on semi-rigid tri-, tetra- and hexacarboxylate linkers, and a series of interesting MOFs with various properties including chirality, magnetism, luminescence, and nonlinear optical and sensing properties have been obtained.10 As an ongoing work, a semi-rigid tetrahedral linker, tetrakis(3-pyridyloxymethylene)methane (L1) was selected to construct CPs in this contribution (Scheme 1). Two layered structures, Cu4(L1)(OAc)8 (1) and Cu2(L1)(C7H5O2)4 (2), and one four-connected open framework, Cu(H2O)2(L1)(OAc)2 (3), have been obtained. We herein report the syntheses, structures and characterizations of these compounds.

Experimental

Materials and measurements

All reagents and solvents for synthesis were commercially available and used without further purification. The ligand L1 was synthesized according to the documented procedures.11 X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) data were collected on a D8 Focus (Bruker) diffractometer at 40 kV and 30 mA with monochromated Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5405 Å), a scan speed of 5 degrees per min and a step size of 0.02° in 2 theta. The elemental analyses of C, H, and N were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer Optima 3300DV spectrometer and a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer, respectively. Thermogravimetric and differential thermal analysis (TG-DTA) data were recorded on a Thermal Analysis Instrument (SDT 2960, TA Instruments, New Castle, DE) from room temperature to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under an air atmosphere.

Syntheses

Synthesis of Cu4(L1)(OAc)8 (1): a solution of Cu(OAc)2·H2O (33 mg, 0.18 mmol) in MeOH (4 mL) was placed in a test tube, and a mixture of MeOH and EtOH (6 mL, 1:1 v/v) was carefully layered on top. A solution of L1 (40 mg, 0.09 mmol) in EtOH (2 mL) was then added on top of these two layers. Then, the tube was sealed, and the contents were allowed to mix slowly. After 24 hours, blue crystals were obtained. Yield: 22 mg (42% based on Cu). Anal. calcd (%) for 1 C41H60Cu4N4O26 (Mr = 1279.09): C, 38.5; H, 4.73; N, 4.38. Found: C, 40.67; H, 4.67; N, 4.43.

Synthesis of Cu2(L1)(C7H5O2)4 (2): Cu(OAc)2·H2O (0.05 mmol, 10 mg), L1 (0.025 mmol, 11 mg) and benzoic acid (0.07 mmol, 9 mg) in a mixed distilled water (4 mL)–EtOH (4 mL) solvent were placed in a 20 mL vial, and then sealed. The sample was heated at 85 °C for 24 h, and cooled to room temperature. After they were washed with distilled water, blue crystals were obtained. Yield: 23 mg (68% based on benzoic acid). Anal. calcd (%) for 2 C53H54Cu2N4O17 (Mr = 1146.08): C, 55.54; H, 4.75; N, 4.89. Found: C, 56.37; H, 4.16; N, 5.01.

Synthesis of Cu(H2O)2(L1)(OAc)2 (3): Cu(OAc)2·H2O (0.05 mmol, 10 mg), L1 (0.025 mmol, 11 mg) and oxalic acid (0.1 mmol, 9 mg) in a mixed distilled water (4 mL)–MeCN (4 mL) solvent were placed in a 20 mL vial, and then sealed. The sample was heated at 85 °C for 24 h, and cooled to room temperature. After they were washed with distilled water, blue crystals were obtained. Yield: 14 mg (75% based on L1). Anal. calcd (%) for 3 C29H56CuN4O23 (Mr = 892.32): C, 39.03; H, 6.33; N, 6.28. Found: C, 38.75; H, 6.91; N, 6.40.

X-ray crystallography study

Single crystals of 1–3 with suitable dimensions were selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses. Data collections were performed on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at a temperature of 293 K. Data reductions and absorption corrections were performed using the SAINT and SADABS software packages, respectively.12 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares using SHELXTL-97 crystallographic software package.13 All of the non-hydrogen atoms were found from the difference Fourier map and refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms associated with the organic ligands were generated geometrically and included in the refinement with fixed positions and thermal parameters. Element analyses and thermogravimetric analysis implied the existence of extra-framework acetate groups and water molecules which could not be located in the difference Fourier maps derived from single crystal data and were removed by the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.14 Efforts to obtain better crystal data have failed. Results and discussion

Syntheses

Compound 1 was synthesized by slow diffusion of Cu(OAc)2 and L1 into a mixture of methanol and ethanol. When water was used instead of ethanol, Cu(H2O)2(L1) with a 3-D framework network was formed, which is isostructural to that reported by Kitagawa et al.8a If a conventional solvothermal method was performed, no crystals of compound 1 were obtained. Compounds 2 and 3 were also synthesized with a L1 ligand, but with different solvent mixtures (water and ethanol for 2, water and acetonitrile for 3). It is noted that additional acids are essential for crystallization of the two complexes, in that benzoate groups serve as co-ligands, coordinating to Cu ions in 2, and oxalic acid acts as a mineralizing agent and pH modulator in the synthesis of 3.

Structure description of 1

The single-crystal diffraction analysis of compound 1 reveals that it crystallizes in the tetragonal space group P42/n. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one crystallographically independent Cu atom, one quarter of a L1 ligand and two coordinated acetate groups. As depicted in Fig. 1, the Cu atom is five coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four acetate groups and by one nitrogen atom of L1, resulting in a distorted square pyramidal geometry. Two CuO4N square pyramids are connected to each other via four acetate groups to form a dinuclear Cu2 cluster. Each L1 ligand acts as a tetragonal linker, bridging four Cu2 dimers with a Cu–N distance of 2.1795(17) Å (Fig. 2). Such a connection between the dimetallic building units and the L1 ligands produces a 2-dimensional (2-D) layered structure which is further packed though π⋯π interactions to form the supramolecular network of compound 1 (Fig. 3). The semi-rigid L1 ligand can be considered as a 4-connected node connecting four Cu2 clusters according to topological analysis. Each dimetallic Cu2 building unit is connected to two L1 and transformed into the edge during simplification. Thus, the network of 1 features a uninodal sql plane net with a point symbol of {44.62}(Fig. S4†).

Structure description of 2

This compound also possesses a 2-D structure, but crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. There are two crystallographically independent Cu atoms, one L1 ligand and four benzoate groups in its asymmetric unit (Fig. 4). Cu1 is four coordinated in a distorted square-planar geometry, by two N atoms from two L1, and two carboxylate O atoms from two benzoate groups (Cu–O: 1.939(3) and 1.950(3) Å, Cu–N: 2.012(4) and 2.0124(4) Å). Cu2 is five-coordinated and displays a distorted pyramid geometry supplied by two N atoms from two L1 ligands (Cu–N: 2.012(4) and 2.025(4) Å) and three carboxylate O atoms from three benzoate groups (Cu2–O5: 1.983(3) Å, Cu2–O7: 1.960(4) Å, Cu2–O8: 2.316(4) Å). Two CuO3N2 square pyramids are connected to each other via two carboxylate groups in two benzoate ligands resulting in a dinuclear Cu2 cluster. The L1 ligand, as a four-connected node, links two Cu1 and two Cu2 atoms. One benzoate adopts a didentate coordination mode (O7, O8) to connect two Cu2 atoms. The other three benzoates are unidentate (O5, O10, O11) and each link one Cu atom. In compound 2, the Cu1-centered planes and the dinuclear Cu2 cluster are linked by L1 ligands to produce the 2-D layered assembly (Fig. 5). Such layers are stabilized by π⋯π interactions between the interlayers to form the whole structure of compound 2 (Fig. 6). Topological analysis reveals a 3,4L13 net with a point symbol of {4.62}2{42.62.82} (Fig. S5†).

Structure description of 3

This compound crystallizes in the tetragonal space group Pn2. The asymmetric unit contains one crystallographically independent Cu site, one quarter of a L1 ligand and one aqua ligand (Fig. 7). The Cu(II) ion is octahedrally coordinated by four N atoms of four L1 at equatorial positions (Cu1–N1: 2.024(2) Å) and two axial aqua ligands (Cu1–O1w: 2.488(3) Å) with Jahn–Teller distortion. Every L1 ligand links four Cu(II) ions to form a 3-D structure (Fig. 8). This structure contains rhombic channels along the [100] and [010] directions (∼6.5 × 6.5 Å C–C distance). Disordered acetates and free water molecules exist in the void space of this structure. The whole framework features a dia topology according to Topos analysis (Fig. S6†). To evaluate the porosity, potential solvent accessible void volume for compound 3 was calculated using PLATON software,14 showing 53.5% pore spaces.

Structure discussion

As we know, Cu(II) usually adopts 4–6 coordination atoms, forming square-planar, square pyramidal and square bipyramidal geometries. All three coordination modes have been observed in these title complexes. In 1, two Cu-centered square pyramids are condensed into one dimeric building unit, and simplified into a line and thus a uninodal sql plane net is formed. In 2, both the dimeric Cu2 cluster and square-planar CuO2N2 act as the inorganic building units, and the Cu-centered plane can be seen as a line, making the semi-rigid L1 a 3-connected node. At last, a 3,4L13 net is simplified. Complex 3, possessing a 3-D framework structure, features a mononuclear square bipyramid as the inorganic building unit. In 3, both the tetrahedral linker L1 and the inorganic unit can be simplified by the same 4-connected nodes, and dia topology is obtained.

Thermal stability of 1–3

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) studies were performed to characterize the thermal stability of complexes 1–3 (Fig. 9). A continued weight loss of 11.4% was observed from room temperature (RT) to 120 °C on the TGA curve of compound 1, this is due to the loss of lattice water molecules. Compound 1 is then stable up to 200 °C, after that it starts decomposing. The TGA curve of compound 2 shows a weight loss of about 8.9% from RT to 200 °C, which is ascribed to water molecules being absorbed by the structure. Then, the compound starts degrading. Compound 3 shows a continued weight loss of 14.7% before 100 °C and then a 5.4% weight loss between 100 and 200 °C is observed, indicating the removal of coordinated water molecules (calc. 5.46%). After that, the compound starts to decompose.

Conclusions

In summary, three new copper coordination polymers constructed from the assembly of semi-rigid tetrahedral pyridine ligands with different Cu-centered inorganic building units have been synthesized and characterized. These compounds represent different structural arrangements: 1 is a layered structure with a sql net which comprises dimeric copper clusters as the units and acetate groups as the co-ligands and counter ions; 2 is also a 2-D assembly formed by square planar CuO2N4 and square pyramidal CuO3N2 polyhedra as well as L1 and benzoate groups. A 3,4-connected net (3,4L13) is simplified for 2; a 3-D framework structure of 3 contains channels along the [100] and [010] directions with dia topology. These results demonstrate that novel coordination polymer architectures will be achievable via modulation of the ligand, co-ligands and additives, as well as the synthetic conditions.
