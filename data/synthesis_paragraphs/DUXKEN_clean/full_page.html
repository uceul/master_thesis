Four nodal self-catenated [{Ni8(Bpy)16}V24O68]·8.5(H2O), combining three dimensional metal–organic and inorganic frameworks†

First published on 1st February 2010

The chiral three dimensional crystal structure of [{Ni8(Bpy)16}V24O68]·8.5(H2O) is the first example of a hybrid vanadate combining a three dimensional metal–organic subnet with a three dimensional inorganic framework. The metal–organic framework consists of 3D + 2D polycatenation of a 3D “cds” like net and two 2D “sql” layers. The vanadium oxide subunit is a complex chain constructed from V5O15 cycles linked through a single tetrahedron. The vanadate chains are located in the channels of the metal–organic framework, and corner linked to the nickel metal centres giving rise to an unprecedented four nodal self-catenated framework. Moreover, as far as we as concerned, this is the first vanadate in which the V5O15 cycles has been isolated in the solid state. The crystallization water molecules are partially encapsulated between the organic ligands and the inorganic framework. The loss of crystallization water molecules gives rise to a contraction of the 0.6% of the unit cell volume. The IR spectrum shows the characteristic bands of the vanadium oxide subunit, and the bpy ligand. The BET measurement gives rise to a type III profile, (3.7 cm2 g−1), suggesting weak adsorbate-adsorbent interactions. The thermal evolution of the magnetic susceptibility is attributed to the zero-field splitting for eight non interacting Ni(II) cations.

Introduction

The construction of new inorganic–organic hybrid compounds through the rational combination of organic ligands, metal nodes and different anions, has become an area of great interest recently due to their rich chemistry and interesting physic and chemical properties.1 In the specific case of the vanadium oxides, recent interest has been focused on their potential use as secondary cathode materials for advanced lithium batteries and their importance in the industrial oxidative catalysis.2

From the point of view of hybrid vanadates with first row transition metal centres, the synergetic interaction between the metal–organic subnets and vanadium oxide subunits gives rise to several structural archetypes whose dimensionality can be partially controlled taking into account different aspects of the structural organization.3 The choose of bidentate ligands allows the possibility of obtaining extended metal–organic subnets, and high dimensionality open crystal structures.4,5

On the other hand, the modification of the hydrothermal synthesis conditions, such as time, temperature, stoichiometry, pH, concentration6 and filling factor, allows for the partial control of the oxoanion polymerization giving rise to different {VxOy} species stable in solution.7 Examples of these are the {V2O7} dimers, {V4O12} cyclic tetramers or {VO3} metavanadate chains. Recently, we reported the effect of the initial concentration and pH value in the hydrothermal synthesis of the Ni/Bpy/VxOy and Ni/Bpe/VxOy systems.8 In that respect, high concentrations and acidic conditions promote five coordinated vanadium, more polymerized {VxOy} subunits, and hence more condensed three dimensional inorganic frameworks stabilized with metal–ligand chains. In the other hand, the crystal structures obtained at neutral or slightly acidic conditions present four coordinated vanadium, more complex metal–organic moieties, and less condensed inorganic architectures.

In this work we present the first hybrid vanadate exhibiting both a three dimensional metal organic and inorganic framework. The topological analysis of the compound reveals self-catenated entanglement. In that respect, the vanadium oxide subunit promotes highly connected frameworks, and hence favoured the self-catenation phenomenon.9 Of the many reported types of entanglements10 the phenomenon of self-catenation has attracted much attention in recent times, because, until now, only a few have been observed in metal–organic frameworks. Moreover, the thermal properties, the thermal evolution of the cell parameters, BET measurement, IR and UV-vis spectra, and magnetic properties are studied.

Experimental

Synthesis and characterization

The title compound was synthesized from a mixture of NaVO3 (0.58 mmol), Ni(NO3)·6H2O (0.58 mmol), and 4,4′-bipyridyl (0.58 mmol) dissolved in 30 mL of distilled water. The pH of the solution was adjusted to 6.0 using HBO3 1 M solution, and the final mixture was transferred to a 40 mL Teflon-lined autoclave (Parr bomb), sealed and heated at 170 °C for five days. The percentage of the elements was measured by atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) and C, N, H elemental analysis. [{Ni8(4,4′-Bpy)16}V24O68]·8.5(H2O) Found: Ni 8.64, V 22.51, C 35.40, N 8.22, H 2.30 required: Ni 8.66, V 22.54, C 35.44, N 8.27, H 2.38. The density was measured by the flotation method in a mixture of bromoform/chloroform, being 1.67(2) g cm−3. The IR spectrum shows the bands corresponding to the vibrational modes of the bpy organic molecule and (VO4)3− vanadate anions, which appear in the 1600–990 and 930–530 cm−1, ranges, respectively. The characteristic bands of the bpy molecule δ(ring), δ(CN) and δ(CC) appear at 990, 1530, 1605 cm−1, respectively.11 The broad band near at 3430 cm−1 confirms the existence of crystallization water molecules into the crystal structure (ESI, Fig. S1).† The studied compound was characterized by X-ray powder diffraction data. The patterns were recorded in a BRUKER D8 ADVANCE Vario Diffractometer (Cu Kα = 1.54053 Å), between 5° and 70° in 2θ, counting 5 s per step (0.015°). The Rietveld analysis of the powder patterns indicates the absence of impurities in the different samples (ESI, Fig. S2).†

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction

A single-crystal diffraction study was carried out on a Xcalibur Nova automatic diffractometer (Cu Kα radiation). The crystal structure was solved by direct methods (SHELXS-97)12 in the triclinic space group P. The first steps of the refinement were performed by conjugate-gradient algorithm based on F2. Once the structural model was completed, the final stages of the refinement were carried out by full-matrix least square refinement until the convergence.13 During the structure refinement a static disorder of certain aromatic rings of the bpy molecules was detected. The disorder was solved assuming equivalence of the chemical equivalent bond lengths and angles, but not torsion angles, of the disordered rings. Equal anisotropic thermal displacements were imposed to the carbon and nitrogen atoms belonging to the same disordered ring. At this stage, various electron maxima were detected near the vanadium atoms belonging to the [100]a and [100]b chains described in the structural analysis. The bond distances between the most intensity residual density peaks and the oxygen atoms belonging to the vanadate chains were in good agreement with a V–O bond distance in four coordinated vanadium. The different VO4 tetrahedra of the vanadate chains were split into two positions with occupation factors of 0.512(2)/0.488(2) for the [100]a chains and 0.824(4)/0.176(4)) for the [100]b chains. Due to the low occupation factor of the second component of the disorder in the [100]b chains, the atoms belonging to the second component were refined with isotropic thermal displacement parameters. The SIMU command was used to make equivalent the thermal displacement of the disordered atoms of the [100]b chains. Equal anisotropic displacements were used for the vanadium atoms, terminal oxygen atoms and bi-coordinated oxygen atoms affected by the static disorder in the [100]a chains. In the final stages of the refinement the hydrogen atoms were fixed geometrically and allowed to ride on their parent carbons (C–H 0.95 Å), and 8.5 disordered water molecules were located. The Flack parameter value, 0.48, indicate the racemic twinning of the crystal. In order to corroborate the lack of symmetry of the crystal structure, missed symmetry analysis was carried out with the PLATON14 software for the nickel, vanadium and oxygen atoms separately without any revealing results. The large number of atomic positions makes the final analysis of the crystal structure difficult, in the respect that the checkcif program has problems making the geometric calculations of the large unit cell of the title compound. In order to carry out the calculations we used the PLATON program for a larger number of atoms. The results of the geometric calculations are included in the ESI†‡.

Physical measurements

Thermogravimetric analysis was carried out under air atmosphere in a DSC 2926 Simultaneous DSC-TGA TA Instrument. A crucible containing ca. 12.24 mg of the sample was heated at 5 °C min−1 in the temperature range 30–500 °C. Temperature dependent PXRD measurement in air atmosphere was performed using a PHILIPS X′PERT automatic diffractometer (Cu Kα radiation) equipped with a variable-temperature stage (Paar Physica TCU2000) with Pt sample holder. The powder patterns were recorded in 2θ steps of 0.04° in the range 5.02° < 2θ < 40.98° counting 2 s per step. Powder patterns were recorded each 15 °C from room temperature up to 595 °C. The Pt (111) maxima 2θ = 39° was used to calibrate the initial displacement of the sample. The difference (approx. +0.02% at 370 °C) between the expected and obtained thermal expansion of the platinum (ESI, Fig. S3)† indicates that the sample holder is approximately in the same position during all the temperature range. Magnetic measurements on powdered samples were performed in the temperature range 5–300 K, using a Quantum Design MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer. The magnetic field was 0.1 T, a value in the range of linear dependence of magnetization vs. magnetic field, even at 5.0 K. BET surface area measurement was performed on a 0.1605 g sample using nitrogen physisorption method on a Micrometrics ASAP 20010 instrument.

Results and discussion

The crystal structure of [{Ni8(4,4′-Bpy)16}V24O68]·8.5H2O is a 3D framework constructed from a polycatenated metal–organic subnet (Fig. 1), corner linked to the vanadium oxide subunit. The metal–organic subnet consists of polycatenation15 of a three dimensional metal–organic “cds” 4-c distorted net (point symbol {65·8}), and two crystallographic pseudo-equivalent “sql” like bi-dimensional metal–organic layers (point symbol {44·62}) (Fig. 1).
Fig. 1 Polycatenation of the 3D “cds” like net (blue) with two “sql” like metal organic layers (green and yellow) (nodes = NiN4O2 octahedra, linkers =bpy ligand). Arrows: the directions of the channels generated from the polycatenation of the metal–organic nets.

The vanadium oxide subunit is located in the [100] and [010] channels of the metal–organic framework, and directly attached to the NiN4O2 octahedra, giving rise to a three dimensional inorganic framework (Fig. 2 (a)).


Fig. 2 (a) Three dimensional crystal structure constructed from the polycatenated metal–organic framework (“cds” net = blue, and “sql” layers = green and yellow) and the vanadium oxide chains ([010] chains: light blue, chains [100]a = purple, chains [100]b = red). (b.1) Simplification of the vanadate chains (b.2) and (b.3) = connectivity between the [100] and [010] vanadate chains and the metal organic net. (c) Self-catenated (3-c)(5-c)(6-c)2 four nodal net generated from the connectivity between the metal–organic nets and the vanadium oxide subunit.

The crystallization water molecules are partially-encapsulated in the channels of the crystal structure (Fig. 2 (a)), between the vanadium oxide subunit and the metal–organic framework.

The vanadium is four coordinated. The vanadate is a complex chain constructed from five members V5O15 rings linked through a single tetrahedron (Fig. 2(b.1)). As far as we are concerned, this is the first compound in which the V5O15 cycles has been isolated in a solid state, despite this vanadium oxide subunit is known to exist in solution under different pH, concentration and temperature conditions.16

There are three crystallographically independent vanadate chains. The vanadate chains located in the [010] channels posses twelve crystallographic independent VO4 tetrahedra, that generates two independent V5O15 cycles in the same chain, one slightly rotated with respect to the other (Fig. 2(b.1)). There are two crystallographic pseudo-equivalent vanadate chains located in the [100] channels of the metal organic framework, [100]a and [100]b chains, as it shown in Fig. 3 (a) and (b). Both of them are constructed from one V5O15 cycle linked through a single tetrahedron. The [100]a and [100]b chains are disordered in two positions with occupation factors of 0.512(2)/0.488(2) and 0.824(4)/0.176(4), respectively (Fig. 3).


Fig. 3 (a) [100]a chains disordered in two positions with occupation factors of 0.488(2) (purple) and 0.522(2) (pink). (b) [100]b chains disordered in two positions with occupation factors of 0.824(4) (red) and 0.176(4) (orange).

The bond distances and angles related to the nickel(II) cations are in good agreement with a slightly distorted NiN4O2 octahedral environment, ranging from 1.995(6) to 2.147(6) Å. The V–O distances are plotted in Fig. 4 (a) and ESI, Fig. S4,† and compared with the those retrieved and analyzed from the CSD17 (Cambridge Structural Database) (Fig. 4 (b)).


Fig. 4 (a) V–O bond distances histogram of the title compound. (b) V–O bond distances histogram of four coordinated vanadium (data retrieved from the CSD database).

The VO4 tetrahedra show a bimodal distribution of the V–O bond distances. Those are strongly depend on the connectivity of the oxygen atoms:

(i) The VO bond distances, with terminal oxygen atoms, range from 1.50 to 1.80 Å (CSD: 1.56–1.72 Å).

(ii) The V–O bonds with the oxygen atoms corner shared to a nickel(II) cations have values from 1.56 to 1.76 Å (CSD data for oxygen atoms shared to Ni(II), Co(II), Zn(II), Cu(II) or Mn(II) range from 1.60 to 1.76 Å). These bonds are slightly longer than the VO ones.

(iii) The V–O bonds with the oxygen atom shared by two VO4 tetrahedra possess values between 1.70 to 1.90 Å (CDS: 1.72–1.78 Å). ESI Fig. S4† shows the plot of Va–O vs. Vb–O bond distances, for the title compound and those ones retrieved from the CSD database. The bond distances related to the disordered [100] vanadate chains show a broader range of values, in comparison with the CSD ones, because of the uncertainties in determining the position of the disordered atoms.

Despite the reasons for the disorder of the [100] vanadate chains are unclear, the comparison between the V–O distances retrieved from the CSD database, and those ones for the [100] chains reveals a good agreement between both sets of data. Moreover, the three kind of bonds are present, with approximately the same range of distances values.

The topological analysis of the crystal structure reveals a self-catenated (3-c)(5-c)(6-c)2 four nodal net with a Schläfli symbol {44·56·63·72}{510}{513·6·8}{53} (Fig. 2 (c)).18 The V5O15 cycles and the VO4 inter cycle tetrahedra have been simplified as two different nodes (Fig. 2(b.1)). The V5O15 cycles are connected to two VO4 tetrahedra and three nickel(II) cations of the metal–organic subnet, defining a five connected node (Fig. 2(b.2) and (b.3)). The VO4 intercycle tetrahedra are three connected, and share with two V5O5 cycles and one nickel(II) ions of the metal–organic framework. This way, each vanadate chain is connected to the “cds” three 3D metal organic net, and to the two crystallographically independent “sql” like metal–organic layers of the metal–organic framework. The six connected nodes are related to the NiN4O2 octahedra of the metal–organic subnet, linked to four nickel nodes of the metal–organic framework, and two vanadium nodes of the vanadate chains.

The crystal structure is an amazing example of the synergistic interaction between the vanadate chains and the metal–organic framework. The connectivity between both structural subunits gives rise to a distortion of the metal–organic framework, and to a slight rotation in the V5O15 cycles of the vanadate chains. Distortion of the metal–organic nets is shown in ESI Fig. S5† and the angles values between the nodes are depicted in ESI Table S2.† The metal organic net is a pseudo-c net, but the displacement of the NiN4O2 octahedra from the ideal position brakes the c net.

The connectivity between the vanadium oxide subunit and the metal–organic nets (Fig. 2(b.2) and (b.3)) imprints the chirality in the final framework, and gives rise to a crystal structure with two pseudo-equivalent subunits along the [010] direction.

The construction of the VDP (Voronoi-Diritchlet polyhedra)19 for the crystallization water molecules reveals that these ones are partially-confined between the organic molecules and the inorganic framework. There are three different positions in which the crystallization water molecules are located, with volumes of 134, 63 and 155 Å3 (ESI, Fig. S7).† The geometry of the channels, in which the crystallization water molecules are located, has been studied with the scan method, previously reported by Blatov et al.20 (ESI, Fig. S8).† The values of the radius along the channel (Rsd) and the channel section (S) are summarized in the ESI, Table S2.† The minimum and maximum radii values of the channels are 1.52 and 1.17 Å, respectively. The irregular geometry of the channels suggests that the crystallization water molecules are partially-encapsulated between the organic ligands and the inorganic framework.

Table 1 summarizes the most common architectures of hybrid vanadates with dipodal ligands, pointing the dimensionality of the metal–organic and inorganic framework and that of the crystal structure. The use of long ligands allows the interpenetration of the metal organic nets in several compounds, but prevents the existence of both three dimensional inorganic and metal–organic frameworks in the same crystal structure.21–23 To our knowledge, the title compound is the first hybrid vanadate combining both 3D inorganic and metal–organic frameworks.

In that respect, the hybrid vanadates with dipodal ligands show two mayor structural archetypes: (i) inorganic layers pillared by the organic ligand and, (ii) three dimensional inorganic frameworks stabilized by the organic molecules. In both of them, the ligands are directly attached to the metal centres, acting as pillars that are an integral part of the framework. Examples of hybrid vanadates with inorganic layers are {Ag(Bpy)}4(V4O12), {Co(Bpy)}(VO3)2, or {Ni(Bpe)}(VO3)2. This kind of structural archetype can be classified in function of the metal–organic subnets. This way, the {Ag(Bpy)}4(V4O12) and {Co(Bpy)}(VO3)2 compounds show a parallel disposition of the organic ligand between the inorganic layers, and hence the metal–organic subnets are parallel metal–ligand chains. On the other hand, the compound {Ni(Bpe)}(VO3)2 exhibits a crossed disposition of the organic ligand between the inorganic layers, and hence, the metal–organic subnets are more complex interpenetrated rhombic-like metal–organic layers. The connectivity of the metal organic part of the crystal structure through the vanadium oxide subunit gives rise to self-catenated framework. The hybrid vanadates exhibiting three dimensional inorganic framework (e.g. [{Ni2(H2O)2(Bpy)3}(V4O12)]·2.5H2O, {Ni(bpp)2}2(V4O12), [{Co3(H2O)4(bpp)4}(V6O18)]·2H2O) show a crossed disposition of the organic ligands in the crystal structure. This fact gives rise to interpenetrated and polycatenated metal organic subnets.

The BET measurement shows that the compound has a small surface area (3.7 m2 g−1) (Fig. 5). The sorption and desorption isotherms have a type III profile, characteristic of weak adsorbate–adsorbent interactions, and most commonly associated with both non-microporous and microporous adsorbents. The radius value of the channel, obtained from BET measurements, is 1.93 Å, which is slightly greater than the calculated from the scan method. This results could possibly be attributed to the method employed for surface area measurement. There are difficulties associated with performing BET nitrogen surface area measurements of organic–inorganic compounds with large organic surface groups.24,25


Fig. 5 N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K (circles = sorption, squares = desorption).

The thermal behaviour of the title compound was studied by thermogravimetry (ESI, Fig S8)† and thermodiffractometry (Fig. 6). The thermogravimetric curve exhibits two steps of weight loss. The first one, with a value of 3.0%, between room temperature and 80 °C, corresponds to the loss of crystallization water (calcd 2.8%); and the second one (380–450 °C), with a value of 44.2%, is related to the exothermic loss of the bpy ligands (calcd 46.3%). The thermal behaviour was also studied by time resolved X-ray thermodiffractometry in an air atmosphere. The thermodiffraction analysis reveals that the crystal structure is stable up to 370 °C, in good agreement with the thermogravimetric analysis. However, the powder patterns show that the crystallinity decreases dramatically by 355 °C (Fig. 6). At higher temperatures, several inorganic vanadates and vanadium oxides are observed: NiV3O8 + NiV2O6 (370–445 °C) → NiV2O6 + V2O5 (445–480 °C).(Fig. 6 (a)). The visual analysis of some reflections, related to the initial compound, shows the existence of significant displacements with temperature (Fig. 6(b)). Qualitatively, two trends are observed during the heating process, the first one between 25 and 100 °C, and the second one in the temperature range 100–370 °C.


Fig. 6 (a) Thermodiffractometry of the title compound. (b) Visual analysis of the thermal displacement of some reflection for the title compound.

Rietveld refinement of the crystal parameters with respect to the temperature was also carried out.26 The introduction of a fixed structural model during the cyclic refinement of the crystal parameters allows a qualitative description of the unit cell change, despite some problems in the fit of some reflections. The relative thermal expansion has been calculated using the following equation: RE(%) = [(Pt(i)/Pt(0))−1] × 100, where, Pt(i) and Pt(0) are the parameter values at t = i and t0 = initial temperature, respectively.

The relative expansion of the cell volume (Fig. 7) suggests three tendencies during the heating process: (i) initial contraction of approximately the 0.6% of the cell volume due to the loss of crystallization water molecules, (ii) the successive crystal expansion up to 355 °C and (iii) at higher temperatures the crystal structure collapses due to the calcination of the organic ligand.


Fig. 7 Relative thermal expansion of the cell volume.

The thermal evolution of the crystal parameters and the cell angles indicate the existence of a distortion and compression of the metal–organic framework due to the removal of the crystallization water molecules (see ESI, Fig. S9).† In the same way, the framework recovers its initial volume cell approximately at 300 °C, due to the thermal expansion.

In the diffuse reflectance spectrum (ESI, Fig. S10),† two absorption bands of strong intensity corresponding to the allowed transitions: 3A2g(3F) → 3T2g(3F), 3T1g(3F), at 9800, and 16000 cm−1 are observed. At higher frequencies a broad band due to the charge transfer is detected. From the spin transitions corresponding to the d8-high spin Ni(II) cation in octahedral geometry, the Dq and Racah B parameters have been calculated, by using the energy expressions given in the Tanabe–Sugano diagrams.27 The values are Dq = 980 and B = 875 cm−1. The B-parameter is, approximately, 85% of that of the free Ni(II) cation (1030 cm−1), which indicates an appreciable covalent character in the chemical bonds inside the NiN4O2 octahedron.

Magnetic measurements for the title compound were performed in a powdered sample from room temperature to 5.0 K, at a magnetic field of 0.1 T. The thermal evolution of the molar magnetic susceptibility, χm, and χmT vs. T is shown in Fig. 8. The χmT value at 300 K is (9.34 cm3 K mol−1) near the expected (9.68 cm3 K mol−1) for eight non-interacting Ni(II) (S = 1, g = 2.2) ions. The χm curve shows a continuous increase of its value between 300 K (0.03559 cm3 mol−1) to 5 K (1.459 cm3 mol−1). Due to the large distance between Ni(II) ions (approx. 8.3 Å through the vanadate chains and 11.2 Å through the bpy ligand) significant exchange interactions between the metal centres are not expected. Therefore, the decrease of the χmT value with decreasing temperature is attributed to the zero-field splitting, typical of an axially distorted d8 high spin isolated Ni(II) system.28


Fig. 8 Plot of χm and χmTvs. temperature. The solid lines represent the best fit assuming a zero-field splitting and temperature independent magnetism characteristic of Ni(II) d8 ion.

The best fitting parameters obtained are D/hc=−10.3(1) cm−1 and g = 2.155(9). The D parameter value is consistent with the observed distorted octahedral coordination environment of NiN4O2 octahedra, and still well within the generally observed rather broad range from −22 to 12 cm−1.29

Conclusions

The title compound is the first example of a hybrid vanadate combining both three dimensional metal–organic and inorganic frameworks. The connectivity between the vanadium oxide subunit, and the metal organic subnet distorts both structural subunits and imprints the chirality in the crystal structure. The topological analysis reveals a four nodal self-catenated net. Moreover, as far as we are concerned, this is the first compound in which the V5O15 cycles appear in the solid state. The highly connected vanadium oxide subunit, and the complexity of the metal organic subnet promote the existence of self-catenation. The crystal structure is stable up to 355 °C, with a structural contraction of a 0.6% due to the removal of crystallization water molecules. The channel radius obtained from both the scan method and BET measurement has similar values. The magnetic measurements indicate that there is no significant magnetic exchange interactions between the Ni(II) centre through the vanadium oxide or the organic ligand.
