Abstract

A multifunctional transition metal CdII coordination polymer based on an amide‐inserted flexible multicarboxylate ligand, bis(3,5‐dicarboxyphenyl)terephthalamide (H4L), [CdL]·[+H2N(CH3)2] (DMF)(H2O)3 (MOF1) was synthesized by the solvothermal method. Its structure was determined by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis and it was characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis. MOF1 shows selective sensitivity to detecting aniline pollutants in both aqueous media and as vapors due to its strong fluorescence emission and microporous structure. In addition, the visible‐light‐driven photocatalytic degradation of Rhodamine B (RhB) by MOF1 and polyaniline (PANI) composite material (named PANI/MOF1), which was prepared by loading PANI onto the surface of MOF1, was also studied. The results obtained illustrate that PANI/MOF1 exhibits improved photocatalytic activity over MOF1, which could be used to effectively treat wastewater containing organic dyes in the future.

Abstract

A multifunctional metal–organic framework for determination of toxic aromatic amines has been constructed and it shows photocatalytic degradation of organic dyes due to the combination of luminescence and accessible porosity within the MOF.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), which are self‐assembled from organic linkers and inorganic connectors of metal ions and/or clusters, are a new class of crystalline porous materials that has shown potential applications in detecting organic pollutants because of their fluorescence, tunable porosity and a high surface area.1 Recently, increased efforts have been focused on the synthesis of luminescent porous metal‐organic frameworks for detecting nitro‐aromatic explosives due to the importance of nitro compounds in homeland security and environmental safety.2–11 Most of the reported MOF‐based sensing materials are constructed with aromatic ligands because the π electron systems of the ligands endow the MOFs with photoluminescence properties and provide potential supramolecular recognition sites for hydrogen bonds, π–π stacking and electrostatic interactions between their skeleton and the analytes.12,13 Such interactions lead to effective charge transfer or energy transfer processes between the analytes and the MOFs. Correspondingly, this triggers a fluorescence response upon interaction, such as a variation in emission intensity or wavelength.14 Similar to nitro‐aromatic compounds, aniline and its derivatives, known as notorious environmental pollutants, are widely used in manufacturing dyes, rubber and agrochemicals. The exposure to them even at very low concentrations is suspected to result in carcinogenicity by ingestion, inhalation and skin contact. Moreover, conventional detection methods require large and expensive instruments as well as complicated experimental procedures.15 The development of a sensitive analytical method for rapid and efficient detection of anilines is therefore not only an exigent requirement in the field of public security, but also critical for environmental protection. Up to now, a few examples in the literature demonstrate this potential application.15–20

On the other hand, organic dyes are one of the major pollutants in water because of their long lifetimes and wide distribution. Recently, numerous methods have been employed for organic dye wastewater treatment.21Among these methods, photocatalytic degradation is an efficient and economical way to decompose organic dyes into less dangerous products.22 In this respect, MOFs have attracted much attention for their applications as a new kind of promising photocatalyst in purifying water by thoroughly decomposing organic dyes.23–28 Thus, the appropriate choice of chemically modified organic ligands and functional metal centers to construct multifunctional porous MOFs is highly necessary for the determination of toxic aromatic amines and degradation of organic dyes under visible‐light irradiation. These unique properties of MOFs can be attributed to their tunable pore structures and functionalized pore surfaces29 or the building blocks or molecular guests as well.

Given the above concerns, we prepared a semi‐rigid ligand with four carboxylate groups as coordination sites and two amide groups as functional sites, namely bis(3,5‐dicarboxypheny1)terephthalamide (H4L), and assembled a multifunctional MOF with a d10 transition metal by the solvothermal method: [CdL]·[+H2N (CH3)2](DMF)(H2O)3 (MOF1). As we expected, the introduction of aromatic base ligands with amide groups into MOF1 provided a promising way to construct new porous materials for selectively sensing aromatic amines as well as photocatalytic degradation of organic dyes.

Results and Discussion

Structure Description of MOF1

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis shows that the asymmetric unit of MOF1 consists of one independent cadmium ion. The coordination environment is shown in Figure 1. The cadmium ion is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from four carboxylate groups of four different L ligands and displays a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd–O distances are in the range of 2.295–2.732 Å. In the compound, all the four carboxylate groups of the L ligand are deprotonated. Each L ligand adopts bi‐monodentate and bi‐didentate chelating mode to link four different cadmium ions. The dihedral angles between the central terephthalic and two terminal isophthalic moieties are 7.0° and 83.3°, respectively, and the dihedral angle between two isophthalic moieties is 83.8°. Thus, every L ligand bridges four different cadmium ions and each cadmium ion connects four individual L ligands to generate a 3D metal–organic framework. The amide groups elongate the arms of the L ligands, which could potentially generate a large inner cavity in the compound. Interestingly, two such identical nets interpenetrate to give rise to the overall structure of MOF1. Despite the interpenetration minimizing the void spaces of the framework, MOF1 still has relatively large open 2D channels along the a and b axes (Figure 2). A PLATON calculation suggests that the channels' volumes are 47.2 % of the total crystal volume (863.1 Å3 out of the 1828.0 Å3 unit cell volume). The channels are occupied by guest molecules of one DMF, two dimethylamine cations and three water molecules. They take part in forming rich hydrogen bonds, which further stabilize the structure of MOF1.

In order to confirm whether the crystal structure of MOF1 truly represent the structure of the obtained bulky sample, a PXRD pattern was recorded, as shown in Figure S1. The PXRD pattern of the synthesized sample of MOF1 is in good agreement with its corresponding simulated pattern, which indicates the phase purity of the synthesized sample.

Detection of Aromatic Amines

The room temperature solid‐state fluorescence spectrum of MOF1 was studied. The strongest emission peak is at 450 nm when excitation at 330 nm takes place and this is similar to that of the free ligand L (λem = 440 nm) (Figure S2). The similarity in emission between ligand L and the compound suggests that the emission of MOF1 could be attributed to ligand centered electronic transitions perturbed by the coordinated metal ions.30 Moreover, the photoluminescence spectrum of a MOF1 suspension in acetonitrile exhibits the strongest emission. In addition, the emission wavelength is consistent with that of a solid state sample. The strong emissions of MOF1 in the solid state and in acetonitrile suspension make it ideal to be employed as a fluorescence sensor. Based on the results of photoluminescence studies and motivated by pioneering work on the use of microporous MOFs for detecting aromatic amines, we carried out fluorescence detection experiments of MOF1 in an acetonitrile suspension with a gradual increase in the aniline concentration. The addition of aromatic amines to the solution, such as aniline, p‐toluidine, p‐nitroaniline, o‐phenylenediamine and diphenylamine, promoted the quenching or enhancement in the fluorescence intensities of MOF1. The percentage of fluorescence quenching or enhancement obtained for different analytes are given in Figure 3. For the selected analytes, with the exception of diphenylamine and p‐toluidine, other anilines quenched the fluorescence of MOF1 to various extents. Among them, p‐nitroaniline gives the best quenching performance under the same experimental conditions. The fluorescence quenching efficiency increased drastically in the presence of p‐nitroaniline even in the low concentration range and reached over 80.7 % with the addition of only 2 μmol of p‐nitroaniline (Figure 4, a). For others, the quenching efficiency is obviously lower and reached only about 10 % (Figure S3, S4). However, for diphenylamine and p‐toluidine, an enhancement occurs in the fluorescence intensity of the MOF1 suspension. The enhancement percentage of the fluorescence efficiency was up to 63.7 % after the addition of 1 μmol diphenylamine (Figure 4, b). Interestingly, MOF1 also shows an emission frequency blue‐shift in the case of diphenylamine. As a result, the fluorescence intensity enhancement coupled with a blue‐shift will give MOF1 an additional variability in the selective identification of different analytes from the same group with similar electron‐donating abilities. For p‐toluidine, the fluorescence efficiency is enhanced by 8.0 % (Figure S5). These results suggest that MOF1 is selectively sensitive to p‐nitroaniline or diphenylamine. Similar to the literature reported complex [Tb(1,3,5‐btc)(H2O)6]n (btc = benzene‐1,3,5‐tricarboxylate),15 MOF1 was seldom reported for fluorescence sensing of anilines with a low limit of detection. For comparison, triethylamine was selected as the probe to investigate the influence of aliphatic amines on the fluorescence of MOF1. No obvious fluorescence intensity change occurred by adding triethylamine into the acetonitrile suspension of MOF1 (Figure 5).

In the present work, the selective luminescence sensing of aromatic amines may be attributed to the effective interactions between the framework of MOF1 and the analytes. Firstly, the analytes are adsorbed on the pores' surfaces of MOF1 in its open channels. Due to the presence of both the conjugated aromatic system and the amide group in ligand L, the aromatic amines interact much more strongly with the pore surfaces of MOF1 through strong supramolecular interactions, such as aromatic π–π stacking and hydrogen bonds, which lead to efficient charge transfer or energy transfer processes between the analytes and MOF1. In general, the electron transfer from the conduction band (CB) of the MOFs in the excited state to the low‐lying lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the analytes will result in a fluorescence quenching effect.31,32 The fact that p‐nitroaniline exhibits the strongest quenching effect may be attributed to the strongly electron‐withdrawing NO2 group. In contrast, if the excited electrons are transferred from a high‐lying π* type LUMO orbital to the conduction band of MOF1, fluorescence enhancement will result. Thus, for diphenylamine, the observed enhancement in emission intensity and the blue‐shift in the emission wavelength demonstrates that the electrons possibly transferred from the LUMO of diphenylamine to the conduction band of MOF1 along with the formation of a strong exciplex between the diphenylamine and MOF1.14,33 For aliphatic amines, the much lower quenching efficiency is due to the weak interactions between the aliphatic amines and MOF1. The reduction potential of MOF1 was also close to that of triethylamine, lacking the driving force for any photoinduced electron transfer.34 Hence, the luminescence properties of the MOF1 material were unaffected by triethylamine. All the experimental results implied that the selectivity of MOF1 is related to the structures and electronic properties of both MOF1 and the analytes as well as the nature of their interactions.

Based on the results of fluorescence detection studies in acetonitrile, we also monitored the time‐dependent fluorescence response of a MOF1‐based film upon exposure to the saturated vapor of aniline at 25 °C. As we expected, the fluorescence intensity of the MOF1‐based film was effectively quenched with the prolonged exposure time (Figure 6). The significantly selective luminescence quenching/enhancement effect for aromatic amines makes MOF1 a potential candidate for sensing aromatic amines both in solution and as vapors.

Photocatalytic Property Study

The photocatalytic activities of MOF1 and PANI/MOF1 composite material were evaluated by the degradation of RhB in aqueous solution. When irradiated with visible‐light, MOF1 exhibited photocatalytic activity and decomposed 24 % of RhB within 4 h (Figure S6). Along with the prolonged reaction time, the absorption peaks of RhB had obviously decreased. In 8 h, 47.9 % of RhB was degraded (Figure S7a). However, under dark conditions in the presence of MOF1, under natural light, or only with illumination in the absence of MOF1, about 5.7 %, 5.3 % and 5.7 % of RhB was degraded, respectively (Figure S8 – S10). Therefore, the presences of both illumination and MOF1 with photocatalytic activity are necessary for the efficient degradation of RhB. However, for MOF1, its narrow photoresponse region and low quantum yield limit the applications as photocatalysts to some extent.35

Compared with MOFs, modified MOF composite materials with polyaniline (PANI) would greatly enhance the photocatalytic performance of MOFs due to the strong photoresponse of PANI in the visible‐light region as well as high quantum yields.36 To improve the photocatalytic activity of MOF1, polyaniline modified MOF1 (PANI/MOF1) was prepared by loading PANI onto the surface of MOF1. The experimental results revealed that PANI modification greatly enhanced the photocatalytic properties of MOF1 (Figure S7b). The photocatalytic efficiency of PANI/MOF1 was about 81.8 % after 8 h of visible‐light irradiation. The degradation rate was considerably elevated. However, the photocatalytic efficiency of RhB was as low as 8.1 % after 8 h of visible‐light irradiation in the presence of PANI alone (Figure S11). The results disclosed that the coupling of PANI and MOF1 gave enhanced photocatalytic activity under the same conditions. Figure 7 presents the photocatalytic degradation profiles of RhB under different conditions. Obviously, the photocatalytic activity of PANI/MOF1 was close to the results in the literature reports,36 and it can decompose 77.5 % RhB in 150 min in visible‐light.

In addition, the photostability of PANI/MOF1 was monitored by using PXRD patterns during the course of the photocatalytic reactions. The PXRD patterns of PANI/MOF1 had no notable differences before and after the photocatalytic cycles under visible‐light irradiation (Figure S12). Also, the recycled PANI/MOF1 exhibits similar photocatalytic performance as the original PANI/MOF1 (Figure 8). All the results illustrate that the structure of PANI/MOF1 is retained during the photocatalytic process.

In order to confirm the interactions between MOF1 and PANI, the FTIR spectra of MOF1, PANI and PANI/MOF1 were studied (see Figure S13). Compared with the characteristic peaks of pure MOF1, most absorption peaks of MOF1 were observed in the spectra of the PANI/MOF1 composite material but slightly shifted. While the peak shift indicated effective interaction between the PANI and MOF1, the occurrence of IR peaks of MOF1 in the composite material revealed MOF1 maintains its structural integrity. The composition of PANI and MOF1 was also confirmed by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) observations (Figure S14). The powdery MOF1 exhibited a coarse surface, while the surface of PANI/MOF1 composite material was smoothened. For PANI/MOF1, the enhancement of the photocatalytic activity can be mainly attributed to the interaction and synergistic effect between PANI and MOF1. PANI possesses a good light response in the visible‐light region as well as efficient photogenerated hole transportation ability, and can improve the photocatalytic activity of MOF1 in the visible‐light region efficiently. Hence, it is a feasible approach to modify MOF photocatalysts by using PANI and this will enable us to design high‐activity, high‐stability and visible‐light driven photocatalysts in the future.

Conclusions

In summary, a luminescent microporous metal–organic framework of MOF1 has been successfully assembled under solvothermal conditions by using Cd2+ cations as the inorganic nodes and an amide‐inserted flexible multicarboxylate ligand as the organic linker. The connections between Cd2+ cations and the ligands generated a network with large cavities. Interpenetration of two identical networks produces the porous structure of MOF1. The prepared MOF1 exhibits various light emissions when exposed to aromatic amines both in aqueous solution and as gases. The electronic structures of the analytes induce different fluorescent behavior from MOF1. While the electron‐rich analytes arouse enhanced fluorescence, the electron‐deficient analytes effect a quenched fluorescence. This phenomenon renders MOF1 selectively sensitive to aromatic rather than aliphatic amines. The emission wavelength shift induced by exposing MOF1 to diphenylamine further enhances the sensing selectivity. Additionally, MOF1 exhibited photocatalytic activity for organic dyes. In particular, PANI modification greatly improves the photocatalytic performance of MOF1. PANI/MOF1 represents a rare example of a MOF composite materials with high photocatalytic activity for dye degradation under visible‐light and good stability toward photocatalysis.35,36 Moreover, the multifunctional properties of MOF1 contributed towards a new strategy for developing new materials for detecting, monitoring and eliminating pollutants.

Experimental Section

General Procedures: All the reagents were obtained from commercial sources and used as received. Bis(3,5‐dicarboxyphenyl)terephthalamide (H4L) was prepared according to the literature.37 IR spectra were recorded on a BIO‐RAD FTS3000 system by using KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm–1. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were measured on a Perkin–Elmer auto‐analyzer. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a D/MAX‐2500 automated diffractometer. The simulated PXRD pattern was derived from the single crystal data through the diffraction‐crystal module of the Mercury program version 3.0. The solid state photoluminescence spectra were measured on a JASCO FP‐6500 Spectrofluorometer. The photoluminescence spectra of samples in acetonitrile were measured on an F‐380 Spectrophotometer. The UV/Vis adsorption spectra were recorded by using a Helios‐γ UV/Vis spectrometer.

[CdL]·[+H2N(CH3)2](DMF)(H2O)3 (MOF1): A mixture of H4L (24.6 mg, 0.05 mmol), CdCl2·2.5H2O (22.8 mg, 0.13 mmol), DMF (8 mL), H2O (3 mL) and 4 drops of HNO3 were homogeneously mixed whilst being stirred. The mixtures was then sealed in a 25 mL stainless steel vessel and statically heated at 85 °C for 3 d under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Colorless block crystals of MOF1 were obtained by filtration, yield 76.2 % based on Cd. C31H41CdN5O14 (820.09): calcd. C 45.32, H 4.99, N 8.53; found C 44.61, H 5.22, N 8.26. IR (KBr pellet): = 3425 (br), 1623 (vs), 1565 (vs), 1423 (w), 1376 (vs), 1163 (s), 1016 (m), 868 (vs), 780 (m), 730 (m) cm–1.

Fluorescence Titrations in Dispersed Media: The fluorescence of MOF1 was measured by dispersing ground up sample (8 mg) in acetonitrile (8 mL), which was treated by ultrasonication for 2 h. All titrations were carried out by gradually adding analytes in an incremental fashion. Their corresponding fluorescence emission spectra were recorded at 298 K. Each titration was repeated several times to get concordant values. For all measurements, the suspension of MOF1 was excited at λex = 330 nm and the corresponding emission wavelengths were monitored from 340 to 650 nm. The fluorescence efficiency was calculated by using the formula [(F0 – F)/F0] × 100 % (F0 is the initial fluorescence intensity).

Photocatalytic Experiments: Photocatalytic experiments on aqueous solutions were performed in a 100 mL test tube. A 500‐W high‐pressure mercury lamp was used as the visible light source. An aqueous solution of RhB (80 mL) with a concentration of 10–5 mol L−1 was mixed with the catalyst (20 mg) and illuminated. Before turning on the lamp, the suspension containing RhB and the photocatalyst was magnetically stirred in the dark for 1 h until an adsorption‐desorption equilibrium was established. At given irradiation time intervals, a series of aqueous solutions of a certain volume were collected, separated by using a centrifuge to remove suspended catalyst particles and then subjected to UV/Vis spectroscopic measurements.

Preparation of PANI/MOF1 Composite Material: Aniline (30 mg) was dissolved in HCl aqueous solution (15 mL, 1 mol L−1) at room temperature. MOF1 (0.3 g) was placed in the above mixture and dispersed with ultrasonication for 20 min. A solution of (NH4)2S2O8 (0.114 g) in HCl aqueous solution (5 mL, 1 mol L−1) was added slowly to the above mixture at room temperature as an oxidant. After the mixture was stirred for 30 min, the PANI/MOF1 composite material was separated and rinsed with water and alcohol, and finally dried at 60 °C for 24 h in an oven.

X‐ray Crystallography: Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction measurements for MOF1 were carried out on a computer‐controlled Bruker SMART 1000 CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation with a wavelength of 0.71073 Å by using the ω scan technique. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares on F2 using the SHELXS 97 and SHELXL 97 programs.38,39 Semiempirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program.40 All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and treated by a mixture of independent or constrained refinements. The crystallographic data for MOF1 are listed in Table S1, and selected bond lengths [Å] and angles [°] are listed in Table S2.

CCDC‐1010783 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.
