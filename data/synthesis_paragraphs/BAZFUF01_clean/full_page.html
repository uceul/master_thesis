Abstract

A combination of topological rules and quantum chemical calculations has facilitated the development of a rational metal–organic framework (MOF) synthetic strategy using the tritopic benzene‐1,3,5‐tribenzoate (btb) linker and a neutral cross‐linker 4,4′‐bipyridine (bipy). A series of new compounds, namely [M2(bipy)]3(btb)4 (DUT‐23(M), M=Zn, Co, Cu, Ni), [Cu2(bisqui)0.5]3(btb)4 (DUT‐24, bisqui=diethyl (R,S)‐4,4′‐biquinoline‐3,3′‐dicarboxylate), [Cu2(py)1.5(H2O)0.5]3(btb)4 (DUT‐33, py=pyridine), and [Cu2(H2O)2]3(btb)4 (DUT‐34), with high specific surface areas and pore volumes (up to 2.03 m3 g−1 for DUT‐23(Co)) were synthesized. For DUT‐23(Co), excess storage capacities were determined for methane (268 mg g−1 at 100 bar and 298 K), hydrogen (74 mg g−1 at 40 bar and 77 K), and n‐butane (99 mg g−1at 293 K). DUT‐34 is a non‐cross‐linked version of DUT‐23 (non‐interpenetrated pendant to MOF‐14) that possesses open metal sites and can therefore be used as a catalyst. The accessibility of the pores in DUT‐34 to potential substrate molecules was proven by liquid phase adsorption. By exchanging the N,N donor 4,4′‐bipyridine with a substituted racemic biquinoline, DUT‐24 was obtained. This opens a route to the synthesis of a chiral compound, which could be interesting for enantioselective separation.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) represent a class of crystalline hybrid materials that are usually synthesized either from metals or metal clusters combined with organic multifunctional ligands. From the early days of MOF chemistry (or reticular chemistry) it was recognized that the shapes of building blocks determine to a large extent the resulting crystalline architectures,1 and several default, highly symmetrical nets have been proposed as most likely to be observed in experiments.2 Further development of reticular chemistry has shown, however, two main problems in the design and synthesis of MOFs: Interpenetration and polymorphism.3 Interpenetration is a natural way to avoid the formation of very open structures, whereas polymorphism originates from the fact that the shapes of building blocks do not fix the framework topology uniquely. Although many building blocks (differing in shape, composition, and functionality) have been discovered and used for MOF synthesis,4 the basic rules that govern the process of MOF formation are not yet understood in sufficient detail to routinely achieve target structures through a priori choice of building blocks. In this paper we propose the combined use of topological rules and quantum chemical calculations to suggest the appropriate building blocks for the construction of a MOF with a predefined topology.

Recently, several highly porous MOFs (UMCM‐1, UMCM‐2, and DUT‐6)5 with framework composition [Zn4O(L)]3(btb)4 (btb=benzene‐1,3,5‐tribenzoate, L=dianion of some dicarboxylic acid) were reported. They consist of octahedral Zn4O clusters connected to four tritopic btb linkers as well as to two neighboring clusters through a linear ligand L. Each btb ligand is in turn bound to three clusters. The underlying topologies of all three MOFs are those of (3,4)‐coordinated A3X4 nets containing two additional links between neighboring 4‐connected nodes (4+2 node). In spite of this similarity, UMCM‐1, UMCM‐2, and DUT‐6 are all topologically different and the differences arise as a result of the nature of an additional linear linker as well as the geometry of the Zn4O cluster itself. Indeed, because the cluster is connected to only four btb ligands, two additional linkers can be arranged in cis (UMCM‐1),5b trans (DUT‐6),5a or (if two or more symmetry‐independent clusters are present) in both cis and trans (UMCM‐2 and MOF‐210)5c, 6 orientations relative to each other. As a consequence, stereochemical control of the resulting architectures is difficult to achieve. Moreover, the M4O cluster is not so common in coordination chemistry (for zinc it has been known for a long time but only recently discovered for beryllium and cobalt).7 To enable the possibility of metal variety or partial mixing of different metals in the same cluster, a different secondary building unit (SBU) is needed. In fact, the known square‐like paddle‐wheel clusters of metals such as zinc, copper, nickel, cobalt, cadmium, and manganese could be used. The paddle‐wheel clusters could also be considered as 4+2 nodes in which two axial sites of the cluster are available for further ligation, like in pillared [M2(bdc)2(L′)]‐type compounds (bdc=1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate, L′=diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane, 4,4′‐bipyridine; see Figure 2a).8 Since only the trans orientation of additional ligands is possible, paddle‐wheels allow for more precise stereochemical control of the resulting crystalline architecture. Furthermore, it is clear that to have both paddle‐wheels and btb in the structure with a possible composition [MII2(L′)x]3(btb)4, the linear linker L′ must be neutral. 11 The topology of UMCM‐1 is derived from the hexagonal phase β‐Si3N4, DUT—6 is related to the Pt3O4 structure, and UMCM‐2 is based on a completely new topology. A fourth topology (toz) was found by Yaghi and co‐workers6 in MOF‐210 containing Zn4O, an elongated version of btb (4,4′,4′′‐[benzene‐1,3,5‐triyltris(ethyne‐1,2‐diyl)] tribenzoate), and biphenyl‐4,4′‐dicarboxylate.

There are very few topological possibilities for combining square‐like tetratopic and tritopic triangular SBUs in a ratio of 3:4. The problem of enumerating such nets goes back to Wells.9 Although around 25 different nets (included in the RCSR and EPINET databases)10 with stoichiometry A3X4 are known to date, only three of them (pto, tbo, sqc5590) allow a simultaneously square‐like (or rectangular‐planar) coordination environment for four‐coordinated sites and a triangular surrounding for three‐coordinated sites (Figure 1). Highly symmetric cubic pto and tbo nets are commonly found in MOFs, whereas no crystal structure has been observed (to the best of our knowledge) based on the tetragonal sqc5590 net. A closer look showed that only in the case of the pto net all 4‐connected SBUs can in principle be bound by one and the same linear linker. In the case of the tbo topology, neighboring 4‐connected SBUs are twisted relative to each other and, therefore, a V‐shaped, conformationally rigid ligand is required which is difficult to realize.

Finally, in the sqc5590 net only 2/3 of all the 4‐connected SBUs can be bound by a linear linker. We expect that a combination of at least two auxiliary ligands is necessary to achieve this kind of arrangement.

Therefore we considered the basic pto topology to be desirable in which two building blocks, namely a 3‐connected btb linker and a square‐shaped metal cluster are the main SBUs responsible for the topological structure, but without interpenetration. Recently, we realized this type of structure in DUT‐6 (space group Pmn),5a a highly porous robust compound with the tritopic btb ligand and octahedral Zn4O clusters further cross‐linked by 2,6‐naphthalenedicarboxylate anions (cf. above). On the other hand, MOF‐14 (space group Im) is known in which copper paddle‐wheels and btb form two interwoven pto nets, which can be described with the aid of a P minimal surface which separates one net from the other (Figure 1).11 Van der Waals interactions (mainly π–π stacking) hold the two frameworks together.11 In this paper we describe how interpenetration can be avoided by using the same SBUs (paddle‐wheels and btb) and keeping the net topology. The combination of modular concept and interpenetration control enables not only the surface area and pore volume of the materials to be increased, but also leads to the possibility of tailoring the frameworks for specific applications.

Results and Discussion

Calculations: Some concepts have already been proposed to control the interpenetration in very open metal–organic frameworks: The use of organic ligands with sterically hindering groups,12 temperature and concentration control,13 solvent size, and a templating strategy.14 In the concept presented herein the aim was (similarly to DUT‐6) to overcome interpenetration by inserting an additional linker of an appropriate length between the neighboring paddle‐wheel units. To this end, a neutral linear ligand with a length of around 7.0 Å (for example, 4,4′‐bipyridine) is required. To estimate theoretically the effect of incorporating 4,4′‐bipyridine into a MOF structure with a pto‐like topology, we have chosen the model compounds [Zn2(H2O)2]3(btb)4 and [Zn2(bipy)]3(btb)4. DFT calculations (within the dispersion‐corrected SCC‐DFTB method)15 have shown that the interwoven structure of a MOF‐14‐type is indeed energetically preferable. A detailed analysis has revealed that the conformations of the hypothetical DUT‐6 analogue with zinc paddle‐wheels, btb, and 4,4′‐bipyridine (assumed space group Pmn) and a single net taken from the MOF‐14 structure (space group Pm) are essentially different. This can again be understood with the P minimal surface: The centroid of the btb ligand in the Pmn structure coincides exactly with a flat point on the surface (Figure 1), whereas in the interpenetrated structure a flat point is exactly in between the centroids of ligands belonging to different frameworks. Furthermore, the role of the linear linker becomes more evident: There are three sets of mutually perpendicular columns Zn2–bipy–Zn2 (arranged in cylinder packing)16 that strut a porous framework and in this way direct its conformation such that interpenetration becomes impossible.

Synthesis and structure: Taking into account the above considerations, we designed an experiment and by using zinc nitrate, H3btb, and 4,4′‐bipyridine (bipy) as the starting materials together with N,N‐diethylformamide (DEF) as solvent, we synthesized a new compound (DUT‐23). Single‐crystal X‐ray studies performed with synchrotron radiation verified that a new material with the composition [Zn2(bipy)]3(btb)4 crystallizes in cubic space group Pmn and has the desired pto‐like topology similar to DUT‐6 (Figure 2c). The dodecahedral mesoporous cages and cage entrances are significantly smaller in DUT‐23 than in DUT‐6. The distance between the planes of the central btb benzene moieties is approximately 24 Å in DUT‐23(Zn) and approximately 26 Å in DUT‐6. Also, the pore windows are smaller and as a result the adsorption of either Reichardt’s dye or C60 molecules in DUT‐23 is not possible in contrast to the case of DUT‐6.

Further, a series of isotypic compounds were prepared by using different metals, namely, copper (DUT‐23(Cu)), cobalt (DUT‐23(Co)), and nickel (DUT‐23(Ni)). The single crystal studies (see the Experimental Section) and powder X‐ray diffraction experiments (Figures 3 and S1 of the Supporting Information) confirm the isomorphism.

To experimentally justify the role of 4,4′‐bipyridine in the formation of noninterpenetrated open porous networks, we carried out the synthesis using copper nitrate, H3btb, and pyridine in two different molar ratios (7.1:1.6:1 and 4.4:1:3.1). Additional benzoic acid was used as crystallization agent. Rather unexpectedly, instead of the interpenetrated MOF‐14 structure, we obtained two new compounds: DUT‐33 and DUT‐34. 11 We expected the formation of the interpenetrated MOF‐14‐type structure because its synthesis was initially carried out with an excess of pyridine.11

[Cu2(H2O)0.5(py)1.5]3(btb)4 (DUT‐33; py=pyridine) has a completely different arrangement of building blocks: The paddle‐wheel SBUs with axial aqua ligands are linked by btb to form the tbo net. The structure (space group Rm) contains two interpenetrated nets (Figure 2b) related by an inversion center and is isotypic with PCN‐6 and MOF‐htb materials based on copper paddle‐wheels and 4,4′,4′′‐s‐triazine‐2,4,6‐triyl tribenzoate (tatb) and s‐heptazine tribenzoate (htb), respectively.17, 18 DUT‐33 was a minor product and only a small amount of crystals have been isolated so far. The second compound [Cu2(H2O)2]3(btb)4 (DUT‐34) is a noninterpenetrated counterpart (so called framework‐catenation isomer)18 of MOF‐14 (space group Pmn) with terminal water molecules bound to copper paddle‐wheels (Figure 2d). Zhou and co‐workers also showed the possibility of synthesizing a counterpart of the interpenetrated network PCN‐6 (tbo net) by adding oxalic acid to the reaction mixture, which resulted in a single net MOF (PCN‐6′).18

Mechanical properties: To visualize the differences in the mechanical properties of the frameworks, the bulk moduli were calculated: The bulk moduli of our DUT‐34 and MOF‐14 (Table 1) are 9.1 and 14.8 GPa, respectively. The incorporation of 4,4′‐bipyridine makes the single pto net more rigid, leading to an increase of the bulk modulus up to 13.2 GPa (Table 1). Note that MOF‐14 and DUT‐23 show remarkable mechanical stability at low mass densities (for comparison, the bulk modulus of the most dense Zn(imidazolate)2 phase with ρ=1.54 g cm−3 amounts to only 14 GPa19 and for MOF‐5 it is equal to 14.5 GPa, whereas ρcalcd(MOF‐5)=0.55 g cm−3). 22 The bulk modulus of MOF‐5 was estimated by using the same computational method (dispersion‐corrected SCC‐DFTB15) as applied in all quantum chemical calculations in this work.

Adsorption experiments: Supercritical CO2 drying (SCD) applied to DUT‐23 led to a crystalline, highly porous material, as demonstrated by various gas adsorption experiments. The experimental multipoint BET surface areas and total pore volumes (VP) calculated for DUT‐23(M) from the N2 adsorption isotherms (Figure 4) measured at 77 K are 4340 m2 g−1 and 1.85 cm3 g−1 for DUT‐23(Zn), 4850 m2 g−1 and 2.03 cm3 g−1 for DUT‐23(Co), and 4730 m2 g−1 and 2.01 cm3 g−1 for DUT‐23(Cu). The pore volume of MOF‐14 is 0.53 cm3 g−1,11 which is about four times smaller than that of the DUT‐23 series. The BET surface area was also geometrically calculated according to a published procedure (Table 2).20 The largest porosity values reported for materials today are 6240 m2 g−1 (Vp=3.6 cm3 g−1) for MOF‐2106 and 6143 m2 g−1 (Vp=2.82 cm3 g−1) for NU‐100.21 Further gas adsorption experiments were carried out with the cobalt‐containing DUT‐23.

Hydrogen adsorption at 19.5 K up to 1 bar22 (Figure 5) shows a saturation uptake for DUT‐23(Co) at p/p0=0.9 of 1634 cm3 g−1 (147 mg g−1, 12.9 wt %), which corresponds to a specific pore volume of 2.07 cm3 g−1 accessible to hydrogen. The specific surface area derived from this high‐resolution low‐temperature hydrogen adsorption isotherm is 5147 m2 g−1. This is in good agreement with the specific surface area of 5131 m2 g−1 determined geometrically with hydrogen as the probe molecule. The value is slightly higher than the surface area measured with nitrogen because for a nonflat surface the accessible surface area depends on the diameter of the probe molecule.20

Similar to the nitrogen adsorption, a step in the isotherm was observed at approximately 2/3 of the saturation value (see the logarithmic pressure representation in the inset of Figure 5).

Hydrogen has the highest gravimetric energy density of all chemical fuels, however, the volumetric density is very low under standard conditions. Common forms of storage like compression or liquefaction suffer from extreme operating conditions (700 bar for compressed hydrogen, 20 K for liquid hydrogen). An alternative is the cryo‐adsorption of hydrogen in porous solids, which requires lower pressures than compression and allows higher temperatures than liquefaction.23 To evaluate the hydrogen storage performance of DUT‐23, hydrogen physisorption isotherms were measured at 77 K. Up to 1 bar, the cobalt‐based compound has the highest uptake (17 mg g−1, 1.67 wt %) in the DUT‐23(M) series (Figure S8). If the pressure increases up to 40 bar, the excess hydrogen uptake for DUT‐23(Co) rises to 74 mg g−1 (6.9 wt %; Figure 6). Taking the crystallographic density into account, this value corresponds to a volumetric excess uptake of 30 g L−1.

Because DUT‐23(Co) is a crystalline powder, a practice‐oriented volumetric uptake (g/v) can be obtained by using the powder packing density. This was determined by geometrically measuring the filling height of the sample holder without any compaction of the material. A packing density of 0.23 g cm−3 was measured DUT‐23(Co). In most MOFs even at saturation the pores are not completely filled with adsorbate but also contain compressed hydrogen gas. The sum of adsorbed and gaseous hydrogen in the porous material is called absolute uptake.24 It is considerably higher than the excess uptake, as shown in Figure 6 for DUT‐23(Co). At 77 K and 100 bar the gravimetric and volumetric absolute uptake reaches up to 124 mg g−1 (11.0 wt %) and 28 g hydrogen per liter of MOF filled vessel (g/v), respectively. Total volumetric uptake of 40 g/v at 77 K and 100 bar was determined.

For further characterization of the hydrogen solid interaction, high‐accuracy hydrogen adsorption measurements were carried out on DUT‐23(Co) up to 25 bar in the temperature range of 77 K to room temperature (Figure S9). From these measurements the isosteric heat of adsorption was calculated by using a variant of the Clausius–Clapeyron equation in the uptake range of 6 to 62 mg g−1 (Figure S10). With an average of 4.06 kJ mol−1, it is comparable to the heat of adsorption of other materials with pore diameters in the order of 20 Å.24, 25 The heat of adsorption shows a small decrease from 4.25 kJ mol−1 at low uptakes to 3.81 kJ mol−1 at higher uptakes, which indicates that the adsorption sites are similar regarding their interaction strength.

Assuming a homogeneous density of the adsorbed hydrogen at 77 K, adsorption sites with different adsorption energies can be suggested: 53 and 26 mg g−1 are adsorbed on the stronger and weaker adsorption sites, respectively. Because the stronger sites are filled first, an average heat of adsorption of 4.12 kJ mol−1 can be assigned to these sites. For the weaker adsorption sites a heat of adsorption of 3.82 kJ mol−1 is obtained.

Natural gas adsorbed on a porous medium is a promising alternative to compressed natural gas technology as a clean vehicular fuel for bulk transportation. For this purpose, the development of a suitable adsorbent is necessary to maximize the methane uptake per storage volume. MOFs are a family of porous materials that also offer great promise for methane storage. The maximum excess of methane adsorbed on DUT‐23(Co) is 268 mg g−1 at 100 bar and 298 K (Figure 7), equivalent to 379 cm3 g−1 of MOF and 152 cm3 cm−3 of MOF, taking the crystallographic density into account. This value is comparable to the gravimetric excess adsorption capacity of MOF‐210 (264 mg g−1 at 80 bar) and is higher in terms of volumetric uptake (92 cm3 cm−3 for MOF‐210).

As a test gas, n‐butane is often used as a representative of volatile organic air contaminants. Activated carbon and zeolites are used for controlling, recovering, recycling, or concentrating volatile organic compounds by adsorption technology. Owing to their excellent adsorption performance, MOFs can also be used for such applications. To determine the n‐butane adsorption capacity of DUT‐23, the adsorption isotherm was measured at room temperature and ambient pressure (Figure 8). The DUT‐23(Co) adsorbs a remarkable 99 mg of n‐butane per gram of activated material, making it one of the best n‐butane adsorbers so far.5a, 26

DUT‐34 has not been obtained as a porous material in a solvent‐free form so far, neither by conventional activation in a vacuum at elevated temperature nor by using SCD. Clearly, the solvent molecules play a major role in the stabilization of a single framework in the absence of auxiliary bipy cross‐linkers. A comparable observation was made for the non‐interpenetrated structure with tbo topology using a substituted btb. Similarly, in this case a porous, solvent‐free framework could not be produced.27 These findings reinforce the role of 4,4′‐bipyridine in directing a non‐interpenetrated, robust pto‐like, highly porous structure. The axial positions of paddle‐wheels in DUT‐34 in the absence of bipy are accessible for potential catalytic applications, and because most catalytic reactions occur in the liquid phase, the accessibility of the pore system of DUT‐34 was proven by liquid‐phase adsorption of ethyl cinnamate in n‐heptane. The uptake of 440 mg g−1 is comparable to other MOFs with accessible metal coordination sites like DUT‐9 (583 mg g−1).28

Chiral functionalization: Molecular functionalization of the inner surface of pores of a MOF in a rational and systematic fashion still remains a significant challenge. As already demonstrated, decoration of the linker with a variety of functionalities is possible and the surface of the material can be functionalized for specific applications, especially for catalysis and separation. One of the most versatile separation techniques is high‐performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). In particular, among the many types of chromatographic separation, the separation of enantiomers by liquid chromatography is of special importance because it is usually required to provide products of very high purity. To achieve an acceptable level of separation, the size of the analyte molecule should be significantly smaller than the pore diameter of the porous material. Therefore, the mesoporous DUT‐23‐like structure would be ideal for such an application.

By using a chiral linker, the surface of the mesoporous DUT‐23 could be functionalized in situ with a chiral moiety. Here we report the incorporation of a racemic chiral linker into the structure of DUT‐23 by exchanging the N,N donor 4,4′‐bipyridine with diethyl (R,S)‐4,4′‐biquinoline‐3,3′‐dicarboxylate (bisqui), which leads to DUT‐24 ([Cu2(bisqui)0.5]3(btb)4(DMF)3(H2O)3, Figure 2a,e). The racemic biquinoline derivative (bisqui) was synthesized in four steps starting from cheap commercially available chemicals (see the Supporting Information).

The powder X‐ray analysis of the compound synthesized from copper nitrate, H3btb and biqui in DMF/EtOH (1:1) revealed that DUT‐23 and DUT‐24 are isoreticular (Figure S3). However, due to the nature of diethyl (R,S)‐4,4′‐biquinoline‐3,3′‐dicarboxylate, DUT‐24 cannot adopt high cubic symmetry because this would imply the presence of a two‐fold rotational axis passing through the N⋅⋅⋅N atoms, which is not the case for the quinoline derivative. Group–subgroup relations showed that the framework [Cu2(biqui)]3(btb)4 with pto topology cross‐linked by one symmetry‐independent quinoline‐based ligand (biqui) in a general position can be realised in the space group R3c. The structure was modeled in this space group and the model was successfully used for the structural refinement of DUT‐24 against the X‐ray data. 22 Furthermore, the rotation of such a bulky linker is sterically hindered, hence, two‐fold “statistical” symmetry due to orientational disordering is hardly possible. 33 The incorporation of biqui provides a much higher stabilization energy (3.72 vs. 2.63 eV) in comparison with bipy (see Section 2 of the Supporting Information).

By a combination of thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S7) and elemental analysis techniques, the composition of the DUT‐24 framework was estimated to be [Cu2(bisqui)0.5]3(btb)4(DMF)3(H2O)3. This leads to the conclusion that on average only one axial side of the paddle‐wheel unit is occupied by the racemic linker molecule and the other by solvent molecules. Calculations revealed that the surface area of DUT‐24 should be smaller than that of DUT‐23 (Table 2). But the surface area (2350 m2 g−1) and total pore volume (1.12 cm3 g−1) of DUT‐24, estimated by nitrogen physisorption measurement, are significantly lower than expected. Clearly, the incomplete occupation of the axial metal sites can lead to reduced crystallinity and partial collapse of the framework during activation. This assumption is supported by the observations on DUT‐34, for which the axial copper sites of the paddle‐wheel units are not cross‐linked resulting in a nonporous, amorphous phase after solvent removal. The integration of an enantiomerically pure biquinoline ligand into the framework and the synthesis of a chiral analogue of DUT‐24 with potential applications in enantioselective separation are currently in progress.

Conclusion

The outcome of the strategy proposed for the design of MOFs using the tritopic btb and neutral bipy linkers is a series of new stable, non‐interpenetrated frameworks (DUT‐23) with a cross‐linked pto‐like topology, uniform mesopores, and very high specific surface areas and pore volumes (up to 2.03 cm3 g−1). They show excellent performance in the adsorption of gases like methane, hydrogen, and n‐butane. Also, the non‐cross‐linked version of DUT‐23 (DUT‐34, the non‐interpenetrated pendant to MOF‐14) was synthesized. However, a solvent‐free framework has not been obtained so far, which indicates the crucial role of integrated auxiliary linkers in the formation of robust, non‐interpenetrated crystalline pto architectures. Indeed, the accessibility of the pores in DUT‐34 was shown by adsorption experiments in the liquid phase and because the axial positions of paddle‐wheels are accessible, this material has potential in catalytic applications. By exchanging the N,N donor 4,4′‐bipyridine with a substituted chiral biquinoline, racemic DUT‐24 was obtained. This opens up a route to the synthesis of an enantiopure form of DUT‐24, which could be interesting for enantioselective separation.

Topological modeling and quantum chemical calculations: Taking into account the desired stoichiometry [MII2(L′)x]3(btb)4 (L′=neutral ligand), we performed a comprehensive search for possible A3X4 (A represents the paddle‐wheel unit and X represents btb) network topologies in the available databases (RCSR10b and EPINET10a). The topology describes the way in which paddle‐wheels and btb are assembled in a 3D network. We decided to focus on the pto topology because in this case all paddle‐wheels can be linked by one and the same linear ligand, which gives rise to the stoichiometry [MII2(L′)]3(btb)4. Two main structural models with the composition [MII2(H2O)2]3(btb)4 (M=Zn) were considered: The interpenetrated structure of MOF‐14 type (space group Im) and a single‐net MOF (DUT‐6 analogue, space group Pmn). The structural models used in the calculation of the total energy were either based on the reported crystallographic data (for MOF‐14; copper was replaced by zinc, hydrogen atoms were added as necessary, and solvent molecules were removed) or built up with a specially designed Python (http://www.python.org/) script (this was the case for DUT‐23 and DUT‐24). Based on the optimized geometry of the single‐net MOF (space group Pmn), we found a possibility for incorporating the bipyridine molecule in between the neighboring paddle‐wheel units (as was suggested by stereochemical considerations). Then a model structure [Zn2(bipy)]3(btb)4 was constructed in space group Pmn and fully optimized. The relative stabilities of the different [Zn2(H2O)2]3(btb)4 polymorphs are given in Table 1. To compare the relative stabilities of the interpenetrated and non‐interpenetrated frameworks, we calculated half of the difference in total energies per formula unit. In other words, we estimated the energetic stabilization per single framework as reported in Table 1. This was necessary in order to consider on an equal footing the energetic stabilization of a single net originating from the insertion of an additional linker (model net; further DUT‐23) or a second translationally equivalent net (MOF‐14). All the calculations were carried out by using a density‐functional‐based tight‐binding method15 as implemented in the DFTB+ program package.34 Optimized unit cells were determined from the fixed‐cell structure optimizations (conjugate gradient method was used) performed for different values of the lattice parameter a. By fitting the energy versus volume curves, bulk moduli were calculated. The Γ point approximation was used for all structures because the unit cells are large enough (cell parameters >25.0 Å). Dispersion correction was also included.35
