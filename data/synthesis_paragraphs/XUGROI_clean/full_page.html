Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 49, November 2014, Pages 34-36

A copper-based metal–organic framework constructed from a new tetracarboxylic acid for selective gas separation

Author links open overlay panelDanYanaBanglinChenbQianDuana
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2014.09.010Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Synthesis of a copper-based MOF constructed from a new tetracarboxylic acid linker.

•

The three-dimensional framework was proved by single-crystal X-ray diffraction.

•

Selective gas sorption property was investigated.

•

The coverage-dependent adsorption enthalpies were calculated by the virial method.

Abstract

A new organic ligand of 1,2,4,5-tetrakis(3-carboxyphenyl)-benzene (m-H4TCPB) and its first metal–organic framework 1 [Cu2(m-TCPB)(H2O)2]n·[(DEF)3]n (DEF = N, N′-diethylformamide) was designed and synthesized. 1 has a three-dimensional (4,4) framework topology. The activated 1a exhibits permanent porosity and demonstrates selective toward adsorption of hydrogen over nitrogen at 77 K, and acetylene and carbon dioxide over methane at room temperature.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a class of permanently microporous hybrid materials, which exist as infinite crystalline lattice comprising inorganic vertices (metals ions or clusters) and organic struts, connected by coordination bonds [1]. One of the most attractive features of MOFs is the ability to incorporate an unlimited range of organic ligands, as well as their simple synthesis and recent advances in purification [2], [3]. In particular, their extremely high surface areas, ultralow densities, fine tunable pore structures, and adjustable chemical functionality make them promising candidates for gas separation, as well as for catalysis, chemical sensing, and drug delivery [4], [5], [6], [7], [8].

By carefully choosing metals and organic linkers, the structure and pore properties, such as pore size and pore volume of MOFs, can be systematically tailored [9]. Scores of organic ligands (such as carboxylic acid, imidazole and tetrazole) have been utilized as struts in MOFs [10], [11], [12]. The ligand 1,2,4,5-tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)-benzene (p-H4TCPB) was first synthesized and applied in MOFs by Hupp's group. It has distinctive features, such as: (1) four carboxylic groups, which are potential metal biding sites allowing for the formation of a topologically diverse family of materials, (2) its rigidness, which can enable the formation of robust MOFs with metal ions, and (3) its bulky long molecular structure, which can give rise to the formation of microporous coordination frameworks through framework interpenetration, making it suitable for gas storage, separation, and catalysis [13], [14]. With this in mind, we tried to synthesize a copper-based paddlewheel framework using H4TCPB as the linker, but failed. On the other hand, altering the position of the functional groups (such as carboxylate, sulfonate, phosphonate, and heterocyclic groups) in the ligands can also influence the network formation and porosity [15], [16], [17]. Here, we describe a new Cu-paddlewheel MOFs with the overall formula of [Cu2(m-TCPB)(H2O)2]n · [(DEF)3]n 1, which shows intriguing structure and properties, such as H2/N2 separation.

1 is constructed from 1,2,4,5-tetrakis(3-carboxyphenyl)-benzene (m-H4TCPB) (Scheme 1), which can be straightforwardly synthesized by the coupling and hydrolysis reaction, from its precursor. A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.02 g) and m-H4TCPB (0.01 g) was dissolved in N, N′-diethylformamide (DEF, 1.5 mL), water (0.4 mL), and con. HCl (8 μL). The final mixture was placed in a 20 mL glass vial, which was sealed and heated at 75 °C for 24 h, and then cooled to room temperature. Rhombus-shaped turquoise crystals of 1 were collected in 42% yield (based on ligand m-H4TCPB). Anal. calcd for C49H55O13N3Cu2: C, 57.64; H, 5.43; and N, 4.12. Found: C, 57.70; H, 5.46; and N, 4.17. IR/cm− 1: 2972 (m), 1615 (m), 1538 (s), 1395 (s), 1371 (s), 820(m), 767 (s), and 710(s). It was formulated on the bases of single-crystal X-ray structure determination, elemental analysis, and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). A single-crystal X-ray crystallographic study revealed that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system and C2/c space group [18]. As quite frequently observed in MOFs, the framework contains paddle-wheel binuclear {Cu2(COO)4} secondary building units (Fig. 1a), which are bridged by organic m-TCPB ligands to form a two-dimensional (2D) hexagonal grid {Cu2(m-TCPB)2} (Fig. 1b). The adjacent 2D hexagonal grid is packed in parallel, leading to the final porous-containing three-dimensional (3D) frameworks with the cavities of 8.5 × 12.6 Å2 (Fig. 1d). The structure of 1 is non-interpenetrated and the topology (Fig. S2) can be best described as a simple 4, 4 connected net (Fig. 1c, d), which exhibits the same topology as well-established MOFs, such as: MOF-2 Zn(BDC)-(DMF), Cu(BDC-OH), Zn2(TBAPy), and UTSA-72 [19], [20], [21], [22]. The fifth coordination sites of paddle–wheel Cu2(COO)4 units were occupied by water molecules, which will be critical for the gas-absorption selectivity. Upon water molecules removal, the open sites will be exposed for other applications. The solvent accessible volume after the removal of the uncoordinated solvents is 59.6% calculated with PLATON [23].

The structure was determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, and the phase purity of the material in bulk amount was confirmed by elemental analysis and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) (Fig. S3). As shown in Fig. S4, the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) studies reveal that 1 gradually loses the free DEF and coordinated water molecules (obsd. 32.8%, calcd. 33.2%) in the temperature range 25–250 °C to form the desolvated framework 1a. Typically, the free solvent molecules were lost first followed by the coordinated solvent molecules. However, because DEF has much higher boiling point (153 °C) than water, DEF and coordinated water molecules might be released simultaneously from 1, so we cannot specifically assign the weight loss steps for these two types of solvent molecules from the TGA data. The temperature dependent PXRD patterns further confirm that desolvated framework keeps crystalline nature until 250 °C, though the diffraction peaks were slightly broaden and shifted. After gas sorption, 1a still exhibits a moderately high crystalline nature, indicating that 1a displays a permanent porous structure.

To check the selective adsorption properties of 1a, hydrogen and nitrogen adsorptions were carried out at 77 K; carbon dioxide, methane, and acetylene adsorptions were carried out at 273 K and 296 K, respectively. As shown in Fig. 2, 1a can take up a relatively significant amount of hydrogen (24.4 cm3/g) but a negligible amount of nitrogen (2.3 cm3/g) at 77 K and 1 atm, underlying a good capacity of 1a for H2/N2 separation [24], [25], [26]. 1a can also absorb 23.3 cm3/g acetylene and 23.0 cm3/g carbon dioxide, but a very limited amount of methane (7.3 cm3/g) at 273 K and 1 atm (Fig. 3). Their adsorption isotherms at 296 K (Fig. S6) further confirmed the different gas uptakes of 1a for these examined gas molecules, underlining the potential of these new MOFs for the selective gas separation of C2H2/CH4, CO2/CH4.

The coverage-dependent adsorption enthalpies of 1a to carbon dioxide, acetylene, and methane were calculated based on the virial method, a well-established and reliable methodology from fits of their adsorption isotherms at 273 K and 296 K (Fig. S7) [27]. The enthalpies at zero coverage of gas were calculated to give the values of 38.3, 34.7, and 28.6 kJ/mol for C2H2, CO2, and CH4 respectively. The adsorption enthalpies for C2H2, CO2, and CH4 in 1a are moderately higher than those reported values [5], [24]. The relatively high Qst of this compound may be due to the pore size and open metal sites in the activated framework, which favors the selective gas separation for these large gas molecules. Henry's law selectivities (Sij) for C2H2/CH4, CO2/CH4 calculated respectively based on the equation Sij = KHi/KHj, are also shown in Table S1, which are moderately high [4], [19].

In conclusion, we synthesized and structurally characterized a new organic linker 1,2,4,5-tetrakis(3-carboxyphenyl)-benzene (m-H4TCPB) and its first metal–organic framework Cu-m-TCPB. The activated 1a demonstrates selective toward adsorption of hydrogen over nitrogen at 77 K, and acetylene and carbon dioxide over methane at room temperature based on Henry's law gas separation selectivity. We are now exploring ways to assemble this new organic linker with other metal ions/clusters to synthesize diverse structure MOFs and explore their potential applications.

The X-ray diffraction data was collected using synchrotron radiation, λ = 0.40663 Å, at Advanced Photon Source on beamline 15ID-C. Indexing was performed using APEX2 (difference vectors method). Data integration and reduction were performed using SaintPlus 6.01. Absorption correction was performed by multi-scan method implemented in SADABS. The structure was solved using SHELXL-2014 (direct methods) and refined using SHELXL-2014 (full-matrix least-squares on F2) contained packages. Despite of using synchrotron source and trying several crystals from different batches, diffraction experiment resulted in low quality diffraction data. This can be attributed to the presence of the solvent disorder and to the presence of bad quality, multiply twinned crystals. All non-H atoms were found in the difference Fourier map and were refined using the distance restraints. Hydrogen atoms were placed in geometrically calculated positions and included in the refinement process using riding model with isotropic thermal parameters: Uiso(H) = 1.2 Ueq(–CH). The DEF solvents could not be located. Contributions to scattering from all solvent molecules were removed using the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON; the structure was then refined again using the data.
