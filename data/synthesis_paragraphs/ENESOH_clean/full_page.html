Introduction

Polyoxometalates (POMs), viewed as excellent inorganic ligands or noncoordinated templates due to their abundant O atoms, have been widely used to construct metalâ€“organic complexes (MOCs) with various structures and properties.1 The octamolybdate- and Keggin-based MOCs are two important branches, in which the Keggin anions usually act as both inorganic ligands and noncoordinated templates, while the octamolybdate was often used as a ligand.2 Reports on octamolybdates as noncoordinated templates are relatively limited.3

From the synthetic point of view, the designing or selecting of appropriate organic ligands with specific length, geometry, coordination sites and coordination ability is crucial to achieve the desired structures.4 Up to now, a large amount of N-donor ligands, O-donor ligands and N/O-donor ligands have been widely used to construct POM-based MOCs due to their abundant coordination sites and excellent coordination ability.5 The N/O-donor ligands mainly include N-containing carboxylic acids, amide ligands and so on.6 Some symmetric amide ligands such as 1,4-bis(3-pyridinecarboxamido)benzene, N,Nâ€²-bis(3-pyridinecarboxamide)-1,2-ethane, N,Nâ€²-bis(isonicotinoyl)-trans-1,2-diaminocyclohexane) etc. have been reported.7 However, research on POM-based MOCs based on asymmetric amide ligands is still limited.8

In this work, a tetrazole- and pyridyl-containing asymmetric amide ligand, namely, 3-(1H-tetrazole-1-acetic acid amido) pyridine (TAAP), was employed to assemble with transition metal ions and POMs under hydrothermal or solvothermal conditions. The TAAP ligand has some remarkable features as follows: (i) both the tetrazole and pyridyl rings may act as excellent coordination sites to coordinate with metal ions, generating diverse structures; (ii) the amide groups with both the Nâ€“H hydrogen donor and the CO hydrogen acceptor may conduce to the formation of hydrogen bonds and supramolecular structures; (iii) the flexible nature of â€“CH2â€“ allows the ligands to bend freely to satisfy the coordination requirement of metal centers. To the best of our knowledge, research on the introduction of TAAP ligand into POM systems has not been reported up to now (Scheme 1).

In the synthesis process of MOCs, some organic ligands may in situ transform to other one or two new ligands.9 Up to now, examples of in situ ligand transformation are relatively rare in POM-based MOCs.10 In this work, by introducing a tetrazole- and pyridyl-containing asymmetric amide ligand into POM systems, four POM-based complexes, H4[Cu2(TAAP)4(H2O)](Mo8O26)2Â·20H2O (1), H2[Co(TAAP)2(H2O)2](Mo8O26)Â·11H2O (2), H2[Ag4(TAAP)4(H2O)2(PMo12O40)2]Â·4H2O (3), and H4[Ag2(AAP)4(PW12O40)2]Â·2AAPÂ·12H2O (4) (AAP = 3-(acetic acid amido) pyridine), have been successfully synthesized under solvothermal (methanolâ€“water mixed solvent) or hydrothermal conditions. In compound 4, the AAP ligand was in situ transformed from TAAP. The influence of the metal ions and POMs on the structures is discussed. The electrochemical behaviors of compounds 1, 3 and 4 and the photocatalytic properties of the title compounds were investigated. Moreover, the luminescence emissions of compounds 3 and 4 were studied at room temperature.

The in situ transformation of AAP

It is interesting that compound 4 and the in situ synthesis of APP are simultaneously generated under the hydrothermal reaction condition. The starting TAAP reagent was converted into APP ligand in the presence of the PW12O403âˆ’ polyanion, AgI ion and NH4VO3 in hydrothermal conditions (Scheme 2). The in situ ligand reaction mechanism is complicated and elusive, as is reported by other groups.14 Further insight into the nature of this reaction process shows that the APP ligand was generated from the single Câ€“N bond cleavage of TAAP.15 In the synthesis process of compound 3, when other Keggin-type H3PMo12O40 instead of H3PW12O40 was used, the APP ligand could not be observed under the same hydrothermal condition. Thus, H3PW12O40 may play a crucial role in the process of in situ transformation of TAAP. Besides, in the synthesis process of compounds 1 and 2, the TAAP ligand also did not transform to AAP in solvothermal conditions, which further illustrated the importance of H3PW12O40 in the in situ ligand transformation process.

The role of metal ions, solvents, temperature, and POMs in the construction of the title compounds

In this work, we selected a tetrazole- and pyridyl-containing asymmetric amide as the organic ligand, and octamolybdate and two kinds of Keggin polyanions as inorganic building blocks to investigate their influence on the assembly and structures of the POM-based MOCs (Scheme 3).
Scheme 3 Schematic illustration of the influence of different POMs and metal ions on the structures of 1â€“4.

As described above, compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural with the octamolybdate polyanions acting as templates, which indicates that the central CuII and CoII ions have no obvious effect on the final networks due to their same charges, similar sizes and similar properties. Compounds 1 and 2 are obtained under solvothermal conditions (methanolâ€“water mixed solvent). We tried to synthesize these compounds under hydrothermal conditions. Unfortunately, no crystals could be obtained, which indicated that the solvents have a great effect on the formation of crystals. Otherwise, the different kinds of Keggin polyanions have a significant effect on the architectures of compounds 3 and 4 in the same conditions. In compound 3, two Keggin PMo12 anions acting as monodentate ligands coordinated to the AgI ions from a tetranuclear [Ag4(TAAP)2(H2O)2]4+ subunit via an Agâ€“O bond, forming a dimeric structure. In compound 4, the Keggin PW12 anions as four-connected inorganic ligands linked the [Ag(APP)2]+ subunits to form infinite 1D chains. It is worth noting that the TAAP ligand in situ transformed to AAP ligand in compound 4. The results indicated that different Keggin polyanions not only can influence the final structures but also have a great impact on the in situ transformation of ligand in this system. We also tried to synthesize compounds 3 and 4 in other reaction temperatures. When the reaction temperature was 160 Â°C, the crystal yields were very low for compounds 3 and 4. When the reaction was performed under 100 Â°C, only precipitates were obtained. Thus, the reaction temperatures are crucial for the formation of crystals and 120 Â°C is the optimum temperature.

Electrochemical property

Photocatalytic property

At present, the POM-based MOCs have been proved to be good photocatalysts in degrading some organic substances under UV or visible light.24 Herein, we selected an organic dye, methylene blue (MB), as a model pollutant in aqueous medium to investigate the photocatalytic performance of compounds 1â€“4 under UV irradiation from a 125 W Hg lamp, visible light irradiation from a 350 W Xe lamp equipped with a long-pass filter (400 nm cutoff) and sunlight irradiation. As shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. S8â€“S10â€  (top), the absorption peaks of MB decreased obviously with increasing reaction time for compounds 1â€“4 under UV irradiation. Moreover, the concentrations of MB (C) versus reaction time (t) for the title compounds were plotted. The degradation ratio of MB reaches 95.86% for 1, 92.22% for 2, 72.73% for 3 and 92.27% for 4 over 100 min. Additionally, the PXRD patterns of the title compounds after the UV irradiation photocatalytic reactions have been recorded, which match with the simulated ones except for some intensity differences (Fig. S3â€ ). The results suggest that the title compounds possess good stability in the MB photodegradation process. Due to the good stability and excellent photocatalytic performances under UV irradiation, we further performed photocatalytic tests of the title compounds towards the degradation of MB under visible light irradiation and sunlight irradiation, which can make the photocatalytic process more conducive to practical application. To the best of our knowledge, reports on the photocatalytic activity of POM-based MOCs under visible light irradiation or sunlight irradiation are still very limited. As shown in Fig. 5 and S8â€“S10,â€  the absorption peaks of MB also decreased with increasing reaction time for compounds 1â€“4 under visible light and sunlight irradiation. The concentrations of MB (C) versus reaction time (t) for the title compounds were also plotted (Fig. 6). The calculation results show that approximately 78.66% for 1, 63.40% for 2, 63.03% for 3 and 79.27% for 4 of MB has been decomposed over 150 min under visible light irradiation and 85.36% for 1, 81.43% for 2, 86.48% for 3 and 87.50% for 4 of MB has been decomposed over 5 hours under sunlight irradiation.

Luminescence properties

Based on the excellent luminescence properties of coordination polymers with d10 metals,25 the photoluminescence properties of compounds 3 and 4 have been investigated in the solid state at room temperature in this work. The emission peaks are shown in Fig. S11.â€  The emission peaks of 3 and 4 are 450 nm (Î»ex = 320 nm) and 448 nm (Î»ex = 320 nm), respectively. To know the emission nature, the luminescence property of the free TAAP ligand was studied. The free TAAP ligand exhibits a strong emission peak at 412 nm upon excitation at 320 nm. Obviously, the emission peaks of 3 and 4 exhibit distinct red shifts compared with the TAAP ligand, which may come from the ligand-to-metal charge-transfer transition (LMCT) involving the TAAP ligand and the central metal AgI ions.26

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have designed and synthesized four metalâ€“organic complexes with various structures by introducing the tetrazole- and pyridyl-containing asymmetric amide ligand into POM systems. This work suggests that a suitable ligand is very important to obtain novel POM-based MOCs and that different Keggin POMs play crucial roles in the construction of final structures and the in situ transformation of ligand TAAP. The title compounds represent the first examples of introducing tetrazole- and pyridyl-containing asymmetric amide ligand into the POM system. Compounds 1, 3 and 4 exhibit good electrocatalytic behaviors toward the reduction of H2O2 and compounds 1â€“4 possess good photocatalytic activities for the degradation of MB under UV, visible light and sunlight irradiation. The luminescence properties of compounds 3 and 4 in the solid state at room temperature were also reported. Further work for preparing POM-based inorganicâ€“organic compounds based on asymmetric amide ligands is in progress.
