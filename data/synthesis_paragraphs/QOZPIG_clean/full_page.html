Polyhedron

Volume 87, 17 February 2015, Pages 361-368

Syntheses, crystal structures, and properties of four coordination polymers with a new bifunctional ligand

Author links open overlay panelChaojunShenYamengLiDaqiangYuan
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2014.12.001Get rights and content

Abstract

Four new coordination polymers, {[Cd2(L)2·H2O]·4DMF·14H2O}n (1), {[Cd (L)]·2DMF·6H2O}n (2), {[Zn2(L)2]·4DMF·5H2O}n (3) and {[Cu (L)]·2DMF}n (4) (H2L = 2,5-bis((4H-1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)carbamoyl)terephthalic acid), have been synthesized under solvothermal conditions and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. All of them exhibit three-dimensional structures possessing one-dimensional channels filled with solvent molecules. 1 is a 3D framework with large 1D channels (6.60 × 8.56 Å2) filled with free DMF molecules and the 3D structure of 1 is a new (4, 8) network with Schläfli symbol {416.612}{44.62}2. 2 and 3 with different metal centers have similar structure except for the numbers of free water molecules. They both feature a (4, 4)-connected 3D network of {65.10} {65.8} topology with left- and right-handed helical chains along b-axis. While 4 is a 3D framework with typical CdSO4 topology. In addition, 1–3 show strong fluorescent emissions at room temperature, which can be assigned to the ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT) emissions.

Graphical abstract

Four new metal–organic frameworks display interesting 3D structures have been synthesized based on a novel organic ligand (H2L) involving both 1,2,4-triazole and carboxylic groups under solvothermal conditions. Compounds 1–3 display intense fluorescent emissions at 435, 444 and 445 nm, respectively.

1. Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have received widespread attention in recent years not only because of their rich structural diversities but also owing to their potential applications in magnetism, gas adsorption, catalysis, fluorescence, optical devices [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], and so on [6]. So far, big progress has been made in this fascinating research area. An effective and facile approach to obtain new metal–organic assemblies for functional materials is still the appropriate choice of well-designed organic ligands together with metal salts with definite coordination number and geometry. Among the various ligands, bifunctional ligand containing both carboxylic coordinating groups and nitrogen donors have been proved to be good candidates for the construction of MOFs with diverse structures and interesting properties due to their excellent coordination capability and variable coordination patterns [7].

With the aim of constructing unusual coordination architectures, a new bifunctional ligand, 2,5-bis((4H-1,2,4-triazol-4-yl)carbamoyl)terephthalic acid (H2L) has been synthesized (Scheme 1). H2L is a ligand with both flexible triazol and grid carboxylic groups, which may be an excellent candidate for the construction of novel coordination polymers with various dimensions and connections. On one hand, dicarboxylic groups manifest various bonding modes and its phenyl ring provides structural rigidity [8]. On the other hand, flexible 1,2,4-triazole groups are able to exhibit several coordination modes, particularly as the N1, N2 bridging unit to link metal (II) ions to form polynuclear metal complexes [9].

Herein, four new compounds {[Cd2(L)2·H2O]·4DMF·14H2O}n (1), {[Cd(L)]·2DMF·6H2O}n (2) {[Zn2(L)2]·4DMF·5H2O}n (3) and {[Cu (L)]·2DMF}n (4) (DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide) formulated based upon single crystal X-ray diffraction studies, elemental analysis, and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) results were obtained based on H2L and corresponding transition metal salts. In addition, the luminescent properties for compounds 1–3 were investigated.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and methods

All reagents and solvents used were received from commercial suppliers without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed with a Vario MICRO CHNOS Elemental Analyzer. The infrared spectra of KBr pellet were recorded in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum One FT-IR Spectrometer. Thermal analyses were performed on a NETZSCH STA 449C-QMS403C apparatus from room temperature to 1000 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under nitrogen flow. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were acquired with a MiniFlexII diffractometer by using Cu Kα α radiation in an ambient environment. The solid-state luminescence emission and excitation spectra were recorded on a FLS920 fluorescence spectrophotometer.

2.2. Synthesis

After being cooled, the suspension was filtered and 250 ml H2O was added into the filtrate and stirred at 30 °C for 1 day. Then the suspension was filtered and the white solid was washed with H2O and dried under vacuum at 100 °C to afford H2L (1.300 g, 33.7%). 1H NMR (DMSO-d6) 8.88 (s, 2H,), 8.68 (s, 4H), 8.22 (s, 2H). 2.3. X-ray crystallography

Crystal data of 1–4 was collected on a SuperNova CCD/AFCR diffractometer. Crystal data were collected at 173(2) K on a SuperNova CCD/AFCR diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) for 1–3 and Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54184 Å) for 4. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the shelxs-97 and shelxl-97 programs [10]. For 1–4, all the hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and refined isotropically using the riding model. The large non-solvent H and C Uiso (max)/Uiso (min) are caused by the disorder of the coordinated solvent molecules. The SQUEEZE routine was applied to remove the contributions to the scattering from the disordered solvent molecules of 1. Additionally, the solvent molecules of DMF and H2O molecules in 1 and H2O molecules in 2 and 3 were determined by TG and element analysis. The crystallographic data and other pertinent information are summarized in Table 1. The selected bond lengths and bond angles for the compounds are listed in Table S1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Crystal structure of {[Cd2(L)2·H2O]·4DMF·14H2O}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction of compound 1 demonstrates that the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one crystallographically independent Cd(II) atom, two isolated one half L2− ligand and one half coordinated aqua molecule. The seven-coordinated Cd(II) centers exhibit distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometries with three nitrogen atoms from three different L2− ligands as well as four oxygen atoms from three isolated L2− ligands and one coordinated H2O molecule (Fig. 1a). The Cd–N and Cd–O bond distances in compound 1 range from 2.270(14) to 2.365(3) and 2.285(3) to 2.401(2) Å, respectively (Table S1), which are comparable with those in the other reported seven-coordinated Cd(II) complexes with nitrogen and oxygen donor ligands [11]. As shown in Fig. S1, two Cd1 atoms are connected by two triazol groups from two distinct L2− ligands and one water molecule to form a dinuclear sub-unit with Cd1⋯Cd1A distance being 3.995(2) Å. Such dinuclear sub-units are further linked by the ligands to afford a porous 3D framework with large channels (6.60 × 8.56 Å2) along the a-axis (Fig. 1b and S2). The potential accessible volume is approximately 3149.0 Å3 per unit cell volume, and the pore volume void ratio is 51.5% calculated by the platon program [12]. Some experiments were made in order to access the pore space in 1 and study its gas-sorption property (Supporting information).

It is noteworthy that L2− ligands in compound 1 have two different coordination modes: (1) [L-1], octadentate mode, both the carboxylic groups from the same L2− ligand employ bidentate chelating mode to coordinate with one Cd(II) atom and four nitrogen atoms of two triazol groups connect to four Cd(II) atoms via unidentate mode (Scheme 2a); (2) [L-2], tetradentate mode, two carboxylic groups and two triazol groups of the L2− ligand coordinated to four different Cd(II) atoms with typical unidentate coordination fashions (Scheme 2b). Two kinds of L2− ligands with different coordination modes both act as four-connected node (Fig. 2a) and each dinuclear sub-unit can be treated as an eight-connector (Fig. 2b). Therefore, compound 1 is a 3D (4,8)-connected 2-nodal net with the Schläfli symbol of {416.612}{44.62}2 (Fig. 2c), which represents a new topology analyzed by the topos 4.0 program [13].

3.2. Crystal structure of {[Cd (L)]·2DMF⋅ 6H2O}n (2)

When the reaction solvent of 1 was changed from DMF/CH3OH/H2O to DMF and the quantity of HBF4 was decreased, compound 2 was obtained. The results of crystal structural analysis show that the asymmetric unit of 2 includes one Cd(II) center, one L2− ligand and two isolated DMF molecules, respectively. As depicted in Fig. 3a, The Cd(II) atom with distorted octahedral coordination geometry is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from two carboxylic groups of two L2− ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two triazol groups of two isolated L2− ligands. The Cd–O distances are ranging from 2.325(4) to 2.393(4) Å and the Cd–N distances are both 2.258(5) Å (Table S1). Interestingly, the Cd(II) atoms are connected by L2− ligands to form 1D left-handed and right-handed helical chains circumgyrated along the a-axis with pitch length of 18.919 Å in the channels (Fig. 4).

In compound 2, there is one independent L2− ligand which adopts the hexadentate coordination mode, two carboxylic groups of the L2− ligand chelated to two Cd(II) atoms and two triazol groups connect to two Cd(II) atoms with a unidentate fashion (Scheme 2c). Eventually, the Cd(II) atoms were connected by the L2− ligands to generate a 3D framework exhibiting 1D channels occupied by the DMF guest molecules (Fig. 3b). From the viewpoint of structural topology, each Cd(II) atom and each L2− ligand can be treated as a 4-connected node, respectively (Fig. 5a and b); thus, the 3D framework of 2 can be simplified to a (4, 4)-connected 2-nodal net of {65.10}{65.8} topology (Fig. 5c).

3.3. Crystal structure of {[Zn2(L)2]·4DMF·5H2O}n (3)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction of compound 3 demonstrates that the asymmetric unit consists of one crystallographically independent Zn(II) atom, two isolated one half L2− ligands, two free DMF molecules and one and a half free aqua molecules. As depicted in Fig. S3a, each Zn(II) center is coordinated to four carboxylic oxygen atoms from two L2− anions with Zn–O distances ranging from 2.0639(19) to 2.3407(19) Å and two triazole nitrogen atoms from two isolated L2− anions with Zn–N distances of 2.032(2) and 2.039(2) Å (Table S1). Therefore, the Zn(II) atom in 3 adopts a considerably distorted octahedral {ZnO4N2} coordination geometry. The 3D framework, topological structure and the coordination mode of the ligand of 3 are as same as compound 2 (Figs. S3b, S4, S5 and Scheme 2c).

3.4. Crystal structure of {[Cu (L)]·2DMF}n (4)

The results of crystal structural analysis show that the asymmetric unit of 4 includes one Cu(II) center, one L2− ligand and two isolated DMF molecules, respectively. Each copper(II) ion is hexacoordinated by two triazole nitrogen atoms [Cu(1)–N(1) = 1.981(5) Å, and Cu(1)–N(1)#1 = 1.981(5) Å, #1 = −x + 2, −y + 1, −z + 1] and four oxygen atoms from two chelating carboxylic groups, with two short and two long Cu–O distances [Cu(1)–O(3)#2 = 2.015(4) Å, Cu(1)–O(3)#3 = 2.015(4) Å, Cu(1)–O(2)#2 = 2.510(5) Å, Cu(1)–O(2)#3 = 2.510(5) Å, #2: x, −y + 3/2, z − 1/2, #3: −x + 2, y − 1/2, −z + 3/2] (Fig. 6a). The difference in Cu–O bond distances indicates a strong Jahn–Teller effect [14].

Similar to compound 2, the L2− ligand exhibits the hexadentate coordination mode in 4 (Scheme 2c). As shown in Fig. 6b, 4 display a 2D layer containing a 30-membered trimetallic macrocycles. In each rectangular ring, the Cu1⋯Cu1#1, Cu1⋯Cu1#2 and Cu1#1⋯Cu1#2 distances are about 8.73338(5), 10.855(2) and 11.6545(11) Å (symmetry code #1: −x + 2, y + 0.5, −z + 1.5; #2 x + 1, y, z + 1). The 2D framework is further connected by the coordinated triazole groups to form a 3D framework with 1D channels occupied by the DMF guest molecules (Fig. 6c). Furthermore, topological analysis is carried out to get insight of the structure of 4. Each L2− ligand can be seen as a 4-connecting node which links with four Cu(II) atoms and Cu(II) atom acts as a 4-connecting node which links with four L2− ligands (Fig. 7a and b). Consequently, the 3D structure of 4 can be reduced as a classical (4, 4)-connected CdSO4 net (cds) with Schläfli symbol {65.8} (Fig. 7c).

3.5. PXRD studies and thermal analysis

To confirm the phase purity of these four complexes, the PXRD patterns were recorded at room temperature and comparable to the corresponding simulated ones calculated from the single-crystal diffraction data. As shown in Fig. S6 the peak positions of simulated and experimental patterns are in good agreement with each other, demonstrating the phase purity of the products. The differences in intensity may be in virtue of the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

To investigate the thermal stability of compounds 1–4, thermal analyses have been carried out on crystalline sample in a nitrogen atmosphere (Fig. S7).

The TGA curve of 1 indicates that there is a weight loss of 17.58% from 25 to 140 °C, which can be attributed to the loss of fourteen free water as well as two coordinated water molecules (calcd. 17.35%) and a weight loss of 18.95% in the temperature range of 140–280 °C is due to the loss of the free solvent DMF molecules (calcd. 18.79%). Then the framework begins to decompose. For 2 a weight loss of 14.16% in the temperature range of 25–100 °C is due to the release of six guest water (calcd. 14.38%) and a weight loss of 19.19% from 100 to 230 °C can be attributed to the loss of two free solvent DMF molecules. Then the observed weight loss is due to the decomposition of the framework. Compound 3 starts to lose its solvent DMF molecules and lattice water molecules from 25 to 190 °C (calcd. 29.9%; found 29.7%), after that there is no weight loss until the decomposition of the framework occurs at 330 °C. While for compound 4, the first step has been assigned to the removal of lattice DMF molecules below 170 °C with a total weight loss of 24.7% (calcd. 24.6%). The second event occurs above 270 °C with the decomposition of ligands and collapse of the framework.

3.6. Photoluminescence properties

Coordination polymers with d10 metal centers and conjugated organic linkers are considered as a promising option for photoactive materials with potential applications such as chemical sensors and photochemistry [15]. With this regard, the solid state photoluminescence properties of the compounds 1–3 and H2L ligand were investigated at room temperature (Fig. S8). The emission of free H2L at 468 nm (λex = 414 nm) and compounds 1–3 display intense emissions band at about 435, 444 and 445 nm (λex = 337 nm), respectively (Fig. 8). Since Cd(II) and Zn(II) ions are difficult to oxidize or reduce due to their d10 electronic configurations, the photoluminescence emission bands of 1–3 are neither metal-to-ligand charge transitions (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transitions (LMCT) in nature and can probably be ascribed to the metal-perturbed intraligand (π–π∗) transitions because similar emissions are observed at 468 nm for the free H2L ligand [16]. Additionally, in comparison to the free H2L, the emission peaks of 1–3 show blue shifts which may result from the effect of metal–ligand coordination interactions [17]. Presumably, the differences in emissions for 1 and 2 are probably due to the different coordination environments around the central Cd(II) ions [18]. The results suggest that the diverse structures have subtle but necessary effect on the photoluminescence signals.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, four new compounds {[Cd2(L)2·H2O]·4DMF·14H2O}n (1), {[Cd (L)]·2DMF·6H2O}n (2), {[Zn2(L)2]·4DMF·5H2O}n (3) and {[Cu (L)]·2DMF}n (4) have been successfully prepared under solvothermal conditions based on a new triazole- and carboxylic-containing bifunctional ligand H2L. These compounds display interesting and versatile 3D coordination features. Interestingly, two Cd(II) complexes with markedly different structures were obtained with same reactants and temperatures but altering the solvent and the quantity of the acid. Furthermore, compounds 1–3 display strong fluorescent emissions in the solid state at room temperature, which may be good blue light-emitting materials. The investigations in this paper suggest that the bifunctional ligand H2L is a good candidate for rational design and synthesis of MOFs.
