    Abstract

    Four novel zinc(II) metal-organic frameworks, {[Zn7(trz)6(1,3-bdc)4(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1), {[Zn7(trz)6(hip)4(H2O)2]·8H2O}n (2), [Zn4(trz)4(Hnip)2(nip)]n (3), and [Zn5(trz)2(Hbtc)4(H2O)4]n (4) (Htrz = 1,2,4-triazole, 1,3-H2bdc = 1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid, H2hip = 5-hydroxyisophthalic acid, H2nip = 5-nitroisophthalic acid, and H3btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid), have been hydrothermally synthesized by reaction of Zn salt and Htrz with four different aromatic acids, respectively. Their structures were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and further characterized by X-ray powder diffraction, elemental analyses, IR spectra, and TG analyses. The structures of compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural and feature the 3D structure containing multidimensional intersecting ring channels formed by the interconnection of 2D layers {[Zn7(trz)6]8+}n with 1,3-bdc (1) or hip (2). Compound 3 consists of {[Zn4(trz)4]4+}n layers with (4, 82) topology arranged in the −ABAB− stacking sequence, which are connected by nip pillars to generate a novel 3D open framework. Compound 4 is also in a 3D structure containing 2D layer motifs, which are further linked by btc ligands to complete the structure. There are two different building units [Zn2(trz)2]2+ and [ZnO6] within the 2D layers of 4. All of the compounds 1−4 exhibit the 3D pillared-layer structures. In the 2D layer motifs of these four compounds, trz ligands connect Zn ions in different coordination modes, which reflect the tendency of the trz ligand to bridge multiple metal sites, and the aromatic carboxylate ligands in compounds 1−4 serve as the pillar between the 2D layers, creating the 3D open frameworks. Moreover, these four compounds exhibit emission properties at room temperature in the solid state.

    Synopsis

    Four novel zinc(II) metal-organic frameworks have been hydrothermally synthesized by reaction of Zn salt and Htrz with four different aromatic acids. Their structures were determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and further characterized by X-ray powder diffraction, elemental analyses, IR spectra, and TG analyses. All of the compounds 1−4 exhibit 3D pillared-layer structures.

    The construction of novel metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) with polynuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs)(1) or with pillared-layer structures(2) is currently attracting significant attention due to their intriguing structural diversities and potential applications as functional materials. Thus, a series of high-dimensional MOFs with di-, tetra-, and even octanuclear zinc(II) clusters as SBUs have been rationally designed and constructed, which exhibit highly compositional stability, good gas storage, and photoluminescent properties.(3) Polyazaheteroaromatic compounds such as pyrazole, imidazole, triazole, and tetrazole have been exploited in the construction of MOFs with multiple metal sites.(4) Triazole (Htrz), specifically, is an attractive ligand that can bridge metal ions to afford polynuclear compounds with diverse bridging fashions (μ1,2, μ2,4, and μ1,2,4) in the neutral and anionic ligand forms, respectively. Up to now, a new family of two-component metal/trz compounds, such as transition metal (Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Cd, Ag, and Hg) triazoles,(5) main group metal (Pb) triazoles, and lanthanide (Yb and Eu) triazoles,(6) has displayed a range of structural motifs. By inspection of the structures, it is found that the 2D layers are the more common structure motifs as compared with trinuclear and tetranuclear clusters, adamantoid cages, chains, and layers, etc.
    Meanwhile, being an important subclass of porous materials, the research on pillared-layer structures becomes very attractive for their merits, such as well-defined pores and high stability by controlling the porous structures and the chemical functionality through simple modification of the pillar module.(7) According to the literature, one of the most rational methods to construct MOFs with pillared-layer structures is to apply rigid carboxylate ligands to bridge 2D layers formed by metal ions and amines.(8, 9) The rigid carboxylate ligands also have a great ability to adopt different bonding modes and satisfy many metal coordination preferences, which enrich the structural complexity of MOFs with pillared-layer structures.(10)
    Because of the inner characters of the trz and rigid carboxylate ligands, introducing rigid carboxylate ligands to the metal/trz system may be a feasible way in constructing 3D pillared-layer structures with polynuclear metal clusters. In our strategy, the rigid carboxylate ligands, that is, the 1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (1,3-H2bdc), two derivatives of 1,3-H2bdc [5-hydroxyisophthalic acid (H2hip) and 5-nitroisophthalic acid (H2nip)], and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3btc) were chosen as the starting materials together with Zn salt and Htrz ligand. Utilizing the hydrothermal technique, a series of fluorescent 3D pillared MOFs were synthesized successfully, namely, {[Zn7(trz)6(1,3-bdc)4(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1), {[Zn7(trz)6(hip)4(H2O)2]·8H2O}n (2), [Zn4(trz)4(Hnip)2(nip)]n (3), and [Zn5(trz)2(Hbtc)4(H2O)4]n (4). The Zn centers and trz ligands in all of these compounds play an important role in constructing the infinite 2D layers with different structural motifs, and the aromatic carboxylate ligands serve as the pillar between the layers. The four compounds are characterized by polynuclear metal clusters as different building blocks {[Zn7(trz)6]8+, 1 and 2; [Zn2(trz)2]2+, 3; [Zn2(trz)2]2+ and [ZnO6], 4} within the 2D layer motifs. Discussion of their syntheses and crystal structures is addressed below.

    2.1 Synthesis

    2.1.1 {[Zn7(trz)6(1,3-bdc)4(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1)

    A mixture of 1,3-H2bdc (0.042 g, 0.25 mmol), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.074 g, 0.25 mmol), Htrz (0.035 g, 0.50 mmol), and water (6 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (23 mL), and then, the pH value was adjusted to 6.5 by NaOH (0.5 M). The mixture was sealed and heated at 160 °C for 3 days; then, the reaction system was cooled to room temperature. Colorless block crystals were collected in 81% yield (0.046 g, based on Zn). Anal. calcd for C44H36Zn7N18O20: C, 33.14; H, 2.28; N, 15.81. Found: C, 32.84; H, 2.18; N, 15.91%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet): ν = 3386, 3111, 1701, 1610, 1545, 1441, 1390, 1097, 1005, 899, 471 cm−1.

    2.1.2 {[Zn7(trz)6(hip)4(H2O)2]·8H2O}n (2)

    The process was similar to 1 except that 1,3-H2bdc was replaced by H2hip (0.045 g, 0.25 mmol), and the pH value was adjusted to 8.4 by NaOH (0.5 M). Colorless block crystals were collected in 60% yield (0.038 g, based on Zn). Anal. calcd for C44H48Zn7N18O30: C, 30.09; H, 2.75; N, 14.35. Found: C, 29.23; H, 2.65; N, 14.19%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet): ν = 3381, 3132, 1618, 1570, 1516, 1417, 1379, 1097, 1003, 897, 474 cm−1.

    2.1.3 [Zn4(trz)4(Hnip)2(nip)]n (3)

    The process was similar to 1 except that 1,3-H2bdc was replaced by H2nip (0.106 g, 0.50mmol), and the pH value was adjusted to 4.8 by NaOH (0.5 M). Colorless block crystals were collected in 77% yield (0.056 g, based on Zn). Anal. calcd for C32H19Zn4N15O18: C, 33.04; H, 1.65; N, 18.06. Found: C, 32.67; H, 1.58; N, 17.82%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet): ν = 3459, 3111, 2619, 1705, 1624, 1531, 1384, 1348, 1178, 1097, 733, 515 cm−1.

    2.1.4 [Zn5(trz)2(Hbtc)4(H2O)4]n (4)

    The process was similar to 1 except that 1,3-H2bdc was replaced by H3btc (0.053 g, 0.25mmol), and the pH value was adjusted to 5.4 by NaOH (0.5 M). Colorless block crystals were collected in 17% yield (0.012 g, based on Zn). Anal. calcd for C40H28Zn5N6O28: C, 35.12; H, 2.08; N, 6.15. Found: C, 34.82; H, 1.99; N, 6.23%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet): ν = 3462, 3014, 1711, 1618, 1554, 1444, 1360, 1228, 1178, 1091, 758, 677, 546, 418 cm−1.

    2.2 Materials and Measurements

    All reagents were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were obtained using SHIMADAZU XRD-6000 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418Å), with the step size and the count time of 0.02° and 4 s, respectively. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Impact 410 spectrometer between 400 and 4000 cm−1, using the KBr pellet method. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer TGA 7 thermogravimetric analyzer in atmospheric environment at a heating rate of 20 °C/min up to 800 °C. Photoluminescence spectra were collected on a Shimadzu RF-5301PC spectrophotometer.

    2.3 X-ray Crystallography

    Crystallographic data for 1−4 were collected on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID diffractometer using graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature with ω-scan mode. No significant decay was observed during the data collection. Empirical absorption correction was applied for all data. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares using SHELXTL-97.(11) All nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. H atoms were placed geometrically and located based on theoretically riding model.     3.1 Description of Crystal Structure

    3.1.1 {[Zn7(trz)6(1,3-bdc)4(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1)

    The structure of 1 is a unique 3D framework in which the asymmetric unit contains 3.5 zinc atoms, two 1,3-bdc anions, three trz anions, one coordinated water molecule, and one free water molecule. The Zn1 (half-occupied) is octahedrally coordinated to two oxygen atoms (O1, O1A) from two equivalent 1,3-bdc anions and two nitrogen atoms (N1, N1A) from symmetrically equivalent trz molecules in the equatorial positions, whereas two oxygen atoms (O1W, O1WA) from two symmetry-related water molecules are in the apical positions. The Zn2 atom is trigonal bipyramidally coordinated by two oxygen atoms (O3, O5) of two 1,3-bdc anions and three nitrogen atoms (N3, N4, and N9) of three trz ligands, in which the O3 and O5 atoms occupy the axial positions. Both Zn3 and Zn4 atoms lie in tetrahedral coordination. The Zn3 atom is bonded to one oxygen atom (O7) from 1,3-bdc anion and three nitrogen atoms (N6, N7, and N8) from three different trz ligands. The Zn4 atom is ligated by two oxygen atoms (O2, O6) from two 1,3-bdc anions and two nitrogen atoms (N2, N5) from two trz ligands. The Zn−O and Zn−N bond distances are consistent with the previously reported values, ranging from 1.945 to 2.440 Å and from 1.986 to 2.116 Å for Zn−O and Zn−N, respectively.(12) It is can be observed that the Zn metal sites in 1 exhibit a variety of coordination polyhedra: tetrahedron, trigonal bipyramid, and octahedron (Figure S1 of the Supporting Information).
    The trz of 1 has displayed μ1,2,4 bridging fashion in the anionic form. As a three-connected node, trz ligands link four crystallographically independent zinc(II) centers to form the building block [Zn7(trz)6]8+ (Figure 1a), which has distinct differences from common building units in other related materials containing trz.(5, 6, 9) Through the linkage of trz in the [Zn7(trz)6]8+, four different subrings, A, B, C, and D (Figure 1a), with 15-, 14-, 16-, 6-membered rings, are produced, respectively. The infinite 2D layer {[Zn7(trz)6]8+}n can also be viewed as being built up from the interlocking motifs [A + B + C + D] through sharing the Zn atoms and trz ligands. The 3D framework of 1 is generated from the connection of the {[Zn7(trz)6]8+}n layers pillared by 1,3-bdc ligand along the b-axis (Figure 1b). All of the 1,3-bdc ligands in 1 act as a μ3−bridge, linking three Zn centers by a monodentate carboxylate and a bridging carboxylate group.
    Interestingly, compound 1 displays intersecting ring channels as viewed from b and c directions (Figure 1c,d). Even after interpenetration, the subring C of {[Zn7(trz)6]8+}n layers still presents rhombic-shaped channels along the b-axis, and the channel size is approximately 4.8 × 5.1 Å2. If 1 is viewed from the c-axis, a larger channel can be seen with cross dimensions of about 5.6 × 6.3 Å2. The wall of the channel is formed by Zn centers with trz and 1,3-bdc ligands. The O atoms (O2w) of lattice water only form weak hydrogen bonds (C12−H12···O5: 3.581 Å, 150.2°) with the C−H group of 1,3-bdc. The total solvent accessible volume of the channel in 1 is estimated to be 456.14 Å3 (28.83%), which shows that 1 is moderately porous. It may possess interesting sorption or exchange characteristics when it is calcined.

    3.1.2 {[Zn7(trz)6(hip)4(H2O)2]·8H2O}n (2)

    Compound 2 is isostructural with 1. There are also some differences in structures of these two compounds. Because of the steric hindrance of hydroxyl group (−OH) in hip ligand, 2 inclines to form framework with higher condensation and density than those of 1. For 2, the channel size formed by {[Zn7(trz)6]8+}n is approximately 4.6 × 5.0 Å2 along the b-axis, and the channel size is about 5.1 × 5.3 Å2 viewed from the c-axis. Both of the two types of channels are smaller than those of 1. The potential solvent-accessible volume in 2 is estimated to be 426.1 Å3 (26.72%), which is slightly less than that of 1. The density of 2 (1.818 Mg/m3) is also larger than that of 1 (1.665 Mg/m3).
    As the hydroxyl group (−OH) in hip ligands serve as a good hydrogen bond donor (O5−H5A···O2w: 2.742 Å, 172.8°; O10−H10A···O3w: 2.742 Å, 131.7°), the hydrogen bond system in 2 is more complicated and stronger than that of 1. Both of the two types of channels in 2 are occupied by free water molecules.

    3.1.3 [Zn4(trz)4(Hnip)2(nip)]n (3)

    Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis has revealed that 3 is also assembled in a 3D framework with two zinc(II) atoms, one protonated nip, one-half nip anions, and two trz anions in each asymmetric unit. The two crystallographically nonequivalent zinc atoms have exhibited two different coordination geometries. Zn1 is trigonal bipyramidally coordinated by two oxygen atoms (O1, O2) from one chelating carboxylate group of a protonated nip and one nitrogen atom (N2) of the trz ligand in the equatorial plane. In the axial positions, it is coordinated to two nitrogen atoms (N1, N6) of two trz ligands. The Zn2 atom is tetrahedrally surrounded by one oxygen atom (O7) from nip anion and three nitrogen atoms (N3, N4, and N5) of two trz ligands (Figure S2 of the Supporting Information). The average bond lengths around Zn2 (1.980 Å) are somewhat shorter than those of Zn1 (2.080 Å), and this is consistent with the fact that bond lengths in a tetrahedral geometry are generally shorter than those in other geometries.
    Two pairs of symmetry-related zinc atoms are linked by two triply bridging trz ligands to generate the basic structural building unit [Zn2(trz)2]2+, and the adjacent [Zn2(trz)2]2+ clusters are then linked by trz molecules to form a 2D {[Zn4(trz)4]4+}n layers (Figure 2a) with (4, 82) topology in the bc plane (Figure S4 of the Supporting Information). The 2D layers give an −ABAB− stacking sequence along the a direction. These adjacent layers are further connected by the bridging and terminal nip ligands with dihedral angles of 22.6° to generate an infinite 3D framework (Figure 2b,c). In detail, the bridging nip anions connect the symmetry-related Zn2 atoms with bis (monodentate) mood, while the terminal nip ligands link the Zn1 atoms through chelating carboxylate groups. The terminal nip ligands are protonated, which is in agreement with the IR data (strong absorption around 1705 cm−1 for −COOH). Compound 3 displays the porosity with 21.72% free area, which can be filled by potential solvents.

    3.1.4 [Zn5(trz)2(Hbtc)4(H2O)4]n (4)

    Similar to compounds 1−3, 4 is an extended 3D pillared-layer framework. In the asymmetric unit, there are 2.5 crystallographically unique zinc(II) atoms, two protonated btc, one trz anion, and two coordinated water molecules. Just as 1, the metal centers in 4 also display distinct coordination geometry. The Zn1 (half-occupied) is octahedrally coordinated to four oxygen atoms (O1, O1A, O2W, and O2WA) from two equivalent Hbtc anions and two symmetrically equivalent water molecules in the equatorial positions, whereas two oxygen atoms (O1W, O1WA) from two symmetry-related water molecules are in the apical positions. The Zn1 atom here shows a novel and unprecedented coordination mode: Six oxygen atoms bond to one metal center, displaying a complete oxygen coordination sphere, which has not been seen in other related coordination polymers containing trz ligands.(5, 6, 9) The Zn2 atom is trigonal bipyramidally coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O3, O4, and O7) of two Hbtc anions and two nitrogen atoms (N1, N2) of two symmetrically equivalent trz ligands, in which the O4 and N2 atoms occupy the axial positions. The Zn3 atom is tetrahedrally surrounded by three oxygen atoms (O2, O8, and O9) from two Hbtc anions and one nitrogen atom (N3) of trz ligand (Figure S3 of the Supporting Information). The Zn−O (1.917−2.268 Å) and Zn−N (2.023−2.028 Å) bond distances are both within acceptable ranges.(12)
    The pairs of symmetry-related Zn2 atoms in 4 are linked by two symmetrically equivalent trz ligands through the N1 and N2 donor groups, generating the basic structural building unit [Zn2(trz)2]2+, while the third N3 donors link to Zn3 sites. At the same time, the carboxylates in Hbtc anions bridge [ZnO6] polyhedra and Zn3 atoms in bis (monodentate) mode to establish the 2D connectivity (Figure 3a). For 4, two carboxylate groups from two types of btc ligands bridge different Zn atoms, while the third carboxylate group is uncoordinated. In one type, the ligand contains one bridging carboxylate group and one chelating bidentate carboxylate group. In the other type, the ligand contains one bridging carboxylate group and one monodentate carboxylate group. The IR spectrum of 4 exhibits a strong absorption peak around 1711 cm−1, which can be attributed to the vibrations of the −COOH. This shows that deprotonation of the H3btc ligands is incomplete, which is in good agreement with the uncoordinated carboxylate groups in the structure of 4. The two types of btc ligands have dihedral angles of 10.8° and further link the adjacent 2D nets into an infinite 3D framework (Figure 3b). The whole structure is dense, and the resulting solvent-accessible cavities are estimated to be only 1.25% of the unit cell volume, and there are no channels that can be observed in 4.

    3.2 Summary of the Structures

    Hydrothermal reactions of Zn salts and Htrz with four different aromatic carboxylate ligands have yielded a series of novel 3D polynuclear frameworks. To date, most of the coordination polymers with trz are simple two-component metal-organic compounds,(5, 6) while seldom study is made in the trz-carboxylate mixed ligands system. To our knowledge, a few structures containing trz and carboxylate have been reported in recent work by Park and his co-workers.(9) Most of their compounds consist of either 2D {[Zn4(trz)4]4+}n layers pillared by linear carboxylate ligands or 1D chain motifs made of alternating Zn ions and trz ligands, which are quite different from the structures of our compounds 1−4. In comparison to some reported polymeric architectures containing substituted triazole ligands,(13) the different coordination modes of trz and these ligand lead to different structures. It is also noteworthy that four new 3D MOFs have been successfully synthesized by incorporating binary aromatic/aliphatic acid as coligands in the Zn(II)/BTA system (BTAH = benzotriazole), in which both the organic ligand and the synthetic method are similar with compounds 1−4.(14) Structural analysis reveals that their structural diversity is highly related to the spacers of dicarboxylate ligands, which can give inspiration to our current work. The synthesis and single crystal studies of 1−4 enhance the structural and compositional complexity of the metal/trz/carboxylate system.
    Compounds 1−4 reflect the structural diversity in the variety of coordination polyhedra through the multiple Zn(II) centers: tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, and octahedral in compounds 1, 2, and 4 and tetrahedral and trigonal bipyramidal in compound 3. These four compounds are also characterized by a range of building blocks: [Zn7(trz)6]8+ for 1 and 2, [Zn2(trz)2]2+ for 3, [Zn2(trz)2]2+, and octahedron [ZnO6] for 4, and the arrangement of these building blocks can generate different 2D layer motifs (Figures 1−3). All of these final 3D open-framework in compounds 1−4 can be viewed as the 2D layers pillared by carboxylate ligands (Figure 4).
    As a bridging ligand between metal centers, the trz in compounds 1−4 exhibits a strong and typical property in the μ1,2,4 fashions. The carboxylate ligands bind and bridge metal centers in four types of coordinated modes, for example, bridging bidentate and monodentate μ3− mode, bis (monodentate) μ2− mode, chelating terminal, and bridging and chelating bis(bidentate) μ3− mode (Scheme 1). The hip ligand shows one type of coordinated mode in compounds 1 and 2, while the nip and btc ligands display two different coordinated modes in compounds 3 and 4. The different coordination modes of carboxylate ligands enrich the structural complexity of the Zn/trz/carboxylate system.
    There also exist some distinct features in the frameworks of compounds 1−4. Compound 1 has intersecting ring channels as viewed from b and c directions. Although compounds 1 and 2 are isostructural, there are some differences in some aspects, such as the dimensions of the channels, hydrogen bond system, and the density of the crystal, etc. Compound 3 has the 2D {[Zn4(trz)4]4+}n layers a (4, 82) topological network held together in the −ABAB− stacking sequence along a direction, and these adjacent layers are further linked by nip ligands to generate a 3D framework. The 2D layers in 4 are built up by octahedra [ZnO6] and building blocks [Zn2(trz)2]2+, which are quite different from two-component Zn-trz layers in compounds 1−3. In summary, compound 1 displays the most porosity among the four compounds in this work (the solvent accessible volume accounts for the total cell volume: 1, 28.83%; 2, 26.72%; 3, 21.72%; and 4, 1.25%). As the increase in steric hindrance of coexistence groups (−OH, 2; −NO2, 3; and −COOH, 4), compounds 1−4 show the tendency to form the dense structures.

    3.3 Spectral and Thermal Characterizations

    The powder X-ray diffraction patterns of compounds 1−4 are shown in Figures S5−S8 of the Supporting Information with the pattern simulated on the basis of single crystal structures. The positions of the diffraction peaks in both patterns correspond well, which indicate that the as-synthesized samples 1−4 are pure. For 1 and 3, the differences in reflection intensities between the simulated and the experimental patterns are due to the variation in the crystal orientation of the powder sample.
    The IR spectra display characteristic absorption band for water molecules, carboxylate, and trz ligands. Compounds 1, 2, and 4 show strong and broad peaks around 3400 cm−1, which indicate the present of water molecules. For all compounds 1−4, the absorption bands in the frequency range 1340−1630 cm−1 correspond to νas(C−O) and νsym(C−O) of the carboxylate groups. The bands in the 3010−3135 cm−1 range and 1170−1300 cm−1 range are related to ν(C−H) and ν(C−N) or ν(N−N) of the trz ligand, respectively.
    TGA was performed to gauge the thermal stability of compound 2. There are two separate weight loss steps in the TGA curse. The first weight loss of 7.80% (calcd 8.20%) occurs at about 100 °C, which is attributed to the loss of free water molecules. The second weight loss of 60.80% between 280 and 550 °C corresponds to the loss of hip and trz ligands. The remaining weight of 31.40% corresponds to the percentage (calcd 32.44%) of the Zn and O components, indicating that the final product is ZnO (Figure S9 of the Supporting Information).

    3.4 Fluorescent Properties

    The emission spectra of compounds 1−4 in the solid state at room temperature are shown in Figure 5. It can be observed that intense emissions occur at 402 and 466 nm (λex = 360 nm) for 1, 410 and 466 nm (λex = 310 nm) for 2, 418 and 467 nm (λex = 360 nm) for 3, and 403 and 468 nm (λex = 380 nm) for 4, respectively. As previously reported, the solid aromatic carboxylate ligands (1,3-H2bdc, H2hip, H2nip, and H3btc) are nearly nonfluorescent in the range 400−600 nm at ambient temperature,(15) and the free Htrz shows an emission band at 421 nm (λex = 318 nm).(5c) Therefore, the emission bands for 1−4 may be due to σ-donation from the cooperation of trz and carboxylate ligands to the Zn center and are tentatively attributed to the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).(5h, 16) The luminescence properties of these metal-organic compounds indicate that they may be candidates for potential photoactive materials.

    In summary, four novel Zn(II) MOFs have been successfully synthesized and fully structurally characterized in the Zn/triazolate/carboxylate system. By appropriate choice of building units for the 2D layers and pillars, a rational synthetic strategy for 3D pillared structures has been developed. The construction of 2D layer motifs may be viewed as the assembly of different polynuclear metal clusters ([Zn7(trz)6]8+ for 1 and 2, [Zn2(trz)2]2+ for 3, [Zn2(trz)2]2+, and octahedron [ZnO6] for 4), which are highly related to the structural diversity of 1−4. The rigid aromatic carboxylate ligands are served as the pillars, which connect the adjacent 2D layer motifs through the M−O bonds. The successful isolation of compounds 1−4 provides not only new types of pillared-layer structures but also a bridge between pillared-layer and MOFs with polynuclear metal clusters.
    Moreover, the synthesis, single-crystal studies, and spectroscopic characterizations of 1−4 reflect the rich structural chemistry of the metal/trz/carboxylate system. By varying time, temperature, stoichiometries of reactants, pH, nucleation, and other reaction conditions, a variety of MOFs with unusual, unprecedented, and inconceivable structures could be designed and synthesized.

        Metal nuclearity modulated four-, six-, and eight-connected entangled frameworks based on mono-, bi-, and trimetallic cores as nodes
        Wang, Xin-Long; Qin, Chao; Wang, En-Bo; Su, Zhong-Min
        Chemistry--A European Journal (2006), 12 (10), 2680-2691CODEN: CEUJED; ISSN:0947-6539. (Wiley-VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA)
        To study the relation between network connectivity and metal nuclearity, the authors designed and synthesized three-dimensional (3D) entangled coordination frameworks based on different metal cores, [Zn2(bdc)2(L)2]·2H2O (1), [Zn(bdc)(L)0.5] (2), [Zn(oba)(L)0.5] (3) and [Cd3(bdc)3(L)2(H2O)2] (4) by self-assembly of d10 metal salts with the flexible long-chain ligand 1,4-bis(1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)butane (L), and with the rigid and nonrigid arom. dicarboxylate ligands 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (bdc) and 4,4'-oxybis(benzoate) (oba). Compd. 1 exhibits a 3-fold interpenetrated diamondoid array typically based on a tetrahedral 2nd building unit (SBU) at a single Zn center. Compd. 2 adopts a 3-fold interpenetrated α-Po-type network that is built from bimetallic cores as six-connected vertexes. The structure of 3 also consists of dinuclear units comprising a novel (3,4)-connected 3-fold interpenetrated net with complex (4.6 × 10)-(4.62 × 103) topol. when single Zn centers act as four-connected nodes (or the α-Po topol. if dinuclear units are considered as six-connected nodes). 4, Derived from a cross-linked 5-fold interpenetrated diamond-like substructure, is an unusual example of a self-penetrating coordination framework displaying an unprecedented eight-connected 42068 topol. with trinuclear Cd clusters as eight-connected nodes which, to the authors' knowledge, not only defines a new topol. for eight-connected coordination networks, but also represents the highest connected topol. presently known for self-penetrating systems. Detailed structural comparison of these complexes indicates that the increase in metal nuclearity induces the progressive increase in the connectivities of the ultimate nets: i.e., the metal nuclearity plays a significant role in tuning the connectivity of a specific network. The thermal and luminescent properties of these compds. are discussed.
