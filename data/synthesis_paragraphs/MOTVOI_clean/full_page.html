Guest-induced reversible structural transitions and concomitant on/off luminescence switching of an Eu(III) metal–organic framework and its application in detecting picric acid†

First published on 16th January 2015

We report a flexible metal–organic framework (MOF), (Me2NH2)3[Eu3(MHFDA)4(NO3)4(DMF)2]·4H2O·2MeCN (1) (H2MHFDA = 9-methyl-9-hydroxy-fluorene-2,7-dicarboxylic acid), which was synthesized by a hydrothermal method and structurally characterized. This MOF has a three-dimensional framework structure with one-dimensional rhombic channels and the framework can be reversibly transformed between two phases upon release/uptake of the guest molecules. The vacuum drying process is accompanied by the loss of luminescence of the MOF, while the luminescence is recovered upon resoaking in DMF. The potential of the material for PA sensing was studied in DMF through the luminescence quenching experiments, which elucidate that this material is a potential turn-off luminescent sensory material for the selective detection of PA.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), crystalline materials constructed from metal ions and organic ligands, have been an active research area since the beginning of this century because of their functional properties and potential applications in materials science. For example, the MOFs have potential in applications such as guest sensing and recognition because they can have a luminescent response to guest absorption and exchange.1 The luminescence properties of MOFs are very sensitive to the environment around the chromophores, just like luminescence of Eu3+ is a very sensitive probe of its coordination sphere. Therefore, luminescent frameworks, particularly lanthanide-based luminescent frameworks, have been the most widely explored type of MOF sensors to date, and have been used as sensors for the detection of cations and anions,2 gases and vapors,3 pH value4 and temperature.5 In addition, it has been generally assumed that open metal sites in frameworks will play a crucial role in molecular recognition processes owing to their ability for highly selective and specific molecular transformations,6 transport,7 and storage.8 In recent years, guest-sensitive properties, such as the guest-dependent luminescence of lanthanide polymers, have also become an interesting focus. However, few examples of guest-dependent luminescence properties of lanthanide metal ions and luminescent response to guest molecules/anions in MOFs with structural dynamism have been reported,9 which have a certain value for the research and application of guest sensing and recognition.

Picric acid, 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (PA), is a common ingredient of industrial explosives which even has superior explosive power than its well-known counterpart 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT) at picomolar concentrations. During commercial production and use in dyes, pharmaceuticals, fireworks, glass, and leather industries, PA is released into the environment, leading to the contamination of soil and aquatic systems. Therefore, the reliable and accurate detection of PA has great significance in homeland security and environmental protection.10 However, only a few MOF materials have been synthesized, and are used in the detection of PA.11

Herein, based on 9-methyl-9-hydroxy-fluorene-2,7-dicarboxylic acid (H2MHFDA), a highly efficient Eu-based luminescent MOF (Me2NH2)3[Eu3(MHFDA)4(NO3)4(DMF)2]·4H2O·2MeCN has been prepared and structurally characterized. Although a number of Ln-MOFs have been documented, the investigations on the relationship between the crystal structures and the photoluminescence properties were relatively few. For this purpose, the structural characterization and luminescence studies of complexes 1, 1a and 1b were carried out. The results indicated that the materials exhibited framework dynamics upon several cycles of vacuum drying and resoaking in DMF solvent and a reversible large change in their luminescence intensities. The ability of 1b for PA sensing in DMF was also investigated through the luminescence quenching experiments. 1b shows efficient luminescence quenching phenomena for nitro compounds,12 especially for PA and it is a potential luminescent sensory material for nitro explosives.

Experimental section

Syntheses

All chemicals were of reagent grade and used as received. Elemental analyses for C, N and H were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Vector22 Bruker spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 regions. The powder XRD patterns were recorded on a Bruker D8-advance X-ray diffractometer equipped with Cu-Kα radiation. TGA (thermal gravimetric analysis) was collected on a Shimadzu DTG-60 under 15 mL min−1 nitrogen gas flow and at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C. Time-resolved fluorescence measurements were performed using a Life-Spec-ps fluorescence lifetime analytical spectrometer (Edinburgh Instruments F900). The absolute quantum yield data were collected on solid samples mounted on quartz slides. The samples were placed within an integrating sphere and excited at 340 nm. Photoluminescence spectra were recorded using a RF-5301PC spectrofluorophotometer. 1H and 13C NMR spectra of samples in d6-DMSO were recorded at room temperature on a Bruker 400 MHz NMR spectrometer.

Synthesis of H2MHFDA

2,7-Dibromo-9-fluorenone (2.03 g, 6 mmol) was dissolved in 220 mL of dry THF, and the solution was cooled to −78 °C. Methyl lithium (7.5 mL of 12 mmol solution) was added to the solution over a period of 10 min. One hour later, t-butyl lithium (24 mL of 31.2 mmol solution) was added over a period of 30 min. Two hours later, the large excess of dry CO2 was introduced over a period of 1.5 h and then the cooling bath was removed. The suspension was stirred for 1 h at room temperature and then evaporated to dryness by heating under reduced pressure. The obtained solid was dissolved in water (90 mL), and dichloromethane (20 mL) and concentrated hydrochloric acid (4.0 mL) were added under agitation to produce a pink solid precipitate, which was filtered off, washed with hot water, and dried at 40 °C for 12 h to give 9-methyl-9-hydroxy-fluorene-2,7-dicarboxylic acid (1.52 g). 1H NMR (400 MHz, DMSO): δ = 8.12–8.08 (m, 2H), 8.01–7.97 (m, 2H), 7.96–7.92 (m, 2H), 5.88 (s, 1H), 1.60 (d, J = 14.1 Hz, 3H) (Fig. S1, ESI†). 13C NMR (101 MHz, DMSO): δ = 167.31, 152.12, 141.62, 131.01, 130.22, 124.37, 121.07, 78.04, 25.20 (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Synthesis of (Me2NH2)3[Eu3(MHFDA)4(NO3)4(DMF)2]·4H2O·2MeCN (1)

A mixture of H2MHFDA (0.1 mmol, 28.4 mg), Eu(NO3)3·6H2O (0.1 mmol, 44.7 mg), DMF (5 mL), EtOH (2 mL), MeCN (1 mL) and H2O (1 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined bomb and heated at 80 °C for 3 days. The reaction mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature. Yellowish diamond-like crystals of 1 suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 74% yield. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C80H92Eu3N11O38: C, 42.30; N, 6.78; H, 4.08%. Found: C, 42.52; N, 7.06; H, 4.46%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 542 w, 673 w, 783 s, 1109 w, 1388 vs, 1429 s, 1553 s, 1589 s, 1658 s, 2927 w, 3368 m.

Synthesis of Eu3(H0.75MHFDA)4(NO3)4 (1a170)

1 was heated at 100–170 °C in a vacuum drying oven for 24 hours to obtain 1a, in which 1a170 is the 170 °C vacuum dried sample and is the fully desolvated product of 1. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C64H43Eu3N4O32: C, 41.87; N, 3.05; H, 2.36%. Found: C, 41.56; N, 2.91; H, 2.54%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 541 w, 673 w, 783 s, 1110 w, 1388 vs, 1427 s, 1549 s, 1588 s, 1658 s, 2927 w, 3370 m.

Synthesis of Eu3(H0.75MHFDA)4(NO3)4·5.5DMF (1b)

1b was obtained by soaking 1a170 in DMF. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C80.5H81.5Eu3N9.5O37.5: C, 43.20; N, 5.95; H, 3.67%. Found: C, 43.51; N, 5.78; H, 3.46%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 542 w, 673 w, 785 s, 1109 w, 1385 vs, 1428 s, 1549 s, 1589 s, 1658 s, 2927 w, 3375 m.

X-ray crystallography

The crystal structure of complex 1 was determined on a Siemens (Bruker) SMART CCD diffractometer using monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 173 K. Cell parameters were retrieved using SMART software and refined using SAINT13 on all observed reflections. Data were collected using a narrow-frame method with scan widths of 0.30° in ω. The highly redundant data sets were reduced using SAINT13 and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. Absorption corrections were applied using SADABS14 supplied by Bruker. The structure was solved by a direct method using the program SHELXS-97 and refined on F2 by applying the full-matrix least-squares method implemented in SHELXL-97.15 The positions of the metal atoms and their first coordination spheres were located from direct-method E maps; other non-hydrogen atoms were found using alternating difference Fourier syntheses and least-squared refinement cycles and, during the final cycles, were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a uniform value of Uiso. Crystallographic details are summarized in Table 1. CCDC reference number for 1 is 1017739.

Results and discussion

Complex 1 was obtained by the hydrothermal reaction of H2MHFDA and Eu(NO3)3·6H2O in the mixed solvents of DMF, EtOH, MeCN and H2O. 1 is a flexible Eu-based MOF, showing the reversible structural and optical conversions. The potential of material for PA sensing was also studied. The molecular formulas of 1a170 and 1b were confirmed by thermal gravimetric analysis and elemental analysis.

Structure of (Me2NH2)3[Eu3(MHFDA)4(NO3)4(DMF)2]·4H2O·2MeCN (1)

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half europium(III) ions, two MHFDA ligands, two nitrate ions, one coordinated DMF molecule, two lattice water molecules, one acetonitrile molecule, and one and a half protonated dimethylamine cations, which should be obtained in situ during the solvothermal reaction due to the decomposition of DMF (Fig. 1). The Eu1 ion is eight-coordinated by seven oxygen atoms from six MHFDA ligands and one oxygen atom from one nitrate ion. The Eu2 ion is also eight-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four MHFDA ligands, two oxygen atoms from two nitrate ions and two oxygen atoms from two terminal DMF molecules. Except the Eu1–O2 distance (2.752 Å), the Eu1–O distances are all in the range of 2.317–2.447 Å. For the Eu2 ion, there are 4 short (2.224–2.265 Å) Eu2–OCOO distances and 4 long (2.962–2.986 Å) Eu(2)–ONO3/ODMF distances. It is suggested that there exist strong bonding interactions between Eu and MHFDA. However, the bonding interactions between Eu2 and NO3−/DMF are very weak. The MHFDA ligands adopt two coordination modes in 1. One class of ligands acts as the μ4-bridge to link four Eu ions (Eu1, Eu1vii, Eu1viii and Eu2, symmetry codes: viix − 1/2, −y + 3/2, z + 1/2, viii−x + 1/2, y − 1/2, −z + 3/2) with each carboxylate oxygen atom coordinating to an Eu ion, respectively. Another class of ligands also acts in the μ4-fashion with a carboxylate group chelating an Eu1 center and simultaneously binding to an Eu1i center with a carboxylate oxygen atom. However, each oxygen atom of a carboxylate group bonds with an Eu ion (Eu1ix and Eu2ix, symmetry code: ix−x + 1/2, y + 1/2, −z + 1/2). The Eu ions are connected by the carboxylic groups to produce the 1D parallel aligned Eu–O–C rods extending along the [010] direction. In the Eu–O–C rod, each two Eu1 ions are four-bridged by four carboxylate groups to form a paddle-like dinuclear unit, and then the Eu1 ion is double-bridged to the Eu2 ion by two carboxylate groups. The Eu–O–C rods are linked along the [101] and [01] directions by the 9-methyl-9-hydroxy-9H-fluorene moieties of the MHFDA ligands forming the three-dimensional rod-packing framework (Fig. 2) with rhombic channels running along the [010] direction, with diagonal dimensions of a × c, that is 15.47 Å × 24.04 Å. The interplanar distances for (101) and (01) crystal planes are 12.99 Å and 13.03 Å, respectively. The side length of rhombic channels is about 14.3 Å. The volume of the channels is 2788.0 Å3 per unit cell or 53.0% of the total volume, calculated using the PLATON routine.16 Nevertheless, besides the solvent molecules, they are also occupied by coordinated NO3− and free Me2NH2+.

Structural flexibility and thermal stability

1 is air stable and can retain its structural integrity and keep the framework unchanged at room temperature. While 1 was heated at 100–170 °C in a vacuum drying oven for 24 hours, the change in the framework was observed. After vacuum drying, the strongest powder X-ray diffraction peak corresponding to the (101) and (01) crystal planes of 1 at 2θmax = 6.71° disappears gradually. Simultaneously, a new strongest peak at 2θmax = 8.76–9.39° is observed depending on the temperature (Fig. 3). Between 50 and 100 °C, this peak shifts from 9.02 to 9.39°. However, it appears at 8.76° for the sample after vacuum drying at 170° and the peak of 1 at ca. 6.71° totally disappears. We attempted to obtain the crystal structure of vacuum dried samples, but were not successful due to many cracks appearing in the crystal after vacuum drying. And, only cell parameters were obtained for the 100 °C vacuum dried samples. The cell parameters are a = 10.5191(21) Å, b = 14.3737(15) Å, c = 26.4560(19) Å, α = 90.00°, β = 90.00°, γ = 90.00°, V = 4000.1(10) Å3. It is interesting to compare these cell parameters with 1. From the structural point of view, the framework of 1 is formed by EuO8 polyhedra and MHFDA links, and solvents and cations stay in the pore channels (Fig. S3, ESI†). Along the b direction, EuO8 polyhedra are only bridged by carboxylic groups (Eu–O–C–O–Eu), while along a and c directions, EuO8 polyhedra are connected by long organic ligands (Fig. S3, ESI†). During vacuum drying, solvents and cations were removed, causing the shrinkage of the framework. Clearly, the deviation in the b direction is difficult while in a and c directions it will occur due to the twist of long organic ligands. Furthermore, it is expected that a will shrink due to the parallelogram shape of the channel. Although 1 crystallized in a monoclinic system, β is very close to 90°. Considering that the linkage of the framework is not broken after vacuum drying at 100 °C (see text below), it is therefore expected that both cells may be related. In accordance with this discussion, we find that the a axis is shortened by 4.95 Å (shortening ratio 32%), the b axis almost unchanged, the c axis elongated by 2.42 Å (elongation ratio 10%), and the cell volume shrank by 1261.5 Å3 (shrinking ratio 24%) after vacuum drying at 100 °C. Consequently, the rhombic window of channels becomes long and narrow, the volume of channels reduces, and this narrow-pore form of the framework is designated as 1a. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the axes of both systems are correlated. Analysis of the powder pattern of the sample vacuum dried at 100 °C was further attempted using the Le Bail fit.17 However, the lack of strong peaks makes the analysis difficult. Since the powder pattern is mainly related to EuO8 polyhedra (Fig. S4, ESI†), it is expected that the twisted organic ligand makes only little contribution. To simplify the analysis, the space group of 1 was used and β was simply set to 90°. The obtained results are acceptable although the quality is low (Fig. 4). The pattern of the sample after drying at 100 °C can be fit using cell parameters obtained from single crystal indexing. As for the pattern of the sample dried at 170 °C, only two peaks (2θ 8.76 and 17.57° or d-space 10.07 and 5.03 Å) can be clearly seen, which are clearly related. Upon resoaking 1a in the mother liquid for 12 h, the PXRD pattern nearly coincident with that observed for the as-synthesized 1 was obtained. Furthermore, the whole cycle can be repeated several times without a significant loss of crystallinity of the material. It is suggested that the framework can be reversibly transformed between two phases by induction of the guest.

Fig. 4 Le Bail fit of powder XRD patterns of the sample dried at 100 °C using single crystal indexing parameters (a = 10.5191 Å, b = 14.3737 Å, c = 26.4560 Å, α = 90.00°, β = 90.00°, γ = 90.00°).

Besides the mother liquid, we soaked 1 and 1a in different solvents in order to check the structural transformation of the framework. In MeCN, EtOH, H2O, MeOH, CHCl3, CH2Cl2 and acetone, the frameworks of both 1 and 1a collapsed into amorphous powders. In cyclohexane, n-butanol, n-propanol, N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA), DMSO and THF, their frameworks remained unchanged. In DMF, the framework of 1 remained unchanged. However, the framework of 1a was transformed to that of 1 in DMF, and the samples obtained by soaking 1a170 (the 170 °C vacuum dried samples) in DMF are designated as 1b. The structural transformation from 1a to 1b shows very high selectivity toward DMF among other solvents.

TGA measurements were performed at 5 °C min−1 and revealed that 1 underwent a weight loss of 18.46% in the temperature range from room temperature to 168 °C, corresponding to the release of four water molecules, two acetonitrile molecules, two DMF molecules and three dimethylamine molecules (calculated value of 19.16%) (Fig. 5). Dimethylamines were produced by a proton transfer reaction that occurred between protonated dimethylamines and frameworks.18 While in the same temperature range, the weight losses are 2.25% and zero for 1a100 (100 °C vacuum dried samples) and 1a170, respectively, which means that 1a100 and 1a170 are the partially and fully desolvated products of 1, respectively. 1b underwent a weight loss of 17.88% in the temperature range from room temperature to 155 °C, corresponding to the release of 5.5 DMF molecules (calculated value of 17.96%).

Photoluminescence properties

When monitored under the characteristic emission (614 nm) of the Eu(III) ions, the excitation spectra of 1, 1a and 1b all exhibit a broad peak with a maximum at around 340 nm (Fig. S5, ESI†). 1, 1a and 1b all exhibited red luminescence when excited at 340 nm, with characteristic emissions at 579, 591, and 614 nm with shoulder peaks at 619, 652, and 700 nm in the emission spectrum, corresponding to 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) transitions of Eu3+, respectively. The blue light emission (Fig. S6, ESI†) in free H2MHFDA completely disappears and the ligands as light-harvesting chromophores (antenna effect) effectively transfer energy to Eu(III) ions. The quantum yields are 24%, 8% and almost zero for 1, 1a100 and 1a170, respectively. The decay dynamics of the emission peak at 614 nm for 1 and 1a can be well fitted by the biexponential curve, suggesting two emission centers, which is consistent with two independent Eu(III) lattice sites in the structure. The lifetimes are τ1 = 0.689 ms (71.6%) and τ2 = 0.253 ms (28.4%) for 1 (R2 = 0.99995); τ1 = 0.44 ms (78.1%) and τ2 = 0.138 ms (21.9%) for 1a100 (R2 = 0.99995); and τ1 = 0.242 ms (32.1%) and τ2 = 0.015 ms (67.9%) for 1a170 (R2 = 0.99823) (Fig. 6). These data are in accordance with the luminescence intensity of 1 and 1a170 dispersed in DMSO (3 mg per 3 mL). Compared with the luminescence intensity of 1, 1a170 shows very weak emission. When 1a170 is dispersed in DMF (3 mg per 3 mL), the luminescence intensity is almost equal to that of 1 (Fig. S7 and S8, ESI†), suggesting that luminescence recovery is accompanied by the structural transition from 1a to 1b.19
Fig. 6 Semilog plots of fluorescence decay versus time for 1 and 1a activated at 100 and 170 °C (excited and monitored at 340 nm and 614 nm, respectively).

Detection of nitro compounds

The characteristic, bright-red luminescence and the extensively aromatic structure of the host material 1b provide the possibility of sensing target aromatic molecules via specific host–guest interactions. To explore the ability of 1b to sense a trace quantity of nitro explosives, the luminescent responses of 1b to various nitro compounds were investigated by addition of PA, 2,6-dinitrophenol (2,6-DNP), 2,4-dinitrotoluene (2,4-DNT), 2-nitrotoluene (2-NT), 1,2-dinitrobenzene (1,2-DNB), 1,3-dinitrobenzene (1,3-DNB), nitrobenzene (NB), 2,3-dimethyl-2,3-dinitrobutane (DMNB), and toluene (TO) to the 1b–DMF emulsion, respectively, at the concentration of 5.0 × 10−4 M (Fig. 7). All eight nitro-compounds can weaken the photoluminescence intensity of the 1b emulsion to varying degrees. The order of quenching efficiency is PA > 2,6-DNP > 2,4-DNT > 2-NT > 1,2-DNB > 1,3-DNB > NB > DMNB, with the largest quenching percentage (QP) of 95% for PA in DMF solution. These results demonstrate that compound 1b has high selectivity for PA compared to other nitro compounds. Unlike the nitro compounds, an opposite effect was observed for toluene, which enhanced the luminescence emission intensity. Therefore, it is speculated that the donor–acceptor electron-transfer is the dominant mechanism of luminescence attenuation and enhancement.
Fig. 7 Luminescence quenching percentage when 1b was dispersed in eight different nitro compounds and toluene (TO) solutions in DMF (excited and monitored at 340 nm and 614 nm, respectively).

To better understand the high quenching efficiency of 1b towards PA, the sensing performance was investigated in detail by the photoluminescence titration experiments, which exhibited that upon addition of PA to the 1b–DMF emulsion, the emission intensity gradually decreased with the increase in the concentration of the quenchers (Fig. 8). The emission of 1b was almost completely quenched when the concentration of PA reached 1.0 × 10−3 M, showing that 1b as a turn-off luminescent sensory material has greater sensitivity for PA. The Stern–Volmer plots of relative luminescence intensity (I0/I) versus the PA concentration are shown in the inset of Fig. 7 to further quantify the quenching efficiency, where I0 and I are the fluorescence intensities of the emulsion in the absence and presence of PA, respectively. It is notable that the I0/I versus PA concentration plots bend upwards instead of being the typically linear plots,20 suggesting that the fluorescence quenching mechanism can be attributed to the presence of simultaneous dynamic and static quenching.


Fig. 8 Concentration-dependent luminescence quenching of 1b (dispersion in DMF) after adding different concentrations of PA excited at 340 nm at room temperature. Inset: Stern–Volmer plot.

Conclusions

In summary, we have elucidated a novel flexible metal–organic framework which could be reversibly transformed between two phases by activation or resoaking in DMF and shows a reversible large change in their luminescence intensities. This unusual reverse role of DMF is probably because the environment around lanthanide ions was softened in the vacuum drying phases while it recovered from stiffness upon resoaking. The significant luminescence changes upon activation or resoaking render the materials potentially useful for sensing DMF. And this understanding of the solvent induced structural transformation from this study might guide us to design and synthesize novel materials for the research and application of guest recognition and detection. The potential of 1b for PA sensing was also investigated in DMF and its luminescence emission was turned off in the presence of PA. The fluorescence quenching mechanism of PA can be attributed to the presence of simultaneous dynamic and static quenching.
