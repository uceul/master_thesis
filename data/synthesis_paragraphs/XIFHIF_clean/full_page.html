Abstract

A rare, robust microporous lanthanide metal–organic framework with 1D honeycomb‐type channels is presented. Excellent adsorption capabilities for N2, H2, and CO2 and significant selective sorption of CO2 over N2 and CH4 were observed. Moreover, the guest‐dependent luminescent behavior of these lanthanide materials shows a potential use for the sensing of small‐molecule pollutants such as benzene and acetone.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), as a new generation of highly porous molecular crystalline materials assembled by coordination bonds between organic ligands and metal ions/clusters, have developed very quickly over recent decades. Their high surface areas, well‐defined porosity, and tunable chemical functionality make them extremely attractive for various applications in the fields of gas storage and separation,1, 2 catalysis,3 and sensing,4 for example. Lanthanide MOFs (Ln‐MOFs), as a sub‐family of MOFs, have received great attention owing to their unusual coordination characteristics, and magnetic and optical properties arising from 4f electrons. The high coordination number and weak coordination bond of lanthanide ions allow the removal of small terminal coordinated molecules such as water molecules, which results in open metal sites. Such open metal sites in transition‐metal MOFs have shown promise in applications such as enhancing the storage ability of clean‐energy resources including hydrogen and methane.5 However, experimental studies concerning the gas storage especially the selective‐adsorption properties of Ln‐MOFs are still rare because the rational design and synthesis of Ln‐MOFs is more difficult owing to the high coordination numbers and weak coordination bonds relative to transition‐metal MOFs.

However, there has been extensive interest in the recognition and sensing of small organic molecules for their important roles in biological and environmental systems. Ln‐MOFs as luminescent sensing materials are certainly very promising because both the inorganic and organic components can provide platforms to generate luminescence. The permanent porosity of luminescent MOFs allows the reversible storage and release of guest substrates and provides the hosts for the recognition of sensing species. Among the lanthanide ions, Eu3+ and Tb3+ are attractive luminescent centers owing to their large Stokes’ shifts, long luminescence lifetimes (milliseconds), and their sharp pure red and green emission.6, 7 Challenges in creating Ln‐MOFs with luminescent sensing properties come from not only the synthetic difficulties mentioned above, but also in finding appropriate ligands to act as “antenna” to effectively transfer the energy to the luminescent centers.

In this context, Ln‐MOFs with intrinsic porosity, open metal sites, and a luminescent chromophore are promising multifunctional materials. To rationally synthesize such multifunctional materials, selection of the organic ligand for the building block is the key factor. The coordination modes as well as the specific symmetries of ligands play an important role in the final structures and properties. For example, in a previous study, we obtained a series of porous Ln‐MOFs and 4f–3d MOFs based on pyridine‐2,6‐dicarboxylic acid (H2PDA, C2‐like symmetry), which exhibited interesting properties including luminescent sensing, radical adsorption, and magnetic properties.8 Thiophene‐2,5‐dicarboxylic acid (H2TDC) with similar symmetry is another good exo‐bidentate ligand for the construction of porous frameworks.9 H2TDC also shows a strong “antenna effect”, effectively transforming the energy to the lanthanide ion.10 With such a symmetry approach, furan‐2,5‐dicarboxylic acid (H2FDA), which shows similar coordination models and molecular symmetry to H2PDA and H2TDC, is beneficial to generate highly ordered porous structures. Moreover, H2FDA was proven recently to show a strong “antenna effect”, and can effectively sensitize the luminescence lanthanide ions such as DyIII in 3d–4f heteronuclear MOFs.11

In this contribution, we report a rare microporous Ln‐MOF containing 1D honeycomb‐type channels, namely, {[Tb(FDA)1.5(DMF)]⋅DMF}n (1⋅n DMF; DMF=N,N‐dimethylformamide), which not only exhibits excellent sorption and separation capabilities for energy‐ and environmentally related gases, but also interesting sensing properties for small‐molecule pollutants such as benzene and acetone. Only a few examples of Ln‐MOFs with open metal sites have been illustrated for their sensor properties,12 and singular multifunctional materials combined with both the highly selective sorption and luminescent sensing properties are rather rare but valuable for both academic studies and future applications.

Results and Discussion

Structural characterization: Reaction of Tb(NO3)3⋅6 H2O and H2FDA in DMF under carefully selected conditions led to the isolation of pure crystalline Ln‐MOF material, 1⋅n DMF, which crystallizes in orthorhombic space group Pnma with one crystallographically independent Tb3+ ion in the asymmetric unit (Figure 1a). Each Tb3+ center lies in a monocapped trigonal‐prism coordination environment completed by six oxygen atoms from six μ2‐η1:η1‐carboxylate groups, and one oxygen atom from a DMF molecule. The TbO bond lengths range from 2.331(7) to 2.440(9) Å. The Tb3+ ions are connected to each other by three μ1,3‐carboxylate groups to form 1D chains (Figure 1a), which are further linked by FDA2− to construct a 3D framework (Figure 1b). The 3D framework contains 1D honeycomb‐type nanotubular channels with a diagonal length of 9.18 Å along the [100] direction (Figure 1c), occupied by DMF molecules. Further study on the nature of the intricate framework can be achieved by topological analysis with freely available computer program TOPOS.13 As shown in Figure 1d, if each Tb3+ ion can be considered as a six‐connected node to link ten FDA2−, in which some FDA2− serves as bridging linkers while some as four‐connected nodes, the entire structure can be simplified as a 4,6‐connected stp net. There are only a few stp nets reported in the Reticular Chemistry Structure Resource (RCSR).14, 15 The point symbol of the topology can be expressed as (44.62)3 (49.66)2. The PLATON analysis revealed that the 3D porous structure contains large voids of 3725.3 Å3 that represent 52.9 % volume per unit cell when the guest and coordinated DMF molecules are removed.16

Gas adsorption and separation: Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were studied to evaluate the stability of the framework under solvent‐removed conditions. As shown in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information, 1⋅n DMF releases the guest and terminal DMF molecules in the temperature range of 25 to 270 °C to form the guest‐free phase of [Tb(FDA)1.5] (1 a). Compound 1 a starts to decompose above 450 °C, thereby showing high thermal stability. PXRD reveals very similar patterns of the simulated curve from the single‐crystal data, the as‐synthesized one, and the activated one (see Figure S2 in the Supporting Information). The results indicate that the porous framework is retained after the removal of the solvent molecules, which provides feasible open metal sites. Moreover, 1⋅n DMF is also stable and insoluble in common organic solvents (such as MeOH (methanol), EtOH (ethanol), nPrOH (propanol), iPrOH (isopropanol), nBuOH (butanol), nPenOH (1‐pentanol), THF, benzene, acetone, and toluene), which were examined by PXRD analyses (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information). These results confirm the stability of the channel structure in the including/releasing of small molecules into/from the channel.

N2 adsorption: Desolvated 1 a was prepared by thermal activation of chloroform‐exchanged samples of 1⋅n DMF at 200 °C under vacuum for 12 h. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (SBET) and Langmuir surface (SLangmuir) areas of 1 a from the N2‐adsorption isotherm are 1005.6 and 1880.3 m2 g−1, respectively. The N2‐adsorption maximum of 394 cm3 g−1 gives a micropore volume of 0.593 cm3 g−1 (Figure 2a). Moreover, the median pore size of 7 Å (using nonlocal density functional theory, NLDFT) is consistent with the result calculated from the single‐crystal structure analysis if the van der Waals radius of the atoms in the channel walls are considered (Figure 2b).17

Hydrogen adsorption: Type I sorption isotherms of H2 on 1 a were obtained at 77 and 87 K, as shown in Figure 3a. They are completely reversible in the adsorption/desorption processes. The H2 uptakes were 0.74 wt % at 77 K/1 bar and 0.36 wt % at 87 K/1 bar, respectively. The isosteric heats of adsorption for H2 were calculated using both the Clausius–Clapeyron equation and virial‐type fitting.18 At low coverage, the H2‐adsorption enthalpy is 12.3 kJ mol−1 calculated by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation and 12.0 kJ mol−1 from virial‐type fitting for 1 a (Figure 3b), which are one of the highest values found in MOFs,19, 20 and higher than the typical van der Waals type interactions (4–6 kJ mol−1).21 This high adsorption enthalpy indicates a strong interaction strength between H2 molecules and open lanthanide metal sites of the framework.

CO2/CH4 adsorption: The CO2‐sorption measurements at 273 K show typical type I isotherms with high uptake (Figure 4a). A hysteresis is visible in the desorption branch, which can be attributed to the strong interaction between CO2 and the framework. The uptake of CO2 at 273 K/1 bar is 61 cm3 g−1, which is about 15 times higher than the uptake of N2 (4.1 cm3 g−1). CH4‐sorption isotherms at 273 K show an uptake of 14.4 cm3 g−1, also higher than that of N2, but much less than that of CO2 at the same temperature.

The selective sorption ability of gas mixtures in porous materials can be reliably calculated from single‐component adsorption isotherms. The CO2/N2 selectivity in a binary mixture was calculated by employing the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) method based on the experimental CO2 and CH4 isotherms at 273 K, as shown in Figure 4b.22 Calculated from the data, the CO2/N2 and CO2/CH4 selectivities are approximately 36:1 and approximately 10:1 at 273 K, respectively, and have no significant reduction throughout the entire pressure range measured. To the best of our knowledge, such high CO2/N2 and CO2/CH4 selective ability of 1 a has only been reported for a few MOFs for which the selectivity are obtained from experimental data (Table 1).23–25 These results reveal that the selective ability of 1 a is among the benchmark MOFs for possible applications in highly selective CO2 capture and the separation of CO2 from CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 mixtures.26

Luminescent properties: The excitation and photoluminescence (PL) spectra of 1 a are shown in Figure 5. The excitation of 1 a by UV light leads to strong green photoluminescence. Under the excitation of 300 nm light, 1 a exhibits six characteristic emission bands with the emission wavelengths of 488, 543, 592, 615, 654, and 701 nm, due to the 5D4→7FJ (J=6–1) transitions of Tb3+ ion, respectively.27

For the luminescent sensing study, samples of 1 a were immersed in various pure solvents for one week and were then removed by filtration. The lifetimes are shown in Table S2 and Figure S4 in the Supporting Information. The solvent effect of 1 a is clear. As shown in Figure S4 in the Supporting Information, by taking the sample obtained in DMF as standard, iPrOH (isopropanol), nBuOH (butanol), nPenOH (1‐pentanol), THF, benzene, and acetone clearly reduce the fluorescence lifetime of 1 a, whereas MeOH (methanol), EtOH (ethanol), nPrOH (propanol), and toluene slightly affect the fluorescence lifetime.

Further studies were performed to investigate the influence of different guest molecules on the luminescent intensities. As shown in Figure 6, the intensities of 1 a in the PL spectra are strongly dependent on the solvent molecules, particularly for benzene and acetone, which exhibit the most significant enhancing and quenching effects, respectively. Such solvent‐dependent luminescent properties are of interest for the sensing of benzene and acetone molecules, which are very harmful to human beings. Therefore we further studied the guest effects of benzene and acetone on the luminescent intensities of 1 a in detail. Compound 1 a was dispersed in iPrOH as the standard emulsion, while the content of benzene/acetone solvent was gradually increased to detect the emissive response. It was found that the fluorescent intensity of the emulsion of 1 a increased with the addition of benzene (Figure 7a). The enhancement was nearly proportional to the concentration of benzene. On the other hand, addition of acetone to the emulsion of 1 a resulted in a significant decrease of the fluorescent intensity, which almost disappeared at an acetone content of 14 vol % (Figure 7b). The trend of the decreasing fluorescence intensity of the 5D0→7F2 transition of Tb3+ at 543 nm versus the volume ratio of acetone was well‐fitted by a first‐order exponential decay (see Figure S5 in the Supporting Information), which indicated that fluorescent quenching of 1 a by acetone is diffusion‐controlled.12

Although the mechanism for such enhancing and quenching effects of small solvent molecules are still not very clear at this moment, the binding interaction of the luminescent open metal sites with guest solvent molecules should definitely play an important role. It is suggested that the weakly coordinated iPrOH molecule on the Tb3+ sites are replaced by benzene and acetone molecules, which results in luminescent enhancement and quenching, respectively. The result suggests that Ln‐MOFs are promising chemical sensors for air‐pollution molecules. Further effort in this field should be given to the rational design and synthesis of porous Ln‐MOFs to show selective luminescent response in the visible‐light range, so that the air‐pollution warning can be directly detected by the eyes.

Conclusion

In summary, a rare, robust microporous lanthanide MOF containing 1D honeycomb‐type channels was reported. The desolvated Ln‐MOF 1 a shows not only excellent adsorption capabilities for N2, H2, and CO2 but also significant selective sorption of CO2 over N2 and CO2 over CH4. Beyond that, luminescent studies of the emulsions of 1 a reveal important guest‐dependent luminescent emissions, which demonstrate their potential use for sensing small‐molecule pollutants, such as benzene and acetone. This study points out that the open metal sites within porous Ln‐MOFs are expected to play an important role in the adsorption and recognition of small molecules. Further studies will be focused on the construction of porous luminescent MOFs with pore‐sieving functions to enhance their recognition selectivity, which will ultimately lead to highly selective luminescent probes based on Ln‐MOFs to explore their application in sensing organic‐molecule and metal‐ion pollutants.

Experimental Section

Materials and methods: H2FDA was purchased from the Sigma–Aldrich Company. Other chemicals were reagent grade and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240 CHN elemental analyzer. IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker TENOR 27 spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. PXRD measurements were recorded on a D/Max‐2500 X‐ray diffractometer using CuKα radiation. The simulated powder patterns were calculated using Mercury 2.0. Fluorescent spectra were measured on a Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer.

Synthesis of Ln‐MOF 1⋅n DMF: A mixture of Tb(NO3)3⋅6 H2O (0.0453 g, 0.1 mmol), H2FDA (0.0312 g, 0.2 mmol), and DMF (2 mL) was stirred for 10 min and then sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave, which was heated at 120 °C for 21 h. The reaction vessel was then removed from the oven and allowed to cool to room temperature. Colorless ribbon crystals of 1⋅n DMF (49 % yield based on Tb) were collected after they had been washed with DMF and dried in air. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.

Low‐pressure sorption experiments: All gas‐sorption experiments were performed on a Quantachrome Autosorb‐IQ2 automatic volumetric instrument. All gases used are of the purity of 99.999 %. The chloroform‐exchanged samples were degassed at 200 °C for 12 h to remove the guest molecules. The temperature of each sample for N2‐ and H2‐sorption experiments were controlled by a refrigerated bath of either liquid nitrogen (77 K) or liquid argon (87 K). The N2 sorption isotherms were recorded in the pressure range from 10−7 to 1 bar at 77 K. The H2 sorption isotherms (77 and 87 K) were carried out in the pressure range from 10−3 to 1 bar. The CO2, CH4, and N2 sorption isotherms at 273 K were measured in a temperature‐controlled water bath in the pressure range from 10−3 to 1 bar.

Fluorescence measurements: The fluorescent property of 1 a was investigated in the solid state and in various solvent emulsions at room temperature. The emulsions were prepared by introducing 1 a (3.00 mg) as a powder into MeOH, EtOH, nPrOH, iPrOH, nBuOH, nPenOH, THF, benzene, toluene, DMF, and acetone (each 2.00 mL), and were then vigorously agitated by using ultrasound. For the experiments sensing small molecules, different amounts of benzene and acetone were added into a standard emulsion in iPrOH, while the concentration of the lanthanide ions was kept constant. The PL spectra of the emulsions were measured after days of aging and were vigorously agitated by using ultrasound before testing.
