Synthesis, structures and luminescent properties of cadmium(II) metal organic frameworks based on 3-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid, 4-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid ligands†

Xiaojing Zhou , Baiyan Li , Guanghua Li *, Qi zhou , Zhan Shi * and Shouhua Feng
State Key Laboratory of Inorganic Synthesis and Preparative Chemistry, College of Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, 130012, People's Republic of China. E-mail: zshi@mail.jlu.edu.cn; Fax: +86 431 85168624; Tel: +86 431 85168662

Received 7th March 2012 , Accepted 17th April 2012

First published on 19th April 2012

Two three-dimensional metal–organic frameworks, [Cd3K(L1)6(H2O)3](OH)(DMF)4 (1) (HL1 = 3-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid) and [Cd(L2)2](DMF)(H2O)0.25 (2) (HL2 = 4-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid), have been synthesized by using two ligands with different configuration and characterized by X-Ray diffraction and other techniques. Compound 1 shows a nanosize channel which is constructed by interlocked six-fold one-dimensional helical chains with the asymmetrical ligand HL1. Compared with compound 1, compound 2 shows a larger size channel with the symmetrical ligand HL2 in a single set of diamond cage. Nevertheless, the whole structure with the 4-fold interpenetrated diamondoid network leads to the absence of void space. The two compounds exhibit fascinating luminescence because of the interaction between Cd ions and organic ligands.

Introduction

In the past few decades, considerable attention has been paid to the synthesis and application of metal–organic framework (MOF) materials, which exhibit a high potential for applications in a variety of areas.1 To date, several thousand MOF materials have been synthesized and their number continues to grow rapidly.2 With the development of MOF materials, nano-sized MOFs have been investigated intensively because of their potential applications in sorption, drug delivery, imaging, luminescence, and enantioselective catalysis.3 At present, many highly ordered nanotubular materials are based on the pure organic ensembles,4 while compounds constructed from MOF materials are not as common. For instance, a novel 1D nanotubular zinc complex based on mixture ligands was reported by Sun.5 A 1D metal–organic nanotube with 1D–2D interdigital architecture was synthesized by Shi.6 Zur Loye and co-workers reported a zinc tubular MOF using the ligand of 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole.7 A chiral tetragonal magnesium-carboxylate framework with nanotubular channels was also reported by the Feng group.8 Furthermore, the study of MOF materials containing helical structures has aroused great interest of chemists. As a fascinating motif, the helix is ubiquitous in nature as well as in human art and architecture.9 Taking the nanostructure and helicity into consideration, the construction of helical nanotubular MOF materials becomes very meaningful.

Although large numbers of MOF materials with interesting structures and excellent properties have been widely reported in recent years, targeted synthesis of MOF materials with designed structures and properties is still a formidable challenge,10 because the structure and topology of MOFs are usually influenced by many factors, such as organic ligands, metal ions, solvent systems, counterions, and metal-to-ligand ratios.11,12 Among these many influential factors, organic spacers with changeable bridging length, symmetry, configuration and flexibility are very important for the construction of MOF materials.13 In particular, the effect of length and configuration have been reported by several excellent works.14 It can be noticed among numerous reported building blocks that two types of ligands are most frequently chosen: one type is multi-carboxylate ligands with suitable spacers for porous MOFs, and the other is pyridine groups separated by various spacers, rigid or flexible, such as 4,4-bipyridine and 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane/ethene/ethyne, from which a rich variety of 1D, 2D, and 3D metal–organic polymeric architectures have been constructed.15 Furthermore, the use of carboxylic acid groups combined with pyridine groups as multidentate building blocks was pursued in order to construct novel functional MOF materials,16 and in previous contributions, many excellent works with pyridine carboxylic acids were reported.17

We have concentrated on the construction of MOF materials based on pyridine or carboxylic acid ligands and have carried out a lot of work in this area.18 As a continuation of this attractive subject, we have been devoted to the construction of unique MOF materials with nanochannels using pyridine carboxylic acids as organic spacers. Here we selected 3-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid (HL1) and 4-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid (HL2) as the ligands for the following reasons: HL1 and HL2 are large size ligands with multiple coordination sites like carboxylic and N-donor groups, and this coordination mode is likely to result in structures with large size channels or chirality.16e,17b This is because, firstly, the asymmetric ligand HL1 is a good candidate for forming structures with 1D nanotubes due to its 120° angle between the N atom and carboxylic acid, which can be effectively used for the construction of a polygon, and thus enhances the possibility of the formation of a framework with nanochanels.19 The symmetric and large size ligand HL2 is liable to form MOF materials with larger size channels compared with HL1. Secondly, the lack of a center of symmetry on the two ligands makes it possible to produce helical polymers.

In this article, we have synthesized one 3D nanotubular framework [Cd3K(L1)6(H2O)3](OH)(DMF)4 (1) with interesting six-fold helical chains, in which alternative right and left-hand helical chains are interweaved. In addition, a 3D compound [Cd (L2)2](DMF)(H2O)0.25 (2) with 4-fold interpenetrated diamondoid network structure is also synthesized using a similar method.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements.

All reagents and solvents were obtained commercially and used without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 element analyzer. IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer using KBr pellet. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max 2550 X-ray Powder Diffractometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was measured using Thermogravimetric experiments, which were performed with a TGA Q500 V20.10 Build 36; data were collected from room temperature to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The emission and excitation spectra of the samples were recorded on an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920 spectrofluorimeter equipped with both continuous (450 W) and pulsed xenon lamps.

Synthesis of compound 1

HL1 (0.1 mmol, 19.9 mg), Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.1 mmol, 30.8 mg), and KOH (0.025–0.1 mmol, 14–56 mg) were dissolved in DMF (5 mL) and one drop of 6N HNO3 was added. The resulting reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature for 30 min. Then the 20 mL reaction vial was placed in an oven and heated at 80 °C for two days. After this time octahedral colorless crystals of 1 were obtained in 60% yield, IR (KBr 4000–400 cm−1) 3400 (w), 3088 (w), 2930 (w), 1670 (s), 1609 (s), 1559 (s), 1506 (s), 1436 (w), 1393 (s), 1093 (m), 1049 (w), 882 (m), 838 (m), 773 (s), 738 (w), 680 (s), 623 (m). Elemental analysis (%): Calcd for: C84H83Cd3 KN10O20: C 52.3 H 4.3 N 7.26; found: C 51.7 H 4.04 N 7.2.

Synthesis of compound 2

HL2 (0.1 mmol, 19.9 mg) was dissolved in DMF (5 mL) and heated at a temperature of 70 °C for 5 min. Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.1 mmol, 30.8 mg), KOH (0.1 mmol, 56 mg) were dropped to the solvent, and one drop of 6N HNO3 was added. The resulting reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature for 30 min. Then the 20 mL reaction vial was placed in an oven and heated at 80 °C for two days. After this time octahedral colorless crystals of 2 were obtained in 50% yield, IR (KBr 4000–400 cm−1) 3442 (w), 2928 (w), 1673 (m), 1614 (s), 1563 (m), 1384 (s), 1226 (w), 1184(w), 1098 (w), 1074 (w), 1009 (w), 836 (m), 783 (s), 740 (w), 705 (w), 658 (w), 560 (w), 489(m). Elemental analysis (%): Calcd for: C27H23.5CdN3O5.25: C 55.3 H 4.04 N 7.17; found: C 55.09 H 3.99 N 7.21.

X-Ray crystallography

The data collection and structural analysis were performed on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID equipped with a narrow-focus, 5.4 kW sealed tube X-ray source (graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å). The data were collected at a temperature of 20 ± 2 °C. The data processing was accomplished with the PROCESS-AUTO processing program. Direct methods were used to solve the structure using the SHELXL crystallographic software package. All non-hydrogen atoms were easily found from the difference Fourier map. As the coordinated water molecules point to the center of the pore, and they have large thermal vibration, we did not add H atoms to the coordinated water molecules in compound 1. All hydrogen atoms of the organic molecule were placed by geometrical considerations and were added to the structure factor calculation. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The coordinates can be obtained, on request, from the Director, Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre, 12 Union Road, Cambridge, CB2 1EZ, UK or at www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/conts/retrieving.html. Results and discussion

Description of crystal structures

Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the trigonal space group P1c. In this structure, the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one half Cd(II) atom, one L1 molecule, 1/6 K atom, 1/3 hydroxyl oxygen atom and 5/8 coordinated water molecule. The Cd2+ atom adopts a distorted heptacoordinated pentagonal bipyramid configuration, which is coordinated by four oxygen atoms from two L1 ligands, two nitrogen atoms from the other two L1 ligands, and one coordinated water molecule. Each K atom is octahedrally bound to six oxygen atoms of the six independent L1 ligands (Fig. S1 and S2 in the ESI†). In addition, the distance of Cd–O and K–O is 2.306 Å–2.588 Å and 2.423 Å respectively, which is in the real bond.21 In compound 1, L1 molecules coordinate with K atoms and Cd atoms in the mode of Scheme 1. The Cd2+ atom is connected by the L1 ligands to form a 1D hexagonal tube with L1 molecules serving as the corrugated walls (Fig. 1a), and with a Cd⋯Cd distance bridged by L1 ligand of about 8.8 Å (taking into consideration the Van Der Waals Radii of Cd atom). This open ended hollow tube possesses windows comprised of hexanuclear crown-like metallamacrocycles [Cd6L6] with a very large 72-membered ring which consists of six Cd atoms and six L1 ligands (Fig. 1a). In this case, each 1D array features a hexastranded helix arrangement and passes through the 61 and 65 screw axis parallel to the c axis. There are two types of helices in the perpendicular channel (left- and right- handed), which are highlighted in Fig. 1b. The pitch of the helix is 57.86 Å. As a whole, this compound does not show chirality as the left and right ligands are averaged (three right handed and three left handed)22 (Fig. 1b). The 1D channel possesses a nanopore with the dimension being 11 × 11 Å without taking the coordinated water into consideration (Fig. 1a). Furthermore, these hexagonal tubes extend infinitely along the ab plane with oxygen atoms from carboxyl group bridging K atoms to construct a 3D framework (Fig. 1c). If we consider the Cd2 and K atoms as a single node, according to the analysis of Topos 4.0, the structure of 1 can be described as a 3D acs network with the point (Schläfli) symbol 49·66. It is a high-symmetry uninodal six-connected semiregular net with the vertex symbol of [4.4.4.4.4.4.4(2).4(2).4(2).6(4).6(4).6(4).6(4).6(4).6(4)] (Fig. 1d).23

Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that the asymmetric unit of crystal 2 consists of one Cd atom and one L2 ligand. Each Cd center adopts a distorted octahedral geometry by coordinating to two pyridine nitrogen and two carboxylate groups in a chelating fashion, and the distance of Cd–O (Cd–O = 2.346 Å–2.357 Å) and Cd–N (Cd–N = 2.306 Å) all falls into the normal range21 (Fig. S3 in the ESI†). The Cd(II) atom serves as the node and links four adjacent Cd atoms around it through L2 molecules constructing a 3D structure with a diamond topology (Fig. 2a). The L2 molecules coordinate with Cd atoms in the mode of Scheme 1. When viewed along the b axis of compound 2, there is an alternative arrangement of right-handed and left-handed helical chains (Fig. 2a and 2b). The pitch of each helical chain is 27 Å, and the adjacent Cd–Cd separation is 10.2 Å taking the Van Der Waals radii of the Cd atom into consideration. A cavity with the pore 17 × 17 Å is generated by an independent, equivalent diamondoid cage. The potential voids are large enough to be filled via mutual interpenetration of three independent equivalent frameworks, generating a 4-fold interpenetrating 3D architecture with a diamond topology (Fig. 2c).


Fig. 2 a) The polyhedron 3D structure of compound 2 with alternative right-handed and left-handed helical chains (Cd: purple, N: dark blue, O: red, C: greyish white); b) The left and right helical chains of compound 2; c) Four-fold interpenetrating diamond net of compound 2.

The two ligands 3-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid (HL1) and 4-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid (HL2) were chosen to construct two 3D compounds, which show different topologies. Compound 1 has a 3D nanotubular framework with six-folded helical chains, and compound 2 is a 4-fold interpenetrated diamondoid network structure with alternative arrangement of right-handed and left-handed helical chains. Our results display that the nanotubular structure of compound 1 is attributed to the 120° angle of ligand 3-pyrid-4-ylbenzoic acid, and the 120° angle is helpful for the formation of MOFs with nanochannels and helicity. As for compound 2, its symmetrical, larger ligand HL2 is liable to form a structure with a larger size channel. As a result, it is easy to form the structure with interpenetration, leaving no void space. As expected, compound 2 is a structure with a 4-fold interpenetrated diamondoid network without available space. It also displays left-handed and right-handed helical chains. The cause for the helices is the diamondoid topology itself, not ligand HL2 .

Luminescent emission

MOFs have been investigated for fluorescence properties owing to their application in the area of luminescent materials, especially for the compounds with d10 metal centers. To further examine the solid state luminescent properties of the d10 metal complexes, we measured the solid state fluorescent spectrum of the two compounds as well as the ligands HL1 and HL2 at room temperature, which are depicted in Fig. 3. The samples of 1, 2, HL1, and HL2 were excited at 340 nm, 340 nm, 310 nm, 300 nm, and the fluorescent emissions with the maximum occurred at (1: λem = 448 nm; 2: λem = 470 nm; HL1: λem = 410 nm; HL2: λem = 386 nm;) respectively. Fig. 3 shows that the ligands display weak luminescence. The emission bands of the free ligands are probably attributable to the π*→π transitions, whereas the emission intensities of the two compounds are much stronger than those of the ligands. The enhancement of fluorescence intensity in the coordination polymers is probably due to the unique coordination of HL1, HL2 and Cd metal center. This coordination enhances the “rigidity” of the ligand and thus reduces the loss of energy through a radiationless pathway.24 Compared with the ligands, there is a little redshift of emission bands for the two compounds, which should be ascribed to neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (LMCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (MLCT) in nature. Cd(II) is difficult to oxidize or to reduce, therefore this kind of fluorescence behavior should be mainly due to the metal perturbed intraligand emission state like other d10 metal complexes and other intermolecular interactions such as π–π packing lying in interpenetration, interdigitation, or simple packing of layers.25 The fluorescent properties of the two compounds indicate that they can be good candidates for potential photoactive materials.

Conclusions

In summary, two MOFs with interesting structural features have been prepared from the ligands HL1 and HL2. The two compounds show different structures as a result of the length and configuration of the ligands. Compound 1 features a unique six-stranded helical motif in the 1D nanotube with a acs topology. And compound 2 is a diamondoid topology with 4-fold interpenetration, leaving no accessible void space. In addition, the photoluminescence properties of compounds 1 and 2 are studied in the solid state at room temperature. This investigation sheds light on the design and synthesis of MOFs with novel structures. And further work about creating other MOF materials with pyridine-type ligand for MOF applications is underway.
