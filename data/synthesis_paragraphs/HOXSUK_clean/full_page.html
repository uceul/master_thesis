Abstract

The reaction of NiII with a tetra‐benzoate pyrene ligand produces a 3D porous framework based on infinite 1D NiII chains. The NiII–O connectivity and the formation of a hydroxo‐bridge (μ3‐OH) responsible for the connection of the central NiII atoms within the 1D NiII–(μ3‐OH)2–NiII chains can be straightforwardly compared with the TiIV–O–TiIV connectivity seen in TiO2. The arrangement of the TBAPy ligand around the 1D rutile‐based chains leads in the generation of a porous framework with two distinct types of pores; based on the chemistries of these two types of pores, one can be labelled as hydrophobic and the other as hydrophilic. The use of different activation methods results in the generation of either a porous framework free of guest molecules or a completely solvent‐free material, in which the terminal H2O molecules bound to NiII were removed, leading thus to a framework with open NiII sites. CO2 isotherms collected on both frameworks at 195 K and one barshowed type I isotherms characteristic of microporous materials (BET surface areas for: guest‐free framework: 257(3) m2·g–1; solvent‐free framework: 362(2) m2·g–1). The affinity of both networks at zero coverage for both CO2 and CH4 was found to be greater when the unsaturated NiII sites are available within the void space.

Introduction

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are nanoporous crystalline solids composed of organic bridging ligands coordinated to metal‐based units to form multi‐dimensional extended networks with uniform pore diameters typically in the range of 3 to 50 Å.1 The rich functionality, designability, and directionality of the organic ligands and the directability and physical properties of the metal ions allow integration of various functions within MOFs.2 The regular nature of the crystalline arrays of the pores formed by the linkage of the organic ligands with the metal units is a key feature that differentiates MOFs from other porous materials such as activated carbon, covalent organic frameworks and others.3 The design of the organic ligand and selection of the metal ion is important for the construction of a MOF, which can be used as gas storage and separation platforms,1a,4 molecular sensors,5 magnets,6 catalysts7 and drug delivery agents.8

The ability to modify the internal surface of a MOF using different strategies such as: post‐modification of the organic ligand or generation of unsaturated metal sites upon removal of the coordinated solvent molecules was found to be of high importance for gas storage and separation applications.9 This strategy can lead to the generation of frameworks with pendant functional groups or unsaturated metal centres responsible for the selective capture of strategically important gasses such as CO2, CH4, or H2.9e,10

Herein, we report the isolation of a 3D porous MOF based on a tetra‐benzoate functionalized pyrene based ligand (H4TBAPy) and NiII. The same ligand, TBAPy, has been previously involved as a building block for the construction of several networks based on InIII,5a ZnII,11 MgII12 and ZrIV13 (in which the reaction solvents and conditions used for their isolation are comparable – DMF and H2O and/or dioxane and/or HCl). Within these frameworks, the following central metal atoms InIII, MgII, ZnII, and NiII (described herein), are octahedrally coordinated. However, none of these frameworks is isostructural to each other due to the formation of (a) 1D chains – In‐(μ2‐OH)‐In,5a Ni‐(μ3‐OH)2‐Ni (described herein), or (b) clusters Mg2(μ2‐OH2),12 Zr6(μ3‐OH)8,13 and the TBAPy ligand was oriented around the metal atom in a different way. The ZnII‐based MOF is a 2D layered framework based on Zn‐paddlewheels and upon activation, the axial coordinated H2O molecule to ZnII was removed resulting in a MOF with different topology in which ZnII was found to be tetrahedrally coordinated.11 These examples clearly demonstrate that the role of the central metal atom (and thus the formation of metal clusters or chains) is dominant for the final topology of the framework and that the orientation of the TBAPy ligand around the metal atom is dependent on the available coordination sites of the metal atom.

The NiII‐TBAPy based MOF is a 3D porous framework based on infinite 1D chains of octahedral NiII atoms, and the connectivity observed between NiII‐(μ3‐OH)2‐NiII can be directly compared with the connectivity with rutile topology TiO2.14 Such NiII‐based 1D chains were previously reported in fumaric acid‐derived MOFs, where the organization of this dicarboxylate linker around the 1D chains afforded a non‐porous framework with ferrimagnetic behavior below 20 K.15 In the presented case, the coordination of the tetracarboxylate TBAPy ligand to the 1D chains produces a porous framework comprising two distinct types of channel. The application of two activation methods resulted in the generation of a guest free material or a completely solvent free (with removal of the coordinated H2O molecules) system with open metal atoms.

Results and Discussion

Structure Determinationof [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(TBAPy)(H2O)4]·Guest Molecules (1)

The reaction between Ni(NO3)2·9(H2O) and the H4TBAPy ligand in an acidic mixture of DMF, dioxane, and H2O (ratio: 2:1:1) at 120 °C for 72 h afforded [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(H2O)4(TBAPy)]·guest_molecules (1) as needle shaped crystals suitable for single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction (Figure 1; for synthetic details see Experimental Section). HCl was used as a mineralizing agent in the synthesis in order to isolate 1 in high yields. Systematic exploration of several solvent mixtures and ratios shows that all three solvent molecules, DMF, dioxane, and H2O, are needed for the isolation of pure bulk 1.5a

X‐ray analysis of a single crystal of 1 revealed a three‐dimensional structure based on infinite chains of edge and corner sharing octahedra, constructed by Ni#1(μ3‐OH)2(COO)4 and Ni#2(H2O)2(μ3‐OH)2(COO)2 respectively (where COO is one carboxylate function of the TBAPy ligand). The linkage of the edge and corner sharing octahedra through the hydoxo‐μ3‐OH bridge results in the formation of a trinuclear NiII cluster Ni#1(Ni#2)2(μ3‐OH)2 constructed from two crystallographic independent octahedrally coordinated NiII atoms, Ni#1 with 50 % occupancy and Ni#2 with 100 % (Figure 2a). Ni#1 is octahedrally coordinated to two μ3‐OH bridges and four oxygen atoms from the carboxylate functions of four consecutive fully deprotonated TBAPy ligands. The coordination environment of Ni#2 is provided by two cis‐μ3‐OH bridges, two carboxylate oxygen atoms from the TBAPy ligand and finally its octahedral arrangement is completed by two terminally coordinated H2O molecules located in cis positions (Figure 2a). The structure is composed of two edge‐sharing [(Ni#2)2(H2O)2(μ3‐OH)2(COO)2] octahedral, which are each corner‐shared with the third [Ni#1(μ3‐OH)2(COO)4] octahedron. The trinuclear NiII cluster propagates 1D chains along the b axis, Ni#1(Ni#2)2(H2O)4(μ3‐OH)2(COO)4 (Figure 2a). MOFs based on isolated trinuclear NiII clusters with comparable connectivity as in 116 are reported in the literature. The repetition of the trinuclear Ni#1(Ni#2)2(μ3‐OH)2cluster in one direction is due to the linkage of the Ni#2 based octahedra with the Ni#1 octahedron through the bridging hydroxo‐groups, μ3‐OH, with a mean Ni#1–OH distance of 2.033(2) Å and Ni#2–OH of 2.009(9) Å, thus yielding 1D chains. The connectivity between Ni–O and isolation of 1D chains observed within 1 can be straight forwardly compared with the rutile topology of TiO2, in which TiIV is octahedrally coordinated to six oxygen atoms and forms edge and corner shared octahedral (Figure 2c–d). This comparison is supported by the M–O bond lengths and M–O(H)–M angle measurements, in which the Ti–O triangles are 99.0(5)° and 130.4(7)°, while in 1 the Ni#2–OH–Ni#2 angle is 96.2(3)° and Ni#2–OH–Ni#1 is 119.9(8)°(Figure S1; Supporting Information).

Each TBAPy ligand is coordinated to eight consecutive NiII atoms, such that each of the benzoate fragments at the 1‐, 3‐, 6‐ and 8‐positions of the pyrene core are bound in a bidentate bridging fashion (Figure 3a–b).5a The benzoate groups are perpendicular to the pyrene core, [torsion angles between benzoate groups and pyrene: +/–106.9(8)° and +/–108.8(0)°] in order to prevent steric clashes between the phenyl hydrogen atoms in positions 1‐ or 3‐ and the pyrene core hydrogen atoms in positions 2‐ and 7‐ (Figure 3c and Figure S2).12 The coordination of TBAPy ligands around the 1D NiII chains results in the generation of a 3D open framework (Figure 3c–d), in which the pyrene cores of the TBAPy ligand are eclipsed along the [010] and are separated by 6.32 Å, however, the packing of the structure affords 1D channels running along b axis (Figure 3c).

Due to the structure of the TBAPy ligand, 1 possesses two distinct channel types; hydrophobic and hydrophilic (Figure 3d–e).2a,11 One channel has a hydrophilic pore surface arising from axial H2O molecules coordinated to the Ni, while the other surface is hydrophobic lined only by the aromatic groups of the ligand. Guest DMF molecules are located within the hydrophilic channels and are strongly hydrogen‐bonded with the hydrogen atoms of the terminal H2O molecules bound to Ni#2 with distances of O1S···H1A 1.734(7) Å (Figure 3d). Highly disordered dioxane molecules occupy the accessible volume of the hydrophobic channel (Figure 3d). The hydrophilic and hydrophobic channel dimensions in 1 are 5.85 × 5.93 Å and 2.93 × 8.41 Å (including van der Waals radii), respectively. The total solvent accessible volume of the static guest‐free structure (1′) of 1 calculated by PLATON17 was found to be 2029.2 Å3 per unit cell 5476.0 Å3 (37.1 %) (Figure 3f). Upon removal of the coordinated H2O molecules bound to Ni#2 (together with the guest molecules 1′′) results in an increase of the accessible volume to 42.8 % (2343.7 Å3) (Figure 3g).

Characterization

The bulk phase purity of as‐made 1 was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD), elemental analysis, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), infrared spectroscopy (IR) and SEM images. Le Bail fitting of the PXRD pattern of 1 collected at 298 K gives refined cell parameters of: C2/c, monoclinic, a = 42.71(4) Å, b = 6.33(5) Å, c = 26.00(6) Å, α = γ = 90°, β = 127.1(6)°, V = 5602.9(4) Å3 with fit indicators of Rwp = 4.84 % and χ2 = 1.66 (Figure 4). This is in good agreement with the parameters obtained from the X‐ray single crystal diffraction data collected at 150 K: a = 42.61(3) Å, b = 6.32(0) Å, c = 25.20(2) Å, α = γ = 90°, β = 126.2(2) °, V = 5475.(6) Å3 (Table 1). The volume of 1 calculated from the powder pattern fit is slightly larger than the volume obtained from single crystal data, because the single crystal data were collected at 150 K while the powder data were measured at 298 K.

The proposed formula of 1 derived from elemental analysis (Experimental Section) is [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(H2O)4(TBAPy)]·(DMF)2.2(dioxane)1.2. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 1 (Figure 5a) shows a weight loss of 7.4 % in the temperature range of 30–165 °C corresponding to the removal of the guest dioxane molecules (expected 8.5 %) from the hydrophobic channels. The loss of 20.8 % observed in the temperature range of 165–305 °C is attributed to the removal of all guest molecules from the cavities of 1 (expected 21.6 %). The additional weight loss (3.6 %) between 305–350 °C (total weight loss: 25.2 %) is thought to be due to the removal of the coordinated H2O molecules bound to Ni#2 (expected 27.1 %). The decomposition of 1 starts at 350 °C and occurs in a single step (Figure 5a).

In order to check the stability of bulk 1, the compound was initially heated at 300 °C in air to remove the guest molecules to give 1′a (the H2O molecules are still coordinated to Ni#2 – 1′a refers to the guest‐free phase exposed in the atmosphere) and at 350 °C in order to remove all guest and coordinated molecules to produce 1′′a (1′′a refers to the solvent‐free phase exposed in the atmosphere) and collected their PXRD patterns. Elemental analysis of 1′a showed 93 % decrease in nitrogen content revealing that a small amount of DMF is still present within the framework and its formula was found to be [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(H2O)4(TBAPy)]·(DMF)0.4(H2O)2.2 (see Supporting Information for the elemental analysis). The presence of H2O molecules within the cavities is thought to be due to the exposure of 1′a in the atmosphere. Le Bail fit of 1′a gives unit cell parameters of: C2/c, monoclinic, a = 42.20(3) Å, b = 6.32(2) Å, c = 25.03(9) Å, α = γ = 90°, β = 125.68°, V = 5422.5(4) Å3 with fit indicators of Rwp = 5.15 % and χ2 = 1.71 (Figure S3; Supporting Information). These parameters are comparable with the cell parameters derived from the fit of the as‐made 1. The Bragg reflections in 1′a were found to be at the same 2θ position with those of 1 but were broadened and their intensities were decreased (Figure 4b).

Elemental analysis of 1′′a revealed that the formula is [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(TBAPy)]·(H2O)0.9 (see Supporting Information for the elemental analysis). The absence of N and increase in C contents clearly suggest that 1′′a is free of any guest molecules and that the H2O molecules coordinated to Ni#2 were removed. However, in the formula there are 0.9 molecules of H2O as 1′′a was exposed to the atmosphere following activation for analysis. The PXRD pattern of 1′′a shows that the framework loses its crystallinity to a significant degree and is less crystalline than 1 or 1′a suggesting that the framework undergoes extensive structural changes and partially collapses (see Figure 4b). This can be attributed to the loss of the coordinated H2O molecules to Ni#2, which are involved in very strong hydrogen‐bonding interactions with the carboxylate oxygen atoms of TBAPy [H1B···O6 1.790(9) Å and H3B···O4 1.798(5) Å, Figure S4, Supporting Information] inducing freedom to the benzene rings of the TBAPy ligand to rotate.11,18 In addition, we observed a color‐change of the bulk solid upon full desolvation, from green (1) to yellow (1′′a) indicating a possible coordination environment change from octahedral to square planar, consistent with reported frameworks based on NiII (Figure S5; Supporting Information).19 The yellow 1′′a was immersed in a liquid mixture of DMF‐dioxane‐H2O and pure liquid DMF in order to check whether the structure of 1 can be recovered but the PXRD patterns revealed that the as‐made phase of 1 cannot be regenerated. This observation suggests that the structural changes occurring upon full desolvation are permanent.

We further investigated the structural changes occurred upon full desolvation by IR and UV/Vis spectroscopy. The C=O band corresponding to the guest DMF molecules observed at 1649 cm–1 in 1 is absent in 1′a and 1′′a confirming the generation of the guest‐ and solvent‐free phases, respectively. The band of the carbonyl group of TBAPy shifts from 1592 cm–1 in 1 to 1581 cm–1 in 1′a and 1585 cm–1 in 1′′a. The observed characteristic band at 3620 cm–1 in 1, corresponding to the coordination of H2O to Ni#2 (Ni–O bond),2b becomes weaker but is still present in 1′a, proving that H2O molecules are still coordinated to NiII, while this band in 1′′a has completely disappeared (Figure 6a) resulting in unsaturated NiII atoms (consistent with elemental analysis). The combination of color change observed upon desolvation, from green, 1, to yellow, 1′′a, and the absence of the Ni–OH2 bond as shown by IR indicates that the NiII coordination environment has changed. This is further investigated by diffuse reflectance (DR, an approximation of absorbance) measurements of 1 and 1′′a (Figure 6b). 1 displays strong absorption bands in the UV spectral region from 200 to 500 nm, arising from the π–π* transition of the aromatic rings of the TBAPy ligand.5a,20 The absorbance of 1′′a shows absorption behavior comparable to that of 1; however, the absorbance maxima are red‐shifted (Figure 6b). Interestingly, the DR spectra also revealed a red shift in the d‐d transition region of the NiII (600–800 nm) from 1 to 1′′a, and the absorption band broadened in 1′′a. The sensitivity of these metal‐based transitions to the arrangement‐change, which occurs upon conversion of the NiII atoms from six to mixed six/four coordination, is reminiscent of the color variations observed for other frameworks, in which open transition metal sites can be generated.9c,21

Porosity Measurements

After establishing the behavior of 1 upon removal of the guest molecules (retaining its structural integrity) and upon full desolvation – removal of the guest and coordinated H2O molecules (structural rearrangements occurred leading to partial collapse), 1 was loaded into the IGA instrument and activated with two methods; at 100 and 150 °C (overnight) under high vacuum (10–6 mbar), and the weight loss was recorded. A weight loss of 20.1 % after activation at 100 °C is in good agreement with the TGA data (20.8 %) resulting in a guest‐free phase of 1′ (1′: guest‐free framework not exposed to the atmosphere; the PXRD pattern of 1′ is almost identical with that of 1′a). 1 was also activated at 150 °C under high vacuum and gave rise to a weight loss of 26.8 % (weight loss derived from TGA: 25.2 %) indicating that both guest and coordinated molecules were successfully removed yielding the solvent‐free phase 1′′ with unsaturated NiII atoms.

Carbon dioxide isotherms were collected on 1′ and 1′′ at 195 K and 1 bar, and both showed the type I behavior characteristic of microporous materials (Figure 7a and b). The Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) model applied over p/p0 = 0.02–0.22 gives a surface area of 257(3) and 362(2) m2·g–1 for 1′ and 1′′, respectively. Dubinin‐Radushkevich analysis22 for 1′ and 1′′ determines pore volumes of 0.165 and 0.221 cm3·g–1, respectively, which are smaller than the calculated pore volume from the static single crystal structure of 1 (0.315 cm3·g–1) (Figure 7c and d). This suggests that the pore volume increase is attributed to the increase of accessible space upon removal of the H2O molecules coordinated to Ni#2.

1′ and 1′′ are both non‐porous to N2 at 77 K and 1 bar (Figure 8a), revealing that the internal pore nature of 1′ and 1′′ is highly selective only to the polar CO2 molecules: MOFs with appropriately sized pores that nevertheless do not sorb N2 are known.23 This observation has led us to investigate the affinity of 1′ and 1′′ for CO2. Carbon dioxide sorption isotherms on 1′ and 1′′ collected at 273 K and 298 K at 5 bar show reversible type I behavior. Isosteric heats of adsorption, Qst, for CO2 on 1′ and 1′′ were derived from a virial‐type expression using the adsorption branches of the isotherms measured at 273 K and 298 K (Figure 8b and Table 2).24 The Qst of 1′′ for CO2 was calculated to be 22.3 kJ·mol–1 at zero coverage, slightly higher than that of 1′, 21.7 kJ·mol–1. This is due to the presence of open NiII atoms within the channels introducing slightly stronger interactions with CO2 molecules. As seen in Figure 8d, the affinity of 1′ and 1′′ for CO2 at high loadings follows the same trend as each other and it is always greater when the open NiII atoms are exposed within the channels.

We also investigated the methane sorption isotherms of 1′ and 1′′. Similarly to the CO2 isotherms, the CH4 isotherms were also reversible with type I behavior. 1′ adsorbs 30.24 and 36.26 cm3·g–1 of CH4 at 298 K and 273 K (both measured at 5 bar), respectively, whereas 1′′ adsorbs higher amounts of CH4 (Figure 8c). The Qst for CH4 for 1′ and1′′ at zero coverage was found to be 18.4 kJ·mol–1 and 20.9 kJ·mol–1, respectively. As observed for CO2, 1′′ with open NiII sites has higher affinity for CH4 at zero coverage compared with the Qst of 1′, but at high loadings, the interactions between 1′ and CH4 are more pronounced compared (decrease from 18.4 to 17.2 kJ·mol–1) with those between 1′′ and CH4 (dramatic decrease from 20.9 to 11.2 kJ·mol–1) (Figure 8d). This is thought to be due to the narrow pore size of 1′ thus inducing strong interactions.

Conclusions

The combination of the planar tetra‐benzoate H4TBAPy ligand with NiII produces a porous framework based on infinite 1D chains with the TiO2 rutile topology. The coordination of TBAPy around the 1D rutile chains results in the generation of two types of channels, which can be defined as hydrophilic and hydrophobic, and are filled with guest molecules with different polarity. The 3D MOF retains its crystallinity upon removal of the guest molecules within the cavities (1′), and partially collapses upon full desolvation – removal of the coordinated H2O molecules (1′′). The combination of the polar and non‐polar channels observed within 1 together with the absence or presence of unsaturated central metal atoms, is an unusual feature in MOFs and this has allowed us to assess the strength of interactions occurring between the host frameworks and polar CO2 or non‐polar CH4 molecules. Both activated phases have higher affinity for CO2 than for CH4 at zero coverage and high loadings.

Experimental Section

Materials: Reagents and solvents were purchased from Sigma‐Aldrich and used as received without further purification. H4TBAPy (C44H26O8) ligand was synthesized as described elsewhere.5a

Synthesis of [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(TBAPy)(H2O)4]·guests (1): A mixture of Ni(NO3)2·9H2O (13 mg, 0.045mmol) and H4TBAPy (10 mg 0.015 mmol) was dissolved in DMF, dioxane, and H2O (5 mL) in a ratio of 2:1:1 and concentrated HCl (10 μL) was sealed in scintillation vials. The mixture was sonicated for 30 min and the green‐yellow slurry was heated at 120 °C for 72 h. The reaction was cooled to 40 °C at a rate of 12 °C·h–1. Green needle‐shape crystals suitable for singe crystal X‐ray diffraction were isolated with a yield of 47 % based on Ni. The crystals were washed with DMF and dried in the atmosphere overnight. The formula of 1 was determined by elemental analysis as [Ni3(μ3‐OH)2(H2O)4(TBAPy)]·(DMF)2.2(dioxane)1.2: calcd. C 54.30, H 4.65, N 2.51 %; found C 54.44, H 4.42, N 2.43 %.

Single Crystal Structure X‐ray Diffraction: Single crystal X‐ray diffraction data on 1 was collected at 150 K with a Rigaku Micro Max–007HF rotating anode source with Mo radiation on beamline I19 at the Diamond Light Source using the Rigaku Crystal Logic Kapppa goniometer at the zirconium adsorption edge (λ = 0.6889 Å). The crystals were transferred into Fomblin‐Y oil prior to mounting on the loops. The reflection data were integrated using the APEXII software program (after converting the frames from Rigaku to Bruker).25 The data were corrected for adsorption, decay and other systematic errors using SADABS V2008–1.26 The structure was solved by direct methods and refined using SHELX‐97.27

Characterization Methods: Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were obtained with a Thermo EA1112 Flash CHNS‐O Analyzer. The X‐ray powder diffraction (PXRD) patterns of the as‐made phase 1 and activated materials were collected in transmission geometry with a STOE Stadi‐P diffractometer with Cu‐Kα1 radiation at 298 K. Thermo gravimetric analysis was performed in an air atmosphere with a TA instrument SDT Q600. A dried crystalline sample was heated at a rate of 5 °C·min–1 to 600 °C and cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C·min–1. The IR spectra of 1, 1′a, and 1′′a were recorded with a Perkin‐Elmer instrument Spectrum 100. UV/Vis absorbance was collected in the solid state at room temperature with a Perkin‐Elmer Lambda 650S UV/Vis spectrometer equipped with Lab sphere integrating over the spectral range 190–900 nm using BaSO4 reflectance standards. The spectra for 1 and 1′′a were collected by dispersing the same amount of solid in a solid‐state holder.

Sorption Measurements and Analysis: CO2 and N2 sorption isotherms were measured at 195 K and 77 K, respectively, at 1 bar with a HidenIsochema (Warrington, UK) Intelligent Gravimetric Analyser (IGA). CO2 and CH4 were both collected at 273 K and 298 K at 5 bar.
