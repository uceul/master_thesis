A robust near infrared luminescent ytterbium metal–organic framework for sensing of small molecules†

Zhiyong Guo ab, Hui Xu c, Shengqun Su a, Jianfeng Cai c, Song Dang a, Shengchang Xiang b, Guodong Qian c, Hongjie Zhang *a, Michael O’Keeffe d and Banglin Chen *b

bDepartment of Chemistry, University of Texas at San Antonio, San Antonio, TX 78249-0698, USA. E-mail: banglin.chen@utsa.edu

The first near-infrared luminescent ytterbium metal–organic framework has been realized for the highly selective and sensitive sensing of small molecules.

Microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been rapidly emerging as very promising functional materials for gas storage, separation, heterogeneous catalysis, sensing and drug delivery.1–7 The unique properties of such new microporous materials are attributed to their tunable pore structures and readily immobilized functional sites such as open metal sites, Lewis basic/acidic sites for their specific recognition and thus chemical transformation. Among the diverse metal–organic frameworks, robust lanthanide metal–organic frameworks of permanent porosity have been much less realized than their transition metal MOF counterparts;8,9 this is because lanthanide ions have higher coordination numbers of typically more than six, thus the in situ formed open lanthanide sites during activation/de-solvation prefer to bind back with the oxygen and/nitrogen donors from the organic linkers, leading to condensed frameworks.

Microporous luminescent lanthanide MOFs are of interest for their diverse functions, particularly for the sensing of small molecules and ions.10–14 In fact, to make use of the luminescent open metal sites and Lewis basic pyridyl sites for their unique recognition of small molecules and cations, we have recently realized a few microporous luminescent lanthanide MOFs for their sensing applications.15 The basic principle revealed within these luminescent lanthanide MOF sensing materials indicates that such microporous lanthanide MOFs can also be principally applied to the sensing of substrates in biological systems; it is thus particularly important to explore microporous luminescent lanthanide MOFs whose luminescent properties/intensities are stable in biological environments. In this regard, the near infrared (NIR) luminescent Yb-MOFs will be practically very useful because the generated NIR emission over 980 nm from these frameworks will have high permeability through biotissues and no competing fluorescence from biomatrices.16 However, to the best of our knowledge, no microporous NIR luminescent MOFs have been realized so far for their sensing applications.17–19 To incorporate an expanded aromatic tricarboxylate into an ytterbium MOF to enforce the framework stability and maximize the “antenna effect” to enhance the NIR intensity, herein we report the first NIR luminescent MOF, Yb(BPT)(H2O)·(DMF)1.5(H2O)1.25 (MOF 1, BPT = biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylate), for sensing of small molecules.

MOF 1 was synthesized by the solvothermal reaction of Yb(NO3)3·6H2O and H3BPT in mixed solvents of dimethylformamide (DMF), ethanol, and water.‡ It was formulated as Yb(BPT)(H2O)·(DMF)1.5(H2O)1.25 based on elemental microanalysis and single-crystal X-ray diffraction (XRD) studies, and the phase purity of the bulk material was independently confirmed by powder XRD (Fig. S1).

Single crystal X-ray diffraction study revealed that MOF 1 crystallizes in a tetragonal space groupP43.§ Each ytterbium atom is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from the carboxylate groups of BPT and one terminal water molecule. Yb atoms are bridged by BPT organic linkers to form a 3D rod-packing structure. There exist one-dimensional square channels of about 7.2 × 7.2 Å along the c axis (Fig. 1c). The structure of MOF 1 has some features in common with the lanthanide rod-structure MOF-76.8a Both feature 4-fold helical rod SBUs with 7-coordinated lanthanide atoms and both have intrinsically chiral nets though the topologies are different with the RCSR symbols rnb for MOF-76 and rnc for MOF 1.20 The terminal water molecules are expected to be removed during thermal activation, leading to the formation of solvent-free MOF 1a with open Yb3+ sites for their recognition of small molecules. The high solvent-accessible volume of 1865.2 Å3 out of the 3073.04 Å3 unit cell volume (60.7% of the total crystal volume), calculated by PLATON, and the moderate pore sizes indicating such 1D channels can be assessible to a variety of small molecules.21


Fig. 1 X-ray single crystal structure of MOF 1 indicating (a) the BPT tricarboxylate linker and coordination environments of all Yb atoms related by symmetry, (b) 1D helical rod [Yb(CO2)3]n as the infinite SBU and (c) 1D micropore of about 7.2 × 7.2 Å along c axis (Yb, blue polyhedra; C, black; O, red: terminal water molecules, large red sphere).

TGA data indicate that MOF 1 releases its free and terminal solvent molecules in the temperature range of 25 to 200 °C, forming its solvent-free phase Yb(BPT)(MOF 1a). The activated Yb(BPT) with the generated open Yb3+ sites retains the identical framework structure of MOF 1 and is thermally stable up to 450 °C, which are both confirmed by TGA and temperature-dependent powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) (Fig. S2).

The robustness and the permanent micropore feature of the activated MOF 1a (activated at 200 °C under high vacuum for 24 h) were further established by N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K, which displays typical type-I sorption behavior (Fig. S3), with the BET surface area and pore volume of 515.6 m2 g−1 and 0.291 cm3 g−1, respectively. We thus examined its potential application in gas and vapor selective separation. As shown in Fig. 2a, MOF 1a takes up different amounts of C2H2 (23.9 cm3 g−1), CO2 (16.7 cm3 g−1) and CH4 (5.4 cm3 g−1) at 296 K and 1 atm, highlighting its potential application for the separation of C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4. The adsorption enthalpies of 1a to C2H2, CO2 and CH4 gases at low coverage are 30.4, 24.1 and 19.1 kJ mol−1, respectively, as calculated based on the virial method, a well established and reliable methodology, from fits of their adsorption isotherms at 273 and 296 K.22 From these results, the Henry’s Law selectivity Sij = KHi/KHj for C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 mixtures is 7.8 and 3.1, respectively. 1a also preferably adsorbs CH3OH over C2H5OH (Fig. 2b) and 1-C3H7OH over 2-C3H7OH (Fig. S6).


Fig. 2 (a) Gas sorption isotherms of MOF 1a for C2H2, CO2 and CH4 and (b) vapor sorption isotherms of MOF 1a for methanol and ethanol.

Fig. 3 (a) The PL spectra and (b) the 2F5/2→2F7/2 transition intensities of 1a introduced into various pure solvent emulsions when excited at 304 nm.

1a exhibits typical NIR emission from the 2F5/2→2F7/2 transition of the Yb3+ ion at 980 nm, when excited at 326 nm (Fig. S7). The most interesting feature is that its PL spectrum is largely dependent on the solvent molecules, particularly in the case of DMF and acetone, which exhibit the most significant enhancing and quenching effects, respectively (Fig. 3). Such solvent-dependent luminescence properties are of interest for the sensing of acetone solvent molecules. When the acetone solvent content was gradually added and increased to 1a1-propanol standard emulsion, the fluorescence intensity of the 1a emulsion gradually decreased with the addition of acetone solvent (Fig. S8). The fluorescence decrease was nearly proportional to the acetone concentration. The decreasing trend of the fluorescence intensity of the 2F5/2→2F7/2 transition of Yb3+ at 980 nm versus the volume ratio of acetone could be well fitted with a first-order exponential decay (Fig. S9), indicating that fluorescence quenching of 1a by acetone is diffusion-controlled.

In conclusion, we have successfully targeted the first robust and highly porous NIR luminescent Yb-MOF for the separation and sensing of small molecules. The highly selective and sensitive sensing function of 1a indicates the promise of this type of NIR luminescent materials for the sensing of substrates in biological systems. It is to be expected that this work will stimulate extensive research and that more microporous NIR luminescent sensing MOFs will emerge in the future.
