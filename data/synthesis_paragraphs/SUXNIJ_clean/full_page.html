An extraordinary boron-mediated 16R-channel-containing trivalent vanadium phosphite with unique solid state redox properties†

Hui-Lin Huang and Sue-Lein Wang *
Department of Chemistry, National Tsing Hua University, Hsinchu, 30013, Taiwan. E-mail: slwang@mx.nthu.edu.tw; Fax: 886-35-711082; Tel: 886-35-721074

Received 24th April 2010 , Accepted 21st June 2010

First published on 27th July 2010

For the first time, an open-framework oxysalt of trivalent vanadium is demonstrated, prepared from a single tetravalent vanadium source under acidic hydrothermal conditions. The borate-incorporated structure features intersecting 2D 16R channels and unusual solid-state redox properties confirmed by K-edge XANES, EPR spectral data and magnetic susceptibility measurements.

In recent years, the synthesis of inorganic open frameworks with occluded organic templates has been progressively developed from silicates, to germanates to phosphorus-based metal oxides (MPOs).1–5 Such crystalline microporous materials attract extensive research because they have shown great versatility in structural topology as well as functionality. With extra-large pores or channels, the demand for performing shape-selective catalysis6 on larger molecules might be fulfilled; higher capacity for hydrogen storage7 or lanthanide-free phosphors for white LEDs8 can be achieved, or even never before perceived properties will be unveiled. It is noteworthy that the phosphate system of trivalent metals possesses the most abundant record-breaking, extra-large channel structures. But these MPOs are nearly all with main-group elements i.e., M = Al and Ga, which require doping of transition metals (TM) to attain active centers for catalysis9 or other functional features. There are few MPOs with trivalent TM element as the major metal centers and to our knowledge, only one example of iron(III) phosphate has been documented before.10

To explore the possibility of using more trivalent TM ions, we have started a search into the reduced vanadium phosphite system. According to a literature survey, a very limited number of single-valence structures with VIII as the metal center have been documented in the organic templated VPO system,11 despite the fact that an abundant number of structures with tetravalent (VIV) or mixed-valence (VIII/VIV or VIV/VV) centers exist. To the best of our knowledge, only two examples of amine-VIII-phosphites have been reported before,12i.e. the 1D chain structure of (C2H10N2)[V(HPO3)F3] and the 3D 8R-channel structure of (C2H10N2)0.5[V(HPO3)2]. These two, like the other trivalent vanadium oxysalts, were typically prepared from a metal source containing pentavalent vanadium if not a direct V3+ source. In this work, we successfully employed a sole tetravalent vanadium reactant to prepare the first nanoporous structure of an unusual solid-state, redox active, trivalent vanadium phosphite, (Hcha)3(H3O)1+x[V9(H2O)6(HPO3)14−x(BO3)x (H2PO3)3]·2H2O (1, cha = C6H11NH2 and x = 0.1 ∼ 1.2), which features a unique 3D open-framework with coplanar-intersecting 16R channels (Fig. 1), high porosity and borate-incorporation (Fig. 2).

All channels have a 16R rabbit-head-like opening. Polyhedra shown in green have V3+ centers and in yellow have P3+ or B3+ centers.

Fig. 2 ORTEP drawing of 1 showing the atomic labelling scheme and different coordination geometries for V3+, P3+ and B3+ centers. The incorporated borate group on the HP(4)O3 site is depicted in the upper-right plot.

A reaction mixture of VOSO4 (1 mmol), H3BO3 (1.5 mmol), H3PO3 (6 mmol), cyclohexylamine (cha, 5 mmol), and H2O (5 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined digestion bomb (internal volume = 23 mL) and heated at 180 °C under autogenous pressure for 72 h and slowly cooled to room temperature at 6 °C h−1. The reaction yielded light green hexagonal plate-like crystals of 1 as a major phase product. By changing the ratio of V:B in a step-wise manner from the original 1:1.5 to the final 0.5:5, a mono-phased product of 1 was obtained in a yield of 65% (based on vanadium). Single-crystal structure analysis was performed to determine the 3D structure and chemical formula. The trivalent state for all three vanadium sites in 1 was confirmed by its green color, results from bond-valence-sum (BVS) calculations, observed magnetic moments, and EPR and XANES spectra (vide infra). It was noted that no trivalent vanadium oxysalts reported so far have been prepared from tetravalent vanadium. This is owing to the fact that the reduction of tetravalent vanadyl VO2+ into V3+ in acidic solution is much less favorable than the reduction of pentavalent vanadyl VO2+. It is unprecedented that successful conversion of VO2+ into V3+ was achievable in the above reactions with pH value = 2 ∼ 3. We presumed that the type of cyclic alkylamine, cha, carried adequate reducing power to make the conversion of VO2+ into V3+ possible. Thereafter, we employed several amines of this type and found reduced products as expected. With the cycloheptylamine (chpa), which is of comparable size to cha, we obtained a second 16R-channel structure of trivalent VPO that is isotypic with 1.13 The strategy of adopting a cyclic alkylamine with suitable strength in reduction power effectively achieves the reduction of V4+ to V3+ in acidic solution, which would not occur otherwise.

The extra-large 16R channels in the unique 3D framework of 1 propagate in [100], [010] and [110] directions (Fig. 1) and intersect with one another to create a large interior space for organic templates and lattice water molecules. The non-framework space was estimated to be 44.9% of its unit cell volume, comparable to the voids inside the 24R channel structure of ND-1 or NTHU-1.5 All channel openings observed in the ab plane have a rabbit-head-like shape, which resembles the clover-shaped channel openings in the renowned structure of CLO.1 The structure of 1 also features exclusively trivalent centers for all types of coordination polyhedra, namely octahedra of VO5(H2O) and VO6, pseudo-tetrahedra of HPO3 and H2PO3, and triangles of BO3. The latter were observed to share the same crystallographic site with HP(4)O3 units (Fig. 2). For the first time a borate-incorporated-VPO structure was prepared and well characterized through single-crystal data.‡ The site of B was observed to slightly deviate from that of P(4) by 0.3 Å and has a refined site occupancy factor of 21.6%, corresponding to 0.43 borons per formula for the crystal under analysis. The actual amount of incorporated boron was found to be crystal size dependent. Based on ICP-AES data, the number of boron atoms per formula could be as high as about 1.2 (equivalent to B site occupancy of 60%) for samples containing bigger crystals, while it could be as low as 0.1 (equivalent to B site occupancy of 5%) for smaller ones.14 However, no individual crystal or sample was detected without boron, which explains why boric acid is essential for the formation of compound 1.

In situ powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) studies revealed that the framework of 1 was sustainable up to 215 °C. At this temperature, however, compound 1 would lose, not only lattice water molecules, but also some coordination water ligands based on TG analysis. We then conducted thermal treatment experiments to dehydrate a sample of 1 at 170 °C under ambient pressure. Interestingly, the original light-green sample turned into a dark-green one (designated as 1T). The change in color implies oxidation of V3+ into V4+ during the dehydration process. Moreover, the light-green color almost completely returned when the sample of 1T was exposed to enough moisture, suggesting that the dehydrated oxidized phase of 1T regained the original reduced state of 1 upon rehydration. To confirm the above conjecture, we performed magnetic susceptibility measurements. Before dehydration, the effective magnetic moment (μeff) derived for 1, 2.803μB, was fitting for a pure d2 system (μs.o = 2.828μB) of V3+. Whereas after dehydration, that for 1T, 2.49μB, was smaller, indicating a mixed system of d2 of V3+ and d1 ((μs.o = 1.732μB) of V4+. The occurrence of oxidation upon dehydration was further supported by powder EPR and XANES data, which were measured on the same batch of samples before and after the thermal dehydration treatments (Fig. 3). In the EPR spectra, the sharp signal peaks at g = 1.98 confirmed the existence of V4+ in the dehydrated sample of 1T. Moreover, the K-edge XANES spectra of 1, 1T, and 1′ (compound 1 regained from 1T) revealed that the process from 1 to 1T and 1T back to 1 was nearly reversible.


Fig. 3 Solid-state EPR (left) and K-edge XANES spectra (right) for 1 (black), 1T (red) and 1′ (cyan). The inset magnifies the pre-edge range which indicates the change in oxidation states during the nearly reversible dehydration/oxidation and rehydration/reduction process.

The unique solid-state redox properties exhibited by 1 have not been encountered before. The driving force for the oxidation, accompanied by dehydration, can be two-fold. First, it should be attributed to the change in coordination. When a coordinating water ligand was removed during dehydration, the regular octahedral environment of V(2) changed from regular octahedron (the only adequate coordination geometry for V3+) into square pyramidal geometry (unsuitable for V3+ but not for V4+). Hence, there was a strong tendency of V3+ ions on the V(2) site to oxidize and hold up the framework (it would collapse otherwise). Second, the presence of borate groups assisted the development of a vanadyl group, VO, a bonding character required by V4+. In the structure, the bond valence sum (BVS) calculated on O(9) was relatively unsaturated, being in the bonding condition of B–O(9)–V(2) rather than P(4)–O(9)–V(2) (refer to Fig. 2). Oxidation of those V(2) sites connected by borate (not those by phosphite) may result in simultaneous formation of a vanadyl VO bond trans to the removed water ligand of O(10), which can be achieved by making a π bond between V(2) and O(9). Thereupon, the bond valence of O(9) was also compensated to better stabilize the partially oxidized structure (Fig. 4). The actual oxidation–reduction mechanism in the solid state can be explained in the following way. During heating, the oxide lattice of 1 takes up gaseous oxygen from the gas phase.15 The oxygen atoms need electrons to form oxide ions on the interstitial sites and the electrons are taken from the solid, which causes the V3+ centers to oxidize into V4+. The resultant reaction can be written as: V3+O5(H2O)(solid) + 1/4O2(g) → V4+O5(solid) + 1/2Oi′′(solid) + H2O, where Oi′′ denotes an interstitial defect of the oxide ion. The emergence of interstitial oxygen defects was confirmed by the broad EPR signal of 1 (referring to the gray dotted curve in Fig. 3). In perspective of reduction accompanied by rehydration, it was the reverse of the above reaction: V4+O5(solid) + 1/2Oi′′(solid) + H2O → V3+O5(H2O) + 1/4O2(g) i.e., depletion of oxygen from the lattice was preferred for coordination and energy reasons when the sixth coordinated water ligand was provided at the borate-connected V(2) sites; thereupon, the interstitial oxide ions gave up electrons to the solid so that the V4+ ions on V(2) sites were reduced back to V3+. The changes in coordination polyhedra of V(2) and corresponding reactions for the reversible dehydration/oxidation and rehydration/reduction are depicted in Fig. 4.


Fig. 4 Structural change and reaction for the reversible dehydration/oxidation and rehydration/reduction process for compound 1. The right plot depicts the partially oxidized structure containing V4+O5 (dark green square pyramids) on the V(2) sites, which are connected to the borate group (orange triangle). Light green octahedra represent V3+O5(H2O) and yellow tetrahedra represent HPO3.

In summary, we have successfully prepared a unique structure of trivalent vanadium boratophosphite with co-planar intersecting 16-ring channels and up to 45% of non-framework space. We made a breakthrough in using tetravalent vanadium as a source for the synthesis of trivalent vanadium oxysalts in acidic solution. Organic amine has been well acknowledged for its use in preventing metal oxidation in hydrothermal synthesis. But in this work the type of cyclic alkylamine was found to exert its reducing power to assist the reduction of V4+ into V3+, which has seldom been seen before. The incorporated borate is believed to play an important role in stabilizing the open framework structure upon losing partial coordination with water by heating. That is to say, it is in boron's presence that oxidation on partial V3+ centers can take place in compound 1. More interestingly, the dehydrated, oxidized form was able to return to its reduced state simply by rehydration, indicating the reduction was coordination-driven by regained coordinate water. The reversibility of the process of simultaneous dehydration with oxidation and rehydration with reduction was scrutinized by magnetic study and confirmed by EPR and XANES spectroscopic analyses. The unique redox properties may bring about potential catalytic applications or help make a water sensor chip out of compound 1. Overall, the discovery of boron-incorporated trivalent vanadium phosphite has brought us a step closer to understanding the chemistry inside. Further investigation into the preparation of nanoporous VPO structures with boron incorporation is in progress.
