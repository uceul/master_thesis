Abstract

The reaction of Keggin‐type polyoxometalate (POM) units, transition‐metal (TM) ions, and a rigid bis(imidazole) ligand (1,4‐bis(1‐imidazolyl)benzene (bimb)) in a hydrothermal environment led to the isolation of four new POM‐based metal–organic networks, [H2L][CuL][SiW12O40]⋅2 H2O (1), [H2L]2[Co(H2O)3L][SiW11CoO39]⋅6 H2O (2), KH[CuL]2[SiW11CoO39(H2O)]⋅2 H2O (3), and [CuL]4[GeW12O40]⋅H2O (4; L=bimb). All four compounds were characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analysis, powder X‐ray diffraction, and single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction. Compounds 1 and 3 are new 3D networks with 1D channels. Compounds 2 and 4 contain 2D networks, which further stack into 3D supramolecular networks. The contributions of pH value, the negative charge of the POM, and the TM coordination modes to the construction of 3D networks were elucidated by comparing the synthetic conditions and structures of compounds 1–4. The photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–4 were investigated using methylene blue (MB) degradation under UV light. All compounds showed good catalytic activity and structural stability. The possible catalytic mechanism was discussed on the basis of active‐species trapping experiments. The different photocatalytic activities of compounds 1–4 were explained by comparison of the band gaps of different POM species and different packing modes of POM units in these hybrid compounds.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks based on polyoxometalates (POMs) have attracted considerable attention in recent years. The introduction of POM units, acting not only as templates but also as nodes or pillared units, has dramatically enriched the family of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs). These compounds have merged the merits of POMs and MOFs and can potentially be applied in heterogeneous catalysis, selective absorption, electronic and magnetic materials.1-8 Especially in the field of catalysis, dispersing catalytically active POM units into MOFs can not only preserve the integrity of POM anions and improve the catalytic activity, but it can also allow catalyst recovery by reducing their solubility.9-13 POMs, transition‐metal (TM) ions, and neutral N‐donor ligands (L) are three elemental building units to construct the final structures. Usually, Keggin‐type POMs are used because of their multiple components, structural stability and strong catalytic activity.14 TM cations are important nodes because of their explicit coordination geometries, lower charge numbers, and similar coordination ability to link with POMs and organic ligands. The choice and design of organic ligands are of great importance in the construction of MOFs. Neutral N‐donor bridging ligands have been widely used to explore new POM‐based metal–organic frameworks.15-25 Three crucial points should be considered in choosing ligands: i) The coordination ability between ligands and TM ions. Initially, pyridine‐based bridging ligands were widely used in the construction of POM‐based networks. Later, imidazole‐based and multiazole‐based ligands were shown to have stronger coordination ability, which increased the number of POM‐based networks.15-18 ii) The rigidity and flexibility of the ligands. Generally, using rigid ligands is essential in constructing frameworks, because flexible ligands can easily cause distortion and instability of the frameworks, producing an entangled network, and finally making pores disappear.19-25 iii) The length of bridging ligands should be similar to the diameter of the Keggin‐type anions. If the organic ligand is too short, the pores of the metal–organic frameworks will be occupied or terminated by POM anions, making it harder to generate frameworks.26 On the basis of the above analysis, it has been found that rigid imidazole‐based bridging ligands are ideal ligands in constructing POM‐based MOFs.

Based on the aforementioned considerations, we chose 1,4‐bis(1‐imidazolyl)benzene (bimb, Scheme 1), in which benzene rigidly bridges two imidazole groups. The length of the bimb ligand (ca. 9.8 Å) is quite similar to the diameter of the Keggin‐type POM (ca. 10.4 Å). Therefore, bimb is an ideal bridging ligand for constructing Keggin‐based networks. Up to now, only a few examples using bimb ligands and Keggin anions have been reported.27-32 Thus, it is worth exploring more new examples to investigate the main factors influencing the formation of POM‐based MOFs in this synthetic system. Herein, four new hybrid compounds are reported: [H2L][CuL][SiW12O40]⋅2 H2O (1), [H2L]2[CoL(H2O)3][SiW11CoO39]⋅6 H2O (2), KH[CuL]2[SiW11CoO39(H2O)]⋅2 H2O (3), and [CuL]4[GeW12O40]⋅H2O (4; L=bimb). Compounds 1 and 3 are new POM‐based 3D networks with 1D channels. Compounds 2 and 4 contain 2D POM‐based metal–organic networks, which further stack into 3D supramolecular networks. It is noteworthy that compounds 2 and 3 are based on the cobalt‐substituted Keggin‐type POM units, which represent the first examples of TM‐substituted POM‐based MOFs in the {POM/TM/bimb} synthetic system. In 2, the Co‐substituted POM units link to form inorganic chains, acting as building blocks to construct 2D networks with Cu–bimb chains. In 3, the isolated Co‐substituted POMs act as unusual eight‐connected nodes to construct a 3D network. The photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–4 were investigated using methylene blue (MB) degradation under UV light. The possible photocatalytic mechanism was also investigated.

Results and Discussion

Crystal Structures of 1–4

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, and the crystallographically asymmetric unit consists of half [SiW12O40]4− anion, half Cu2+ ion, half bimb bridging ligand, half biprotonated guest bimb ligand, and one lattice water molecule (Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). In 1, there are two atoms in special positions. Cu(1) atom is half‐occupied and located in an inversion center (0.5, 0.5, 1.0), while Si(1) atom is half‐occupied and located in the site (0.5, −0.00085, 0.75) with twofold symmetry. The polyoxoanion unit [SiW12O40]4− exhibits typical Keggin‐type structural features. Each Cu center is six‐coordinated with four oxygen atoms derived from four SiW12 anions and two nitrogen atoms from two bimb ligands. The Cu−O bond lengths are in the range of 2.16(1)–2.28(2) Å and the Cu−N bond lengths are in the range of 1.95(8)–1.95(9) Å. The oxidation state of the CuII center is confirmed by its coordination mode and the bond valence sum (BVS) calculation. The bimb bridging ligands possess similar structural configuration linked with CuII centers. Based on above connection modes, the Keggin‐type POM units are linked by the CuII centers to form a 2D network (Figure 1 a) on the bc plane. These adjacent inorganic 2D networks are parallel with each other and further pillared by the organic bimb bridging ligands, forming a 3D network with 1D channels viewed along the b axis (Figure 1 b and Figure S2 in the Supporting Information). From the topological viewpoint, the CuII centers can be reduced to a six‐connected node and POM units can be viewed as a four‐connected node. Thus, the whole network adopts the 2‐nodal 4,6‐c net with stoichiometry (4‐c)(6‐c), and the point symbol for the net is {44.610.8}{44.62} (Figure 1 c). Furthermore, the guest protonated bimb ligands and lattice water molecules fill the vacant pores of the host 3D network (Figure 1 d). There are weak intermolecular π⋅⋅⋅π interactions between the protonated bimb guests and the bridging bimb ligands on the host network. The typical distance between two adjacent aromatic rings is ca. 3.651(1) Å. There are also extensive hydrogen bonding interactions between lattice water molecules and the surface O atoms of the polyoxoanions. The typical H‐bonds are O1W⋅⋅⋅O17 (with the distance of 2.836(2) Å) and O1W⋅⋅⋅O5 (with the distance of 2.795(2) Å, Figure S3 in the supporting information).

Compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, and the crystallographically asymmetric unit contains one Co‐substituted [SiW11CoO39]6− unit, one Co2+ ion (Co1) connected to [SiW11CoO39]6−, two half bimb bridging ligands that coordinate to Co1, three coordinated water molecules, two biprotonated guest bimb ligands and six lattice water molecules. (Figure 2 a, b and Figure S4 in the Supporting Information). In the inorganic TM‐substituted POM‐based chain, the basic building block is the Keggin‐type [SiW11CoO39]6− cluster (Figure 2 a). In such a POM cluster, there are two site‐occupancy disordered positions shared by W(50 %)/Co(50 %) centers (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information). These Co‐substituted Keggin anions are linked together through the disordered {Co‐O‐W} bridges into a chain (Figure 2 a). In the CoII–bimb metal–organic chain, each Co(1) center is six‐coordinated with two nitrogen atoms derived from two bimb ligands, one oxygen atom from the POM anion, and three coordinated water molecules. The Co−N bond lengths are in the range of 2.10(4)–2.12(6) Å, and the Co−O bond lengths are in the range of 2.04(4)–2.11(6) Å (Figure 2 b, c). In the space packing arrangement, all the inorganic TM‐substituted POM‐based chains are parallel with each other along the a axis, perpendicular and entangled with the CoII–bimb metal–organic chains extended along the b axis, forming a wavelike 2D entangled network with a square mesh. It is noteworthy that both inorganic TM‐substituted POM‐based chains and metal–organic chains are linked together via the Co(1)‐O(16)‐W(3) bridges. In this 2D network, there are also two types of protonated H2bimb ligands, which are hydrogen‐bonded with the host 2D network and fill the mesh vacancies of the 2D network (Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). The existence of such protonated H2bimb ligands further stabilizes the whole 2D network. Moreover, the adjacent wavelike 2D networks are further interlocked with each other, forming a 3D supramolecular network (Figure 3). The adjacent layers are also linked with each other through the extended hydrogen bonding interactions between coordinated water molecules on the CoII centers and the terminal O atoms on the POM units. The typical hydrogen bonds are O(1W)⋅⋅⋅O(19) 2.834(1) Å and O(3W)⋅⋅⋅O(18) 2.837(1) Å (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information). The solvent‐accessible voids in this 3D supramolecular network are occupied by the lattice water molecules.

It is worth mentioning that Keggin‐type POM‐based hybrid networks are usually constructed by isolated Keggin‐type POM units. In this case, however, the TM‐substituted POM‐based chain acts as building block, which is the first such example in the Keggin‐type POM‐based high‐dimensional composite network system.

Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, and the crystallographically asymmetric unit includes half Co‐substituted [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6− unit, one Cu2+ ion connected to [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6−, two half bimb bridging ligands that coordinate to Cu2+, half K+ cation, and one lattice water molecule. (Figure S7 in the Supporting Information). In 3, there are two atoms in special positions. Both Si(1) atom and K(1) atom are half‐occupied and located in the sites (0.5, 0.07, 0.25) and (0.5, 1.17, 0.25), respectively, with twofold symmetry. In contrast to compound 2, the Co‐substituted Keggin anion in compound 3 is an isolated anionic unit, in which there are two site‐occupancy disordered positions shared by W (75 %)/Co(25 %) (Figure S7 in the Supporting Information). In 3, the potassium cation is coordinated with eight oxygen atoms derived from the POM unit. The K−O bond lengths are in the range of 2.748(9)–2.798(10) Å. Each CuII center is five‐coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two bimb ligands and three oxygen atoms from three Keggin anions. The Cu−N bond lengths are in the range of 1.93(8)–1.97(5) Å, and the Cu−O bond lengths are in the range of 2.00(5)–2.25(3) Å. The oxidation state of CuII centers are confirmed by the BVS calculation. In this connection mode, the POM units are linked by the K+ centers into 1D chains. These 1D chains are further connected by the CuII centers into flat 2D layers on the bc plane (Figure 4 a). All these inorganic 2D layers are parallel and are further pillared by the bimb ligands into a 3D network with 1D channels extending along the b axis (Figure 4 b, c and Figure S8 in the Supporting Information).

From the topological viewpoint, the CuII centers in the network can be reduced to five‐connected nodes and POM units can be viewed as eight‐connected nodes. Thus, the whole network adopts the 2‐nodal 5,8‐c net with stoichiometry (5‐c)2(8‐c), and the point symbol for the net is {3.42.66.8}2{34.46.56.610.72} (Figure 5).

Compound 4 crystallizes in the triclinic space group Pī, and the crystallographically asymmetric unit is composed of half [GeW12O40]4− anion, two CuI ions, two bimb bridging ligands, and half lattice water molecule. (Figure 6 and Figure S9 in the Supporting Information). In 4, the Ge(1) atom is half‐occupied and located at an inversion center (0.5, 0, 0.5). The [GeW12O40]4− anion exhibits the typical Keggin‐type POM structure, in which the central {GeO4} group possesses two possible positions due to the two‐fold disorder, with each site having 50 % occupancy (Figure S9 in the Supporting Information). Each CuI center is three‐coordinate with two nitrogen atoms derived from two bimb ligands and one oxygen atoms from a [GeW12O40]4− anion. The Cu−N bond lengths are in the range of 1.85(1)–1.88(5) Å, and the Cu−O bond lengths are in the range of 2.28(1)–2.77(9) Å. The oxidation state of CuI centers are confirmed by the BVS calculation. In the above coordination mode, the CuI centers are linked by the bimb bridging ligands to form 1D chainlike building units. Furthermore, these chainlike units are connected by the Keggin‐type POM units to form a 2D layer through the {W‐O‐Cu} bridges (Figure 6 a, c).

From the topological viewpoint, the CuI centers in the 2D network can be reduced to three‐connected nodes and POM units can be viewed as four‐connected nodes. Thus, the whole network of 4 displays the 2‐nodal 3,4‐c net with stoichiometry (3‐c)4(4‐c), and the point symbol for the net is {62.8}4{62.84} (Figure 6 b). In the packing arrangement, the adjacent networks are parallel and interlocked through π⋅⋅⋅π interactions between adjacent benzene groups of the bimb ligands (Figure 7 and Figure S10 in the Supporting Information). The typical π⋅⋅⋅π interaction distance between two adjacent benzene planes is ca. 3.65 Å. The lattice water molecules reside in the interspaces of the 2D layers of 4.

In the hydrothermal reaction system, many factors can influence the final structure of the POM‐based metal–organic networks. In this case, three main factors, that is, pH value, the negative charge of POM anions, and the TM centers with different coordination modes, seem to play the most important roles in assembling the POM‐based metal–organic networks of compounds 1–4.

Influence of pH Value

Compounds 1 and 2 were synthesized in the pH range of 1.5–2.5, which led to some of the bimb ligands being biprotonated. In contrast, compounds 3 and 4 were prepared in the higher pH range of 3.0–4.0, in which all bimb ligands coordinate to TM centers. Because of the protonation, the isolated H2bimb fragments cannot link with TM centers and fully occupy the voids and channels of the final networks in compounds 1 and 2. Obviously, this is a disadvantageous aspect for the construction of networks, and strong acidic reaction conditions should be avoided during the preparation.

Influence of POM Negative Charge

Generally, the negative charge of POM anions can be modified by the substitution of addenda metal centers (e.g. WVI or MoVI) with lower‐valent TM ions. As a result, a higher negative charge of the POM anions corresponds to more TM centers in the hybrid compounds, which can act as nodes to construct high dimensional, complicated networks. Simultaneously, the POMs with high negative charge can also act as high‐connection nodes to link with TM centers, or even adjacent POM units, thus forming high‐dimensional networks. In this case, when Co‐substituted POM anions [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6− are introduced into the reaction system, they not only connect with each other to form 1D inorganic chain building blocks in compound 2, but also act as eight‐connected nodes to construct a 3D network in compound 3. Thus, the POM anions with high negative charges should have advantageous influence on the construction of high‐dimensional networks.

Influence of TM Coordination Mode

TM centers are the key nodes to link with bridging ligands, forming the final metal–organic networks. It is always feasible to introduce more TM centers into the hybrid system so as to obtain high‐dimensional or complicated networks. However, the coordination mode of TM centers is somewhat more important than the number of TM centers in the construction of high‐dimensional networks. In this case, compounds 1 and 4 use the POM anions with negative charge of −4. The copper ions in compound 1 are +2, and six‐coordinated, which led to a 3D network. However, the copper ions in compound 4 are +1, and just three‐coordinated, which results in a 2D layer. It seems that TM centers with high coordination number are a key factor for the construction of 3D networks.

Based on aforementioned analysis, we noticed that high pH synthetic conditions, high negative charge of POM species, and high connection mode of TM nodes in the {Keggin‐POM/TM/bimb} synthetic system are three advantageous factors for the construction of POM‐based 3D networks.

Photocatalytic Properties

POMs can potentially be good photocatalysts in the purification of polluted water, thus, the photocatalytic properties of compounds 1–4 of decomposing waste organic molecules were investigated. In this case, the methylene blue (MB) was used as a typical difficult‐to‐degrade dye contaminant model in waste water. Compounds 1–4 are insoluble in aqueous solution, and thus they are used as heterogeneous catalysts during the study. In the experiment, aqueous MB solution (400 mL, 10.0 mg L−1) containing one of compounds 1–4 (50 mg) as the catalyst was stirred under UV light (125 W). The blank solution without any catalyst was also monitored. As shown in Figure 8, the absorption peaks of aqueous MB solution in the presence of compounds 1–4 dramatically decreased within 90 min under UV irradiation. The plots of the ratio of remaining concentration to initial concentration (Ct/C0) versus reaction time (Figure 9) further show that the photocatalytic activities increase from 31.1 % (no catalyst) to 89.5, 85.7, 90.1, and 84.7 % for compounds 1–4, respectively, after 90 min of irradiation. These results confirm that compounds 1–4 possess excellent photocatalytic properties for the degradation of MB. In order to verify the active ingredient in the catalytic process, a series of comparative experiments were further performed by using the mixture of CuCl2⋅2 H2O and bimb, (Bu4N)4[SiW12O40], and compound 1 as photocatalysts, respectively (Figure S21 and Figure S22 in the Supporting Information). The catalytic activity of compound 1 is a little higher than that of (Bu4N)4[SiW12O40], but much higher than that of the mixture of CuCl2⋅2 H2O and bimb. This result indicates that the POM anion should be the effective species in the photocatalytic decomposition of MB. Furthermore, the catalyst lifetime was also investigated with compound 1 as a representative catalyst. In the initial experiment, the catalytic activity of compound 1 was maintained during four cycles (Figure S23 in the Supporting Information). Moreover, the stability of the catalysts was investigated based on the PXRD data. In comparison to the PXRD patterns before and after the catalytic process, there are no obvious changes, suggesting that compounds 1–4 are stable in the photocatalytic reaction system (Figure S20 in the Supporting Information).

The photocatalytic mechanism of POMs in dye molecule degradation has been proposed in previous reports33-36. Generally, the UV‐induced excited state of POMs may react with H2O to generate the active hydroxyl radical (OH⋅), which can oxidize the MB into the degradation products. Simultaneously, the reduced POM species were easily recovered into oxidative POMs by O2 in the reaction system. In order to confirm the above supposed mechanism, especially to verify that the hydroxyl radicals (OH⋅) do emerge during the reaction process and participate in the catalytic reactions, we used isopropyl alcohol (IPA) as the hydroxyl radical (OH⋅) scavenger and introduced IPA into the photocatalytic MB degradation reaction system. As shown in Figure S24 in the Supporting Information, the photocatalytic degradation of MB was dramatically depressed by the addition of 1 mm IPA (a quencher of OH⋅), which indicated that OH⋅ radicals play a crucial role in the process of MB oxidation.

Furthermore, the Keggin‐type species in compounds 1–4 display a little different photocatalytic efficiency with the sequence of [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6− (in 3)>[SiW12O40]4− (in 1)>[SiW11CoO39]n6n− (in 2)>[GeW12O40]4− (in 4). We attempted to find a clue for these differences from the structural difference of four compounds and the band gap of different POM species. The band gaps of the corresponding polyoxoanions in 1, 3, and 4 were initially determined based on the electrochemical cyclic voltammetry measurement and the solid‐state UV/Vis diffuse reflectance (Figure S25, Table S5 and Figure S26 in the Supporting Information). Since the chainlike polyoxoanion structure in 2 is different from the other three typical Keggin‐type polyoxoanions, compound 2 is not considered in this case. As shown in Table S5 and Figure S26, the band gap of [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6− (2.55 eV) is smaller than that of [SiW12O40]4− (3.24 eV). Considering that compounds 1 and 3 possess similar 3D networks, the relatively small band gap of [SiW11CoO39(H2O)]6− may promote the electron transition under light irradiation, exhibiting more active photocatalytic property. Moreover, the energy level of the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the band gap of [SiW12O40]4− (+3.27 eV and 3.24 eV) and [GeW12O40]4− (+3.18 eV and 3.11 eV) are similar to each other, suggesting similar photocatalytic activity. However, [GeW12O40]4− in compound 4 exhibits a close‐packing mode, decreasing the probability of POM units coming in contact with MB substrates. Thus, compound 4 displays a lower photocatalytic property.

It is also possible that the CuI centers in compound 4 could be oxidized by the OH⋅ radical during the catalytic process, which can decrease the whole photocatalytic activity of compound 4. The oxidation state of CuI centers before and after catalytic experiments were checked by XPS. As shown in Figure S27 in the Supporting Information), the oxidation state of CuI centers does not change before and after the catalytic process, suggesting that CuI center in compound 4 may not have an important influence on the whole catalytic activity.

Conclusions

In summary, four new POM‐based metal–organic networks have been synthesized by mixing Keggin‐type POM units, TM ions, and the rigid bimb ligand in hydrothermal reaction conditions. Compounds 2 and 3 represent the first hybrid compounds based on the Co‐substituted Keggin‐type POM species in the {Keggin‐POM/TM/bimb} reaction system. Furthermore, three important factors for the assembly of POM‐based metal–organic networks have been discussed, suggesting that high pH value, high negative charge of the POM anions, and high connection mode of TM centers may play key roles in constructing POM‐based 3D networks. All four compounds exhibit good photocatalytic activity and high structural stability in degrading MB under UV irradiation. POM clusters have been confirmed as the active catalytic species. The proposed mechanism is that the UV‐induced excited state of the POMs reacts with H2O to generate the active hydroxyl radical (OH⋅), leading to the oxidization of MB. Furthermore, the different photocatalytic activities of compounds 1–4 are related to the different band gaps of the POM species and the arrangement of POM units in hybrid compounds. These results indicate that the modification of band gaps of POM species and construction of POM‐based 3D networks may pave new ways to explore better catalyst systems for photocatalytic dye degradation. This work is still ongoing in our group.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods

All chemicals and organic solvents used for synthesis were of reagent grade without further purification. H4[SiW12O40]⋅26 H2O, K6[SiW11CoO39(H2O)]⋅3 H2O and K4[GeW12O40]⋅14 H2O were synthesized according to the literature methods and characterized by IR spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis.37, 38 The 1,4‐bis(1‐imidazolyl)benzene (bimb) ligand was prepared by the literature method.39 Element analyses (C, H, and N) were performed using a PerkinElmer 2400 CHN element analyzer. The FTIR spectra were analyzed with KBr pellets using a Mattson Alpha‐Centauri spectrometer in the range 400–4000 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out under a N2 flow using a Paris Diamond TG instrument at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) studies were performed with a Rigaku D/max‐IIB X‐ray diffractometer with a scanning rate of 1° per minute ranging from 5° to 50°, using CuKα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å). Photocatalytic experiments in aqueous solution were carried out in a 500 mL water‐cooled cylindrical quartz container. The reaction mixture in the container was kept at room temperature by a continuous flow of water through an external cooling coil. A high‐pressure 125 W mercury lamp was used as the UV light source. A suspension of powdered catalyst (50 mg) in a fresh aqueous solution of methylene blue (MB; 300 mL, 10.0 mg l−1) was magnetically stirred in the dark for 30 min before irradiation to establish an adsorption/desorption equilibrium of MB on the sample surface. At given intervals (15 min) during the irradiation period, aliquots of 3 mL were removed from the beaker and analyzed by UV/Vis spectroscopy. The organic dye concentration was estimated from the absorbance at λ=665 nm.

X‐Ray Crystallography

Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction data for compounds 1–4 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at a temperature of 298(2) K by using an ω scan technique with MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å). All structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 using full‐matrix least‐squares methods in the SHELXTL package.40, 41 During the refinement of compounds 1–4, nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, except for some lattice water molecules. The hydrogen atoms on organic carbon centers were fixed in calculated positions. Hydrogen atoms on lattice water molecules cannot be assigned from the weak reflection peaks but were directly included into the final molecular formula. During the refinement of compounds 2 and 3, the commands “EXYZ” and “EADP” were introduced to refine the site‐occupancy disorder positions of W/Co sites on the POM units. On the POM anions of compound 4, a number of O centers (O5–O15) are disordered in two possible positions, and their site occupancies were refined accordingly. Note that the restraint commands “ISOR” were introduced in the refinement of compounds 1–4 to solve the obvious NPD and ADP problems on many O, N, and C atoms. Furthermore, the bond lengths and angles of bimb ligands were also refined with a series of restrained commands including “AFIX 59”, “AFIX 69”, “DELU” and “DFIX”. All above restrained refinements led to relatively high restraint values of 317, 574, 257, and 408 in compounds 1–4, respectively. In the final refinement, the contributions of disordered solvent to the structure factors in compounds 2 and 3 were removed with the SQUEEZE program,42 and the crystal structures were then further refined with new hkl files generated by the SQUEEZE calculation. Based on the elemental analysis, TG analysis, and the SQUEEZE calculation results, another four and two water molecules were directly included in the final molecular formula of 2 and 3, respectively. Crystal data and structure refinement for 1–4 are given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for 1–4 are listed in the Supporting Information, Tables S1–S4.
