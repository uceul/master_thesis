Three new three-dimensional organic–inorganic hybrid compounds based on PMo12O40n− (n = 3 or 4) polyanions and CuI–pyrazine/CuI–pyrazine–Cl porous coordination polymers†

Ming-Li Qi ab, Kai Yu ab, Zhan-Hua Su ab, Chun-Xiao Wang ab, Chun-Mei Wang ab, Bai-Bin Zhou *ab and Chun-Cheng Zhu *ab
aKey Laboratory for Photonic and Electronic Bandgap Materials, Ministry of Education, Harbin 150080, P. R. China. E-mail: zhou_bai_bin@163.com; zhuccshs@yahoo.com.cn
bKey Laboratory of Synthesis of Functional Materials and Green Catalysis, College of Heilongjiang Province, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Harbin Normal University, Harbin 150080, P. R. China

Received 26th January 2013 , Accepted 4th March 2013

First published on 5th March 2013

Three new organic–inorganic hybrid compounds based on PMo12O40n− (n = 3 or 4) polyanions and CuI–pz/CuI–pz–Cl porous coordination polymers: [CuI(pz)]3[PMoVI12O40] (1), [CuI(pz)1.5]4[PMoVMoVI11O40]·pz·2H2O (2), [CuI3(pz)3Cl][CuI2(pz)3(H2O)][PMoVMoVI11O40] (3) (pz = pyrazine) have been hydrothermally prepared and characterized by elemental analysis, IR, TG, XRD, XPS and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 presents a three-dimensional CuI–pz framework with cube-like chambers, into which PMoVI12O403− Keggin ions are incorporated. Compound 2 shows a three-dimensional sandwich-like framework, and PMoVMoVI11O404− polyanions are located in the octagonal voids of every two-dimensional CuI–pz 4182 network structure. Compound 3 exhibits a two-dimensional Cl-bridged CuI–pz–Cl double-layer structure, and two kinds of PMoVMoVI11O404− polyanions as bridging linkers connect two adjacent double-layers to form a three-dimensional organic–inorganic framework through CuI–O bonds. Additionally, their electrochemical characters, electrocatalytic behaviors and solid state fluorescent properties at room temperature have been investigated in detail.

Introduction

As metal–oxygen cluster species, polyoxometalates (POMs) have been widely used as molecular building blocks for the synthesis of extensive inorganic–organic hybrid materials due to their unequalled structural variety and diversity in composition, sizes and shapes, which endow them with a large number of applications in acid and photo-oxidation catalysts, electronic sensors, molecular conductors, single molecule magnets, ferroelectric response and other optical activities.1–6 Recently, the construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has been a field of rapid growth owing to their controllable, adjustable pore size/shape, chemical functionality, and potential applications, for example, catalysis, separation, chemical sensors, gas storage, molecular recognition and luminescent function.7 Hence, using the coordination ability of POMs to combine with kinds of metal–organic units is a prominent approach for constructing multifunctional materials.8–10 This method may bring together the merits of each, such as structural diversity with unique framework topologies and the combination of the unique physical and chemistry properties of POMs with the features of MOFs.11,12 Many efforts have focused on the construction of such coordination polymers in the past decades, and to date, a variety of POM-based coordination polymers with interesting structure and properties have been widely reported, in which POMs as templates or inorganic building blocks induce the self-assembly of the MOFs.13–17 However, in comparison with other inorganic–organic coordination polymers, the rational design and assembly of POMs-based coordination polymers still remains an arduous task for coordination chemists. There are many important influence factors on the structure of POM-based hybrids, for instance, the use of different POM anions,18 the nature of organic ligands,19 the coordination geometry of the metal ions,20 the topological and geometrical relations between the metal ions and the ligand,21 the pH values of the reaction system,22 and so on.

In this work, we chose PMo12O40n− polyanion, copper(I) ion and pyrazine ligand (pz). The following points are taken into consideration: (i) compared to a lot of reported inorganic–organic hybrid compounds based on Keggin-type POMs, compounds containing PMo12O40n− POM remain comparatively fewer and only a few compounds have been reported;23–25 (ii) the Cu ion, as a soft Lewis acidic metal ion, generally shows a variable coordination number and tunable coordination sphere; (iii) pyrazine (pz), as a kind of small rigid bridging ligand, commonly exhibits simple and controllable coordination modes to combine various transition metal cations18a,20a,26–28 and possesses small steric hindrance which may spare more space to accommodate the POM templates with large size. Herein, we have successfully synthesized three novel organic–inorganic hybrid compounds based on PMo12O40n− polyanions and CuI–pz/CuI–pz–Cl porous coordination polymers, namely, [CuI(pz)]3[PMoVI12O40] (1), [CuI(pz)1.5]4[PMoVMoVI11O40]·pz·2H2O (2), [CuI3(pz)3Cl][CuI2(pz)3(H2O)][PMoVMoVI11O40] (3). Their electrochemical characters, electrocatalytic behaviors and fluorescent properties in the solid state at room temperature have been studied, indicating that all the title compounds have good electrocatalytic activity and are potential fluorescent-emitting materials.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. H3PMo12O40·13H2O was prepared according to a literature method29 and verified by the IR spectrum. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. P, Mo and Cu analyses were performed on a PLASMA-SPEC (I) inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometer. The IR spectra were obtained on an Alpha Centaurt FT/IR spectrometer with KBr pellet in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. The thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in N2 on a Perkin-Elmer DTA 1700 differential thermal analyzer with a rate of 10 °C min−1. XRD patterns were collected on a Rigaku Dmax 2000 X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.154 nm) and 2θ ranging from 5 to 50°. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analyses were performed on a VG ESCALAB MK II spectrometer with an MgKα (1253.6 eV) achromatic X-ray source. The vacuum inside the analysis chamber was maintained at 6.2 × 10−6 Pa during analysis. Fluorescence spectra were performed on a Hitachi F-4500 fluorescence/phosphorescence spectrophotometer with a 450 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. Electrochemical measurements were performed with a CHI660 electrochemical workstation. A conventional three-electrode system was used. The working electrode was a carbon paste electrode (CPE), a Pt wire as the counter electrode and Ag/AgCl (3 M KCl) electrode was used as a reference electrode.

Synthesis

Compound 1. A mixture of H3PMo12O40·13H2O (0.6178 g, 0.3 mmol), CuCl2·2H2O (0.0686 g, 0.4 mmol), pz (0.026 g, 0.2 mmol) and H2O (18 mL) was stirred for 30 min in air until it was homogeneous. When the pH value of the mixture was adjusted to about 4 with 1 M HCl, the solution was transferred and sealed in a 30 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave, which was heated at 160 °C under autogenous pressure for 4 days. The dark block crystals were isolated and collected by filtration, washed thoroughly with distilled water, and dried at room temperature (63% yield based on Cu). Anal. Preparation of 1-, 2- and 3-CPEs

Complexes 1, 2 and 3 modified carbon paste electrode 1- to 3-CPEs were fabricated as follows: 300 mg of graphite powder and 30 mg of complex were mixed and ground together by agate mortar and pestle to achieve a uniform mixture. To the mixture, three drops of nujol were added with stirring. The homogenized mixture was packed into a glass tube with 3 mm inner diameter, and the surface was pressed tightly onto weighing paper with a copper rod through the back. Electrical contact was established with a copper rod through the back of the electrode.

X-ray crystallography

The crystal structures of the three compounds were determined from single-crystal X-ray diffraction data. Intensity data were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromatized Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods and difference Fourier map with SHELXL-97 program,30 and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2. Anisotropic thermal parameters were used to refine all non-hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen atoms on C and N atoms of the organic ligands were included in their calculated positions. Hydrogen atoms attached to hydroxyl group and lattice water molecules were found from the difference Fourier maps. Results and discussion

Synthesis

Compounds 1–3 were synthesized under similar hydrothermal conditions. Parallel experiments show that there are two key factors influencing the formation of the resulting products: one is the pH value, and the other is the molar ratio of the starting materials (refer to Scheme 1). In our work, the pH of the reaction solution must be kept under acidic environment (pH ∼ 2–5). At the beginning, no crystal was formed at higher pH (pH > 5). Possibly, the higher pH value does not favor the stability of the PMo12O40n− polyoxoanions. Compound 1 was prepared in the pH range 4–5. In 1, there is a kind of Cu(I) center which is two-coordinated by two N atoms of two pz ligands, so that 1-D straight chains along different spatial directions are generated. When the pH value was decreased in range of 3–4, compound 2 was obtained. In 2, there is a kind of Cu(I) center which is tri-coordinated by three N atoms of three pz ligands, thus an unusual 2D 4182 network is formed; whereas when the pH value was decreased in range of 2–3, another new compound 3 was prepared. In 3, there are five kinds of Cu(I) center which are all four-coordinated by N and O/Cl atoms, then 2-D Cl-bridged CuI–pz–Cl double-layer structures are bridged by two kinds of Keggin polyanions to form a three-dimensional organic–inorganic framework. These results demonstrated that the increase in the acidity of the solution is favorable to further coordination of the Cu ions with pz and POM. However, when the pH of the reaction solution is lower than 2, only 0-D compound constructed from {PMo12O40} and isolated Hpz could be obtained. Maybe at lower pH values, the pz groups tend to be protonated, which restricts them to bonding to CuII ions. In addition, the selection of a suitable molar ratio of the starting materials is the second key factor in our syntheses of the title compounds, especially the molar ratio of H3PMo12O40·13H2O, CuCl2·2H2O and pz. The optimal molar ratio of H3PMo12O40·13H2O, CuCl2·2H2O and pz is about 3:4:2, 3:8:4 and 3:6:4 for 1, 2 and 3, respectively.

Crystal structure

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that all the compounds contain Keggin polyoxoanions PMo12O40n− as the fundamental building blocks, which is composed of a central PO4 tetrahedron surrounded by four vertex-sharing Mo3O13 trimers emanating from the association of three edge-sharing MoO6 octahedra. In compounds 1–3, the PMo12O40n− polyoxoanion exhibits a distorted Keggin structure. The P atom is located at the inversion center and surrounded by a cube of eight oxygen atoms with each oxygen site half-occupied. The P–O distances range from 1.491(8) to 1.63(2) Å and O–P–O bond angles from 66.5(4) to 180.0(8)°. These data reveal that the polyoxoanion in 1–3 is slightly distorted. This structure feature often appears in Keggin structures, which has been explained by several groups.31,32 All Mo atoms have a distorted {MoO6} octahedral environment. The Mo–O distances can be grouped into three sets according to their bonding features: Mo–Od (terminal) 1.653(6)–1.687(5) Å, Mo–Ob/c (bridge) 1.830(6)–1.984(6) Å and Mo–Oa (central) 2.409(11)–2.516(14) Å.

In addition, bond valence sum calculations33 show that: the oxidation state of all the Cu atoms in compounds 1–3 is +I; all Mo atoms in compound 1 exhibit the oxidation state +VI (average calculated value = 5.99), while in compound 2 and 3, one Mo atom is in the +V oxidation state while other Mo atoms are in the +VI oxidation state, further confirmed by XPS spectra, charge balance, coordination environments and crystal color. The changes of CuII→CuI and MoVI→MoV may be closely related to the reducibility of the pz ligand. The phenomenon frequently appeared in the containing N-donor ligand system under hydrothermal conditions.18a,34

Compound 1 consists of three copper(I) ions, three pz ligands, one PMo12O403− anion, as shown in Fig. S1.† Crystal structure analysis reveals that each independent copper(I) center is two-coordinated by two nitrogen atoms of two pz ligands and each pz ligand is two-coordinated by two copper(I) centers, forming an infinite chain. The Cu–N distance is 1.890(8) Å. Interestingly, these CuI/pz chains can be categorized into three groups: a series of parallel chains stretching straight along the crystallographic a, b and c axes, respectively, as shown in Fig. 1a. Consequently, these criss-cross CuI/pz arrays give rise to cubic-like chambers with dimension 13.0382 × 13.0382 × 1 3.0382 Å, in which the PMo12O403− anions as a ‘guest’ happen to be embedded to stabilize the whole structure (Fig. 1b). Each PMo12O403− anion surrounded by six adjacent chains of [Cu(pz)]nn+, and each chain surrounded by four sets of chains generates a 3D structure. In the 3D structure, the parallel chains made of PMo12O403− anions are arranged in ABAB mode, so as to form two kinds of voids: one is round, the other is flower-like (Fig. 1c).

Compound 2 consists of four copper(I) ions, seven pz ligands, one PMo12O404− anion, and two water molecules (Fig. S2†). As shown in Fig. 2a, each copper(I) atom is tri-coordinated by three nitrogen atoms of three pz ligands and each pz ligand is two-coordinated by two copper(I) atoms, which results in an unusual 2D 4182 network with each copper(I) center surrounded by one square void (6.8260 × 6.9287 Å) and two octagonal voids (14.0875 × 17.4966 Å), respectively. The bond length of Cu–N is 1.938 (10)–2.096(11) Å. An interesting feature of this structure is the PMo12O404− polyanions rightly locate on the octagonal voids in every layer structure (Fig. 3a). At the same time, each PMo12O404− polyanion in the octagonal void directly faces the square void in the adjacent layer (Fig. S3†). As a result, two adjacent layers are further connected via the direct collaboration of the PMo12O404− anions in one layer and the remaining voids in the adjacent layers, inducing a 3D structure. The distance of the adjacent layers is 12.0436 Å (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, the isolated pz ligands and water molecules regularly fill in the residual cavities between the adjacent layers in the manner of every one pz ligand and two water molecules for a group, as illustrated in Fig. 3b. The whole 3D structure looks like a sandwich. Seen from the b axis, the groups of pz ligands and water molecules just face the square voids in the adjacent layers (Fig. 3a). It was noted that there is only a report of a 4182 network in CuI–pz complexes in the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD) up to now.28

Fig. 3 (a) Stick/polyhedral view of the 3D structure of 2 along the crystallographic b-axis. (b) Stick/polyhedral view showing the arrangement of the adjacent layers, the isolated pz ligands and the water molecules in 2. All H atoms were omitted for clarity.

Compound 3 consists of five copper(I) ions, one Cl atom, six pz ligands, one PMo12O404− anion, and one water molecules, as shown in Fig. S4.† Crystal structure analysis reveals that there are five crystallographically independent copper(I) centers in 3, and all the Cu ions are four-coordinated in a tetrahedron mode: the Cu1 ion is coordinated by three N atoms from three pz ligands and one Cl atom; the Cu2 ion is coordinated by three N atoms from three pz ligands and one O atom from one water molecule; the Cu3 ion is coordinated by one N atom from one pz ligand, one Cl atom and two O atoms from two PMo12O404− anions; the Cu4 ion is coordinated by three N atoms from three pz ligands and one O atom from one PMo12O404− anion; the Cu5 ion is coordinated by two N atoms from two pz ligands, one Cl atom and one O atom from one PMo12O404− anion. Besides, each Cl atom is three-coordinated by Cu1, Cu3 and Cu5 ions; each pz ligand is two-coordinated by two copper ions. All bond distances of Cu–N are in the range of 1.923(6)–2.505(2) Å; the bond length of Cu–Cl is 2.1541(19)–2.529(2) Å; the bond length of Cu–O is 2.141(5)–2.450(11) Å. Firstly, copper(I) ions, Cl atoms, pz ligands, and water molecules construct a layer structure, in which there are two kinds of rings: the larger one (A) is a decagon unit constituted of six pz ligands, two Cl atoms, one O atom and eight Cu ions with the dimensions of 16.4194(15) × 11.6554(20) Å; the smaller one (B) is a hexagon unit including six pz ligands, six Cu ions and two O atoms (13.0677(17) × 11.6554(108) Å), and they are axisymmetrical about the Cu1–Cu2 axis and the Cu4–Cu5 axis, respectively, as shown in Fig. 4. The adjacent A rings form a chain through sharing three Cu ions, one Cl atom and one pz; the adjacent B rings form a chain through sharing two Cu ions and one pz, too; the adjacent A ring and B ring share two Cu ions and one pz so as to form the layer. Secondly, two adjacent layers are further formed into a double-layer structure through the bridging interaction between the Cl atom and Cu1 ion. Seen from the b axis, the double-layers show central symmetry but interlace with each other. In the double-layers structure, there additionally exist three kinds of rings, which are almost vertical to the layer constructed from A and B rings: the C ring is built by four pz ligands, two Cl atoms, two O atoms and six Cu ions (12.0451(22) × 8.6604(16) Å); the D ring includes four pz ligands, six Cu ions and two Cl atoms (12.7600(27) × 9.6875(16) Å); the E ring is constructed from four pz ligands, six Cu ions, two Cl atoms and one O atom (11.6554(33) × 9.1854(15) Å), all of them are octagonal, C and D rings are center symmetrical figures, and the E ring is axisymmetrical about the Cu2–Cu4 axis (Fig. 5). Therefore, compound 3 has five kinds of rings in all. Furthermore, PMo12O404− anions as bridging linkers connect two adjacent double-layers to form a 3D organic–inorganic framework through Cu–O bonds. It is noteworthy that there exist two kinds of PMo12O404− anions of different coordination ways: one as a bidentate bridging ligand uses its two terminal oxygen atoms (O2) to connect two Cu4 ions from two hexagon cavities in different layers of the double-layer structure; the other as a hexa-dentate bridging ligand uses its two terminal oxygen atoms (O17) and two bridge oxygen atoms (O30) to connect four Cu ions (Cu5 and Cu3) from two decagon cavities in different layers of the double-layer structure, then uses its two terminal oxygen atoms (O3) to connect two Cu3 ions from two decagon cavities in different layers of the double-layer structure (Fig. 6).

IR spectra

The IR spectra of compounds 1–3 are shown in Fig. S5.† All of them exhibit the typical bands of the Keggin anions in the 700–1100 cm−1 region of the spectra, characteristic bands at 964, 873, 790, and 1063 cm−1 for 1, 962, 877, 801, and 1064 cm−1 for 2 and 955, 851, 780, and 1057 cm−1 for 3, corresponded to ν(Mo–Od), ν(Mo–Ob–Mo), ν(Mo–Oc–Mo) and ν(P–O) of the PMo12O40n− polyanions, respectively.29 The peaks from 1100 to 1610 cm−1 could be assigned to the characteristics of the pz ligands.27

XRD

The simulated and experimental XRD patterns of the title compounds are shown in Fig. S6.† From that, we see that the diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns of 1–3 match in the key positions, respectively, indicating the phase purities of the three compounds. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the powder samples.

XPS spectra

The oxidation states of Cu and Mo are further confirmed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements (Fig. S7†). The XPS spectrum of 1 presents two peaks at 933.6 eV and 953.3 eV, in the Cu2p region, which should be ascribed to Cu+ (Fig. S7a†).35 The XPS spectrum presents two peaks at 232.4 eV and 235.6 eV, in the Mo3d region, which should be assigned to Mo6+ (Fig. S7b†).35 The XPS spectrum of 2 exhibits two peaks at 933.8 eV and 953.6 eV in the Cu2p region, which are attributed to Cu+ (Fig. S7c†).35 The XPS spectrum presents four overlapped peaks at 235.7 eV, 234.3 eV, 232.5 eV and 231.3 eV in the Mo3d region, which should be ascribed to the mixture of Mo5+ and Mo6+ (Fig. S7d†).35 The XPS spectrum of 3 is also similar to that of 2. It presents two peaks at 953.0 eV and 933.1 eV in the Cu2p region which should be assigned to Cu+ (Fig. S7e†).35 The XPS spectrum presents four overlapping peaks at 235.5 eV, 234.2 eV, 232.3 eV and 231.2 eV in the Mo3d region, which should be ascribed to the mixture of Mo5+ and Mo6+ (Fig. S7f†).35 All these results further confirm the valence sum calculations and the structural analyses.

Thermal analyses

The thermal stabilities of compounds 1–3 were determined under the nitrogen atmosphere by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (see Fig. S8†), and were carried out from 25 to 800 °C. For compound 1, the TGA curve displays one weight loss process. The weight loss (10.45%) from 240 to 445 °C is attributed to the removal of the pz ligands (Calcd: 10.65%). The decomposition of 2 mainly proceeded in two stages. The initial weight loss of 1.73% in the temperature range of 79–141 °C corresponds to the elimination of all lattice water molecules, which is in accordance with the calculated value of 1.35%. The second weight loss (20.23%) from 221 to 537 °C corresponds to the pz ligands (Calcd: 20.95%). For compound 3, the weight loss of 17.84% (Calcd: 18.62%) in the temperature range of 67–546 °C corresponds to the loss of coordinated water molecules and decomposition of pz ligands. These results further confirm the formula of compounds 1–3.

Fluorescent properties

The fluorescent properties of compounds 1–3 have been investigated. Fig. 7 presents the fluorescent spectra of 1–3 in the solid state at room temperature. It can be observed that the maximum emission wavelength located at 422 nm (λex = 371 nm) for 1, 423 nm (λex = 372 nm) for 2 and 422 nm (λex = 371 nm) for 3, which are similar to the emission band of pz ligands with a maximum wavelength of 410 nm (λex = 346 nm).36 It suggests that the luminescence of 1–3 mainly originates from ligand-to-metal charge transfer and/or the intra ligand (π*–π) fluorescent emission.37 The results indicate that all the title compounds are potential fluorescent-emitting materials.

Voltammetric behavior of 1- to 3-CPEs in aqueous electrolyte

The electrochemical behavior of 1- to 3-CPEs has been studied in detail. The cyclic voltammograms for them in 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates are presented in Fig. S9.† Three reversible redox peaks appear in the potential range from −0.8 to 1.0 V for 1, from −0.7 to 0.9 V for 2 and from −0.8 to 1.0 V for 3. The half-wave potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 (scan rate: 20 mV s−1) are 0.4968 (I–I′), 0.0927 (II–II′) and −0.2442 (III–III′) V for 1-CPE, 0.4957 (I–I′), −0.0003 (II–II′) and −0.2659 (III–III′) V for 2-CPE, 0.4811 (I–I′), 0.0390 (II–II′) and −0.2966 (III–III′) V for 3-CPE, respectively, which are responsible for the redox processes of the Mo(VI/V) couples from PMo12.38 Compared to the reported PMo12O40n− system, the potential of the three redox peaks in 1- to 3-CPEs slightly shifts due to the incorporation of the copper ions. However, the expected oxidation peak of the copper centers is not observed perhaps due to the overlap of the MoVI/MoV redox peak. This phenomenon was also usually observed in the similar PMo/Cu systems.39 Furthermore, the peak potentials vary gradually when the scan rate was increased from 20 to 120 mV s−1: the cathodic peak potentials shift to the negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials to the positive direction. Namely, the peak-to-peak separation between the corresponding cathodic and anodic peaks increased, but the average peak potentials did not change on the whole.

Electrocatalytic activity of 1- to 3-CPEs for the reduction of H2O2

Fig. 8 shows cyclic voltammograms for the electrocatalytic reduction of hydrogen peroxide at 1-, 2- and 3-CPEs in 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution in the potential range from −0.8 to 1.0 V for 1, from −0.7 to 0.9 V for 2 and from −0.8 to 1.0 V for 3. It can be clearly seen that with increasing H2O2 concentration (from 0.0 to 8.0 mM), all three reduction peak currents gradually increased while the corresponding oxidation peak currents markedly decreased, suggesting that H2O2 is mediated by the reduced species of PMo12O40n− polyanions. It is worthwhile to note that the catalytic activities were improved with the increasing extent of the POM anion reduction. In contrast, the reduction of H2O2 at a bare electrode generally requires a large overpotential and no obvious response was observed at a bare CPE. The results indicate that 1- to 3-CPEs have good electrocatalytic activity toward the reduction of H2O2.
Fig. 8 Cyclic voltammograms of the 1- to 3-CPEs in 1 M H2SO4 solution containing 0.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0 mM H2O2, scan rate: 40 mV s−1.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have prepared and structurally characterized three new organic–inorganic hybrid compounds based on PMo12O40n− polyanions and CuI–pz/CuI–pz–Cl coordination polymers. In compounds 1–3, Keggin-type POMs (PMo12O40n−) together with CuI–pz/CuI–pz–Cl coordination polymers form three unprecedented 3D frameworks. The successful synthesis of the three compounds may provide a new model for the construction of POM-based organic–inorganic hybrid compounds and further studies are being conducted in our laboratory.
