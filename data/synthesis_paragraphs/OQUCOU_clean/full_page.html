Volume 241, September 2016, Pages 105-109

A pair of novel Cd(II) enantiomers based on lactate derivatives: Synthesis, crystal structures and properties

Author links open overlay panelZhong-XuanXuabKe-HouAoaJianZhangb
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jssc.2016.06.001Get rights and content

Enantiomers

Abstract

A pair of novel 3D homochiral metal−organic frameworks (HMOFs), namely [Cd2.5((R)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O (1-D), [Cd2.5((S)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O (1-L), have been synthesized using lactic acid derivative ligands ((R)-H3CIA and (S)-H3CIA) and 1,4-DIB. Crystallographic analyses indicate that the complexes 1-D and 1-L are packed by cage substructures. Some physical characteristics, such as solid-state circular dichroism (CD), thermal stabilities and photoluminescent properties are also investigated. Our results highlight the effective method to apply lactic acid derivative ligands to form interesting HMOFs.

Graphical abstract

Using lactic acid derivative ligands ((R)-H3CIA and (S)-H3CIA) and 1,4-DIB to assemble with Cd2+ ions, a pair of novel 3D homochiral metal-organic frameworks (HMOFs) with cage substructures have been synthesized.

1. Introduction

In the field of supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering, homochiral metal organic frameworks (HMOFs) have received extensive attention not only for their fascinating architectures but also for potential applications in enantioselective processes, nonlinear optical materials and so on [1]. Thus far, tremendous efforts have been devoted to obtain HMOFs and three general approaches have been developed, namely spontaneous resolution, chiral induction and use of enantiopure ligands [2]. Among these synthesis methods, self-assembly of enantiopure linkers with metal ions has proved to be the most effective strategy [3]. So rational design and selection of chiral ligands is a key factor to synthesize functional HMOFs.

Naturally abundant enantiopure organic molecules, such as amino acids, lactic acid, tartaric acid and so on, should be the ideal linkers for the formation of HMOFs because they are inexpensive, nontoxic and readily available [4]. However, it is difficult to develop multidimensional HMOFs from these natural products for their flexibility and typical small size [5]. In order to overcome these shortcomings, we have presented a relatively simple and cheap synthesis procedure for chiral ligands ((S)-H3CIA and (R)-H3CIA) using lactic acid and dimethyl 5-hydroxyisophthalat as starting materials [6]. Containing lactic acid and isophthalate units, (S)-H3CIA and (R)-H3CIA have provided a feasible approach to construct 3D HMOFs [7]. Furthermore, adding some auxiliary ligands in the system of metal ions and chiral linkers is also an effective approach to construct higher dimensional HMOFs [8] (Scheme 1).

Based on the above considerations, we chose a C2 symmetric rigid ligand, 4-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl) benzene (1,4-DIB), to help (S)-H3CIA and (R)-H3CIA to build HMOFs. Under the hydrothermal condition, a pair of Cd-based HMOFs have been obtained, namely [Cd2.5((R)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O(1-D) and [Cd2.5((S)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O(1-L). Their synthesis, crystal structures, CD spectra, thermal stabilities and luminescent properties are described blow.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and general methods

The chiral ligands (R)-H3CIA and (S)-H3CIA were prepared from l-(−)-lactic acid methyl ester, d-(−)-lactic acid methyl ester and dimethyl 5-hydroxyisophthalate as described previously by our group [6]. The other reagents and solvents were purchased commercially without further purification, unless otherwise indicated. All Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded on a Rigaku Dmax2500 diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ=1.54056 Å) in a range of 3.00–50.00°. Thermal stability studies were performed on a NETSCHZ STA-449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C/min from 25°C to 800°C under nitrogen. FT-IR spectra were measured as KBr pellets on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR spectrometer in the range of 400–4000 cm−1. The solid-state circular dichroism(CD) spectra were carried out on a MOS-450 spectropolarimeter. Elemental analyses were performed by the analysis center of Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter. Luminescence spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu RF-5301 spectrophotometer.

2.2. Synthesis of [Cd2.5((R)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O(1−D)

The mixture of Cd(CH3COO)2·2H2O (0.2 mmol, 0.053 g), (R)-H3CIA (0.026 g, 0.1 mmol), 1, 4-DIB (0.15 mmol, 0.032 g), DMA(3 ml) and H2O (1 ml) was sealed in a 20 ml vial and heated to 100 °C for three days. After cooling to room-temperature, the colourless block crystals 1-D were separated from the mixture by filtration (Yield: 35%). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for 1-D (C36H31Cd2.50N5O16.50): C 40.08, H 2.90, N 6.49; found: C 38.76, H 3.02, N 6.27. IR (solid KBr pellet, cm−1): 3426.5 s, 1612.1 s, 1569.2 s, 1528.5 s, 1451.8 m, 1381.4 s, 1305.1 m, 1257.7 m, 1132.1 m, 1070.3 m, 779.2 m, 725.1w.

2.3. Synthesis of [Cd2.5((S)-CIA)6(1,4-DIB)(H2O)2]·((CH3)2NH2)·H2O(1-L)

The same procedure as 1-D, except (S)-H3CIA was used. The colourless block crystals 1-L were separated from the mixture by filtration (Yield: 40%). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for 1-L (Cd4C46H45N8O20.50): C 40.08, H 2.90, N 6.49; found: C 38.54, H 3.12, N 6.12. IR (solid KBr pellet, cm−1): 3433.5 s, 1603.2 s, 1569.2 s, 1528.5 s, 1451.8 m, 1381.9 s, 1305.3 m, 1257.7 m, 1126.7 m, 1070.7 m, 772.2 m, 731.8w.

2.4. X-ray determination

Single crystal X-ray data of compounds 1-D and 1-L were collected at room temperature on a SuperNova CCD diffractometer using graphite monochromated CuKα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å). The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 full-matrix least-squares by the SHELXTL-97 program package [9]. Some disordered guest molecules in compounds 1-D and 1-L were subtracted from the reflection data by the SQUEEZE method for they cannot be further identified [10]. The crystal and refinement data were summarized in Table 1.

3. Results and discussion

X-ray crystallography have revealed that complexes 1-D and 1-L crystallize in the monoclinic space group C2 with Flack parameters of −0.017(10) and −0.018(5), respectively, which indicate enantiomeric purity of the single crystals (Table 1). The complexes 1-D and 1-L are enantiomers, so we mainly focused on the structural details of 1-D. The asymmetric unit of 1-D contains two and a half CdⅡions, two (R)-CIA3− ligands, a 1,4-DIB ligand, two coordinated water molecules and a guest water molecule. A [NH2(CH3)2]+ cation as charge-balancing ion also exists in 1-D, which is in situ generated by the decomposition of DMA. In compound 1-D, each (R)-CIA ligand connects four Cd(II) ions and acts as a κ6-linker (Fig. 1). For the above coordination modes of (R)-CIA3− ligand, a dinuclear Cd (Cd2, Cd3) unit Cd2(CO2)4 is formed. In the Cd2(CO2)4 unit, Cd2 has a distorted pentagonal bipyramid geometry, which is coordinated four O atoms from two carboxylate groups, a N atom from 1,4-DIB ligand and a water molecule. Cd3 shows an octahedral coordination geometry, which is coordinated by five carboxylate O atoms from four (R)-CIA ligands and a water molecule. Besides the above Cd3, the Cd1 also has an octahedral coordination geometry, which is coordinated by six O atoms from four carboxylate groups.

The first outstanding structural feature of 1-D is the presence of irregular cage-type substructures, which is constructed by Cd (Cd2, Cd3) unit, Cd1 ions, (R)-CIA3− ligands and (R)-CIA fragments (Fig. 2(a)). It’s important to note that the (R)-CIA fragments are only rigid isophthalate units of (R)-CIA ligands and do not involve lactic acid units. Furthermore, considering that the structure of 1-D contains two distinct ligands (1, 4-DIB and (R)-CIA), to simplify the complicated 3D frameworks, we divide the whole structure into two parts. Interestingly, if only the connectivity between Cd(II) ions and CIA ligands are considered, a 3D Cd-CIA framework is generated (Fig. 2(b)), which is packed by irregular cages. As pillars, the 1, 4-DIB ligands provide additional bridges between Cd2 ions to stabilize the framework of 1-D (Fig. 2(c)). From the viewpoint of structural topology, each κ6-CIA ligand acts as a 3-connected node, Cd1 is a 4-connected node and dinuclear Cd (Cd2, Cd3) unit Cd2(CO2)4 is a 6-connected node. Thus, the final framework of 1-D can be regarded as a (3, 4, 6)-connected net with point symbol of (4.8.9)(420.580.640.7)2(820.920.102) (Fig. 2(d)).

At room temperature, solid-state circular dichroism spectra (CD) with KCl plates between 200 and 600 nm are performed to further demonstrate the enantiomeric characteristic of 1-D and 1-L (Fig. 3). The CD curve of 1-D exhibits an obvious positive cotton effect (CE) peak at about 313 nm, revealing its homochiral nature. Moreover, a mirror image is also observed for 1-L, confirming that compounds 1-D and 1-L are enantiomers.

To characterize thermal behaviours of the complexes 1-D and 1-L, their thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) were carried out from room temperature to 800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere, respectively (Fig. S4). The TG curves for 1-D and 1-L show the first weight loss of 10.0% from room temperature to 155 °C, corresponding to the loss of solvent molecules. After a gradual weight loss from 155 °C may be attributed to the release of coordinated water molecules, the whole framework began to decompose at 330 °C. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was also carried out to check the purity of complexes 1-D and 1-L. All the peaks displayed in the measured patterns are similar to those in the simulated patterns generated from single-crystal diffraction data, indicating that single phases of 1-D and 1-L are formed.

Considering the excellent luminescent properties of d10 metal inorganic–organic hybrid coordination polymers, the solid-state luminescence of compounds 1-D is investigated in the solid state at room temperature (Figs. 4 and S5). Excitation of the microcrystalline samples of 1-D at 330 nm produces the intense luminescence with the peak maxima at 353 nm. The luminescence property of the free (R)-H3CIA ligand was also measured to better understand the nature of above emission bands, showing a maximum emission peak at 404 nm under excitation at 336 nm. For Cd(II) ions with d10 electron configuration are difficultly oxidized or reduced, compared to the emission peak of (R)-H3CIA ligand, the emissions of 1-D should be assigned to intraligand (n-π* or π-π*) emission.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a pair of novel 3D HMOFs have been synthesized by using a pair of predesigned lactic acid derivative ligands ((R)-H3CIA and (S)-H3CIA) to assemble with 1, 4-DIB ligand and Cd(II) ion. These complexes consist of cage substructures, which are constructed by CIA ligands, CIA fragments and Cd(II) ions. The enantiomorphism and phase purities of two complexes are proved by CD and PXRD studies, respectively. The work not only affords two new homochiral complexes with interesting structures but also further prove that self-assembly of metal ions with enantiopure ligands is the most effective synthesis method to construct HMOFs.
