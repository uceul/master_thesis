Template-directed synthesis of three new open-framework metal(II) oxalates using Co(III) complex as template†

Qinhe Pan , Qiang Chen , Wei-Chao Song , Tong-Liang Hu and Xian-He Bu *
Department of Chemistry, and Tianjin Key Laboratory of Metal & Molecule-based Material Chemistry, Nankai University, Tianjin, 300071, China. E-mail: buxh@nankai.edu.cn; Fax: +86-22-23502458

Received 10th February 2010 , Accepted 24th May 2010

First published on 2nd August 2010

In our efforts to construct new metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with open frameworks, three new metal oxalates, a 2D layered zinc oxalate [Co(en)3][Zn4(ox)7]0.5·5H2O (1), a 2D saw-tooth layered manganese oxalate [Co(dien)2][Mn4(ox)7]0.5·6H2O (2), and 3D open-framework cadmium oxalate [Co(dien)2][Cd4(H2O)2(ox)7]0.5·4.5H2O (3), have been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions by using Co(en)3Cl3 or Co(dien)2Cl3 as template. Compared to the previously reported oxalate compounds that possess only medium sized 12-ring channels, the three open-framework oxalates exhibit larger channels, 16- and 20-ring channels. Compound 1 is a layered structure with large elliptical 20-ring channels linked by ten ZnO6 and ten oxalates. Compound 2 exhibits a 2D saw-tooth sheet structure with 20-membered corrugated rings, and also presents an elliptical 16-ring aperture along [101] direction. Interestingly, compound 3 presents a 3D open framework, and Cd polyhedra and oxalate units alternate to form a 2D channel system that contains 12- and 20-ring channels, and it exhibits an unprecedented 3,4-connected mbc topology. Pairs of Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality are encapsulated inside the large channels as template. Our results suggest that using metal complexes as templates should be an effective strategy to construct MOFs.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with open frameworks have attracted increasing attention in recent years because of their potential applications in drug delivery, shape-selective sorption/separation, and catalysis.1–6 Several synthetic strategies have been developed in the past few years for synthesizing such structures. One of the feasible approaches is employing large size organic species as the ligands.7–9 The second powerful strategy is utilizing metal–organic polyhedra (MOP) as the secondary building unit.10,11 More recently, a template-directed method that was extensively used in the synthesis of inorganic porous compounds has been used to assembly such MOFs, and some remarkable framework architectures have been reported by using large and complicated complex groups as templates or structure-directing agents (SDAs).12–19 Typical groups are polyoxometalates, water clusters, and some organic species. Notable examples are [M2(bpy)3(H2O)2(ox)]6[P2W18O62]·2(H2-bpy)·nH2O (M = Co(II), n = 3; M = Ni(II), n = 2) templated by [P2W18O62],12 [Co4(dpdo)12][H(H2O)27(CH3CN)12][PW12O40]3 templated by [P2W18O62]3− and H+(H2O)27 clusters,14 and usf-ZMOF templated by organic amine 1,2-DACH.17 However, it still remains a great challenge for constructing MOFs with open frameworks by the template-directed method for such results are a tiny part among all of the explored MOFs.

One more suitable type of templates are transition metal complexes owing to their controllable sizes by different organic ligands. Especially, chiral metal complexes (CMCs) have been well used in the synthesis of open-framework phosphates and germanates,20–22 because they are versatile and can be made with a wide of shapes, charges and particularly chirality. Also the new concept of chirality transfer of the metal complex into the inorganic host framework has been demonstrated.20,21 However, only few attempts have been made in the synthesis of MOFs by using metal complexes as templates.23–25 To explore the effect of CMCs in MOFs, we introduced Co(en)3Cl3 into metal oxalate systems, and obtained the first zeolite-type indium oxalates NKB-1 with multidimensional elliptical 16-ring intersecting channels.26 Therefore, employing Co(III) complexes as templates should be an effective synthetic strategy for the exploration of MOFs with open frameworks.

As a simplest connectors of multidentate organic ligands, oxalate ligand has been focused upon for a long time because of its ability to bind metals strongly with diverse connection modes. Up to now, various interesting oxalate structures from 0D to 3D have been reported.24–35 Remarkably, Rao et al. introduced structure-directing amines (SDAs) into the synthesis of oxalates, and a series of transition metal oxalates have been synthesized. Layered and other types of architectures have been prepared and investigated.29–33 However, most of oxalates are layered structures with honeycomb-like 12-member rings linked by six metal centers and six oxalate units.30–35 Therefore, it is important to design and synthesize new topological structures of oxalates for the development of metal carboxylate compounds.

Accordingly, to further construct MOFs with open frameworks templated by CMCs, our continued research interest has been focused on the synthesis of microporous open-framework metal oxalates by using Co(III) complexes (such as Co(en)33+ and Co(dien)23+) as templates. In this work, we prepared a 2D layered zinc oxalate [Co(en)3][Zn4(ox)7]0.5·5H2O (1), a 2D undulated layered manganese oxalate [Co(dien)2][Mn4(ox)7]0.5·6H2O (2), and a 3D open-framework cadmium oxalate [Co(dien)2][Cd4(H2O)2(ox)7]0.5·4.5H2O (3), templated by Co(en)3Cl3 or Co(dien)2Cl3, respectively. Different to the reported oxalate compounds that possess only 12-ring channels,30–35 all the three oxalate structures have larger channels such as 16- and 20-ring channels. In addition, the pairs of Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality are found to exist in the large pores.

Experimental section

Syntheses of compound 1

A mixture of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (1 mmol), Co(en)3Cl3 (0.43 mmol), K2C2O4·H2O (2 mmol) and H2O (10 mL), was added to a 20 mL Teflon-lined reactor under autogenous pressure at 120 °C for 3 days. The autoclave was then cooled to room temperature. Orange plate-shaped crystals of compound 1 were obtained along with some orange slice-shaped crystals. Yield: ∼10% based on Co(en)3Cl3. Anal. Calc. for C13H34CoN6O19Zn2: C, 20.33; H, 4.46; N, 10.94. Found: C, 20.91; H, 4.35; N, 11.15%.

Syntheses of compound 2

A mixture of Mn(OAc)2·4H2O (1 mmol), Co(dien)2Cl3 (0.40 mmol), K2C2O4·H2O (2 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was added to a 20 mL Teflon-lined reactor under autogenous pressure at 120 °C for 3 days. The autoclave was then cooled to room temperature. Salmon pink rod-shaped crystals of compound 2 were obtained along with some brown polyhedral crystals. Yield: ∼20% based on Co(dien)2Cl3. Anal. Calc. for C15H38CoMn2N6O20: C, 22.77; H, 4.84; N, 10.62. Found: C, 22.39; H, 4.57; N, 10.39%.

Syntheses of compound 3

A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (1 mmol), Co(dien)2Cl3 (0.40 mmol), K2C2O4·H2O (2 mmol) and H2O (10 mL) was added to a 20 mL Teflon-lined reactor under autogenous pressure at 120 °C for 3 days. The autoclave was then cooled to room temperature. Yellow rod-shaped crystals of compound 3 were obtained along with colorless block crystals and brown rod-shaped crystals.

Structure determination

Suitable single crystals of compounds 1, 2 and 3 were selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses. X-Ray single-crystal diffraction data of 1–3 were collected on a Rigaku SCX-mini diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The program SAINT36 was used for integration of the diffraction profiles. All the structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods with SHELXL.37 Metal atoms in each complex were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier maps. Hydrogen atoms associated with the organic species of Co(III) complexes were placed geometrically, and refined in a riding model. H atoms on the water molecules were not added. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. Experimental details for crystal structure determinations of compounds 1–3 are listed in Table 1.

Characterizations

Elemental analyses (C, H and N) of compounds 1 and 2 were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. X-Ray powder diffraction (XRPD) data for compounds 1 and 2 were collected on a Rigaku X-ray diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). The experimental XRPD patterns of compounds 1 and 2 agree well with the simulated patterns generated on the basis of single-crystal structural analysis (Fig. S1, ESI†). Thermogravimetric analysis (TG) for compounds 1 and 2 was performed on a Perkin-Elmer TGA7 unit in the air with a heating rate of 10 K min−1 (Fig. S2, ESI†). The crystals of compound 3 are too small and adhered with impurities, and we did not have enough sample for the measurement.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of 1

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis indicates that 1 is triclinic and crystallizes in the P space group. The structure of 1, [Co(en)3][Zn4(ox)7]0.5·5H2O, consists of macroanionic [Zn4(ox)7]6− sheets involving ZnO6 octahedra and oxalate units. Charge neutrality is achieved by the Co(en)33+ cations located in the interlayer region, and free water molecules fill the voids. The structure contains two types of oxalate units with different coordination modes, one of which binds to two Zn atoms as a bis-chelating bridging oxalate ligand, and the other binds to one Zn atom and has two terminal O atoms as a hanging oxalate ligand. The two Zn atoms in the asymmetric unit are in different coordination environments, but each is surrounded by six oxygen atoms from three different oxalate groups to adopt a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. Zn(1) is linked by three bridging oxalate ligands, whereas Zn(2) is coordinated by two bridging oxalate ligands and one hanging oxalate ligand. The Zn–O distances range from 2.076(4) to 2.215(4) Å, while the O–Zn–O angles range from 77.58(16) to 173.53(18)°.

For the bis-chelating bridging oxalate ligand, two Zn(2) centers are linked to form a metal cluster Zn2(C2O4)56−, and the Zn(1) centers are connected to each other to form a zigzag chain [Zn(C2O4)2]n2n−, two such kinds of zinc oxalates have been reported by Rao.32 Interestingly, the zigzag chain is transferred from the metal clusters, and can be further condensed to form a honeycomb 12-ring layered structure. However, in this compound, the metal clusters and the zigzag chains further linked along bc plane to form a 2D [Zn4(ox)7]6− macroanion sheet with a large elliptical 20-ring (see Fig. 1a), which, to the best of our knowledge, presents the largest pores in layered zinc oxalates until now. The elliptical pore is formed by ten Zn centers and ten bridging oxalate groups lying in the same plane with the hanging oxalate ligands out of the plane. Such a [Zn4(ox)7]6− macroanion sheet is the first example in layered zinc oxalates, although it has been found in the 3D structure of 2[C3H7NH3]+[Zn2(C2O4)3]2−·3H2O as a layered building block.30


Fig. 1 (a) The 20-ring of 1 linked by ten Zn atoms and ten oxalate units; (b) structure of 1 viewed along the b axis.

These elliptical pores are occupied by pairs of chiral Co(en)33+ cations which reside in the interlayer region along with the free water molecules (see Fig. 1b). There are extensive hydrogen-bonding interactions between the amino groups of the complex cations and the lattice oxygen atoms, with the N⋯O distances in the range of 2.902(7)–3.285(11) Å. In such a way, the 2D macroanions are connected to form a 3D superamolecular framework.

Crystal structure of 2

Compound 2, [Co(dien)2][Mn4(ox)7]0.5·6H2O, crystallizes in space group P21/c with a = 13.133(3) Å, b = 22.410(5) Å, c = 10.267(2) Å, β = 98.96(3)°, and Z = 2. It is also a 2D layered structure consisting of macroanionic [Mn4(ox)7]6− sheets involving MnO6 octahedra and oxalate units. The counterions of Co(dien)23+ cations and the free waters are located in the interlayer region to balance the charge and fill the space. Similar to compound 1, the structure contains two kinds of oxalate units, bis-chelating bridging oxalate ligand and hanging oxalate ligand. Two Mn atoms were six coordinated and surrounded by either such three bis-chelating bridging oxalate groups or two bis-chelating bridging oxalate units and a hanging oxalate unit to adopt distorted octahedral coordination geometries, with the Mn–O distances ranging from 2.120(5) to 2.194(5) Å, while the O–Mn–O angles range from 75.34(19) to 171.55(19)°.

Similar to compound 1, the metal cluster Mn2(C2O4)56−, and the zigzag chain [Mn(C2O4)2]n2n− also have been found to exist in compound 2, and linked to form a layered network with the same linkage mode as that in compound 1. However, different to the planar sheet in compound 1, it can be clearly seen in Fig. 2a that the sheet in compound 2 exhibits a saw-tooth pattern, which could be affected by the different templates of Co(en)33+ and Co(dien)23+. To our knowledge, such kind of saw-tooth sheet is rarely reported in oxalate complexes.38,39 Like the sheet in compound 1, the sheet in compound 2 also contains 20-membered rings built up with ten Mn atoms and ten oxalate units. Differently, such windows in compound 2 present corrugated rings (shown in Fig. 2b). For the superposition of part of the corrugated rings, an elliptical 16-membered ring window containing eight Mn atoms and eight oxalate units (Fig. 2c), has been found along the [101] direction. The Co(dien)23+ cations reside in the interlayer region and are held together through hydrogen-bonding interactions with the oxygen atoms of the saw-tooth layer and water molecules, with N⋯O distances in the range 2.833(8)–3.233(8) Å.


Fig. 2 (a) Structure of 2 viewed along the c axis; (b) the corrugated 20-ring of 2 connected by ten Mn atoms and ten oxalate units; (c) the elliptical channels of the 16-rings for 2 along the [101] direction.

Crystal structure of 3

To obtain 3D frameworks of oxalate complexes and further investigate the effect of CMCs, the larger Cd2+ ion which can coordinate more oxalate units was introduced, and compound 3, [Co(dien)2][Cd4(H2O)2(ox)7]0.5·4.5H2O, was obtained under similar synthetic conditions as that for compound 2. It crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, and exhibits a 3D open-framework structure. It has the anionic framework of [Cd4(H2O)2(ox)7] linked by CdOx polyhedra (CdO6 and CdO7) and oxalate units, whose negative charges are compensated by Co(dien)23+ cations, with free water molecules filling available space. There are two kinds of bridging oxalate units in the structure: one is the well-known bis-chelating bridging oxalate, and the other is a simple two-coordination bridging oxalate units. Both can be regarded as 2-connected linkers. In the host framework, there are two crystallographically distinct Cd atoms. The geometry of the Cd(1) center is octahedral, being bound to six oxygen atoms from two bis-chelating bridging oxalate ligands and two simple bridging oxalate units, which can be regarded as a distorted 4-connected node (Fig. 3a). Cd(2) atoms are seven-coordinated by three different bis-chelating bridging oxalate groups and one coordinated water, which can be regarded as a 3-connected node (Fig. 3b). The Cd–O distances range from 2.258(7) to 2.387(7) Å, while the O–Cd–O angles range from 69.4(3) to 167.4(3)°.
Fig. 3 (a) Four-connected building unit and (b) three-connected building unit of Cd centers in 3.

Interestingly, bridged by oxalate units, Cd(1) centers are linked to each other along the bc plane to form a sheet structure [Cd(C2O4)2]n2n− containing 12-rings (Fig. 4a), and Cd(2) centers are connected to form a zigzag chain [Cd(H2O)(C2O4)2]n2n− (Fig. 4b), similar to that found in compound 1. Different to the layered structures of compounds 1 and 2, the sheets and the chains in compound 3 further form a 3D [Cd4(H2O)2(ox)7] macroanion (Fig. 5a). After simplification, the 3D framework presents a binodal 3,4-connected network (Fig. 5b) with the short point symbol of (63)(65.8) (td10 = 938), which is a subnet of an unprecedented mbc topology with the space group transferred from Cmcm to P21/c.40,41


Fig. 4 (a) The sheet containing 12-rings linked by Cd(1) centers; (b) the zigzag chain connected by Cd(2) centers in 3.

Fig. 5 (a) Structure of 3 viewed along the c axis; (b) simplified representation of the network of 3 (only Cd(II) centers are connected).

Further investigation on this 3D structure suggests that compound 3 possesses a 2D intersecting channel system, which contains a large elliptical 20-ring channel along the b axis and a 12-ring channel along the c axis, respectively. Especially, as shown in Fig. 5a, the large elliptical 20-ring channel presents a similar pattern as that in compound 1, which is alternatively linked by ten Cd polyhedra and ten oxalate units (two simple bridging and eight bis-chelating bridging oxalate units). Such kind of channels is the largest channel found in cadmium oxalates.27,28 The structure has the appearance of a 12-ring channel system made from six Cd polyhedra and six oxalate units. For the 2D intersecting channels with large elliptical 20-ring channels and 12-ring channels, the host open framework gives rise to a large free volume accessible to guests, approximately 60.9% (calculated by PLATON analysis), which is one of the largest values among the known open-framework metal oxalates.

The negative charges of the whole host framework were compensated by the counterions, the chiral metal complex Co(dien)23+ cations. Pairs of chiral metal complexes filled the large elliptical 20-ring channels of compound 3 along with the free water molecules, similar to compound 1. The Co(dien)23+ cations are encapsulated inside the intersecting apertures of the large elliptical 20-ring channels and 12-ring channels (Fig. 6) and interact with the oxygen atoms of host framework and water molecules. The N⋯O distances are in the range of 2.861(11)–3.352(11) Å.


Fig. 6 The location of Co(dien)23+ cations inside the intersecting apertures of the large elliptical 20-ring channels and 12-ring channels (the host framework is only connected by Cd(II) centers) of 3.

Notably, compared to most reported oxalate compounds which contain only 12-ring channels,30–35 the oxalates templated by chiral Co(III) complexes exhibit larger channels of elliptical 20-ring channels in compounds 1 and 3, and elliptical 16-ring channels in compound 2. Interestingly, Fig. 7 shows the template Co(III) complexes filling the large elliptical channels of the three compounds, and all the elliptical channels are encapsulated by a pair of chiral Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality. Those results indicate that such pairs of chiral metal complexes play an important template role, and have a great effect on the formation of MOFs with open frameworks. Therefore, the appearance of pairs of chiral Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality can be regarded as a large template. Such results reveal that it is possible to prepare more novel MOFs with open frameworks by using such pairs of metal complexes as templates.


Fig. 7 Pairs of Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality inside of the large elliptical channels for: (a) 1, (b) 2 and (c) 3.

In summary, three new open-framework metal oxalates have been hydrothermally synthesized by employing Co(en)3Cl3 or Co(dien)2Cl3 as template. Filled by pairs of chiral Co(III) complexes with opposite chirality, these oxalate structures present large elliptical channels of 20-ring channels for compounds 1 and 3, and 16-ring channels for compound 2. It is suggested that the appearance of chiral pairs of metal complexes play an important template role, and thus it should be an effective strategy to obtain MOFs with open frameworks by using such templates.
