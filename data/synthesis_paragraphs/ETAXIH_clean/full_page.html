The design and synthesis of novel metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have provoked significant interest owing to their intriguing variety of architectures as well as their potential applications in gas storage, carbon dioxide capture, multiphase catalysis, magnetism, and ion exchange.1â€“3 Although it is well-known that a lot of factors, such as coordination environment of the metal centers, the choice of ligands, metalâ€“ligand ratio, reaction conditions, etc., can affect the self-assembly process, the judicious selection for multifunctional organic ligands has proved to be a more effective approach to construct MOFs with unique structures and properties.

The organic ligands containing N and O donors, especially the N-heterocyclic carboxylates,4,5 are excellent candidates for preparing novel MOFs, because of their versatile coordination modes and potential hydrogen bonding donors and acceptors. In this context, imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3IMDC), a planar rigid ligand containing two nitrogen and four oxygen atoms, has attracted much interest. It can be successively deprotonated to generate various species with different proton numbers, and hence may result in a large diversity of MOF architectures. Moreover, the two nitrogen atoms in the imidazole ring can direct the coordination orientation, sometimes strengthened by the cooperative coordination of the adjacent carboxyl groups, to produce large stable molecular motifs which can be reticulated into novel topologies. To date, a large number of MOFs5,6 based on imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligand possess interesting topological structures and useful properties have been reported. For example, Eddaoudi et al. have reported In48(IMDC)96â€“(C7N3H15)24(DMF)36Â·(H2O)192 and In(IMDC)2(C3N2H5)(DMF)4â€“(CH3CN)(H2O)4 with zeolite like RHO and SOD topologies,7 respectively. [Ln2(IMDC)2(H2O)3]Â·1.625H2O (Ln = Eu, Dy)8 and [LnCd(IMDC)(SO4)(H2O)3]Â·0.5H2O (Ln = Tb, Eu, etc.)9 with helical structures have been isolated by Yang and co-workers.

These fascinating MOFs based on H3IMDC prompt us to seek more similar ligands to synthesize more desired complexes. Thus, we chose herein one analogous ligand, 2-propyl-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3PIMDC) with a propyl-substituent in the 2-position of the imidazole group, because it may introduce additional structural constrain in controlling the assembly of metalâ€“organic frameworks.10 By doing so, we hope to reveal some structural factors of the H3PIMDC for dominating the self-assembly, and this will provide more useful information on the propyl substituent effect in such a ligand. As a result, the self-assembly of Zn2+ and H3PIMDC ligands under hydrothermal conditions generated a 3D MOF constructed from tetrahedral Zn4 building blocks, namely,

The detailed structure of 1 can be described in a stepwise manner. As depicted in Fig. 3(a), each O5 serves as a Î¼3-bridge to link three Zn1 centers to form a triangular [Zn3O] motif, which is further connected by the octahedral Zn3 center via three carboxylic O1 atoms. As a result, three Zn1 and one Zn3 are linked by three Î¼2-O1 atoms and one Î¼3-O5 to generate a tetrahedral Zn4 building block (Fig. 3(b)), which is surrounded by six PIMDC ligands. Each Zn4 motif links the other three tetranuclear subunits through the six organic ligands to generate a 2D metalâ€“organic layer in the a,b-plane as shown in Fig. 3(c) and S6â€ . Hexagonal cavities entrapped with propyl groups are observed in the 2D net. Furthermore, adjacent 2D layers are joined together by Zn2 centers via the Zn2â€“O2 and Zn2â€“O4 bonds to give the 3D framework of 1, and the hexagonal cavities are extended to form a 1D channel along the c-axis direction. A better insight into this 3D network can be achieved by application of a topological approach. As mentioned above, the Zn4 motifs generated by Zn1 and Zn3 can be regarded as a 6-connected node, and the PIMDC ligand will be transformed to a 3-connected node if Zn2 atom is viewed as a linker. Thus, the architecture of 1 can be reduced to a bimodal structure with six-connected and three-connected nodes, adopting a (4Â·62)3(43Â·66Â·86) topology (the first symbol for PIMDC ligand, the second for Zn4 motif), as shown in Fig. 3(d) and S7â€ . Up to now, although more than fifteen examples have been reported with 3D (3,6)-connected topology,14 such a network constructed by stacking 63 sheets in parallel is unprecedented. It is undoubted that the unprecedented topology of 1 is useful at the basic level in the crystal engineering of coordination frameworks.

The solid-state PL spectrum study at room temperature shows that compound 1 emits a strong blue light centered at 402 nm upon photo-excitation at 270 nm (Fig. S8â€ ). Since the free H3PIMDC ligand exhibits a similar emission band at 488 nm (Î»ex = 398 nm),11a the emission nature of 1 is probably due to a ligand-centered emission state including the significant charge-transfer character induced by the polar cation, as reported for other d10 metal complexes with O- or N-donor ligands based on the position and shape of the emission band.15 On the other hand, as mentioned above, three basically co-planar chelating rings around each PIMDC ligand boost the electronic delocalization in the ligand's backbone and charge transfer to the metal center and thus greatly affects the molecule orbits and the energy gap between Ï€* and Ï€ molecule orbits of the ligand and finally results in an obvious blue shift. The TGA of complex 1 under a N2 atmosphere demonstrates that part of the coordinated water molecules were released in the temperature range of 100â€“200 Â°C, then, the 3D framework is stable up to ca. 320 Â°C (Fig. S9â€ ).

Gas-adsorption measurements (N2 and CO2) were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2010 surface-area and pore-size analyzer, which confirmed the permanent microporosity of 1. The crystal sample was degassed at 150 Â°C for 24 h under vacuum prior to the measurement. Repeated experiments show that no obvious N2 adsorption occurred. While a significant increase in the amount of CO2 gas adsorbed by 1 was observed with an increase in the CO2 pressure (Fig. 4), indicating the diffusing of CO2 molecules into the channels. The uptake capacity is 19.1 cm3 gâˆ’1 at 1 atm and 273.15 K, which is comparable to some reported porous hybrid solid or MOF materials such as BIF-316 or SCIF-3.17 By fitting the adsorption data to the Dubininâ€“Radushkevich (DR) equation, the surface area and pore volume were determined to be 181.9 m2 gâˆ’1 and 39.8 cm3 gâˆ’1, respectively. In our opinion, the difference between N2 and CO2 adsorption is likely caused by the smaller dynamic diameter of CO2 (0.33 nm vs. 0.36 nm for N2) and the existence of the entrapped propyl groups in the channels. Because of the small kinetic diameter of CO2, the large pore window or large cage cavity is not essential for efficient adsorption. In compound 1, the pore space in the 3D framework is further partly occupied by the propyl groups into smaller pore regions, which may also help enhance the CO2 storage. This result is consistent with the previous observations that interpenetration and extra framework species can enhance gas sorption properties, even though the pore space may be reduced.18 This result indicates that a suitable substituent group in the 2-position of H3IMDC ligands can not only inherit the outstanding coordination properties in fabricating novel building blocks that direct the resultant assemblies into novel MOF materials like those in H3IMDC complexes but also subtly tune and/or enhance the properties of MOFs.

In summary, we have prepared a new 3D microporous MOF material by using a propyl-substituted analogous ligand of the well-known imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid. The framework shows an unprecedented (3,6)-connected topology on the base of [Zn4] tetrahedron motifs. Notably, while no obvious N2 adsorption was observed, significant CO2 uptake capacity was found for this 3D MOF material. Thus, the present work encourages us to further design novel porous MOFs via engrafting suitable groups into the backbone of H3IMDC ligand at the 2-position.
