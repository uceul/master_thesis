Construction of metal–organic coordination polymers derived from 4-substituted tetrazole–benzoate ligands: synthesis, structure, luminescence, and magnetic behaviors†

Jia-Yin Sun a, Li Wang a, Dao-Jun Zhang b, Da Li a, Yu Cao a, Li-Ying Zhang a, Shuang-Li Zeng a, Guang-Sheng Pang a, Yong Fan *a, Jia-Ning Xu *a and Tian-You Song a

Using 4-substituted tetrazole–benzoate ligands, 4-aminophenyl-1H-tetrazole (HL1) and 4-carboxyphenyl-1H-tetrazole (H2L2), five metal–organic coordination polymers formulated as [Co3(OH)2(L1)4(H2O)2]2n (1), [Zn(L1)2]n (2), [Ag(L1)]n (3), [Co(L2)(H2O)]n (4) and [Pb(L2)]n (5) have been hydro(solvo)thermally synthesized and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Due to the effects of the different metal centers and the substituted groups of ligands, such as –NH2 and –COOH, compounds 1–5 demonstrate different characteristics. Compound 1 features a 3D framework with a rare (3,6)-connected rtl (rutile) topology. Compound 2 crystallizes in a noncentrosymmetric space group Pn2 and manifests a 3D non-interpenetrated dia framework. Compound 3 shows a 2D layer structure with 3-connected 4·82-fes topology. Compound 4 exhibits a uninodal 8-connected body-centered cubic (bcu) net with a Schläfli symbol of 424·64. Compound 5 possesses a 3D framework with 5-connected 46·64-bnn (hexagonal boron nitride) topology. Luminescent studies show that compounds 1–5 exhibit good solvent-dependent properties, especially in their response towards acetonitrile, carbon tetrachloride and acetone molecules. In addition, compound 1 shows spin-canted antiferromagnetism at low temperature.

Introduction

As an important branch in the field of supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering, the design and assembly of metal–organic coordination polymers (MOCPs) has become a very attractive research field over the past ten years,1 due to the MOCPs' fascinating architectures and topologies as well as their potential application as functional materials in many areas, such as gas separation and storage, ion-exchange, molecular and chiral recognition, catalysis and magnetic properties.2 Driven by the structures of MOCPs being rationally modified and tailored with organic groups and their potential physico-chemical properties, a large number of MOCPs have been reported, with a specific focus on tuning the topology and functionality by ligand design.3 Therefore, the judicious choice and design of organic moieties is very important and there exists a great interest in the search for new bridging ligands. Tetrazole and their derivatives as five-membered N-heterocyclic azoles have received considerable attention, owing to their multi-connectivity abilities and various applications in pharmaceuticals, photography and information recording systems.4–11 As anticipated, metal–tetrazole compounds provide interesting properties such as hydrogen storage, magnetic, luminescent, ferroelectric, and selective ion sensing.12,4b Although these kinds of tetrazolate ligands proved to be very interesting and useful in various applications, particularly in the construction of polynuclear and/or chain building blocks, these systems are less explored compared to the other five-membered aromatic heterocycles such as imidazole, pyrazole, triazole, and their derivatives.

On the other hand, the introduction of substituent groups such as amine, amide, methyl and nitro groups attached to organic linkers may bring about changes of electronic, space, solubility, and coordination modes, which may bring on significant conversion in the types of framework structures and functionalities. For example, by introducing an amino substituent group on the dicarboxylate ligand, Wang and co-workers adjust a 2-fold interpenetration nonporous structure of [Co2(bdc)2(bpy)2] (bdc = 1,4-benzene dicarboxylate, bpy = 4,4′-bipyridine) to a porous doubly pillared-layer structure [Co2(abdc)2(bpy)2]·8DMF (abdc = 2-amino-1,4-benzene dicarboxylate), which represents a shape/size modulation of the layer apertures.13 Yaghi's work shows that introducing different functional groups into MOF-5 type structures can greatly improve the selective adsorption properties.14 As far as we know, the systematic studies on the effect of substituent groups to the structures of MOCPs are still in an initial stage.

As a part of our ongoing investigations of synthesizing MOCPs with tetrazolate ligands,12c and discussing the role of introducing a substituent group on ligands in MOCPs, we selected two 4-substituted tetrazole–benzoate ditopic rigid ligands as organic linkers, namely, 4-aminophenyl-1H-tetrazole (HL1) and 4-carboxypheyl-1H-tetrazole (H2L2), based on the following reasons: (1) –NH2 group is an electron-donating group, which might provide a polar environment in the resulting MOCPs. Moreover, the –NH2 group can participate in either coordination or hydrogen bonding, which will play an important role in the assembly of MOCPs. (2) –COOH group is regarded as electron-withdrawing group, which combined with tetrazole groups with versatile coordination modes can induce higher dimensional structures. Additionally, the dihedral angles of the carboxylate and tetrazole out of the plane of the aromatic ring are flexible and adaptable to meet the requirements for constructing a helical structure. The synthesis of coordination networks by conjugated organic spacers and metal centers can be an efficient method for obtaining new luminescent materials. 4-Aminophenyl-1H-tetrazole (HL1) and 4-carboxypheyl-1H-tetrazole (H2L2) may be good candidates for enhanced emissive materials, tunable, in principle, by the coordination to different metal ions.15

Herein, we report the syntheses, structures, luminescent and magnetic properties of five different MOCPs, [Co3(OH)2(L1)4(H2O)2]2n (1), [Zn(L1)2]n (2), [Ag(L1)]n (3), [Co(L2)(H2O)]n (4) and [Pb(L2)]n (5). These MOCPs demonstrate various 2D and 3D network architectures, in which the substituent groups of organic ligands play important roles in construction of final structures. In addition, the luminescent response to the small solvent molecules of these MOCPs has been investigated as well as the magnetic properties of compounds 1 and 4.

Experimental section

General procedures

The ligands 4-aminophenyl-1H-tetrazole (HL1) and 4-carboxypheyl-1H-tetrazole (H2L2) were prepared according to the literature method.16 All other chemicals used in this work were of reagent grade and used without purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max-2550 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on a Nicolet Impact 410 spectrometer using the KBr pellet method. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer TGA 7 instrument thermogravimetric analyzer in atmospheric environment with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Photoluminescence (PL) spectra were measured with an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920 spectrophotometer. The magnetic measurements of the compounds were carried out by use of Quantum Design SQUID MPMS-VSM magnetometer in the temperature range of 2–300 K and fields up to 2 T.

Synthesis of [Co3(OH)2(L1)4(H2O)2]2n (1)

Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.4 mmol, 116.4 mg), HL1 (0.3 mmol, 48.3 mg), NaOH (0.3 mL, 1 M) and H2O (4 mL) was stirred at room temperature until a homogeneous mixture was obtained. The mixture was transferred into a Teflon-lined autoclave (15 mL) and heated at 100 °C for 2 days and then cooled to room temperature under ambient conditions. Orange block crystals of 1 were recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (63% yield based on HL1). Anal. Calc. for 1: C, 37.98; H, 3.19; N, 31.64. Found: C, 38.24; H, 3.30; N, 31.95%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet cm−1): 3407 (s), 1619 (s), 1578 (m), 1448 (s), 1266 (w), 1059 (s), 847 (m), 771 (m).

Synthesis of [Zn(L1)2]n (2)

Compound 2 was prepared similar to that of compound 1 by using Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.4 mmol, 119.0 mg), HL1 (0.4 mmol, 64.4 mg), NaOH (0.4 mL, 1 M) dissolved in 4 mL H2O. The mixture was heated at 110 °C for 3 days and pale brown block crystals were obtained in 76% yield based on HL1. Anal. Calc. for 2: C, 43.59; H, 3.14; N, 36.31. Found: C, 43.74; H, 3.38; N, 36.53%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet cm−1): 3462 (s), 3361 (s), 2937 (w), 1618 (s), 1452 (s), 1365 (m), 1293 (m), 1130 (s), 839 (m).

Synthesis of [Ag(L1)]n (3)

A mixture of Ag(NO3)2 (0.4 mmol, 67.9 mg), HL1 (0.4 mmol, 64.4 mg) was dissolved in 4 mL of H2O/CH3CN (3:1, v/v). The mixture was transferred into a Teflon-lined autoclave (15 mL) and heated at 85 °C for 3 days and then cooled to room temperature under ambient conditions. Pale brown block crystals of 3 were recovered in 45% yield based on HL1. Anal. Calc. for 3: C, 31.37; H, 2.26; N, 26.13. Found: C, 31.61; H, 2.54; N, 26.67%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet cm−1): 3431 (s), 3286 (s), 3152 (s), 1611 (s), 1446 (s), 1258 (w), 1126 (s), 891 (m).

Synthesis of [Co(L2)(H2O)]n (4)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.8 mmol, 231.8 mg), NaN3 (0.7 mmol, 45.5 mg), 4-cyanobenzoic acid (0.6 mmol, 88.26 mg) and H2O (8 mL) was stirred at room temperature until a homogeneous mixture was obtained. The mixture was transferred into a Teflon-lined autoclave (15 mL) and heated at 140 °C for 3 days and then cooled to room temperature under ambient conditions. Purple block crystals of 4 were recovered in 78% yield based on 4-cyanobenzoic acid. Anal. Calc. for 4: C, 36.24; H, 2.28; N, 21.13. Found: C, 36.57; H, 2.47; N, 21.59%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet cm−1): 3343 (m), 2949 (w), 1648 (w), 1572 (m), 1424 (m), 1127 (s), 856 (w).

Synthesis of [Pb(L2)]n (5)

A mixture of Pb(Ac)2·6H2O (0.3 mmol, 113.8 mg), H2L2 (0.3 mmol, 57.0 mg), NaOH (0.2 mL, 1 M), and H2O (3 mL) was stirred at room temperature until a homogeneous mixture was obtained. The mixture was transferred into a Teflon-lined autoclave (15 mL) and heated at 180 °C for 3 days and then cooled to room temperature under ambient conditions. Pale brown block crystals of 5 were recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (87% yield based on HL2). Anal. Calc. for 5: C, 24.30; H, 1.02; N, 14.17. Found: C, 24.58; H, 1.13; N, 14.46%. Selected IR data (KBr pellet cm−1): 3431 (s), 2941 (2), 1564 (m), 1420 (s), 1122 (s), 856 (m).

The intensity data collection were performed on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation in the ω scanning mode at room temperature. No significant decay was observed during the data collection. Data processing was accomplished with the RAPID.

AUTO processing program. The structures were solved by direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXTL crystallographic software package.17,18 The structures were solved by direct method. All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. All the hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were placed geometrically and located based on theoretically riding model. The solvent included fluorescence properties of compounds 1–5 were investigated in various solvent emulsions at room temperature. Results and discussion

Structural description

Crystal structure of [Co3(OH)2(L1)4(H2O)2]2n (1). Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis revealed that compound 1 crystallizes in the space group P21/c. There are two crystallographically unique Co atoms coordinated with two types of 4-aminophenyl-1H-tetrazole (HL1) ligands, three μ3-OH− groups, and one terminal water molecule (Fig. 1a). Co1 is coordinated to two N atoms of tetrazole groups from two different L1 ligands, one N atom from the amino group of one L1 ligand, one O atom from water, and two O atoms from two individual μ3-OH− groups. In comparison with the distorted [CoN3O3] octahedral geometry of the Co1 atom, Co2 is on a crystallographic inversion center and is octahedral with four N atoms of the tetrazole groups from four ligands occupying the corners of a square and two O atoms from two μ3-OH− bridges at the apical positions. The Co–O and Co–N bond lengths range from 2.126(7) to 2.180(0) Å and 2.124(2) to 2.254(2) Å, respectively, which are similar to those reported in the literature.20 There are two kinds of coordination modes for L1 ligands in the asymmetric unit. One (Scheme 1, mode I) serves as a μ2-didentate bridging ligand using two tetrazole nitrogen atoms (N1, N2) to bridge two adjacent Co atoms and the other (Scheme 1, mode II) is a μ3-tridentate bridging ligand and uses two tetrazole nitrogen atoms (N6, N7) to bridge another two adjacent Co atoms and the nitrogen atom (N10) of the amino group to bond atom Co1.
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination environment of Co(II), bonding mode of L1 in the coordination polymer 1. All H atoms are omitted for clarity; (b) view of the 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] (tz = tetrazole) chain decorated with the tetrazole groups; (c) view of the 3D network constructed from the 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] chain in the bc plane.

Scheme 1 Coordination modes of ligands in compounds 1–5.

In this compound, the hydroxyl acts as a μ3-bridge linking two equivalent Co1 atoms and one Co2 atom, generating a 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] (tz = tetrazole) chain decorated with the tetrazole groups (Fig. 1b). The Co1–O1–Co2 and Co1–O1–Co1(A) angles are about 117.6, 118.6 and 93.4°, respectively. So the OH− group is displaced out of the Co1–Co2–Co1(A) plane, which results in the 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] chain featuring a zig-zag motif. The 1D chain, which behaves as an SBU, is further connected with four neighboring SBUs through the μ3-bridged L1 to form a 3D network (Fig. 1c). It is noted that a similar 1D metal–oxygen chain and the same coordination mode of L1 ligand have been reported by Zou et al. in the compound [Cd3(OH)2L4(H2O)2],16b which displays a 2D sheet motif. The arrangement of the 1D metal–oxygen chain in [Cd3(OH)2L4(H2O)2] and compound 1 is different. In [Cd3(OH)2L4(H2O)2], the 1D {[Cd3(OH)2]4+}n chains arranged in –AAAA– sequence and L1 ligands as linkers bridge the adjacent 1D {[Cd3(OH)2]4+}n chains in the same direction, whereas in compound 1, the arrangement of 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] chain is –ABAB– and the neighbor 1D [Co3(OH)2(tz)] chains are gathered by L1 ligand with cross connection (see Fig. S1, ESI†).

The study of framework connectivity and topological analysis has been carried out for 1. In this framework, two μ3-bridged hydroxyl groups linked two equivalent Co1 and Co2 metal centers serving as a node, and connects six L1 ligands in six directions which can be viewed as a 6-connected node as depicted in Fig. S2, ESI,† while each μ3-bridged L1 ligands bridging three SBUs can be treated as 3-connected node. Such connectivity repeats infinitely to give a (3,6)-connected net. According to the simplification principle and the analysis of TOPOS,21 the resulting structure of compound 1 is a 2-nodal rtl (rutile) net,22 and its point (Schläfli) symbol is (4·62)2(42·610·83).

Crystal structure of [Zn(L1)2]n (2). The X-ray diffraction experiment reveals that compound 2 is crystallized in the noncentrosymmetric space group Pn2. In 2, each asymmetric unit contains two unique Zn atoms and one L1 ligand. The coordination geometry around Zn1 and Zn2 adopts a regular tetrahedron (Fig. 2a). Zn1 is bonded to four N1 atoms from four L1 ligands with the Zn1–N1 bond distance of 2.032 Å, while Zn2 is bridged by four N4 atoms from another four L1 ligands with the Zn2–N4 bond distance of 2.023 Å. Each L1 ligand acts as a bidentate spacer, using the two N atoms (N1 and N4) of the tetrazole ring to bridge Zn1 and Zn2 (Scheme 1, mode III). That is, one metal ion links to four ligands and one ligand coordinates to two metal centers, the Zn(II) ions lead to an overall tetrahedral geometry (Fig. 2b) and then a noninterpenetrated 3D framework is formed (Fig. 2c). It is worth noting that the amino group of the ligand presented in 2 is uncoordinated to the Zn atom. From a topological perspective view, each Zn center may be considered as a 4-connecting node, which links to equivalent 4-connecting centers through bridging tetrazole ligands. Since each of these ligands is only two-connecting, they are only considered as connections and not nodes. Consequently, this results in the formation of a noninterpenetrated 3D network with a diamondoid structure, as shown in Fig. 2d. The Zn⋯Zn separations are 6.227 Å within the diamond-like network and the Zn–Zn–Zn angles are revealed to be 109.32 and 109.55° and much the same the expected 109.47° value for an idealized diamond network (see Fig. S3, ESI†). As evidenced in Fig. 2c, a large cavity exists within a single diamond-like network. However, compound 2 avoids a potentially large open space by the amino group directed towards the pore.

Crystal structure of [Ag(L1)]n (3). An X-ray single-crystal diffraction study reveals that 3 crystallizes in a monoclinic space group P21/c. In the asymmetric unit of 3, there is one crystallographically distinct Ag+ center and one L1 ligand (Fig. 3a). Ag1 is bonded to three N atoms (two tetrazole N atoms and one amino N atom) from the different L1 ligands resulting in a three-coordinated T-shaped geometry (Ag1–N1A = 2.148(4), Ag1–N4B = 2.160(4), Ag1–N5 = 2.493(4) Å, N–Ag1–N 102.44–145.37°). The Ag–N bond lengths fall within the normal ranges and are comparable to related compounds.23 In addition, the phenyl and tetrazole rings of L1 are not located in the same plane and the corresponding interplanar angle is 32.3°. Each L1 anion acts as a tridentate ligand (Scheme 1, mode IV) and uses the two N atoms (1 and 4-position) of the tetrazole group to link a pair of symmetry-related Ag1 ions along the c axis to form an infinite 1D zig-zag chain, and the N atom (N5) of the amino group links the third Ag1 in the adjacent chain to form a 2D sheet structure, which incorporates two different subrings, i.e. 24-membered ring A and 16-membered ring B (Fig. 3b). To our knowledge, the coordination mode of L1 ligand in compound 3 is firstly found in MOCPs. The 2D MOCP lying in the ab and ac plane stacks in parallel without interpenetration to generate the 3D supermolecular structure. As shown in Fig. S4, ESI,† there are interlayer π⋯π stacking interactions among the layers, which are among the parallel tetrazole groups of L1 ligands with centroid-to-centroid distances of ca. 3.363 Å. To get a better insight into the present 2D structure, topological analysis was carried out for 3. Each Ag1 and L1 ligand can be considered as a 3-connecting node. Consequently, the 2D network can be regarded as a 3-connected fes (4·82) structure, as shown in Fig. 3c.

All H atoms are omitted for clarity; (b) views showing the 2D sheet structure features a infinite 1D zig-zag chain and two type of rings A and B; (c) topological view showing the 3-connected 2D (4·82)-fes network for compound 3.
Crystal structure of [Co(L2)(H2O)]n (4). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction of 4 reveals that it crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit of 4 contains one Co2+ cation, one unique L2 anion, and one terminal coordinated water molecule. Each distorted octahedral coordination Co center is coordinated by three O atoms, two chelating carboxylate O atoms (Co1–O1 = 2.051(4), Co1–O2 = 2.263(1) Å) from one L2 ligand, one terminal coordinated water O3 with a Co–O distance of 2.120(3) Å, and three tetrazole N atoms through the 1, 2 and 4-positions (Co1–N1A = 2.189(2), Co1–N3A = 2.140(2) Co1–N4A = 2.176(2) Å) from three different L2 ligands (Fig. 4a). In turn, each ligand coordinates to four Co2+ ions with η1:η1:η1:η1:μ4 bridging mode and the tetrazole group acts in a tridentate bridging coordination mode, whereas the carboxylate group shows a chelating coordination mode (Scheme 1, mode V). The dihedral angle 54.38° between the tetrazole ring and the benzene ring of L2 is responsible for the formation of infinite (L) left- and (R) right-hand helices. The helical chain grows around a crystallographic 21 screw axis formed by tetrazole bridging Co1 atoms, with one helical pitch comprised of two tetrazole groups and three Co2+ ions with pitch lengths of 7.373 Å and the Co⋯Co distance bridged by a tetrazole group is about 6.013 Å. These 1D (L) left- and (R) right-hand helical chains along the c axis with opposite chirality are united alternately together through 4-carboxyl of ligands to exhibit a 2D metal–organic sheet (Fig. 4b). Such layers are further linked by the 4-position of the tetrazole group to give a 3D MOCP (Fig. 5a).
Fig. 4 (a) Coordination environment of Co(II), bonding mode of L2 in the coordination polymer 4. All H atoms are omitted for clarity; (b) view of the left- and right- hand helix formed by tetrazole bridging Co atoms.

Fig. 5 (a) View of the 3D framework of coordination polymer 4 in the ac plane; (b) topological view showing the 3D binodal four-connected PtS net for compound 4; (c) perspective view of the 2D layer formed by tetrazole group and Co atom; (d) views showing the topological structure of the 2D layer.

To get a better insight into the present 3D framework structure, topological analysis was carried out for 4. Each Co(II) atom connects four distinct L2 ligands which can be viewed as a 4-connected node, while each L2 ligands in turn connects four Co(II) atoms, and thus each L2 ligand also can be treated as a 4-connected node. Such connectivity repeats infinitely to give the 3D framework as schematically shown in Fig. 5b. According to the simplification principle and the analysis of TOPOS,21 the resulting structure of compound 4 is a binodal four-connected PtS net, and its point (Schläfli) symbol is (42·84). By further carefully looking into the structure, we surprisingly discovered that, in 4, it involved a 2D planar sheet along the bc plane, which is formed by each tetrazole group adopting a μ3-1, 2, 4 bridging mode connected to three Co ions (Fig. 5c). The 2D sheet contains a 6-membered ring (Co2N4) and a 16-membered ring (Co4N10C2). According to the regulation of the topology, the connections between the μ3-bridged tetrazole group and Co(II) ion form a 2D uninodal 3-connected (4·82) network, where both the Co(II) and μ3-bridged tetrazole group are regarded as two kinds of three-connected nodes (Fig. 5d). If the Co2N4 six-ring is further simplified as square-planer four-connected node, the 4·82 net has a much simpler (4,4) topology containing a square rhombus grid network of dimensions (Fig. S5a, ESI†). Thus, the 3D binodal four-connected PtS net can be further simplified to be a uninodal 8-connected body-centered cubic (bcu) net with a Schläfli symbol of 424·64, which is rare for MOCPs (Fig. S5b, ESI†).24

Crystal structure of [Pb(L2)]n (5). X-ray diffraction analysis shows that compound 5 possesses an interesting 3D network. Fig. 6a displays the coordination environment of the Pb(II) center and bridging mode of L2 in compound 5. Pb(1) adopts an irregular hexacoordinated mode with hemidirected geometry and is coordinated by four carboxylate O atoms and two tetrazole N atoms from five different L2 ligands with Pb–O bond lengths in the range of 2.454–2.648 Å and a Pb–N distance of 2.269 Å. Each L2 ligand links five Pb(II) centers through the η1:η1:η2:η2:μ5 coordination mode (Scheme 1, mode VI), with two tetrazole N atoms coordinating to two Pb2+ ions and the carboxylate groups linking three Pb2+ ions acting as μ3-bridging linkers via anti–anti and chelate bridging coordination fashion. To the best of our knowledge, the coordination mode of the L2 ligand in 5 is firstly observed in M–L2 frameworks. The hemidirected geometry of Pb(II) centers can be attributed to the L2 ligands being not distributed through the entire sphere surrounding the lead atom. Moreover, there is a clearly identifiable gap in the lead coordination sphere, suggesting that the Pb(II) center contains a stereochemically active lone pair of electrons. In Fig. 6b, the adjacent Pb(II) atoms are linked by μ2-bridged (1,4-position) tetrazole groups to form a 1D linear chain, then the 1D chain is bridged by chelate bridged 4-carboxyls of ligands to construct a 2D planar layer, which is comprised of a 23-membered ring. The neighbouring 2D layers are interconnected by the carboxylate groups with anti–anti coordination mode and stacked in an –ABAB– fashion along the direction of the c-axis to result in a 3D framework (Fig. 6c). Interestingly, there is another 2D planar layer in the ac plane, which is constructed from the tetrazole group with the μ2-bridged mode joining each side of the Pb2(COO)2 chain. Each pair of adjacent lead(II) atoms are bridged by the μ3-bridged acid to form an infinite chain along the c-direction. The significant structural feature of this chain motif is envisioned as being a built up parallelogram, formed by two Pb(II) ions and two μ2-O-bridges of acid, with a Pb⋯Pb distances of 4.265 Å (Fig. 6d). Such a Pb2(COO)2 polymeric chain is some similar to that found in the other lead(II) MOCPs.25 From the topological view, another prominent feature of 5 is the presence of the uninodal 5-connected bnn topology,26 if each Pb atom and L2 ligand can be considered as a five-connecting node (Fig. 6e).

All H atoms are omitted for clarity; (b) view of the 2D planar layer; (c) view of the 3D framework of 5 in the bc plane; (d) view of the Pb2(COO)2 chain bridged by tetrazole group; (e) topological view showing the 5-connected 3D bnn topology for compound 5.
Effect of the central metals on the framework. It is noteworthy that the central metals also have a significant effect on the formation of the final structures. The varieties in coordination numbers of the central metals are the key reasons for the structural differences. For compounds [Co3(OH)2(L1)4(H2O)2]2n (1), [Zn(L1)2]n (2) and [Ag(L1)]n (3), the coordination numbers of Zn(II) and Ag(I) ions are lower than those of Co(II) ions. In compound 1, the six-coordinated Co(II) cations are bridged by L1 ions to furnish a 3D framework with (3,6)-connected rtl (rutile) topology. However, in 2 and 3, the four and three coordinated metal centers are linked by L1 anions to form a 3D non-interpenetrated dia net and a 2D layer structure with 3-connected 4·82-fes topology, respectively. For compounds [Co(L2)(H2O)]n (4) and [Pb(L2)]n (5), the coordinated numbers of Co(II) ions in 4 are same as those of the Pb(II) ions in 5, but they exhibit different structures. In 4, the six-coordinated Co(II) ions are bridged L2 anions yielding a binodal four-connected PtS net with the Schläfli symbol being (42·84), while the Pb(II) ions in 5 are linked by L2 anions to give a 3D framework with 5-connected bnn topology. The difference may be attribute to the Pb(II) ion adopting an irregular hexacoordinated mode with hemidirected geometry.

Thermal analysis and PXRD results

Thermal stability of these new crystalline materials has been investigated by thermogravimetric (TG) analysis experiments of mass loss (see Fig. S6, ESI†). For compound 1, the weight loss of 5.9% observed from 25 to 300 °C corresponds to the departure of the two coordination water molecules (calcd 4.1%), and the structure then decomposed starting at 300 °C. The weight loss from 300 to 420 °C is attributed to the collapse of the framework of 1. There is no mass loss for 2 and 3 until 375 and 318 °C, respectively, whereupon expulsion of the organic components occurs and the final mass remnant is of 82.0% for 2 (calcd 83.1%), and 61.7% for 3 (calcd 59.7%). The TG curve of 4 is less stable with a 7.1% (calcd 6.8%) weight loss of one coordination water molecule from 160 to 220 °C, and the framework collapsed at 355 °C. For 5, the TG curve shows a weight loss of 45.8% between 325 to 480 °C is assigned to the network collapsing (calcd 47.6%). To confirm whether the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk materials, PXRD experiments were carried out for 1–5. The PXRD experimental and computer-simulated patterns of the corresponding complexes are shown in Fig. S7, ESI.† They show that the synthesized bulk materials and the measured single crystals are the same.

Photoluminescent properties

Photoluminescent measurements of the two ligands and five compounds were carried out at room temperature. Fig. S8, ESI,† shows a comparison of the two free ligands that were substituted with amino or carboxylate group. It can be observed that the intense broad emission bands were at about 432 and 468 nm (λex = 360 nm) for HL1, and 416 and 466 nm (λex = 335 nm) for H2L2. The emission bands of the two ligands can be assigned to the π* → π or π* → n transition.27

The luminescence properties of the five compounds in the solid state at room temperature were investigated. In Fig. 7, compounds 1, 2, and 4 exhibit intense fluorescent emissions at 440 and 478 nm for 1, 416 and 474 nm for 2, and 442, 478, and 586 for 4 when excited at 380, 344, and 316 nm, respectively. Compared with the luminescence of ligands, the emissions of 1, 2, and 4 are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal transfer (LMCT) in nature, the emission of the complexes is tentatively attributed to the intraligand transition (n → π*, π → π*) of HL1 or H2L2 modified by metal coordination.27 Meanwhile for compounds 3 and 5, the intense broad emission bands are present at 490 and 488 nm when excited at 376 and 316 nm, respectively. The emission of 3 and 5 may be proposed to originate from the coordination of tetrazole rings to the metal centers (ligand-to-metal charge transition, LMCT). The difference in their emissions and excitations is probably due to the differences in metal ions and coordination environment around them because luminescence behavior is closely associated with the local environments around metal ions. To examine the effects of small solvent molecule incorporation, the emission spectra of compounds 1–5 were recorded after immersion in different solvents. Crystals of 1–5 are sensitive to solvent change. Interestingly, the addition of acetonitrile into compounds 1–5 led to enhanced luminescence intensity, while carbon tetrachloride and acetone led to sharply reduced luminescence intensity. Compound 4 is illustrated as shown in Fig. 8, and the spectra of the other compounds are found in Fig. S9–S12, ESI.† Although the mechanism of response to organic solvents is still not clear at this moment, such solvent-dependent luminescent properties of MOCPs might be utilized as the novel functional sensing materials.

Magnetic properties of compounds 1 and 4

Magnetic study of 1. The magnetic susceptibility was measured on a polycrystalline sample of 1 under an applied field of 1000 Oe as shown in Fig. 9. The χMT value at 300 K is 2.80 cm3 mol−1 K (μeff per Co(II) = 4.73 μB), which is higher than that expected for a high-spin Co(II) ion through the spin-only formula, ca. 1.87 cm3 mol−1 K with g = 2.0 (μeff = 3.87 μB). The χMT value decreases gradually as the temperature is lowered from 300 K to 52 K, reaching a minimum of 1.77 cm3 mol−1 K at 52 K, which is attributed to spin–orbit coupling effects and indicates the presence of dominant antiferromagnetic interaction between two Co(II) paramagnetic centers. Then as the temperature is lowered the χMT value increases abruptly to a maximum of 25.31 cm3 mol−1 K at 12 K, and then drops quickly and goes down to a minimum of 4.80 cm3 mol−1 K, which could be due to intermolecular antiferromagnetic interaction or spin canting followed by the effect of saturation phenomena. The dominant antiferromagnetic coupling mainly mediates through the μ3-OH− pathway between two Co(II) centers in compound 1 at high temperature. Moreover, the reciprocal molar magnetic susceptibility data obey the Curie–Weiss law in the high temperature region of 75–300 K with a Curie constant of C = 3.31 cm3 mol−1 K (close to the value of 2.8–3.4 cm3 mol−1 K expected for an octahedral Co(II) ion) and a Weiss constant of θ = −57.89 K. The negative value of the Weiss temperature indicates the dominant interactions between the Co(II) ions are of antiferromagnetic type. However, an abrupt rise in χMT value below 52 K suggests a magnetic transition in compound 1. Below 30 K, the susceptibility data is field dependent, as shown in Fig. 10, consistent with spin canting. For further proof of the presence of spin canting, zero-field-cooled (ZFC) and field-cooled (FC) magnetization experiments were carried out at an applied field of 10 Oe (Fig. 11). The ZFC curve increases as the temperature rises from 2 K and reaches a maximum at 10 K followed by gradual fall upon further increasing temperature, which is the signature of magnetic ordering. Upon cooling, the FC curve increases rapidly and diverges from the ZFC curve at 13.7 K. The divergence observed in FC and ZFC indicates the history dependence of the magnetization process. It is well-known that the occurrence of spin canting usually is caused by either single-ion magnetic anisotropy or antisymmetric exchange in magnetic entities.28 In 1, the observed spin canting may be attributed to the antisymmetric magnetic exchange, which is related to the symmetry of the magnetic entities.

Magnetic properties of 4. Magnetic susceptibility measurements were carried out on a crystalline sample of 4 with an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe in the temperature range 2–300 K using a quantum designed SQUID magnetometer, and shown as χMT and χM−1versus T plots in Fig. 12. The χMT value of 4 at room temperature is 3.07 cm3 mol−1 K, which is larger than the spin-only value of 1.87 cm3 mol−1 K expected for an orbital contribution (S = 3/2). As the temperature is lowered to 2 K, the χMT values decrease first slowly and then rapidly. This behavior suggests the existence of weak antiferromagnetic exchange behavior even if the single-ion effects, such as spin–orbit coupling, distortion from regular stereochemistry, electron delocalization, and crystal field mixing of excited states into the ground state, may also be present.28b The molar magnetic susceptibility data in the temperature range of 30–300 K can be well fitted to the Curie–Weiss law giving Curie constant C = 3.30 cm3 mol−1 K and a negative Weiss constant θ = −23.91 K. This further confirms an overall antiferromagnetic interaction between the Co(II) ions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, using two tetrazol–benzoate ligands HL1 and H2L2 with different substituent groups –NH2 and –COOH, five M–tetrazole compounds have been hydro(solvo)thermally synthesized and these MOCPs exhibit various 2D and 3D network architectures. It suggests that the substituent groups of tetrazol–benzoate ligands play significantly roles in construction of the final structures. In addition, the metal centers also have great influence on the structure of the final assembly and different properties of these MOCPs. The luminescent and magnetic properties of compounds 1 and 4 indicate that they may be good candidates for multifunctional materials.
