New topological Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) as a highly active heterogeneous catalyst for the amination of oxazoles via oxidative C–H/N–H couplings†

Thach N. Tu‡ ab, Khoa D. Nguyen‡ a, Truong N. Nguyen a, Thanh Truong *a and Nam T. S. Phan *a
aFaculty of Chemical Engineering, HCMC University of Technology, VNU-HCM, 268 Ly Thuong Kiet, District 10, Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam. E-mail: tvthanh@hcmut.edu.vn; ptsnam@hcmut.edu.vn
bCenter for Molecular and NanoArchitecture (MANAR), VNU-HCM, Ho Chi Minh City 721337, Vietnam

Received 21st July 2015 , Accepted 21st September 2015

First published on 22nd September 2015

Herein, we report the synthesis, structural characterization, and catalytic properties of a new cobalt-based metal–organic framework, Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) (where BDC = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate and DABCO = 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane), namely VNU-10 (where VNU = Vietnam National University). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that VNU-10 crystallizes in the hexagonal space group P6/mmm. The structure of this material is comprised of two-dimensional kgm layers which are interconnected by DABCO pillars to afford an overall three-dimensional porous architecture. VNU-10 exhibited exceptional catalytic activity toward the direct amination of oxazoles via C–H/N–H couplings while a previously reported topological isomer, Co2(BDC)2(DABCO), with the sql topology, displayed poor activity. Leaching tests indicated that homogeneous catalysis via leached active cobalt species is unlikely. Furthermore, the VNU-10 catalyst was facilely isolated from the reaction mixture and reused several times without degradation of the catalytic reactivity.

1. Introduction

Molecules containing aryl- and heteroarylamine moieties are frequently found in a variety of biologically active natural products, polymers, as well as functional materials.1,2 Traditional routes to access these molecules most often employ nitrenoid chemistry.3,4 Since the pioneering work by Buchwald and Hartwig,5,6 metal-mediated C–N bond formation directly from C–H bonds have attracted an increasing amount of interest.7 To perform such transformations, nitrogen moieties have been commonly installed by using protected amines or hydroxylamine derivatives as starting materials.8,9 Simple amine coupling partners employed in the direct C–N bond formation of unfunctionalized arenes or heterocyclic compounds have rarely been reported. Typically, palladium and rhodium were exploited as catalysts for these transformations, mostly in the intramolecular fashion.10–13 The first method for the copper-catalyzed directed amination of arene C–H bonds was developed by the Yu group.14 Subsequent reports have demonstrated the ortho amination of 2-phenylpyridine derivatives through the use of copper salts.15–17 Furthermore, Miura, Chang, and Schreiber independently described procedures for deprotonative thiazole and oxazole amination under copper catalysis.8,18,19 The first example of silver-mediated benzoxazole amination by formamides or parent amines has also been disclosed.20 Furthermore, it was recently reported that cobalt and manganese salts are catalytically active for the transformation.21 By performing reactions under acidic conditions, Chang and co-workers were able to direct the oxidative amination of a variety of azoles in the presence of a peroxide oxidant.21 Despite the significant progress that has been made toward developing highly active homogeneous catalysts, the development of more economically and environmentally efficient protocols, most notably by using heterogeneous catalytic systems, for this transformation has yet to be reported.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a new class of porous materials with distinct structural properties, which has led to the intensive examination of their properties for potential applications in several areas including gas storage, sensors, thin films, optics, drug carriers, and catalysis.22–26 MOFs have also been pursued as heterogeneous catalysts by virtue of inherent catalytically active sites within the frameworks or as supporting materials for other catalysts.27–31 There have been many reports of MOFs having been investigated as heterogeneous catalysts or catalyst supports for a variety of organic transformations including cross coupling, oxidation, and multicomponent reactions.32–39 Among the most popular MOF catalysts, zinc-,28–31 copper-,40–44 and nickel-based45 structures have received the most interest. The use of Co-based MOFs as catalysts has mainly focused on oxidation or olefin epoxidation reactions.46–48 Reported procedures using heterogeneous Co(II)-MOFs for cross coupling reactions are rare due to the fact that mostly low-valent cobalt species, Co(0), are found to be active under organometallic viewpoints.49 It has been recently demonstrated that the catalytic activities of MOFs containing transition metals were greatly affected by their structural topologies as well as the organic linkers.50 In this work, by modifying the synthetic conditions, we report the synthesis and discovery of a new topological isomer of Co2(BDC)2(DABCO), namely VNU-10. A full structural analysis was performed in conjunction with single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis to support the new structure. Remarkably, VNU-10 exhibited exceptional catalytic activity toward the direct amination of azoles by N–H bonds at room temperature while no activity was observed when the previously reported Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) topological isomer was employed.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and instrumentation

All reagents and starting materials were obtained commercially from Sigma-Aldrich and Merck, and were used as received without any further purification unless otherwise noted. The single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for VNU-10 was collected on a Bruker D8 Venture diffractometer equipped with a PHOTON-100 CMOS detector. Monochromatic microfocus Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å) operated at 50 kW and 1.0 mA was used. Prior to data collection, a single crystal of VNU-10 was cooled down to 100 K using chilled nitrogen flow controlled by a Kryoflex II system. Unit cell determination was performed using the Bruker SMART APEX II software suite. The data sets were reduced and a multi-scan spherical absorption correction was implemented in the SCALE interface.51 The structures were solved with direct methods and further refined by the full-matrix least-squares method in the SHELX-97 program package.52 After locating the framework atoms in the difference Fourier maps, the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON was run to remove scattering from the disordered guest molecules inside the pores.53 Crystallographic data for VNU-10 has been deposited in the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre (deposition no. 1063411). Powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) were collected using a Bruker D8 Advance equipped with a Ni filtered Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å) source. The diffractometer was also equipped with an anti-scattering shield that prevented incident diffuse radiation from hitting the detector. Nitrogen physisorption measurements were conducted using a Micromeritics 2020 volumetric adsorption analyzer system. The samples were pretreated by heating under vacuum at 150 °C for 3 h. A Netzsch Thermoanalyzer STA 409 was used for thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Scanning electron microscopy studies were conducted on a JSM 740 scanning electron microscope (SEM). Transmission electron microscopy studies were performed using a JEOL JEM 1400 Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM) at 100 kV. In a typical experiment, VNU-10 sample was dispersed on holey carbon grids prior to TEM observation. Elemental analysis with atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) was performed on an AA-6800 Shimadzu. The Fourier-transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained on a Nicolet 6700 instrument, with samples being dispersed on potassium bromide pellets. The chemisorption experiments were studied in a Micromeritics 2020 analyzer. For hydrogen temperature-programmed reduction (H2-TPR), the sample was outgassed at 100 °C for 30 min with helium, then cooled down to room temperature, and exposed to 50 mL min−1 of 10% H2/Ar as the temperature ramped at 2.5 °C min−1 to 600 °C. The amount of hydrogen consumption was determined from TCD signal intensities, which were calibrated using an Ag2O reference sample.

Gas chromatographic (GC) analyses were performed using a Shimadzu GC 2010-Plus equipped with a flame ionization detector (FID) and an SPB-5 column (length = 30 m, inner diameter = 0.25 mm, and film thickness = 0.25 μm). The temperature program for GC analysis held the samples at 120 °C for 1 min; heated them from 120 to 180 °C at 50 °C min−1; held them at 180 °C for 1 min; heated them from 180 to 280 °C at 50 °C min−1 and held them at 280 °C for 3 min. The inlet and detector temperatures were set constant at 280 °C. Diphenyl ether was used as an internal standard to calculate reaction conversions. GC-MS analyses were performed using a Hewlett Packard GC-MS 5972 with a RTX-5MS column (length = 30 m, inner diameter = 0.25 mm, and film thickness = 0.5 μm). The temperature program for GC-MS analysis heated the samples from 60 to 280 °C at 10 °C min−1 and held them at 280 °C for 2 min. The inlet temperature was set constant at 280 °C. The MS spectra were compared with the spectra gathered in the NIST library. The 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded in CDCl3 using TMS as an internal standard on a Bruker NMR spectrometer at 500 MHz and 125 MHz, respectively. Elemental microanalyses (EA) were performed in the Microanalytical Laboratory of the College of Chemistry at UC Berkeley, using a Perkin Elmer 2400 Series II CHNS elemental analyzer.

2.2. Synthesis of the metal–organic framework Co2(BDC)2(DABCO), VNU-10

In a typical synthesis procedure, a mixture of 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2BDC) (0.1 g, 0.60 mmol), 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane (DABCO) (0.075 g, 0.67 mmol), and Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.1 g, 0.34 mmol) was dissolved in a solvent mixture of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (20 mL), CH3COOH (2 mL, 0.01 mmol), and HCl (20 μl, 0.24 μmol). The resulting solution was then dispensed equally to ten different 10 mL vials. The vials were heated at 120 °C in an isothermal oven for 12 h. After cooling the vials to room temperature, the solid product was removed by decanting the mother liquor and then washed with DMF (3 × 10 mL) to remove any unreacted species. The DMF solvent was exchanged with dichloromethane (DCM) (3 × 10 mL) at room temperature. The product was then dried at 120 °C for 4 h under vacuum, yielding green needle-shaped crystals of VNU-10 (76% based on Co(NO3)2·6H2O).

2.3. Catalytic studies

A mixture of benzoxazole (0.101 mL, 1 mmol), acetic acid (0.114 mL, 2.0 mmol), and diphenyl ether (0.15 mL, 0.95 mmol), as an internal standard, in acetonitrile (5 mL) was added together into a 25 mL round bottom flask containing VNU-10 catalyst (0.0143 g, 5 mol%). The catalyst amount was calculated with respect to the cobalt/benzoxazole molar ratio. The reaction mixture was magnetically stirred for 3 min to disperse the catalyst throughout the liquid phase. Piperidine and tert-butyl hydroperoxide (TBHP) were then added. The resulting mixture was continuously stirred at room temperature for 1 h. Reaction conversion was monitored by withdrawing aliquots from the reaction mixture at different time intervals, followed by quenching with aqueous KOH solution (5% (w/w), 1 mL). The organic components were then extracted into ethyl acetate (2 mL), dried over anhydrous Na2SO4, and then analyzed using GC with reference to diphenyl ether. The product identity was further confirmed using GC-MS, 1H NMR, and 13C NMR. In the recycling studies, the catalyst was separated from the reaction mixture by centrifugation, washed, and heated with copious amounts of DMF at 100 °C for 2 h. The recovered VNU-10 was then activated under vacuum at room temperature for 4 h, and reused for the next run under identical conditions. For the leaching test, the catalytic reaction was stopped after 5 min, analyzed by GC, and centrifuged to remove the solid catalyst. The reaction solution was then stirred for an additional 55 min. The reaction progress, if any, was monitored by GC as previously described.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Catalyst synthesis and characterization

Kaskel and co-workers previously reported the formation of a Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) framework with a pillared sql topology composed of square-grid layers and DABCO pillars (Fig. 1).54 Isoreticular (having the same topology) structures have also been reported for Zn and Ni.55 In the presence of CH3COOH and HCl additives to the synthetic conditions, a different topology for Co2(BDC)2(DABCO), which we name VNU-10, was obtained.

(c) Crystal structure of DABCO-pillared 4,4-connected sql framework (Co2(BDC)2(DABCO)) along the c axis. (d) Expansion node in the 3,4-connected kgm grid. (e) Crystal structure of DABCO-pillared 3,4-connected kgm framework (VNU-10) along the c axis. Atom colors: C, black; O, red; N, blue; and Co, purple polyhedra.

To clarify the effect of added acids on the formation of new topology, experiments using different amounts of acids were conducted (see the ESI†). Specifically, the color of the DMF solution containing Co(NO3)2·6H2O shifted from purple to deep blue when HCl was added. This suggests the formation of (CoCl4)2− from Co(H2O)62−. Interestingly, the results also indicated that a pure phase of the new topology was selectively formed under restricted conditions. Thus, using less or more amount of acids than the reported numbers led to the formation of the previous sql topology. Though the effect of CH3COOH and HCl to the formation of VNU-10 is abundantly unclear, the changes in pH and anionic environment (AcO–, Cl–) are likely the key factors.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of this topological isomer revealed that the structure consisted of cobalt paddlewheel units that are connected, in a slightly bent fashion, by four BDC linkers (Fig. 1, Table S1†). This cluster formation coupled with the bent nature of the linkage allowed the formation of two-dimensional kagome (kgm) sheets (Fig. 1). Furthermore, the DABCO ligands on the apical sites of the paddlewheel cobalt clusters link each kgm sheet to one another. Consequently, a three-dimensional framework is formed with 4.5 Å triangular and 15 Å hexagonal channels running along the c axis and a ~3.5 Å rectangular channel along the a and b axes (Fig. 1). It is noted that the pillared kgm topology has been observed for Zn2(BDC)2(DABCO) and Ni2(BDC)2(DABCO) isomers;55–58 however, this is the first report of the Co-based version.

Phase purity of the bulk sample was assessed by PXRD analysis. For this analysis, the PXRD patterns were calculated and generated based on the structural models of Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) for both the pillared kgm and sql topological isomers and compared with the measured experimental pattern for VNU-10. The results indicated that the structure of the as-synthesized VNU-10 was consistent with the single crystal data for the pillared kgm Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) isomer (Fig. 2a). Furthermore, elemental analysis by AAS indicated a cobalt amount of 20.0%, which is line with the theoretical value of 20.9%. SEM observation displayed well-shaped, high quality needle crystals (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, the TEM micrograph revealed a complex porous structure as expected (Fig. S3†). Nitrogen isotherm measurements at 77 K confirmed the permanent porosity of VNU-10 with calculated Brunauer–Emmett–Teller and Langmuir surface areas of 2235 and 2256 m2 g−1, respectively (Fig. 2c). Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) of activated VNU-10 demonstrates the material's high thermal stability and architectural robustness (>340 °C) (Fig. 2d). In addition, the FT-IR spectra of VNU-10 indicated the presence of the DABCO ligand and bonded carboxylate organic linkers (Fig. S7†). Finally, elemental analysis results supported the molecular formula of Co2C22H26O11N2 (see the ESI†).

Initial studies addressed the effect of the reagent molar ratio, which was determined to have a significant effect on the reaction conversion. The amination reaction with 3% catalyst could proceed to 81% conversion after 1 h when using two equivalents of piperidine, whereas a remarkable decrease in reaction efficiency was observed when lower amounts of piperidine was employed (entries 1–3). Interestingly, close to the quantitative conversion was achieved for the reaction using 5% catalyst (entry 4). With respect to the solvents, reasonable reaction rates were also observed in dichloromethane and ethanol (entries 6, 9). Other solvents, such as dioxane and toluene, were shown to be less sufficient when <60% conversions were obtained (entries 7, 8). Previously reported optimized conditions for homogeneous copper-catalyzed amination of sp2 C–H bonds involved the use of nonpolar solvents such as toluene and xylene.18,59,60 In addition, when compared to acetic acid, benzoic and formic acid afforded slightly lower activity while only 75% was obtained with trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) (entries 11–13). Reactions with 1 equiv. of acid or no acid produced conversions of 69% and 8%, respectively (entries 10, 14).

The effect of various oxidants on the reaction yields was also investigated (Table 2). The direct amination reaction was carried out at room temperature in CH3CN using 5 mol% VNU-10 catalyst in the presence of two equivalents of the tested oxidant and two equivalents of acetic acid. In particular, the reactions using hydrogen peroxide and di-tert-butyl peroxide proceeded with difficulty, affording only 19% and 30% conversions, respectively (entries 1, 2). The inorganic oxidant, K2S2O8, was found to be unsuitable with only a trace amount of product being detected (entry 3). Similarly, low reaction conversion was observed when only air was employed as the reaction oxidant (entry 4). In contrast, the reactions that used PhI(OAc)2 as the oxidant resulted in good conversion while the role of air in the reaction atmosphere was showed to be negligible (entries 5, 6). The use of a peroxide oxidant in the pioneering work of Chang was proposed to form corresponding alkoxy and alkylperoxy radicals, which further abstract the hydrogen from the reaction intermediates.21

To highlight the advantages of our catalyst, the activity of the VNU-10 was compared to other similarly related MOFs, including Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) with square-grid layers (Co2(BDC)2(DABCO)sql), as well as other common Co(II) salts (Table 3). Under the optimized conditions, only 12% and 21% conversions were detected for the case of Ni2(BDC)2(DABCO)kgm and Cu2(BDC)2(DABCO)kgm, respectively (entries 2, 3). Similar results were obtained when the DABCO ligand was removed from the Co-MOFs structure (entry 4). Unprecedentedly, low reaction conversion was obtained when the reported Co2(BDC)2(DABCO)sql was employed (entry 5). The remarkably high catalytic activities of the pillared kgm-layered frameworks can be rationalized by their larger cavities. Co-ZIF-67 is ineffective with an unappreciable amount of product being observed (entry 6). Other solid cobalt-based catalysts such as magnetic ferrite CoFe2O4 and Co-Zeolite X also exhibited poor activity under tested conditions (entries 7, 8). Interestingly, common Co(II) salts resulted in similar or slightly slower conversions when compared to the VNU-10 catalyst. However, it is worth mentioning that purification of contaminated metals in the final products of homogeneous catalysis often represents a problematic issue, especially in the pharmaceutical industry.61

In order to determine if active cobalt species that leaches out from the solid VNU-10 catalyst contribute to the total conversion of the direct benzoxazole amination reaction, a control experiment was carried out (Fig. 3). In such an experiment, the direct benzoxazole amination reaction was performed at room temperature in acetonitrile with 5 mol% VNU-10 catalyst, two equivalents of piperidine, two equivalents of tert-butyl hydroperoxide, and two equivalents of acetic acid. The VNU-10 catalyst was removed by simple centrifugation from the reaction mixture 5 min after the reaction had been initiated. The reaction solution was then transferred to a new reactor vessel, and stirred for an additional 55 min at room temperature with aliquots being sampled at different time intervals. It was observed that almost no further conversion was detected for the direct benzoxazole amination reaction after the VNU-10 catalyst was separated from the reaction mixture. Moreover, ICP-MS of the reaction filtrate revealed that less than 20 ppm of Co was presented. These observations confirm the heterogeneous nature of the developed protocol and that catalysis by leached active species is unlikely.

In addition, to further address the issue of where the catalysis takes place, experiments using Co-MOF catalysts with different particle sizes were performed under identical conditions (Fig. S21†). It was found that similar reaction yields were obtained (Scheme 1). About a 10% drop with Co-MOFs possessing a large particle size might come from the diffusion limitation. In addition, the size of the pore according to the Co-MOFs structure is 15 Å running along the c axis while the kinetic diameters of aromatic reactions and product are calculated to be 5 Å and 8–10 Å, respectively.62 Though further studies are needed for better understanding, it is likely that the reactions occur inside the cavities.

For the development of more environmentally benign procedures, the ability to recover and reuse the VNU-10 catalyst was investigated. The reaction was then carried out and upon completion, the catalyst was separated from the reaction mixture by centrifugation. The recovered VNU-10 was washed with acetonitrile and DMF, activated under vacuum at room temperature for 4 h, and then reused in a following reaction under identical conditions as the previous run. It was found that the VNU-10 catalyst could be recovered and reused several times without any significant degradation in catalytic activity. Indeed, a conversion of 80% was achieved in the 10th run (Fig. 4). PXRD analysis of the reused catalyst confirmed that the crystallinity of VNU-10 was retained over the course of the recycling studies (Fig. 5). Furthermore, the FT-IR spectra exhibited a similar absorption as compared to that of the fresh VNU-10. These results demonstrated the potential of VNU-10 as a heterogeneous catalyst for modern organic transformations.

Reactions under optimized conditions using different amines were described (Table 4). Functionality with secondary amines occurs at the weakest C–H bond in benzoxazole. Piperidines with substituents at C3 and C4 are active and the aminated products were obtained in excellent yields (entries 2, 3). Amination by other cyclic secondary amines such as morpholine and 4-methylpiperazine afforded reasonable yields of 53% and 57%, respectively (entries 4, 5). The optimized conditions were also applicable for the cross coupling reactions of N-methylbenzylamine (entry 6). Significant improvement in reaction efficiency of aliphatic acyclic amine was achieved when the reactions were conducted at 60 °C (entry 7). Interestingly, a heterocyclic amine, which has not been reported under homogeneous conditions, was cross-coupled in moderate yield (entry 8).

4. Conclusions

In summary, a new topological isomer of Co2(BDC)2(DABCO), VNU-10, was synthesized by using a solvothermal method. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of VNU-10 revealed that the structure was built from DABCO-pillared kagome layers that produced triangular and hexagonal channels of sufficient size to carry out important organic transformations. VNU-10 was also characterized by a host of techniques including PXRD, SEM, TEM, FT-IR, TGA, AAS, and N2 physisorption measurements. Furthermore, this material was used as an efficient heterogeneous catalyst for the direct amination reactions of oxazoles. Excellent conversions with a variety of amines were obtained. Most remarkably, VNU-10 offered significantly higher activity than that of Co2(BDC)2(DABCO) with the pillared sql structure as well as other Co-based heterogeneous catalysts. VNU-10 was confirmed to be recyclable without a significant degradation in catalytic activity. Finally, leaching tests showed no contribution from homogeneous leached active cobalt species.
