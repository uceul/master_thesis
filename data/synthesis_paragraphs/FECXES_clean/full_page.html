By using an octadentate ligand tetrakis[(3,5-dicarboxyphenoxy)methyl] methane (H8L), a new microporous metalâ€“organic framework [Cu4L(H2O)4](H2O)31 (1) was solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. MOF 1 is built from the 4-connected square planar [Cu2(O2CR)4] cluster and 8-connected cube ligand L to have a highly open framework with scu topology. It possesses permanent porosity as confirmed by N2, H2, C2H2, CO2 and CH4 gas adsorption measurements and exhibits high acetylene storage of 193 and 154 cm3 gâˆ’1 at 273 and 296 K under 1 atm, respectively.

1. Introduction

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as very promising materials for H2, CH4, CO2 and C2H2 storage and capture.1â€“17 This is because the high permanent porosities within MOFs can provide the necessary space to take up gas molecules, while the tunable pore sizes/curvatures and functionalized pore surfaces can be utilized to direct their strong interactions with gas molecules. In fact, some world record MOF materials for the gas storage have been realized over the past decade,8,9c Typically, it is necessary to have rigid organic linkers and metal-containing clusters (generally termed as secondary building units (SBUs)) to stabilize the frameworks and establish their permanent porosities for their gas uptake; thus previous research to construct robust MOFs has been mainly focused on the deliberate design and suitable choice of rigid organic linkers and SBUs. With these in mind, a variety of rigid organic linkers such as terephthalic acid, 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate, 1,1â€²-ethynebenzene-3,3â€²,5,5â€²-tetracarboxylate (EBTC)8d and benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylate, and SBUs such as paddlewheel cluster M2(CO2)4 (M = Cu, Zn, Co and Ni) and Zn4O(CO2)617 have been successfully incorporated into a series of robust MOFs. Further development on porous MOFs has motivated us to address the possibility of making use of flexible organic linkers to synthesize robust MOFs.18 The success of this new strategy will significantly broaden the dictionary database of porous MOFs, providing the fundamental basis to tune and optimize pore structures to target high performance gas storage and separation of MOF materials. Given the fact that m-benzenedicarboylate moieties are very powerful organic groups to stabilize the frameworks by the formation of paddlewheel Cu2(CO2)4 clusters and the corresponding cages of moderate sizes,2,3 herein we report the synthesis of a new flexible organic linker tetrakis[(3,5-dicarboxyphenoxy)methyl] methane (H8L, Scheme 1) and its resulting robust MOF [Cu4L(H2O)4]Â·(H2O)31 (MOF 1) of the rare (4,8)-connected scu topology for acetylene storage at ambient temperature.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

1 was prepared solvothermally under the acidic condition from corresponding Cu(NO3)2Â·4H2O and H8L in an ethanolâ€“water mixture at 100 Â°C even without using the typical solvent DMF. The as-synthesized product is of pure phase as further confirmed by XRPD and elemental analyses. MOF 1 loses 31 lattice water molecules and 4 coordinated water molecules in the temperature range from room temperature to about 150 Â°C with the experimentally determined water weight loss of 37.4% (Fig. S2â€ ), which matches with the calculated one of 37.8%. The desolvated 1 can be thermally stable up to about 300 Â°C.

3.2. Crystal structure descriptions

MOF 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pbcm with four asymmetric units in one cell. Each asymmetric unit contains a half L8âˆ’ ligand, three Cu(II) ions, and three coordinated water molecules (Fig. S1â€ ). As shown in Fig. 1a, the three CuII are five-coordinated in a square pyramidal geometry by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different L8âˆ’ ligands at the basal positions and one water molecule at the apical position. The two adjacent CuII ions are linked together by four carboxylate groups to form a paddlewheel dinuclear cluster [Cu2(O2CR)4]. The Cuâ€“O, Cuâ€“Ow and Cuâ‹¯Cu distances range 1.914(2)â€“1.969(2), 2.144(3)â€“2.162(2), and 2.6371(7)â€“2.6395(7) Ã…, respectively, which are consistent with those found in other [Cu2(O2CR)4] paddlewheel based MOFs.22 The dicopper paddlewheel cluster is linked by four L8âˆ’ ligands via carboxylate groups in a square coordination geometry, while the L8âˆ’ ligand, with the center quaternary carbon atom sitting on a 2-fold symmetry axis, adopts a distorted cube coordination geometry to connect eight neighbouring dicopper paddlewheels. This gives rise to a non-interpenetrated (4,8)-connected network with the scu topology23 (SchlÃ¤fli symbol of {4462}2{416612}) (Fig. 1b), in which the dicopper paddlewheel cluster and organic linker are regarded as 4-connected and 8-connected nodes, respectively. It is noted that 1 is one of the few known MOFs with scu topology reported to date.24
Fig. 1 Single-crystal X-ray structure of 1, showing (a) the organic ligand which is connected to eight [Cu2(O2CR)4] SBUs, (b) the resulting 4,8-connected framework with scu topology, and structures viewed along (c) the a axis and (d) the c axis to illustrate the pore windows and corresponding interconnecting cages (color scheme: C, gray; H, white; O, red; and Cu, blue).

Viewed along the a and c axes in 1, there exist two types of micropore windows of approximately 5.5 Ã— 5.5 Ã…2 and 5.5 Ã— 5.8 Ã…2, respectively, taking into account van der Waals distances. Close examination of the structure reveals that 1 consists of two types of interconnecting cages: one is small octahedral cages (purple spheres in Fig. 1c and d) of approximately 4.7 Ã… in diameter, built from four dicopper paddlewheel clusters and two half L ligands; another is larger cuboctahedral cages (green and yellow spheres in Fig. 1) with an approximate diameter of 9.0 Ã…, composed of eight dicopper paddlewheel clusters and four half L ligands. Each cage connects with eight neighbouring ones of different types to form a highly porous framework. PLATON calculations show that the guest accessible void (5125.5 Ã…3 per unit cell) comprises 61.9% of the unit cell volume.

3.4. Adsorption properties

The permanent porosity of MOF 1 was confirmed by gas adsorption measurements of N2, H2, CO2, CH4 and C2H2. As shown in Fig. 3a, the N2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K for 1 shows type-I behavior with a steep rise in the very low pressure region, suggesting a microporous nature of the activated 1. The Brunauerâ€“Emmettâ€“Teller (BET) area, the Langmuir surface area and pore volume were calculated from the N2 adsorption isotherm to be 1115 m2 gâˆ’1, 1722 m2 gâˆ’1, and 0.612 cm3 gâˆ’1, respectively. The Horvathâ€“Kawazoe (HK) model calculations indicate a median pore diameter of 5.8 Ã…, which is consistent with that calculated from the single crystal structure analysis.
Fig. 3 Gas sorption isotherms of the MOF 1 for (a) N2 and H2 at 77 K; (b) CO2 and CH4 at 273 K; and (c) C2H2 at 273 and 296 K.

The H2 sorption measurements at 77 K showed that the activated 1 exhibited reversible hydrogen adsorption isotherm curves (Fig. 3a), indicating a strong affinity of hydrogen molecules toward pore surfaces. At 77 K and 760 Torr, the H2 uptake of 1 is 238 cm3 gâˆ’1 (2.12 wt%), which is moderately high.25 The activated 1 also takes up certain amount of carbon dioxide (140 cm3 gâˆ’1, 6.25 mmol gâˆ’1) and methane (32 cm3 gâˆ’1, 1.42 mmol gâˆ’1) at 273 K and 760 Torr (Fig. 3b).

The most important feature of MOF 1 is its high acetylene uptake of 193 and 154 cm3 gâˆ’1 under 1 atm at 273 K and 296 K, respectively (Fig. 3c). Although MOF 1 is less porous than MOF-5058c and Cu2(EBTC),8d the acetylene storage capacity of MOF 1 (154 cm3 gâˆ’1) is comparable to 148 cm3 gâˆ’1 for MOF-505 and 160 cm3 gâˆ’1 for Cu2(EBTC), and thus is amongst the few MOFs for high acetylene storage.8bâ€“d,16 The storage capacities of MOF 1 for acetylene are still not saturated, which means that their uptake can be further maximized until the limit of safe storage of acetylene under a pressure of 0.2 MPa.26

The high acetylene storage capacities of MOF 1 are attributed to the high density of open copper(II) sites within MOF 1. As established in HKUST-1 in which each open copper(II) site binds one acetylene molecule, the copper(II) site density of 3.85 mmol gâˆ’1 in MOF 1 can contribute 86.3 cm3 storage capacity of the total 154 cm3 at 1 atm and 296 K. The remaining acetylene (67.7 cm3) can be easily stored inside the pore space of moderate sizes (cages of 4.7 and 9.0 Ã…) within MOF 1.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized a novel robust open metalâ€“organic framework [Cu4L(H2O)4]Â·(H2O)31 (1) with (4,8)-scu topology based on a flexible tetrahedrally branched octadentate ligand (H8L) and the paddlewheel Cu2(O2C)4 cluster. The new MOF possesses interconnecting cages with unsaturated open metal sites after thermal activation. It exhibits permanent porosity and high acetylene uptake of 193 and 154 cm3 gâˆ’1 at 273 and 296 K under 1 atm, respectively, highlighting its potential as an acetylene storage material. This study shows that it is also feasible to construct robust MOFs from flexible organic linkers once the organic linkers are suitably decorated to favor the formation of robust metal-containing secondary building blocks, thus providing a new strategy to explore new porous MOF materials for high gas storage and separation in the near future.
