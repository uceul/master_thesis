    Abstract

    A new three-dimensional Zn(II) metal–organic framework (MOF) of {[Zn4(bpydb)3(datz)2(H2O)](DMF)4(EtOH)5(H2O)8}n (1, bpydbH2 = 4,4′-(4,4′-bipyridine-2,6-diyl) dibenzoic acid, Hdatz = 3,5-diamino-1,2,4-triazole) with amino- and pyridine-functionalized pores has been successfully constructed under solvothermal conditions by using the mixed-ligand strategy, which consists of twisty 63 layers pillared by datz– ligands. The adsorption properties of this MOF were studied by CO2, N2, and CH4 sorption measurements. Compared with the analogous 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) pillared MOF {Zn(bpydb)(bpy)}n (2) we reported earlier, the activated 1 showed enhanced CO2 sorption capacity and good sorption selectivity for CO2 over N2 and CH4 at room temperature.

    Synopsis

    A new three-dimensional Zn(II) metal−organic framework (MOF) with amino- and pyridine-decorated pores has been successfully constructed under solvothermal conditions by using the mixed-ligand strategy, which consists of twisty 63 layers pillared by the datz− ligand. This MOF shows high CO2 sorption capacity and good sorption selectivity for CO2 over N2 and CH4 at room temperature.

    The petrol and automobile industries have developed rapidly in the recent two decades, with an accompanying large emission of CO2 into the atmosphere. The escalating level of atmospheric CO2 has led to severe climate change and global warming.(1) Hence selective capture and separation of CO2 are of great significance in environmental and industrial aspects. In recent years, a new family of porous crystalline materials named metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has gained intensive attention not only for their intriguing variety of structures but also for their potential applications in many fields such as CO2 storage/separation, asymmetry catalysis, and luminescent sensors.(2-8) Some of their properties, such as CO2 sorption and separation, largely depend on the framework–CO2 interactions.(9-11) An efficient way to modify the CO2 sorption capacity and selectivity is to take advantage of the high quadrupole moment of CO2, which endows CO2 with strong interaction with some polar functional groups (such as -NH2, -COOH, -CF3) in the pores of MOFs.(12) The functionalization of the organic ligands with polarity groups should be a reasonable strategy for the construction of targeted MOFs for CO2 capture and separation. However, the fabrication of the functional organic ligands especially those with a larger number of aromatic cores by means of organic synthesis strategies usually needs expensive starting materials and multireaction steps, which makes this strategy costly and difficult to manipulate.(13-15) Introduction of functional groups into MOFs by using some N-rich auxiliary ligands seems to be feasible. For example, Shimizu et al. have successfully synthesized a microporous MOF by using 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole as an auxiliary ligand; the free NH2 groups in the framework endow this MOF with high CO2 uptake and adsorption enthalpy.(16) 3,5-Diamino-1,2,4-triazole (Hdatz) should be another particularly effective auxiliary ligand for the following reasons: (i) Its rigid conformation helps to build MOFs with permanently pores. (ii) Its free NH2 groups make this ligand an ideal building block for the construction of MOFs for CO2 capture. In our previous study, we demonstrate structural transformations from a two-dimensional (2D) layer to three-dimensional (3D) pillar-layered MOFs by using the mixed ligands of 4,4′-(4,4′-bipyridine-2,6-diyl)dibenzoic acid (bpydbH2) and 4,4′-bipydine (4,4′-bpy).(17) Though the 2-fold interpenetrated MOF {Zn(bpydb)(bpy)}n (2) possesses a high solvent-access volume and high Qst value, it only showed moderate CO2 sorption capacity and selectivity. We speculate that the datz– pillared 63 layers may result in an MOF with enhanced sorption capacity and selectivity for CO2. With this in mind, the ligand Hdatz was selected as the pillar, and a new MOF {[Zn4(bpydb)3(datz)2(H2O)](DMF)4(EtOH)5(H2O)8}n (1) with 2-fold interpenetrated structure was harvested. The sorption properties of activated 1 were studied by CO2, N2, and CH4 sorption measurements. Compared with MOF 2, the activated 1 showed enhanced CO2 sorption capacity and good sorption selectivity for CO2 over N2 and CH4 at room temperature.

    Materials and Methods

    The ligand 4,4′-(4,4′-bipyridine-2,6-diyl) dibenzoic acid (bpydbH2, Scheme 1) was prepared according to the modified literature procedure, and other materials and reagents are commercially available and were used as received.(18, 19) Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were obtained on a PerkinElmer analyzer. Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained using the Bruker Tensor 27 spectrometer on KBr disks in the 4000–400 cm–1 region. Thermal analyses (under N2 atmosphere, heating rate of 10 °C min–1) were performed on the Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus from room temperature to 800 °C. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were performed on the D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer with Cu–Kα radiation. CH4, N2, and CO2 sorption data were measured on a gas adsorption analyzer Autosorb-IQ2 (Quantachrome Instruments).

    Synthesis of {[Zn4(bpydb)3(datz)2(H2O)](DMF)4(EtOH)5(H2O)8}n (1)

    A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.060 g, 0.2 mmol), bpydbH2 (0.020 g, 0.05 mmol), Hdatz (0.010 g, 0.1 mmol), DMF (1.5 mL), EtOH (0.5 mL), and H2O (0.5 mL) was sealed in a 5 mL glass bottle at 70 °C for 72 h under autogenous pressure. After cooling of the mixture to room temperature, yellow block crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were obtained. The yield was 70% for 1 (based on bpydbH2). IR (KBr, cm–1): 3386 (br), 3187 (w), 1677(s), 1655(s), 1615 (s), 1384(s), 1256(m), 1097(m), 867 (m), 819 (s), 791 (s); elemental analysis calcd (%) for 1 (C98H126N20O30Zn4): C: 50.61; H: 5.46; N: 12.05; found: C 50.93, H 5.77, N 12.32.

    X-ray Crystallography

    The crystal data for 1 was obtained using an Oxford Supernova TM diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo–Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) with the ω-scan technique at 130 K. The structure was solved by direct method and refined with the full matrix least-squares technique embedded in SHELXL-97 program.(20) The non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and the hydrogen atoms of the ligand were placed to their geometrically ideal positions. For the highly disordered nature of the guest solvents, they could not be well modeled in the refinement, so they were removed by using the SQUEEZE option of PLATON.(21) Details for structural analysis are summarized in Table 1.

    Synthesis and Structure

    The solvothermal reaction of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, bpydbH2, and Hdatz in a 4:1:2 molar ratio in a mixed solvent of DMF–EtOH–H2O at 70 °C for 72 h gave pure yellow block crystalline products of 1 with high yield. The detailed structure of 1 was studied by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1̅ and shows a 2-fold interpenetrated 3D framework based on the twisty 63 layers and datz– pillars (Figures S1 and S2, Supporting Information). There are four Zn2+ ions, three deprotonated bpydb2–, two datz–, and one coordinated water molecule in one asymmetric unit. All the Zn2+ ions adopt similar tetrahedral coordinated modes (Figure 1a). Zn1 is connected with three N atoms from two different datz– and one bpydb2– ligands, and the remaining coordinating site was finished by one O atom from the bpydb2– ligand. Zn2 is bonded with two N atoms from two datz– ligands, two O atoms from one bpydb2– ligand, and one water molecule. Zn3 is associated with two O atoms from two bpydb2– ligands, two N atoms from one bpydb2–, and one datz– ligand. Zn4 possesses a similar coordinating surrounding as Zn3. The Zn–O bond distances are in the range of 1.882(3)–2.166(3) Å, and the Zn–N bond distances are in the range of 1.962(3)–2.034(3) Å. It is interesting to notice that a Zn-datz “paddle-wheel” cluster was found in 1, in which two Zn2+ ions are bridged by two datz– ligands. In the structure of 1, the bpydb2– ligand connects with three adjacent Zn2+ ions to give the 63 layer along the a axis (Figure S1), which are further pillared by datz– ligand along the b and c axes to furnish a pillar-layered 3D framework with large pores (Figure 1b, and Figure S2). Such a large porous structure may be not stable except by further interpenetration. In this case, a 2-fold interpenetrated network is formed by interpenetration of two identical frameworks (Figure 2).
    The pore size of the single framework is 21.8 × 11.9 Å2 running along the [100] direction, which reduces to 8.72 × 5.92 Å2 due to the 2-fold interpenetration, as revealed by software Poreblazer.(22, 23) To simplify the structure of 1, ligands, Zn3, Zn4, and Zn-datz clusters could be viewed as three, four, four, and five connected nodes, respectively. The whole framework could be simplified to a (3,3,3,4,4,5)-c 6-nodal net with a point symbol of {63}3{65.8}{66}{68.82}, which represents a new topological structure as calculated by TOPOS (Figure 3).(24) The solvent accessible void in 1 was 50% as calculated by software PLATON.(25) Along with the high solvent accessible void, the pore surface of 1 is decorated with free amino- and pyridine -N atoms pointing to the 1D channels (Figure 4, and Figure S3, Supporting Information).

    Powder X-ray Diffraction (PXRD) and Thermogravimetric Analyses (TGA)

    As shown in Figure S4, Supporting Information, the PXRD patterns of 1 measured at room temperature match the simulated ones from the single-crystal structure analysis well, indicative of the phase purity of the as-synthesized samples. The thermal stability of 1 was evaluated by TGA in N2 atmosphere from 25 to 800 °C (Figure S5, Supporting Information). A weight loss of 29.8% from 25 to 278 °C was observed for 1, which can be attributed to the loss of four DMF, five EtOH, and eight H2O molecules in the lattice (calcd 29.5%). Further heating led to the decomposition of whole framework.

    Gas Adsorption Studies

    As 1 possesses large solvent accessible voids as well as amino- and pyridine-decorated pores, we intend to study its application in CO2 capture and separation. The as-synthesized 1 was soaked in CH2Cl2 for 3 days to exchange the high-boiling point DMF. The CH2Cl2-exchanged sample was outgassed overnight under a vacuum at 70 °C to afford 1a (the activated 1). The absence of bands at 1677 and 1655 cm–1 on the desolvated samples indicated a complete removal of DMF molecules in the framework (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The PXRD patterns of 1a indicate that the framework of 1 changed significantly after removing guest solvent molecules, although it is not changed upon solvent-exchange. The CO2 sorption isotherm performed at 195 K showed an inconspicuous stepwise and hysteresis sorption behavior in the range of 0.20–0.25 bar, which could be evident from the curve of log(P/P0) vs uptake (Figure 5). After 0.25 bar, the uptake volume increased with the maximum uptake value of 136.8 cm3/g. The Langmuir surface areas calculated from 195 K CO2 adsorption isotherm is 583 m2/g, and the calculated Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area is 415 m2/g.
    The CO2, N2, and CH4 adsorption isotherms were measured around room temperature, which are completely reversible without hysteresis (Figure 6a). At 273 K and 1 bar, 1a shows good CO2 adsorption performance with a capacity of 80.9 cm3/g (3.6 mmol/g). This value is much higher than that of the similar 4,4′-bpy pillared 2 in our previous study (40 cm3/g, 1.8 mmol/g).(17) The enhanced CO2 uptake of 1a should due to its uncoordinated amino- and pyridine-N atoms decorated on the pores.(26) In contrast to CO2, the CH4 and N2 uptakes at 273 K are only 12 cm3/g and 3.7 cm3/g, respectively. At 298 K and 1 bar, 1a still holds a moderate CO2 uptake value of 50.2 cm3/g (2.24 mmol/g), while only limited sorption amount was observed for CH4 and N2 (5.4 cm3/g and 2.3 cm3/g). The CO2/N2 selectivities of 1a were calculated by Henry’s law to make a comparison with 2, which gave 85 and 54 at 273 and 298 K, respectively (Figure S7, Supporting Information). These values are much higher than that of 2 calculated under similar conditions (22.5 at 273 K and 34.7 at 298 K). To further investigate the gas separation ability of 1a, ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) was used to calculate the CO2/N2 (15:85 mol ratios) and CO2/CH4 (50:50 mol ratios) selectivity at room temperature. It has been shown that the IAST model can predict gas mixture adsorption accurately on many MOF materials.(27) As shown in Figure 6b, the selectivity of CO2/N2 displays an inconspicuous increasing trend from 54.4 to 55.8 in the range of 0–1 bar. It is interesting to notice that the selectivity of CO2/CH4 shows an increasing trend from 21 to 35 in the range of 0–1 bar, which is very rare in MOF chemistry.(28, 29) To the best of our knowledge, MOFs with such a high selectivity of CO2/CH4 at room temperature were rarely reported.(12, 27-32) Such high selectivity may be attributed to the different electrostatic interactions between adsorbate and porous surface. In the framework of 1a, both free NH2 groups and pyridine-N atoms locating in the channels are more attractive to CO2 than N2 and CH4 due to forming strong quadrupole–quadrupole interactions. To better understand the strength of interactions between the framework and CO2, the isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) were calculated via virial method by fitting the adsorption isotherms collected at 273 and 298 K (Figure S7). It is interesting to notice that the curve of Qst shows an increasing trend, with the value of 30.33 kJ/mol at zero loading, indicative of a strong CO2–framework interaction (Figure 7). The upward trend may be attributed to favorable guest–guest interactions between CO2 molecules trapped in the pores at high guest loading.(33)

    In summary, by employment of the mixed-ligand strategy, a new datz-pillared MOF with amino- and pyridine-functionalized pores was successfully synthesized and structurally characterized. Compared with the analogous 4,4′-bpy pillared MOF 2, the activated 1 showed enhanced CO2 sorption capacity and excellent CO2 sorption selectivity over N2 and CH4. We ascribe these favorable CO2 adsorption behaviors to the presence of amino- and pyridine-functionalized pores in 1. This work presents a good case for constructing MOFs with functional groups for CO2 capture and CH4 purification via mixed-ligand strategy.
