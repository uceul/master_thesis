A microporous lanthanum metal–organic framework as a bi-functional chemosensor for the detection of picric acid and Fe3+ ions†

Chuanqi Zhang a, Yan Yan a, Qinhe Pan b, Libo Sun a, Hongming He a, Yunling Liu a, Zhiqiang Liang *a and Jiyang Li *a
aState Key Lab of Inorganic Synthesis and Preparative Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, 130012, P. R. China. E-mail: liangzq@jlu.edu.cn; lijiyang@jlu.edu.cn
bDepartment of Materials and Chemical Engineering, Hainan University, Haikou, 570228, P. R. China

Received 17th March 2015 , Accepted 5th June 2015

First published on 9th June 2015

A microporous lanthanum metal–organic framework [La(TPT)(DMSO)2]·H2O (La-MOF (1)), has been synthesized using a rigid unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligand of p-terphenyl-3,4′′,5-tricarboxylic acid (H3TPT). The structure of 1 is constructed by bi-nuclear lanthanum clusters and fully deprotonated TPT3− ligands, which can be simplified into a 3,6-connected flu-3,6-C2/c topology with a point symbol of (44·6)2(4·62·87·102). The π-electron rich ligand H3TPT enables 1 to have blue luminescence when excited at 342 nm at ambient temperature. Meanwhile, 1 exhibits the selective detection of picric acid (PA) and Fe3+ ions in ethanol solution over other nitroaromatic compounds and metal ions. The high quenching efficiency and selectivity of 1 makes it a potential bi-functional chemosensor for both PA and Fe3+ ions.

Introduction

In the past two decades, the design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted great interest due to the variety of their fascinating structures and their potential applications in gas storage,1 separation,2 luminescence3 and catalysis.4 In addition, MOFs possessing luminescence and porosity present potential applications as chemical sensors.5 For example, luminescent MOFs are competent chemosensors for the detection of explosives due to their high sensitivity, quick response and recyclability.6 Luminescent MOFs, a promising candidate sensor, have also been used for the detection of ions, solvents and small molecules.7

Nitroaromatic compounds (NACs) are well-known explosives which are also widely used in the agrochemical industry. The serious situations of terrorist attacks and pollution to the environment have aroused people's concerns on the effect of NACs on the security and the environment. Therefore, the effective detection of NACs is very important to homeland security and environmental protection. On the other hand, Fe3+ ions, as one of the most important elements for humans and other living organisms, play a crucial role in biological and environmental systems.8 The selective detection of Fe3+ ions is important for living organisms because the deficiency and excess of Fe3+ ions can induce a number of physiological disorders.9 Developing new MOF materials that can directly detect both Fe3+ ions and NACs is of great significance.

In recent years, great efforts have been devoted to the synthesis of lanthanide MOFs (Ln-MOF) due to their excellent luminescence properties, as well as their potential applications in luminescence sensing. However, compared to the extensive study of transition metal MOFs, little success has been achieved in the synthesis of Ln-MOFs with desired structures and properties because lanthanide metals have larger coordination spheres and more flexible coordination modes.10 Chen and Qian reported several pioneering works based on Ln-MOF materials for the detection of various ions, small molecules and explosives.11 For example, a lanthanide Eu(PDC)1.5 with free Lewis basic pyridyl sites lined on the inner surface of the channels exhibited a selective sensing ability to Cu2+ ions,12 and a nanoscale luminescent Eu2(BDC)3(H2O)2·(H2O)2 showed highly sensitive sensing of nitroaromatic explosives.13 However, luminescent Ln-MOFs that can detect both Fe3+ ions and NACs are relatively rare.14

The selection of appropriate organic ligands is crucial to obtain effective Ln-MOF-based fluorescence sensors. Rigid aromatic polycarboxylates are one of the most important linkers and have been widely utilized to synthesize luminescent Ln-MOFs, not only due to the preference of the coordination between lanthanide ions and carboxylic groups but also because aromatic polycarboxylates themselves are excellent luminescent chromophores.15 In the past few years, we have focused on the design and synthesis of new luminescent MOFs and porous organic polymers for the detection of NACs or metal ions.16 More recently, we designed and synthesized a rigid unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligand, p-terphenyl-3,4′′,5-tricarboxylic acid (H3TPT), which has been used to construct a luminescent Cd-MOF with an alb-4,8-C2/c topology that shows a high quenching efficiency for the detection of picric acid.17 There are two advantages of using H3TPT as a ligand to synthesize Ln-MOFs. Firstly, the H3TPT, with an extended backbone, serves as a connective building block and can facilitate the formation of three-dimensional (3D) frameworks with a pore structure. Secondly, the π-electron rich ligand H3TPT is a good luminescence chromophore or “antenna” and may endow the resulting Ln-MOFs with interesting luminescent properties. As part of our continuing work on MOF-based chemical sensors, herein we present a new microporous and luminescent lanthanum MOF (La-MOF (1)) based on H3TPT ligands. La-MOF (1) possesses a non-interpenetrating 3D framework constructed by bi-nuclear [La2(COO)6] and fully deprotonated TPT3− linkers. It exhibits porous and luminescent properties, as well as a high quenching efficiency in the selective detection of picric acid and Fe3+ ions.

Experimental

Materials and methods

H3TPT was prepared according to our previous work.17 All metal salts, solvents and organic reagents were of reagent grade quality and used without further purification. The IR absorption spectra were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Nicolet 6700 FT-IR spectrometer with KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Rigaku D-Max 2550 diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 0.15418 nm) in a 2θ range of 4–40° at room temperature. The elemental analyses (C, H, S and La) were performed by using a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer and a Perkin Elmer OPTIMA 3300DV inductive coupled plasma emission spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer TGA-7 thermogravimetric analyzer from room temperature to 800 °C in an air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. N2 adsorption isotherms at 77 K and CO2 adsorption isotherms at 273 K and 298 K were measured with a Micrometrics ASAP 2020 instrument. The fluorescence spectra were recorded on a SHIMADZU RF-5301 PC fluorescence spectrophotometer. The luminescence lifetime measurements were performed on an Edinburgh FLS920 phosphorimeter. The point symbol and topological analysis were conducted by using TOPOS program package.18

Crystal structure determination

The single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements were performed on a Bruker AXS SMART APEX-II diffractometer for 1 with graphite monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at 293 K. Data processing was completed with the SAINT processing program.19 The structure of 1 was solved by the direct method and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares with the SHELX-97 program.20 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters except for the terminal coordinated DMSO molecules and guest solvent molecules. Some restraints, like dfix, simu, delu and sadi were added to the terminally coordinated DMSO molecules for a better geometry configuration. Hydrogen atoms on the aromatic rings were placed geometrically with isotropic thermal parameters 1.2 times of those of the attached carbon atoms. The SQUEEZE21 model was used to remove the guest solvent molecules in the void spaces because they are highly disordered and cannot be precisely located using X-ray crystallography. The guest solvent molecules were further determined by elemental analysis and TG analysis. A summary of the crystallographic data and refinement parameters are given in Table S1.† The selected bond lengths and angles of 1 are given in Table S2.†

Synthesis of [La(TPT)(DMSO)2]·H2O (La-MOF (1))

La(NO3)2·6H2O (17.32 mg, 0.04 mmol) and H3TPT (4.52 mg, 0.0125 mmol) were dissolved in a mixture of DMSO (2.4 mL) and H2O (1.6 mL). The solution was sealed in a 20 mL vial and heated at 85 °C for 24 hours. When it was cooled to room temperature, the lozenge-shaped crystals were collected by filtration, and then washed with DMSO and H2O. CHSLa elemental analysis (%) for 1: calcd for C25H25S2LaO9: C 44.64, H 3.74, S 9.53, and La 20.6; found: C 43.63, H 3.87, S 10.42, and La 20.0. IR (cm−1): 1637(s), 1584(s), 1529(m), 1450(w), 1414(s), 1359(s), 1016(s), 945(w), 846(w), 778(s), 706(w), 656(w), and 505(w).

Detection experiments for metal ions and nitroaromatic compounds

To prepare the 1-enthanol suspension, 0.5 mg of 1 powder was well dispersed in 2.0 mL ethanol using ultrasonic agitation for 30 minutes. Different NACs or metal ions were added into a quartz cuvette containing 2 mL 1–ethanol suspension for the luminescence detection.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

1 crystallizes in a monoclinic space group C2/c. Its asymmetric unit contains one La(III) ion, one TPT ligand, two coordinated DMSO molecules and one free water molecule. The La center binds to nine oxygen atoms, seven of which are from five different carboxylate groups of the TPT linkers, and the other two are from the coordinated DMSO molecules. The La–O distances vary from 2.48 to 2.77 Å, which are close to the previously reported data.22 In the structure of 1, three carboxylic groups of one TPT linker adopt three distinct coordination modes to link the La ions (Fig. 1a). Four carboxylate groups from isophthalic moieties connect two La ions at equatorial positions, and two carboxylate groups from the benzoic acid moieties occupy the axial positions to link the same two La ions, forming a distorted [La2(COO)6] cluster (Fig. 1b). The adjacent six-connected clusters are linked together through three-connected TPT ligands to construct a 3D neutral framework with interconnected 3D channels along the [−101] and [110] directions with dimensions (taking the VDW radii into consideration) of ca. 9.7 × 9.7 Å (Fig. S2, ESI†), and the [001] direction with dimensions of ca. 16.7 × 8.9 Å (Fig. 1c). These channels are partially blocked by coordinated solvent molecules.
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination modes of carboxyl groups of the TPT ligand. (b) The distorted binuclear [La2(COO)6] motif. (c) The 3D framework of 1 showing the channels along the [001] direction. The coordinated DMSO molecules are simplified to be coordinated oxygen and free water molecules are omitted for clarity. (Colours: La green, O red, and C gray).

Using a topological approach can help us to better understand the 3D framework of 1. Each bi-nuclear La2(COO)6 cluster connected by six carboxylate groups can be viewed as a 6-connected node, and each TPT ligand acts as a 3-connected node. The cross link of these two kinds of nodes results in the 3D (3,6)-connected framework (Fig. 2c). Topological analysis suggests that 1 possesses a flu-3,6-C2/c topology with point symbol of (44·6)2(44·62·87·102). Such a topology has also been reported in some Ln-MOFs constructed by other unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligands, such as biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylate, 5-((4-carboxyphenyl)ethynyl)isophthalic acid and 3,4′,5-azobenzenetricarboxylic acid.23


Fig. 2 (a) and (b) Ball-and-stick and schematic representations of 3-connected (gray) and 6-connected nodes (green), respectively (the coordinated DMSO molecules are simplified to be coordinated oxygen for clarity). (c) Schematic representation of the 3,6-connected flu-3,6-C2/c topology of 1.

PXRD, IR and TG analyses

The PXRD study indicates a pure phase of 1 (Fig. S3, ESI†). IR spectra are used to characterize the functional groups of 1. The absence of an absorption around 1700 cm−1 confirms that the H3TPT ligands are completely deprotonated (Fig. S4, ESI†). As shown in Fig. S5,† the TGA curve of 1 shows three weight losses. The first weight loss of 2.95% from 25 to 100 °C is due to the removal of the guest H2O molecules (calcd 2.84%). The second weight loss of 25.86% from 150 to 400 °C corresponds to the loss of the two coordinated DMSO molecules (calcd 24.64%). Then, there is a plateau between 400 and 550 °C. The sample starts to decompose at 550 °C and ends at 750 °C with a weight loss of 75.95% to form La2O3 (calcd 25.73%). In addition, to investigate the stability of 1, it was heated at different temperatures for 6 hours, then the PXRD patterns of these samples were collected. As shown in Fig. S3,† the framework started to collapse with the removal of the coordinated DMSO above 150 °C.

Gas adsorption properties

The gas adsorption properties of 1 are studied to examine the pore characteristics. The samples are treated at 100 °C for 10 h under vacuum before the adsorption experiments. The N2 adsorption of 1 at 77 K shows a type I isotherm of a microporous material, and the calculated Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area is 115 m2 g−1 (Fig. S6, ESI†). The CO2 adsorption isotherms of 1 show that it can adsorb a certain amount of CO2, e.g., 44.2 cm3 g−1 and 30.5 cm3 g−1 under 0.95 bar at 273 and 298 K, respectively. Notably, no gas adsorption capacity is reported in the previously reported Ln-MOFs with a flu-3,6-C2/c topology constructed by unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligands.23 The gas adsorption properties of 1 indicate that the extended backbone of H3TPT ligands can facilitate the formation of 3D porous frameworks.

Luminescence properties and detection of Fe3+ ions

As expected, 1 exhibits luminescence properties. As shown in Fig. S7 (ESI†), 1 shows an emission at 374 nm upon excitation at 342 nm, which can be assigned to the luminescence from the intra-ligand emission, although an obvious blue-shift is observed relative to the emission band of the H3TPT ligand (about 71 nm).24 In addition, the emission of the ethanol suspension of 1 is located at 368 nm, which is similar to its solid state emission. The quantum yield increases from 5.66% for H3TPT to 15.91% for 1 and it shows a relatively short lifetime with τ1 = 0.2473 ns and τ2 = 1.3603 ns for 1 (Fig. S12, ESI†).

The bright-blue luminescence of 1 motivated us to investigate its potential application in the detection of common metal ions. In order to study the selectivity of the sensing ability for various metal ions, a fine powder of 1 is immersed in an ethanol solution containing different nitrate salts of Fe3+, Pb2+, Cd2+, Cr3+, Zn2+, Sr2+, Cu2+, In3+, Ni2+, Co2+, Ba2+, Li+ and Mg2+, as well as FeCl2, with concentrations of 95.2 μM. Interestingly, a significant luminescence attenuation with a QP (quench percentage) of 56% is observed in the presence of Fe3+ ions (Fig. 3). However, the QP of 1 for other metal ions is much lower (less than 7.7%). These results indicate that 1 possesses a highly selective sensing ability to Fe3+ ions. It is well known that the sensing ability and selectivity of chemosensors are susceptible to the interference of other metal ions. The influences of coexisting metal ions on the quenched fluorescence intensity of 1–ethanol suspensions by Fe3+ ions have also been investigated. As shown in Fig. S8,† the luminescence intensities display slight changes in the presence of other coexisting metal ions with the same amount of Fe3+ ions, which further confirms the high selectivity of 1 for detecting Fe3+ ions.


Fig. 3 Reduction of the luminescence intensity (plotted as the quench percentage) of a 1–ethanol suspension in the presence of different metal ions with a concentration of 95.2 μM (λex = 329 nm).

For better understanding of the sensing ability of 1 for Fe3+ ions, the luminescence titration of the 1–ethanol suspension has been studied with the gradual addition of a Fe(NO3)3–ethanol solution with a concentration of 2.0 × 10−3 M. As shown in Fig. 4a, the luminescence intensity of the 1–ethanol suspension decreases gradually with the increasing concentration of Fe3+ ions. Quantitatively, the quenching effect can be rationalized for the low concentrations by the Stern–Volmer equation: Io/I = 1 + Ksv × [M], where Io and I are the luminescence intensities of 1–ethanol suspensions before and after the addition of Fe3+ ions, respectively; Ksv is the quenching constant, and [M] is the molar concentration of Fe3+ ions. As demonstrated in the Stern–Volmer plot of 1 (Fig. 4b), there is a non-linear and upward curvature at higher concentrations of Fe3+ ions, suggesting that the mechanism of the luminescence quenching could be attributed to the combination of dynamic and static quenching.6a,14 As shown in Fig. 4b, the Ksv is 1.36 × 104 M−1 at low concentrations of Fe3+ ions, which is comparable to those of previously reported MOF-based sensors, such as the Eu3+ incorporated MIL-53-COOH (Al) (5.12 × 103 M−1),25 [Ln(cptpy)3]n (4.1 × 103 M−1)9a,26 and Eu-MOF-LIC-1 (2.87 × 104 M−1).27 The static quenching mechanism may be attributed to the formation of a ground-state non-fluorescent complex between the Fe3+ ions and terphenyl moieties, and the dynamic quenching mechanism may be governed by the diffusive collisions between the electron-deficient Fe3+ ions and the terphenyl moieties of the TPT ligands.14a,28


Fig. 4 (a) Luminescence quenching of 1 dispersed in ethanol by the gradual addition of an Fe3+–ethanol solution. The concentration of 1 is fixed at 4.0 × 10−4 M and the excitation wavelength is 329 nm. (b) Stern–Volmer plot of I0/I versus the concentration of Fe3+ ions in the 1–ethanol suspension (insert: enlarged view of a selected area).

Detection of nitroaromatic compounds

The luminescence responses of 1 to various NACs have been investigated by the addition of 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2,4-dinitrotoluene (DNT), 4-nitrotoluene (4-NT), 2-nitrotoluene (2-NT), 4-nitrobenzaldehyde (4-NBA), 4-chloronitrobenzene (4-Cl-NB), 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP) and picric acid (PA) to the 1–ethanol suspension (Fig. S9, ESI†). The addition of 60 μL of the analytes ([analytes] = 29.1 μM) gives rise to different degrees of fluorescence quenching (Fig. 5). Although all of the nine NACs can decrease the emission of 1, PA was the most effective quencher and can effectively quench the emission of 1 with a QP of 75.2%, which is three times higher than that of the second best quencher (4-nitrophenol with a QP of 23.1%). Other than PA and 4-nitrophenol, the QPs of 2,4-DNT and TNT are less than 5%. This result indicates that 1 has a highly selective response to PA.
Fig. 5 QP of luminescence of the 1–ethanol suspension in the presence of different NACs with a concentration of 29.1 μM (λex = 329 nm).

The sensing properties of 1 for PA were also investigated by recording the emissive spectra of the 1–ethanol suspension with the gradual addition of PA. As shown in Fig. 6a, the luminescence intensity decreases clearly with the increasing PA concentration. When the concentration of PA in the 1–ethanol suspension reaches 47.6 μM, the fluorescence is almost completely quenched with a QP of 90.3%. The luminescence titration results can also be rationalized by the Stern–Volmer equation. As shown in Fig. 6b, there is a non-linear and upward curve for PA at higher concentrations, indicating that the quenching mechanism can be explained by a combination of the dynamic and static mechanisms.14 The quenching constant (Ksv) of 1 for PA at low concentration is found to be 9.89 × 104 M−1, which is higher than our previously reported, [Cd3(TPT)2(DMF)2]·(H2O)0.5] (6.56 × 104 M−1),17 and other known MOF-based sensors such as [Cd(NDC)0.5(PCA)] (3.5 × 104 M−1),29 [Tb(1,3,5-BTC)] (3.42 × 104 M−1)30 and UiO-67-dcppy (2.9 × 104 M−1).6a Such a value is lower than the fluoranthene-based fluorescence chemosensors (1.24 × 105 M−1)31 and AIE-functionalized mesoporous materials (2.5 × 105 M−1).32 The dynamic quenching could be attributed to the collision encounters between the terphenyl moieties and PA, while the static one may be due to the hydrogen-bonding interactions between the hydroxyl groups of PA and the carboxylate groups of the TPT ligands.14a In addition, the absorption spectra of the nitroaromatic compounds were compared with the emissions of 1 (Fig. S10, ESI†), the overlap of PA toward 1 indicates that an energy transfer process might exist between them.6a,29


Fig. 6 (a) Luminescence quenching of 1 dispersed in ethanol with the gradual addition of a 1 mM PA–ethanol solution. (b) Stern–Volmer plot of I0/I versus PA concentration in the 1–ethanol suspension (insert: enlarged view of a selected area).

Conclusions

In conclusion, a microporous luminescent La-MOF (1) ([La(TPT)(DMSO)2]·H2O) has been synthesized by using the π-electron rich ligand of H3TPT. It possesses the flu-3,6-C2/c topological structure with intersecting 3D channels, which is constructed by bi-nuclear lanthanum clusters and TPT linkers. Such a compound presents a moderate adsorption capacity of CO2 at ambient temperature. Moreover, the La-MOF exhibits a high efficiency for the detection of both PA and Fe3+ ions with Ksv values of 9.89 × 104 M−1 and 1.36 × 104 M−1, respectively. It shows high selectivity for PA with the quenching efficiency being three times higher than those of 4-NP and other NACs. The sensing effect of 1 for Fe3+ ions is not disturbed by the presence of other coexisting metal ions. The mechanism of the luminescence quenching of PA and Fe3+ ions was attributed to the simultaneous dynamic and static mechanisms. The high quenching efficiency and excellent selectivity of 1 for both PA and Fe3+ ions make it a potential bi-functional material for the detection of explosives and metal ions.
