Two photoluminescent metal–organic frameworks based on a BODIPY-derived bipyridine ligand†

Lian Zhou ab, Yun-Shan Xue a, Yan Xu c, Jun Zhang d and Hong-Bin Du *a
aState Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing University, Nanjing, 210093, China. E-mail: hbdu@nju.edu.cn; Fax: +86-25-8331 4502
bSchool of Chemical Engineering, Qinghai University, Xining, Qinghai 810006, China
cDepartment of Chemistry, Nanjing University of Technology, Nanjing 210009, China
dSchool of Materials and Chemical Engineering, Anhui University of Architecture, Hefei, 230601, China

Received 3rd June 2013 , Accepted 10th July 2013

First published on 11th July 2013

Two new metal–organic open frameworks (MOFs) [Zn3L2(btc)2(H2O)]·(guests) (1), [CdL(bdc)]·(guests) (2), were successfully prepared by using a photoluminescent BODIPY-based bipyridine linker, L, and auxiliary ligands 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (H2bdc) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate (H3btc), respectively. MOF 1 has a 2-fold interpenetrating three-dimensional (3D) framework with an 8-connecting body centered cubic (bcu) net, built upon a paddlewheel cluster [Zn2(O2CR)4], a tetrahedrally connecting Zn and a 3-connected btc3− ligand. MOF 2 has a highly open framework with a kagome-type net. It is a pillared layered structure made of 2D [Cd(bdc)] kagome sheets and L as pillars. MOF 1 was shown to exhibit permanent porosity and adsorption of N2, H2, CO2 and CH4. Both MOFs exhibited strong photoluminescent emissions at room temperature with peaks at 540 nm upon excitation, owing to a ligand L-based excited state.

Introduction

Multifunctional metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) that possess porosity and photoluminescence,1 magnetism,2 conductivity,3etc., have recently drawn great attention because of their potential applications in light-harvesters, chemical sensors and devices.4–6 To construct such materials, one often uses judiciously chosen multidentate ligands of a certain coordination geometry to coordinate certain metal ions. Up to now, a vast number of organic ligands with specified geometry and functionality have been prepared and used in the rational synthesis of porous coordination polymers with specific structures and properties.

To construct photoluminescent MOFs, both the metal and the linker are often used to create luminescence. The latter includes abundant π-conjugated organic compounds, such as di- or multi-carboxylate and pyridine-containing species.1 Some known organic lumophores, e.g. pyrene, have also been incorporated in these ligands, which has provided numerous favorable properties.1,7 Boron dipyrromethenes (BODIPYs) are well known lumophores with narrow and intense absorption and fluorescence bands, high fluorescence quantum yields, negligible triplet-state formation and high stability under physiological conditions, which make them suitable for a wide range of applications as labeling reagents, OLEDs, fluorescent switches, chemosensors etc.8,9 A pyridine-functionalized BODIPY ligand (Scheme 1, R = ph) has been employed recently to construct a MOF material with cooperative light-harvesting properties, in which the BODIPY struts serve as antenna-type light harvesters for the excitation of porphyrinic struts.10 We recently explored the synthesis of MOFs by the co-assembly of a new pyridine-functionalized BODIPY ligand, L (Scheme 1, R = CH3), and secondary carboxylate ligands 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (H2bdc) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate (H3btc), and obtained two MOFs, namely, [Zn3L2(btc)2(H2O)]·(guests) (1), [CdL(bdc)]·(guests) (2). We herein report their synthesis, structure, thermal stability, gas adsorption and photoluminescent properties.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All the solvents and starting materials for the synthesis were purchased commercially and were used as received. Ligand L was prepared according to the literature.10,11

Elemental analyses of C, H, N were performed on an Elementar Vario MICRO Elemental Analyzer. Fourier transformed Infrared (FT-IR) spectra were obtained on a Bruker Vector 22 FT-IR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer thermal analyzer under nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected in the 2θ = 5–50° range with a scan speed of 0.1 s per deg on a Bruker D8 Advance instrument using Cu–Kα radiation at room temperature. Photoluminescence spectra were performed on a Jobin Yvon FluroMax-4 fluorescence spectrophotometer for the solid samples and a Hitachi F-4600 spectrophotometer for the liquid. A Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area porosimetry system was used to measure the gas adsorption. In order to remove guest molecules in the framework, the freshly prepared sample was immersed in CH2Cl2 for 48 h with fresh CH2Cl2 added every 8 h. After the solvent exchange, the sorption measurements for N2 were carried out at 77 K with liquid nitrogen, while those for CH4 and CO2 sorption were performed at 273 K with an ice–water bath.

Synthesis

Synthesis of [Zn3L2(btc)2(H2O)]·(guests) (1). MOF 1 was readily synthesized by a solvothermal reaction: a mixture of L (12 mg, 0.029 mmol), H3btc (8 mg, 0.038 mmol), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (16 mg, 0.054 mmol), DMF (2 mL) and an aliquot of HNO3 in EtOH (1 mL of a 0.03 M solution) was sealed in the autoclave at 80 °C for 2 days and then cooled to room temperature. Black rod crystals of 1 were collected in a ca. 10% yield based on Zn2+. The phase purity was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S1 and S2 in the ESI†). The formula was estimated as [Zn3L2(btc)2(H2O)]·3.5DMF·4H2O based on the single crystal structure analysis and IR, elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

Suitable crystals of MOFs 1–2 were selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The data collections were carried out on a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD diffractometer at 296 K using graphite-monochromated Mo–Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reductions and absorption corrections were performed using the SAINT and SADABS programs,12 respectively. The structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS-97 program and refined with full-matrix least squares on F2 using the SHELXL-97 program.13 All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms were generated theoretically onto specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. In both MOFs 1 and 2, the atoms of the ligand L are disordered, which were split into two sites. Therefore, some C–C bond distances and thermal parameters of the disordered atoms were restrained. The hydrogen atoms on the highly disordered ligand L and coordinated water in 1 were not located. In addition, because of the highly disordered guest molecules in 1 and 2, the PLATON/SQUEEZE route was employed to calculate the diffraction contribution from the guest molecules and thereby, to produce a set of guest-free diffraction intensities.14 The final formula were derived from the crystallographic data, elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. Results and discussion

Structure description

Crystal structure of [Zn3L2(btc)2(H2O)]·(guests) (1). MOF 1 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I4/m with four asymmetric units in one cell. There are two crystallographically independent Zn atoms (a quarter-occupied Zn1 and a half-occupied Zn2), one half of an L ligand and a half of H3btc. As shown in Fig. 1, each Zn1 atom coordinates to four carboxylate groups from four different btc3− ligands and a water molecule in a square-pyramidal geometry. The two adjacent Zn1 atoms are linked together by four carboxylate groups to form a paddlewheel dinuclear cluster [Zn2(O2CR)4]. Zn2 is in a tetrahedral coordination surrounded by two N atoms from two L ligands and two O atoms from two carboxylate groups from two different btc3− ligands. The Zn–O, Zn–N, Zn–Ow and Zn⋯Zn distances are 1.939(2)–2.025(2), 2.026(5)–2.027(5), 1.938(5) and 2.9465(9) Å, respectively, which are consistent with those found in other [Zn2(O2CR)4] paddlewheel-based MOFs.15 The btc3− ligand adopts two coordination modes: one carboxylate group adopts a bismonodentate coordination mode to bridge the Zn1 and Zn1A centers in a paddlewheel unit, while the other two carboxylate groups coordinate to two Zn2 centers in a monodentate coordination mode.
Fig. 1 The coordination environments of the metals and ligands in 1. The H atoms and solvated molecules are omitted for clarity.

A better understanding of the intricate 3D framework of 1 can be achieved by the application of a topological analysis,16 reducing the multidimensional structure to simple nodes and connection nets. As shown in Fig. 2, each paddlewheel dinuclear cluster, [Zn2(O2CR)4], connects four btc3− ligands to form an 8-connecting cuboid strut, [Zn2(btc)4], while four Zn2 centers are linked by four linear L ligands to form a planar square unit, Zn4L4. The square units are aligned parallel with each other and are each connected by eight cuboid [Zn2(btc)4] struts to form a 3D framework with large square 1D pore channels of dimensions 19.0 × 19.0 Å2 (Zn2⋯Zn2 distances) along the c-axis direction. This gives rise to a uninodal 8-connected body centered cubic (bcu) net, in which the cuboid strut and square unit have the same point group of {424.64} (Fig. S5 in the ESI†). The structure is two-fold interpenetrated, which eliminates the large voids, leaving entrapped cuboid free spaces with triangular openings of 9.0 × 8.0 × 9.0 Å3 (Fig. 2b). The solvent-accessible space was estimated using the PLATON14 program to be 24.5% of the unit cell volume (not taking account of the disordered hydrogen). Inside the voids reside the highly disordered guest molecules. From another point of view, each Zn2 center can be considered as a tetrahedral node, each L ligand acts as a linear linker and [Zn2(btc)4] acts as an 8-connected cuboid node. Thus, MOF 1 can be reduced into a doubly-interpenetrating 2-nodal (4,8)-connected sqc928 net17 with a point (Schläfli) symbol of {412.612.84}{42.64}4 (Fig. S6 in the ESI†).


Fig. 2 Views of (a) the single and (b) 2-fold interpenetrating structure of 1.
Crystal structure of [CdL(bdc)]·(guests) (2). MOF 2 crystallizes in the trigonal crystal system of P6/m. As shown in Fig. 3, the asymmetric unit contains half a Cd(II) cation, one bdc ligand and half of an L ligand. The metal Cd cation displays a pentagonal bipyramid coordination geometry, coordinating to five O atoms from three carboxylate groups at the basal plane and two N atoms from two L ligands in the axial directions. The Cd–O distances range from 2.291(4) to 2.457(4) Å and the Cd–N distance is 2.318(4) Å, which are all similar to those found in other Cd(II) compounds.18 Two neighbouring Cd(II) ions are bridged by two carboxylate O atoms, forming a four-membered [Cd2O2] unit. The Cd–Cd distance across the binuclear unit is 3.9838(7) Å. The bdc ligand is fully deprotonated and connects to three Cd(II) ions from two [Cd2O2] units. Of the three Cd ions, one is monodentately coordinated via a μ3-O atom, while the other two are chelated by two O atoms from the carboxylate group. The L ligand acts as a linear bidentate linker, connecting two neighbouring [Cd2O2] units.
Fig. 3 The coordination environments of the metals and ligands in 2. The H atoms and solvated molecules are omitted for clarity.

MOF 2 possesses a 3D framework with a pillared layered structure. As shown in Fig. 4, each binuclear [Cd2O2] unit connects to four neighbouring ones via bdc bridges to form a 2D sheet, in which all the atoms lie in a mirror plane. This gives rise to a kagome plane consisting of three- and six-membered rings. The kagome planes stack in an AAA fashion along the c-axis, separated by about 19.894(1) Å. The adjacent sheets are linked by linear bidentate L ligands via [Cd2O2] units to form a 3D open framework. From the topological viewpoint, the framework of 2 can be viewed as a known kagome net with a point symbol of {32.48.65}, in which the [Cd2O2] binuclear unit can be regarded as a 6-connected node and the ligands L and bdc both act as linkers (Fig. S7 in the ESI†). Open channels exist along the c-axis with opening sizes of approximately 13.7 × 14.4 Å2 (taking account of the van der Waals radii). Inside the channels reside disordered guest molecules. PLATON14 calculations show that the guest accessible void (7260.1 Å3 per unit cell) comprises 65.3% of the unit cell volume.


Fig. 4 (a) View of the 3D kagome structure of 2, showing large apertures along the c-axis. (b) Projection of the 3D structure of 2 along the b-axis showing the 2D sheets are pillared by L ligands. The H atoms and solvated molecules are omitted for clarity.

Thermal properties

To investigate the thermal stabilities of compounds 1 and 2, the TG analyses were carried out (Fig. S8 in the ESI†). Both compounds display similar thermal decomposition behaviours. There were small amounts of weight losses of ca. 2.7% for both compounds before 120 °C, followed by slow releases of guest molecules (13.7% for 1 and 12.6% for 2). The abrupt weight losses (25.3% for 1 and 28.7% for 2) starting at ca. 350 °C could be attributed to the evaporation of parts of the ligands in both compounds owing to the decomposition of the structures. Further decompositions occurred above 600 °C.

The X-ray structure analyses showed that compounds 1 and 2 consist of solvent accessible voids, inside which lie disordered guest molecules. To check if the guest molecules can be removed while the structure is still intact, the as-synthesized samples were immersed in CH2Cl2 or acetone for 36 h with the solvent refreshed every 12 h. After the solvent exchange, the samples were then degassed at 100 °C under a dynamic vacuum for 24 h. The thermal stabilities of compounds 1 and 2 after activation were checked by X-ray powder diffraction. The main XRD peaks of activated 1 matched those of the as-synthesized and the simulated ones on the basis of the single crystal structure. The results suggest that the framework of activated 1 remained after activation, although its crystallinity has deteriorated, as evidenced by the broadening of some peaks (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). However, the framework of activated 2 had almost converted into an amorphous phase (Fig. S3 in the ESI†).

Adsorption properties

Gas adsorption measurements were carried out over the two activated compounds. The results show that only MOF 1 exhibited permanent porosity, while 2 showed very little gas adsorption with a Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 31 m2 g−1, which are consistent with the thermal stability studies. As shown in Fig. 5, N2 adsorption isotherms at 77 K for 1 display a type-II behavior, with noticeable hysteresis between the adsorption and desorption isotherms. This is probably due to the retaining of N2 in the flexible, small channels of MOF 1 with pore opening comparable to the size of the N2 molecule. The BET and the Langmuir surface areas were calculated from the N2 adsorption isotherm to be 239 and 320 m2 g−1, respectively. The density functional theory (DFT) calculations of the adsorption isotherm indicate a pore diameter of 7.0 Å, which is consistent with the crystal structure analysis. The H2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K over activated 1 is of type-I (Fig. S9 in the ESI†), indicating a strong affinity of H2 molecules toward the pore surfaces. At 77 K and 760 Torr, the H2 uptake capacity reaches 50 cm3 g−1 (0.44 wt%), which is rather small in comparison with other high H2 adsorbent MOFs.19 There is remarkable hysteresis between the H2 adsorption and desorption isotherms. Such hysteresis is often observed in porous MOFs owing to the retaining of H2 in the flexible, small channels of MOFs.19
Fig. 5 Gas sorption isotherms of MOF 1 for N2 at 77 K. The inset is the pore size distribution based on the DFT theory.

Recently, the gas storage and separation of CO2 and CH4 over MOFs have been of great interest.20,21 CO2 and CH4 adsorption measurements over activated MOF 1 have been carried out at 273 K. As shown in Fig. 6, MOF 1 takes up small amounts of carbon dioxide (46 cm3 g−1, 2.11 mmol g−1), which are much higher than that of methane (16.1 cm3 g−1, 0.70 mmol g−1) at 273 K and 760 Torr. The selective adsorption of CO2 over CH4 could be attributed to the smaller kinetic diameter and larger quadrupole moment of CO2 than of CH4. In comparison with other highly selective MOF adsorbents,20,21 however, the adsorption capacity and separation selectivity of MOF 1 are rather low.


Fig. 6 Gas adsorption (filled symbols) and desorption (empty symbols) isotherms of MOF 1 for (a) CO2 and (b) CH4 at 273 K.

Photoluminescent properties

The room temperature photoluminescent spectra of the two MOFs together with those of the L, H2bdc and H3btc ligands in the solid state are shown in Fig. 7. The photoluminescent spectra of both the free H2bdc and H3btc ligands showed broad peaks centered at 408 nm upon excitation at 350 nm, which can be attributed to π* → π transitions.22 Ligand L, on the other hand, displayed broad orange emissions at 618 nm, with a shoulder at 660 nm in the solid state. These emission could be due to an intermolecular charge transfer state in the solid L because L in solution emitted at ca. 542 nm upon excitation (λex = 485 nm). Both solid MOFs 1 and 2 exhibited similar emission spectra upon excitation at 512 nm. They emitted strong green light centered at 540 nm, similar to ligand L in solution. Therefore, the emissions of MOFs 1 and 2 can be attributed to linker L-based luminescence, which is explained by the S1 → S0 transitions of the BODIPY group.8,22
Fig. 7 Room temperature photoluminescent spectra of (a) H2bdc (λex = 350 nm), (b) H3btc (λex = 350 nm), (c) L (λex = 557 nm), (d) MOF 1 (λex = 512 nm), (e) MOF 2 (λex = 512 nm) in the solid state, and (f) L (λex = 485 nm) in CH2Cl2 solution.

Conclusions

Two new metal–organic open-frameworks were solvothermally synthesized by using a photoluminescent BODIPY-based bipyridine linker, L, and auxiliary ligands 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (H2bdc) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate (H3btc), respectively. MOF 1 has a 2-fold interpenetrating three-dimensional framework with a body centered cubic (bcu) network, while 2 has a highly open framework with a kagome-type net. MOF 1 exhibited permanent porosity, while 2 was not stable after the removal of guest solvent molecules. Both MOFs 1 and 2 exhibited strong fluorescent emissions upon excitation at room temperature. The results show that the use of a functional ligand, e.g. organic lumophores, is a convenient method for the construction of new coordination polymers with diverse structures and interesting properties.
