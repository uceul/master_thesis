A new strategy to obtain tetranuclear cobalt(II) metal–organic frameworks based on the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] cluster: synthesis, structures and properties†

Ke Li , Xiu-Xiu Lv , Lu-Lu Shi , Lu Liu , Bao-Long Li * and Bing Wu
State and Local Joint Engineering Laboratory for Functional Polymeric Materials, College of Chemistry, Chemical Engineering and Materials Science, Soochow University, Suzhou 215123, P. R China. E-mail: libaolong@suda.edu.cn

Received 21st July 2016 , Accepted 16th August 2016

First published on 16th August 2016

Three metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) {[Co4(OH)2(adc)6(H2O)5][Co2(OH)(btrb)]2·8H2O}n (1), {[Co4(OH)2(btrb)(nip)3(H2O)3]·4H2O}n (2·4H2O) and {[Co4(OH)2(btrb)(btc)2(H2O)2]·9H2O}n (3·9H2O) (btrb = 1,4-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-ylmethyl)benzene, H2adc = 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid, H2nip = 5-nitroisophthalic acid and H3btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid) were synthesized by a hydrothermal method. 1 shows an unusual (3,4)-connected 2D network with a point symbol of (4·62)2(42·62·82) based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] and [Co2(μ3-OH)] units. 2 is a 6-connected pcu topology based on the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit. 3 is a rare (3,8)-connected 3D framework with a rare point symbol of (4·5·6)2(42·56·616·72·82) based on the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit. The magnetic measurements reveal that 1–3 exhibit antiferromagnetic interactions between the adjacent cobalt(II) centers. 1–3 are good photocatalysts for the degradation of methylene blue (MB). The 1,2-position nitrogen atoms of the 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligand act as strong σ-donators to form a quite stable coordination quadrangle with two metal ions. The btrb ligand containing two 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole rings is favorable to form tetranuclear metal units. By the regulation of the multicarboxylate ligands, the connection number of [Co4(μ3-OH)2] clusters increases from 4 in 1 to 6 in 2 and then 8 in 3. This work presents a novel but effective method to design and regulate the connection number (4, 6 and 8 connected in this work) of MOFs with 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole and rigid multicarboxylate ligands.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), metal-cluster based metal–organic frameworks (MCOFs) or coordination polymers (CPs) constructed from multinuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) have been developed rapidly in the past few decades and various properties and applications of these materials have been reported.1,2 They break through the geometrical and coordination limits of classical mononuclear metal organic frameworks (MOFs), and usually have some unprecedented properties.3 For example, Xu and co-workers reported a novel 2D network CP based on nano-sized Ho48 clusters, and provided a useful method to obtain high-nuclearity Ln–O clusters.3a Murugesu and co-workers constructed an octanuclear cobalt(II) cluster based MCOF with a 12-connected framework.3b Li and co-workers reported a Fe3(μ3-O) based MOF with good photocatalytic selectivity in the hydroxylation of benzene to produce phenol.3c

Tetranuclear hydroxyl-metal cluster based MOFs have been extensively studied. To our knowledge, tetranuclear hydroxyl metal clusters with a transition-metal of the 4th period have five kinds of basic structures: linear, parallelogram, triangle, tetrahedron and quadrangle.4 Tetranuclear cobalt and copper(II) based MOFs always show interesting magnetic properties on account of the magnetic transmissibility of the bridging hydroxyl, coupled with some other novel characteristics.5

In previous work, we synthesized many CPs or MOFs using bis(imidazole) and bis(triazole) ligands.6 For example, {[Cu5(bime)(μ3-OH)2(phth)4](H2O)2}n shows ferromagnetic interactions (H2phth = phthalic acid, bime = 1,2-bis(imidazol-1-yl)ethane).6b {[Cu9(OH)6(bte)2(sip)4(H2O)3]·6H2O}n has antiferromagnetic coupling interactions and good photocatalytic activity for degrading methyl orange (bte = 1,2-bis(1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)ethane, sip = 5-sulfoisophthalate).6e

In this context, we focus on the hydrothermal synthesis of tetranuclear cobalt(II) based MOFs with flexible 4-substituted bis(1,2,4-triazole) ligands and multicarboxylate ligands. The lone pair electrons of the 1,2-nitrogen atoms in 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligands make them strong σ-donators when binding to two metal ions in the assembling process. The structure and diverse coordination modes of carboxylate ligands usually lead to diversified structures. Metal ions can form complicated hydroxyl-metal complexes within water hydrothermal/solvothermal systems, which are the basis of multinuclear structures. There is a very good chance to obtain MOFs based on tetranuclear cluster SBUs by combining 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole and multicarboxylate ligands.6i–k,7 The MOFs based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] are reported sporadically.5c–n No systematic research on tetranuclear cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] MOFs has been reported, to the best of our knowledge.8

Herein, we report our work on tetranuclear cobalt(II) MOFs by the combination of the 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligand 1,4-bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-ylmethyl)benzene (btrb) and multicarboxylate ligands under controlled conditions. Three tetranuclear cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] based MOFs {[Co4(OH)2(adc)6(H2O)5][Co2(OH)(btrb)]2·8H2O}n (1), {[Co4(OH)2(btrb)(nip)3(H2O)3]·4H2O}n (2·4H2O) and {[Co4(OH)2(btrb)(btc)2(H2O)2]·9H2O}n (3·9H2O) were successfully constructed with btrb and three multicarboxylate ligands adc, nip and btc using a hydrothermal method (H2adc = 1,3-adamantanedicarboxylic acid, H2nip = 5-nitroisophthalic acid and H3btc = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid). 1 exhibits an unusual (3,4)-connected 2D network based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] and [Co2(μ2-OH)] units. 2 shows a 6-connected pcu topology based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units. 3 shows a rare (3,8)-connected 3D network based on the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit. The connection number of tetranuclear units is successfully regulated from 4 in 1 to 6 in 2 and then 8 in 3 by multicarboxylate ligands. We have found a general stoichiometry law in the synthesis of tetranuclear MOFs. The magnetic measurements reveal that 1–3 exhibit antiferromagnetic interactions between the adjacent cobalt(II) centers. 1–3 are good photocatalysts for the degradation of methylene blue (MB).

Experimental

General procedure

All reagents were of analytical grade and used without further purification. 1,4-Bis(1,2,4-triazol-4-ylmethyl)benzene (btrb) was synthesized according to the literature method.9 Elemental analyses (EA) for C, H, and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. FT-IR spectra were obtained on a Bruker VERTEX 70 FT-IR spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was performed on a D/MAX-3C diffractometer with the Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) at room temperature. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis was carried out using a Thermal Analyst 2100 TA Instrument and SDT 2960 Simultaneous TGA-DTA Instrument with nitrogen flow at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Magnetic susceptibility data were collected on a Quantum design MPMS-XL-7 SQUID magnetometer.

The number of lattice water molecules for compounds 2 and 3 is deduced from the TGA and elemental analysis. The positions of hydrogen atoms of btrb and carboxylate ligands were determined with theoretical calculation. Photocatalytic experiment

1 (0.080 g), 2 (0.080 g) or 3 (0.080 g) was added to a solution of methylene (MB) (200 mL, 10 mg L−1). After stirring in the dark for 30 min, 1 mL of 30% H2O2 was added and then the solution was stirred continuously under UV irradiation from a 175 W high pressure mercury vapour lamp. The absorbance of the solution was measured by using a UV-Vis spectrophotometer every 10 min at the maximum absorption wavelength of 664 nm for MB. The blank experiment was carried out by the same way without these MOFs.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of {[Co4(OH)2(adc)6(H2O)5][Co2(OH)(btrb)]2·8H2O}n (1)

Single-crystal analysis shows that compound 1 is an unusual 2D network based on the tetranuclear cobalt(II) cluster [Co4(μ3-OH)2] and the binuclear cobalt(II) cluster [Co2(μ2-OH)]. In the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] moiety, the Co1(II) ion has a slightly distorted octahedral geometry with a CoO5N chromophore and is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O6A) from two adc, two μ3-OH (O13, O13A), one water molecule (O22) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N1) from one btrb ligand (Fig. S1, ESI†). The Co2(II) ion is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1A, O5), one μ3-OH (O13), one triazole nitrogen atom from one btrb ligand (N2) and two water molecules (O15, O16) in the octahedral geometry. In the [Co2(μ2-OH)] moiety, Co3(II) and Co4(II) ions both have a distorted octahedral geometry and are coordinated with four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O3A, O4A, O7 and O8 for Co3 and O9, O10, O11C and O12C for Co4), one μ2-OH (O14) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N4B for Co3 and N5B for Co4). One μ3-OH group (O13) connects three Co(II) ions (Co1, Co2, Co1A) and deviates from the Co atom plane over 0.834(2) Å. The Co2–O13–Co1, Co2–O13–Co1A and Co1–O13–Co1A bond angles are 118.427(3)°, 95.066(3)° and 99.638(5)° respectively. Two μ3-OH groups (O13, O13A) link the Co(II) ions and generate a tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2] moiety with a chair arrangement with the Co⋯Co separations in the range of 3.0520(1)–3.5703(1) Å (Co1⋯Co2, 3.5703(1) Å; Co2⋯Co1A, 3.0520(1) Å; Co1⋯Co1A, 3.1688(1) Å).

There are three kinds of adc ligands in 1. One carboxylate group (O1AO2A) of one adc ligand shows a μ2–η2 bridging coordination mode connecting Co1A and Co2 ions. The other carboxylate group (O3AO4A) exhibits a monodentate mode linking the Co3 ion (Fig. 1a). One carboxylate group (O5O6) of the other adc ligand presents a μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging mode linking Co1A and Co2 ions. The other carboxylate group (O7O8) is in a chelating mode coordinated with Co3. Two carboxylate groups (O9O10 and O11O12) of the third kind of adc ligand act as a chelating mode linking one Co(II) ion (Co4 or Co4C) (Fig. 1b).

Two other adc ligands (O9–O12, O9C–O12C) connect two binuclear cobalt(II) clusters [Co2(μ2-OH)] (Fig. 1b). Each btrb ligand link one [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit and one [Co2(μ2-OH)] unit (Fig. 1c). Each [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit connects four [Co2(μ2-OH)] units through two double adc and two btrb ligands (Fig. 1d). Crystal structure of {[Co4(OH)2(btrb)(nip)3(H2O)3]·4H2O}n (2·4H2O)

Compound 2 is a 3D MOF based on two kinds of cobalt(II) tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2] clusters. In the first kind of [Co4(μ3-OH)2] cluster, the Co1(II) ion has a distorted octahedral geometry with a CoO5N chromophore and is coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1A, O5B, O6B) from two nip ligands, one μ3-OH (O7), one water molecule (O21) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N1C) from one btrb ligand (Fig. S2, ESI†). The Co2(II) ion is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2A, O11) from two nip ligands, two μ3-OH (O7, O7D), one water molecule (O19) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N2E). In the second kind of [Co4(μ3-OH)2] cluster, the Co3(II) ion is coordinated with four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O3, O4, O8F, O13) from three nip ligands, one μ3-OH (O10), and one nitrogen atom (N5) in a distorted octahedral geometry (Fig. S3, ESI†). The Co4(II) ion is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O9, O14F) from two nip ligands, two μ3-OH (O10, O10F), one water molecule (O20) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N6).

One μ3-OH (O7) in the first tetranuclear cobalt(II) cluster connects three Co(II) ions (Co1, Co2, Co2D) and deviates from the cobalt atom plane over 0.669(6) Å (Fig. S2, ESI†). The other μ3-OH (O10) deviates from the cobalt(II) atom plane (Co3, Co4, Co4F) over 0.728(5) Å (Fig. S3, ESI†). The Co1–O7–Co2, Co1–O7–Co2D, Co2–O7–Co2D, Co3–O10–Co4, Co3–O10–Co4F and Co4–O10–Co4F bond angles are 116.976(3), 115.795(3), 96.797(3), 101.883(3), 121.495(4) and 99.618(3)° respectively. Two μ3-OH groups (O7, O7D or O10, O10F) connect four Co(II) ions to form tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2] clusters with a chair arrangement. The distances of Co⋯Co ions are 3.5119(1) Å for Co1⋯Co2, 3.5033(1) Å for Co1⋯Co2D, 3.1440(1) Å for Co2⋯Co2D, 3.2060(1) Å for Co3⋯Co4, 3.5320(1) Å for Co3⋯Co4F, and 3.1766(1) Å for Co4⋯Co4F.

There are three kinds of nip ligands in 2. Two kinds of nip ligands (O1–O4 or O5O6O8O9) exhibit the same coordination mode and two nip ligands both connect two adjacent [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units (Fig. 2a). One carboxylate group (O1O2 or O8O9) is μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging mode with two Co(II) ions (O1O2 for Co1A and Co2A, O8O9 for Co3F and Co4). One carboxylate group (O3O4 or O5O6) is chelated to one Co(II) ion (O3O4 for Co3, O5O6 for Co1G). One carboxylate group (O11O12) acts as a monodentate mode linking the Co2 ion. The other carboxylate group (O13O14) shows a μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging mode coordinating Co3 and Co4F ions (Fig. 2b). Each btrb ligand connects four Co(II) ions (Co1H, Co2E, Co3, Co4) from two [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units with the distances of 13.9044(4), 14.7357(5), 13.9371(4) and 13.9139(4) Å for Co1H⋯Co4, Co1H⋯Co3, Co2E⋯Co4 and Co2E⋯Co3, respectively (Fig. 2c). Two kinds of tetranuclear cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units both connect six [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units (Fig. S4 and S5, ESI†). The tetranuclear cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] units are linked by nip and btrb ligands to construct a 3D network (Fig. 2d). The cavity in the framework is occupied by lattice water molecules with the solvent accessible volume 378.6 Å3, 14.5% of the cell volume analysed by PLATON.

In the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] moiety, the Co1(II) ion has an octahedral geometry with a CoO5N chromophore and is coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1, O5B) from two btc, two μ3-OH (O7, O7A), one water molecule (O8) and one triazole nitrogen atom (N1) from one btrb ligand. The distorted octahedral geometry of the Co2(II) ion is coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2, O3C, O4C, O6B), one μ3-OH (O7) and one triazole nitrogen atom from one btrb ligand (N2A) (Fig. S6, ESI†).

The Co2–O7–Co1, Co2–O7–Co1A and Co1–O7–Co1A bond angles are 103.420(2)°, 121.471(2)° and 99.562(2)° respectively. Two μ3-OH groups (O7, O7A) link the Co(II) ions and generate a tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2] moiety with a chair arrangement with the Co⋯Co separations in the range of 3.1714(1)–3.5305(1) Å (Co1⋯Co2, 3.2097(1) Å; Co2⋯Co1A, 3.5305(1) Å; Co1⋯Co1A, 3.1714(1) Å).

There is only one kind of btc ligand in compound 3 (Fig. 3a). Two carboxylate groups (O1O2 or O5O6) of the btc ligand have a μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging mode and connect two Co(II) ions (O1O2 for Co1 and O5O6 for Co2). Topological analysis

Topologically, the tetranuclear cobalt(II) clusters in 1 are simplified as one nodes which are 4-connected and the binuclear units are 3-connected nodes. Double adc ligands in 1 are 2-connected. The btrb ligands in 1 are 2-connected. The 2D networks of 1 can be described as a (3,4)-connected network based on the tetranuclear and binuclear cobalt(II) clusters with a rare point symbol of (4·62)2(42·62·82) (Fig. 1e).11 Two kinds of tetranuclear units in 2 are both 6-connected. The btrb ligands are 2-connected. Double nip ligands (Fig. 2a) and nip ligand (Fig. 2b) act as 2-connected. The 3D network in 2 can be simplified as a pcu network with the point symbol of 412·63 (Fig. 2e). The tetranuclear cobalt(II) clusters in 2 can be simplified as 8-connected nodes, while the btc ligands can be regarded as 3-connected nodes. The btrb ligands are still 2-connected. The 3D networks in 3 can be described as a (3,8)-connected network with a rare point symbol of (4·5·6)2(42·56·616·72·82) (Fig. 3e). The connection number increases from 4 in 1 to 6 in 2 and 8 in 3 by the regulation of the multicarboxylate ligands. To our knowledge, regulating the connection number of the tetranuclear cobalt(II) units by changing the multicarboxylate ligands has never been reported. The strategy to obtain MOFs based on tetranuclear hydroxyl [Co4(μ3-OH)2]

The condition controlling of the hydrothermal/solvothermal method has always been the key point and also a challenge in crystal growth. Hydrothermal and solvothermal conditions are controlled by numerous macroscopic factors, such as pH,14a,b temperature,14c ion effects14d–f and solvent effects.14g–k Herein, we will discuss the influence of the ligands and the control of the conditions of the hydrothermal/solvothermal method.
Influence of the ligands and construct coordination quadrangle. As a common phenomenon, 4-substituted (1,2,4-triazole) ligands can easily form a quadrangle with two metal ions (Scheme 1). In our case, all the btrb ligands link four metal ions with the anti-conformation. The quadrangle formed by two nitrogen atoms from btrb and two cobalt ions is slightly distorted. In order to compare the stabilities of different coordination quadrangles, the bond angle of ∠metal(M)–N–N and the bond distance of the adjacent atoms selected from different compounds with the metal–N–N–metal quadrangle units are listed in Table S3 (ESI†).7a,b,9a,b,15 The angles of ∠M–N–N, d(M–N), d(N–N), and d(M–M) are in the range of 109.828°–132.043°, 1.956–2.450 Å, 1.289–1.422 Å, and 3.136–4.155 Å, respectively. The phenomenon that the coordination quadrangle formed by triazole ligands and two metal ions can warp in a large range illustrates the fact that 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligands have outstanding coordination adaptability and the coordination quadrangle is very stable. The nitrogen atoms at the 1- and 2-positions of the 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligands can easily catch two metal ions during assembly. As a semi-rigid 4-substituted bis(1,2,4-triazole) ligand, btrb also has excellent coordination adaptability to coordinate with two metal ions to form a quadrangle. Two groups of coordination quadrangles can be linked by hydroxyl groups to form a tetranuclear unit.
Scheme 1 (a) The condensed structural formula of the coordination quadrangle. (b) The quadrangle formed by btrb and two cobalt ions is slightly distorted.

The carboxylate groups of the three multicarboxylate ligands show four kinds of coordination modes: monodentate, chelating, μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging and μ2–η2 bidentate bridging (Scheme 2). The connection number of the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] unit increases from 4 in 1 to 6 in 2 and 8 in 3 by the regulation of the multicarboxylate ligands. The network also becomes more and more complicated from 1 to 3. It is clear that the structure of MOFs can be designed and regulated by multicarboxylate ligands.


Scheme 2 The coordination mode of carboxylate groups in this work: (a) monodentate, (b) chelating, (c) μ2–η1:η1 bidentate bridging, (d) μ2–η2 bidentate bridging.
Synthesis condition controlling. Freshly prepared metal hydroxyl compounds are highly active in solution. But sometimes the metal hydroxyl compounds are too active for assembling. The introduction of certain organic solvents will moderate the activity of these species making it feasible to form desirable compounds. Good crystals of 2 were obtained in 50% ethanol solution by a hydrothermal method. But unknown precipitates in water solution were obtained under the same synthetic conditions. The ethanol solution may reduce the solubility of the polar species directly thus reducing the activity of reactants and finally resulting in the formation of 2.

In order to obtain tetranuclear cluster compounds, the stoichiometry should be controlled precisely. A dilute solution is preferable to obtain high quality crystals for single-crystal X-ray diffraction study.

For the hydrothermal/solvothermal method, the best stoichiometry is chosen when the solvent and single-crystals are the only things left in the Teflon vessel after cooling down to room temperature and should better conform to the concept of green chemistry. During the synthesis of 3, different concentrations under the same other conditions were screened to give the best result. Flowerlike crystals of 3 can be achieved at a relatively high concentration (2.5 mmol L−1 of Co(II)) nonetheless it is very difficult to pick suitable crystals for single crystal diffraction. The shape of the crystal flowers changes from tight radial clusters to sticks by reducing the concentration gradually. Suitable crystals were finally obtained at a quite low concentration (0.26 mmol L−1 of Co(II)).

Based on our observation, the formula of tetranuclear cluster [MII4(μ3-OH)2] based MOFs can be reduced to be {[MII4(μ3-OH)2(L)a(acid)b(solvent)c]·guest}n (L is a electroneutral ligand, a = 0 or 1 or 2; acid = dicarboxylate, b = 3; acid = tricarboxylate, b = 2).5,6b,i,k,7 The ratio of the metal, L and acid can be 4:1:3, 4:2:3, 4:1:2, or 4:2:2. This result indicates that the actual stoichiometry can be predicted according to the chemical formula. The best stoichiometry for parallelogram-like tetranuclear MOFs is estimated to be 4:1:3 or 4:2:3 for dicarboxylate and 4:1:2 or 4:2:2 for tricarboxylate, and our results match well with this law.

In short, the 4-, 6- and 8-connected MOFs based on tetranuclear Co(II) clusters were synthesized by using different multicarboxylate ligands adc, nip and btc. The structures of MOFs were tuned by the multicarboxylate in the presence of btrb ligands. The btrb ligand plays the key role in the formation of tetranuclear Co(II) clusters. The combination of 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligands and multicarboxylate ligands is an effective strategy to obtain tetranuclear cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] based MOFs under elaborately controlled conditions.

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependent magnetic susceptibilities of compounds 1, 2 and 3 were collected under a 1 kOe field in the temperature range of 2–300 K. On the basis of the structure, 1 contains one tetranuclear cobalt(II) and two binuclear cobalt(II). The observed χmT value of 1 at 300 K is 21.12 cm3 K mol−1, which is much larger than the spin-only value of 15.00 cm3 K mol−1 for eight isolated high-spin d7 ions, indicating significant orbital contributions of the distorted octahedral Co(II) ions. The χmT value decreases slowly to 6.75 cm3 K mol−1 at about 20 K, and then decreases to 0.83 cm3 K mol−1 quickly at 2 K (Fig. 4a), which indicates strong antiferromagnetic couplings between the Co(II) ions. The χm−1vs. T data from 20 to 300 K conform to the Curie–Weiss law to give θ = −53.65 K and C = 12.46 cm3 K mol−1.
Fig. 4 The χmT vs. T and χm−1vs. T curves (a) for 1, (b) for 2 and (c) for 3.

The χmT value of 2 at 300 K is 10.49 cm3 K mol−1, which is larger than the theoretically calculated value 7.50 cm3 K mol−1 for four magnetically isolated high-spin Co(II) atoms, indicating the orbital contributions of the distorted octahedral Co(II) ions (Fig. 4b). The χmT value smoothly decreases to 5.22 cm3 K mol−1 at 50 K, and then rapidly drops to 0.66 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The χm−1vs. T obeys the Curie–Weiss law above 50 K and gives θ = −77.17 K and C = 13.18 cm3 K mol−1 between 50 and 300 K. The big negative Weiss constant indicates strong antiferromagnetic couplings between the Co(II) ions. The χmT value of 3 at 300 K is 8.13 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly larger than the theoretical value 7.50 cm3 K mol−1 for four magnetically isolated high-spin Co(II) atoms (Fig. 4c). The χmT value smoothly decreases to 4.50 cm3 K mol−1 at 50 K, and then rapidly drops to 0.41 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K due to the strong antiferromagnetic couplings between the Co(II) ions. The degradation of methylene blue

Photocatalysts have attracted much attention due to their potential applications in purifying water and air by decomposing organic pollutants.16 Thus, methylene blue (MB), as a model dye contaminant, was selected to evaluate the photocatalytic effectiveness in the purification of waste water.16a–d The degradation experiment of MB was tracked by spectroscopy and the results are depicted in Fig. 5. It was found that the degradation rates of MB are 66.3% for 1, 61.6% for 2, 60.0% for 3 and 30.2% for blank experiment after 90 min. 1–3 remain stable after photocatalytic reaction. 1–3 are good photocatalysts for the degradation of the organic dye MB.

PXRD and thermal stability

The measured and simulated PXRDs confirm the purity of 1, 2 and 3 (Fig. S7, S8 and S9, ESI†). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of 1, 2 and 3 was performed on crystalline samples heated from 30 to 800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere (Fig. S10, ESI†). The lattice and coordinated water molecules of 1 were completely lost at 148 °C (calcd: 9.05%, found: 9.25%), and the remaining substance was stable up to 210 °C. The residue of 1 at 800 °C should be CoO (calcd: 23.17%, found: 23.13%). The lattice and coordination water molecules of 2 and 3 were completely lost at 147 and 143 °C, respectively (calcd: 9.98, found: 10.02% for 2; calcd: 17.66, found: 17.38% for 3). The networks of 2 and 3 were stable up to 253 and 244 °C, respectively. The residues of 2 and 3 at 800 °C should be CoO (calcd: 23.72%, found: 23.13% for 2; calcd: 26.70%, found: 27.62% for 3).

Conclusions

In summary, three tetranuclear hydroxy cobalt(II) [Co4(μ3-OH)2] MOFs were successfully synthesized under hydrothermal/solvothermal conditions. 1 shows a rare (3,4)-connected 2D network based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] and [Co2(μ3-OH)] clusters. 2 shows a 6-connected pcu topology based on [Co4(μ3-OH)2] clusters. 3 is a (3,8)-connected 3D framework with a rare point symbol of (4·5·6)2(42·56·616·72·82) based on the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] clusters. The magnetic measurements reveal that 1–3 have antiferromagnetic interaction in the cobalt(II) centers. 1–3 are good photocatalysts for the degradation of the organic dye methylene blue (MB).

The connection number of tetranuclear cobalt(II) clusters increases from 4 in 1 to 6 in 2 and then 8 in 3 with the regulation of the multicarboxylate ligands adc, nip and btc. The combination of 4-substituted 1,2,4-triazole ligands and different rigid multicarboxylate ligands is an effective method for the designation and tuning of metal cluster based MOFs. We will continue to design and tune the structures of MOFs based on tetranuclear metal or high-nuclear metal clusters to tune and improve their photocatalytic and magnetic properties.
