Construction of Cd(II) coordination polymers used as catalysts for the photodegradation of organic dyes in polluted water†

First published on 2nd January 2014

Four Cd(II) coordination polymers formulated as {[Cd3(tpcb)2)(η,η-μ-SO4)2(H2O)6]SO4·16H2O}n (1), {[Cd(tpcb)0.75(OH)(H2O)2](NO3)}n (2), {[Cd2(tpcb)(SO4)2(H2O)6]·2MeOH·3H2O}n (3), and {[Cd(tpcb)(NO3)(H2O)2](NO3)}n (4) were synthesized by reactions of CdSO4 or Cd(NO3)2 with tetrakis(4-pyridyl)cyclobutane (tpcb) under solvothermal conditions or at ambient temperature. Compounds 1–4 were characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy, thermal analysis, powder X-ray diffraction analysis and single crystal X-ray diffraction. Compounds 1, 2, and 4 possess three different complicated 3D frameworks with the Schläfli symbols of (46)(412·54·64·88), (63)4(62·84)3 and (83)(86), respectively, while 3 presents a 1D chain structure. Compounds 1–4 exhibited relatively good photocatalytic activity towards the degradation of methyl orange (MO), methyl blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB) in aqueous solution. These results offered a good insight into the temperature effects on the assembly of Cd(II) coordination polymers and their photocatalytic applications.

Introduction

In recent years, rational design and synthesis of functional coordination polymers with desired structures and properties have received increasing attention due to their fascinating topologies1 and potential applications in catalysis,2 adsorption,3 separation,4 sensing5 and drug delivery.6 Among these applications, photocatalysis using coordination polymers as catalysts for specific inorganic or organic reactions such as the degradation of organic dyes, water splitting, and photoreduction of CO27 is emerging. Motivated by the research trend and our project on the synthesis of functional coordination polymers,8 we are interested in the preparation of some polydimensional coordination polymers and would like to investigate whether they could be used to photocatalytically decompose organic dyes in polluted water. As we all know, the assembly of functional coordination polymers is significantly affected by various kinds of external physical or chemical factors such as temperature, pH values, ratios of reactants, and solvents.9 Among them, the reaction temperature is quite critical in determining the outcomes of the specific reactions with the same components.9j In this work, we selected a tetrapyridyl-based ligand tetrakis(4-pyridyl)cyclobutane (tpcb) to react with two different Cd(II) salts (CdSO4 and Cd(NO3)2) under solvothermal conditions or at ambient temperature. Four coordination polymers with different topological structures {[Cd3(tpcb)2)(η,η-μ-SO4)2(H2O)6]SO4·16H2O}n (1), {[Cd(tpcb)0.75(OH)(H2O)2](NO3)}n (2), {[Cd2(tpcb)(SO4)2(H2O)6]·2MeOH·3H2O}n (3), and {[Cd(tpcb)(NO3)(H2O)2](NO3)}n (4) were isolated. These four coordination polymers were found to be able to photocatalytically degrade three common organic dyes (methyl orange (MO), methyl blue (MB) and rhodamine B (RhB)) in water in a relatively efficient way. Herein, we report their synthesis, structures and photocatalytic degradation performances.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

The ligand tpcb was prepared as reported previously.10 Other chemicals and reagents were obtained from commercial sources and used as received. Infrared spectra were obtained (KBr disk, 400–4000 cm−1) using a Perkin-Elmer Model 1320 spectrometer. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed using a Carlo-Erba CHNO-S microanalyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a Mettler Toledo Star system (heating rate of 5 °C min−1). The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were carried out using a PANalytical X'Pert PRO MPD system (PW3040/60). The photoluminescent spectra were obtained using a Perkin-Elmer LS55 spectrofluorometer. Solid-state UV-Vis-NIR spectra were obtained using a Shimadzu UV-3150 spectrometer at room temperature in the range 200–800 nm.

A mixture containing CdSO4·8/3H2O (38 mg, 0.05 mmol) and tpcb (9 mg, 0.025 mmol) in 2 mL of H2O was sealed in a glass tube and heated under autogenous pressure to 170 °C for 24 h. It was allowed to cool to room temperature at the rate of 5 °C per hour to produce colorless needle crystals of 1, which were collected by filtration, washed with water and EtOH, and dried in air. X-Ray data collection and structure determination. Single crystals of 1–4 suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained directly from the above preparations. The measurement of 1 was made using a Saturn 724 diffractometer with an enhanced X-ray source Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å), while the measurements of 2 and 3 were made using a Xcalibur, Atlas, Gemini diffractometer with an enhanced X-ray source Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å), and the measurement of 4 was made using a Bruker APEX-II CCD diffractometer with an enhanced X-ray source Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å). Each single crystal was mounted on a glass fiber at 298 K for 1, 153 K for 2, 200 K for 3 and 173 K for 4. Cell parameters were refined by using the program CrysAlisPro, Agilent Technologies (version 1.171.36.28) and the Bruker-SAINT software package. The collected data were reduced by using the program CrysAlisPro, and absorption corrections (multi-scan) were applied. The reflection data were also corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects.

The crystal structures of 1–4 were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares methods with the SHELXL-97 program.11 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. For 1, the coordinates of the H atoms on the coordinated and free water were estimated using WinGX and were subsequently fixed (AFIX 3) with their thermal parameters set to Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(O). A large amount of spatially delocalized electron density in the lattice was found but acceptable refinement results could not be obtained for this electron density. The solvent contribution was then modeled using SQUEEZE in the Platon program suite. The occupancy of the H2O solvent molecule in 1 was fixed to be 16 to give acceptable thermal parameters. For 2, the O atom coordinated to Cd is suggested to be an admixture of 1/3 OH and 2/3 H2O to maintain the overall charge neutrality. The H atoms on these disordered OH and H2O could not be identified from the Fourier map and were therefore not located but added in the formula. For 3, the coordinates of the coordinated and the free water molecules were suggested using the Calc-OH program in the WinGX suite with their O–H distances fixed at O–H = 0.85 Å and thermal parameters constrained to Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(O). The water molecules were then treated as a rigid group in the subsequent refinement. The H atoms on the methanol O atom were located by estimating the possible H bonding interactions involving these OH groups and the located H atoms were then treated as a riding model in subsequent refinement cycles. The structure was twinned about the [001] lattice direction and the twin matrix was suggested using the ROTAX program while the HKLF5 type file was prepared using MAKE HKLF5 in the WinGX suite. The ratios of the two twinned domains were fined to 0.25/0.75. The H atoms of all other water solvent molecules in 1–4 were located from the Fourier maps with the O–H distances being fixed at 0.85 Å. Other hydrogen atoms were placed in geometrically idealized positions and constrained to ride on their parent atoms. A summary of key crystallographic data for 1–4 is given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths for 1–4 are listed in Table 2.

Photocatalytic activity study. The photocatalytic activities of the solid samples 1–4 were evaluated by the degradation of MO, MB and RhB in aqueous solution. 20 mL of a MO aqueous solution with a concentration of 0.1 g L−1 and 20 mL of a MB aqueous solution with a concentration of 0.5 g L−1 and 20 mL of a RhB aqueous solution with a concentration of 0.14 g L−1 were mixed with 10 mg of the catalysts, respectively. A lamp (365 nm) was used as the UV resource. During the degradation, the mixture was stirred continuously with a magnetic stirrer. At regular time intervals, a 1 mL solution was sampled, filtered and diluted to 10 mL in a volumetric flask. The UV-Vis absorption spectra of MO, MB and RhB aqueous solutions were measured using a Hitachi U-4100 spectrophotometer.

Results and discussion

Synthetic and spectral aspects

Hydrothermal reaction of CdSO4·8/3H2O with tpcb (molar ratio 2:1) in H2O at 170 °C in a sealed Pyrex glass tube for one day afforded colourless blocks of 1 in 80% yield. Similar reactions of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with tpcb (molar ratio 1:1) in DMF/H2O (v/v = 1/3) at 130 °C generated colourless crystals of 2 in 45% yield. For 1 or 2, when we carried out the analogous solvothermal reactions of the same components in other solvents such as H2O/MeCN, H2O/EtOH, only a large amount of insoluble precipitate was observed. When the similar reactions were carried out at a higher temperature (e.g. 170 °C), the same product 1 or 2 was generated in a relatively lower yield. Intriguingly, layering an MeOH solution of tpcb onto an aqueous solution of CdSO4·8/3H2O (molar ratio 1:2) at room temperature afforded colourless crystals of 3 in 60% yield. Analogous reactions of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with equimolar tpcb produced colourless crystals of 4 in 70% yield. For the same components (CdSO4·8/3H2O/tpcb or Cd(NO3)2·4H2O/tpcb), the same reactions under solvothermal conditions or at ambient temperature did give rise to two different products (1vs.3 or 2vs.4), which could be ascribed to the temperature effects, though the solvent effects may not be ruled out.

Complexes 1–4 are stable in air and insoluble in water and common organic solvents such as CHCl3, MeCN, DMF and DMSO. The elemental analyses were consistent with their chemical formula. For 1–4, the PXRD showed that the observed patterns for each compound correlate well with the simulated ones generated from single-crystal X-ray diffraction data (Fig. S1, ESI†). The IR spectra of 1 and 3 exhibited strong absorption bands at 1117 cm−1 and 1112 cm−1 attributable to the coordinated sulfate groups. The IR spectra of 2 and 4 had strong absorption bands at 1354 cm−1 and 1326 cm−1 attributable to the coordinated and uncoordinated nitrate groups.

Crystal structure of 1. Complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n, and its asymmetric unit consists of a [Cd3(tpcb)2(η,η-μ-SO4)2(H2O)6]2+ dication, one SO42− ion, and one and half H2O solvent molecule. Each Cd(II) centre is octahedrally coordinated by four N atoms from four tpcb ligands and two O atoms from two bridging SO42− ions (Cd1) (Fig. 1a) or two N atoms from two tpcb ligands, one O atom from one SO42− ion, and three O atoms from three coordinated H2O molecules (Cd2 and Cd3) (Fig. 1b and c). The mean Cd–N bond length (2.333(4) Å) is comparable to that of [Cd(tpcb)0.5(1,3-BDC)]n (2.326(4) Å).12a The average Cd–O(SO42−) bond length (2.344(4) Å) is almost the same as that of the corresponding one in [Cd5Cl2(μ-Cl)4(μ3-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(H2O)2(Htitmb)2]n (2.345(3) Å, titmb = 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene),12b while the mean Cd–O(H2O) bond length (2.325(4) Å) is shorter than that found in [Cd(μ-Cl)(L)(H2O)]2 (2.407(3) Å, L = 2,2′-dipyridylamine)12c and [Cd5Cl2(μ-Cl)4(μ3-Cl)2(η,η,η-μ3-SO4)2(H2O)2(Htitmb)2]n (2.378(4) Å). The four pyridyl groups of each tpcb ligand in 1 adopt an up-down-up-down conformation as showed in Scheme 1a with the dihedral angles between the two opposite pyridyl groups being 78.22° and 67.31°. The Cd atoms are coordinated by tpcb to form a [Cd3(tpcb)2]n layer extending along the ac plane (Fig. S2a, ESI†), which are further connected by the η,η-μ-SO42− ions to form a 3D framework (Fig. 1d). In its framework, there exist hexagonal 1D channels along the a axis (Fig. S2b, ESI†), and the effective solvent accessible volume is 1317.2 Å3 per unit cell (20.0% of the total cell volume calculated by the Platon program). If the Cd3 unit (Cd1–Cd3) and the tpcb ligand are considered as a 8-connecting node and a 4-connecting node, respectively, the whole structure of 1 can be described as a (4,8)-connected net with a (46)(412·54·64·88) Schläfli symbol (Fig. 1e).

Crystal structure of 2. Complex 2 crystallizes in the cubic space group I3m, and its asymmetric unit contains one-sixth of a [Cd(tpcb)0.75(OH)(H2O)2]+ cation and one-sixth of a NO3− anion. As showed in Fig. 2a, each Cd atom was octahedrally coordinated by three N atoms from three tpcb ligands, and three O atoms, each of which is suggested to be an admixture of 1/3 OH and 2/3 H2O to maintain the overall charge neutrality. The average Cd–N bond length (2.369(10) Å) in 2 is slightly longer than that in 1 and [Cd(tpcb)0.5(1,3-BDC)]n (2.326(4) Å). The average Cd–O(H2O) bond length (2.443(8) Å) is much longer than those of the corresponding ones in 1 and {[Cd2(tpcb)(5-NO2-1,3-BDC)2(OH2)2]·4H2O}n (2.276(6) Å).12a The four pyridyl groups of each tpcb ligand in 2 take the similar conformation to that of 1 with the dihedral angle between the two opposite pyridyl groups being 0°. Compound 2 holds an adamantane-type unit within a single 3D [Cd(tpcb)0.75(OH)(OH2)2]nn+ cationic net (Fig. S2c, ESI†). Such a net could be considered as a 3D (3,4)-connected framework with a vertex symbol of (6·6·6)(62·62·8·8·8·8) if each Cd and tpcb are viewed as a 3-connecting node and a 4-connecting node, respectively. Taking the stoichiometry into account, the Schläfli symbol denotes the (63)4(62·84)3 topology (Fig. 2b). This Schläfli symbol is the same as that observed in {[Ag4(4,4′-tpcb)3][(NO3)4(H2O)6]}n.13 Two such nets are further interpenetrated to generate a two-fold interpenetrating 3D net (Fig. 2c).

Crystal structure of 3. Complex 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and its asymmetry unit consists of one [Cd2(tpcb)(OSO3)2 (OH2)6] molecule, two MeOH, and three H2O solvent molecules. Each Cd(II) centre is octahedrally coordinated by two N atoms from two separate tpcb ligands, one O atom from one monodentate SO42− ion, and three O atoms from three H2O molecules (Fig. 3a and S2d, ESI†). The average Cd–N, Cd1–O(SO42−) and Cd–O(H2O) bond lengths (2.310(6) Å vs. 2.300(6) Å vs. 2.302(7) Å) are somewhat shorter than those of the corresponding ones of 1. Different from those of 1 and 2, the four pyridyl groups of each tpcb ligand in 3 adopt an up-up-down-down conformation as showed in Scheme 1b with the dihedral angles between the two opposite pyridyl groups being 57.12° and 49.63°. Two pairs of the adjacent pyridyl groups of one tpcb ligand combine four [Cd(SO4)(H2O)3] fragments to afford one [Cd2(tpcb)(SO4)2(H2O)6]2 unit. Such a unit is further connected to its neighboring ones by sharing Cd(II) centers to form a 1D [Cd2(tpcb)(SO4)2(H2O)6]n chain (Fig. 3b).

Crystal structure of 4. Complex 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c and its asymmetry unit has one [Cd(tpcb)(NO3)(H2O)2]+ cation and one NO3− anion. Each Cd(II) centre is also octahedrally coordinated by three N atoms from three separate tpcb ligands, one O atom from one NO3− ion and two O atoms from two H2O molecules (Fig. 4a). The average Cd–N and Cd–O(H2O) bond lengths (2.3204(17) Å vs. (2.3089(17) Å) are close to those of the corresponding ones of 1 and 3. The mean Cd–O(NO3−) bond length (2.3937(18) Å) is shorter than that in {[Cd(4,4′-bpy)2](NO3)2)} (2.489(7) Å).14 The four pyridyl groups of each tpcb ligand in 4 take the similar conformation to that of 3. Interestingly, the tpcb ligands in 4 show two coordination modes. One is the tetradentate mode in which the dihedral angle between the two opposite pyridyl groups is 66.37° (Scheme 1b). The other is the bidentate mode in which the dihedral angle between the two opposite pyridyl groups being 48.36° and 47.89° (Scheme 1c). Such a mode is observed for the first time, to our knowledge. The tpcb ligand with the tetradentate mode links the [Cd(NO3)(H2O)2]+ fragments to form a 2D [Cd(NO3)(H2O)2]nn+ network (Fig. S2e, ESI†), while the tpcb ligand with the bidentate mode further connects the 2D network to form a 3D [Cd(tpcb)(NO3)(H2O)2]nn+ cationic framework (Fig. 4b and S2f, ESI†). The 3D cationic net of 4 can be considered as a (3,4)-connected framework with a (83)(86) Schläfli symbol if each Cd(II) centre and each tpcb ligand are considered as a 3-connecting node and a 4-connecting node, respectively (Fig. 4c). Two such nets are further interpenetrated to generate a two-fold interpenetrating 3D net (Fig. 4d). Within the framework, there exist several evident H-bonding interactions between one uncoordinated pyridyl group of a tpcb ligand and one coordinated H2O (O4⋯N4, 2.796(3) Å) and between one coordinated H2O and the monodentate nitrate (O5⋯O3 2.782(3) Å). The free nitrates are encompassed into the 1D channels of this 3D net and one H-bonding interaction with one H2O (O5⋯O7, 2.772(3) Å) is observed.

Photocatalytic properties. Organic dyes such as MO, MB and RhB are extensively used in the textile industry and resistant to biodegradation. Decomposition of such dye molecules into some simple molecules can reduce the environment pollution caused by them. In this work, we employed their photodegradation as a model route to demonstrate the photocatalytic activities of 1–4 in the purification of the contaminated water. The decomposition of MO, MB or RhB was monitored by the characteristic absorption band at 464 nm for MO, 593 nm for MB or 553 nm for RhB. When no catalyst was added to the system, the degradation of MO was negligible. In fact, MO could be slightly decomposed even after irradiation under UV light for a long time (Fig. S4a, ESI†). Upon UV irradiation without any catalysts, MB was relatively active and could be degraded by 50% after 4 hours (Fig. S4b, ESI†) while RhB could be degraded by 30% after 4 hours (Fig. S4c, ESI†). It is noted that the pure tpcb ligand had no any photocatalytic activity and only caused a slight fading of the dye solution, which may be due to their self-degradation mentioned above. A comparison of the photocatalytic activities of 1–4 was presented in Fig. 7. The degradation of MO in water was completed by 1–4 after ca. 5 hours, showing a similar photocatalytic activity (Fig. S5a–d, ESI†). In all cases, ca. 50% of MO was decomposed upon UV irradiation for 1.5 hours, while using the known coordination polymer [Mn2(L1)4/3(L2)2]n (L1 = 4′-(4-pyridyl)-4,2′:6′,4′′-terpyridine, H2L2 = (4-phenyl)-2,6-bis(4-carboxyphenyl)pyridine) as a catalyst, ca. 40% of MO was degraded in 1.5 hours.17c This manifested that the photocatalytic performances of 1–4 were slightly better than that of the latter compound. For MB, its degradation in water was almost completed after 2.5 hours (1) or 1.5 hours (2–4) of irradiation, indicating that 1 has a lower photocatalytic activity compared with 2–4 (Fig. S5e–h, ESI†). For RhB, the degradation in water was reached after 4.5 hours for 1 and 3, and 3 hours for 2 and 4 (Fig. S5i–l, ESI†). Comparatively speaking, 2 and 4 showed the better catalytic efficiency for the degradation of MO, MB or RhB. Thus, we took 2 as an example for the recycling purpose. Compound 2 could be recovered from the catalytic system by centrifugation and then reused for the fresh photodegradation and showed no obvious catalytic efficiency decay even after recycling 2 five times (Fig. S6, ESI†). The PXRD patterns of the recycled catalyst (Fig. S1, ESI†) were almost identical to those of the corresponding pure compound, indicating that its original structural framework was retained during its photocatalysis. The final colorless solution was extracted by acetic ether, and the organic phase was analyzed by gas chromatography (GC). No other organic species was observed, and thus we assumed that all the dyes might be converted into CO2 and H2O.7,15 Furthermore, when 2 was placed into an aqueous solution of MO, MB or RhB in the dark for two days, no obvious decrease of the characteristic absorption of MO, MB or RhB was observed, which may avoid the possibility of adsorbing such a dye molecule into its framework.
Fig. 7 (a) Curves of absorbance of the MO solution degraded by 1–4 of under UV light. (b) Curves of absorbance of the MB solution degraded by 1–4 of under UV light. (c) Curves of absorbance of the RhB solution degraded by 1–4 of under UV light.

The possible photocatalytic mechanism17 for the above degradation reactions is proposed as follows (Scheme S1, ESI†). Upon irradiation using UV light, electrons (e−) in the valence band (VB) are excited to the conduction band (CB), with the same amount of holes (h+) left in the VB. Then O2 may be reduced into O2− by the combination of electrons (e−), which may further turn into hydroxyl radicals (˙OH). At the same time, interaction of holes (h+) with hydroxyl (OH−) may produce a hydroxyl radical (˙OH). Such a hydroxyl radical (˙OH) is known to have high activity to demolish the organic dyes. The photocatalytic efficiencies (4 ≥ 2 > 3 > 1) may be due to the fact that the energy gap between the UV light (wavelength = 365 nm, hν = 3.40 eV) and the coordination polymer (for 1, Eg = 2.96 eV; for 2, Eg = 3.10 eV; for 3, Eg = 2.68 eV; for 4, Eg = 3.24 eV) decreased from 3 (0.72 eV) to 1 (0.44 eV) to 2 (0.30 eV) to 4 (0.16 eV). Such decadences may result in the faster generation speed of hydroxyl radicals in 2 or 4 and the faster decomposition of MO, MB or RhB initiated by 2 or 4.

Conclusions

In the work reported here, we demonstrated the formation of four Cd(II) coordination polymers 1–4 from the reactions of CdSO4 or Cd(NO3)2 with tpcb under solvothermal conditions or at room temperature. The different outcomes of the products using the same components may be due to the temperature effects, though the solvent effects may not be excluded. The photocatalytic activities of 1–4 were evaluated by the decomposition of three organic dyes (MO, MB and RhB) in aqueous solutions under UV irradiation. Compounds 2 and 4 showed the better catalytic performance for the degradation of MO, MB or RhB. Such a good performance of 2 or 4 may be attributed to the smaller energy gap between the UV light and 2 or 4 and the higher formation rate of hydroxyl radicals. Further studies on exploring their photocatalytic activity towards the decomposition of other toxic organic dyes in contaminated waters are in progress.
