Abstract

Abstract. The metal organic framework, [Cu5(C5H2N2O3)4(H2O)4(NO3)2·10H2O]n (1), was synthesized by a hydrothermal reaction via copper nitrate and in situ generated 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate from 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid. Compound 1 was characterized by elemental analysis, infrared spectroscopy, TG analysis, and single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction. It has a 3D structure constructed from Cu2+ cations, bridging NO3– anions, and 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate units. The NO3– anions act as μ2‐bridging ligand and the 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate units coordinate pentadentately through the two oxygen atoms of their carboxylate group, the oxygen atom of the hydroxyl group and two nitrogen atoms. The ligands link the CuII ions to form trinuclear units. These are further connected to form a novel 3D MOF, with a novel 4‐nodal 3,3,3,4‐connected net topology of an unprecedented point (Schläfli) symbol (6.7.9)(64.8.9)(7.102)(72.10).

Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal organic frameworks has attracted extensive interest owing to their intriguing architectures and topologies and their versatile promising applications in fields such as catalysis, luminescence, magnetism, sorption, ion exchange, non‐linear optics, and electricity.1–5 Over the past decades, a large number of metal organic frameworks based on the linkage of metal ions (or clusters) with the organic bridging N/O/S‐donor ligands have been reported. However, the exploration of new ligands has remained a challenge for researchers in coordination chemistry.6–8

In situ ligand transformations/synthesis under hydro‐/solvothermal conditions, such as cycloaddition of organic nitriles to azides or ammonia,9–11 decarboxylation of arene carboxylates,12 hydroxylation of aromatic rings,13 carbon–carbon bond formations by reductive or oxidative coupling,14 and cleavage and formation of C–N, C–O, and S–S bonds15,16 represent a potential new direction for the construction of novel metal organic frameworks.17 Among the varieties of ligands used in the in situ reaction mentioned above, pyrazinecarboxylic acid and its derivatives have been found as challenging candidates for the in situ ligand transformations. Oxidation hydrolysis, decarboxylation, oxalate formation, and hydroxylation of pyrazinecarboxylic acid derivatives as ligands have been reported under hydrothermal condition.18–23

Herein, we describe the in situ formation of 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid into 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate (L) and its 3D metal organic framework with CuII, [Cu5(C5H2N2O3)4(H2O)4(NO3)2·10H2O]n (1), under hydrothermal condition. Weng et al. also reported the in situ formation of 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate from decarboxylation and hydroxylation of 2, 3‐pyrazinedicarboxylic acid in hydrothermal condition.22

Results and Discussion

Compound 1 was obtained from the hydrothermal reaction of Cu(NO3)2 and 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid (molar ratio = 2:1) in an aqueous solution at 120 °C for three days. This compound is stable in air at ambient temperature and is almost insoluble in the most common solvents such as water, acetonitrile, chloroform, acetone, and toluene.

A series of parallel experiments [molar ratio of Cu(NO3)2:L = 2:1, 1:1, and 1:2; T = 120 to 210 °C in steps of 10 °C, t = 3 d] demonstrated that factors such as molar ratio and reaction temperature were important in isolation of the final crystalline product. Compound 1 can only be prepared using the molar ratio of reagents and temperature mentioned in the Experimental Section. Hydroxylation of aromatic rings and N‐heterocycle ligands,24–26 and decarboxylation and hydroxylation of 2, 3‐pyrazine dicarboxylic acid and pyrazine‐2, 3, 5‐tricarboxylic acid in the presence of NaOH has also been found in hydrothermal preparation of metal organic frameworks.21–23 Herein, deamination and hydroxylation of 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid occurred and then 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate, (L) formed a new 3D metal organic framework, 1, in the presence of Cu(NO3)2. Kim also prepared 3‐hydroxypyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid from 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid using diazotization at refluxed condition.27

Demetallation of 1 demonstrates in situ formation of the 3‐hydroxypyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid. Complex 1 was dissolved in HCl solution (3 M) and heated to 40 °C. The solution was allowed to stand at 50 °C overnight. The green color of the solution faded gradually and became colorless. The mixture was cooled and neutralized with NH4OH. The precipitate was filtered, washed with ice water, and dried to ligand as a colorless solid. The 1H NMR spectrum and also mass spectrum of the extracted ligand [(500 MHz, [D6]DMSO, 25 °C): δ = 7.71 (d, J = 4 Hz, 1 H), 7.86 (d, J = 3 Hz, 1 H), 11.51 (b) ppm; m/z = 140] clearly show in situ formation of 3‐hydroxypyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid.

The IR spectrum of 1 has a strong band at 1610 cm–1 due to the antisymmetric stretching of the carboxylate [νasym(COO–)] and symmetric stretching of the carboxylate [νsym(COO–)] appears at 1363 cm–1. The stretching vibrations of C–O bands appear at 1196 and 1233 cm–1. The IR spectrum of 1 exhibits the characteristic band corresponding to NO3–, which appears at 1384 cm–1. The broad band at 3435 cm–1 can be attributed to the O–H stretching of the coordinated and guest H2O molecules.28,29 The absorption bands were observed at 360 and 376 nm due to π–π* and/or charge transfer transition. The visible region of the spectrum for 1 displays a single broad band at 681 nm due to d–d transitions.30

The perspective view of 1 indicating atom‐numbering scheme is illustrated in Figure 1. The crystallographic data for compound 1 is summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths, and bond angles are listed in Table 2. Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 has 3D framework. Cu1 is six coordinated, displaying a distorted octahedral coordination environment (Figure 1). The equatorial positions are occupied by two oxygen atoms of L, nitrogen and oxygen atoms of another L, and the axial positions are both coordinated to the oxygen of nitrate ions. The bonding parameters (Table 2) clearly indicate the differences caused by Jahn‐Teller effect in the central Cu1 atom, elongation along the axial direction and compression around the equatorial positions. Such effect is widely observed in CuII complexes.31,32

Cu3 (Cu3 is on a symmetry center) is also six coordinated with four oxygen atoms from two L ligands and two oxygen atoms from coordinated H2O. The equatorial positions are occupied by four oxygen atoms from two L ligands. The axial positions are both coordinated to water molecules and show significantly longer bond lengths. The elongation of the axial Cu–O bond [Cu3–O15 2.440(4) Å] may be ascribed to the Jahn‐Teller effect.

Unlike Cu3 and Cu1, Cu2 is five coordinated to oxygen and nitrogen atoms from one L ligand and one oxygen atom from a water molecule, two nitrogen atoms from two different L ligands, resulting in a distorted square pyramidal arrangement. The structural index τ,33 which is a measure of square pyramidal distortion, is 0.23 for Cu2, indicating a distorted square pyramidal environment. However, the Cu2 atom has an additional weak interaction with the oxygen atom of the hydroxo (3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate). The Cu–O distance [Cu2–O3, 2.734(3) Å] is considerably longer than that of the other Cu–O bond lengths in 1.

In the asymmetric unit, L ligands can be divided into two distinct types. Type A ligand acts as a μ3‐bridge pentadentate mode to link two Cu1 atoms and one Cu2 atom, whereas type B acts as a μ3‐bridge to link two Cu2 atoms and one Cu3 atom. On the other hand, NO3– ligands link Cu1 atoms as a μ2‐bridge. The various coordination environments of the copper atoms and the interesting arrangements of L and NO3– ligands result in the formation of the 3D framework with channels, which are occupied by the H2O molecules. L (type B) is chelated to central Cu2 atoms through N4 and O8 atoms and is also linked to another Cu2 atom by N4 atom to from a chain along the c axis. This ligand is also chelated to central Cu3 atoms through O9 and O10 atoms, which leads to the formation of a 2D layered structure (Figure 2). This layer contains a 26 membered ring (Cu2–L–Cu2–L–Cu3– Cu2–L–Cu2–L–Cu3) extended along the bc plane (Figure 3). On the other hand, each Cu1 is linked to another Cu1 atom by two oxygen atoms of NO3– to form [Cu(NO3)]n chain along the c axis, where each [Cu(NO3)]n chain doubly links to neighboring chains by L ligands (type A) and these L ligands further link to Cu2 atoms by N1 resulting in the formation of the 3D framework (Figure 4).

To classify the net, we define the copper atom (Cu2), L (type A and B) as 3 connected nodes and another copper atom (Cu1) as 4 connected node. Then, complex 1 can be simplified as a (3, 4)‐connected four nodal net. This net topology is unprecedent with (6.7.9)LA(64.8.9)cu1(7.102)LB(72.10)cu2 Schläfli symbol.34

The crystal water molecules fill the channels and coordinated water molecules bind to each other and to the oxygen atoms of hydroxyl, carboxyl, and nitrate groups through hydrogen bonding (Table 3). The total solvent accessible volume of the channels in the unit cell is 654.1 Å3 (after removing of the lattice water molecules), which accounts for 30.1 % of the total cell volume as calculated by PLATON.35 However, the PXRD pattern of the sample after dehydration shows structural collapse of 1. It must be noted that all reported spectroscopic data here was performed on the sample before dehydration.

The thermogravimetric (TG) behavior of 1 was investigated in the temperature range of 25–800 °C with a heating rate of 10 K·min–1 in air (Figure 5). It exhibits two weight loss steps. The first weight loss of 12.96 % (calculated: 14.44 %) in the range of 30–126 °C corresponds to the release of guest water molecules. The decomposition of compound 1 starts beyond 230 °C with a total weight loss of 65.3 % (calculated: 67.90 %) at the second stage. The remaining weight of compound corresponds to the calculated value for CuO.

Conclusions

The in situ generation of 3‐hydroxopyrazine‐2‐carboxylate (L) from 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylic acid in the presence of copper(II) nitrate during hydrothermal syntheses leads to formation of a 3D metal organic framework, 1. Compound 1 presents a new (3, 3, 3, 4)‐connected 4‐nodal net with (6.7.9)(64.8.9)(7.102)(72.10) topology symbol. The result may facilitate the exploration of new ligand synthesis and construction of MOFs with novel topology. The thermal behavior of 1 exhibit two steps of weight losses and the final residue is calculated to be CuO.

Experimental Section

General: All reagents were analytical grade commercial products and were used without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed with a Heraeus CHN‐ORAPID elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Bruker Tensor 27 spectrophotometer. NMR spectra were obtained with a Bruker Avance DRX 250(250 MHz) spectrometer. Proton chemical shifts are reported in part per million (ppm) relative to an internal standard of Me4Si. UV/Vis spectra were obtained with a Shimadzu UV‐260 spectrophotometer. Thermogravimetric analysis was carried out with a Perkin‐Elmer Pyris thermal analysis system.

Synthesis of 1: A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (481 mg; 2 mmol), 3‐aminopyrazine‐2‐carboxylicacid (139 mg; 1 mmol), and H2O (9 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon‐lined reactor, which was heated in an oven to 120 °C for 72 h, then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 3 °C·h–1 to obtain green plate crystals. Elemental analysis for C20H16Cu5N10O22): calcd. C 22.53; H 1.51; N 13.14 %; found: C, 22.59; H, 1.48; N, 13.10 %. The crystals were dried at 40 °C at reduced pressure for 10 h before the elemental analysis and a crystal taken for X‐ray investigations before drying proved to contain ten molecule of H2O per formula unit. IR (KBr): = 3435 (b), 3298 (m), 1610 (s), 1570 (m), 1526 (m), 1499 (m), 1443 (m), 1423 (m), 1384 (s), 1363 (m), 1319 (w), 1233 (m), 1196 (s), 1089 (w), 946 (w), 820 (m), 621 (w), 506 (w) cm–1.

Structural Analysis and Refinement: Crystals of 1 suitable for X‐ray crystallography were obtained as described above. Diffraction data for 1 were collected with a STOE‐IPDSII diffractometer using graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data were collected and integrated using the STOE X‐AREA software package.36 A numerical absorption correction was applied using X‐RED and X‐SHAPE software.37 The structure was solved by direct methods (SHELXS‐97).38 The structure refinement was performed by a full‐matrix least‐squares method against F2 (SHELXL‐97). All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, all hydrogen atoms were inserted in the calculated positions.

Crystallographic data (excluding structure factors) for the structure in this paper have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre, CCDC, 12 Union Road, Cambridge CB21EZ, UK.
