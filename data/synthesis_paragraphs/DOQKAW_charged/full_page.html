Metal–organic replica of γ-Pu: the first uninodal 10-connected coordination network based on pentanuclear cadmium clusters†

Xin-Long Wang , Chao Qin , Ya-Qian Lan , Kui-Zhan Shao , Zhong-Min Su * and En-Bo Wang *
Institute of Functional Materials, Department of Chemistry, Key Laboratory of Polyoxometalates Science of Ministry of Education, Northeast Normal University, Changchun, 130024, China. E-mail: zmsu@nenu.edu.cn; wangenbo@public.cc.jl.cn

Received (in Cambridge, UK) 8th September 2008 , Accepted 13th October 2008

First published on 14th November 2008

The first uninodal 10-connected metal–organic framework, based on pentanuclear cadmium cluster building blocks, exhibits an unprecedented γ-Pu topology, which adds a new member to the series of metal–organic analogues which have a natural materials topology.

Current interest in coordination polymer frameworks not only stems from their potential applications in microelectronics, nonlinear optics, porous materials, and catalysis, but also from their intriguing variety of molecular architectures and topologies.1 An important subject within this area is the study and analysis of network topology, which is not only a powerful tool for simplifying the structure of complicated compounds but also plays an instructive role in the rational design of certain functional materials with desirable properties. To date, network topologies in coordination polymers have been discussed in several notable reviews2 following a seminal compilation by Wells decades ago.3 The analysis of data for existing materials reveals that the nets with high frequency are topologically symmetrical and, in particular, have small numbers of types of nodes and edges,4 such as 3-connected srs, 4-connected dia, 6-connected pcu, and 8-connncted bcu nets. Compared to these commonly encountered 3–8-conneted topologies, the development of higher connectivity nets (>8), neither with high-symmetry nor low-symmetry structures, is still in its early stages, which can be attributed, at least partially, to the limited coordination numbers of single metal centers and steric hindrance of the most commonly used organic ligands. To our knowledge, no uninodal 10- or 11- connected nets have been found in coordination network chemistry before this work, although 9- and 12-connected examples have been sporadically reported more recently.5 Not only do we lack knowledge for the construction of such unusual nodal geometries, but it is also necessary to build real crystal structures based on these nodes so as to find topological relations between them and related three-periodic nets.

On the other hand, an important feature of coordination network chemistry is its ability to produce metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) having identical topological paradigms with those of natural mineralsvia replacement of elements and element–element bonds with molecular building blocks and organic linkers, respectively, whereby extending the applied area for these materials. Thus far, the following prototypes have been successfully imitated in MOFs: 3-connected SrSi2 and perovskite, 4-connected diamond, feldspar, CdSO4, NbO, PtS, quartz, and sodalite, 6-connected α-Po and CaB6, 8-connected CsCl, 12-connected metals (Cu, Ag, Au), (3,4)-connected boracite, (3,6)-connected pyrite and rutile, (4,6)-connected Pt3O4, (4,8)-connected CaF2.6 However, no metal–organic replica of inorganic minerals with connectivity equal to ten has been reported to date.

One promising approach for the creation of highly connected topologies is to use polynuclear metal clusters as nodes; their large surface areas means that they can more readily accommodate the steric demands of organic linkers.7 Borrowing ideas from triazole–metal coordination chemistry,8 where because of the position of the donor atoms in the five-membered ring, the triazole ligands possess the possibility of linking metal ions together, and thereby constitute a bridge between the metal ions, we chose 1,2,4-triazole (Htrz) and benzotriazole (Hbtrz) as initial objects of study to generate high-nuclearity metal clusters. Meanwhile, to ensure the articulation of the clusters into an extended three-dimensional framework, a rigid, linear linker, 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2bdc), is used concurrently. Herein we report, for the first time, a ten-connected network based on dodecahedral building blocks, consequently filling a lacuna for uninodal coordination nets. Of particular interest is that the resulting 3D network is defined as the first metal–organic replica of γ-Pu, a structure type in minerals that has never been observed in metal–organic or organometallic compounds prior to this work.

Reaction of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with mixed ligands H2bdc, Htrz, and Hbtrz in N,N′-dimethylacetamide (DMA) solution yields homogeneous microcrystalline materials.‡ The as-synthesized compound was characterized and formulated by elemental microanalysis and single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies§ as [(CH3)2NH2]2[Cd5(bdc)4(trz)2(btrz)2] ( 1). The purity of 1 was confirmed by similarities between its simulated and experimental powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S1†). The structure of 1 contains pentanuclear cadmium clusters, in which each μ3-btrz interlinks three crystallographically unique Cd atoms, with non-bonding Cd⋯Cd distances of 3.591(2)–3.806(2) Å (Fig. 1). The Cd(1) atom lies on a two-fold axis and coordinates to two μ2-carboxylate oxygen atoms (Cd–O 2.379(4) Å) from two independent bdc ligands and four nitrogen atoms (Cd–N 2.284(5)–2.324(5) Å) from two trz ligands and two btrz ligands giving a distorted octahedral geometry. In contrast, the Cd(2) and Cd(3) atoms exist in a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometry, being ligated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms (Cd–O 2.277(4)–2.632(4) Å) from three different bdc ligands and two nitrogen atoms (Cd–N 2.255(5)–2.340(5)Å) from a trz ligand and a btrz ligand, and their symmetry-related atoms (Cd2A and Cd3A) are generated by the symmetry operation −x, y, 0.5 −z. It is interesting to note that there are two binding modes for the carboxyl groups of bdc ligands in 1, namely chelating fashion and tridentate bridge mode (Fig. S2†).


Fig. 1 The pentanuclear cadmium cluster of 1, which can be viewed as a dodecahedral building unit.

Each pentanuclear metal cluster of 1 is surrounded by fourteen organic ligands—eight bridging bdc, four bridging trz, and two terminal btrz ligands that are not essential parts of the connectivity of the network. Although each metal cluster is ligated by twelve bridging ligands, it is virtually linked to ten nearest neighbors, with distances of 9.010(4)–18.349(4) Å (center to center), because two pairs of ligands trz form two “double-bridges” (Fig. S3†). This, therefore, from the topological point of view, defines a ten-connected node. Thus, the 3D structure that is formed adopts an unprecedented uninodal ten-connected topology gpu (γ-Pu 10/3/o3) with the short Schläfli symbol of 312·426·57 (Fig. 2). To the best of our knowledge, only one example has been reported to be a uninodal ten-connected framework9 although a few binodal (3,10)-connected nets have been characterized.10 However, the material reported in ref. 9 is not really a ten-connected net in that it is only true if the isolated metal atoms in the framework are considered, and it is actually a binodal net involving 8-coordinate metal centers. Thus, compound 1 represents the first 10-connected topological network observed in coordination polymers.


Fig. 2 Ball-and-stick (left) and polyhedral (right) presentations of the gpu net.

To aid understanding of this intricate structure, the new approaches to the analysis of highly connected frameworks proposed by Hill et al.,11 are applied, namely visualization of the structures as combinations of interconnected 2D sheets or subnet tectons instead of considering 3D structures solely in terms of topology via“Schläfli” symbols. As a result, the ten-fold connectivity of 1 can be described as being formed from parallel 36 nets (in the crystallographic (001) plane with node–node distances of 13.80 and 18.35 Å), with each center providing four links (9.01 and 16.42 Å long) to four different centers in adjacent nets, two on each side (Fig. 3, left). Meanwhile, drawing special inspiration from Blatov’s statement “Knowing the relations between nets, one can find possible ways of transitions from one net to another”,12 we compare the 10-connected gpu net with the well-known 12-connnected face-centered cubic lattice fcu that is considered as a net relation graph generator because all other nets can be obtained as its descendants. The topological relationship between them is schematically illustrated in Fig. 3. Both can be described as being formed from parallel 36 nets with each center providing four or six links to centers in adjacent nets, half on each side. Their mutual transformations can be realized by breaking or forming net edges, for example, breaking the green dotted edges in the initial fcu net results in a gpu net.


Fig. 3 Relations between gpu and fcu nets.

Another characteristic structural motif in the net is the ellipsoidal shaped cage shown in Fig. 4. Twelve μ2-bdc, eight μ2-trz ligands, and sixteen terminal btrz ligands bridge eight pentanuclear cadmium clusters (a total of forty Cd atoms) to generate a large nanoscale [Cd40(bdc)12(trz)8(btrz)16] cage with the diameter of its largest inscribed ellipsoid being 13.9 × 9.0 Å. The cage can be considered to consist of eight triangles and two quadrangles, forming a 3842 cage building unit, in which [(CH3)2NH2]+ cations are located around the surface windows. The polyhedron of the 3842 cage contains 8 vertices, 16 edges, and 10 faces, meeting with the Euler rule of “edge + 2 = vertex + face”. Each cage behaves as a supramolecular building block, sharing its planar quadrangles with six neighboring cages. In this way, the 3D framework of 1 is generated (Fig. 4, right), and the assembly of such 6-connected nodes can be rationalized as having pcu topology (Fig. S4†).


Fig. 4 Structure of cage in 1 (left) and the 3D framework formed by cage-to-cage connections (right).

Encouraged by the single-crystal X-ray diffraction results which reveal the presence of pentanuclear cadmium [Cd5(1,4-bdc)4(trz)2(btrz)2] subunits in 1, photovoltage (PV) transients for 1 were recorded under atmospheric pressure and at room temperature.13PV transients of 1 at various intensities of exciting laser pulse (355nm) are shown in Fig. S5†. Although the intensities of the exciting laser pulses are different (0.05, 0.10, 0.20, and 0.30 mJ), the times of their PV maxima are similar (2.6 × 10−7 s). The maxima are related to the separation of charge at the 1/ITO (indium tin oxide) interface, and the gradient of excess electron and hole concentrations is caused by the non-homogeneous absorption of light with hv larger than the optical bandgap (Eg). Additionally, the PV transient signals are positive, which means that the photoexcited electrons move faster than holes towards the 1/ITO interface. It is known that a PV signal will arise whenever light-induced excess charge carriers are separated in space, and that PV transients can be used in the characterization of semiconductor materials.14 Thus, compound 1 may be a potential candidate for semiconductor materials due to its unique pentanuclear cluster subunits.

In conclusion, the first ten-connected MOF with γ-Pu topology, based on a pentanuclear cadmium cluster as a ten-connected node, has been synthesized and its photovoltage transient property characterized. The concept of using triazole ligands to construct high-nuclearity metal clusters proved to be feasible again. The key to the work reported herein is to provide a new avenue for generating novel high-connectivity networks by combination of linear linkers with relatively simple triazoles.
