A new 3D four-fold interpenetrated dia-like polymer: gas sorption and computational analyses†

Jian Wu a, Jian-Qiang Liu *b, Zhen-Bin Jia b, Qing-Lin Li b, Kai-Bang Li a, Hao Li a, Carole Daiguebonne c, Guillaume Calvez c and Olivier Guillou *c
aGuangxi Key Laboratory of Chemistry and Engineering of Forest Products, Guangxi University for Nationalities, College of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanning, Guangxi 530006, China
bSchool of Pharmacy, Guangdong Medical College, Dongguan 523808, China. E-mail: jianqiangliu2010@126.com

First published on 10th September 2014

A new metal–organic framework, namely, {[Cu(bib)2]·NO3·4H2O}n (1) (bib = 2,3-bis(4-pyridyl)butane), was synthesized and characterized. In the structure of 1, nitrogen atoms of four bib ligands bind to the tetrahedrally coordinated metal ion. The bib ligands act as linear bidentate linkers to form a four-fold interpenetrated 3D framework with a dia-like topology. The H2 uptake of the dehydrated coordination framework of 1 was estimated using a computational method based on Connolly's algorithm, which indicated that adsorbed H2 is predominantly located around the outer surface of the pore through multipoint interactions with the inner surface of dehydrated 1′. The material has no significant adsorption for CO2 or N2 gas upon desolvation by long-time thermal activation, indicating that access to the void space is blocked by the large and immobile anions. The luminescence properties of sample 1 and dehydrated sample 1′ were also explored.

Introduction

Materials known as metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), a term introduced in the early 1990s by Robson, Yaghi, Zaworotko and James, describe a class of porous polymeric materials that consist of metal ions linked together by organic bridging ligands.1 The porous yet rigid nature of these coordination networks has since then attracted substantial attention, mainly because of their potential to sustain permanent porosity with specific surface areas surpassing even those of the well-known zeolites and porous silicates. Their extraordinarily low densities and high surface areas make these materials extremely useful in a wide range of applications that range from adsorption, storage and separation of gases such as hydrogen or methane, capture of carbon dioxide, or heterogeneous catalysis, to name just a few.1 Thanks to the large variety of metal centers, commonly called nodes, and of organic bridging ligands, referred to as linkers, there is a virtually limitless possibility of combinations of secondary building units that can be assembled into interesting MOFs. This extreme diversity does also pose challenges, both for categorizing the different classes of MOFs, and for predicting which molecular architecture or topology might form from a selection of nodes and linkers under particular reaction conditions.2–6 Both of these problems have led to the establishment of a completely new subject in solid state sciences now commonly called “Reticular Chemistry”, which attempts to describe and explain the design of solid materials from molecular building blocks.

One gateway towards the formation of highly porous MOF-like materials is the use of long extended linkers. A side effect of this strategy is the possibility to also form interpenetrating networks, i.e. structures with entangled frameworks not connected through any covalent interactions. This subcategory of MOFs encompasses a variety of interesting topological species such as e.g. polyknot and polycatenation compounds. The formation of entangled coordination polymers is promoted not only by the use of elongated ligands but also by flexible organic linkers. For example, the flexible organic linker 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane is an excellent building block to construct different MOFs with well physical properties.7–11 Thus, one effective way to modulate the type of entangled topological motif is to vary the flexibility and length of the ligand. We have been interested in the syntheses and characterization of entangled MOFs containing the extended organic linker 2,3-bis(4-pyridyl)butane, which so far has led to the formation of 2D → 2D parallel interpenetrated frameworks with polyrotaxane and polycatenane character.12

The two main factors which affect the adsorption of gases in a microporous solid are: (a) the affinity of the surface for the adsorbates, and (b) the confinement effects generated within the cavities from the overlap of the potential energy surfaces of the guest molecules with the atoms of the host framework. The former factor can be controlled through functionalization of the MOFs, which has been actively investigated.13 The functionalization method utilizes e.g. linkers containing halogen atoms or amine, amide, or alkyl groups. Postsynthetic modifications have also been described. However, the above method is restricted by chemical incompatibilities of functional groups with the MOF assembly process, and also by the rigidity of the MOF itself. Recently, a few MOFs were prepared using flexible linkers.14,15 Extending our previous work on the self-assembly of novel topological coordination polymers based on the conformationally flexible bib ligand, herein we report the compound {[Cu(bib)2]·NO3·4H2O}n (1) (bib = 2,3-bis(4-pyridyl)butane), which comprises a 4-fold interpenetrated 3D framework with a dia-like topology. Furthermore, its gas adsorption and luminescence properties are discussed in detail.

Experimental

Materials and method

All reagents were purchased from commercial sources and used as received. IR spectra were recorded with a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum One spectrometer in the region 4000–400 cm−1 using KBr pellets. TGA was carried out with a Mettler-Toledo TA 50 in dry dinitrogen (60 mL min−1) at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) data were recorded on a Rigaku RU200 diffractometer at 60 kV, 300 mA with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å), with a scan speed of 2 °C min−1 and a step size of 0.013° in 2θ. Luminescence spectra for solid crystal samples were recorded at room temperature on an Edinburgh FLS920 phosphorimeter (USA). All the gas sorption isotherms were measured using a ASAP 2020M adsorption instrument.

X-ray crystallography

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis of the compound was carried out on a Bruker SMART APEX II CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using a ϕ/ω scan technique at room temperature. The intensities were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects as well as for empirical absorption based on multi-scan techniques; the structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 using SHEXL-97.16 Absorption corrections were applied using the multi-scan approach with the program SADABS.17 The unit cell volume includes a large region of disordered nitrate and water molecules which could not be modeled as discrete atomic sites, but their compositions can be confirmed by XPS, elemental analysis, IR and TGA. Thus, they are included in the final formula, as supported by other chemical analyses. The crystal structure analysis is, however, unambiguous in the context of connectivity. The core fragment of the ligand, consisting of two C–H and two methyl groups, is disordered over two alternative orientations. The disordered atoms were subjected to a rigid bond restraint. Subject to these conditions, the occupancies refined to 0.67(4) and 0.33(4). The nitrate ion located within the void space of the metal–ligand framework is highly disordered and/or incompatible with the tetragonal symmetry. It was included in the structural model using a rigid model, where it is disordered over four symmetry equivalent sites with each one a quarter occupancy, and it was refined as disordered with four water molecules. Results and discussion

Compound 1 was synthesized in a solvent system which contains methanol. 1 cannot be obtained at low temperature; the reaction was repeated with the same reaction conditions employed for the synthesis of 1 but with the temperature at 120 °C, which led to the formation of powder. Moreover, the pH value of the reaction solution does not play a role key in determining the final products, although we tried to adjust the pH (e.g. to 6 and 7.5) to different degrees. During the reaction process, the Cu(II) ions were reduced in situ to form Cu(I). The XPS results (932.8 eV Cu(I) character) indicate that Cu(I) is present in the coordination polymer of 1 (Fig. S1†). Compound 1 crystallizes in the tetragonal crystal system with the space group P4/n. In the asymmetric unit there are two bib linkers, one crystallographically independent Cu(I) centre, one nitrate anion and four water molecules. The Cu1 atom lies on the four fold symmetry axis (Fig. 1). The charge of the cationic framework is balanced by the guest nitrate anions. In 1, each Cu(I) ion is coordinated by four nitrogen atoms from four independent bib ligands (Fig. 1), or in other words, the bib linkers connect Cu(I) centers in four directions, generating a 3D porous MOF along the crystallographic c axis (Fig. 2a). The Cu(I) centers serve as 4-connected tetrahedral nodes to result in a dia topology with a point symbol of (62·62·62·62·62·62) (Fig. 2b).18 The spaciousness of the net leads to the interpenetration of four identical nets (Fig. 2c). Compound 1 comprises a four-fold parallel interpenetrating array to minimize the big void space and stabilize the framework. Viewed along the a axis, there are grid channels with a diameter of 10.1 × 8.9 Å. The effective free volume of 1, calculated by PLATON analysis, is 6.2% (of 1778.3 Å3 per unit) without consideration of the water molecules.19 Polymer 1 has an unusual 4-fold interpenetrated 3D architecture with helical channels (Fig. 3). To the best of our knowledge, complex 1 is the first example of this kind of 4-fold interpenetrated framework with only bib ligands (Fig. 4).

IR, thermogravimetric analyses and XRPD

As for the FTIR spectra (Fig. S2†), the compound shows a broad band centered around 3482 cm−1 which may be attributed to the O–H stretching frequency of the water molecules in 1. The O–H stretching vibration for Ic appears as a broad band centered around 3500 cm−1.20 The peak at around 1620 cm−1 is assigned to the υ(NO3−) asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations in 1.

To study the stability of the coordination polymer, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of complex 1 was performed (Fig. S3†). Compound 1 shows three weight loss steps. The first weight loss begins at 20 °C and is completed at 115 °C. The observed weight loss of 12.3% corresponds to the loss of the four crystallized water molecules (calcd 11.6%). The second weight loss occurs in the range 140–~220 °C and can be attributed to the elimination of one organic bib ligand. Removal of all organic components was completed by 429 °C, indicated by a further weight loss of 78.77% (calcd 78.13%), roughly consistent with expulsion of the bib and nitrate molecules.

To confirm the phase purity of the compound, the original sample was characterized by X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) at room temperature. The pattern simulated from the single-crystal X-ray data of compound 1 was in agreement with the observed experimental pattern, as shown in Fig. S4.† Heating of 1 at 120 °C for 4 h removes the free water to form the “evacuated” framework, defined as 1′. The XRPD pattern of 1′ is not similar to that of compound 1. Moreover, in the process the pale yellow color of sample 1 changed to the greenish-gray color of sample 1′, and the crystals of 1′ are brittle and can be easily broken. We tried to collect the single crystal XRD data of 1′, however, the sample 1′ diffracts much weaker than the original sample 1 and no usable single crystal XRD data could be obtained. We attribute this difference to a stabilizing effect of the guest water molecules in 1 through strong hydrogen bonding interactions with the nitrate anion. Loss of the water molecules can also be expected to lead to a more flexible and less rigid framework, thus leading to overall weaker diffraction.

Organic–inorganic coordination polymers, especially those with d10 metal centers, have been investigated for their fluorescence properties and potential applications as fluorescence-emitting materials, such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs).21 Therefore, the solid-state fluorescence properties of complex 1 were studied at room temperature (Fig. 5). Excitation of the microcrystalline sample at 346 nm leads to generation of fluorescence emission, with the peak maximum occurring at 585 nm for 1. The spectrum of 1′ is similar to that of complex 1, but the intensity is weaker and the main peak of 1′ is blue-shifted by about 28 nm compared to compound 1. The above luminescence phenomenon is presumably due to the more rigid nature of the architecture of 1, when compared to dehydrated 1′. In the crystal of 1, abundant lattice water molecules fill the voids of the network and interact with the framework lattice through weak forces such as hydrogen bonds. These weak forces not only stabilize the framework and enhance the rigidity of the structure, but they also affect the effectiveness of the transfer of energy from the bib ligand to the metal center. Accordingly, as guest molecules are removed from 1, the weak interactions undoubtedly decrease. Therefore, the emission becomes weaker, and the emission maximum is shifted. Moreover, this compound exhibits strong photoluminescence at room temperature and may be suitable as a candidate for luminescent materials.22


Fig. 5 View of the luminescence spectrograms at room temperature for 1 and 1′.

The fluorescence lifetime τ values of 1 and 1′ are on the nanosecond timescale at room temperature, as shown in Fig. S3.† The χ2 values are found to be close to 1.0 in both compounds. Interestingly, the complexes have lifetimes in the range of 4.2 ns for 1 and 2.8 ns for 1′ at the same excitation wavelength (λem = 346 nm), as shown in Fig. S5.†

Gas sorption properties

From Fig. 6, it can be observed that the amount of CO2 sorption rises gradually with increasing pressure from P/P0 = 0 to 1.0. This is not type I isotherm behavior which is typical for microporous materials, but is similar to type II or type III isotherms, as defined by the IUPAC classification.23 This unusual sorption feature could be attributed to one of two reasons: on the one hand, 1 is a cationic framework and its channels may be blocked by the large and immobile nitrate anions; on the other hand, the interaction between CO2 molecules and the cationic framework may be very weak. Comparison of the CO2 sorption curve with the N2 curve indicates that the latter reason can be discounted as the main cause of the weak adsorption of CO2 into the framework of 1. The highest N2 gas sorption amount shows 29 cm3 (STP)/g at P/P0 = 1.0 and 77 K, and exhibits no significant hysteresis between sorption and desorption traces, indicating that 1 is not a microporous material.24
Fig. 6 (a) Gas sorption isotherms for 1 at 195 K for CO2; (b) gas sorption isotherms for 1 at 77 K for N2.

To understand the solvent dependency of the gas adsorption behavior for this MOF, grand canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations using standard algorithms were performed to predict H2 adsorption isotherms. The simulation cell consists of 2 × 2 × 1 unit cells of the MOF. Periodic boundary conditions were applied in all three dimensions. For each state point, the GCMC simulation consisted of 1 × 107 steps to guarantee equilibration followed by 1 × 107 steps to achieve the desired thermodynamic properties. Interactions between H2 and the MOF are modeled by Lennard–Jones potentials between all pairs of sites. GCMC simulations were performed at 77 K,25 in the pressure range P = 0–1000.0 kPa. From Fig. 7 we can see that H2 uptake in the MOF containing solvent molecules is 10 mmol g−1 at 77 K and 1000 kPa, but it is 45 mmol g−1 in the MOF which doesn’t contain solvent molecules.


Fig. 7 Simulated adsorption isotherms for H2 at 77 K and P = 0–1000 kPa for the MOF with (left) and without (right) solvent molecules.

In order to understand the reason that the H2 adsorption of the MOF containing solvent molecules is lower than the MOF without solvent molecules, we calculated the Connolly surfaces of both MOFs. The calculated results show that the MOF surface area is reduced significantly from 734.83 Å2 to 189.72 Å2 by the solvent molecules. This conclusion can be confirmed by a molecular calculation (see Table S1 and Fig. S6†). This could suggest that upon dehydration, the molecular framework collapses. The XRD pattern and TGA curve confirm this hypothesis (Fig. S3 and S4†). The decomposition of sample 1′ starts at over 150°. Thus, 1′ shows little gas sorption at low pressure.

The bonding sites of H2 within the MOFs are given in Fig. 8. From Fig. 8(a), it can be seen that the first adsorbed H2 molecule is located at the centre of a group of solvent molecules, with an average distance between H and O atoms of 2.583 Å. The interaction between H2 and the MOF is mainly through van der Waals interactions with an energy of about −0.318 kcal mol−1. In comparison, Fig. 8(b) shows that the first H2 molecule is absorbed by a CH3 group of the ligand in the MOF without solvent molecules, and that the nearest distance between H and C atoms is 2.584 Å. The main interaction between H2 and the MOF is also due to van der Waals interactions (−0.204 kcal mol−1). Snapshots of the MOFs upon H2 adsorption at 1000 kPa pressure are shown in Fig. 9. For the framework containing solvent molecules, the negative potential localises around the solvent molecules and the majority of H2 molecules are located close to solvents. In contrast, for the framework without solvent molecules, the negative potential spreads along the edges of the pores and a number of H2 molecules are adsorbed around the ligands. Please note that the Cu scarcely absorbs any H2, whether for the MOF containing solvent or the MOF without solvent. In conclusion, the solvent molecules in the MOF reduce the surface area and negative potential ranges, leading to smaller amounts of gas absorbed in comparison to the desolvated MOF.26

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized a new 3D porous cationic framework constructed from a flexible N-donor ligand. It comprises a four-fold interpenetrated 3D framework with a dia-like topology. The compound exhibits strong photoluminescence at room temperature and may be suitable as a candidate for luminescent materials. The H2 adsorption isotherm of the dehydrated coordination framework of 1′ was estimated by Connolly's algorithm. We hope that the synthetic methods described herein will further facilitate the exploration of new types of multifunctional materials with interesting properties, including the combination of porosity and luminescence.
