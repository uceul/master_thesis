An anionic metal–organic framework constructed from a triazole-functionalized diisophthalate featuring hierarchical cages for selective adsorptive C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separation†

Zhen Liu a, Lingzhi Lv a, Yabing He *a and Yunlong Feng *ab
aCollege of Chemistry and Life Sciences, Zhejiang Normal University, Jinhua 321004, China. E-mail: heyabing@zjnu.cn
bInstitute of Physical Chemistry, Zhejiang Normal University, Jinhua, 321004, China. E-mail: sky37@zjnu.cn

Received 6th April 2017 , Accepted 26th April 2017

First published on 26th April 2017

Exploring organic linkers is very important to target porous metal–organic frameworks with desired structures and properties. In this work, we devised a triazole-functionalized bent diisophthalate ligand, 5,5′-(triazole-2,5-diyl) diisophthalate (H4L), which was used to successfully construct an anionic copper-based MOF ([Cu6(L)3(H2O)4(HCOO)]·Me2NH2+·20DMF) under solvothermal reaction conditions. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that the title MOF compound features three types of polyhedral cages with different shapes and sizes. More importantly, the title MOF compound after desolvation exhibits promising potential for industrially important C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separations. At 298 K and 1 atm, the C2H2 and CO2 uptakes reach 107.6 and 65.6 cm3 (STP) g−1, respectively. The adsorption selectivities are predicted to be 33.4–120.3 for an equimolar C2H2–CH4 gas mixture and 6.4–7.6 for an equimolar CO2–CH4 gas mixture at 298 K and pressures varying from 1 to 109 kPa. The highly selective adsorption of C2H2 and CO2 over CH4 might be attributed to the synergistic interplay between suitable pore sizes, functional active sites, such as open metal sites and Lewis basic nitrogen atoms in the pore surface, and the ionic nature of the framework.

1. Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), also known as porous coordination polymers (PCPs), constructed from self-assembly of suitable metal ions/clusters that serve as nodes and organic ligands that act as linkers are emerging as a rapidly expanding class of porous materials. Due to the adjustable shape, size, and chemical nature of their pores, MOFs exhibit a broad spectrum of potential applications including but not limited to gas storage and separation,1 chemical sensing,2 drug delivery,3 heterogeneous catalysis,4 protonic conductivity5 and MOF-based devices.6

Ligand engineering is very important to target some new porous MOFs with desired structures and properties since the structural characteristics of the organic ligands such as their coordination mode and geometrical configuration play a very critical role in determining the MOFs' final structures. In terms of designing organic ligands to construct porous MOFs for gas adsorption, recent advances have shown that organic linkers incorporating m-benzenedicarboxylate/isophthalate moieties are very promising in this regard because it has well been demonstrated that isophthalate moieties are very powerful subunits for generating polyhedral cages in the frameworks upon their coordination with the metal ions.7 In view of the above-mentioned considerations, we have recently constructed a series of linear as well as angular diisophthalate ligands to fabricate a variety of polyhedral cage-based MOFs for gas storage and separation.8 For example, a porous MOF ZJNU-54 based on an aminopyrimidine-functionalized bent diisophthalate exhibits three different types of cages and more importantly shows exceptionally high C2H2 and CO2 uptakes of 211 and 120 cm3 (STP) g−1 at 295 K and 1 atm.8e As a continuation of our work, by replacing the central six-membered aminopyrimidine ring with a five-membered triazole ring, we designed a triazole-functionalized diisophthalate, 5,5-(triazole-2,5-diyl) diisophthalate (H4L, Scheme 1), as our ligand. The choice of a triazole unit as a spacer between two isophthalate moieties is based on the following considerations. (1) Compared to the six-membered ring, the five-membered heterocyclic ring can render a much larger angle between the two terminal isophthalate moieties, thus providing a possibility of producing porous MOFs with different topological structures. (2) The triazole unit has abundant Lewis basic nitrogen atoms capable of enhancing the interactions of the framework with incoming gas molecules and thus the gas adsorption properties. Solvothermal combination of this ligand with Cu2+ ions yields an anionic MOF [Cu6L3(H2O)4(HCOO)]·(CH3)2NH2+·20DMF featuring hierarchical cages as revealed by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. It should be mentioned that during our work the ligand has been used by other groups to fabricate MOFs.9 For example, Liu et al. reported a copper-based MOF JLU-Liu21 derived from the same ligand, whose structure is however different from that of our compound.10 Herein, we wish to report the solvothermal synthesis, single-crystal X-ray structure, and selective gas adsorption properties of this MOF compound.

2. Experimental

2.1 Materials and methods

All of the chemical reagents were commercially available and used without further purification. The organic ligand (purity >95%) was purchased from Jinan Henghua Sci. & Tec. Co., Ltd. FTIR (Fourier transform infrared) spectra were recorded on a Nicolet 5DX FT-IR spectrometer using a KBr pellet method. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. TGA (thermogravimetric analyses) were performed in a nitrogen atmosphere using a Netzsch STA449C apparatus with a heating rate of 10 K min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were obtained using a Philips PW3040/60 automated powder diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.542 Å) in a 2θ range of 5–50°. Simulation of PXRD data was performed by using single-crystal data and the powder pattern module of the Mercury CSD software package (version 1.4.1). Topological analyses were performed using the TOPOS 4.0 program package. Gas adsorption isotherms were collected on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 surface area and porosity analyzer. The temperature of 77 K was maintained using liquid nitrogen, and other specified temperatures were controlled using a circulating water bath (Julabo F12ED). Highly pure gases (N2: 99.9999%, C2H2: 99.9% and CH4: 99.99%) were used for the measurements.

2.2 Single-crystal X-ray diffraction

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data were collected using a Bruker SMART APEX II with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 296(2) K. Data intensities were corrected by Lorentz–polarization factors and empirical absorption. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined using full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2. All calculations were performed with the SHELXT-97 program package.9 The solvent molecules are highly disordered, and attempts to locate and refine solvent molecules were unsuccessful. Therefore, the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON11 was used to remove the diffraction contribution from these solvents to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities. The structure was then refined again using the data generated. CCDC No. 1542148 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for the title MOF compound. Crystal and structural refinement data are summarized in Table S1 in the ESI.†

2.3 Synthesis and characterization of the title MOF compound

A mixture of H4L (5.0 mg, 12.6 μmol) and Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O (15.0 mg, 64.5 μmol) was dissolved in a mixed solvent of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 1.5 mL) and 6 M HCl (0.02 mL), and then the mixture was heated at 343 K for 4 days. The blue block-shaped crystals were collected, washed with DMF and dried in air. Anal. calcd. for C117H178Cu6N30O50 (%): C, 44.11; H, 5.63; N, 13.19; found: C, 43.72; H, 5.31; N, 13.40. Selected infrared (KBr, cm−1): 3420, 1665, 1548, 1425, 1340, 1103, 787, 752, 722, 681.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Synthesis and characterization

The title MOF compound was obtained as block-shaped crystals via a solvothermal reaction of the organic ligand and Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O in DMF under acidic conditions at 343 K for 4 days. The structure was characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. A good match of the experimental PXRD pattern with the one simulated from the cif file verifies the phase purity of the title MOF compound (Fig. S1†). It should be mentioned that the difference in reflection intensities between the experimental and simulated patterns might be due to the preferred orientation of the powder samples during the collection of the experimental PXRD data. TGA analyses show that approximately 44.96% weight loss occurs from room temperature to 562 K, which is attributed to the loss of solvent molecules, dimethylammonium counterions, and coordinated water molecules (calcd. 49.56%, Fig. S2†). Upon further temperature increases, the compound starts to decompose. Based on the single-crystal X-ray diffraction, TGA and microanalysis, the title MOF compound can be best formulated as [Cu6(L)3(H2O)4(HCOO)]·Me2NH2+·20DMF.

3.2 Structural description

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses revealed that the title MOF compound possesses a three-dimensional (3D) network crystallizing in the orthorhombic space group P4/mnc with unit cell parameters of a = b = 26.9768(15) Å and c = 39.206(3) Å. Apart from the guest molecules and counterions, the asymmetric unit consists of three Cu2+ ions (four crystallographically unique Cu2+ ions with occupancies of 1.0, 1.0, 0.5 and 0.5 for Cu1, Cu2, Cu3 and Cu4, respectively), one and a half of deprotonated ligands, two coordinated water molecules, and half of a formate ion (Fig. S3†). The formate ion originates from the in situ decomposition of DMF under solvothermal reaction conditions, which has been well established in the MOF literature. With regard to the organic ligands, two different configurations (λ-L and τ-L) were observed in the framework (Fig. 1a). For all ligands, the central triazole-ring nitrogen atoms are not involved in metal coordination, while all four carboxylate groups are deprotonated, each adopting a bidentate bridging mode (μ2:η1η1) to link two copper ions. Thus, the whole ligand has a μ8 connectivity to link 8 copper ions. As for the metal ions, all Cu2+ ions are five-coordinated but have different coordination atoms in their coordination geometries. Their equatorial sites are occupied by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different organic ligands, but the apical sites are defined by different molecules. Both Cu1 and Cu2, as well as both Cu3 and Cu4, are bridged by four carboxylate groups to form typical dicopper paddlewheel secondary building units (SBUs) Cu1Cu2(COO)4 and Cu3Cu4(COO)4, which are further connected by the organic ligands to form a complex 3D network. It is worth noting that formate ions were also involved in connecting adjacent Cu1Cu2(COO)4 SBUs in the network. Careful examination of the structure reveals that there exist three types of polyhedral cages with different sizes and shapes in the network (Fig. 1e). As shown in Fig. 1b, cage A with dimensions of ca. 21.9 × 22.8 Å2 (calculated using the Cu–Cu distances, the same as below) consists of 12 dicopper paddlewheel SBUs and 8 organic ligands (λ-L). Cage B of ca. 16.2 Å in diameter is formed by 12 dicopper paddlewheel SBUs and 24 isophthalate units (Fig. 1c). Cage C with an irregular shape is composed of 12 dicopper paddlewheel SBUs and 6 organic ligands (4 λ-L + 2 τ-L) (Fig. 1d). Viewing along the crystallographic a and b axes, there exists a one-dimensional channel with dimensions of 9.0 Å × 9.5 Å which is lined with open copper sites and Lewis basic nitrogen sites, while viewing along the crystallographic c axis, one larger one-dimensional channel of ca. 11.1 Å is observed, which is formed by the alternate packing arrangement of cage A and cage B through sharing four dicopper paddlewheel SBUs. PLATON analyses indicate that the total solvent-accessible volume is 69.3% of the volume of the unit cell (19762 Å3 of the 28532 Å3 per unit cell volume) after removal of the free solvent molecules, terminal water molecules, and disordered counterions. Topologically, the framework can be simplified into a (4,5)-c 4-nodal net with a Schläfli symbol of {3·5·64}2{32·4·52·65}2{4·52·63}{4·52·72·8} if the organic ligand is regarded as a 4-connected node, while the Cu1Cu2(COO)4 and Cu3Cu4(COO)4 SBUs are taken as 5-connected and 4-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. S4†).
Fig. 1 Single-crystal X-ray diffraction structure of the title MOF compound. (a) The organic ligand adopts two different configurations (λ-L and τ-L) in the framework; (b–d) view of the three different types of polyhedral cages; (e) the overall 3D packing of the three different types of polyhedral cages in the network. The terminal water molecules and formate ions are omitted for clarity.

3.3 Permanent porosity

To establish the permanent porosity, N2 adsorption–desorption measurement was performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD 88 surface area and porosity analyzer at 77 K. Prior to the measurement, the as-synthesized sample was guest-exchanged with dry low-boiling-point acetone followed by evacuation at 373 K until the degassing rate reached 2 μmHg min−1. The integrity of the framework after activation was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S1†). As shown in Fig. 2, the activated sample exhibits a pseudo-type-I adsorption behaviour, with a small hysteresis at the relative pressure range of 0.40–0.56. Based on the N2 adsorption isotherm, the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and Langmuir surface areas were calculated to be 1599 and 2291 m2 g−1, respectively (Fig. S5†). The pore volume calculated from the maximum amount of N2 adsorbed is 0.768 cm3 g−1. Compared to the theoretical value of 0.926 cm3 g−1 calculated using the PLATON program, the experimental pore volume is smaller, which is due to the disordered counterions occupying the pore space. The analyses of pore size distribution (PSD) by nonlocal density functional theory based on the N2 adsorption isotherm collected at 77 K reveal two main bands centred at 10.8 and 13.0 Å, which are close to the sizes of two one-dimensional channels (Fig. 2 inset).
Fig. 2 N2 adsorption–desorption isotherm of the title MOF compound at 77 K. The PSD profile is shown in the inset. The solid and open symbols represent adsorption and desorption, respectively. STP = standard temperature and pressure.

3.4 Selective gas adsorption

Establishment of permanent porosity, combined with the active functional sites such as rich nitrogen atoms as well as open copper sites on the hierarchical polyhedral cages, has encouraged us to evaluate the compound's performance for C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 separations, which are industrially important and scientifically challenging due to their similar molecular sizes and physical properties.1e,h,12 Accordingly, we systematically measured the C2H2, CO2 and CH4 adsorption isotherms at three different temperatures of 278, 288 and 298 K up to 1 atm. As shown in Fig. 3, no hysteresis phenomenon was observed for the three gases investigated, indicating that the adsorption and desorption processes are completely reversible. At 298 K and 1 atm, the C2H2 and CO2 uptakes reach 107.6 and 65.6 cm3 (STP) g−1 (Fig. 3a and b). Compared to those reported for traditional porous materials under similar experimental conditions,13 the C2H2 uptake of the title MOF compound is relatively high. Although the C2H2 uptake at 298 K and 1 atm is lower than those of top-performing MOFs such as ZJNU-47 (213 cm3 (STP) g−1),8d NJU-Bai17 (222.4 cm3 (STP) g−1),14 FJI-H8 (224 cm3 (STP) g−1),15 MFM-188 (232 cm3 (STP) g−1),16 and ZJU-12 (244 cm3 (STP) g−1),17 its value is higher than those for some reported MOFs such as ZIF-8 (25 cm3 (STP) g−1),18 MOF-5 (26 cm3 (STP) g−1),18 Cu2(pzdc)2(pyz) (42 cm3 (STP) g−1),19 UTSA-10 (43 cm3 (STP) g−1),20 [Zn2(trz)2]8(FA)4 (46.4 cm3 (STP) g−1),21 ZJNU-61 (48 cm3 (STP) g−1),22 Mg(HCOO)2 (65.7 cm3 (STP) g−1),23 BUT-52 (71.6 cm3 (STP) g−1),24 UTSA-33 (83.6 cm3 (STP) g−1),25 and BUT-70B (87.1 cm3 (STP) g−1)26 under similar conditions. When the temperature is lowered to 278 K, the C2H2 and CO2 uptakes increase to 147.1 and 101.9 cm3 (STP) g−1 at 1 atm, respectively (Fig. 3a and b). In sharp contrast to C2H2 and CO2 adsorption, the title MOF compound only adsorbs very limited amounts of CH4. The CH4 uptake capacities at 1 atm are 24.6 and 15.6 cm3 (STP) g−1 at 278 and 298 K, respectively (Fig. 3c). Such a discrimination of adsorption capacities enables the title MOF compound to be a very promising material for the selective separation of C2H2–CH4 and CO2–CH4 gas mixtures. To evaluate its separation performance, we estimated the adsorption selectivities for C2H2 and CO2 over CH4 employing ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST).27Fig. 4a and b present the predicted IAST adsorption selectivities for equimolar C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 gas mixtures using the title MOF compound as a function of bulk pressure, respectively. The selectivities for C2H2 and CO2 with respect to CH4 are 33.7 and 6.4, respectively, at 298 K and 1 atm, which increase to 44.6 and 8.0 when the temperature is lowered down to 278 K. Such impressive selectivities further confirm that this compound has considerable potential for the adsorptive separation of C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 gas mixtures. It should be mentioned that only a few ionic MOFs have been explored for C2H2/CH4 separation so far.8g,22,28
Fig. 3 C2H2 (a), CO2 (b) and CH4 (c) sorption isotherms of the title MOF compound at three different temperatures of 278 K, 288 K and 298 K. The solid and open symbols represent adsorption and desorption, respectively. STP = standard temperature and pressure.

Fig. 4 IAST-predicted adsorption selectivities for equimolar C2H2/CH4 (a) and CO2/CH4 (b) gas mixtures at three different temperatures.

The isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) for the MOF compound is estimated using the Clausius–Clapeyron equation based on the adsorption isotherms collected at 278 K, 288 K and 298 K, and the dependence of Qst values on gas loading is depicted in Fig. 5. The Qst values for C2H2, CO2 and CH4 at near zero coverage are found to be 35.3 ± 7.3, 29.6 ± 0.83 and 24.0 ± 0.35 kJ mol−1, respectively. The Qst of C2H2 adsorption is comparable to those values for organic functional group-modified NOTT-101 analogues (33.6–37.8 kJ mol−1),8a FJI-H8 (32 kJ mol−1),15 and MFM-188 (32.5 kJ mol−1).16 The Qst of CO2 is higher than those of PCN-88 possessing CO2 molecule traps in the framework (27.0 kJ mol−1)7d and NOTT-122 incorporating open copper sites and 1,2,3-triazole units (24.5 kJ mol−1).29 Importantly, it can be seen from Fig. 5 that the title MOF compound exhibits Qst values much higher for C2H2 and CO2 than that for CH4 through the overall adsorption process, suggesting that C2H2 and CO2 are bonded more strongly in the title MOF compound compared with CH4, thus responsible for the highly selective separation of C2H2 and CO2 over CH4. As demonstrated in the literature,8j the higher Qst values for C2H2 and CO2 relative to CH4 might be attributed to the Lewis acidic open copper sites and the basic sites (such as triazole-ring nitrogen atoms and carboxylate oxygen atoms) in the pore surface as well as the ionic nature of the framework.

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, an anionic MOF was synthesized by the solvothermal reaction of a custom-designed angular diisophthalate ligand and Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O. Structurally, the MOF compound incorporates three different types of polyhedral cages in the overall framework. In terms of selective gas adsorption, the MOF compound exhibits promising potential for selective adsorptive separation of C2H2–CH4 and CO2–CH4 gas mixtures under ambient conditions. The preferential adsorption of C2H2 and CO2 from these two gas mixtures is due to the immobilization of Lewis acidic copper sites and Lewis basic nitrogen and oxygen atoms on the pore surface, which enforces the interaction of the framework and the polarizable C2H2 and CO2 molecules. Considering that this MOF is anionic, further investigation of the effect of post-synthetic ion exchange on gas adsorption and separation is ongoing.
