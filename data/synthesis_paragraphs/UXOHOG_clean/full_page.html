Abstract

A new methyl‐functionalized metal–organic framework, {Cu3(CMPEIP)2·2H2O}n {m‐NB13, H3CMPEIP = 5‐[(4‐carboxy‐2‐methylphenyl)ethynyl]isophthalic acid} was solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. {Cu3(CMPEIP)2·2H2O}n exhibits a high BET surface area of 3215.4 m2 g–1, a large H2 uptake (1.92 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 77 K), and excellent CO2 storage capacity (9.24 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 298 K).

Abstract

A new methyl‐functionalized metal–organic framework, {Cu3(CMPEIP)2·2H2O}n {m‐NB13, H3CMPEIP = 5‐[(4‐carboxy‐2‐methylphenyl)ethynyl]isophthalic acid} is synthesized and characterized. {Cu3(CMPEIP)2·2H2O}n exhibits a high BET surface area of 3215.4 m2 g–1, a large H2 uptake (1.92 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 77 K), and excellent CO2 storage capacity (9.24 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 298 K).

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are porous crystalline materials with regular arrays of metal ions, or metal clusters, and organic linkers. Over the past decade, MOFs have been regarded as one of the most promising candidates for potential applications in areas such as gas storage and separation, catalysis, sensing, and drug delivery owing to their large surface areas, high pore volumes, controllable pore sizes, and tunable properties.1-9 Among them, gas storage and separation of H2, CO2, and CH4 have been the most investigated in recent years.10-13

To improve the gas adsorption performance of MOFs, the easy tunability of MOFs has been employed thoroughly. Ligand functionalization, regardless of the presynthetic approach or postmodification, works quite well towards specific gases if some functional groups are embedded into the framework.14-16 Considering the potential practical applications of MOFs, framework stability against water is critical in most scenarios. The introduction of hydrophobic groups to create a hydrophobic environment in the framework has been demonstrated as a possible way to achieve MOFs with water stability. As a hydrophobic functional group, the methyl group has become one of the most commonly studied.17-23 By creating smaller pockets (or pores) in the cages by methyl substitution, improvements in hydrogen adsorption enthalpy have been observed.21-23 The introduction of electron‐donating methyl groups has also been confirmed to benefit CO2 adsorption, for which hydrogen‐bond‐like interactions with the H atoms of the methyl groups play a dominating role in stabilizing the CO2‐loaded complex.17 Furthermore, on the basis of the different types of MOFs, an enhancement in water stability by methyl decoration has been reported.17, 19

To systematically investigate the influence of methyl groups on the gas adsorption and water stability of MOFs, on the basis of an agw‐type MOF of NJU‐Bai13,24 we deliberately introduced methyl groups in the 5‐[(4‐carboxyphenyl)ethynyl]isophthalic acid (H3CPEIP) ligand and successfully synthesized an isostructural analogue, {Cu3(CMPEIP)2·2H2O}n {H3CMPEIP = 5‐[(4‐carboxy‐2‐methylphenyl)ethynyl]isophthalic acid}, namely, m‐NB13 (m‐NB13 for methyl‐NJU‐Bai13) (Scheme 1). Although a small decrease in the surface area and porosity are observed in m‐NB13, it has a high Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 3215.4 m2 g–1, a large H2 uptake (1.92 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 77 K), and excellent CO2 storage (9.24 wt.‐% at 0.1 MPa and 298 K), which endow it with better adsorption capacities than NJU‐Bai13. These results indicate the positive effect of methyl group on gas adsorption.

Results and Discussion

Under conditions similar to those used to prepare NJU‐Bai13, green hexagonal plates of m‐NB13 were synthesized through solvothermal reaction of H3CMPEIP and CuCl2·2H2O in N,N‐dimethylacetamide (DMA)/methanol/H2O solvent system at 80 °C for 48 h. As expected, the structure of m‐NB13 is similar to that of NJU‐Bai13, and this is supported by the high degree of correspondence between the powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of m‐NB13 and NJU‐Bai13 shown in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). For better understanding of the inner environment of m‐NB13, single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction measurements were conducted, and they revealed that m‐NB13 crystallizes in the hexagonal P63/mmc space group. The asymmetric unit of NJU‐Bai13 consists of 1/2 CMPEIP3–, two types of crystallographically independent CuII atoms with occupancies of 1/4 and 1/2, and 1/2 coordinated H2O molecule. Interestingly, methylation reduces the symmetry of the ligands, and in the scope of the crystal the methyl group shares both sides of the ligands, each with occupancy values of 1/2 (Figure 1, a). The cupric paddlewheel clusters as well as the isophthalate moieties of the ligands are involved in the formation of two‐dimensional layered Kagomé lattices.3, 25 Trinuclear Cu3(O2CR)6 clusters and the remaining parts of the ligands play roles that interconnect the Kagomé layers to form a (3,4,6)‐connected agw‐type network26-28 (Figure 2). Three cages exist in the framework, as illustrated in Figure 1c by cage A, cage B, and cage C, and they have diameters of 10.6, 13.6, and 13.4 Å, respectively. Clearly, only methyl groups of cage B stretches into the pore, and thus, the pore sizes of cage B (diameter of 13.9 Å) are smaller than those in NJU‐Bai13.

To investigate the influence of the methyl groups on gas adsorption, the as‐synthesized sample of m‐NB13 was initially soaked in dry acetone for 3 days by refreshing the solvent every 8 h. The phase purity of the sample was confirmed by PXRD, and the framework retained its crystallinity after removal of the guest molecules under vacuum at 80 °C for 12 h (Figure S1). Then, the permanent porosity of m‐NB13 was determined by N2 adsorption at 77 K, as shown in Figure 3a. The N2 uptake amount of m‐NB13 was about 936 cm3 g–1 at 0.1 MPa. On the basis of the N2 adsorption isotherm, the BET surface area of m‐NB13 was calculated to be 3215.4 m2 g–1. Although it is smaller than that of prototypical NJU‐Bai13 (3337.0 m2 g–1) because of the incorporation of the methyl groups, this value still classifies m‐NB13 as a MOF with one of the highest BET surface areas.29 The total pore volume of m‐NB13 obtained from the N2 isotherm (P/P0 = 0.974) is 1.45 cm3 g–1, which is higher than that of some well‐known MOFs, such as PCN‐6130 (1.36 cm3 g–1) and NOTT‐10221 (1.14 cm3 g–1).

Low‐pressure hydrogen adsorption measurements of m‐NB13 were performed at 77 and 87 K. The adsorption isotherms are fully reversible (Figure S4), and the H2 uptake of m‐NB13 under the conditions of 77 K and 0.1 MPa was observed to be 1.92 wt.‐% (Figure 3, b). Clearly, the hydrogen uptake of m‐NB13 is higher than that of NJU‐Bai13 (1.79 wt.‐% at 77 K and 0.1 MPa), which indicates a positive effect of the methyl groups on hydrogen adsorption. Thus, on the basis of the adsorption isotherms measured at 77 and 87 K, the zero‐coverage hydrogen adsorption enthalpies of m‐NB13 and NJU‐Bai13 were calculated to be 6.16 and 6.13 kJ mol–1, respectively, which showed no marked difference. However, as the hydrogen amount was increased, the hydrogen adsorption enthalpy of m‐NB13 became clearly higher than that of NJU‐Bai13 (Figure S5), which further contributed to the higher amount of hydrogen uptake in m‐NB13. This may be correlated to the reactive sequence of the functional sites. Open metal sites are the primary sites that attract hydrogen molecules.11, 31, 32 Therefore, with the same quantities of open metal sites, the zero‐coverage H2 adsorption enthalpies of m‐NB13 and NJU‐Bai13 show no marked differences. As the amount of H2 molecules increases, most of the primary sites in the structures are occupied, and then the methyl groups start to create a discrepancy. The methyl groups in m‐NB13 create comparably smaller pores; this makes van der Waals interactions between the hydrogen molecules and the frameworks more feasible, and therefore, the smaller pores contribute to higher hydrogen uptake and comparably higher adsorption enthalpy.

The CO2 adsorption properties of m‐NB13 were also measured at 273 and 298 K (Figure S6). As shown in Figure 3 (c), at 298 K and 0.1 MPa, m‐NB13 absorbed 51.8 cm3 g–1 (or 9.24 wt.‐%) of CO2. This value is clearly lower than that of HKUST‐133 (20.4 wt.‐%) with a high density of open metal sites; however, it is still significantly higher than that of PCN‐6630, 33 (6.36 wt.‐%), PCN‐6833, 34 (4.84 wt.‐%), en‐CuBTTri35 (5.5 wt.‐%), Cu‐HBTB36 (5.0 wt.‐%), and UMCM‐137 (3.8 wt.‐%). Under similar conditions, its unmodified counterpart, NJU‐Bai13, could only adsorb 47.9 cm3 g–1 of CO2. Similar behavior was observed at 273 K, with values of 96.8 and 89.4 cm3 g–1 for m‐NB13 and NJU‐Bai13, respectively. To further understand the interaction between the CO2 molecules and the framework, we calculated the CO2 adsorption enthalpy of m‐NB13 by the virial method, and the zero‐coverage value was 22.01 kJ mol–1, which was higher than that of NJU‐Bai13 (21.40 kJ mol–1). Even at every loading, the CO2 adsorption enthalpy of m‐NB13 was higher than that of NJU‐Bai13, which indicates that the methyl groups also benefit the adsorption of CO2. As methyl groups are not polar functional groups preferred by CO2 molecules, the enhancement in the framework–CO2 interactions can be attributed to the electron‐donating nature of methyl groups that strengthen CO2–aromatic interactions.18, 38

We also measured the CH4 adsorption of m‐NB13 at 298 K, as shown in Figure 3 (d). The CH4 uptake of m‐NB13 at 298 K and 0.1 MPa was 13.1 cm3 g–1 (0.58 mmol g–1), which was higher than that of NJU‐Bai13 (11.8 cm3 g–1), Cu (IMTA)39 (< 0.4 mmol g–1), Cu(pmc)240 (<5 cm3 g–1), and some ZIFs.41 The similarity between CH4 and the methyl group also makes us believe that the incorporation of methyl groups may improve the CH4 adsorption capacity of m‐NB13.

The water stability of m‐NB13 was measured by directly exposing it to air with humidity of 80 %. As a comparison, the water stability of NJU‐Bai13 was also measured by the same method. As shown in Figure S9, NJU‐Bai13 lost most of the peaks in the XRD pattern after 2 days. However, for m‐NB13, although most of the peaks disappeared after 2 days, some new peaks gradually appeared and grew, which may represent the formation of a new phase. Thus, we can hardly reach the conclusion of improving the water stability of m‐NB13 by methylation.

Conclusions

In summary, we successfully designed and synthesized a highly porous three‐dimensional agw‐type metal–organic framework (m‐NB13) with a methyl group. Activated m‐NB13 has high porosity with a BET surface area of 3215.4 m2 g–1 and a pore volume of 1.45 cm3 g–1. Although a decrease in the porosity was observed relative to that of the prototypical MOF NJU‐Bai13, m‐NB13 still possesses higher adsorption capacities for H2, CO2, and CH4, which indicates the positive effect of methyl groups on gas adsorption capacity.

Experimental Section

Synthesis of m‐NB13: A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (17.05 mg, 0.1 mmol), H3CMPEIP (6.2 mg, 0.02 mmol), and DMA/methanol/H2O (5:3:1, 1.8 mL) was stirred for 10 min in air and was then sealed in a 20 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave, which was heated at 80 °C for 48 h. After cooling to room temperature, green hexagonal‐plate‐shaped crystals were obtained, yield 60.8 %.

Crystal Data: Single crystals suitable for X‐ray structure determination were selected and sealed in a capillary under a microscope. The X‐ray diffraction intensity data were measured with a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at room temperature by using graphite monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reduction was made with the Bruker Saint program. The structures were solved by direct methods and were refined with the full‐matrix least‐squares technique by using the SHELXTL package. Non‐hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters during the final cycles. Details of the crystal data for m‐NB13 are summarized here: C36H18Cu3O14·x(solv.), M = 865.15, hexagonal, P63/mmc, a = 18.868(4) Å, b = 18.868(4) Å, c = 51.015(15) Å, α = β = 90°, γ = 120°, V = 15728(9) Å3, Z = 6, Dcalcd. = 0.548 g cm–3, GOF = 1.086 based on F2, final R1 = 0.085, wR2 = 0.266 [for 5138 data I > 2σ(I)]; data for structure was treated with Squeeze. CCDC 1471812 (for m‐NB13) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre.
