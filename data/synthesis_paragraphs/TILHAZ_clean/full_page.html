Metal–organic frameworks constructed from flexible ditopic ligands: conformational diversity of an aliphatic ligand†

Tae Kyung Kim a, Kyung Joo Lee a, Min Choi a, Noejung Park *a, Dohyun Moon *b and Hoi Ri Moon *a
aInterdisciplinary School of Green Energy, Ulsan National Institute of Science and Technology, Ulsan 689-798, Republic of Korea. E-mail: hoirimoon@unist.ac.kr; noejung@unist.ac.kr
bBeamline Division, Pohang Accelerator Laboratory, San-31, Hyoja-Dong, Nam-Gu, Pohang, Kyungbuk 790-784, Republic of Korea. E-mail: dmoon@postech.ac.kr

Received (in Victoria, Australia) 23rd July 2013 , Accepted 23rd September 2013

First published on 24th September 2013

The solvothermal reaction of adipic acid as a flexible ditopic ligand and the metal ions MnII, CoII, and TbIII afforded three novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), {[Mn2(adipate)2(DMA)]} (1), {[Co2(adipate)2(DMF)]·1DMF·1.5H2O} (2), and {[Tb3(adipate)4.5(DMF)2]} (3) (DMA = N,N-dimethylacetamide; DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide), respectively, which were structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Depending on the kind of metal ion and solvent system, the conformations and coordination modes of the adipate ligands were diverse and governed the entire MOF structure. Compound 1 consists of the secondary building units (SBUs) of Mn–O chains that were linked by adipate ligands extending in two-dimensional sheets, which were infinitely stacked in a layer-by-layer manner. Compound 2 presented a three-dimensional MOF constructed from Co–O chains and bridging adipate ligands extending in four different directions. Compound 3 also had a three-dimensional structure which was formed by Tb–O chains connected with adipate ligands in six directions. From these structures, ten different adipate ligands with diverse conformations were found, and the potential energy of each conformation was calculated by the first-principles density function. In addition, the luminescence properties of the Tb-based MOF 3 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are well-ordered crystalline solids, which are composed of metal–oxygen coordinating clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) and organic linkers.1,2 A high degree of structural tunability via both the organic and inorganic building blocks enables the use of MOFs in a wide range of potential applications, including gas storage and separation, membranes, catalysis, sensing, and biomedical imaging.3–8 Because the interesting properties of MOFs are greatly influenced by their structures,9,10 intensive efforts have been devoted to the design of new MOF architectures. Critical factors in generating a variety of MOF structures include the shapes and sizes of the building blocks, solvent systems, and reaction temperatures. Among these, the introduction of organic building blocks with different geometries is a direct synthetic approach toward the construction of novel MOFs.1,2 When rigid organic ligands are utilized in the synthesis of MOFs, the formation of a predetermined network usually results. This strategy is well-developed and feasible for the construction of the target MOF structures. On the other hand, flexible organic ligands as molecular building blocks of MOFs can produce various unexpected structures by imparting a high degree of conformational freedom. In particular, aliphatic multitopic ligands allow diverse conformations due to the energetically facile rotation of central carbon chains, and thus during crystallization with metal ions, various possible geometries and coordination modes can be generated. In this context, we selected adipic acid (HOOC(CH2)4COOH), a linear dicarboxylic acid linked by a flexible four carbon chain, to construct new MOFs.

The conformations of adipic acid have been investigated due to its usefulness not only in polymer synthesis, but also, in model studies of peptide chains.11–14 In particular, to understand the structures and properties of adipates in different environments, co-crystal formation was conducted using pharmaceutical agents11–13 or water.14 Recently, Jones et al. demonstrated that the respective co-crystallizations of lamotrigine and caffeine with adipic acid were achieved through strong hydrogen bonding and the incorporation of solvent guest molecules.11,12 The Zhang group also discovered co-crystals of caffeine and adipic acid using a thermodynamically driven solution-mediated phase transformation.13 In addition, the conformational changes of adipate dianions encapsulated in water clusters were crystallographically characterized by the Dai group, and this result was helpful in understanding the hydration-mediated folding process of adipate at the molecular level.14 The other approach for the conformational study of adipic acid is the formation of MOFs through its use as an organic building block.15–20 To date, approximately 130 crystal structures of adipate-based MOFs (based on the Cambridge Structure Database) have been reported. Herein, we present three new MOFs which were constructed by the solvothermal reaction of adipic acid as a flexible ditopic ligand and the metal ions MnII, CoII, and TbIII. Depending upon the metal ions and solvent systems, the conformations and coordination modes of adipate ligands were diverse and governed the entire MOF structure. From these structures, ten different adipate ligands with diverse conformations were found, and the potential energy of each conformation was compared by computational studies. In addition, the luminescence properties of a Tb-based MOF, 3, were investigated in the solid state at room temperature.

Experimental section

General methods

All chemicals and solvents used in the syntheses were of reagent grade and they were used without further purification. Infrared spectra were measured using a Thermo Fisher Scientific Nicolet 6700 FT-IR spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed under a N2(g) atmosphere at a scan rate of 5 °C min−1 using Q50 from TA instruments. Elemental analyses were done at the UNIST Central Research Facilities center (UCRF) in Ulsan National Institute of Science and Technology (UNIST). The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) data were collected using a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer at 40 kV and 40 mA with copper radiation (Kα1 = 1.5406 Å and Kα2 = 1.54439 Å).

Syntheses

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

Single-crystals of 1 and 2 were coated with paratone-N oil and the diffraction data measured at 100 K with synchrotron radiation (λ = 0.749999 Å for 1 and λ = 0.75002 Å for 2) on an ADSC Quantum-210 detector at 2D SMC with a silicon (111) double crystal monochromator (DCM) at the Pohang Accelerator Laboratory, Korea. The ADSC Q210 ADX program21 was used for both data collection, and HKL3000sm (Ver. 703r)22 was used for cell refinement, reduction and absorption correction. The crystal structures of 1 and 2 were solved by the direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares calculations using the SHELX-TL (Ver. 2008) program package.23 The final refinement of compound 2 was performed with the modification of the structure factors for the contribution of the disordered solvent electron densities using the SQUEEZE option of PLATON.24 The single crystal of 3 was sealed in a glass capillary with the mother liquor. Diffraction data were collected at room temperature on an Enraf Nonius Kappa CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Preliminary orientation matrices and unit cell parameters were obtained from the peaks of the first ten frames and then refined using the whole data set. Frames were integrated and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects by using DENZO.25 The scaling and the global refinement of crystal parameters were performed using SCALEPACK.25 No absorption correction was made. The positions of all non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement factors. The hydrogen atoms were positioned geometrically and refined using a riding model. Computational studies

For computations, we used the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) to calculate the ground state of many electron systems in the framework of density functional theory.26–29 The plane-wave basis set with an energy cut-off of 400 eV and the PBE-type gradient-corrected exchange–correlation potential were employed.30 With the given lattice constant from the single crystal X-ray diffraction, the atomic structures are optimized. To evaluate the potential energy of the adipate ligands, metal–ligand structures surrounding each adipate ligand were extracted from the respective MOF structures. The total energy difference between the clusters with and without the ligation of the ten adipate ligands was counted as the potential energy.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

Compounds 1–3 were synthesized as single crystalline solids by the solvothermal reaction of adipic acid and the corresponding metal ions MnII, CoII, and TbIII. To obtain suitable crystals for single crystal structure determination, the solvent systems were optimised over multiple attempts. For compound 1, a DMA and methanol (4/1, v/v) solvent mixture, for 2, a DMF and ethanol (4/2, v/v) solvent mixture, and for 3, neat DMF, were used for crystallization. By elemental and thermogravimetric analyses, it was revealed that the methanol and ethanol were not incorporated in compounds 1 and 2; however, these solvents played important roles in crystal growth. It should be noted that the solvothermal reaction of adipic acid and CoCl2·6H2O led to a small quantity of polycrystalline solids being formed as purple lumps along with pink rod-shaped crystals of 2. Despite various efforts to synthesize monophasic samples of compound 2, the purple solids were unavoidably formed as minor products, and they were manually separated for elemental and thermal analysis, and XRPD measurements.

Infrared spectra results agreed well with the structural components determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction as well as elemental and thermogravimetric analyses. For the adipate ligands in the MOFs, the CH2 asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations appeared at 2948 and 2897 cm−1 for 1; 2955 and 2873 cm−1 for 2; and 2952 and 2862 cm−1 for 3, respectively. The carboxylate groups of the adipate ligand showed characteristic bands at ∼1590 cm−1 (asymmetric stretch) and ∼1400 cm−1 (symmetric stretch) for all compounds. In compound 1, the band for the carbonyl groups of the DMA guest molecules was observed at 1627 cm−1. The IR spectra of 2 and 3 displayed the presence of the guest molecule of DMF, whose carbonyl stretching band appeared at 1662 and 1667 cm−1, respectively. Structural description of 1

Single crystal X-ray diffraction studies revealed that {[Mn2(adipate)2(DMA)]} (1) crystallized in the monoclinic P21/c space group. The structure of 1 was composed of MnII–O chains as the secondary building units (SBUs) and adipate bridging ligands, forming 2D sheets. As shown in Fig. 1a, compound 1 possessed two crystallographically independent manganese atoms and adipate ligands. Mn1 was coordinated by six oxygen atoms from six different adipate ligands, resulting in a distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The Mn1–O bond lengths fell between 2.131(2) and 2.261(2) Å, and bond angles around Mn1 ranged from 77.00(7) to 177.62(8)°. Mn2 also formed six coordination bonds with oxygen atoms donated from four adipate ligands and one DMA solvent molecule, and the bond lengths and angles ranged from 2.068(2) to 2.380(2) Å, and from 56.56(7) to 163.74(8)°, respectively. In one adipate, designated as 1A, the central (CH2)4 chain adopted a nearly extended conformation [torsion angle C2A–C3A–C4A–C5A = –170.2(2)°] with a gauche–anti–gauche conformation, while the terminal carboxylate carbons were rotated to enable coordination with the metal centres incorporated in one Mn–O chain (Fig. 1b) [torsion angles C1A–C2A–C3A–C4A = −70.0(3)° and C3A–C4A–C5A–C6A = −64.1(3)°]. 1A-adipate adopted the coordination mode (a) described in Scheme 2 with (1111) connectivity, which indicated that each of the four oxygen atoms of the adipate unit was coordinated to only one manganese atom.31 On the other hand, 1B-adipate, which bridged the SBUs of the Mn–O chains (Fig. 1c), had folded central chains [torsion angles C2B–C3B–C4B–C5B = 68.5(3)°] with anti–gauche–gauche conformations, and adopted the coordination mode (b) described in Scheme 2, with (1222) connectivity. Thus, the Mn–O chain as the SBU comprised MnII, the 1A-adipate and the carboxylate groups of 1B-adipate, and was elongated toward the c axis. In the chain, the Mn⋯Mn distances bridged by the carboxylate groups were 3.34 and 3.59 Å. The SBUs were linked by 1B-adipate ligands (denoted in green in Fig. 1c) extended in the ac plane, and the 2D sheets were infinitely stacked along the b axis to construct the structure of compound 1.

Structural description of 2

The structure of {[Co2(adipate)2(DMF)]·1DMF·1.5H2O} (2), which crystallized in the space group I41/a, was composed of CoII–O chains as SBUs and adipate bridging ligands, which constituted a 3D network. As shown in Fig. 2a, compound 2 possessed two crystallographically independent cobalt atoms and adipate ligands. Co1 was coordinated by six oxygen atoms belonging to five different adipate ligands, resulting in a distorted octahedral coordination geometry: one carboxylate group chelated CoII to form a four-membered ring [O3B–Co1–O4B angle = 60.69(13)°]. The Co1–O bond lengths ranged from 2.046(3) to 2.198(3) Å, and the bond angles around Co1 ranged from 60.69(13) to 173.35(13)°. Co2 also formed six coordination bonds with oxygen atoms supplied by five adipate ligands and one DMF solvent molecule; the bond lengths and angles fell between 2.024(3) and 2.139(3) Å, and ranged from 77.49(13) to 177.55(14)°, respectively. In Fig. 2b, Co1 and Co2 were bridged by 2A-adipate and the carboxylato groups of 2B-adipate, which generated a cylindrical CoII–O chain. The cylindrical pore was not solvent accessible, and possessed a hydrophobic nature due to the exposed hydrocarbon chains of the 2A-adipate ligands. The central (CH2)4 chain of 2A-adipate was in the nearly extended conformation [torsion angle C2A–C3A–C4A–C5A = 175.4(4)°] with an anti–anti–gauche configuration. A C1A of one terminal carboxylate group in 2A-adipate had an extended conformation [torsion angle C1A–C2A–C3A–C4A = −177.2(4)°], while C6A of the other carboxylate group was twisted to coordinate with the metal centres incorporated in one Co–O chain (Fig. 2b) [torsion angle C3A–C4A–C5A–C6A = 74.6(6)°]. 2A-adipate adopted the coordination mode (c) described in Scheme 2 with (1212) connectivity. On the other hand, 2B-adipate, which bridged the SBUs of the Co–O chains (Fig. 2c), had extended chains [torsion angles; C2B–C3B–C4B–C5B = 179.6(4)°] with anti–anti–gauche conformations, and adopts the coordination mode (d) in Scheme 2 with (1112) connectivity. Similar to 2A-adipate, one carboxylate has an extended conformation [torsion angle C1B–C2B–C3B–C4B = 170.5(4)°], whereas, the other one was folded [torsion angle C3B–C4B–C5B–C6B = −65.8(6)°]. In comparison, the dihedral angles between the two carboxylate planes in each adipate ligand were 19.83(11)° for 2A-adipate, and 77.50(55)° for 2B-adipate. Co–O SBUs were connected with four neighbouring chains by 2B-adipate ligands in four orthogonal directions (Fig. 2c). As a result, a 3D {[Co2(adipate)2(DMF)]}n network was constructed with rectangular channels along the c-axis. In the chain, the Co⋯Co distances bridged by the carboxylate groups were 3.18 and 3.46 Å. PLATON calculations indicated that 2 contained 35.8% void space (3851.9 Å3 per unit cell volume) for guest molecule access per unit cell.

Structural description of 3

The colourless parallelepiped crystals of [Tb3(adipate)4.5(DMF)2] (3), of space group P21/n, were synthesized from a DMF solution of Tb(NO3)3·5H2O and adipic acid that was heated at 100 °C for 24 h. The asymmetric unit of 3 contained three Tb ions, 4.5 adipate ligands, and two coordinating DMF molecules. The adipate ligands comprised six different adipates, three whole and three half adipate ligands. As shown in Fig. 3a, the three TbIII ions were bridged by carboxylato groups to form a 1D TbIII chain as a SBU that extended along the [101] direction. The intrachain Tb⋯Tb distances were 4.042 Å for Tb1⋯Tb2, 3.897 Å for Tb2⋯Tb3, and 4.020 Å for Tb1⋯Tb3′. Tb1 was coordinated to one oxygen atom of a coordinating DMF, and seven oxygen atoms of six carboxylato groups, including one chelating carboxylate [O1A–Tb1–O2A angle = 52.46(13)°]. Tb2 was coordinated to one oxygen atom of a coordinating DMF, and eight oxygen atoms of six carboxylato groups, including two chelating carboxylates [O1C–Tb2–O2C and O1C′–Tb2–O2C′ angles = 50.59(12)°]. Tb3 was coordinated to nine oxygen atoms of six carboxylato groups, including three chelating carboxylates [O1D–Tb3–O2D angle = 52.40(12)°; O3D–Tb3–O4D angle = 50.42(12)°; O1E–Tb3–O2E angle = 51.82(12)°]. The carboxylic O–Tb bond distances ranged from 2.282(4) to 2.643(4) Å, and the Tb–ODMF distances were 2.350(5) Å for Tb1–O1G, and 2.397(5) Å for Tb2–O1H. The SBU of the 1D TbIII chain was linked with six neighbouring chains via adipate carbon chains, forming a 3D framework (Fig. 3b and c). The planes comprising adipate alkyl chains ran parallel to the (11), (010), and (11) planes, and met across the 1D TbIII chains, with dihedral angles of 56°, 68° and 56°.

Six kinds of adipates had different conformations as well as various coordination modes. 3A-adipate had a nearly extended conformation [torsion angle C2A–C3A–C4A–C5A = 167.3(7)°] with anti–anti–anti configuration, and adopted the coordination mode (d) in Scheme 2 with (1112) connectivity. The terminal carbons, C1A and C6A, of the carboxylate groups were not twisted and were in the extended conformation of the central chain [torsion angles C1A–C2A–C3A–C4A = 178.0(6)° and C3A–C4A–C5A–C6A = 179.5(6)°]. In 3B-adipate, the central (CH2)4 chain was in a nearly extended conformation [torsion angle C2B–C3B–C4B–C5B = 168.4(6)°] with a gauche–anti–gauche conformation, while the terminal carboxylate carbons were rotated to link two SBUs (Fig. 3b) [torsion angles C1B–C2B–C3B–C4B = 55.6(8)° and C3B–C4B–C5B–C6B = −72.3(8)°]. 3B-adipate adopted coordination mode (d) with (1112) connectivity. The central (CH2)4 chain of 3C-adipate had a perfectly extended conformation [torsion angle C2C–C3C–C3C′–C2C′ = 180.0(0)°] with a gauche–anti–gauche configuration, and adopted the coordination mode (e) with (1212) connectivity. Due to the existence of a centre of symmetry on the C3C–C3C′ bond, the torsion angle of C1C–C2C–C3C–C3C′ was the same as that of C3C–C3C′–C2C′–C1C′ at 71.8(9)°, which was twisted from the central chain. On the other hand, 3D-adipate had a significantly rotated central (CH2)4 chain, with a torsion angle for C2D–C3D–C4D–C5D of −57.8(8)° with an anti–gauche–anti conformation. Two carboxylates of 3D-adipate coordinated Tb ions with the (e) mode and (1212) connectivity, and were extended from the central chain [torsion angles C1D–C2D–C3D–C4D = −169.5(5)° and C3D–C4D–C5D–C6D = −176.5(6)°]. Similarly to 3C-adipate, 3E-adipate had a perfectly extended conformation [torsion angle C2E–C3E–C3E′–C2E′ = 180.0(0)°] with a gauche–anti–gauche mode, and adopted coordination mode (e) with (1212) connectivity. The torsion angle between carboxylate carbons and the central chain was rotated by −65.0(9)°. Finally, 3F-adipate also had 180.0(0)° as the torsion angle for C2F–C3F–C3F′–C2F′ with a gauche–anti–gauche configuration, but adopted the coordination mode (a) with (1111) connectivity. Two carboxylates were rotated to connect the SBUs with torsion angles of −75.7(11)° for both C1F–C2F–C3F–C3F′ and C3F–C3F′–C2F′–C1F′. The void volume that may be generated after removal of the coordinating DMF molecules was 24.4% of the total volume as estimated using PLATON.15

Optical properties of 3

It is well-known that the lanthanides, especially europium and terbium, absorb ultraviolet radiation efficiently through an allowed electronic transition and are converted to the excited state 5D4. These excited states are deactivated to the multiplet 7FJ states radiatively via the emission of visible radiation, the phenomenon known as luminescence.32,33 The luminescence properties of Tb-based MOF 3 were investigated and its solid-state photoluminescence spectra upon UV irradiation at room temperature were presented in Fig. 4. There are two emission groups for compound 3 in the ranges 360–450 nm and 450–650 nm (λex = 350 nm). The latter emission (450 to 650 nm) was attributed to the typical peaks of the TbIII ion, which can be assigned to the excitation of 5D4 to 7FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, and 3).32,33 The emission from 360 to 450 nm might arise from a ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).
Fig. 4 Photoluminescence spectra of as-synthesized compound 3 (black solid line) and desolvated solid 3 (blue dashed line), excited at 350 nm.

For comparison, the desolvated solid of compound 3 was prepared by heating at 220 °C under vacuum for 20 h. In the desolvated compound, the DMF molecules that coordinated TbIII ions were completely removed as evidenced by IR spectroscopy, in which the CO stretching of DMF at 1667 cm−1 disappeared. Upon photoluminescence measurements under the same conditions as the as-synthesized compound 3, the desolvated solid also emitted in two regions. Whereas the emission ranging from 450 to 650 nm for the desolvated compound was the same as that for the as-synthesized compound 3, which is typical for lanthanide compounds, the λmax for the emission from LMCT was red-shifted from 422 to 450 nm. This might have resulted from the change in the chemical environment of the fluorophore upon loss of the coordinating DMF molecules.

XRPD

As shown in Fig. 5, the measured XRPD patterns of as-synthesized compounds 1, 2, and 3 were compared with simulated patterns based on the single crystal X-ray diffraction data. The XRPD patterns of compounds 1 and 3 were coincident with the corresponding simulated patterns, which implied that the bulk samples were the same as the single crystals. However, compound 2 displayed several mismatched and split peaks, especially those which were assigned as (101) and (211) reflections, between the measured and the simulated data. This might be attributed to the coexistence of the small amount of polycrystalline solids (purple lumps) as a minor product along with the pink crystals of compound 2. Another reason for the split in the peak can be the irregularities in the lattice orientation throughout the structure.

Thermogravimetric analysis

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on compounds 1–3 to investigate their guest molecule compositions and the thermal stabilities of the frameworks. In the TGA trace of as-synthesized 1 (Fig. S4, ESI†), a temperature of around 280 °C was necessary to remove coordinating DMA molecules from Mn2, with the experimentally determined weight loss, 18.3%, showing good agreement with the calculated one (18.0%). This was followed by the decomposition of 1. In the TGA trace of as-synthesized 2 (Fig. S5, ESI†), the guest solvent molecules occupying the channels could be removed over the range from room temperature to ca. 150 °C, with a calculated weight loss of 17.3%. This showed the differences compared with the experimentally determined weight loss of 22.2%, which might be attributed to the small amount of purple impurity. To remove coordinating DMF molecules from Co2, a temperature of around 300 °C was required, followed by decomposition of 2. The TGA results of compound 3 showed complete removal of all coordinating DMF molecules at 220 °C (calcd 11.5%, found 11.7%); and no chemical decomposition occurred up to 370 °C (Fig. S6, ESI†).

Computational study for conformational diversities of adipate

In the present work, ten different adipate ligand conformations were directly detected by single crystal X-ray diffraction. The coordination modes and the torsion angles of the carbon chains were discussed in the respective structural description sections and summarized in Table 2. To investigate the geometry and binding strength of each adipate ligand onto the coordinated metal centers in each MOF structure, we performed the first-principles density functional calculations. With the given lattice constants as obtained from the X-ray diffraction measurement the atomic geometries were optimized. A large cluster part around the ten adipate ligands was extracted from the respective MOF to simulate the bulk MOF structures. The full geometry optimization well converged into each of the ten adipate ligand structures in good agreement with the experiments. The relative potential energy was measured by comparing the total energy of the cluster with and without the ligand. The computation results, as shown in Fig. 6 and Table 2, showed that 3B-adipate has the lowest potential energy, and 3A, 3C, and 3D-adipate reveal relatively high potential energy levels. Diverse factors are thought to affect the stability of adipate ligand binding configuration. As discussed in the previous sections, the end carbons of carboxylates were rotated to coordinate with metal centers, and it resulted in the various torsion angles of successive four carbon atoms in –CH2–CH2–CH2–COO−. In other words, the conformations of molecules varied with the sp3 carbon linked to the sp2 sites. However, such a change in the shape of adipate ligands didn't raise the measurable difference in the energy. We explicitly calculated the energy difference for the ten adipate ligands, and found that the energy differences between the configurations were smaller than 1.4 kcal mol−1. The coordination number of the metal center, torsional angles, and the increased metal–oxygen bond lengths incurred by the distortion in the metal sites seem to interplay to constitute the potential energy.

Conclusions

In summary, three metal–organic frameworks were synthesized under solvothermal synthetic conditions by reacting adipic acid and the metal ions, MnII, CoII, and TbIII, respectively. Because of the flexibility of the adipate ligand, various MOF structures were constructed, and the conformations and coordination modes of ten different adipate ligands in the frameworks were discussed in detail. The potential energies of those ligands constructing the MOFs were also examined in terms of binding strengths by first-principles density functional calculations. According to the calculation, various conformational adipate ligands, which showed significantly different potential energy (maximum 87.6 kcal mol−1), were crystallized in a structure. Therefore, the introduction of other sorts of metal ions and solvents, and various synthetic conditions might be expected to generate a number of novel structures of MOFs as well as diverse conformations of adipate ligands with different stability. From the point of view that adipic acid is investigated as a model of peptide chains, its conformational understanding upon metal binding should provide important information for the prediction of interactions between peptides and metal ions in vivo.
