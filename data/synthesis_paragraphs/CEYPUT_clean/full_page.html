A chiral porous cobalt–organic framework based on reinforced sinusoidal-like SBUs involving in situ-generated formate†

Zongze Li‡ , Lin Du‡ , Jie Zhou , Lin Li , Yan Hu , Yongfeng Qiao , Mingjin Xie and Qihua Zhao *
Department of Chemistry, Key Laboratory of Medicinal Chemistry for Natural Resource, Ministry of Education, Yunnan University, Kunming, 650091, P.R.China. E-mail: qhzhao@ynu.edu.cn; Fax: +86 871 5032929; Tel: +86 871 5032929

Received (in Montpellier, France) 1st March 2013 , Accepted 28th April 2013

First published on 1st May 2013

The assembly of Co(NO3)2·6H2O and achiral isonicotinic acid (HIN) under solvo(hydro)thermal conditions yields a three-dimensional chiral Co-based MOF, {[Co6(μ3-OH)2(IN)4(HCOO)6]·4DMF·5H2O}n (1). Polymer 1 consists of a reinforced sinusoidal-like hydroxyl–formate–carboxylate mixed-bridged chain as a rod-shaped secondary building unit (SBU), which is constructed from two types of asymmetric triangular Co3 clusters. This compound also has open irregular channels in the framework and presents a severely distorted pcu net based on two different Co3 clusters of SBUs as six connected nodes. The presence of a few drops of water in the DMF reaction solution is important in in situ HCOO− generation, which is beneficial for the formation of 1. Compound 1 is further characterized by IR, EA, PXRD, TGA-DSC, N2 adsorption, and magnetic measurement.

Introduction

The rational design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has constantly attracted significant attention because of their structural diversities and fascinating topologies; these materials also exhibit promising applications as new materials in various processes, such as catalysis, magnetism, molecule sensing, gas storage, separation and drug delivery.1,2 Numerous structural features, such as framework stability, surface area, pore size and shape, and nature of building blocks, have important functions in designing MOFs for such applications.3 One of the efficient synthetic strategies to generate MOFs is the use of metal–carboxylate clusters known as the secondary building units (SBUs) that can be interconnected via rigid organic linkers, and many examples of porous materials based on discrete SBUs (triangles, squares, tetrahedra, etc.) have been reported.4 Similar studies involving infinite rod-shaped SBUs (linear, ladder-like, and helical rods) remain largely unexplored.5 Current studies on MOFs have indicated that such one-dimensional (1D) rod-shaped SBUs can prevent the porous framework interpenetration and rigidify the framework. Such SBUs also exhibit intriguing magnetic properties that can be manifested in the whole framework. For example, MOF-74 and its isoreticular structures with uniform etb topologies are constructed from helical rod-shaped metal oxide SBUs; 2,5-dihydroxy-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2DHBDC) and its progressively longer links have pore apertures with dimensions ranging from 14 Å to 98 Å.6 In addition to the typical MOF-74, a novel Fe-based MOF containing a paddle wheel dimeric unit and a rod-shaped Fe-chain as two distinct SBUs have been reported to show antiferromagnetic interactions via a new magnetic exchange pathway.7 However, prediction and assembly of novel MOFs based on rod-shaped SBUs remain challenging because the same starting materials can result in completely different framework structures depending on the reaction conditions.

Studies have been devoted to chiral MOFs because these materials can be applied in enantioselective catalysis, molecular recognition, and non-linear optics.8 Several approaches can be employed to prepare chiral materials, such as introducing enantiopure ligands, adding chiral co-agents, and conducting spontaneous resolution without any chiral auxiliaries.9 In this study, a chiral Co-based MOF was obtained from achiral components by using the third approach. One of the first examples to achieve an enantiomerically pure crystallization of a chiral MOF built from achiral building blocks was published in 1999 by Aoyama and co-workers.10 However, such a phenomenon has been rarely observed because spontaneous resolution from achiral precursors only occurs occasionally and the resulting MOFs normally contain a racemic mixture of two enantiomorphs.

In this study, a chiral porous MOF {[Co6(μ3-OH)2(IN)4(HCOO)6]·4DMF·5H2O}n (1) based on sinusoidal-like rod-shaped SBUs is described. The mechanism by which the presence of water in the DMF solvent directly affects the synthetic products from the reaction between Co(NO3)2·6H2O and HIN (IN− = isonicotinate) is also reported. Remarkably, a Co-based hydroxyl–formate–carboxylate mixed-bridged SBU with triple links between two different triangular Co3 clusters, namely, Co3(μ3-OH)(μ2-COO)4(μ2-O) and Co3(μ3-OH)(μ2-COO)3(μ2-O)2, is present. The IN− ligand links neighboring 1D SBUs to form a three-dimensional (3D) pcu framework containing irregular channels. This ligand also enhances the stability of infinite SBUs by the strong ligation between two adjacent crests and troughs of a sinusoidal-like chain. In contrast to other cobalt–isonicotinate MOFs based on triangular Co3 cluster and 1D rod-shaped SBUs,11 complex 1 represents a new 3D chiral material with different structural topology constructed from unique reinforced sinusoidal-like SBUs involved in situ-generated formate.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

Polymer 1 was prepared in a bright red block crystalline form from the solvo(hydro)thermal reaction of HIN and Co(NO3)2·6H2O in a mixed DMF (10 mL) and H2O (two drops) solution. In situ-generated HCOO− participates directly in the formation of the new chiral porous framework of 1. After Co(NO3)2·6H2O reacted with HIN in 10 mL of fresh DMF under the same conditions, purple block-shaped crystals were isolated from the solution. X-ray crystallography analysis of one of these crystals reveals a twofold interpenetrated diamond network, {[Co(IN)2]·DMF}n (2) (Fig. S1, ESI†). The Flack parameter [+0.07(4)] was indicative of the correct absolute structure. The structure of 2 is almost the same as that of a previously reported threefold interpenetrated diamond network, [Co(IN)2]n,12 except complex 2 crystallizing in the space group Pn instead of P43212 and free DMF molecules filling the square channels. Guest DMF molecules in the channels have an important function in controlling the degrees of interpenetration in these two frameworks.13 However, we will not discuss complex 2 in detail below. The same reactants were treated with excess H2O (1, 3, or 5 mL) in DMF, yielding an unknown gray precipitate. These experimental results demonstrate that a few drops of water can facilitate thermal decomposition of DMF solvent during synthesis to produce the required HCOO− ligands necessary to assemble 1 (Scheme 1).

Structure of {[Co6(μ3-OH)2(IN)4(HCOO)6]·4DMF·5H2O}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray study reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic chiral space group P212121 with a Flack parameter of +0.010(12), indicating enantiomeric purity of the single crystal. As depicted in Fig. 1, the asymmetric unit of 1 has six crystallographically independent Co(II) ions, two hydroxyl groups, four deprotonated IN− ligands, six formate anions, four unligated DMF molecules, and five lattice H2O molecules. All of the Co(II) ions have hexa-coordinated slightly compressed octahedral geometry but exhibit four different types of coordination environments (Table S1, ESI†). A summary of selected bond lengths and angles of atoms within the coordination spheres is also given in Table S2 in the ESI.†
Fig. 1 The molecular structure of complex 1 indicating the coordination and linkage modes of Co(II) ion and ligands. Hydrogen atoms and free guest molecules have been omitted for clarity.

The Co–O/N bond lengths are found within the normal range of 2.026(3) Å to 2.175(3) Å, which are comparable to the bond lengths of other Co(II) cluster-based complexes.14 As observed in the reported isonicotinate-based coordination polymers, the IN− ligand taking the μ3-κ3O:O′:N coordination fashion bridges three Co(II) ions using all its heteroatoms. The formate anion adopts the frequent syn,syn,anti-κ3O:O′:O′ mode to connect three Co(II) centers with the Co–O–Co bond angles being in the small range of 93.385(122)° to 113.588(134)°. HCOO− anion generated from in situ hydrolysis of DMF provides the necessary building blocks for 1 because of its minor steric hindrance; this anion is closely associated with the kinetics of crystallization.15

Each hydroxyl anion links three adjacent Co(II) ions in a typical μ3 coordination mode to build two types of asymmetric triangular trinuclear cobalt units [Co3(μ3-OH)(μ2-COO)4(μ2-O) (A) and Co3(μ3-OH)(μ2-COO)3(μ2-O)2 (B); Fig. 2a]. The central O atoms of μ3-OH in A and B are displaced by 0.5788(29) and 0.7353(27) Å out of the trigonal Co3 planes, respectively. The dihedral angle between these two Co3 planes is 56.109(27)°. The neighboring Co3 triangles A and B are triply linked by three μ3-HCOO− moieties, forming a sinusoidal-like hydroxyl–formate–carboxylate mixed-bridged Co(II) chain along the b direction. This chain contains two repeat units (–A–B–) per complete rotation (Fig. 2b). The sinusoidal-like Co(II) chains can be viewed as infinite rod-shaped SBUs, which provide effective means to prevent the interpenetration of the final framework of 1.4a,16 The most important structural property is that the μ3-IN− ligands reside between two adjacent crests and troughs of the sinusoidal-like chain to form a more stable motif. Such a packing arrangement of reinforced sinusoidal-like SBUs constructed from two asymmetric triangular Co3 clusters is unprecedented in a crystal structure. These sinusoidal-like chains are also extended farther with a nearest-neighbor Co(II)⋯Co(II) distance of 9.0137(11) Å between the two adjacent chains to build a 3D framework with the aid of μ3-IN− linkages (Fig. 2c). Irregular channels are also found running along the b axis with disordered guest molecules filling the void space. Several porous MOFs based on rod-shaped SBUs have been reported, in which the rigid bridging ligands provide the walls of the square channels.17 By contrast, the walls of the irregular channels in this study were completed by the rigid IN− and the sinusoidal-like SBUs. The wall-to-wall separations across the channel are ca. 8.1 and 10.3 Å, whereas the corner-to-corner distances are ca. 12.3 and 14.7 Å. Hence, the cavities within the material are of considerable capacity. Each 1D rod SBU functions as either walls or corners in the formation of six neighboring irregular channels. In complex 1, 47.5% of the void space (2899.3 Å3 out of the 6108.2 Å3 unit cell volume) is accessible to the solvent molecules (as calculated using the program PLATON).18

(b) Structural representation of the reinforced sinusoidal-like hydroxyl–formate–carboxylate mixed-bridged Co(II) chain (SBU). Topological analysis19 was performed to understand the complicated structure of complex 1. In this study, each Co3 cluster (A or B) is connected to three distinguishing Co3 units (B or A) by one IN− ligand and six formate anions in the rod-shaped SBU. Each Co3 cluster is also connected to three other Co3 clusters (B or A) originating from different adjacent SBUs by three bridging IN− ligands. Thus, these Co3 clusters can be regarded as six connected nodes. The IN− ligand and HCOO− anions can be considered as linkers. The combination of two different six-connected Co3 clusters (A and B) and the bridging linkers resulted in a non-interpenetrated 3D network with pcu topology (Fig. 2d). The severely distorted pcu net of 1 is attributed to the significant differences in the distances between the six connected nodes and the angles around the nodes.

TGA-DSC and PXRD

The TG curve of 1 under flowing Ar gas shows a multistep weight loss (Fig. 3). Two evident stages of weight loss of 25.02% at <267 °C correspond to the loss of three unligated DMF molecules and three lattice water molecules per formula unit (calcd 24.65%). Afterward, the resulting porous framework begins to decompose, accompanying the combustion of organic ligands. One endothermic peak at 278 °C and one exothermic peak at 441 °C are shown in the DSC curve. The measured PXRD patterns are also consistent with simulated patterns derived from single-crystal analysis, indicating the reasonable crystalline phase purity of the bulk sample (Fig. S2, ESI†). The difference in reflection intensities may be attributed to the variation in the preferred orientation of the powder samples during data collection.
Fig. 3 TGA-DSC for complex 1.

N2 adsorption properties

The permanent porosity of activated framework (1a) was established by N2 adsorption at 77 K (Fig. 4). The activated sample was prepared by vacuum heating as-synthesized 1 at 150 °C for 7 h. The porous framework remained stable upon activation based upon comparison of the PXRD pattern to the simulated one (Fig. S2, ESI†). N2 adsorption isotherms of 1a exhibit a typical type IV isotherm with a maximum N2 uptake of 246 cm3 g−1 at approximately 0.99 P/P0. This behavior indicates the presence of mesopores in the activated sample in addition to the micropores resulting from the crystal structure of the MOF. The calculated pore size distribution is given in Fig. S3, ESI.† The total pore volume is 0.374 cm3 g−1. The BET and Langmuir surface areas are approximately 771 and 1016 m2 g−1, respectively. Such surface areas of 1a are higher than those of trinuclear Co(II) MOFs (Langmuir surface area = 544 m2 g−1 for Co-INA-1 and 842 m2 g−1 for Co-FINA-1).11a An even higher N2 adsorption capacity is expected for 1a at high pressures because of the unsaturated adsorption amount.
Fig. 4 N2 adsorption isotherms with typical type IV behaviors of the activated sample, 1a, at 77 K.

Magnetic studies

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility of complex 1 was determined at an applied field strength of 1 kOe at a temperature range of 2 K to 300 K. The temperature-dependence of χmT and 1/χm plots are shown in Fig. 5. The room-temperature product of χmT (18.96 cm3 K mol−1) is higher than that of six uncoupled S = 3/2 Co(II) cations (11.22 cm3 K mol−1). This finding is expected for an orbital contribution. After the temperature is decreased, χmT decreases continuously until a minimum of 4.13 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K is reached. Best-fitting the susceptibility data between 25 and 300 K yields a Curie constant C = 22.07 cm3 K mol−1 and a Weiss temperature of θ = −51.76 K. This finding is consistent with the expected result of high-spin Co(II) in an octahedral site.20 The large negative value of θ and the decrease in χmT indicate the occurrence of strong antiferromagnetic exchange interactions between Co(II) centers. To probe the low-temperature magnetic properties of 1, isothermal field-dependent magnetizations M(H) and ac susceptibility measurements were performed (Fig. S4, ESI†). Magnetization of this complex per Co6 unit at 2.0 K reaches 5.91 Nβ at 70 kOe without achieving saturation. In addition, no frequency-dependence and peaks are found in the in- and out-of-phase ac susceptibility signals at 2.0 K. Despite the 3D structural features, complex 1 can be described magnetically as an isolated 1D sinusoidal-like chain because the large Co(II)⋯Co(II) separation across the IN− linker excludes an efficient direct exchange between Co(II) ions.
Fig. 5 Temperature dependence plots of χmT and 1/χM products at 1 kOe for complex 1.

Conclusions

In summary, a rare example of the spontaneous asymmetrical crystallization of 3D porous chiral MOF was formed, in which the sinusoidal-like rod-shaped SBUs were reinforced and interlinked by μ3-IN− ligands. Complex 1 is considered as a severely distorted pcu net. An open irregular channel with occupied and disordered guest molecules is observed. Magnetic studies have shown the dominant antiferromagnetic coupling within 1D sinusoidal-like Co(II) chain. The influence of the amount of water on the formation of the final product has also been investigated. The results of the present study provide basic information to construct novel MOFs involving in situ-generated formate from DMF hydrolysis.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All of the reagents and solvents used for synthesis were of analytical grade and available commercially. C, H, and N elemental analyses were conducted using an Elementar Vario ELIII analyzer. The IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the region from 400 cm−1 to 4000 cm−1 by using an FTS-40 IR spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetric (DSC) measurements were performed with argon by using a NETZSCH STA 449F3 simultaneous thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded using a Bruker D8-Advance diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å) at a scan speed of 1° min−1. N2 adsorption isotherms were obtained at 77 K by using a Micromeritic TriStar 3020 adsorption apparatus. Magnetic measurements of the crystalline samples were performed on a Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 SQUID magnetometer equipped with a 7 T magnet. The diamagnetic corrections for the compounds were estimated using Pascal's constant, and an experimental correction for a sample holder was applied.

Synthesis of {[Co6(μ3-OH)2(IN)4(HCOO)6]·4DMF·5H2O}n (1)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (58.2 mg, 0.2 mmol) and HIN (24.6 mg, 0.2 mmol) was treated in DMF (10 mL) and deionized H2O (two drops) under ultrasonic irradiation to produce a clear solution. The resulting mixture was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined reaction vessel, heated at 140 °C for 3 d, and slowly cooled to room temperature. Bright red block crystals of 1 were handpicked under a microscope and washed by DMF several times, yielding 32% based on HIN. Elemental analysis (%) for C42H62Co6N8O31 (1528.58): calcd C 33.00, H 4.09, N 7.33; Found C 33.04, H 3.91, N 6.99. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3297 s, 3215 s, 2970 w, 2897 w, 2345 w, 2312 w, 1589 vs, 1380 vs, 1225 w, 1061 w, 829 w, 771 m, 694 s. Two infrared bands of approximately equal intensity at 2970 and 2897 cm−1 are attributed to the C(O)–H stretching vibration, which confirms the presence of disordered DMF molecules.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 1 and 2 were collected at 298 K by using a Bruker Smart AXS CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program.21 The structures were determined using direct methods and then refined to convergence by a full-matrix least-squares method on F2 by using the SHELXL-97 software.22 All of the non-hydrogen atoms were easily found in different Fourier maps and then refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. All of the hydrogen atoms of the pyridine rings and formate ligands were located in the calculated positions and then refined isotropically with fixed U values by using a riding model. In 1, the guest molecules within the pores of the framework are extremely disordered and cannot be satisfactorily modeled. Therefore, the SQUEEZE routine implemented in PLATON18 was applied to remove the contribution of the solvent electron density, and the outputs from the SQUEEZE routine were added to the CIF file. The final chemical formula was estimated from the SQUEEZE results combined with the EA and TGA results. The details on crystallographic data and structure determination are summarized in Table 1. The selected bond lengths and angles of 1 are listed in Table S2 (ESI†). CCDC-871835 (for 1) and CCDC-921457 (for 2) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper.
