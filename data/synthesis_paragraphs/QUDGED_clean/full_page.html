Abstract

Two Zn‐based metal‐organic frameworks (Zn‐MOFs) were solvothermally synthesized using two pyridine‐based carboxylate linkers to generate unique pore topologies, as evidenced by single‐crystal XRD analysis. These Zn‐MOFs provided coordinatively unsaturated Zn2+ sites with square‐pyramidal geometry, leading to an enhanced interaction with CO2, as demonstrated by sub‐atmospheric pressure isotherms. The presence of open Zn2+ sites in these MOFs was validated by Raman spectroscopy under atmospheric CO2, revealing the presence of CO2 chemisorbed on surface sites. Additionally, in situ DRIFT spectra of chemisorbed acetic acid provided evidence for the formation of acetate‐Zn2+ species after adsorption compared to IRMOF‐1, which showed only physisorbed acetic acid species.

Abstract

Zn‐based MOFs with unique pore structures were solvothermally synthesized, while imparting open Zn2+ centers that can interact with CO2 molecules.

Introduction

Zn‐based metal‐organic frameworks (Zn‐MOFs) have been employed as versatile functional materials for applications in gas storage1 and catalysis.2 Due to the myriad functionalities inherent to the organic linkers used in Zn‐MOF syntheses (e.g., –CO,3 –NH2,1f,4 –OH,1b,5 –CHO,6 –CN,7 and –OCH38), the acidity/basicity,3 polarity,9 and coordinatively unsaturated metal sites3,9 can be controlled for various applications (Table S1). Recently, a pyridine‐containing organic linker (2,2′‐bipyridine‐5,5′‐dicarboxylate, BPDC) was synthesized and used to form an Al‐based MOF (MOF‐253), in which dicarboxylates of BPDC were coordinated to Al centers.10 This framework motif provided free pyridine functionalities for the coordination of either metal precursors [e.g., PdCl210b and cis‐Pt(DMSO)2Cl210a] by post‐synthetic modification or CO210b from simulated flue gas. To the best of our knowledge, however, there have been few reports detailing the use of 2,5‐pyridinedicarboxylate (PDC) and BPDC for the synthesis of Zn‐MOFs, while incorporating free pyridine functionalities. One such example exists for the synthesis of ruthenium‐incorporated MOF‐1.11 However, all of the pyridine sites on the organic linkers in Ru‐MOF‐1 were coordinated to Ru compounds {i.e., cis‐[Ru(bipyridine)2Cl2]} prior to MOF‐1 synthesis, thus eliminating free pyridine functionalities in the resulting MOF structure.11 Although a few reports exist for the creation of various unique Zn‐MOFs using BPDC as a ligand, the resulting pyridine functionalities were coordinated to Zn centers (Table S2).12 Therefore, we studied the synthesis of Zn‐MOFs that could resemble either IRMOF‐113 or IRMOF‐10,13 with the ultimate goal of creating free pyridine functionalities by using PDC or BPDC ligands. This manuscript highlights the synthesis of two unique Zn‐MOFs (ZnMOF‐PDC and ZnMOF‐BPDC, Figures 1 and 2) and their detailed characterization.

Results and Discussion

For this contribution, the Zn‐MOFs were solvothermally synthesized using PDC and BPDC as organic linkers with Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and N,N′‐dimethylformamide (DMF) as the Zn source and solvent, respectively. Although it was possible to incorporate free pyridine functionalities in these Zn‐MOFs, the presence of Zn coordination to pyridine functionalities was also highly likely, as noted in other reports.14 As evidenced by the single‐crystal XRD results (Figures 1 and 2), we synthesized two new Zn‐MOFs (ZnMOF‐PDC and ZnMOF‐BPDC) with quite different pore structures. However, we observed the presence of Zn coordination to the pyridine functionalities with both PDC and BPDC ligands.

A single crystal of as‐synthesized ZnMOF‐PDC was observed as the primitive, centrosymmetric, monoclinic space group P21/n (Table S3). It should be noted that there are both six‐coordinate octahedral Zn species and five‐coordinate square‐pyramidal Zn species, yielding an accessible coordination site within the structure (labelled as Zn2 in Figure 1a and b). In addition, the expansion of the ZnMOF‐PDC asymmetric unit revealed a three‐dimensional network (Figure 1c). The void volume within the lattice was observed as 620 Å3, 26 % of total unit cell volume (2410 Å3) after the elimination of uncoordinated DMF from the lattice (Table S3).

Although a synchrotron radiation source was used to enhance the X‐ray intensity for the single‐crystal XRD analysis of ZnMOF‐BPDC, the data quality was low. Despite the low resolution of the dataset and the utilization of a synchrotron source to aid in obtaining data, the connectivity is unequivocal. This might be ascribed to either the disorder of the as‐synthesized ZnMOF‐BPDC or the guest solvent (DMF) within the void space of its lattice. Therefore, the contribution of the disordered solvent to the structure was removed from the data to allow a more feasible model of the core of ZnMOF‐BPDC to be determined (Figure 2a and b). From this analysis, it did provide clear evidence of the atom connectivity between the Zn center and BPDC linker. The structure of ZnMOF‐BPDC was determined as part of the sub‐standard monoclinic space group I2/a (Table S4), while revealing the presence of two five coordinate Zn centers (square‐pyramidal Zn1; coordinativley saturated trigonal‐bipyramidal Zn2) incorporated in its asymmetric unit (Figure 2a and b). This asymmetric unit propagated through the three dimensional network within the lattice (Figure 2c). A void space analysis of the ZnMOF‐BPDC structure using PLATON15 and Mercury16 revealed a void volume of 8500 Å3, accounting for 63 % of the total unit cell volume (Table S4, 13600 Å3). This void space is illustrated in Figure 2c, where the cavity can be observed. A topological analysis of the two structures revealed that ZnMOF‐Py forms a [4.82] [4.85] 3,4‐c bi‐nodal network. In contrast, ZnMOF‐BPy forms a [43.62.8] 4‐c uni‐nodal network.17 The successful formation of three‐dimensionally expanded frameworks was also demonstrated by powder XRD analysis. As shown in Figures 3 and 4, the XRD patterns were in agreement with corresponding XRD patterns simulated based on their unit cell parameters.

Notably, in spite of using the same BPDC linker as previous reports, the condition used for this ZnMOF‐BPDC synthesis was different from those of previously reported ZnMOF‐BPDC materials (e.g., molar ratio of Zn source and BPDC linker, solvent, temperature, and time in Table S2).12 These results provided evidence that the synthesis conditions played a large role in obtaining the specific crystal structure of each ZnMOF‐BPDC material.12 Therefore, aside from showing differencees in the XRD patterns (Figure 4), the structural difference among the reported ZnMOF‐BPDC materials was further evidenced by their single‐crystal XRD studies (Table S2), yielding different unit cell parameters, different base units, and different atom connectivities.12

Importantly, as previously mentioned, both Zn‐MOFs revealed the incorporation of open Zn2+ sites with penta‐coordination (one per base unit for both Zn‐MOFs). The open Zn2+ center, which has been incorporated into several Zn‐MOFs3,18 [e.g. MOF‐74 (Zn)19 and MOF‐220], has been of high interest due to its potential to bind and/or capture CO2,3,18a,19a,19b H2S,21 and NH3.22 Herein, to demonstrate the ability of these open Zn2+ centers to bind with CO2, the Zn‐MOFs were thermally desolvated at 170 °C for 6 h under an Ar atmosphere.4a,4b This step was used to eliminate any residual, uncoordinated DMF and H2O, allowing CO2 more access to open Zn2+ sites present in the porous material. As compared with the XRD pattern of as‐synthesized ZnMOF‐PDC, desolvated ZnMOF‐PDC showed a decrease in peak intensities and a slight shift in peak locations (Figure 3), which was attributed to the change in the porous geometry of the Zn‐MOF in the presence of guest solvent.23 The interaction of open Zn2+ centers inherent to desolvated ZnMOF‐PDC with CO2 was demonstrated by its low‐pressure CO2 isotherms measured at –10, 2, and 24 °C (Figure 5).

This interaction was determined using the isosteric heat of adsorption (–QST)18b determined as ca. 17 kJ mol–1 at near‐zero coverage of CO2 (Figure S3). In addition, the amount of CO2 adsorbed on ZnMOF‐PDC was compared with those of other Zn‐MOFs (IRMOF‐1,13 IRMOF‐3,13 and MOF‐220) desolvated under identical conditions as that to prepare desolvated ZnMOF‐PDC (Table 1). These IRMOFs were selected due to similarities in the size of the organic linkers incorporated in these Zn‐MOFs.13 MOF‐2 was also considered because it contains open, penta‐coordinated Zn2+ centers (Figure S4).20 Notably, the amount of CO2 adsorbed on ZnMOF‐PDC at 24 °C and a PCO2 of 1 bar was measured as 1.73 mmol CO2 g–1, which was higher than those of IRMOF‐1 (1.44 mmol CO2 g–1) and IRMOF‐3 (1.54 mmol CO2 g–1), despite the fact they possessed relatively higher porosity than that of ZnMOF‐PDC (Figure 5 and Table 1). Additionally, although IRMOF‐3 contains free primary amine functionalities, the steric effects of –NH2 occupying the pores limits its accessibility to CO2.24 Although MOF‐2 contains over twice the number of open Zn2+ centers (4.36 mmol Zn2+ g–1 vs. 1.87 mmol Zn2+ g–1 for ZnMOF‐PDC), MOF‐2 had a fairly low CO2 capacity of 0.61 mmol CO2 g–1 (Figure 5 and Table 1), which is likely attributed to the smaller micropore volume (0.07 cm3 g–1) of MOF‐2 than that of ZnMOF‐PDC (0.18 cm3 g–1) and limited accessibility of CO2 to open Zn2+ sites of MOF‐2.25 These results demonstrated that a MOF with more accessible sites, such as ZnMOF‐PDC, can outperform other MOFs that have enhanced physicochemical properties.

In contrast, desolvated ZnMOF‐BPDC exhibited an amorphous powder XRD pattern (Figure 4), which indicated the structural deformation of ZnMOF‐BPDC during thermal desolvation. Even with a supercritical CO2 (Sc CO2) extraction process at 150 °C and PCO2 of 7.5 MPa for 30 min, the structural deformation was not prevented for ZnMOF‐BPDC (Figure 4). Interestingly, regardless of the low porosity (Table 1), ZnMOF‐BPDC (Sc CO2) still showed accessibility to CO2, as evidenced by three different experiments to collect CO2 isotherms measured at 2 °C (Figure 5). ZnMOF‐BPDC (Sc CO2) exhibited a CO2 capacity of 0.6 mmol CO2g–1 at 2 °C and a PCO2 of 1 bar. Similar results were reported by Hupp and co‐workers26 with Zn2(TCPB)(DPG) [TCPB: 1,2,4,5‐tetrakis(4‐carboxyphenyl)benzene; DPG: meso‐1,2‐bis(4‐pyridyl)‐1,2‐ethanediol] modified with succinic anhydride. In their study, despite having a low surface area measured at –196 °C, the MOF showed a CO2 capacity of ca. 0.8 mmol CO2g–1 at 0 °C.26

With the potential that the active sites in both Zn‐MOFs were open Zn2+ sites, we employed Raman spectroscopy to observe CO2 molecules adsorbed on these sites. Desolvated ZnMOF‐PDC was selected for this study because it yielded the highest amount of CO2 adsorbed, thus incorporating the highest amount of accessible open Zn2+ centers available to CO2. The coordination of carboxylate and pyridine groups in PDC linkers to Zn centers was apparent when comparing the Raman spectra of 2,5‐pyridinedicarboxylic acid and ZnMOF‐PDC under a N2 atmosphere (Figure 6c). A peak assigned to the symmetric stretching vibration of C=O, νsym(C=O),27 was blueshifted by 50 cm–1, whereas a peak assigned to in‐plane stretching vibration of pyridine, ν(C=N),27 was redshifted by 30 cm–1 in ZnMOF‐PDC. In addition, the peak assigned to the asymmetric stretching vibration of C=O, νasym(C=O),27 was redshifted by 15 cm–1 and split into two peaks after with MOF formation, which is likely due to the presence of the free CO group (C‐O7 in Figure 1a and b).27a

A Raman spectrum of this MOF was also recorded under a CO2 atmosphere to investigate CO2 adsorbed on open Zn2+ sites. As shown in Figure 6c, a new peak assigned to the symmetric stretching vibration of C=O in CO2 adsorbed on this MOF located at 1380 cm–1 was observed. This peak was redshifted by 8 cm–1 with respect to a peak corresponding to unperturbed gaseous CO2.27a This corroborated a previous study that showed a similar peak of CO2 adsorbed on open Ni2+ centers in MOF‐74 [Ni2(2,5‐dioxido‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate)].27a Similar to our Raman study, this peak was redshifted by 6 cm–1 compared to the peak position of the unperturbed CO2,27a thus providing spectroscopic evidence of open Zn2+ centers in ZnMOF‐PDC.

In addition, in situ diffuse reflectance FT‐IR spectroscopy (DRIFTS) was used to observe the interaction between open Zn2+ centers and acetic acid. For this investigation, we used acetic acid (AA) as a probe molecule because it can readily bind to transition metals in various configurations depending on the coordination geometry of the metal.28 In previous reports,28 structural configurations of acetate‐Zn2+ complexes were assigned from peaks located between 1350 and 1650 cm–1 in DRIFT spectra. Indeed, we could observe the formation of acetate‐Zn2+ complexes in the DRIFT spectrum of ZnMOF‐PDC (Figure 7). This spectrum showed two peaks of gaseous AA located at ca. 1800 cm–1, ca. 1780 cm–1 assigned to the asymmetric stretching vibration of C=O bond in a monomeric AA gas and a dimeric AA gas, respectively.29 This spectrum also exhibited a region assigned to the asymmetric stretching vibration of the C=O bond (vasym) innate to acetate‐Zn2+ species located at 1490–1640 cm–1.28 In addition, the formation of these complexes on ZnMOF‐PDC was further demonstrated by background‐subtracted DRIFT spectrum of two control materials, MOF‐2 and IRMOF‐1. In MOF‐2, each Zn center was penta‐coordinated to four carboxylates from four separate 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate (BDC) struts and a nearby oxygen innate to BDC of an adjacent layer, which generated a distorted square‐pyramidal geometry (Figure S4),20 thus providing a control material with known accessible Zn2+ sites. As expected, MOF‐2 showed a similar result to ZnMOF‐PDC (Figure 7), suggesting that penta‐coordinated Zn2+ centers innate to Zn‐MOF formed acetate species. However, in IRMOF‐1, octahedral Zn4O nodes were coordinatively saturated through their complexation with BDC struts.13 Expectedly, the peaks assigned as vasym of acetate‐Zn2+ species were absent in Figure 7, due to the fully coordinated Zn4O clusters in IRMOF‐1.

Conclusions

In summary, the synthesis and characterization of two novel Zn‐MOFs with open Zn2+ sites were reported, leading to materials with enhanced interactions with reactant gases (i.e., CO2 and acetic acid). ZnMOF‐PDC exhibited high efficiency (92.5 %) of open Zn2+ sites available to CO2 capture, whereas ZnMOF‐BPDC showed porosity accessible to CO2 in spite of its structural deformation. The use of PDC as an organic linker to form robust ZnMOF‐PDC with the unique structure can likely be extended to other PDC‐based MOFs by incorporating various open metal centers such as Mg, Co, and Ni. As compared with Zn, these transition metals are expected to show enhanced interaction with CO2.19a,19b Similar to the MOF‐74 series,19 where these transition metals are incorporated as a metal node, these other PDC‐based MOFs could be promising for gas separations. The synthesis of these MOFs and their applications in membranes for gas storage/separation and templates for post‐synthetic modification are on‐going research topics in our lab.

Experimental Section

Chemicals: The following materials were commercially purchased and employed as received: 5,5′‐dimethyl‐2,2′‐bipyridine (Alfa–Aesar, 98 %), KMnO4 (AMRESCO, ACS grade), Celite® 545 (EMD), diethyl ether (BDH, stabilized, ACS grade), 1 M HCl (EMD, ACS grade), D2O (Sigma–Aldrich, 99.9 atom‐% D), 2,5‐pyridinedicarboxylic acid (Alfa–Aesar, > 98.0 %), 2‐amino‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid (Alfa–Aesar, 99 %), 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid (Aldrich, 99 %), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (Alfa–Aesar, 99 %, metal basis), Zn(O2CCH3)2·2H2O (Alfa–Aesar, > 97.0 %) N,N‐dimethylformamide (DMF, EMD, ACS grade), chloroform (Alfa–Aesar, > 99.8 %, ACS grade), and glacial acetic acid (AA, Acros, ACS grade).

Characterization: The surface areas and pore volumes of Zn‐MOFs were evaluated via N2 adsorption at –196 °C using a Quantachrome NOVA 2200e. Materials were degassed under 10–3 Torr dynamic vacuum at 125 °C for 18 h. Surface areas of Zn‐MOFs were evaluated using nonlocal density functional theory (NLDFT),30 providing the value of SNLDFT calculated based on the volume of N2 adsorbed (cm3 g–1) under the relative pressure range of 1 × 10–7 < P/P0 < 1.0. The pore volume (VPORE) of the Zn‐MOFs was also estimated using NLDFT based on the assumption that all pores were silt‐shaped nanopores.30 X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were obtained using a D8 Advance Davinci diffractometer operated at 40 mA and 40 kV with monochromated Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) at a step size of 0.02° per step and a scan speed of 0.75 second per step. 1H nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (1H NMR) analysis was carried out on a Bruker AVANCE III HD 400 in D2O. In situ FT‐IR spectra of Zn‐MOFs were recorded using a diffuse reflectance infrared Fourier transform (DRIFT) mode in a JASCO FT/IR‐6300 spectrometer under a gaseous acetic acid (AA) atmosphere. For this FT‐IR analysis, Zn‐MOFs were loaded into a closed cell (Praying MantisTM High Temperature Reaction Chamber, Harrick Scientific Products) inside a glove box purged with the N2 (>99.99 %), tightly sealed, and removed from the glove box. The DRIFT spectrum of Zn‐MOF was collected under a N2 atmosphere and set as a background. After changing the gas atmosphere of the closed cell from the N2 to the gaseous AA and confirming no change in the intensity of characteristic AA signals with time was observed, the closed cell was heated to 130 °C for 30 min to remove physisorbed AA. After cooling the closed cell to 25 °C, a background subtracted DRIFT spectrum of AA‐adsorbed Zn‐MOF was then recorded at this temperature under the N2 atmosphere. Raman spectra of Zn‐MOFs were recorded using a Jasco Micro‐Raman NRS‐5100 spectrometer equipped with a 532 nm excitation laser and a 1200 lines/mm grating. These spectra were obtained through a X 20 objective microscope in an extended scan mode in the range of 10–2000 cm–1 with a resolution of 0.7 cm–1. For Raman analysis of ZnMOF‐PDC and 2,5‐pyridinedicarboxylic acid under a N2 atmosphere, 20 mg of the material was loaded into a custom‐built closed cell inside the glove box purged with N2 (>99.99 %), tightly sealed, removed from the glove box, and subsequently analyzed. For Raman analysis of ZnMOF‐PDC under a CO2 atmosphere, 20 mg of this Zn‐MOF was loaded into a custom‐built closed cell inside the glove box and subjected to vacuum for 3 h prior to charging this cell with CO2 for 10 min inside the glove box. Then, this cell was tightly sealed, removed from the glove box, and subsequently analyzed.

Synthesis of Organic Linker: 2,2′‐Bipyridine‐5,5′‐dicarboxylic acid was synthesized using a slightly modified procedure reported in previous literature.31 Approximately, 4 g (21.7 mmol) of 5,5′‐dimethyl‐2,2′‐bipyridine, 22 g (139.21 mmol) of KMnO4, and 350 mL of deionized water (D. I. water) were loaded into a 500 mL round‐bottomed flask equipped with a condenser and were heated to reflux at 120 °C whilst stirring for 18 h. During this period, the color of the mixture changed from purple to black. After cooling to 25 °C, the aqueous solution was filtered through Celite to remove precipitated MnO2. A diethyl ether extraction (400 mL) was carried out to eliminate partially carboxylated products and organic impurities. The aqueous solution was subsequently acidified using a 1 M HCl solution (200 mL), filtered, washed several times with D. I. water (2 L), and then dried under vacuum (65 mTorr) overnight to give a fine, colorless powder (yield, 88 %). The successful synthesis of 2,2′‐bipyridine‐5,5′‐dicarboxylic acid was confirmed via 1H NMR (Figure S1), TGA, and DSC (Figure S2, N2, 50 °C min–1). NMR spectroscopic data: 1H NMR (300 MHz, D2O, 25 °C): δ = 8.9 (s, 2 H, Ar‐H‐c), 8.2 (d, 2 H, Ar‐H‐b), 7.9 (d, 2 H, Ar‐H‐a) ppm , m.p. (DSC) > 400 °C.

Synthesis of ZnMOF‐PDC and ZnMOF‐BPDC: Both Zn‐MOFs were synthesized using a solvothermal method. For the synthesis of ZnMOF‐PDC, 2.4 mmol (0.4 g) of 2,5‐pyridinedicarboxylic acid and 7.18 mmol (2.14 g) of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O were dissolved in 200 mL of DMF. This mixture was heated at 80 °C for 18 h in a Schott Duran® bottle under autogenous pressure. After cooling to room temperature, the liquid was decanted and the yellow crystals were washed with fresh DMF several times and stored in fresh DMF to prevent pore collapse. For the synthesis of ZnMOF‐BPDC, 0.82 mmol (0.2 g) of 2,2′‐bipyridine‐5,5′‐dicarboxylic acid and 2.21 mmol (0.657 g) of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O were mixed with 200 mL of DMF. The solution was heated to 85 °C for 72 h in a Schott Duran® bottle under autogenous pressure. After being cooled to room temperature, the liquid was decanted. Consequently, the remaining white microcrystals were washed with fresh DMF several times and soaked in fresh DMF to prevent pore collapse.

Preparation of Desolvated Zn‐MOFs via Thermal Drying: For the preparation of the Zn‐MOFs (thermal), guest solvent (DMF) present in the pores of as‐synthesized Zn‐MOFs was exchanged with chloroform under ambient conditions (once per day). After three days, the chloroform‐exchanged Zn‐MOFs were filtered, then desolvated inside the tube furnace (Thermo Scientific Lindberg® Blue M) under the inert atmosphere with the following conditions: Ar flow at 10 mL min–1, ramp rate of 0.5 °C min–1, and 120 °C for 3 h followed by 170 °C for 6 h.4a,4b

Preparation of Desolvated ZnMOF‐BPDC via Supercritical CO2 Extraction (Sc CO2) Process: As‐synthesized ZnMOF‐BPDC was desolvated using a Sc CO2 process to form ZnMOF‐BPDC (Sc CO2), following a modified procedure suggested by Kim and co‐workers.5a,32 Prior to conducting Sc CO2 process, a guest solvent filling the pores of the as‐synthesized ZnMOF‐BPDC was exchanged using chloroform under ambient condition for three days. In a typical Sc CO2 process, 0.1 g of chloroform‐exchanged ZnMOF‐BPDC was loaded in a 20 mL vial. This vial was then transferred into a stainless steel autoclave reactor (Parr 4590) manipulated by PID controller. The reactor was purged using 2 MPa of CO2 gas three times before it was pressurized to 6.2 MPa of CO2 at 25 °C with no stirring. Then, the reactor was heated to 150 °C, resulting in a pressure of 7.5 MPa inside the reactor. This pressure and temperature indicated the change in the atmosphere inside the reactor from a gaseous CO2 to a supercritical CO2. After maintaining the reactor under this condition for 30 min, its pressure was slowly released to 0 MPa prior to cooling the reactor to 25 °C.

Synthesis of IRMOF‐1, IRMOF‐3, and MOF‐2: Both IRMOF‐1 and IRMOF‐3 were synthesized based on a modified procedure reported by Eddaoudi and co‐workers.13 For IRMOF‐1 synthesis, a solution comprised of 18.0 mmol (5.35 g) of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and 5.99 mmol (0.995 g) of 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid in 180 mL DMF was heated at 105 °C for 18 h in a Schott Duran® bottle under autogenous pressure. For IRMOF‐3 synthesis, 22.9 mmol (6.81 g) of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and 8.28 mmol (1.5 g) of 2‐amino‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid were dissolved in 200 mL of DMF. This solution was heated at 105 °C for 18 h in a Schott Duran® bottle under autogenous pressure. After being cooled to room temperature, each reactant mixture was decanted, and the remaining crystals were washed with fresh DMF several times. MOF‐2 was synthesized following a procedure suggested by Sanchez‐Sanchez and co‐workers.33 For this synthesis, 7.24 mmol (1.56 g) of Zn(O2CCH3)2·2H2O was dissolved in 11 mL of D.I. water to form zinc solution, whereas 4.1 mmol (0.68 g) of 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid was dissolved in 15.8 mL of DMF to form organic linker solution. The zinc solution was then added to the organic linker solution under continuous stirring at room temperature forming a white precipitate after 24 h. This white precipitate was filtered and washed with fresh DMF several times. The resulting as‐synthesized Zn‐MOFs were then soaked in fresh DMF for stable storage. Desolvated IRMOFs and MOF‐2 were prepared following the procedure to form Zn‐MOF (thermal), as previously stated. The successful synthesis of these MOF materials was confirmed by their XRD patterns, which were in good agreement with their simulated XRD patterns (Figure S5, data for IRMOF‐1, IRMOF‐3 and CIF‐file20 for MOF‐2). The retention of the porous structures of these MOFs after being subjected to thermal drying process was demonstrated by their type I N2 adsorption isotherms at –196 °C (Figure S6) indicating the presence of micropores, as evidenced by abrupt increase in the amount of N2 adsorbed in the low P/P0 region (< 0.1). Their permanent porosity after desolvation was further confirmed by their surface areas (Table 1, SNLDFT: ca. 780 m2 g–1 for IRMOFs; ca. 210 m2 g–1 for MOF‐2) as well as pore volumes (Table 1, VPORE: ca. 0.49 cm3 g–1 for IRMOFs; ca. 0.19 cm3 g–1 for MOF‐2).

Determination of Zn‐MOF Structure and Topology: Structural determination of both ZnMOF‐PDC and ZnMOF‐BPDC using single‐crystal XRD analysis is described in the Supporting Information. Topological determination of both Zn‐MOFs was performed using a program package ToposPro (TOPOS).17

CCDC‐175572 (for IRMOF‐1), ‐CCDC‐1016577 (for ZnMOF‐PDC), ‐CCDC‐1016578 (for ZnMOF‐BPDC), and ‐CCDC‐175574 (for IRMOF‐3) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.

Low‐Pressure CO2 Uptake Measurement: CO2 (99.99 %, Airgas) adsorption isotherms of Zn‐MOFs over the relative pressure (P/P0) range between 1.0 × 10–4 to 1.0 were obtained on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 instrument. The Zn‐MOF was loaded into the bulb and capped with TranSeal prior to being thoroughly degassed at 150 °C for 4 h until the outgas rate became 0.001 Torr min–1 or below. After degassing the MOF, it was transferred into the analysis port, soaked in a Dewar vessel filled with D. I. water, iced D. I. water, or NaCl combined with iced D. I. water, and analyzed at 24 °C, 2 °C, or –10 °C, respectively. The retention of bulb temperature was confirmed by a thermometer detecting the deviation of temperature was ± 0.5 °C throughout the entire analysis.

Estimation of Isosteric Heat of Adsorption for CO2 Adsorption: The isosteric heat (–QST) of CO2 for ZnMOF‐PDC was evaluated using CO2 adsorption isotherms measured at –10, 2, and 24 °C. These isotherms were best‐fitted to the Virial Equation (1) composed of am (m = 0–6) and bn (n = 0–1) (Figure S3a).34 In (1), T is the measurement temperature [K], whereas Na and P indicate the amount of CO2 adsorbed (mmol CO2 g–1) and pressure [bar], respectively. m and n are the coefficients depicting the CO2 adsorption isotherms. Values of –QST for ZnMOF‐PDC was determined using Clausius‐Clapeyron Equation (2), in which fitted parameters were used to derive values of ln (P) at the identical value of Na at different temperatures (Table S5).18b In (2), P1 and P2 indicate the pressure at T1 and T2, respectively, whereas R indicates the ideal gas constant [8.3145 J (K mol)–1]. However, due to the unusual behavior of CO2 adsorption on ZnMOF‐BPDC (thermal and Sc CO2) measured at 24 °C, its isotherm was not well‐fit to Virial equation (not shown). Thus, its –QST was not calculated.
