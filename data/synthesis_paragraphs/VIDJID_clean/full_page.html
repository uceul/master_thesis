Novel Co-based metal–organic frameworks and their magnetic properties using asymmetrically binding 4-(4′-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole†

Tomoko Aharen a, Fatemah Habib a, Ilia Korobkov a, Tara J. Burchell a, Rémy Guillet-Nicolas b, Fredy Kleiz b and Muralee Murugesu *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, University of Ottawa, 10 Marie Curie Private, Ottawa, ON, Canada K1N 6N5. E-mail: m.murugesu@uottawa.ca; Fax: +1 (613) 562 5170; Tel: +1 (613) 562 5800 ext. 2733
bDépartement de Chimie, Pavillon Alexandre-Vachon, local 2240-F 1045 avenue de la medicine, Université Laval, QC, Canada G1V 0A6

Received 14th December 2012 , Accepted 12th March 2013

First published on 3rd April 2013

Two novel Co-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) were synthesised and characterised using an asymmetrically binding ligand, 4-(4′-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4 triazole (Hcpt). The isolated [Co3II(μ3-O)(OH)(cpt)3(H2O)2]n·xH2O·yDMF, Co-MOF1, exhibits a rhombohedral crystal structure (space group, P63/mc), with a trinuclear cobalt core that resembles the MIL88 series. This MOF shows paramagnetic behaviour down to 2 K with no saturation of magnetisation up to 7 T. This is presumably due to a geometrically frustrated triangular arrangement of Co spins. The two-dimensional complex, [CoII(cpt)(N3)]n, Co-MOF2, crystallises in a monoclinic crystal system (space group, C2/m). The magnetic measurements reveal metamagnetic behaviour for this complex with a critical field in the range of 700–1000 Oe.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs) are a class of materials which have been extensively investigated for applications in gas storage/separation, sensors, catalysis, magnetism and drug delivery.1 Structures of these materials consist of metal centres/clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) and organic ligands as linkers in order to create suitable pores. Due to the coexistence of rigid crystalline structures and highly tuneable organic ligands, this family of compounds has become attractive in materials chemistry. Numerous attempts have been made to increase functionalities of MOFs at desired conditions.1 Furthermore, post-synthetic modifications of ligands have been carried out to functionalise these structures after their assembly, which significantly expands potential design strategies of MOFs.2 When focusing on binding modes of ligands, most of the ligands employed in the synthesis of MOFs are highly dependent on carboxylate groups and/or nitrogen containing groups such as tetrazole-, pyridine- and porphyrin-based materials due to their ability to effectively bind metal ions.3 Moreover, such symmetric linkers often exhibit ‘symmetric’ binding modes where often the same bridging mode is repeated throughout the molecular structure. Therefore, in order to obtain various molecular architectures with different bridging types, ligands with unsymmetrical coordination sites are required. Furthermore, unsymmetrical ligands containing different chelating groups can provide multiple magnetic superexchange pathways when bridging between metal centres leading to distinct magnetic properties. With the aim of synthesising new MOFs with particularly unique structures and properties, we focused our attention on a rigid linker containing carboxylate as well as triazole chelating groups. The 4-(4′-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt, Scheme 1) ligand contains bidentate chelating groups with very similar bite angles yet different coordinating atoms (N vs. O). We have recently explored the potential use of Hcpt in coordination chemistry of lanthanide ions, which yielded a supramolecular 2D arrangement of {Dy4} cubane molecules.4
Scheme 1 Structure of 4-(4′-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt).

Continuous work using Hcpt and transition metal ions led to the synthesis of MOFs and allowed us to study the relationship between metal ions and the functional groups of the ligand. Moreover, in keeping with the theme of introducing novel magnetic exchange pathways, our synthetic strategy also involved the use of azide as a secondary ligand which can mediate magnetic interactions between magnetic ions depending on its bridging modes.1c Herein we report the synthesis and magnetic properties of a Co-based MOF, [CoII(μ3-O)(OH)(cpt)3(H2O)2]n·xH2O·yDMF (Co-MOF1), as well as an azide-bridged low dimensional MOF, [CoII(cpt)(N3)]n (Co-MOF2).

Experimental section

All reagents were used as received (Aldrich, Acros Organics, Alfa Aesar). Although we did not experience any difficulty in handling azide materials, small-scale synthesis is strongly advised as they are potentially explosive.

Synthesis of 4-(4′-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole (Hcpt)

Ligand synthesis closely followed the method previously reported by our group;4 however, the reaction temperature was reduced to 150 °C for microwave setting. Formylhydrazide (90%, 0.45 g, 7.5 mmol) with ethanol (20 mL, 99%) was placed in a microwave tube. After dissolution, triethylorthoformate (TEOF, 1.87 mL, 7.5 mmol) was added and the sealed tube was mounted in a Biotage initiator microwave. After 5 min at 150 °C and 10 bars of pressure a clear solution was obtained. 4-Aminobenzoic acid (1.0 g, 7.3 mmol) was added immediately after the first reaction (the formation of diformylhydrazide) and the vial was placed back in the microwave. The reaction conditions for the formation of Hcpt were the same as in the previous step. The beige-white powder was obtained in a slightly pink solution and the product was purified by recrystallisation in 30 mL of dimethylformamide (DMF). After recrystallisation, the product was washed with diethyl ether and ethanol (99%). Hcpt was obtained as fine white powder; the purity was checked by 1H NMR and compared with the reported data (d6-DMSO, 400 MHz): δ (ppm) 13.20 (s, 1H), 9.21 (s, 2H), 8.06 (d, J = 8.8 Hz, 2H), 7.83 (d, J = 8.8 Hz, 2H). Yield: 25%.

Synthesis of [Co3II(μ3-O)(OH)(cpt)3(H2O)2]n·xH2O·yDMF, (Co-MOF1)

A mixture of Hcpt (5.3 mg, 0.028 mmol) and Co(NO3)2·6H2O (220 mg, 0.75 mmol) was placed in a scintillation vial (20 mL) along with a mixture of solvents composed of DMF (10 mL), EtOH (99%, 3 mL) and water (0.3 mL). The vial was kept at 90 °C for 24 hours and upon slow cooling orange-red block shaped crystals were obtained in a vivid pink solution. Yield: 18%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3401(m), 1662(s), 1541(s), 1491(s), 1381(s), 1252(s), 1182(w), 1092(s), 1051(m), 1011(m), 996(w), 982(w), 866(m), 836(m), 825(m), 784(s).

Synthesis of [CoII(cpt)N3]n, (Co-MOF2)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (15.5 mg, 0.052 mmol), Hcpt (19.1 mg, 0.10 mmol) and NaN3 (26 mg, 0.40 mmol) was dissolved in water (8 mL) in a 23 mL Teflon-Bomb. The bomb was kept at 120 °C for 3 days and slowly cooled to room temperature (approx. 3 °C h−1). Pink block crystals were obtained from a clear solution. Yield: 58%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3422(w), 3145(w), 2097(s), 1603(s), 1563(s), 1539(s), 1397(s), 1311(m), 1250(s), 1094(s), 1045(m), 1018(w), 989(w), 979(w), 865(s), 839(m), 782(s).

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction

The obtained crystals were analysed using a Bruker AXS SMART single crystal diffractometer with a sealed Mo tube APEX II CCD detector at the University of Ottawa. The Mo radiation wavelength, 0.71073 Å, was used. Data collection and processing were performed with APEX software from Bruker AXS.5 A total of 60 data frames were used for the initial determination of cell parameters. Semi-empirical absorption collection was performed based on equivalent reflection.6 The crystal structures were solved by direct methods and refined using SHELEX program.7 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and the positions of all hydrogen atoms were obtained from the Fourier map analysis. Solvents in pores of Co-MOF1 were treated as disordered due to the lack of corresponding reflections. It is noteworthy that SQUEEZE was not used to refine the structure. There was some electron density observed in the ‘empty’ space; however, the strongest peak obtained in that region was only 0.5 e; therefore the solvents in the pore have been treated as disordered. All the information regarding the atomic arrangements for Co-MOF1 and Co-MOF2 are listed in Tables 1, S1 and S2.† The crystallographic files can be obtained from the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre with the following reference numbers: CCDC 863671 (Co-MOF1) and 863672 (Co-MOF2).

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and isotherm gas absorption

Thermogravimetric analysis was performed at the Department of Chemistry, University of Laval to determine coordinating solvents. Isotherm gas absorption measurements were also made to determine the absorption capacity of the samples. Thermogravimetric–differential thermal analysis (TG–DTA) measurements of the metal–organic framework materials were performed using a Netzsch STA 449C TGA analyser (Germany). The analyses were carried out under air or nitrogen flow (20 mL min−1) with a heating rate of 2 or 5 °C min−1. In all analyses, a complementary nitrogen flow (20 mL min−1) was used as a protective gas. Argon adsorption–desorption isotherms were measured at liquid argon temperature (−186 °C) using a Quantachrome Autosorb-1MP volumetric adsorption analyser (Quantachrome Instruments, Boynton Beach, Florida, USA). Prior to the measurement, the sample was activated by heating for 24 hours at 150 °C in a vacuum oven and then degassed at 150 °C for 12 hours on the autosorb under turbomolecular pump vacuum. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) equation was used to calculate the apparent surface area from adsorption data following recent recommendations for MOFs given by Moellmer et al.8 Total pore volume of micro-mesopores was estimated from the amount of argon adsorbed at P/P0 = 0.95, assuming that adsorption on the external surface was negligible compared to adsorption in the pores.

Powder X-ray diffraction for bulk samples

Powder X-ray diffraction for bulk samples of Co-MOF1 and Co-MOF2 was carried out using a Rigaku Ultima IV X-ray powder diffractometer at the University of Ottawa. The Parallel Beam mode was used to collect the data (λ = 1.541836 Å).

Variable temperature powder X-ray diffraction

The powder X-ray diffraction data of Co-MOF1 at various temperatures (50–200 °C in 25 °C increments) were collected using a Panalytical powder X-ray diffractometer at the University of Ottawa, and CuKα (λ = 1.54056 Å) was used.

Magnetic susceptibility measurements

The magnetic susceptibilities of polycrystalline Co-MOF1 (10 mg) and Co-MOF2 (13 mg) were measured using a SQUID magnetometer (MPMS XL-7, Quantum Design) at the University of Ottawa. Diamagnetic corrections were applied for the sample holder. The temperature dependences of zero-field-cooled (ZFC) and field-cooled (FC) data were collected in the temperature range of 1.8–300 K with an applied field of 500 Oe for Co-MOF1. The isotherm magnetisation data of Co-MOF1 were also collected at 1.8, 3, 5 and 8 K under applied fields up to 7 T. The same measurements were carried out on Co-MOF2 including ZFC/FC measurements under various applied fields (100, 200, 700, 1000 and 2500 Oe). Alternating current (ac) susceptibility measurements were carried out on both Co-MOFs with a driving field of 3 Oe. For Co-MOF2, an additional ac measurement was carried out using an applied dc field of 700 Oe. For both Co-MOF1 and Co-MOF2, the bulk samples were checked for their phases with X-ray powder diffraction. It was confirmed that both samples retain the structures (Fig. S7 and S8†).

Results and discussion

Syntheses

Solvothermal synthesis was employed in order to isolate both Co-MOF1 and Co-MOF2 complexes. More specifically, for Co-MOF1 a scintillation vial was utilised as the reaction was carried out at 90 °C whereas the use of a Teflon-Bomb was necessary for Co-MOF2 as the temperature required was 120 °C. In the latter case, slow cooling is essential for the isolation of X-ray quality crystals. The crystallisation of Co-MOF1 was optimised by varying the ratio of DMF, ethanol and water in the solvent mixture. Especially, the quality of the Co-MOF1 crystals was influenced by the amount of water in the solvent mixture. It was observed that a smaller amount of water (0.3 mL) yielded better crystals than the water-rich reaction (i.e. 2 mL) for single crystal X-ray diffraction. It was also of interest to synthesise structurally related Co-MOFs and use azide as a secondary ligand as we expected to see some enhanced magnetic properties between CoII ions. This is due to the end-on bridging mode of azide which mediates magnetic interaction; thus in our synthetic strategy azide was added as a secondary ligand. This led to a two-dimensional structure (Co-MOF2) where both cpt− and N3− ligands bridge and coordinate to metal ions. For Co-MOF2, water as the primary solvent was employed and the obtained complex seemed to be in a metastable state in the mother solution. The isolated complexes are relatively stable at room temperature, and crystal degradation can be observed only after several months. Attempts to vary the ratios of reagents in the reaction mixture did not yield new complexes. Mostly powder samples were isolated and the IR data (Fig. S1 and S2†) correlate with the isolated Co-MOF1 or Co-MOF2 complexes.

Crystal structures and structural properties

The crystal structure of [Co3II(μ3-O)(OH)(cpt)3(H2O)2]n·xH2O·yDMF, Co-MOF1, was obtained using single crystal X-ray diffraction. This compound crystallises in the hexagonal space group, P63/mc, with cell constants of a = 15.9295(5) Å, c = 19.7200(7) Å and γ = 120°. All other crystallographic information is listed in Tables 1 and S1.† The chemical formula was assigned based on the single crystal data and charge considerations. The molecular unit is composed of a CoII trinuclear oxo-centred (O2) equilateral triangle (Fig. 1). Each CoII ion exhibits octahedral coordination with two O atoms (O1) from the carboxylate group of a cpt− ligand, two N atoms (N1) from the triazole rings of other cpt− ligands, a μ3-O (O2) atom and a hydroxide anion or a water molecule according to the refinement of the structure. A hydroxide anion, which is presumably generated during the reaction, completes the charge balance of the entire framework. This hydroxide group was considered to be highly disordered; therefore the average atomic position of CoII ions appears to be identical. It is also worth considering the existence of hydroxide anions in the crystal lattice instead of being in the terminal position. However, hydroxides are relatively small anions with excellent binding affinity to metal centres. Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that terminal hydroxide groups are more likely to occur.
Fig. 1 Molecular structural unit of the μ3-O-bridged equilateral triangle {Co3} cluster. The carboxylate groups are bound from one side, while the triazolyl groups are bound to the opposite side.

Within the cluster, all bridging ligands are coordinating asymmetrically; all carboxylate moieties bind from one side (top) of the Co3II triangular core and triazole groups binding from the other side (bottom) (Fig. 1). It is noteworthy that the topology of the trinulear {Co3} cluster is similar to that of MIL1019 and MIL88,10 where three CrIII ions are bridged via μ3-O and terephthalic acid. Also similar to the MIL88 series, hexagonal honeycomb channels are constructed along the c axis (Fig. 2, top) with an aperture size of approximately 10 Å. The layers of {Co3} clusters stack along the c axis and are separated by approximately 10 Å (Fig. 2, bottom). The volume of the generated pore in the structure is calculated to be approximately 0.69 cm3 g−1 using PLATON software.11,12 It is noteworthy that Co-MOF1 crystallises in a similar fashion to the MIL88 series since it allows us to potentially develop another 3D Co-MOF network with large pores.


Fig. 2 The packing arrangement of Co-MOF1 along the c axis (top) and b axis (bottom). Six {Co3} clusters generate a hexagonal channel along the c axis.

According to TGA, weight changes of the material were observed around 100 and 160 °C where it is suggested that the loss of water and DMF solvent molecules is accompanied by the removal of coordinating water molecules and μ3-oxygen atoms (Fig. S3†). The weight changes above these temperatures were assigned to decomposition of the cpt− ligand. According to the variable temperature X-ray diffraction data, the compound easily decomposed and became amorphous at 50 °C and above (Fig. S4†). This amorphous structure is retained up to 200 °C where significant weight loss due to the removal of solvents occurred in the TGA data. This was also easily detectable by a colour change of the material from orange-red to purple (Fig. S5†). This result suggests that a change in the coordination environment of the metal had occurred and this compound does not exhibit the breathing effect seen in the MIL series.1m The lack of breathing ability could originate from the coordination bonds between either carboxylate groups or triazole groups of the ligand and CoII metal ions. This is because breaking these bonds will immediately affect the structural endurance and parameters (i.e. cell constants) as opposed to the bonds between the metal centre and water or μ3-O. The physisorption experiments suggest that there is no accessible pore in the MOF as the BET surface area (15 m2 g−1) and measured accessible pore volume (0.03 cm3 g−1, Fig. S6†) of this material are quite small. These small values may be due to the pre-treatment of the sample in these experiments which requires degassing/removal of any solvents and contaminants from pores causing the collapse of the MOF structure.

Hydrothermally synthesised [CoII(cpt)N3]n, Co-MOF2, crystallises in a monoclinic space group, C2/m, with cell constants of a = 14.5002(3) Å, b = 7.1357(2) Å, c = 11.3090(3) Å and β = 116.939(1)° (Fig. 3). The crystallographic information, bond distances and bond angles are listed in Tables 1 and S2.† In this structure, the CoII ion adopts an octahedral geometry where the coordination environment is filled by two O atoms from two different carboxylate groups (O1) and two N atoms from two different triazole rings (N1, N1a) of cpt− ligands as well as two azide ions (N3, N3a) as shown in Fig. 3. It is interesting to note that CoII ions are bridged by three different groups (end-on azide, syn–syn carboxylate and triazole) to form a 1D chain structure along the b axis. The chains are linked by the cpt− ligands with an inter-chain distance of 11.3 Å (direction of c axis) forming a 2D network. The inter-chain distances between the layers are 7.2 Å providing clear separation between the sheets. Although Co-MOF2 has a 2D structural framework, the large separation between the chains results in a linear magnetic system. Recently, Li et al. reported a structurally similar 2D network with a ligand exhibiting similar coordination modes.13 However, the reported 1-(carboxylatomethyl)-4-(5-tetrazolato)pyridinium ligand is slightly larger and more flexible than Hcpt thus providing a slightly less rigid network.


Fig. 3 Crystal structure of Co-MOF2 in the ac plane (top) emphasising the parallel arrangement of the chains (middle). Interconnected chains visualised along the b axis (bottom).

Magnetic properties

Co-MOF1 . The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility (2–300 K) was measured, and ZFC/FC data were collected with an applied field of 500 Oe. There was no divergence observed between both curves and the susceptibility shows paramagnetic behaviour (Fig. 4) down to 2 K. The inverse susceptibility kept a linear regression down to around 150 K and the Weiss constant, θ, as well as the Curie constant, C, were calculated to be around −65 K and 8.53 cm3 K mol−1, respectively (Fig. S9†). The negative value of the Weiss constant suggests that the interactions between CoII ions are antiferromagnetic. The effective magnetic moment, μeff, is calculated to be 3.28μB using the obtained Curie constant and this is slightly lower than the spin-only value for the high-spin divalent CoII ion (3.87μB). The χT vs. T plot suggests that the antiferromagnetic interaction between CoII ions is developed at higher temperatures than room temperature since the plot does not show any saturation of χT in the measured temperature range (Fig. S10†). Therefore, the reduced μeff of this material could arise from the non-negligible antiferromagnetic contribution seen even at higher temperature. The isotherm magnetisation data collected at lower temperatures show a shoulder around 20000 Oe (≈ 1.4μB) and slowly increase; however, no saturation is reached even at 7 T where the theoretical saturation value for S = 3/2 is 6.45μB for g = 4.3. The shoulder disappears at 8 K and the isotherm curve becomes almost linear, which is usually observed for paramagnets. The magnetisation may deviate from the theoretical value due to a geometrically frustrated arrangement of the Co spins, where antiferromagnetically coupled Co spins in an equilateral triangle topology prevent spin alignment to the external magnetic field. There is a case where the angle between Co spins is 120°, where the spins point towards the vertices of an equilateral triangle. In this case, the overall moment can be reduced as well, considering the anisotropy of Co ions. In order to determine which case is applicable for Co-MOF1, further experimentation is required. Additionally, the majority of the interactions between CoII ions would occur only between the nearest neighbours; in other words, confined within clusters since no indication of a long range correlation/transition was observed.
Fig. 4 Temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility (χ) for Co-MOF1. Zero-field cooled (ZFC) and field-cooled (FC) curves are completely overlapping. The inset shows the isotherm magnetisation of Co-MOF1 at various temperatures.

Alternating current (ac) susceptibility measurements were performed; however, no frequency dependent signal was observed in both the in-phase and out-of-phase susceptibilities. This supports the absence of superparamagnetic behaviour in the Co trinuclear cluster.

Co-MOF2 . The χT product as a function of temperature at various applied dc fields is constant down to about 50 K (2.6–3.0 cm3 K mol−1) and then increases rapidly; below 10 K the increase is abrupt (Fig. 5). In this region, the products corresponding to an applied field over 700 Oe remain above the constant, suggesting that ferromagnetic interaction is dominant, whereas the remaining products collected below an applied field of 700 Oe, suggesting that antiferromagnetic interaction is dominant.
Fig. 5 Temperature dependence of the χT product for Co-MOF2. The inset shows the lower temperature region (<20 K). Solid lines are guides for the eyes.

The isotherm magnetisation measurements at various temperatures show shoulders with applied fields around 10000 Oe (Fig. 6, inset) depending on the collection temperatures. The magnetisation gradually increases above 10000 Oe, reaching approximately 2.2μB at 7 T, where the theoretically expected saturation magnetisation is 6.4μB (g = 4.3) for the S = 3/2 anisotropic high spin CoII ion. The obtained results clearly indicate that magnetization is far from saturation even at 7 T and at 2.2 K. Complete alignment of all the spins would require a much higher applied field for this compound. The data collected at 2.2 K show a sigmoidal curve with an onset around 700 Oe and an inflection point around 1000 Oe (Fig. 6), which is often observed in metamagnets.14 The dc magnetic susceptibilities with various dc-applied fields show antiferromagnetic transitions up to an applied field of 1000 Oe (Fig. 7), where the onset of spin-flip is observed in the isotherm magnetisation data. Above this applied filed, dc magnetic susceptibilities show ferromagnetic behaviours supporting the transition from antiferromagnetic to ferromagnetic. The inverse susceptibility data show the presence of small ferromagnetic interactions (Weiss constant, θ = 11.1 (K), Fig. S11†). There was no divergence between ZFC and FC data indicating the absence of canted antiferromagnetic behaviour (Fig. S12†). Therefore, the origin of ferromagnetic interactions is mainly due to the metamagnetic behaviour of this compound. This transition behaviour is commonly observed in MOFs reported as metamagnets.14


Fig. 6 Isotherm magnetisation of Co-MOF2 in an applied low field region with the full measurement up to 7 T shown in the inset. Solid lines are guides for the eyes.

Fig. 7 Temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility for Co-MOF2 at various applied fields. Only ZFC data are shown here. Solid lines are guides for the eyes.

The ac susceptibility measurements at various frequencies (1 Hz–1500 Hz) show maxima between 2.6 and 2.8 K in both in-phase and out-of-phase susceptibility measurements (Fig. 8). These peak positions are slightly lower than the transition temperatures observed in the dc susceptibility (around 3 K, Fig. 7). The absence of a frequency dependent signal simply precludes single-molecule magnet or single-chain magnet behaviour. Rather, the presence of peaks in the in-phase susceptibility suggests that some magnetic transition occurred at this temperature, which is consistent with other collected data. A hysteresis measurement was not available since the temperature for the completion of antiferromagnetic transition was not reachable due to the instrument limit (lower than 2 K).


Fig. 8 In-phase (χ′, top) and out-of-phase (χ′′, bottom) ac susceptibility for Co-MOF2 measured at various frequencies and an applied dc field of 700 Oe. Solid lines are guides for the eyes.

Metamagnetic compounds usually have a 1D or 2D ferromagnetic structure with a weak antiferromagnetic interaction between chains or planes. The origin of the metamagnetic behaviour of Co-MOF2 is due to its 1D chain structure constructed along the b axis, where CoII ions are bridged via azide (N3) ligands in the EO mode. The EO mode is where only the terminal nitrogen forms the bridge. The bridging azide ions in an EO fashion are well known to mediate ferromagnetic coupling between magnetic ions.15 Insights into potentially weak antiferromagnetism as well as the spin orientation in this compound could be obtained when a large crystal is available in the future for measurements of the angular (orientation) dependence of magnetisation. The detailed mechanism of spin transfer in the chains would require a computational analysis. Overall, combined with the absence of frequency dependence in the ac susceptibility measurements and the presence of a transition from antiferromagnetic to ferromagnetic interactions, it is suggested that Co-MOF2 exhibits metamagnetic behaviour.

Conclusion

Two novel Co-based MOF compounds using the asymmetrically coordinated cpt− ligand were synthesised and characterised. Co-MOF1 shows a hexagonal structure and channels with an aperture size of approximately 10 Å. The magnetic properties of this material suggest no saturation up to 7 T due to locally antiferromagnetically coupled CoII ions in a frustrated geometry. This prevents alignment of the spins to an external field where the localisation of magnetism is probably due to the cpt− ligand. The 2D Co-MOF2 compound exhibits metamagnetic behaviour with a critical field in the range of 700–1000 Oe. This is attributed to the EO bridging azide ligands which often promote ferromagnetic interactions among magnetic ions. Exploiting the binding preference of the ligand to the metal would be valuable for designing magnetic ion arrangements to control the dimensions of a network/magnetic pathway in MOFs.
