Abstract

Herein, we report a unique self‐catenated metal‐organic framework (MOF) with an underlying (3,12)‐connected topology based on Co4 clusters. Moreover, the special dumbbell‐like pore configuration, the exact match between the pore aperture and the kinetic diameter of CO2, as well as the coexistence of acylamide and NH2 groups in the MOF enhances the selectivity of adsorption of CO2 over other gases such as CO, CH4, O2, and N2.

Abstract

We report a complex self‐catenated MOF showing a highly rare (3,12)‐connecting underlying net built on a 12‐connecting Co4 node and a 3‐connecting ligand node. This MOF is the first that features a (3,12)‐connecting net and self‐catenation.

Introduction

One of the driving forces for the design and preparation of metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) is the intriguing topology matrix and broad applications they may have in many fields such as catalysis, ion exchange, magnetism, photoelectricity, and gas adsorption.1–3

The topological aspect mainly focuses on the discovery of a unique topology matrix, especially that with an entanglement feature.4 Generally, 0D and 1D structures afford catenane or rotaxane character, whereas interpenetration is often encountered in 2D and 3D structures.5 However, in some cases, the 2D and 3D structures are also accompanied by both interpenetration and catenane character.6 Besides, in some limited cases, another entanglement mode, self‐catenation, should also be highlighted, as self‐catenated nets possess peculiar features having the smallest topological rings when passed or catenated by other components of the same network.7

On the other hand, many efforts have also been directed toward the discovery of highly connected nets, especially nets that are built on mixed nodes such as (3, 10)‐, (3,12)‐, (3, 24)‐connected nets, as these synthesis are challenging because of steric hindrance.8 For example, to construct a (3,12)‐connected net, mixed three‐ and twelve‐connecting nodes are required. Generally speaking, three‐connecting nodes could be formed by trigonal organic ligands; however, twelve‐connecting nodes need more effort and are usually formed by clusters rather than single ions. To the best of our knowledge, until now, only four (3,12)‐connected nets have been reported, in which expected trigonal organic ligands form the three‐connecting node and the twelve‐connecting clusters involve Eu4, Zn8, Co5, and Co8 clusters.9 Notably, some high‐connecting MOFs with mixed nodes contain the self‐catenation feature, and present an exceptional structural chemistry and new challenges in coordination chemistry.

In this work, we report a highly rare case of a complex self‐catenated MOF showing an underlying (3,12)‐connected topology based on Co4 clusters, namely Co2(NH2‐BDC)1.5(L)(HCO2)·solvent [1, NH2–H2BDC = 2‐aminoterephthalic acid, L = 1,3,5‐N,N′,N″‐tri(3‐pyridyl)benzamide]. Furthermore, we also explored the gas adsorption and magnetism properties of 1, which indicate selective adsorption of CO2 and antiferromagnetic interactions in the Co4 cluster.

Results and Discussion

Compound 1 was obtained by solvothermal reaction of NH2–H2BDC, L, and Co(NO3)2·6H2O in DMF (5 mL) over four days. The phase purity of the bulk samples is confirmed by EA (element analysis) and XRD (powder X‐ray diffraction) investigations.

The single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction reveals that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/c space group and contains two crystallography independent CoII ions (Figure 1). Co1 is six coordinated in a regular octahedral geometry by four oxygen atoms from both the NH2‐BDC2– and formate ligands and two pyridyl nitrogen atoms from the L ligands. The formate ligands are formed in situ from the decomposition of DMF during the solvothermal reaction. Whilst, the six‐coordinated Co2 site has a distorted octahedral geometry with five oxygen atoms from both the NH2‐BDC2– and formate ligands and one pyridyl nitrogen atom from the L ligand. Notably, there are two types of position disorder for the NH2 groups of the NH2‐BDC2– ligand, viz. one in the 2,3‐sites and one in the 2,5‐sites. This leads to different coordination modes – the NH2‐BDC2– ligand with the disorder of NH2 groups in the 2,3‐sites coordinates in a bi(bidentate) fashion and the NH2‐BDC2– ligand with the disorder of NH2 groups in the 2,5‐sites in a bi(chelate) fashion. As for L ligand, all the pyridyl nitrogen atoms are coordinated with metal ions, which gives a cis–cis–cis conformation, where cis means that the pyridyl nitrogen atom and the acylamide NH unit are located in the same orientation. Interestingly, except for the N‐donor L ligand and the O‐donor NH2‐BDC2– ligand, there is also the small formate ligand that results from the hydrolysis of the DMF molecule and shows a tridentate coordination mode.

The most fascinating and interesting structural characteristics of 1 is that the 3D network can be described as an underlying (3,12)‐connected net, built on 12‐connecting Co4 nodes and 3‐connecting L nodes. As shown in Figure 1, the Co4 cluster is generated by the combination of Co1, Co2, and symmetry‐related Co1A and Co2A (symmetry code: 2 – x, 1 – y, 1 – z) through two formate units. The Co1–Co2 distance and Co1–O10–Co2 angle are ca. 3.6 Å and 112.7°, respectively. Around each Co4 cluster, there are six NH2‐BDC2– ligands and six L ligands. Each NH2‐BDC2– ligand connects to two Co4 clusters, whilst each L ligand connects to three Co4 clusters. Thus, we can consider each Co4 cluster as a 12‐connecting node. Corresponding to this is the three‐connecting L node. Topology analysis by the Topos40 program gives a (3,12)‐connected net with a (32·4)2(38·418·526·612·72) symbol.10 Note that further topology analysis suggests that this (3,12)‐connected topology matrix is composed of two sub‐matrixes. When we only consider the NH2‐BDC2– ligands, then the Co4 clusters are reduced to six‐connecting nodes and further create a six‐connected pcu net. By contrast, when we only consider the L ligands, then although the Co4 clusters are also reduced to six‐connecting nodes but further assemble a (3, 6)‐connected rtl net, because, in this situation, the L ligands also act as three‐connecting nodes. As discussed above, several clusters such as Eu4, Zn8, Co5, and Co8 have been developed for constructing a (3,12)‐connected net. But because of the difference in connecting modes between three‐ and twelve‐connecting nodes, the resulted matrixes are distinct, for example (43)4(420·628·818) for Eu4, (32·4)2 (38·422·516·618·72) for Zn8, (43)4(419·627·820) for Co5, and (43)4 (412·642·812) for Co8.9 By contrast, our case gives (32·4)2 (38·418·526·612·72), which is different from the documented examples, thus presenting a unique (3,12)‐connecting net.

However, careful inspection of the structure discloses that the simplification of the above topology ignores another structural feature, the self‐catenation phenomenon. It is clear that the NH2‐BDC2– ligand penetrates the ring formed by two L ligands and two Co4 clusters. Thus, taking this into account, polymer 1 should present a complex self‐catenated framework with an underlying (3,12)‐connected topology. To the best of our knowledge, interpenetration or self‐penetration has never been observed in MOFs showing (3,12)‐connected nets, thereby, our case should be the first with this penetrating feature.

Moreover, along the a direction, polymer 1 provides a potential solvent‐accessible channel, which is 42.8 % of the cell volume as estimated by Platon.11

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out in a nitrogen atmosphere (flow rate control, 10 mL/min) in the temperature range 35–800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C/min. From Figure 2 it can be seen that there is continuous weight loss from room temperature as the temperature increases. Thus, the solvents in 1 could not be exactly determined.

The potential porosity and coexistence of the NH2 and acylamide groups prompt us to explore the CO2 adsorption capability.12 Before carrying out experiments of gas adsorption, the solid samples of 1 were treated by immerging them into methanol and then acetone solution for 2 d in turn, and then degassed under high vacuum at 100 °C for 24 h by using a Belsorp apparatus. XRD was used to evaluate the skeleton integrity of the guest‐free framework. As shown in Figure 3, the XRD patterns of the degassed samples are mostly consistent with that simulated from the single‐crystal data. However, we found that polymer 1 shows almost no N2 adsorption at 77 K and 293 K, whereas it has a higher uptake of CO2 (15.3 cm3 g–1) at 293 K and 100 kPa. The results prompted us to check the pore structure of 1, again. Along the a axis, polymer 1 exhibits 1D dumbbell‐like channels with the largest pore size of 3.3 Å (Figure 4). This value is smaller than the kinetic diameter of 3.64 Å for a N2 molecule, but matches that for CO2 molecule (3.3 Å). Thereby, we can conclude that the size of the 1D dumbbell‐like pores in 1 should be responsible for this selective CO2 adsorption over that of N2. Of course, in some MOFs, the existence of NH2 or acylamide groups also result in selective CO2 adsorption over that of N2,11 mainly because of the affinity between CO2 molecules and NH2/or acylamide groups. In this regard, the coexistence of NH2 and acylamide groups in 1 should also account for the selective adsorption of CO2 over that of N2. Further, to evaluate the generality of selective adsorption of CO2 over other gases, the adsorption of CH4, CO and O2 was carried out. The results show almost no adsorption of these gases; the maximum adsorption capability at 293 K and 1 bar is less than 1.5 cm3 g–1 (Figure 5). This also suggests selective adsorption of CO2 over CH4, CO and O2, mainly because of the kinetic diameter of these molecules (CH4: 3.8 Å, CO: 3.76 Å, O2: 3.46 Å), which is larger than the aperture in this MOF (3.3 Å). Furthermore, in contrast to other famous MOFs,11,12 the amount of CO2 adsorbed by 1 is relatively smaller, most likely, because the guest DMF molecules in 1 could not completely be exchanged by CH3OH molecules and next activated under vacuum.

In addition, the affinity of the MOF skeleton towards CO2 molecules is estimated by the enthalpy of CO2 adsorption (Qst).11–12 This is calculated on the basis of the Clausius–Clapeyron equation from the CO2 adsorption isotherms at 273 K and 293 K (Figure 6). The value at the onset of CO2 adsorption is 49.4 kJ mol–1, which indicates a strong affinity of the MOF skeleton towards CO2 molecules. However, because of adsorption saturation of the adsorption sites from the NH2 or acylamide groups, the Qst value gradually decreases and finally show a platform during next adsorption process. Note that the CO2 desorption isotherms both at 293 K and 273 K do not trace the adsorption isotherms, resulting in a hysteresis loop, which is mainly due to the strong affinity of the MOF skeleton towards CO2 molecules and the exact match between the aperture and the CO2 kinetic diameter.

Moreover, the Co4 cluster in polymer 1 also prompted us to explore its magnetic properties (Figure 7). However, usual magnetic properties are observed. The χMT value at room temperature of 5.6 cm3 mol–1 K is larger than those of the sum of two CoII ions (3.75 cm3 mol–1 K, g = 2.0, S = 3/2), mainly because of spin–orbit coupling of the octahedral CoII ions.13 Above 100 K, the relatively slow increase in the χMT values is mainly driven by the thermal retreat of the spin–orbit coupling of the octahedral CoII ions, while the sharp decrease below 100 K suggests that strong antiferromagnetic interactions between the CoII ions is dominant. This is further supported by the M vs. H plot, which shows a slow increase in M with increasing field. This indicates strong antiferromagnetic interaction. Even at a high field of 70000 Oe, it does not reach saturation and gives a value of 3.4 Nβ, smaller than the expected value of 6 Nβ for two CoII ions, which is also indicative of strong antiferromagnetic interactions.

Conclusions

This work presents an unprecedented self‐catenated MOF showing an underlying (3,12)‐connected topology built on a Co4 node and an L node. The special dumbbell‐like pore configuration, the exact match between the aperture and the kinetic diameter of CO2, as well as the free‐standing acylamide and NH2 groups are responsible for selective adsorption of CO2 over a broad range of gases, which includeCH4, N2, O2, and CO. To some extent, this work not only enriches the database of MOFs built on hybrid nodes, self‐catenated MOFs, but also provides a new MOF candidate for potential application in CO2 capture and separation.

Experimental Section

Synthesis of 1: A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0582 g, 0.2 mmol), NH2–H2BDC (0.0362 g, 0.2 mmol) and L (0.0876 g, 0.2 mmol) in DMF (5 mL) was sealed in a Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 115 °C for 4 d. The reaction system was then cooled to room temperature. Red crystals were obtained. Yield: 69 % based on Co(NO3)2·6H2O.

Crystal Data: Single‐crystal diffraction studies were performed by phi and omega scan measurement methods with Mo‐Kα radiation (0.71073 Å) on a Bruker Smart Breeze CCD at 296 K, and corrected with the SADABS program. The structures were solved by direct methods with the SHELXTL program and all non‐hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic refinement by full‐matrix least‐squares on F2. Details of the crystal data for the polymer are summarized here: monoclinic, P21/c space group, a = 14.7883(6) Å, b = 19.4401(8) Å, c = 19.3386(10) Å, α = 90.00°, β = 99.722(3)°, γ = 90.00°, V = 5479.7(4) Å3, Z = 4, GOF = 1.237, Dcalcd. = 1.230 g cm–3, reflections collected/unique = 28102/9339. All data final R1 = 0.1036, ωR2 = 0.2880. CCDC‐1402607 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.
