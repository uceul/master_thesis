Introduction

Metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) with metal centers/clusters linked by functional organic ligands1 have shown great potential applications, such as gas adsorption,2â€“5 separations,6 catalysis,7 magnetism8,9 and sensing.10,11 Luminescent MOFs have been widely researched as luminescent materials for sensing ions and small organic molecules in recent years,12â€“14 because they possess excellent optical properties, relatively long emission wavelengths, and tunable structures.15 Metal ion sensing and detection are essential for environmental and biomedical applications.16 In particular, real-time and easy detection of trace amounts of Fe3+ ions are very important for the treatment of iron deficiency ailments, such as skin disease, insomnia and anemia.17 Therefore, the selective detection of Fe3+ ions has attracted great interest.18 A large number of MOF materials exhibiting metal ion-centered luminescence, such as lanthanide elements, have been reported in the literature.19 For example, Ln-MOFs, as fluorescent sensors, were used in detecting Fe3+ ions by cation-exchange.20â€“22 These MOFs, as chemosensors, attract great interest due to their simple manipulation, fast response and selective processes.

Recently, the study on using ligand-based emission in MOFs has become one of the fascinating fields.23,24 The significant advantage for the latter is that the structures of the frameworks in MOFs could be readily tuned through the variation of organic ligands.25 In organic ligands, the pyridyl nitrogen,26 amide group,27,28 anionic sulfonate group29 and hydroxy group,30 as binding sites, can be immobilized in luminescent MOFs. In addition, active functional groups (â€“COOH), as Ï€-conjugated organic molecules in rigid carboxylate ligands, provide luminescence and rigid backbones for luminescent MOFs.24

Herein we present four MOFs, Zn2(HL1)(H2O)0.8 (1), Cd2(L1)(DMF)2 (2), [Zn3(HL2)2]Â·[(CH3)2NH2]4 (3), and [Cd3(L2)2]Â·[(CH3)2NH2]6 (4) (H5L1 = 3,5-di(3â€²5â€²-dicarboxyphenyl)benzoic acid, H6L2 = 3,4-di(3,5-dicarboxyphenyl)phthalic acid, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide). They are synthesized based on rigid carboxylate ligands and display three-dimensional (3D) open framework structures. Compounds 1â€“4 are characterized by elemental analysis, infrared (IR) spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), single-crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and fluorescence spectroscopy. Remarkably, we find that compounds 1â€“4 exhibit luminescence changes in the presence of metal ions. In particular, they all show high sensitivity for Fe3+ ions and the luminescence is quenched in a 1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M DMF solution of Fe3+ ions. In addition, the four MOFs show reasonable recyclability and reproducibility of luminescence after regeneration. The extraordinary sensitivity and reusability of the four compounds make them wonderful sensors of Fe3+ ions in the future.

Experiments of metal ion sensing

(I) Luminescence measurements: the fully ground powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 (5 mg, respectively), as microcrystalline solids, were immersed in 3 mL of 1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M DMF solutions of 14 metal ions: M(NO3)x (M = Na+, K+, Li+, Ag+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Pb2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Cd2+, Zn2+, Al3+, Fe3+, respectively), at room temperature for 24 h.

(II) Different concentrations of Fe3+ dependent-luminescence measurements: the fully ground powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 (5 mg, respectively), as microcrystalline solids, were immersed in 3 mL of different concentrations (1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’4â€“1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M) of Fe(NO3)3 DMF solution at room temperature for 24 h.

(III) Reusability measurements in DMF solvent: the powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 were collected from the DMF solution of Fe3+ ions in (I). Then they were centrifuged and washed with DMF three times. After drying, the powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 were immersed in 3 mL of DMF solvent at room temperature for 24 h. Then the luminescence of regenerated MOFs in DMF solvent (R-1-DMF, R-2-DMF, R-3-DMF and R-4-DMF) was investigated.

(IV) Reusability measurements in DMF solution of Fe3+ ions: the powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 from (III) were centrifuged and dried. Then they were immersed in 3 mL of 1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M DMF solution of Fe3+ ions (R-1-Fe, R-2-Fe, R-3-Fe and R-4-Fe) at room temperature for 24 h.

The data were the means obtained from three measurements on independent luminescence sensing experiments.

Crystal structure of compound 1

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic P2(1)/c space group. Structural analysis of 1 shows that it contains one dinuclear [Zn2(HO2CR)(O2CR)4] cluster (Fig. S3aâ€ ). The ZnII(1) center is coordinated to four O atoms from the carboxylate group of four HL1 ligands. The ZnII(2) center is coordinated to six O atoms from the carboxylate group of four HL1 ligands and one solvent water molecule. The ZnII(1)â€“O bond lengths range from 1.960(2) to 1.994(2) Ã…, and the ZnII(2)â€“O bond lengths fall in the range 1.998(3)â€“2.224(3) Ã… (Table S1â€ ). Each ZnII is linked with four HL1 ligands and each HL1 ligand coordinates eight ZnII ions. In the structure, the HL1 ligand adopts one type of coordination mode, and the dihedral angles between the central and terminal benzene rings are 34.43Â° and 36.46Â°, respectively (Fig. S1aâ€ ). Two HL1 ligands linked with two dinuclear [Zn2(HO2CR)(O2CR)4] clusters form a distorted metalâ€“organic square (MOS), which interconnects to form 1D chains along the c axis (Fig. S3bâ€ ). These 1D chains are alternately connected by HL1 ligands and the dinuclear [Zn2(HO2CR)(O2CR)4] clusters to generate a 3D framework (Fig. 1c). Sensing metal ions

Compounds 1â€“4 are assembled from the luminescent rigid ligands H5L1 and H6L2 and the d10 configuration ions ZnII and CdII, which all show strong emission peaks at 342 nm, 358 nm, 390 nm and 420 nm, respectively (Î»max = 481 nm, Î»ex = 410 nm for H5L1, Î»max = 480 nm, Î»ex = 350 nm for H6L2, Fig. S10â€ ). Compared to the ligands, the emission intensities of the compounds are enhanced. The emission spectra of 1 and 2 display an obvious blue shift compared to that of the H5L1 ligand, and the emission spectra of 4 exhibit an obvious red shift compared to that of the H6L2 ligand, which could be attributed to aggregation.36 The coordination of the ligands and the metal ions can decrease the freedom of the ligands and their non-radiative transitions.24 The emission of compound 3 is very similar to that of the H6L2 ligand, which may be attributed to the ligand-centered Ï€â€“Ï€* electronic transitions.

Subsequently, the study of the four luminescent materials as potential sensors was conducted. The fully ground powder samples of compounds 1â€“4 (5 mg, respectively) were immersed in 3 mL of 1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M DMF solutions of 14 metal ions: M(NO3)x (M = Na+, K+, Li+, Ag+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Pb2+, Co2+, Ni2+, Cu2+, Cd2+, Zn2+, Al3+, Fe3+, respectively), at room temperature for 24 h, forming Mn+@1, Mn+@2, Mn+@3 and Mn+@4 for the luminescence study. Fig. 5 indicates that the luminescence intensities have changed for compounds 1â€“4 in the presence of the 14 metal ions. In addition, compared to the original luminescence intensities, those of Mn+@1 are weakened; while both enhancement and weakening occur in Mn+@2, Mn+@3 and Mn+@4. Fig. 6 shows that the influence of the 14 metal ions on the luminescence intensities of 1 is greater than those of 3, and the influence on 4 is greater than that on 2. The results demonstrate that the different ligands of compounds 1â€“4 have important effects on the luminescence intensity variations of Mn+@MOFs. Fig. 5 and 6 also show that Fe3+ ions have significant quenching effects on the luminescence.

Compared with the initial luminescence intensities of 1â€“4 immersed in DMF solvent, those of Fe3+@1, Fe3+@2, Fe3+@3 and Fe3+@4 are decreased by 99.75%, 99.87%, 99.95% and 99.91%, respectively, under the same experimental conditions. The above results prove that compounds 1â€“4 can be used for highly selective sensing of Fe3+ ions.

To study the influence of Fe3+ concentration on the luminescence intensities of compounds 1â€“4, we prepared the DMF solution of Fe3+ ions in the concentration range of 1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’4â€“1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M. Fig. 7 shows that the luminescence intensities of Fe3+@1, Fe3+@2, Fe3+@3 and Fe3+@4 gradually decrease with increasing concentration of Fe3+ ions. The unique quenching behavior herein may be attributed to the interaction between Fe3+ ions and oxygen atoms of carboxylates from ligands in compounds 1â€“4, and the intramolecular charge-transfer process.40 In the structures of compounds 1â€“2, each metal ion is linked with four HL1 ligands. In compounds 3â€“4, each metal ion is linked with four HL2 ligands and four L2 ligands. The large number of O atoms might offer active sites for the effective interaction between Fe3+ and 1â€“4, which causes effective quenching of the luminescence intensity.

PXRD data (Fig. S8â€ ) confirmed that the regenerated R-1-DMF, R-2-DMF, R-3-DMF and R-4-DMF are identical to the initial samples. Remarkably, the fluorescence quenching effects happened once again (R-1-Fe, R-2-Fe, R-3-Fe and R-4-Fe). PXRD data also identified that compared with the pristine structures of 1â€“4, no obvious change was found in the four immersed solid samples in Fe3+ ion solution. The above experiments reveal that compounds 1â€“4 show reasonable recyclability and reproducibility of luminescence after regeneration.

Conclusions

In summary, four compounds 1â€“4 have been constructed based on rigid carboxylate ligands (H5L1 and H6L2) and feature 3D structures with remarkably selective sensing of Fe3+ ions by the fluorescence quenching effect. Different concentrations of Fe3+ ions dependent-luminescence measurements in DMF solvent in the range of 1.0 Ã— 10âˆ’4â€“1.1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 M confirm that the luminescence intensities of 1â€“4 gradually diminish as the concentration of Fe3+ increases. The reason may be that the interaction between Fe3+ ions and oxygen atoms of carboxylates from ligands in compounds 1â€“4 has weakened the luminescence intensities. Interestingly, the luminescence intensities of 1â€“4 and the concentration of Fe3+ ions could match a first-order exponential equation well, indicating that the crystalline products of compounds 1â€“4 can be used as luminescent sensors to detect Fe3+ ions. Finally, through centrifugation and washing with DMF solvent, certain recoveries of the luminescence intensities appear in 1â€“4 without structural changes. The reasonable recyclability and reproducibility of luminescence make compounds 1â€“4 excellent Fe3+ sensors in the future.
