Abstract

A porous rtl metal–organic framework (MOF) [Mn5L(H2O)6⋅(DMA)2]⋅5DMA⋅4C2H5OH (1⋅Mn) (H10L=5,10,15,20‐tetra(4‐(3,5‐dicarboxylphenoxy)phenyl)porphyrin; DMA=N,N′‐dimethylacetamide) was synthesized by employing a new porphyrin‐based octacarboxylic acid ligand. 1⋅Mn exhibits high MnII density in the porous framework, providing it great Lewis‐acid heterogeneous catalytic capability for the cycloaddition of CO2 with epoxides. Strikingly, 1⋅Mn features excellent catalytic activity to the cycloaddition of CO2 to epoxides, with a remarkable initial turnover frequency 400 per mole of catalyst per hour at 20 atm. As‐synthesized 1⋅Mn also exhibits size selectivity to different epoxide substrates on account of their steric hindrance. The high catalytic activity, size selectivity, and stability toward the epoxides on catalytic cycloaddition of CO2 make 1⋅Mn a promising heterogeneous catalyst for fixation and utilization of CO2.

Introduction

Carbon dioxide (CO2) as the primary greenhouse gas has raised a great deal of concern owing to its resulting linkage effect on global warming and climate change.1 Thereby, the development of efficient CO2 capture technologies has received global attention.2 In this regard, CO2 activation and efficient transformation into valuable chemicals by chemical fixation are highly attractive because it is a nontoxic, cheap, and renewable carbon resource for making carbohydrates.3 In terms of chemical fixation of CO2, one of the most green approaches is the synthesis of cyclic carbonates from CO2 and epoxides by 100 % atom‐economical reaction.4 In particular, the promising target cyclic carbonates could be widely utilized as precursors for producing polycarbonates as well as a large number of fine chemicals.5 Thus far, numerous heterogeneous and homogeneous catalysts have been developed for CO2 cycloaddition to epoxides, in which homogeneous catalysts, such as quaternary ammonium and phosphonium salts, ionic liquids, metal complexes, and alkali metal salts, have been extensively studied.6 These homogeneous catalysts are effective, and some metal complexes are very active in the presence of co‐catalysts, but separation of the catalysts from the products is greatly difficult, restricting their wide application.7

In recent years, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), as a special class of crystalline porous materials, have received extensive attention for their applications in heterogeneous catalysis as well as gas storage.8 The modular nature and facile tunability of MOFs make them ideal heterogeneous catalysts with uniform active sites through wisely chosen building blocks.9 In this regard, porphyrin‐based porous MOFs have captured widespread research interest because of their excellent catalytic and biomimetic properties very recently.10 During the catalytic process, porphyrin‐based MOFs always function as mild Lewis acids.11 More importantly, the inclusion of porphyrin or metalloporphyrin in MOFs may lead to improving the stability and enriching the Lewis acid sites, which supports a high yield of the catalytic products.12

In the early reports, rigid porphyrin linkers have been studied and they played a significant role in targeting porous MOFs exhibiting superior catalytic performance.13 In contrast, flexible porphyrin‐based porous frameworks with fascinating structural motifs have been received less attention as the promising catalysts.14 Taking inspiration from the above consideration, for the first time, we explored the flexible octacarboxylate‐based 5,10,15,20‐tetra(4‐(3,5‐dicarboxylphenoxy)phenyl)porphyrin (H10L; Scheme 1), and present the successful fabrication of a porous rtl MOF [Mn5L(H2O)6⋅(DMA)2]⋅5DMA⋅4C2H5OH (1⋅Mn; DMA=N,N′‐dimethylacetamide). Remarkably, 1⋅Mn exhibits high MnII density in the porous framework, providing it great Lewis acid heterogeneous catalytic capability for the cycloaddition of CO2 with epoxides. More importantly, catalyst 1⋅Mn is stable, very active, and could be easily separated and reused.

Results and Discussion

Structural description of 1⋅Mn

Crystals of 1⋅Mn were synthesized with H10L and MnCl2⋅4 H2O under solvothermal conditions. During the refinement of 1⋅Mn, the disordered solvents were removed by applying the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.15 Thermal gravimetric (TG) analysis (Supporting Information, Figure S1), elemental analysis, and diffuse electron density in the void suggest the presence of five lattice DMA and four free ethanol molecules per formula unit. 1⋅Mn crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit of 1⋅Mn is composed of half an L anion, two and a half MnII atoms, one coordinated DMA molecule, and three coordinated water molecules. Three MnII atoms (Mn1, Mn2, and Mn3) exhibit the same octahedral coordination sphere but different coordination environments. Mn1 is coordinated with four pyrrolyl nitrogen atoms of porphyrin core, and two DMA oxygen atoms lying at the axial positions. The bond distances of Mn1−N1 and Mn2−N2 are 2.021 and 2.009 Å, respectively, which are located in the range of known MnII−N bonds.16 Mn2 is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms of three different porphyrin‐based L anions, whereas the coordination geometry of Mn3 is completed by three water oxygen atoms and three carboxylate oxygen atoms from two porphyrin‐based L anions. Three carboxylate groups bridge Mn2 and Mn3 atoms to afford a paddlewheel‐like Mn2(COO)4(H2O)3 secondary building unit (SBU), as illustrated in Figure 1 a. The backbone of each L anion bridges six paddlewheel‐like SBUs, and each SBU is linked by three L anions (Figure 1 a), then resulting in a remarkable 3D framework with an open channel along the a axis (16×7 Å2) (Figure 1 b–d). Topologically, the 6‐connected L anion and 3‐connected Mn2(COO)4(H2O)3 SBU correspond to the six‐coordinated Ti atom and three‐coordinated O atom in the rutile (rtl) net, respectively. Thus, the structure of 1⋅Mn will be simplified as a rtl net with a point symbol of (4⋅62)2(42⋅610⋅83). The total accessible volume of the unit cell was estimated to be 4260.1 Å3 by using PLATON, which accommodates approximately 55.8 % of the unit cell volume (7633.0 Å3).15

Gas adsorption of 1⋅Mn

The gas adsorption properties of activated 1⋅Mn were studied by CO2 adsorption at 298 K and N2 adsorption at 77 K. For 1⋅Mn, there is nearly no N2 adsorption at 77 K (Supporting Information, Figure S2). 1⋅Mn exhibits selective uptake for N2 and CO2. When the pressure is equal to the atmospheric pressure, CO2 uptake reaches to 25.51 cm3g−1 at 298 K. It can be found that about 4.24 CO2 molecules in each unit cell of 1⋅Mn is adsorbed at 1 bar when the adsorption amount is converted to the numbers of CO2. The selective adsorption of CO2 over N2 makes 1⋅Mn a possible catalyst candidate in capturing and converting CO2.17 Noticeably, TG analysis combined with FTIR analysis of activated 1⋅Mn indicates that the DMA molecules still remain coordinated after desolvation (Supporting Information, Figures S1, S3), which reduces the amount of space available for CO2 and then leads to the low uptake of CO2. Moreover, the PXRD pattern after the gas adsorption showed no change in the peak position (Supporting Information, Figure S4), demonstrating that the structure of 1⋅Mn was maintained.

Catalytic cycloaddition of CO2 to epoxides

Recently, MOFs have been shown to serve as heterogeneous Lewis acid catalysts for chemical conversion of CO2 into cyclic carbonates.18 Given the high affinity toward CO2 molecules and the high density of MnII ions confined in 1⋅Mn, detailed catalytic performance of CO2 fixation was evaluated for 1⋅Mn at both 1 atm and 20 atm, respectively. The catalytic experiments were conducted at 80 °C for 4 h with 1⋅Mn and nBu4NBr as co‐catalysts. The reaction of epichlorohydrin with CO2 at 1 atm was utilized as the model reaction to investigate the effect of various parameters on the catalytic performance (Scheme 2). The product carbonates were confirmed by 1H NMR studies (Supporting Information, Figures S5–S9) and the yields of the cyclic carbonates were calculated by gas chromatography (GC; Supporting Information, Figures S10–S13). When only 1⋅Mn was used as catalyst at atmospheric pressure of CO2, the product 4‐chloromethyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one was achieved in a 45 % yield with heating at 80 °C after 4 h (Supporting Information, Table S1, entry 1), demonstrating that 1⋅Mn could activate CO2 in this reaction. Once only the nBu4NBr was chosen as catalyst, just 31 % yield of the 4‐chloromethyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one was obtained for the cycloaddition of CO2 to epichlorohydrin (Supporting Information, Table S1, entry 2). Particularly, when 1⋅Mn was combined with the nBu4NBr as binary catalytic system, the substrates were completely converted to the carbonates after 4 h of reaction (Table 1, entry 1). The results indicate the excellent synergistic effect of 1⋅Mn and nBu4NBr on the catalysis of CO2 cycloaddition.19

To check the generality for such a cycloaddition reaction of CO2, a family of epoxides with variable dimensions were selected as substrates in following catalytic reactions (Supporting Information, Table S2). As shown in Table 1, the small substrate 1,2‐epoxyhexane was fully converted into the 4‐butyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one after 4 h of the reaction (entry 2). This observation suggests that the small substrate was easily carried out within the porous framework of 1⋅Mn for the catalytic reaction. Nevertheless, when the large substrates 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene, glycidyl phenyl ether, and benzyl glycidyl ether were utilized, the corresponding product yields revealed sharp decreases under the same conditions (entries 3–5); they were just 30 %, 41 %, and 43 %, respectively. This lower activity mainly accounts for the high steric hindrance of the substrates during the catalytic reaction, which influenced the transport of the substrates and products through the channels of 1⋅Mn.20 These results manifest that 1⋅Mn exhibits the size selectivity to small and large substrates in the catalytic process.21 To achieve high conversion rates, we have tried to extend the catalytic reaction time. As illustrated in Figure 2, the product yields were enhanced after 12 h of the catalytic reaction.

To further understand the catalytic activities of 1⋅Mn, a detailed kinetic study was also conducted in the presence of nBu4NBr as co‐catalyst. The catalytic reaction was performed at different time intervals from 1 to 4 h. As depicted in Figure 3, just 40 % of the epichlorohydrin was transformed to the 4‐chloromethyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one after 1 hour of the reaction, and the total conversion (>99 %) of the epichlorohydrin was accomplished after 4 h.

Moreover, to verify the heterogeneous nature of the reaction, catalyst 1⋅Mn was removed after 1 hour of the catalytic reaction, and then the reaction was further performed under the same condition as listed in entry 1. As shown in Figure 3, no obvious increase in the formation of the product carbonate was observed. Furthermore, inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analysis was employed to detect the reaction mixture filtrate at the end of the reaction. The ICP data suggested no MnII leaching, demonstrating the catalytic reaction is heterogeneous.

Further experiments were also conducted to examine the reusability of catalyst 1⋅Mn using the epichlorohydrin as the substrate. During the cycloaddition of CO2, catalyst 1⋅Mn can be easily separated in successive runs by filtrating and drying. As shown in Figure 4, 1⋅Mn keeps very high catalytic activity after four runs, indicating the good reusability of catalyst 1⋅Mn. The comparative PXRD patterns of as‐synthesized 1⋅Mn and recovered 1⋅Mn after 4 runs during the cycloaddition reaction was determined. All of the characteristic peaks of 1⋅Mn are well repeated, demonstrating a good structural integrity in successive runs of catalysis (Supporting Information, Figure S4).

In industrial applications, high pressure is always employed in CO2 coupling reactions.22 Thereby, the cycloaddition of CO2 by 1⋅Mn was also investigated at 20 atm (Supporting Information, Figure S14). In a typical experiment, the catalytic reaction was conducted in a high‐pressure microreactor using epoxides (20 mmol) as substrate with CO2 pressurized to 20 atm at 80 °C. As shown in Table 2, among various epoxides, the epichlorohydrin is the most active in producing the cyclic carbonate with a high turnover frequency (TOF) of 400 h−1 (entry 1). The substrate 1,2‐epoxyhexane was almost completely converted into the product carbonate within 4 h with a TOF of 200 h−1 (entry 2). The glycidyl phenyl ether (entry 4) and benzyl glycidyl ether (entry 5) were catalyzed by 1⋅Mn in conversion rates of 91 % and 92 %, respectively, after 4 h. Nevertheless, when the 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene was employed as the substrate, only 44 % of the cyclic carbonate was achieved after 4 h of the reaction. The results indicate that the catalytic activity and selectivity of 1⋅Mn at 20 atm are in accordance with those as demonstrated under ambient pressure.

Moreover, 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene was selected as the model reaction during the coupling reaction of CO2, as shown in Figure 5. The catalytic performance was detected at different time intervals. In the presence of 0.3 mmol of nBu4NBr, the loading of 0.125 mol % ratio of 1⋅Mn (based on Mn) afforded a 44 % conversion of the 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene with a turnover number (TON) 352 after 4 h of the reaction. Finally, 94 % of the 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene was converted to the product 4‐phenyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one and the TON value was 752 per mole of catalyst 1⋅Mn after 10 h. Remarkably, in comparison with the cycloaddition of CO2 taken at ambient pressure (Supporting Information, Table S1), approximately 20 times of the TON value of 1,2‐epoxyethylbenzene were achieved with the same mole catalyst 1⋅Mn at 20 atm, indicating that the conversion rate was improved markedly with the increasing CO2 pressure.

Furthermore, the stability and recyclability of catalyst 1⋅Mn were also examined at 20 atm. The PXRD patterns show that the structural integrity of 1⋅Mn was well maintained after catalysis at 20 atm (Supporting Information, Figure S4). After 5 runs, the yields of the product 4‐phenyl‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one slightly decreased and a total TON value of 3536 was achieved, demonstrating that the catalytic activity of 1⋅Mn was still retained during the cycloaddition of CO2 under high pressure (Figure 6).

On the basis of previous mechanistic studies as well as current catalytic results, a plausible synergistic catalytic mechanism is proposed in Scheme 3.23 At the beginning, the Lewis acidic MnII sites confined in 1⋅Mn interact with the oxygen atom of the epoxide, which then activate the epoxide ring. Meanwhile, the Br− anion from the nBu4NBr makes a nucleophilic attack on the less sterically hindered β‐carbon atom of the epoxide, producing the intermediate species (A) via a ring‐opening reaction. Subsequently, the oxygen atom of the intermediate (A) combined with CO2 to form a new intermediate (B). Oxygen atom of the intermediate (B) further attacks its inner carbon atom to release the Br− anion, forming the cyclic carbonate and regenerating the nBu4NBr. Obviously, the synergistic effect of the high density of Lewis acid MnII sites in 1⋅Mn and nBu4NBr as the co‐catalysts make the reaction proceed smoothly.

Conclusion

In summary, we have synthesized a unique porous rtl MOF catalyst 1⋅Mn by employing a new flexible porphyrin‐based octacarboxylic acid ligand. Remarkably, the constructed MOF 1⋅Mn, featuring selective CO2 adsorption and rich Lewis‐acid MnII sites, could serve as an excellent heterogeneous catalyst for CO2‐based chemical fixation, which has been clearly verified by the highly efficient catalytic cycloaddition of CO2 to epoxides at both 1 atm and 20 atm. 1⋅Mn also exhibits the highest catalytic activity with a TON of 800 per mole of catalyst 1⋅Mn in the first cycle at 20 atm. Importantly, 1⋅Mn shows the size selectivity to small and large epoxides in the catalytic process. The excellent catalytic activity, size selectivity, and good stability suggest that porphyrin‐based porous MOF 1⋅Mn is an efficient heterogeneous catalyst for the cycloaddition reaction of ubiquitous CO2 to epichlorohydrin and is quite promising for potential applications.

Experimental Section

Materials and methods: All reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial sources. The FTIR spectrum was measured in 4000–400 cm−1 range on a Mattson Alpha‐Centauri spectrometer. Elemental analysis was determined on a PerkinElmer 240 CHN elemental analyzer. PXRD patterns were recorded on a Rigaku Dmax 2000 X‐ray diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Cu Kα radiation (λ=0.154 nm). 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Varian 500 MHz. TG measurement was determined on a PerkinElmer TG‐7 analyzer under nitrogen gas. Gas adsorption–desorption properties were measured on an automatic volumetric adsorption apparatus (V‐Sorb 2800S). The catalytic products were measured by GC equipment with a capillary (30 m long × 0.25 mm i.d., WondaCAP 17) and FID detector (GC‐2014C, Shimadzu, Japan). ICP data were determined on a Leeman Labs Prodigy inductively coupled plasma‐optical atomic emission spectrometer (ICP‐AES).

Sample activation: Before the gas sorption and catalysis experiments, the as‐synthesized samples of 1⋅Mn were washed with DMF followed by methanol and then soaked in methanol for 2 days. The samples were then filtrated and activated at 120 °C for 12 h under vacuum to remove the guest solvents.

Cycloaddition of CO2 to epoxides at 1 atm: In a typical procedure, epoxide (1 mmol), nBu4NBr (32 mg, 0.1 mmol), and activated 1⋅Mn (13 mg, 0.005 mmol) were placed in a 10 mL reactor. The reactor was purged several times with CO2 and bubbled with 1 atm CO2. Then the reactor was heated to 80 °C and stirred for 12 h. The product structure was confirmed by 1H NMR and the yields were calculated by GC.

Cycloaddition of CO2 to epoxides at 20 atm: Typically, epoxide (20 mmol), nBu4NBr (97 mg, 0.3 mmol), and activated 1⋅Mn (13 mg, 0.005 mmol) were placed in a 25 mL stainless steel reactor. The reactor was pressurized with CO2 up to 20 atm and stirred at 80 °C. The product structure was also confirmed by 1H NMR and the yields were calculated by GC.

Synthesis of [Mn5L(H2O)6⋅(DMA)2]⋅5 DMA⋅4 C2H5OH: A mixture of H10L (20 mg, 0.015 mmol), MnCl2⋅4H2O (20 mg, 0.10 mmol), DMA (6 mL), ethanol (1 mL), and H2O (0.1 mL) was placed in a Teflon reactor (15 mL) and heated at 150 °C for 72 h. The Teflon reactor was then cooled to room temperature. The dark‐purple crystals were obtained in a 39 % yield based on H10L. Anal. calcd for C112H126Mn5N4O44: C 53.66, H 5.06, N 2.23; found: C 53.45, H 5.11, N 2.15. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3732 (w), 3433 (m), 2927 (w), 1658 (s), 1614 (s), 1565 (s), 1498 (s), 1448 (s), 1376 (s), 1246 (m), 1208 (m), 1164 (m), 1098 (m), 1008 (m), 967 (m), 860 (w), 811 (w), 778 (w), 715 (w), 670 (w).

X‐ray crystallography: Crystallographic data of 1⋅Mn was determined on an Oxford diffraction Gemini R CCD diffractometer with graphite‐monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Absorption correction was performed by a multi‐scan technique. The structure was solved by direct methods with SHELXS‐97 and refined on F 2 by full‐matrix least‐squares using the SHELXTL‐97 program within WINGX.24 Non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms on carbon were generated geometrically.
