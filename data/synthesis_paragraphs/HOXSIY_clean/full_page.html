Abstract

The zinc(II) coordination polymer [Zn3(BPT)2(μ2‐H2O)2(H2O)2]n·n(DMA) (1) (H3BPT = biphenyl‐3,4′,5‐tricarboxylic acid, DMA = N,N′‐dimethylactamide) was obtained by the solvothermal reaction of H3BPT with Zn(NO3)2 in DMA/H2O mixed solvent. Single crystal X‐ray analysis reveals that compound 1 has a complicated 3D framework containing linear trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] clusters as building subunits, which can be simplified into a (3,6)‐connected rtl topological network with the Schläfli symbol {4.62}2{42.610.83}. The calculated results of total and partial density of states (DOS) indicate that the luminescence of 1 mainly originates from intraligand charge transfer.

1 Introduction

In recent years, the design and syntheses of metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) have been extensively investigated not only owing to their fascinating crystal structures but also because of their potential applications in the areas of luminescence, gas storage, catalysis, magnetism, and sensing.1–3 Considering the potential applications of MOFs materials, chemists and material scientists have devoted considerable efforts on studying the relationship between the structures and properties, expecting to achieve target synthesis. However, up to now, the rational design of the target crystalline materials remains a difficult work. In the past few years, several synthetic strategies (such as mixed‐ligand self‐assembly strategy, pillaring strategy, post‐synthetic modification, building block method and so on) have been established.4–7 Among these strategies, the building block method is one of the most effective methods to synthesize new materials with predictable structures. Studies have proven that the versatile coordination modes and strong coordination abilities of polycarboxylate ligands make them easily link metal ions into variety of building subunits (such as di‐, tri‐, tetra‐, hexa‐nuclear clusters, rod‐shaped chains and so on).8–10 Therefore, the key factor for the building block method is the judicious selection of an appropriate organic polycarboxylate ligand. As an unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligand, biphenyl‐3,4,5‐tricarboxylic acid (H3BPT) is worthy of research because the three carboxylate groups may adopt distinct coordination modes, and may link different metal atoms into polynuclear building blocks. Based on the above considerations, in this work, the organic ligand H3BPT assembled with ZnII ions under solvothermal conditions. Thus, the new compound [Zn3(BPT)2(μ2‐OH2)2(H2O)2]n·n(DMA) (1) (H3BPT = biphenyl‐3,4′,5‐tricarboxylic acid, DMA = N,N′‐dimethylactamide), which is a rtl‐type 3D framework based on a linear trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunit, could be obtained.

2 Experimental Section

2.1 Materials and Methods

All starting materials and reagents used in this work were obtained commercially and used without further purification. Element analyses (C, H and N) were determined with an elemental Vairo EL III analyzer. FT‐IR spectra were measured as KBr pellets with a Nicolet Magna 750 FT‐IR spectrometer in the range of 400–4000 cm–1. TGA analyses were carried out with a NETSCHZ STA‐449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 in a nitrogen atmosphere. Fluorescence spectra of the solid samples were performed with an Edinburgh Analytical instrument FLS920. The calculation of DOS was carried out with density functional theory (DFT) using one of the three nonlocal gradient corrected exchange‐correlation functionals (GGA‐PBE) and performed with the CASTEP code in the Materials Studio V4.0 software package. Single crystal X‐ray diffraction was carried out with an Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer.

2.2 Synthesis of [Zn3(BPT)2(μ2‐ H2O)2(H2O)2]n·n(DMA) (1)

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.2 mmol, 0.060 g), H3BPT (0.1 mmol, 0.026 g), DMA (3 mL), and H2O (2 mL) was sealed in a small vial, which was kept at 100 °C for 60 h and finally cooled to the room temperature slowly. Colorless prism crystals yield in 43 % based on H3BPT. C34H31NO17Zn3: calcd. C 44.26; N 1.52; H 3.36 %; found C 44.23; N 1.48; H 3.35 %. IR (KBr): = 3468 (s), 2916 (w), 1632 (m), 1542 (s), 1508 (vs), 1436 (m), 1395 (s), 1364 (s), 1296 (w), 1112 (w), 1034 (w), 855 (w), 774 (w), 726 (w) cm–1.

2.3 X‐ray Crystallography

Single crystal X‐ray structure analysis of 1 was performed with an Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer (Mo‐Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å, graphite monochromator) at 293(2) K. Empirical absorption corrections were applied to the data using the SADABS program.11 The structure was solved by the direct method and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares on F2 using the SHELXL‐97 program.12 All of the non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and the hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were located at their ideal positions. 3 Result and Discussion

3.1 Crystal Structure Description of Compound 1

Single crystal X‐ray analysis reveals that compound 1 features a 3D rtl‐type framework. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains three crystallographically independent ZnII ions, two BPT3– ligands, two μ2‐water molecules, two terminal water molecules, and one free DMA molecule. As shown in Figure 1a, three crystallographically independent ZnII ions adopt distinct coordination arrangements. Zn1 is six‐coordinate with a octahedral coordination environment, defined by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four individual BPT3– ligands and two μ2‐water molecules. Zn2 is five‐coordinate by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different BPT3– ligands, one μ2‐water molecule and one terminal water molecule, displaying a trigonal‐bipyramidal arrangement. Zn3 is also five‐coordinate but with a slightly distorted square‐pyramidal environment defined by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different BPT3– ligands, one μ2‐water molecule and one terminal water molecule. The Zn–O distances range from 1.956(5) to 2.417(5) Å, which are all in the normal range according to previously reported ZnII compounds.13 It is noteworthy that two μ2‐water molecules and four carboxylate groups bridge three different ZnII ions into a linear trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunit with Zn···Zn distance of 3.5 Å (Figure 2a). Further, these trinuclear subunits are interconnected by BPT3– ligands, forming the final 3D framework of 1 with the free DMA molecules captured in the skeleton (Figure 2b). The carboxylate groups of each BPT3– ligand show two kinds of coordination modes: two are in bridging mode and the third in monodentate mode. In the framework of 1, each BPT3– ligand connects three different trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunits, and each trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunit is surrounded by six different BPT3– ligands. Topologically, if BPT3– ligands and trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunits can be viewed as 3‐, 6‐connected nodes, respectively, the complicate framework of 1 can be simplified into a (3,6)‐connected rtl topological network with the Schläfli symbol of {4.62}2{42.610.83} (Figure 2c).

In the past few years, H3BPT has been widely used as the organic building block, and a lot of BPT3–‐based coordination polymers have been reported.14–17 Among the reported BPT3–‐based ZnII compounds,18,19, most of them are based on dinuclear or tetranuclear subunits and to the best of our knowledge, such compound presented herein are the first example based on linear trinuclear subunits. Therefore, such framework further enriches the coordination chemistry of H3BPT.

3.2 PXRD and Thermal Analysis

The powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of 1 is in agreement with that simulated from the single crystal diffraction data, indicating the purity of 1 (Figure 3a). Thermogravimetric analysis for 1 was performed on bulk samples in a nitrogen atmosphere from 30 to 800 °C. As shown in Figure 3b, the first weight loss of 9.22 % (calcd. 9.44 %) occurring between 85 °C and 152 °C corresponds to the removal of the free DMA molecules. The second weight loss of 7.98 % (calcd. 7.81 %) occurred in the temperature range of 152–210 °C can be assigned to the departure of the coordinated water molecules. Then, no further weight loss appeared up to 300 °C, at which point the compound begins to decompose. The final residue (obsd. 26.06 %, calcd. 26.36 %) may be ZnO powder.

3.3 Luminescent Properties

The luminescent property of compound 1 was investigated in the solid state at room temperature (Figure 4a). Upon excitation of 336 nm, compound 1 exhibits luminescence with emission maxima at 378 nm. From previous reports it is known that pure H3BPT ligand has a maximum emission at 395 nm (λex = 340 nm).19 Notably, the emission spectrum of 1 is similar with that of the pure H3BPT with a slight blue‐shift of 17 nm. Therefore, the luminescent emission of 1 is neither ligand‐to‐metal charge transfer nor metal‐to‐ligand charge transfer in nature, and may be assigned to intraligand charge transfer. To get better understanding of the relation between the electronic structure and photoluminescent property, the total and partial density of states (DOS) of 1 is theoretically calculated using the Materials Studio software package. As shown in Figure 4b, the valence bands between energy –5.0 eV and the Fermi level (0.0 eV) mainly originate from the p–π orbitals of BPT3– ligand, and the conduction bands between energy 0 and 5 eV are mostly contributed by the p–π* anti‐bond orbitals of BPT3– ligand, whereas the zinc atoms make no contribution to the top of the valence bands or the bottom of the conduction bands (CBs). These results further indicate that the luminescence of compound 1 originates from the intraligand charge transfer.

4 Conclusions

A ZnII coordination polymer was solvothermally synthesized by using an unsymmetrical organic linker. Compound 1 features a complicated 3D framework based on trinuclear [Zn3(COO)4(μ2‐H2O)2] subunits, and can be reduced into a (3,6)‐connected rtl topological network. The relevant density of states (DOS) calculation result showed that the luminescence of 1 mainly originates from intraligand charge transition.
