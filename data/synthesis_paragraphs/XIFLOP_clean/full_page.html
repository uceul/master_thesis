Alkyl substituents introduced into novel d10-metalimidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate frameworks: synthesis, structure diversities and photoluminescence properties†

Quan-Guo Zhai *, Rong-Rong Zeng , Shu-Ni Li , Yu-Cheng Jiang and Man-Cheng Hu *
Key Laboratory of Macromolecular Science of Shanxi Province, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Shanxi Normal University, Xi'an, 710062, Shaanxi, P. R. China. E-mail: zhaiqg@snnu.edu.cn; hmch@snnu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-29-81530727; Tel: +86-29-81530767

Received 4th July 2012 , Accepted 13th November 2012

First published on 14th November 2012

Seven novel d10-metal–imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate compounds namely [Zn(H2EIMDC)2(H2O)2]·2.25H2O (1), [Ag2(HPIMDC)(HPIM)2] (2), [Zn(HPIMDC)(H2O)]n (3), {[Cd(H2PIMDC)2]}n (4), {(H3O)[Cd(MIMDC)]}n (5), {(H3O)[Cd(EIMDC)]}n (6) and {(H3O)[Cd2(PIMDC)(HPIMDC)]}n (7), (H3MIMDC = 2-methyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, H3EIMDC = 2-ethyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, and H3PIMDC = 2-propyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, HPIM = 2-propyl-1H-imidazole), have been synthesized by careful control of the synthetic conditions such as the solvent, pH value and the metal-to-ligand molar ratios. X-ray single-crystal structural analyses reveal that the change of alkyl groups on the 2-position of the ligand leads to the diversity of their structures, which range from discrete compounds (1 and 2), one-dimensional chain (3), two-dimensional layer (4) to three-dimensional frameworks (5, 6 and 7). Specially, under the same reaction conditions, the extending of the 2-position alkyl substitutents gradually reduces the symmetry of the ultimate 3D frameworks, which vary from tetragonal I4/mmm (5), tetragonal P42/nmc (6) to orthorhombic Pbam (7) space groups. Alkyl-substituted imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate ligands exhibit either singly, doubly, or triply deprotonated fashion, and coordinate to Zn(II), Cd(II) or Ag(I) ions in terminal μ2-, μ3- or μ4-modes. Compounds 1–7 have also been characterized by means of elemental analyses, X-ray powder diffractions, FT-IR, TGA and gas-adsorption. Furthermore, solid-state photoluminescence measurements show that all these d10-metal compounds produce strong blue emissions at room temperature.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) via the deliberate selection of metals and multifunctional ligands have provoked significant interest, not only owing to their intriguing variety of architectures1 but also because of their potential applications in ion-exchange,2 gas storage,3 multiphase catalysis,4 magnetism,5 luminescence6 and so on. It is well-known that a lot of factors, such as the coordination environment of the metal centres, the choice of ligands, metal–ligand ratio, reaction conditions etc. can affect the self-assembly process.7 Among these factors, the judicious selection for multifunctional organic ligands is a more effective approach to construct MOFs with unique structures and properties. The organic ligands containing N and O donors, especially the N-heterocyclic carboxylates,8 are excellent candidates for preparing novel MOFs, because of their versatile coordination modes and potential hydrogen bonding donors and acceptors.

In recent years, imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3IMDC) and its derivatives have attracted much attention in the preparation of interesting polymeric frameworks. These ligands containing two N and four O atoms have been chosen as multidentate ligands based on their multiple coordination sites for rigid and stable higher dimensional structures. Meanwhile, the arrangement of the two N atoms with a N–X–N angle of about 144° in the imidazole ring, which is close to the Si–O–Si angle typically found in zeolites, can favorably gear the coordination orientation, sometimes strengthened by the cooperative coordination of the adjacent carboxyl group and by the orientation of carboxyl groups from the plane of the imidazole ring which may allow IMDC ligands to be used for the construction of MOFs with helical structures. Furthermore, it has been found that the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligand can be partially or completely deprotonated to generate various species like H2IMDC−, HIMDC2− or IMDC3− at different pH values, hence may exhibit very flexible coordination modes and result in a large diversity of MOF architectures.

Up to now, a large number of MOFs based on the IMDC ligand possess interesting topological structures and useful properties have been reported.9 Accordingly, similar ligands, which only differ in groups on the 2-position of the imidazole ring also have been designed and studied extensively. For example, Wu et al. have reported [Ni4(HPyIMDC)4(bpy)(py)-(H2O)5]2·0.5H2O and {[Fe(HPyIMDC)(H2O)]·2H2O}n10 which grafted pyridyl groups into the backbone of H3IMDC at the 2-position, which was expected to directly result in assemblies like that in H3IMDC complexes. Comparatively, the additional pyridyl group in the backbone of H3IMDC will modulate its coordination ability to give more potential coordination modes and enlarge the conjugation π-system. Jing and co-workers also have reported a series of MOFs based on H3PyIMDC as the organic ligand to induce a spatial effect on adjacent carboxylate groups, permitting diverse coordination modes.10g Zheng and co-workers introduce a hydrophilic hydroxymethyl group on the 2-position of imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid.11 This group is flexible where it can either change its coordination mode or undergo in situ ligand formation by using different metal ions.

However, to the best of our knowledge, no systematic research about the effect of the bulk of the 2-position alkly-substituted groups has been reported to date.12 Considering what we have mentioned above, herein, we chose a series of flexible alkyl-substituted ligands, that is, 2-methyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, (H3MIMDC), 2-ethyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3EIMDC) and 2-propyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3PIMDC) to assemble with d10-metals (Zn(II), Cd(II) and Ag(I)) under hydrothermal conditions. As a result, we successfully obtained seven novel d10-metal–imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate frameworks, namely, [Zn(H2EIMDC)2(H2O)2]·2.25H2O (1), [Ag2(HPIMDC)(HPIM)2] (2), [Zn(HPIMDC)(H2O)]n (3), {[Cd(H2PIMDC)2]}n (4), {(H3O)[Cd(MIMDC)]}n (5), {(H3O)[Cd(HEIMDC)]}n (6) and {(H3O)[Cd2(PIMDC)(HPIMDC)]}n (7). The 2-position alkyl groups show notable influence on their structures, which range from discrete compounds (1 and 2), one-dimensional chain (3), two-dimensional layer (4) to three-dimensional frameworks (5, 6 and 7). In addition, solid-state properties such as gas-adsorption and photoluminescence properties have also been investigated.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals were of reagent grade quality obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. C, H, and N elemental analyses were performed using an Elementar vario EL III elemental analyzer. The Fourier-transform infrared spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded using a Nicolet Avatar 360 FT-IR Spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. X-ray powder diffraction data were recorded on a Rigaku multi-flex diffractometer with a scan speed of 0.02 min−1. Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on NETSCHZ STA-449C thermoanalyzer under a nitrogen atmosphere (40–1000 °C range) at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Fluorescence spectra were measured with a Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature using powdered crystal samples. Gas-adsorption measurements (N2) were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2010 surface-area and pore-size analyzer.

Preparation of [Zn(H2EIMDC)2(H2O)2]·2.25H2O (1)

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.1487 g, 0.5 mmol), H3EIMDC (0.2762 g, 1.5 mmol), and deionized water (7 mL) was stirred for 20 min in a 25 mL Teflon-lined reactor then sealed and heated at 180 °C for 5 days. After the mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 4 °C h−1, colorless plate-like crystals were obtained in 54.1% yield. Anal. calcd for C14H22.5N4O12.25Zn (%): C, 33.10; H, 4.43; N, 11.03. Found (%): C, 33.04; H, 4.28; N, 11.12. IR data (solid KBr pellet cm−1): 3405(br), 1690(w), 1542(vs), 1396(m), 1272(m), 1131(m), 1048(w), 968(w), 871(w), 779(w), 739(m), 658(m).

Preparation of [Ag2(H2PMIDC)(HPIM)2] (2)

A mixture of Ag(OAc) (0.084 g, 0.5 mmol) and H3PIMDC (0.0991 g, 0.5 mmol) in a molar ratio of 1:1 was dissolved in deionized water (6 mL) and stirred for 20 min in air. The resulting solution was transferred into a 25 mL Teflon-lined reactor and heated at 180 °C for 5 days. Colorless block crystals were obtained in 32% yield after the mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 4 °C h−1. Anal. calcd for C20H27N6O4Ag2 (%): C, 38.06; H, 4.31; N, 13.31. Found (%): C, 37.94; H, 4.65; N, 13.88. X-ray crystallography

Suitable single crystals of 1–7 were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to thin glass fibers. Crystallographic data for all compounds were collected with a Siemens smart CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at T = 293(2) K. Absorption corrections were made using the SADABS program.13 The structures were solved using the direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 by using the SHELX-97 program package.14 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Positions of the hydrogen atoms attached to carbon and nitrogen atoms were fixed at their ideal positions. Results and discussion

Syntheses

It is well-known that many factors can influence the assembly of metal and organic ligands under hydrothermal conditions. In our cases, the temperature is one key factor, which must be kept in the range of 160–180 °C. Our repeated experiments show that no crystals can be obtained if the temperature was decreased below 160 °C. On the other hand, the M:L ratios also play an important role in the formations of complexes 1–7. Our reported molar ratios of 1:3, 1:1, 1:1, 1:1, 1:2, 1:1 and 1:2 respectively have produced the crystals of 1–7 successfully, while other stoichiometry has failed to produce suitable crystals or has just given small crystalline powders. Furthermore, the metal source is also important. For the same organic ligand and reaction condition, the selection of Cd(OAc)2 led to a 2D layer compound 4, while the utilization of Cd(NO3)2 generated the 3D porous framework of 7. In our opinion, this is due to the buffer effect of OAc− which led to the formation of H2PIMDC− in 4, but the ligand in 7 is HPIMDC2− and completely deprotonated PIMDC3−.

Crystal structures of discrete compounds [Zn(H2EIMDC)2(H2O)2]·2.25H2O (1) and [Ag2(HPIMDC)(HPIM)2] (2)

X-ray single-crystal diffraction analysis shows that 1 and 2 are two discrete compounds, which both crystallize in the triclinic space group P. For compound 1, the asymmetric unit consists of a half independent Zn atom, one H2EIMDC− ligand and one coordinated water molecule. As is shown in Fig. 1a, the Zn(II) ion is six-coordinated by two nitrogen and two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two individual H2EIMDC− in the equatorial plane, and by two water oxygen atoms in axial positions, showing a slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The Zn–L (L = O, N) distances are in the range of 2.107(2)–2.169(2) Å, and the L–Zn–L bond angles vary from 88.36(8)° to 180.00(1)°. The H2EIMDC− anion acts as a chelating ligand through N, O atoms to form a well-known five-membered {ZnNC2O} ring (Scheme 1a). The hydrogen bonds (O5–H5C⋯O3) link the adjacent mononuclear molecule to form 1D chains along the b-axis direction, which are further connected by the crystallized water molecules via the hydrogen bonds (N2–H2⋯O6 and O6–H6E⋯O1) to generated a 2D layer structure in the b,c-plane. Through the O5–H5D⋯O4 hydrogen bonds, the 2D layers are packed along the a-axis direction to form the 3D supramolecular framework of compound 1 (Fig. 1b).

The asymmetric unit of 2 consists of two independent Ag(I), one μ2-HPIMDC2− and two in situ generated 2-propyl-imidazole ligands (HPIM), as shown in Fig. 2a. It should be noted that the propyl groups are 2-fold disordered. Two Ag atoms are both of two-coordinated linear geometry with the Ag–N bonds and N–Ag–N angles of 2.107–2.117 Å and 167.59(16)–171.63(16)°, respectively. The HPIMDC2− acts as a bidentate ligand adopting the μ2-kN:kN′ mode (Scheme 1b) to connect two Ag(I) centers, and two terminal 2-propyl-imidazole ligands help to form the binuclear structure of 2. Just like compound 1, the N6–H6⋯O1 hydrogen bonds link this binuclear cluster to form an infinite chain, which is further connected by the strong hydrogen bonds (N4–H4⋯O3) among the carboxyl–O and imidazole–N to give a 2D (4,4) supramolecular network of 2 (Fig. 1b).

Crystal structure of 1D compound [Zn(HPIMDC)(H2O)]n (3)

Complex 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, which exhibits a 1D zigzag chain. The asymmetric unit consists of one Zn(II) ion, one doubly deprotonated HPIMDC2− ligand with disordered propyl groups and one coordinated water molecule. As shown in Fig. 3a, the Zn(II) ion is five-coordinated to two chelating μ2-HPIMDC2− ligands through O3, N1 and O5, N2, and one oxygen atom from the coordinated water molecule. With two stable five-membered rings around Zn(II), it displays as a distorted square pyramid. The Zn–L (L = O, N) distances are in the range of 1.980(2)–2.252(2) Å, and the L–Zn–L bond angles are in the range of 81.12(8)–177.59(8)°. Each HPIMDC2− anion adopts a μ2-kN,O:kN′,O′ mode (Scheme 1c) to bridge two adjacent Zn(II) ions. Such a linking mode leads to a 1D zigzag chain along the b-axis direction as shown in Fig. 3b. The intermolecular hydrogen bonds (O4–H4⋯O2) contribute to stabilize the complex 3 to form a 2D sheet (Fig. 3c) in the b,c-plane.

Crystal structure of 2D compound [Cd(H2PIMDC)2]n (4)

Complex 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2(1)/n, and displays a 2D sheet structure. It should be noted that although all the cell angles are 90°, an esd of (3) for β is the key factor for the monoclinic space groups. The asymmetric unit consists of half a Cd(II) and one singly deprotonated H2PIMDC− ligand. Each Cd(II) ion shows a slightly distorted octahedral geometry, being chelated by two individual μ2-H2PIMDC− ligands in an N, O mode in the equatorial plane to form two stable five-membered rings, and two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2 and O2′) from different ligands occupy the axial positions, as shown in Fig. 4a. The Cd–O bond lengths vary from 2.3587(18) Å to 2.3613(18) Å, and the Cd–N bond length is 2.2459(19) Å. The bond angles around the Cd ion fall in the range of 73.22(6) to 180.00(8)°. Each H2PIMDC− anion adopts a μ2-kN,O:kO′ mode (Scheme 1d) to interconnect two Cd atoms to form a 2D infinite grid-like (4,4) layer with four cadmium atoms at the corners and H2PIMDC− ligands as linkers (Fig. 4b), which is similar to the structure reported by Liu et al.15
Fig. 4 View of the basic coordination environments (a-top) and the 2D (4,4) layer structure (b-bottom) of compound 4.

Crystal structure of 3D frameworks {(H3O)[Cd(MIMDC)]}n (5), {(H3O)[Cd(HEIMDC)]}n (6) and {(H3O)[Cd2(PIMDC)(HPIMDC)]}n (7)

Complex 5 crystallizes in the tetragonal I4/mmm space group and displays a 3D microporous metal–organic framework. As is shown in Fig. 5a, the coordination environment around each Cd(II) ion can be described as a slightly distorted octahedron. It is coordinated by two MIMDC3− ligands with O, N chelating donors (N1, O2, N1′ and O2′) to form two stable five-membered chelating rings, and is monodentate with two carboxyl oxygen atoms from the other two organic ligands. The Cd–L (L = O, N) distances are in the range of 2.266(7)–2.431(9) Å. It should be pointed out that each MIMDC3− ligand is a 2-fold disorder (Fig. 5b), which adopts a μ4-kN,O:kO′′:kN′,O′:kO′′′ mode (Scheme 1e) to bridge four Cd(II) ions in N, O chelating and O monodentating fashion. Thus, the μ4-MIMDC3− ligands and Cd centers are linked to each other to form the 3D anionic framework of 5. In this framework, hydrophilic channels and hydrophobic channels are arranged alternately along the c-axis direction (Fig. 5c) with the dimensions of about 3.2 × 3.2 Å2 and 1.8 × 1.8 Å2, respectively. The H3O+ cations are entrapped in the hydrophilic channels to balance the charge of the anionic framework.
Fig. 5 The basic coordination environments (a-top), 2-fold disorder ligand (b-bottom), and the 3D microporous framework (c-bottom) of compound 5.

Under the same reaction condition, when H3EIMDC was used instead of H3MIMDC, we obtained complex 6, the other 3D microporous framework crystallizing in the tetragonal P42/nmc space group. Each Cd(II) is six-coordinated by two N and two O atoms from two completely deprotonated EIMDC3− ligands in a bis-N,O-chelating fashion and two carboxylate oxygen atoms from the other two individual organic ligands, forming an octahedral geometry. The Cd–L (L = O, N) distances and the L–Cd–L bond angles are in the range of 2.267(5)–2.439(4) Å and 66.98(16)–163.6(2)°, respectively. Each EIMDC3− has the μ4-kN,O:kO′′:kN′,O′:kO′′′ coordination mode, functioning as tetradentate ligand to interconnect the cadmium atoms (Scheme 1f). Thus, two [CdNC2O] five-membered chelating rings and two [Cd2O2] four-membered rings are observed around each Cd(II) center. Both the five-membered rings and four-membered rings contribute to the stabilization of complex 6 to form a 3D anionic network. Just like 5, two kinds of 1D open channels (Fig. 6b) are observed along the c-axis direction, of which one is partially filled with the ethyl from EIMDC3−, while the other is filled with guest H3O+ cations as well as oxygen atoms from the carboxyl groups. It is noteworthy that although the anionic frameworks of 5 and 6 are similar to each other, the slight change of the 2-position substituents on the organic ligands leads to different crystallization (I4/mmm for 5 and P42/nmc for 6).


Fig. 6 View of the basic coordination environments (a-top) and the 3D microporous framework (b-bottom) of compound 6.

When we further prolonged the 2-position substituent by using 2-propyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3PIMDC) as the ligand, compound 7, another 3D metal–organic framework crystallizing in the orthorhombic Pbam space group was obtained. Single-crystal X-ray analysis revealed that two crystallographically independent Cd(II) ions, one PIMDC3− anion (Scheme 1g), one HPIMDC2− anion (Scheme 1h), and one H3O+ ion exist in each asymmetric unit. The propyl groups are badly disordered. The two independent Cd ions both exhibit six-coordinated slightly distorted [CdN2O4] octahedral geometry generated by three different organic ligands (Fig. 7a). Two [CdNC2O] five-membered chelating rings and one [Cd2O2] four-membered ring are observed around each Cd(II) center to stabilize the octahedral geometry. The Cd–L (L = O, N) bond distances vary from 2.204(11) to 2.424(12) Å, and the L–Cd–L bond angles are in the range of 71.6(4)–163.4(4)°. Two independent Cd(II) atoms are connected to each other through μ2-O5 and μ2-O7 carboxyl oxygen atoms to form a [Cd2L3] binuclear cluster, which is further symmetrically joined by the other two organic ligands to give a [Cd4L8] linear tetranuclear motif. Furthermore, each [Cd4] cluster connects the adjacent six tetranuclear units (Fig. 7b) to form the 3D metal–organic framework of compound 7, which has the familiar NaCl-topology if the tetranuclear clusters are regarded as 6-conneted nodes. The 1D channels in this network along the c-axis direction are totally occupied by the disordered propyl groups from the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligands (Fig. 7c). The successful isolation of the low-symmetrical complex 7 (Pbam) by introducing a longer propyl group clearly shows that the 2-position substituent of imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligands can greatly affect the ultimate metal–organic framework.


Fig. 7 View of the basic coordination environments (a-top), the linking mode between tetranuclear motifs (b-bottom), and the 3D microporous framework (c-bottom) of compound 7.

Ligand introduced structural diversity of compounds 1–7

The elaborate selection of organic ligands is vital to the construction of novel metal–organic frameworks. Herein, we show a series of d10-metal–imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylate frameworks introduced by the change of the 2-position substituent. Under the same reaction conditions, the methyl-substituted ligand (H3MIMDC) led to the formation of 5 crystallizing in tetragonal I4/mmm space group, the ethyl-substituted ligand (H3EIMDC) generated compound 6 of tetragonal P42/nmc space group, and the propyl-substituted ligand (H3PIMDC) further gave birth to complex 7 in orthorhombic Pbam space group. In our opinion, the extending of 2-position substituents reduces the symmetry of the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligand, which further reduces the symmetry of the ultimate 3D frameworks. On the other hand, the deprotonation of the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligands is the other important factor for the structural diversities. As shown in Scheme 1a–1h, H3MIMDC, H3EIMDC and H3PIMDC can exhibit various coordination modes via two carboxylate groups and two imidazole nitrogen atoms under appropriate reaction conditions. H3MIMDC only displays one coordination mode in 5, removing the imidazole–H and COO–H to form the HMIMDC2− unit. For H3EIMDC, the imidazole–H is removed to form the H2EMIDC− anion in 1, but in 6, both the imidazole–H and COO–H are removed to give the HEIMDC2− unit. With respect to H3PIMDC, under different reaction conditions and reacting with different metals, it can show different deprotonation behaviors. As mentioned above, the buffer effect of OAc− led to the formation of H2PIMDC− in 4, but this ligand in 7 is HPIMDC2− (imidazole–H and COO–H removed) and completely deprotonated PIMDC3−. The doubly deprotonated HPIMDC2− units can also be found in 3. Moreover, compound 2 exhibits a decarboxylation process. This is possibly caused by the nature of the Ag(I) ions, such as the lower coordination number and softer acidity than that of the Zn(II) and Cd(II) ions. It should be noted that the similar decarboxylation from azole has been reported previously, for example, the in situ generation of 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole from 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole-5-carboxylic acid.16

FT-IR, XRPD and TGA

Since the organic ligands in complexes 1–7 are all imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid derivatives, their FT-IR spectra are similar (Fig. S1, ESI†). The strong and broad absorption bands in the range of 3400–3500 cm−1 indicate the presence of the νN–H and the νO–H stretching frequencies of the imidazole ring and/or water molecules. Furthermore, the carboxylate groups in these compounds exhibit strong characteristic absorptions around 1550–1561 cm−1 (νasCOO−) and 1400–1466 cm−1 (νsCOO−).

As shown in Fig. S2–S8, (ESI†) complexes 1–7 were characterized via X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) at room temperature. All the XRPD patterns measured for the as-synthesized samples were in good agreement with the XRPD patterns simulated from the respective single-crystal X-ray data using the Mercury 1.4 program.17

Since thermal stability is an important quality for the application of metal–organic crystalline materials, we also performed the thermal gravimetric analysis for these materials. The TG–DTA curves for complexes 1–7 are also provided in Fig. S2–S8, ESI.† For 1, the first weight loss of 14.70% from 40 to 180 °C is attributed to the release of the free water molecules and the coordination water molecules (calcd 15.10%). The second weight loss of 71.50% occurred between 180 to 990 °C, which is probably due to the decomposition of the organic ligand (calcd 72.07%). Complex 2 is stable up to 190 °C, and then a rapid weight loss up to 300 °C may be attributed to the loss of organic ligands. On further heating, the second weight loss in the range of about 520–650 °C may correspond to the decomposition of Ag2O, and the ultimate residue should be Ag (exp. 34.30%, calcd. 34.22%). For complex 3, the TG curve exhibits an initial weight loss starting at about 120 °C with the observed weight loss of 5.72% corresponding to the release of coordinated water molecules (calcd. 6.43%). Then, a series of sharp weight losses of 68.58% occurs from 250 to 600 °C corresponding to the decomposition of the organic group (calcd. 70.25%). The remnants are 25.76%, which should be ZnO (calcd. 27.32%). Complex 4 is stable up to 260 °C, and then the sharp weight loss of 72.8% was observed up to 655 °C, which may be attributed to the gradual release of two H2PIMDC− organic groups (calcd. 74.55%). The residue accounts for 27.2%, which is nearly in agreement with the calculated value of 25.45%, by assuming the final product is CdO. As shown in Fig. S6–S8, (ESI†) compounds 5–7 show similar thermal behaviors. The TG curves of 5, 6 and 7 indicate the slow release of guest water molecules from room temperature to approximately 350 °C to give their dehydrated forms. On further heating, the imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid ligands start to be released, and sharp weight losses for 5, 6 and 7 occur at about 330, 360 and 335 °C, respectively. The residue for these three compounds all should be part of CdO because CdO can be sublimated above 700 °C.

Photoluminescence properties

It is well known that d10-metal coordination polymers are good candidates for luminescence materials.18 The luminescent properties of complexes 1, 3, 5, 6, 7 and the free ligands were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. Compounds 1, 6 and H3EIMDC ligands exhibit blue photoluminescence with emission maxima at 375, 378, and 398 nm upon excitation at 302, 323 and 302 nm, respectively (Fig. 8 top, S10, S12 and S15, ESI†). Although the maximum emission wavelengths of complexes 1 and 6 undergo a slight blue-shift, the emission bands for complexes 1 and 6 are very similar to that found for the free ligand in terms of position and band shape. Therefore, the emission bands of complexes 1 and 6 are mainly due to an intraligand emission state. As shown in Fig. 8 middle (Fig. S9, ESI†), strong photoluminescence emission bands at 450 nm (λex = 364 nm) are observed for the free ligand H3MIMDC. However, compound 5 shows a strong blue emission band (Fig. S14, ESI†) at 339 nm (λex = 270 nm), which should not be due to the intraligand emission. In our opinion, the emission of compound 5 may be attributed to the metal to ligand charge transfer (MLCT). Compounds 3, 7 and H3PIMDC also show strong emission with maxima at 325, 335 and 464 nm upon the excitation of 271, 265 and 364 nm, respectively (Fig. 8 bottom, S11, S13 and S16, ESI†). Compared to the free ligands, compounds 3 and 7 display an obvious blue shift of 135 and 125 nm. Thus, just like 5, the emission nature of 3 and 7 are probably due to the significant MLCT charge-transfer character, as reported for Cd(II) or other d10 metal complexes based on the position and shape of the emission band.19

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized and characterized a series of novel metal–organic crystalline compounds under hydrothermal conditions by using 2-methyl-, 2-ethyl-, or 2-propyl-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid as bridging ligands. The bulk of the 2-position groups and the different deprotonation process of the ligands are two important factors for the structural diversities of these compounds. All the compounds show high thermal stability and strong blue photoluminescence emissions ascribed to the intraligand charge transfer or MLCT. Further investigations for the influence of 2-position groups on metal–organic frameworks are ongoing in our lab.
