A ketone functionalized luminescent terbium metal–organic framework for sensing of small molecules†

Zhiyong Guo a, Xuezhi Song b, Huaping Lei c, Hailong Wang a, Shengqun Su b, Hui Xu d, Guodong Qian d, Hongjie Zhang *b and Banglin Chen *a

cKey Laboratory of Materials Physics, Institute of Solid State Physics, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Hefei 230031, China

A ketone functionalized luminescent terbium metal–organic framework has been realized for the highly selective and sensitive sensing of aniline.

There has been an extensive interest shown in the recognition and sensing of aromatic amines because of their important roles in biological and environmental systems. Aniline and its derivatives (e.g., nitroanilines) are very toxic to human beings.1 Although aniline and its derivatives can be precisely and quantitatively detected using gas chromatography and other instrumental analyses,2 very few facile methods for the detection of trace amounts of aromatic amines have been realized.3

The collaborative permanent porosity and luminescence properties have enabled luminescent metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) to be a very promising new type of chemical sensor.4 Lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (LnMOFs) are particularly suitable for such an application due to their unique optical properties such as large Stokes shift value, high color purity, and relatively long luminescence lifetimes,5 which originate from f–f transitions via an “antenna effect”.6 Indeed, we and others have realized a few microporous luminescent LnMOFs for their sensing of small molecules such as acetone and nitrobenzene7 though we have not been able to target those unique LnMOFs for the sensing of aniline and its derivatives.8 Since it is known that some compounds containing aldehyde or ketone chromophores can detect amines and amino acids,9 we have aimed to incorporate ketone moieties into the corresponding organic linkers and thus develop their LnMOFs for their potential applications on the sensing of aniline and its derivatives. Herein, we report a multifunctional lanthanide metal–organic framework Tb–BCB (1, H3BCB = 4,4′,4′′-benzenetricarbonyltribenzoic acid, Scheme 1 and Fig. S1, ESI†) for the sensing of aniline.‡

1 was synthesized by the solvothermal reaction of [Tb(NO3)3] (H2O)6 and H3BCB9 in mixed solvents of dimethylformamide (DMF), ethanol, and water at 80 °C for 2 days. It was formulated as [Tb(BCB)(DMF)]·(DMF)1.5(H2O)2 by elemental microanalysis and single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies, and the phase purity of the bulk material was independently confirmed using powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) (Fig. S2 and S3a, b, ESI†).

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in an orthorhombic space group Fdd2.§ The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically independent Tb3+ ions, two completely deprotonated BCB3− anions, two terminally coordinated DMF ligands, three free DMF molecules and two free water molecules (Fig. S4, ESI†). The Tb1 center is coordinated by eight oxygen atoms: six from six different BCB3− ligands, and two from the terminally coordinated DMF molecules. Meanwhile, the Tb2 site is surrounded by eight oxygen atoms from six different BCB3− ligands. The local coordination geometries of the Tb1 and Tb2 ions are trigonal dodecahedron and square antiprismatic, respectively. The Tb–O bond lengths in 1 are in the range of 2.251(12) to 2.646(10) Å, a range that is comparable with that observed in other terbium(III)-containing compounds.10 The BCB3− ligand acts as a μ6-bridge, in which two carboxylate groups adopt a μ2-η1:η1-bridging mode, while the other adopts a μ2-η2:η1-bridging mode (Fig. S5, ESI†). Adjacent Tb atoms are bridged by BCB organic linkers to form a 3D rod-packing structure. The structure of 1 has some similar structural features to MOF-7610b and Yb-BPT.7a It adopts a 3-fold helical rod SBU with 8-coordinated lanthanide atoms and there exist two types of one-dimensional trapezoid channels along the c axis (Fig. 1 and Fig. S6, ESI,† channel A size of about 13.7 × 6.2 Å and channel B size of about 4.2 × 6.2 Å). The moderate pore sizes indicate that such 1D channels can be accessible to a variety of small molecules, while the ketone groups on the 1D channel surfaces could be potentially useful for the selective binding of guest molecules. PLATON analysis reveals that the 3D porous structure contains moderate voids of 3951.4 Å3, which represents 25.5% of the volume of the unit cell when the disordered solvent molecules are removed.11


Fig. 1 X-ray single crystal structure of 1 indicating: (a) 1D helical rod [Tb(CO2)3]n as the infinite SBU and (b) 1D channel along the c axis (Tb, yellow polyhedra; C, grey; O, red).

TG analysis of 1 indicates that all the free and terminal solvent molecules can be removed around 250 °C and the framework is stable up to 500 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere. The as-synthesized 1 was washed with fresh DMF and exchanged with methanol several times before it was thermally activated at 200 °C under high vacuum for 24 h to get the activated sample 1a. The FTIR spectra (Fig. S7, ESI†) indicate that 1a has much weaker vibration at 1666 cm−1 than the as-synthesized 1 (this IR vibration is attributed to the ketone groups of the organic linker and DMF molecules).7e,9 The TG curve shows that 1a contains negligible amount of solvent molecules. Although we were not able to get the element analysis data for the freshly activated 1a, the data of the water re-adsorbed 1a confirm that the DMF molecules were removed. The powder XRD pattern of the guest free phase 1a is almost identical to that of as-synthesized 1, indicating that the framework is robust (Fig. S3c, ESI†).

The robustness and the permanent microporous feature of the activated 1a were further established by N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K, which displays typical type-I sorption behavior (Fig. 2a). 1a has the BET surface area and pore volume of 716 m2 g−1 and 0.40 cm3 g−1, respectively. Hydrogen sorption isotherm shows that 1a can take up 1.1 wt% hydrogen at 77 K and 1 atm (Fig. S8, ESI†). The hydrogen adsorption data follow the Langmuir–Freundlium equation, from which we predicted that the maximum saturated hydrogen storage capacity at 77 K and high pressure can reach up to 2.1 wt%. We further examined its potential application on the gas and vapor selective separation. As shown in Fig. 2b, 1a takes up differential amounts of C2H2 (60.5 cm3 g−1), CO2 (32.1 cm3 g−1) and CH4 (8.8 cm3 g−1) at 296 K and 1 atm, highlighting its potential application for the separation of C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4. The adsorption enthalpies of 1a to C2H2, CO2 and CH4 gases at low coverage are 25.8, 21.2 and 19.6 kJ mol−1, respectively, as calculated based on the virial method,12 a well-established and reliable methodology from fits of their adsorption isotherms at 296 and 273 K (Fig. S9, ESI†). To predict C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 binary mixture selectivity, an ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) calculations,13 coupled with a dual-site Langmuir–Freundlich simulation, was employed on the basis of single-component isotherms (see details in the ESI,† Fig. S19 and S20). The separation selectivity for C2H2/CH4 (C2H2/CH4 = 50:50) and CO2/CH4 (CO2/CH4 = 50:50) mixtures is 19.7 and 4.1, respectively, at 273 K. 1a also preferably adsorbs CH3OH over C2H5OH (Fig. S10, ESI†) and 1-C3H7OH over 2-C3H7OH (Fig. S11, ESI†).


Fig. 2 (a) Nitrogen sorption isotherm at 77 K; (b) gas sorption isotherms of 1a for C2H2, CO2 and CH4 at 296 K.

The excitation and photoluminescence (PL) spectra of activated 1a are shown in the Fig. S12 (ESI†). The excitation spectrum of 1a, monitored under the characteristic emission (545 nm) of the Tb3+ ion, exhibits a broad band with a maximum at 382 nm and a shoulder peak around 300 nm. The emission spectrum of 1a, excited at 382 nm, indicates that the characteristic emission from BCB ligands around 440 nm almost disappears, and there exists energy transfer from BCB ligands to Tb3+ ions which “magnifies” the luminescence of the f–f transitions. 1a exhibits the well-resolved peaks of Tb3+ centered at 491, 545, 586, and 621 nm, respectively, which result from the deactivation of the 5D4 excited state to the corresponding 7FJ ground state of the Tb3+ cation (J = 6, 5, 4, 3). The most intense emission corresponds to the hypersensitive transition of 5D4 → 7F5 (545 nm). Inspired by the studies of Zimmerman and Glass on making use of the ketone moieties for the detection of amines, we explored the potential of 1a for the sensing of aniline. As shown in Fig. 3, the addition of a small amount of different guest molecules to methanol solution of 1a led to the completely different degrees of luminescence quenching effects in the order of aniline > phenol > nitrobenzene > benzaldehyde. In fact, a tiny amount of aniline could even turn off the luminescence.


Fig. 3 The PL spectra of 1a introduced into various guest molecules when excited at 304 nm.

In order to understand the sensing mechanism, the luminescence lifetimes of 1a with the addition of aniline or nitrobenzene were measured (Fig. S14, ESI†). It shows that the addition of aniline or nitrobenzene does not change the luminescence lifetimes, suggesting that there are no direct interactions between analytes and Tb3+, so emission intensity changes upon the addition of these solvent molecules are attributed to the interactions between the analytes and the ligands BCB.14 Based on the fact from IR data that no CN bonds were formed between the aniline and the BCB linker, while two easily oxidized analytes aniline and phenol have the most significant quenching effects, it is reasonable to speculate that the electron transfer between the analytes and BCB chromophores plays the most important role for the quenching effect. The stronger hydrogen bonding interactions between aniline/phenol molecules and CO groups within BCB might further enhance the electron transfer.15,16 The different behaviors of 1a from MOF-76a for their sensing of small molecules are apparently attributed to their different organic linkers: the much more highly conjugated BCB in 1a compared with the simple benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylate (BTC) in MOF-76a generates a very efficient electron transfer of 1a. It should be noted that after sensing, both MOFs still keep their crystalline forms (Fig. S3 and S18, ESI†).

In conclusion, we have successfully designed a robust microporous luminescent Tb-MOF (1a) for the highly selective sensing of small molecules. Our work has demonstrated that tuning organic linkers and thus forming new luminescent LnMOFs might generate new LnMOF sensing materials for the sensing of a variety of substrates. The highly selective and sensitive sensing of 1a for the aniline might lead to its applications in biological and environmental systems.
