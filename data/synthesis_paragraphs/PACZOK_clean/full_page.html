Abstract

A new porous 3D vanadophosphate, [NH3(CH2)6NH3][P2V5O17]·3.83H2O (1) is synthesized by using NH2(CH2)6NH2 as template, and characterized by single crystal structural analysis, X‐ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy, TG analysis, and powder XRD. Single crystal analysis shows that compound 1 crystallizes in cubic shape, space group Imm with cell dimensions: a = b = c = 26.5068(8) Å, V = 18624.0(10) Å3, Z = 24. Structural refinement indicates that the inorganic framework of 1 is constructed from nanosized P–O–V wheels.

Introduction

New open‐frameworks porous materials constructed by either pure tetrahedra or mixed polyhedra are currently receiving considerable attention because of their wide‐spread application in adsorption, catalysis, and radioactive waste remediation 1. Vanadium exhibits not only three kinds of oxidation states (III, IV, and V), but also four types of coordination modes (tetrahedral, square pyramidal, trigonal bipyramidal, and octahedral). The flexibility of polyhedral structure for vanadium allows the formation of various 3D vanadate open‐frameworks with large channels. Unfortunately, although lots of vanadophosphates (or phosphites) [2–19] were prepared, the reported 3D organic‐amine templated vanadophosphates are very limited [20–27]. Recently, one of important advances in the phosphate chemistry is to explore the 2D and 3D solid materials with nanosized Mo–O–P wheels [28, 29].

One of the synthesis strategies in the preparation of porous materials is to employ a specific organic amine as template. According to the host‐guest theory, an inorganic framework with large pores may be synthesized by large templates. Herein by using 1,6‐hexanediamine as template, a novel 3D porous organic templated vanadophosphate [NH3(CH2)6NH3][P2V5O17]·3.83H2O (1) with extra‐large 16‐membered ring channels was successfully prepared. Particular interestingly, the inorganic framework of 1 is constructed from nanosized P–O–V wheels.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. The elemental analysis for the C, H, and N analysis were performed with a Perkin‐Elemer 2400 elemental analyzer. IR spectra were recorded with a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses were carried out in nitrogen atmosphere with a Diamond thermogravimetric analyzer from 50 to 700 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1. Powder XRD diffractograms were obtained with a Bruker D8X diffractometer equipped with monochromated Cu‐Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation at room temperature.

Synthesis and Characterization of Compound 1

A mixture of V2O5 (0.3025 g), Zr(NO3)4·5H2O (0.7156 g), 1,6‐hexanediamine (0.7746 g), and H2O (9 mL) in molar ratio 1:1:4:100 was stirred for 2 h briefly in air, then was neutralized to pH = 4 with 50 % H3PO4. This mixture was then transferred to a 20 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and heated to 170 °C for 6 days. After cooling to room temperature, dark octahedral crystals were isolated mechanically, washed with water, and dried at ambient temperature (0.16 g, yield 31 % based on vanadium). In addition, without the addition of Zr(NO3)4 compound 1 can be obtained as powder. The elemental analysis: found C 9.40 %, H 3.40 %, N 3.69 % (calcd. C 9.28 %, H 3.31 %, N 3.61 % ).

Determination of Crystal Structure of 1

Single crystals of 1 were mounted onto a thin glass fiber by epoxy glue in air for data collection, and the diffraction data were collected with a Bruker Apex 2 CCD with Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K using ω‐2θ scan method. An empirical absorption correction was applied. All the non‐hydrogen atoms (except partial occupied organic amine and water molecules) were refined anisotropically, whereas the hydrogen atoms of the organic part of 1 were not located because of disordered carbon and nitrogen atoms. Also, some C–C and C–N distances were restrained during the refinement. All calculations were performed using the SHELX97 program package. Further details of the X‐ray structural analysis for 1 are given in Table 1 and the selected bond lengths and angles for 1 are listed in Table 2.

Results and Discussion

Powder XRD Pattern of 1

The experimental and simulated XRD patterns of 1 are shown in Figure 1. The experimental peak positions are in agreement with simulated XRD patterns, which indicate the phase purity of 1. The difference in reflection intensity between experimental and simulated XRD patterns is probably due to the preferred orientation effect of the powder sample during collection of the experimental XRD data.

Structure Description

Single crystal structural analysis indicates that the inorganic framework of compound 1 consists of novel nanosized P–O–Vwheels (structural building unit). As shown in Figure 2, each nanosized P–O–V wheel is composed of 28 VO5 square pyramids and 16 PO4 tetrahedra. All vanadium atoms are coordinated by five oxygen atoms including one terminal oxygen atom and four bridging oxygen atoms in square pyramidal model with V–O bond lengths of 1.588–1.959 Å. Five VO5 square pyramids are connected to each other by sharingμ3‐oxygen atoms to make a [V5O21] cluster, which was already reported for the vanadophosphate Cs3[V5O9(PO4)] 20. Due to the higher symmetry (mm), only three independent crystallographic vanadium atoms are observed. Four PO4 tetrahedra are attached to the [V5O21] cluster by sharing μ2‐oxygen atoms to generate [V5P4O29] as a secondary building unit. Eight VO5 pyramids link four [V5P4O29] clusters to form an interesting nanosized P–O–V wheel with eight‐membered ring windows. The outer diameter of the building unit nanowheel of 1 is 22.267(11) Å, whereas the largest ring for nanowheel is a24‐membered ring (Figure 3) constructed by 8 PO4 tetrahedra and 16 VO5 pyramids, whereas a 32‐membered ring was found in Cs3[V5O9(PO4)] 20. Each V–O–P nanowheel connects four adjacent wheels by sharing the eight‐membered ring windows to make a building block including five wheels, as shown in Figure 4. The adjacent building blocks are further connected each other to form 3D open‐framework of 1 with extra‐large 16‐membered ring channels, as shown in Figure 5.

Although two kinds of channels in 1 have the different size (8 and 16‐membered ring), they can be orderly arranged in closed packing. Each eight‐membered ring channel is surrounded by four 16‐membered ring channels, these are alternately surrounded by four eight‐membered ring and four16‐membered ring channels. The approximate pore size (except terminal oxygen atoms) for the 16‐membered ring is 13.445(7) Å. Because the extra‐large channels are occupied by organic amine cations and water molecules, the density of 1 is very low (1.66 g·cm–3), 9.02 polyhedra per 1000 Å3, which is smaller than those reported values, 12.1 for ND‐1 (24‐membered ring) [30], 11.1 for FDU‐4 (24‐membered ring) [31], 11.6 for ZnHPO‐CJ1 (24‐membered ring) [32], and 11.9 for NTHU‐1(24‐membered ring) 33.

The assignment of the oxidation state of vanadium atoms is based on the bond valence sum calculations, which gives the average value 4.35 for oxidation state of vanadium (expected value for 1 is 4.4), and the calculation result is consistent with the formula of compound 1. The EPR spectrum of 1 indicates that vanadium atoms exists in oxidation state IV (g = 1.97), as shown in Figure S1.

In compound 1, 1,6‐hexanediamine has a very weak templating effect. The P–O–V inorganic framework adopts the high symmetry (Oh) and the 1,6‐hexanediamine molecules conform to the symmetry of P–O–V inorganic framework by adjusting their orientations and disorder. There are 3.83 guest water molecules per [P2V5O17] cluster. These water molecules are involved in strong hydrogen bonding with the oxygen atoms of inorganic framework of 1. The shortest distance between water and an oxygen atom of the inorganic framework is 2.85(2) Å.

IR Spectrum of 1

The IR spectrum of 1 shows that the band around 3520 cm–1 can be attributed to the presence of water. The typical sharp signals for 1,6‐hexanediamine are in the region 1350–1610 cm–1. The bands at 1103, 1004, 850 and 623 cm–1 are ascribed to ν(P–O), ν(V=O), and ν(O–V–O), respectively.

TG Curve of 1

The thermal analysis of 1, which is depicted in Figure 6, shows that a weight loss of 10.1 % in the range of 50–270 °C corresponds to the removal of guest water (the calculated value is 8.9 % ), whereas the weight loss of 18.6 % in the range of 271–700 °C can be attributed to the loss of the 1,6‐hexanediamine and water (the calculated value is 17.3 % ).

Conclusions

A novel 3D vanadophosphate [NH3(CH2)6NH3][P2V5O17]·3.83H2O (1) with an extra‐large 16‐membered ring was synthesized by using a larger organic amine (1,6‐hexanediamine) as template. Structural analysis indicated that the inorganic framework of 1 is constructed by P–O–V nanowheels. The formation of 1 demonstrates that large organic amines could be used as templates in the synthesis of porous vanadophosphates with extra‐large channels. Syntheses of new porous materials with large channels by careful selection of templates and an exploration of their properties are currently under way.
