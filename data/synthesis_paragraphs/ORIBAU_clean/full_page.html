Abstract

Two microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) based on zinc clusters, that is, [Zn3(BDC)2(HBDC)2]·2DMA·4CH3OH·2H2O (1; H2BDC = 1,4‐dicarboxybenzene, DMA = N,N‐dimethylacetamide) and [(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn7(µ4‐O)2(NDC)6]·DMF·H2O (2; H2NDC = 1,4‐naphthalenedicarboxylic acid, DMF = N,N‐dimethylformamide), were synthesized by a solvothermal method. Both compounds possess three‐dimensional porous skeletons with square channels and exhibit interesting blue fluorescence. Compound 1 consists of trinuclear ZnII building units bridged by both BDC2– and HBDC– ligands, which results in the formation of a 2,8‐connnected topological net (hex). Compound 2 features another 2,8‐connected topological net (bcu) that is constructed by eight‐connected Zn7(µ4‐O)2 clusters and two‐connected NDC2– ligands. The solid‐state and solvent‐dependent fluorescence of the two MOFs revealed that 1 and 2 showed fluorescence enhancement effects in the presence of acetone and chloroform, respectively, which thereby demonstrates their potential as fluorescent sensors for selective sensing towards acetone and chloroform.

Abstract

Two fluorescent metal–organic frameworks constructed with d10‐configured metal clusters (i.e., Zn3 and Zn7O2) and organic carboxylic acid ligands (i.e., 1,4‐dicarboxybenzene and 1,4‐naphthalenedicarboxylic acid) are synthesized. They show interesting fluorescence enhancement effects towards acetone and chloroform, respectively.

Introduction

In recent years, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with intrinsic luminescence have received increasing attention as chemical sensors for the sensitive and selective detection of pollutants (e.g., organic solvents,1 poisonous gases,2 heavy metal ions,3 etc.4). The unique sensing ability of fluorescent MOFs as chemosensors is attributed to their intriguing structural topologies in combination with several promising advantages including fast response, high sensitivity, easy operation, and low cost. Accordingly, a variety of fluorescent MOFs have been reported that exhibit promising sensing ability towards ketones,5 explosives,6 and metal ions7 through fluorescence quenching or an enhancement effect. For example, Li and co‐workers reported two luminescent MOFs with fluorescence enhancement properties towards ketones, and they were therefore used indirectly for the sensitive detection of plastic explosives such as RDX (1,3,5‐trinitroperhydro‐1,3,5‐triazine).8 For most fluorescent MOFs as chemical sensors, their fluorescence sensing ability mainly relies on interactions between the MOF backbone and the guest molecules.9 In general, the sensing mechanism involves a four‐step process: (1) the guest molecules are first adsorbed onto the exterior surface of the materials; (2) the guest molecules diffuse into the framework's channels; (3) the guest molecules are adsorbed onto the interior surface; (4) excited‐state charge or energy transfer occurs between the guest molecules and MOFs, which thereby results in fluorescence quenching or enhancement. It is known that MOFs built from d10‐configured metal ions and organic carboxylic ligands always have interesting fluorescent properties, and they can therefore be used as potential sensors for sensing organic molecules. Against this background, more and more fluorescent MOFs with unique sensing abilities have been synthesized and applied in this field over the past several years.10

On the other hand, it is well known that the selective sensing abilities of MOFs are closely related to their structure, including topology, pore size, apparent surface area, and structural stability. It has been demonstrated that interactions between the MOF backbone and the guest molecules more readily take place in micropore environments.11 In this context, a variety of microporous MOFs (MMOFs) with functional luminescence have been synthesized and used for the selective detection of organic small molecules.12 Very recently, Yang and co‐works reported a 3D MMOF material for the naked‐eye detection of Cu2+ ions.13 In general, two strategies can be used to construct MMOFs: (1) large organic carboxylic ligands are widely employed to construct the intersected frameworks; (2) simple organic carboxylic ligands (e.g., oxalate, 1,4‐dicarboxybenzene, and 1,4‐naphthalenedicarboxylic acid) are suitable for the preparation of novel MMOFs.14 However, examples for which highly microporous voids are combined with significant thermal stability and unique fluorescence features on the basis of host–guest chemistry in MMOFs are rare.

In our continuing efforts to develop novel MMOFs and to explore their potential applications, two fluorescent MOFs constructed from zinc clusters and organic carboxylic acids were synthesized by the solvothermal method. Interestingly, the two MOFs, that is, [Zn3(BDC)2(HBDC)2]·DMA·CH3OH·H2O (1; H2BDC = 1,4‐dicarboxybenzene, DMA = N,N‐dimethylacetamide) and [(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn7(µ4‐O)2(NDC)6]·DMF·H2O (2: H2NDC = 1,4‐naphthalenedicarboxylic acid, DMF = N,N‐dimethylformamide), exhibited strong blue fluorescence upon excitation with ultraviolet light, and their fluorescent properties were extensively studied in both the solid state and under solvent‐dependent conditions at room temperature. More importantly, 1 and 2 showed significant fluorescence enhancement effects in the presence of acetone and chloroform, respectively, which demonstrated their potential as chemical sensors for the selective detection of acetone and chloroform.

Results and Discussion

Structure Description

Colorless crystals of 1 were readily obtained by the solvothermal reaction of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O, 9‐anthracencarboxylic acid (9‐AC), and H2BDC in MeOH/DMA. The phase purity of 1 was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses, in which the experimental pattern was almost consistent with the simulated one on the basis of single‐crystal data, as shown in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). The difference in the reflection intensities between the simulated and experimental patterns is due to variations in crystal orientation for the powder samples.

The single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction pattern shows that 1 is crystalline in the orthorhombic space group Pccn (no. 56). There are one‐and‐a‐half unique Zn2+ ions, one HBDC– ligand, and one BDC2– ligand in the asymmetric unit (Figure S5). Each Zn atom is six coordinate in a distorted octahedral geometry by oxygen atoms. The Zn–O distances range from 1.951(3) to 2.231(3) Å. As shown in Figure 1 (b), compound 1 has a 3D framework structure based on a trinuclear Zn3(COO)6(COOH)2 secondary building unit (Figure 1, a) with 2D intersected square channels. Viewed along the [010] and [001] directions, 1 exhibits two kinds of rectangular channels with windows sizes of 9.9 × 10.9 and 9.9 × 18.9 Å2, as shown in Figure 1 (b, c). The guest solvent molecules were highly disordered in the channels and could not be modeled properly, so the diffused electron densities resulting from them were removed by the SQUEEZE routine in the PLATON program, and the total accessible volume of 1 after removal of the guest solvent molecules was about 56.4 % by using the PLATON program. The guest solvent molecules were confirmed by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and CHN analysis. The 3D framework of 1 can be viewed as a layer‐pillared structure. The layer structure is constructed by trimer Zn3 cluster units and HBDC– anion ligands, as shown in Figure 1 (a, c). The layer structures are pillared by BDC2– ligands to build the 3D structure of 1 (Figure 1, b). The 3D structure can also be looked at as being constructed by Zn3(COO)6(COOH)2 clusters and BDC2–/HBDC– ligands (Figure 1, a), in which each cluster is bridged by six surrounding BDC2– ligands and two HBDC– ligands, and then bridge to eight different neighboring Zn3(COO)6(COOH)2 clusters (Figure S6). Several types of zinc clusters such as Zn4O, Zn3, and Zn2 have been reported in some MOFs based on the BDC2– ligand.15 For example, MOF‐5, a well‐known porous MOF, is constructed from a Zn4O cluster and a BDC2– ligand. The trimer Zn3 cluster contained in 1 can be found in several reported compounds such as Zn3(BDC)3(DEF)2 (H2BDC = 1,4‐dicarboxybenzene, DEF = N,N‐diethyformamide), Zn3(BDC)3(H2O)2·4DMF, Zn3(p‐BDC)4·2HPIP (HPIP = partly protonated piperazine), and Zn3(p‐BDC)4(H2BDC)·(C6H15NO)·H2O·3DMF.[15], 16 From a topological point of view, if the BDC2–/HBDC– ligands are considered as two‐connected linkers and the Zn3(COO)6(COOH)2 clusters are looked as eight‐connected nodes, 1 holds a hex topology (Figure 1, d). The structure data of 1 including bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S2.

Moreover, if the H2NDC ligand was used to replace H2BDC in the synthesis of 1, compound 2 was obtained as yellow crystals. The phase purity of 2 was confirmed by PXRD analyses, in which the experimental pattern was almost consistent with the simulated one (Figure S3). CHN elemental analysis confirmed the stoichiometry (calcd. C 48.47, H 3.07, N 2.15; found C 48.33, H 2.95, N 2.01). Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction revealed that compound 2 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Cmca (no. 64). There are one, one‐half, and one‐quarter unique Zn2+ ions in the asymmetric unit (Figure S8). Both Zn1 and Zn2 are tetrahedrally coordinated by four oxygen atoms with Zn–O bond lengths in the 1.970(6)–2.000(6) Å range. Zn3 is octahedrally coordinated by six oxygen atoms with Zn–O bond lengths in the 2.091(8)–2.287(14) Å range.

Compound 2 exhibits a 3D anionic framework with 1D quadrate channels along the [010] direction that are occupied by protonated [(CH3)2NH2]+ counterions. The [(CH3)2NH2]+ ions may be generated from decomposition of DMF during the solvothermal reaction. The 3D anionic framework of 2 features a heptanuclear [Zn7(µ4‐O)2(COO)12] secondary building unit that is constructed from two equivalent oxygen‐centered [Zn4(µ4‐O)] tetrahedra sharing one zinc corner (Figure 2, a). The [Zn7(µ4‐O)2] cluster has been rarely reported, and it can be found only in several compounds, for example, {(EMIM)2[Zn7(µ4‐O)2(1,4‐ndc)6]}n (EMIM = 1‐ethyl‐3‐methylimidazolium, 1,4‐ndc = 1,4‐naphthalenedicarboxylate) and [Zn7(µ4‐O)2(BDC‐NO2)5(DMF)]·H2O (BDC‐NO2 = 2‐nitro‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate).17 The quadrate channels in the framework are composed of [Zn7(µ4‐O)2(COO)12] units and NDC2– ligands with a pore size of 9.7 × 9.7 Å2 (Figure 2, b). The total accessible volume of 2 after removal of the countercations and guest solvent molecules is about 25.0 % by using the PLATON program. The structure can also be looked as a layer‐pillared structure. The layer structures constructed by [Zn7(µ4‐O)2] clusters and NDC2– ligands (Figure 2, c) are further pillared by NDC2– ligands to build the 3D anionic framework of 2.

In the anionic framework, each [Zn7(µ4‐O)2(COO)12] unit is bridged by 12 surrounding NDC2– ligands and is connected to 8 different neighboring units (Figure S9). From a topological point of view, if the NDC2– ligands are looked at as two‐connected linkers and the [Zn7(µ4‐O)2(COO)12] units are considered as eight‐connected nodes, the 3D anionic framework can be simplified as a bcu topology (Figure 2, d). The detailed structural data of 2 are listed in Table S3. Notably, the counterions and guest solvent molecules in the channels of 2 were highly disordered and could not be modeled properly, so the diffused electron densities resulting from them were removed by the SQUEEZE routine in PLATON.

Thermal Stability

The TGA curves of compounds 1 and 2 are shown in Figure 3. The curves of the two compounds are quite different, because the larger accessible volume of framework 1 relative to that of 2 allows almost complete occupation by guest molecules. TGA analysis of 1 demonstrated that there are five main weight‐loss steps in the heating process ranging from 30 to 800 °C. The first weight‐loss step (30–90 °C) may correspond to the release of free guest molecules contained in the channels. The observed weight loss was 13.82 wt.‐%. The weight‐loss step from 140 to 380 °C may be attributed to the loss of DMA molecules (exp. 8.47 wt.‐%; calcd. 8.68 wt.‐%). Upon further heating, the frameworks may start to decompose, and this would result in the production of powder ZnO and tiny unknown powders up to 800 °C; the calculated weight based on ZnO is 24.5 wt.‐% and the observed value was 26.4 wt.‐%. The TGA diagram of 2 reveals that there are three weight‐loss steps in the heating process. The first weight‐loss step (30–80 °C) may be due to the release of guest water molecules. The second weight loss (140–300 °C) corresponds to the loss of one DMF molecule (exp. 3.52 wt.‐%; calcd. 3.73 wt.‐%). The third weight‐loss step (370–500 °C) could be caused by collapse of the framework. Decomposition may result in the production of powder ZnO and tiny unknown powders; the calculated weight based on ZnO is 70.86 wt.‐%, and the total observed weight loss was 63.84 wt.‐% up to 800 °C.

After TGA analysis, the N2 adsorption properties of 1 were investigated with the aim of further confirming the robustness and permanent microporous nature, which was indicated by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction. Sample 1 was activated at 90 °C under high vacuum for 12 h prior to N2 sorption isotherm, and the test was conducted at 77 K. As shown in Figure 4, the isotherm curves of 1 display typical type I sorption behavior. The uptake of N2 at 77 K and atmospheric pressure was 70 cm3 g–1. The calculated Langmuir and BET surface areas of 1 were 263 and 196 m2 g–1, respectively. An average pore size of 2.24 nm of 1 was calculated. Different from 1, activated sample 2 (activated at 90 °C under high vacuum for 12 h) took up negligible amounts of nitrogen, which indicated the channels in its framework were too small (filled by counterions) to allow the incorporation of N2.

Fluorescence Measurement

The solid‐state fluorescence properties of H2BDC and 1 were studied. As shown in Figure S10, the H2BDC ligand exhibited emission peaks around λem = 431 nm upon excitation at λex = 365 nm, which should correspond to π*→n transitions. After formation of the complex between H2BDC and the zinc ions, resulting 1 revealed one strong emission band at λmax = 445 nm and a shoulder emission band at around λem = 535 nm upon excitation at λex = 325 nm (Figure 4, a), for which the former was ascribed to emission of the organic ligand and the latter was attributed to ligand‐to‐metal charge transfer (LMCT) from the OH₂BDC–Zn bond.18 Apart from the solid‐state fluorescence of 1, solvent‐dependent fluorescence emissions were also studied by dispersing activated samples of 1 in different organic solvents. It is interesting that the fluorescent emission spectra of 1 dispersed in different solvents are much different, as shown in Figure 5 (b). If dispersed in DMA, acetone, chloroform, and DMF, the LMCT emission intensity of 1 was clearly enhanced; this emission was aggregation quenched in the solid state.19 A significant redshift in the emission bands was observed upon dispersing 1 in CH2Cl2 and dioxane. PXRD analysis confirmed that framework 1 was unchanged after dispersing in the solvents (Figure S15). Therefore, it is speculated that this interesting chromaticity shift may be caused by interactions between the solvent molecules and 1.

To gain deeper insight into the fluorescence enhancement effects of compound 1 in the presence of different solvent molecules, the fluorescent emissions of 1 with saturation absorptions of different solvent molecules were measured. As shown in Figure 5 (c), the emission intensity of 1 was variously enhanced before and after saturated absorption of organic solvent molecules. About 250 % EE [enhancing efficiency = (F/F0 – 1) × 100 %; in which F0 and F are the fluorescence intensities before and after saturated absorption of the organic solvent molecules, respectively] could be obtained after saturated absorption of acetone. In contrast, for other solvents such as DMA, DMSO, and dioxane, tiny fluorescence enhancement effects on activated 1 were also found. These results showed that 1 had a more sensitive and selective fluorescence response to acetone than the other solvents tested in this work. To gain deeper insight into the sensitivity limit of 1 towards acetone, several activated samples of 1 were used to monitor the emission changes upon gradually adding acetone into a DMF solution. A gradual enhancement in the fluorescence intensity was observed upon adding acetone into the DMF solution, as shown in Figure 4 (d). The enhancing trend of the fluorescence intensity at λem = 445 nm for 1 versus the volume ratio of acetone in DMF could be diffusion controlled.20 The fluorescence intensity was further enhanced by 37 % at 0.5 vol.‐% acetone in DMF, and it was almost doubled in the case of 3.0 vol.‐% acetone in DMF. That is, compound 1 is a promising fluorescent sensor for the selective sensing of acetone.

Similarly, the solid‐state fluorescent emissions of free H2NDC and compound 2 were also tested at room temperature. As shown in Figure S11, free H2NDC displayed strong emission at λem = 484 nm upon excitation at λex = 389 nm, whereas 2 showed a blueshift in the emission and gave an intensive emission band at around λem = 432 nm upon excitation at λex = 365 nm (Figure 5, a). This emission band at 432 nm can be ascribed to the emission of the NDC2– ligand. For further evidence, the solid salt Na2(NDC) was also synthesized, and it gave a blue emission band at λem = 435 nm upon excitation at λex = 363 nm (Figure S12), which supported the assignment of the emission band of the NDC2– ligand. In the excitation spectra of 2, there is a platform region ranging from 350 to 375 nm (Figure S13); it may result from several factors, including the local coordination environment of the ligands and the interaction of the ligands with each other and with the guest molecules trapped in the channels.21 The fluorescent emissions of 2 dispersed in different solvents were also studied extensively, and the results are shown in Figure 6 (b). For compound 2, there are two cases for its fluorescence after saturation absorption of different solvent molecules. For example, 2 reveals an emission platform (formed by overlapping emissions bands) in the region from λem = 425 to 465 nm in chloroform, DMA, and DMSO, whereas in CH2Cl2, DMF, and acetonitrile it has only one emission band at around λem = 430 nm. This phenomenon may be caused by interactions between the guest molecules and the backbone of 2. Interestingly, the emission intensity of 2 was significantly enhanced after saturated absorption of chloroform in the solid state, and about 110 % EE was achieved (Figure 6, c, d). Further studies showed that the fluorescence intensity was enhanced by 15 % at 0.5 vol.‐% chloroform in DMF and by about 30 % in the case of 3 vol.‐% chloroform in DMF (Figure 6, d), which indicated that compound 2 is also a promising probe for the selective sensing of chloroform.

Conclusions

In summary, two fluorescent MOFs constructed from d10‐configured metal clusters (i.e., Zn3 and Zn7O2) and organic carboxylic acid ligands (i.e., H2BDC and H2NDC) were synthesized by a solvothermal method. Both compounds revealed 3D open frameworks with square microporous channels. Compound 1 based on a Zn3 cluster is a 2,8‐connnected topological net (hex). Compound 2 based on a Zn7O2 cluster features another 2,8‐connected topological net (bcu). Interestingly, 1 showed more selective fluorescence enhancements towards acetone than other solvent molecules, whereas 2 displayed a more selective emission response to chloroform. Therefore, both microporous MOFs with functional luminescence are potential chemosensors for sensing acetone and chloroform. It is expected that more MOFs as novel sensing materials will be exploited for the sensing of small molecules in the near future.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods: All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and were used as received without further purification. Powder X‐ray diffraction data were collected by using a Rigaku D/MAX‐rA diffractometer with Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). CHN analyses were performed by using a Euro EA3000 analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses with heating from room temperature to 800 °C were performed by using a Netzsch STA 449c analyzer in a flow of N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min–1. IR spectra (KBr pellets) were acquired with a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer in the range of 400 to 4000 cm–1. Fluorescent spectra were obtained with a PTI QM‐40 luminescence spectrometer equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp. N2 gas adsorption measurements performed with an ASAP 2020 apparatus. Prior to measurements, the sample was degassed for 12 h at 90 °C.

Zn3(BDC)2(HBDC)2·2DMA (1): A mixture of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (82 mg), 9‐AC (55 mg), H2BDC (42 mg), methanol (1 mL), and DMA (4 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL, Teflon®‐lined, stainless‐steel autoclave and heated at 150 °C for 4 d. The autoclave was subsequently cooled to room temperature. Colorless crystals were recovered by filtration, which were washed with DMA and ethanol and finally dried at ambient temperature (16.8 % yield based on H2BDC).

[(CH3)2NH2]2Zn7(µ4‐O)2(NDC)6·DMF·H2O (2): A mixture of Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (82 mg), 1‐naphthoic acid (43 mg), H2NDC (54 mg), methanol (3 mL), and DMF (4 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL, Teflon®‐lined, stainless‐steel autoclave and heated at 150 °C for 4 d. The autoclave was subsequently cooled to room temperature. Light yellow crystals were recovered by filtration, which were washed with DMF and ethanol and finally dried at ambient temperature (22.6 % yield based on H2NDC).

X‐ray Crystallographic Structure: A suitable single crystal of each MOF was carefully selected under an optical microscope and was glued to a glass fiber with epoxy resin. The single‐crystal data were collected with a Bruker SMART APEXLL diffractometer equipped with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using a ω scan at room temperature. The crystal structures of the two MOFs were resolved by direct methods and were refined by using full‐matrix least‐squares methods on F2 by using the SHELXTL program package.22 Anisotropic refinement was applied to non‐hydrogen atoms, except disordered guest molecules. PLATON/SQUEEZE program was used to obtain non‐solvent diffraction of as‐synthesized compounds owing to highly disordered guest molecules, which could not be modeled properly.23 The crystallographic data for 1 and 2 are summarized in Table S1.

CCDC 1443885 (for 1), and 1443884 (for 2) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre.

Fluorescence Measurements: The fluorescent properties of compounds 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state, in different solvent emulsions, and in the cases of absorbing a variety of organic solvents with different contents at room temperature. The solid samples included the as‐synthesized compounds, activated samples, and the sensing samples after different solvents were adsorbed. Activated samples were prepared by treating as‐synthesized compounds 1 and 2 under high vacuum at 120 °C for 4 h. The solvent emulsions were prepared by introducing powdered compound 1 or 2 (3 mg) into 20 mL of chloroform, acetone, DMF, DMA, CH2Cl2, DMSO, or acetonitrile. To obtain the fluorescent spectra, solvent emulsions of 1 and 2 were treated by ultrasonication for 30 min and were then aged for 3 days to form stable emulsions before the fluorescent study. The sensing samples were obtained by soaking activated 1 or 2 (20 mg) in different solvents or acetone/DMF solutions with different acetone contents for 2 h.
