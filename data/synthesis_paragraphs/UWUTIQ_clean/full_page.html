Abstract

A new approach has been realized to construct a three‐dimensional doubly interpenetrated cubic metal–organic framework Zn2(PBA)2(BDC)⋅(DMF)3(H2O)4 (UTSA‐36, HPBA=4‐(4‐pyridyl) benzoic acid, H2BDC=1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid) through the self‐assembly of the pyridylcarboxylate linker 4‐(4‐pyridyl) benzoate and bicarboxylate linker 1,4‐benzenedicarxylate with paddle‐wheel [Zn2(COO)4]. The activated UTSA‐36 a exhibits highly selective gas sorption of C2H6, C2H4 and C2H2 over CH4 with the Henry law’s selectivities of 11 to 25 in the temperature range of 273 to 296 K attributed to the unique 3D intersected pore structure of about 3.1 to 4.8 Å within the framework, indicating that UTSA‐36 a is a potentially very useful and promising microporous material for such industrially important separation of C2 hydrocarbons over methane.

Introduction

It has been very challenging to systematically and subtly tune the micropores within traditional porous materials. Since small gas separation and purification can be realized through the size‐exclusive effect in which smaller molecules can go through the microporous channels whereas larger substrates are blocked, the exact control of the micropores is of significant importance for their practical applications in gas separation and purification. Although extensive efforts have been pursued, very few traditional microporous materials have been targeted to exhibit tunable pore characteristics. The realization of Engelhard titanium silicate (ETS‐4), the so‐called molecular gate adsorbent the pores of which can be rationally tuned simply by different thermal activation, has led to breakthrough on the economical N2 and CO2 removal.1

Microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been rapidly emerging as very promising materials for gas separation.2–15 Such a promise is based on our capability to design and construct the reticular MOFs, the pore sizes of which can be varied by the systematical change of organic linkers, as exemplified in IRMOFs by Yaghi and to immobilize functional sites, such as open metal sites and Lewis basic ‐NH2 sites, to differentiate their different interactions with gas molecules.3–5 Furthermore, the flexible framework nature in some MOFs has allowed us to tune the micropores in another dimension because such pores are pressure/temperature dependent through the “kinetically controlled” pore contract/expansion mechanism, as demonstrated in a few examples, particularly by Kitagawa,6 Zhou,7 Chen,8 and us.15

Although the methodology to tune the pores within porous MOFs has been conceptually established, subtle control of pore sizes has been rarely fulfilled. This is mainly because there will typically be a big pore expansion when one shorter organic linker has been replaced by another longer organic linker in the non‐interpenetrated reticular MOFs. In this regard, framework interpenetration can not only enforce the framework and thus the pore structure, but can also deliberately control the pore size/curvature. Over the past several years, we have successfully made use of paddle‐wheel secondary building units (SBUs) [M2(COO)4] (M=Zn2+, Cu2+, Co2+, and Ni2+), bicarboxylate linker R(COO)2, and bidentate pillar linker L to assemble doubly and triply interpenetrated MOFs [M2(R(COO)2)2(L)⋅Gx] (Scheme 1a, G=guest molecules) of primitive cubic nets for their selective gas sorption.15 For example, a microporous MOF‐508 [Zn2(BDC)2(4,4′‐Bipy)⋅Gx] (BDC=1,4‐benzenedicarxylate; 4,4′‐Bipy=4,4′‐bipyridyl) of a pore size of about 4.0×4.0 Å has been successfully applied to the gas chromatographic separation of alkanes.15a Given the limited availability of the bidentate pillar linker L, we have been not able to target some microporous MOFs of specific pore size/curvature through this approach. Recently, we have been particularly interested in the microporous MOFs the pore sizes of which are comparable to small hydrocarbons and thus for their selective separation, thus we have been thinking of the alternative approach to construct interpenetrated microporous MOFs of primitive cubic nets whose pore sizes are about 3.5 to 4.5 Å by the slight modification and combination of two different types of organic linkers. With this in mind, we speculated that such primitive cubic nets can be also fulfilled by the incorporation of pyridylcarboxylate linker (K‐COO) and bicarboxylate linker R(COO)2 with paddle‐wheel [M2(COO)4] (M=Zn2+, Cu2+, Co2+, and Ni2+) into [M2(K(COO))2(R(COO)2)⋅Gx] (Scheme 1b, G=guest molecules). Although, this approach was used to construct cubic nets, the frameworks obtained were condensed because of a shorter pyridylcarboxylate linker.16 Herein we report the first such constructed microporous MOF [Zn2(PBA)2(BDC)⋅(DMF)3(H2O)4] (HPBA=4‐(4‐pyridyl) benzoic acid; H2BDC=1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid; for convenience, we term this MOF as UTSA‐36; UTSA stands for University of Texas at San Antonio) of 3D intersected pore structures from 3.1 to 4.8 Å for highly selective sorption of small hydrocarbons C2H2, C2H4, and C2H6 over CH4.

Results and Discussion

The solvothermal reaction of 4‐(4‐pyridyl) benzoic acid, 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid and [Zn(NO3)2⋅6H2O] in DMF at 90 °C for 24 h yielded colorless block‐shaped crystals of UTSA‐36. It was formulated as [Zn2(PBA)2(BDC)⋅(DMF)3(H2O)4] by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction and elemental microanalysis studies. The phase purity of the bulk sample was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA). The crystalline product once formed is insoluble in most of the common organic solvents.

A single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction study17 shows that UTSA‐36 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and the asymmetric unit consists of two ZnII ions, two PBA, one BDC and two types of lattice solvent molecules (three DMF and four water molecules). The framework of UTSA‐36 is composed of paddle‐wheel dinuclear {Zn2(COO)4} secondary building units (Figure 1a) that are bridged by the PBA ligands to form a distorted 2D square grid. These square grids are pillared by BDC moieties to form a 3D framework of an elongated primitive cubic (α‐Po) net, as speculated in Scheme 1b. Double framework interpenetration of UTSA‐36 has led to the small pores of about 3.4×3.8 Å along the a axis (Figure 1b), 2.0×3.8 Å along the rectangular square (Figure 1c), and of 3.1×4.4 Å along the c axis (Figure 1 d). The void space is composed of 49.4 % (2245 out of 4549 Å3) of the total crystal volume as estimated by PLATON.18

UTSA‐36 exhibits a dynamic/flexible framework nature involving framework transformation and/or deformation, which has been widely observed in such 3D interpenetrated frameworks of primitive cubic nets, such as [Zn(BDC)(4,4′‐Bipy)0.5]15a and [Zn(NDC)(4,4′‐Bipy)0.5].15b When the as‐synthesized UTSA‐36 is immersed in MeOH, it shows a slight change in d‐spacing and the disappearance of a few peaks in the PXRD pattern (Figure 2), which is a common phenomenon for the doubly interpenetrated MOFs as established by Kitagawa et al.2b The PXRD peaks of the activated UTSA‐36 a are a little bit broadened. Once the activated UTSA‐36 a is immersed back into DMF, the regenerated UTSA‐36 a is basically transformed back to the as‐synthesized UTSA‐36, as shown in their identical PXRDs, indicating that the framework transformation/deformation might be reversible.

TG analysis of UTSA‐36 indicates that all the solvent molecules can be removed at 200 °C and the framework is stable up to 480 °C (Figure 3). A different TGA behavior of the methanol‐exchanged UTSA‐36 might be due to the framework squeeze because the as‐synthesized UTSA‐36 liberates more guest molecules than its MeOH‐exchanged counterpart, which is also supported by the change in PXRD pattern as stated above. However, the TG curve for the activated sample UTSA‐36 a shows the presence of no solvent molecules within the framework.

To check the porosity and thus its potential for selective gas sorption applications, methanol‐exchanged UTSA‐36 was activated at 120 °C under high vacuum to obtain the activated sample UTSA‐36 a. The N2 sorption isotherm of UTSA‐36 a (Figure 4) at 77 K indicates a typical type I behavior with a Langmuir (BET) surface area of 806 m2g−1 (495 m2g−1) and a pore volume of 0.33 cm3g−1, which is slightly lower than the value of 946 m2g−1 for MOF‐508,15a but much higher than values of 303 and 484 m2g−1 for [Zn(NDC)(4,4′‐Bipy)0.5]15b and [Co(NDC)(4,4′‐Bipy)0.5], respectively.14l The adsorbed amount is 212 cm3 g−1 (standard temperature and pressure (STP)) at P/P0=1. The nitrogen desorption shows a hysteretic behavior because of the dynamic feature of the framework.2b, 15a,15b The hydrogen adsorption isotherm (Figure 4) indicates an uptake of 123 cm3g−1 (1.1 wt %) at 77 K and 1 atm.

The high thermal stability and permanent porosity of UTSA‐36 a encouraged us to examine its potential application for selective separation of small hydrocarbons. Figure 5 represents the different adsorption capacity of UTSA‐36 a for C2H6, C2H4, C2H2, and CH4 at two different temperatures (295 and 273 K). The most significant feature is that UTSA‐36 a systematically takes up much more C2 hydrocarbons (C2H2, C2H4, and C2H6) than C1 methane, indicating the promise of this new MOF for the highly selective separation of C2H2/CH4, C2H4/CH4, and C2H6/CH4.

To figure out why UTSA‐36 a exhibits such high selective separation for small hydrocarbons, we did the virial analyses studies on the temperature dependent sorption isotherms. Some virial parameters are summarized in Table 1. The isosteric enthalpies of adsorption (Qst,n=0) at zero surface coverage, calculated from the A0 virial parameters, are 36.1, 32.7, 29.0, and 24.4 kJ mol−1, respectively, for C2H6, C2H4, C2H2, and CH4. Because of the comparable pore sizes in UTSA‐36 a with these small hydrocarbons, the pore confinement within UTSA‐36 a enforces the systematically strong interactions between the host framework and gas hydrocarbons. In fact, the isosteric enthalpies of adsorption for CH4 and C2H2 in UTSA‐36 a are much higher than those found for MIL‐53 (with larger pores), which are less than 20 kJ mol−1.4a, 14h Accordingly, the Henry’s law selectivities for C2H6, C2H4, and C2H2 over CH4, calculated based on the equation Sij=KH(i)/KH(CH4), are 24.7, 14.7, and 16.1 at 273 K, and 16.6, 11.1, and 13.8 at 296 K, respectively, which are significantly high. Thus, the enhanced C2 hydrocarbon selectivities over methane are due to both the size‐exclusive sieving effect and their diverse interactions with the host framework. Such high selectivities further confirm the bright promising potential of UTSA‐36 a for the industrially important gas separation.

Natural gas that contains 75–90 % methane must undergo extensive processing to remove almost all other small hydrocarbons (such as ethane, ethylene, acetylene) to get a clean carbon energy source for its practical usage, it is thus very important to develop efficient technology for C2/C1 separation. Traditional separation technology of cryogenic distillation for such small hydrocarbons is highly energy consuming, so there is an increasing demand to develop novel materials and technologies as alternative processes to traditional distillation. In this regard, the microporous materials are very promising for such separation of small hydrocarbons, particularly after the technological realization and improvement of pressure swing adsorption (PSA) and temperature swing adsorption (TSA) over the past two decades. Until now, several traditional microporous materials such as ETS‐10, ALPO‐5, and SAPO‐5 have been examined for their potential applications in C2/C1 separation, although no selectivities have been reported.19 Our work highlights the first example of the emerging microporous metal–organic framework materials for the highly selective separation of C2/C1. The high separation selectivities for C2/C1 of 11 to 25 in the temperature range of 273 to 296 K features UTSA‐36 a as the potentially very useful and promising microporous material for such industrially important separation of C2 hydrocarbons over methane.

Conclusion

We have successfully developed a new approach to construct a 3D microporous MOF UTSA‐36 of primitive cubic net by making use of a pyridylcarboxylate and a bicarboxylate linker. The activated UTSA‐36 a exhibits significantly high separation of C2H6, C2H4, and C2H2 over CH4 with Henry’s law separation selectivities of 11 to 25 in the temperature range of 273 to 296 K. Such high separation capacity is attributed to the unique pore sizes within UTSA‐36 a in the range of 3.1 to 4.4 Å, which are comparable to the size of small hydrocarbons, for their enhanced pore confinement and different interactions with these small hydrocarbons. The realization of this first example of microporous MOFs for highly selective separation of C2/C1 hydrocarbons indicates the bright promise of the emerging microporous MOFs for such industrially important hydrocarbon separation. Because the pores within reticular MOFs can be rationally tuned by the different combination of these two types of organic linkers, it is expected that our novel approach will initiate extensive research on the construction of a series of reticular MOFs with small pores in the range of 2.5 to 5.5 Å, thus new microporous MOFs will be emerging in the near future, not only for the separation of small hydrocarbons, but also for the separation of industrially important small gases such as O2, N2, and Ar.

Experimental Section

Materials: 4‐(4‐Pyridyl) benzoic acid was acquired from Alfa Aesar and used as received. All other reagents and solvents were used as received from Aldrich without further purification.

Physical measurements: Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed with a Shimadzu TGA‐50 analyzer under nitrogen atmosphere with a heating rate of 3 °C min−1, from 22–900 °C. X‐ray powder patterns were measured with a Rigaku Ultima IV diffractometer at 40 kV, 40 mA for CuKα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å), with a scan rate of 3 deg min−1. The elemental analyses were performed with Perkin–Elmer 240 CHN analyzers form Galbraith Laboratories, Knoxville. A Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area analyzer was used to measure gas adsorption isotherms. To have a guest‐free framework, the fresh sample soaked in methanol (exchanged at least 15 times) was filtered and vacuumed at 120 °C overnight until the outgas rate was 5 μmHg min−1 prior to measurements. A sample of 126.7 mg was used for the sorption measurement and was maintained at 77 K with liquid nitrogen, 273 K with an ice–water bath (slush) and at 296 K with a water bath.

Synthesis of UTSA‐36: A mixture of 4‐(4‐pyridyl) benzoic acid (0.02 g, 0.1 mmol), terephthalic acid (0.0083 g, 0.05 mmol) and [Zn(NO3)2⋅6H2O] (0.06 g, 0.2 mmol) in DMF (3 mL) was sonicated for 10 min and heated in a 20 mL scintillation vial with a screw cap at 90 °C for 24 h. Colorless block‐shaped crystals were collected, washed with DMF and dried in air with 82 % yield. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for [Zn2(PBA)2(BDC)⋅(DMF)3(H2O)4] (C41H49N5O15Zn2): C 50.07, H 4.98, N 7.13; found: C 50.55, H 4.51, N 7.34.

Single‐crystal X‐ray structure determination: Crystal data for UTSA‐36 was collected at 98 K with a Rigaku Saturn724 diffractometer equipped with graphite‐monochromated MoKα radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) using an ω‐scan technique. The structure was solved by direct methods and subsequent difference Fourier syntheses, and refined with the SHELXTL software package.20–21 The non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of the lattice water molecule of UTSA‐36 could not be found in the difference Fourier map but are included in the molecular formula. All of the other hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions using idealized geometries (riding model) and assigned fixed isotropic displacement parameters. CCDC‐809395 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. This data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.
