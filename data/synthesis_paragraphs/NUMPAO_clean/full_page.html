Abstract

Abstract. Two coordination polymers, namely, [Zn(bpe)0.5(Htbip)(tbip)0.5]·H2O (1) and [Cd(bpe)0.5(tbip)] (2) [H2tbip = 5‐tert‐butylisophthalic acid and bpe = 1, 2‐ bis(4‐pyridyl) ethane] were synthesized through hydrothermal reactions. Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 presents a three‐dimensional (3D) six‐connected uninodal structure with the type of topology of svi‐x/I4/mcm → Ibam, whereas complex 2 holds a 2D 44‐sql layer structure. Moreover, the photoluminescent properties of the complexes at room temperature were investigated.

Introduction

Metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) as a new class of hybrid material constructed from metal ions or clusters linked by organic ligand have attracted considerable attention from scientists in chemistry and material science.1–5 Their tunable structures and excellent properties in the fields of luminescence, magnetism, gas adsorption, catalysis, and so on provide impetus for continuous investigation during the past two decades.6–10 Many factors as pH value, temperature, solvent molecules, metal‐to‐ligand ratio, counterion have been taken into account in designing and assembling of MOFs with expected structure and property.11–16 However, understanding the relationship between reaction parameters and the final structure is still a great challenge and of great importance.

We have long been focusing on the research of constructing MOFs with interesting structures and properties. For example, we have successfully isolated several novel entangled MOFs through tuning reaction parameters.17,18 With the aim of further exploring the assemble process and mechanism of MOFs with novel structure, we now pay our attention to a mixed ligand system of 5‐tert‐butylisophthalic acid (H2tbip) and 1, 2‐bis(4‐pyridyl) ethane (bpe). We choose this system as starting material for the following reasons: (i) the H2tbip can fully or partly deprotonated under different pH value and afford different coordination modes when connecting to metal centers and thus result in MOFs with various intriguing topological structures; (ii) H2tbip is anionic when coordinating to metal cation and thus fulfills the charge balance of the coordination complex; (iii) the tert‐butyl group in H2tbip ligand together with the π‐conjugated system in the benzene ring acting as auxochromic group may shift the absorption wavelength and promote charge transfer interactions of the resulting compounds; (iv) the H2tbip is somewhat less explored than the other 5‐R‐isophthalic acid (R = –CH3,–Br,–OH,–NH2, etc.); (v) the bpe ligand may display different configuration with respect to the relative orientations of the –CH2CH2– group.

Bearing all these factors in mind, in this paper, we report the syntheses, crystal structures, and luminescent properties of two new coordination complexes formulated as [Zn(bpe)0.5(Htbip)(tbip)0.5]·H2O (1) and [Cd(bpe)0.5(tbip)] (2). Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 presents a three‐dimensional (3D) six‐connected uninodal structure with the type of topology of svi‐x/I4/mcm → Ibam, whereas complex 2 holds 2D 44‐sql layer structure. The two complexes were further characterized by luminescent spectra, elemental analysis (EA), infrared spectroscopy (IR), powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD), and thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) (see Supporting Information).

Results and Discussion

Structural Analysis of Compound 1

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pnam (Table S1, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure 1a, every asymmetric unit for 1 consists of one central ZnII atom, one half completely deprotonated tbip2– anion (marked by O1–O2), one partially deprotonated Htbip– anion (marked by O3–O6), one half bpe molecule, and one uncoordinated water molecule. The central ZnII ion is coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O1, O2B, O3) from three distinct H2tbip molecules and one nitrogen atom (N1) from a bpe molecule in a slightly distorted tetrahedral arrangement. The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.9322(15)–1.9871(16) Å while the Zn–N bond length is 2.0181(17) Å, which are similar to those found in other zinc complexes.19–21 The bond angles around the central ZnII ion of ideal 109.47° for tetrahedron range from 99.98(6)° to 120.69(7)°. Compared with a regular tetrahedron, the bond lengths and bond angles apparently show a certain distortion. The completely deprotonated tbip2– anion holds a η2μ2‐η2μ2 coordination mode, whereas the partial deprotonated Htbip– anion displays monodentate η1 coordination mode (shown in Scheme 1a and b, respectively). The bpe molecule exhibits a planar conformation when coordinating to two ZnII ions with the dihedral angle between two phenyl rings being 0.000(93)°. Further insight into the structure of 1, it is found that the central ZnII ions are first linked by the fully deprotonated tbip2– anions to form 1D ladder‐like chains as shown in Figure 1b. These chains are further bridged by two groups of bpe molecules in two different directions to form a 3D coordination network. Topological analysis from the TOPOS program suggests that the final 3D framework holds a 6‐c uninodal structure with point symbol {36;44;64;7} and the type of topology is svi‐x/I4/mcm → Ibam as shown Figure 1c and Figure S1 (Supporting Information). Additionally, multiple hydrogen bonds between guest water molecules and oxygen atoms from H2tbip ligand exist in complex 1. As listed in Table S3 (Supporting Information), the O–H···O hydrogen bond length is 2.7003 Å, and the bond angle is 166°. The C–H···O hydrogen bond lengths are in the range of 2.7658–3.2647 Å, whereas the bond angles range from 100° to 147°. However, these hydrogen bonds don't change the topological structure.

Structural Analysis of Compound 2

X‐ray single crystal diffraction analysis reveals that complex 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As shown in Figure 2a, each asymmetric unit contains one crystallographically independent CdII cation, one completely deprotonated tbip2– anion, one bpe molecule, and one coordinated water molecule. The central CdII ion exhibits a six‐coordinate environment in a distorted octahedron arrangement. The equatorial positions are occupied by three oxygen atoms from two tbip2– anions and one nitrogen atom from the bpe molecule. The axial positions are occupied by one oxygen atom from a tbip2– anion and one oxygen atom from the coordinated water molecule. The Cd–O distances are in the range of 2.337(3) to 2.387(3) Å, whereas the Cd–N distance is 2.305(3) Å, which are all in agreement with those reported.22 The tbip2– anions in complex 2 hold η2μ2‐η2 coordination mode as shown in Scheme 1c, which is different from that in 1, and further link CdII cations to form Cd2(COO)4 dinuclear units as shown in Figure S2 (Supporting Information). As shown in Figure 2b, these dinuclear units are further linked by tbip2– anions and bpe molecules to form 2D 44‐sql layer structure.

According to the above structural description, we find that several reasons account for the structure differences of the two complexes. It is notable that the two different complexes were synthesized with the same ligand under the similar reaction condition just by modulation of the pH value indicating that pH value has great influence on the structures. From the structure analysis above, we can find that half of the H2tbip molecules in complex 1 are partly deprotonated. This is because complex 1 was synthesized at a lower pH value without addition of NaOH. By comparison, in the synthesis of complex 2 the pH was adjusted to a higher value by addition of NaOH and all the H2tbip ligand is fully deprotonated. Coordination modes of the H2tbip molecules are to some extent influenced by whether deprotonated or not. Further, coordination nature of the central metal cations also affects the final structures as ZnII ion in complex 1 is four‐coordinated, whereas the CdII ion in 2 is six‐coordinated.

Thermalgravimetric Analysis for 1 and 2

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on polycrystalline samples of complexes 1 and 2 to explore their stability between 30–900 °C in a nitrogen atmosphere. Their thermal decomposition curves are similar, containing two obvious steps as shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information). The weight loss of complex 1 from about 140 to 230 °C is about 3.65 % corresponding to the loss of the one free lattice water (calcd, 3.57 %). The structure collapse process occurred from 310 °C and the remaining residue is assumed to be ZnO (obsd. 15.86 %; expected 16.13 %). For complex 2, there is only one step of weight loss in temperature range of 305 to 580 °C is in consistent with combustion of organic groups (obsd. 70.68 %; expected 70.86 %).

Luminescent Properties for 1 and 2

The photoluminescence properties of complexes 1 and 2 and the free H2tbip, bpe ligands were investigated in dilute DMF solvent as emulsion (5.0 × 10–3 mol·L–1) at room temperature. The emission spectra of the samples under the excitation of 330 nm are shown in Figure 3. The free H2tbip ligand displays a weak emission band with a maximum at 350 nm, which could be attributed to the π→π* intraligand fluorescence. The bpe ligand shows no emission in the range of 320 to 500 nm. In contrast, complexes 1 and 2 give emission at 375 and 390 nm, respectively. Both complexes show much stronger fluorescence intensities compared with the free ligand because rigidity of the ligand is increased through coordinating to ZnII or CdII ion and reduce the loss of energy by radiationless thermal vibration.23,24 The red shifts are mostly attributed to the intraligand transition, which is in good agreement with other d10 coordination compounds.25,26 These results show that the central ZnII or CdII ions have great influence on both the emission peaks and the emission intensity of the complexes. Note that complex 2 is 15 nm red‐shifted compared to 1. This is probably due to the different nature and coordination environments of the central ZnII or CdII ions and the different structures of complexes 1 and 2.27 Unfortunately, the luminescent lifetimes of complexes 1 and 2 are not observed, since they are too short to be measured.

Conclusions

Two coordination polymers were synthesized from ZnII/CdII salts, 5‐tert‐butylisophthalic acid and 1, 2‐bis(4‐pyridyl) ethane under hydrothermal condition, and their crystal structures are solved using single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction data. Complex 1 exhibits interesting structure with svi‐x/I4/mcm → Ibam topology, whereas complex 2 holds a 2D 44‐sql layer structure. Luminescence analyses indicate that both complexes display enhanced emissions. These results may provide valuable information for construction of new functional coordination polymers with novel structures.

Experimental Section

Materials and General Methods: All commercially available reagents and starting materials were of reagent‐grade quality and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were carried out with an Elementar Vario EL III analyzer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded with a PerkinElmer Spectrum One as KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm–1. Thermogravimetric analysis was recorded with a NETZSCH STA 449C unit at a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 in a nitrogen atmosphere. X‐ray powered diffraction (XRPD) patterns of the samples were recorded with an X‐ray diffractometer (Bruker D8 FOCUS). Emission and excitation spectra were carried out with an Edinburgh FLS920 phosphorimeter equipped with a continuous Xe‐900 xenon lamp and an nF900 ns flash lamp.

Synthesis of {[Zn(bpe)0.5(Htbip)(tbip)0.5]·H2O}n (1). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.2 mmol), bpe (0.2 mmol), H2tbip (0.2 mmol) in deionized water (6 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave, heated at 130 °C for 72 h, and slowly cooled to room temperature during 24 h. Colorless rod crystals were recovered by filtration, washed by distilled water, and dried in air at ambient temperature. Yield: 57 % (based on Zn). C24H27NO7Zn (506.88): calcd. C 56.87, H 5.37, N 2.76 %; found: C 56.69, H 5.31, N 2.80 %. IR (KBr): = 3372 (m, br), 2963 (m), 2870 (w), 1661 (vs), 1606 (vs), 1562 (s), 1542 (s), 1419 (s), 1366 (m), 1320 (m), 1252 (s), 1169 (w), 1125 (w), 1070 (w), 1012 (w), 923 (m), 809 (m), 775 (s), 691 (m), 539 (m) cm–1.

Synthesis of [Cd(bpe)0.5(tbip)]n (2): A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·6H2O (0.2 mmol), bpe (0.2 mmol), H2tbip (0.2 mmol) in deionized water (6 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave, and the pH value of the mixture was adjusted to 7.5 with 1 M NaOH solution. Afterwards it was heated at 130 °C for 72 h and slowly cooled to room temperature during 24 h. Colorless block crystals were recovered by filtration, washed by distilled water, and dried in air at ambient temperature. Yield: 39 % (based on Cd). C18H18NO5Cd (440.73): calcd. C 49.05, H 4.12, N 3.18 %; found: C 49.11, H 4.11, N 3.15 %. IR (KBr): = 3459 (m, br), 2974 (m), 2865 (m), 1621 (s), 1572 (s), 1430 (m), 1355 (s), 1306 (w), 1267 (w), 1232 (w), 1174 (w), 1070 (w), 1030 (w), 928 (w), 829 (m), 775 (m), 736 (m), 603 (w), 534 (w) cm–1.

X‐ray Crystallography: Data collection was performed with a Rigaku Saturn 70 CCD (for complex 1) and an Xcalibur, Eos, Gemini (for complex 2) diffractometer with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at room temperature. Data reductions and absorption corrections were performed using the SAINT and SADABS software packages, respectively.28 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares on F2 using the SHELXTL‐97 program.29 All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The positions of hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were generated geometrically (C–H bond fixed at 0.99 Å). Idealized positions of hydrogen atoms belonging to nitrogen atoms and water molecules were located from Fourier difference maps and refined isotropically. Crystallographic data and structure determination summaries are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information). Selected bond lengths and angles of complexes 1 and 2 are listed in Tables S2–S4 (Supporting Information). The topological analysis and some diagrams were produced using the TOPOS program.30

Crystallographic data (excluding structure factors) for the structures in this paper have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre, CCDC, 12 Union Road, Cambridge CB21EZ, UK. Copies of the data can be obtained free of charge on quoting the depository numbers CCDC‐1008748 http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif (1) and CCDC‐1042674 http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif (2) (Fax: +44‐1223‐336‐033; E‐Mail: deposit@ccdc.cam.ac.uk, http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk).
