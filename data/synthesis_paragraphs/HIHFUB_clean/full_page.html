Introduction

In recent years, the rational design and synthesis of metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) by exploiting a crystal engineering strategy have drawn enormous interest not only due to their fascinating structural architectures and topologies, but also for their potential applications in catalysis,1 separation,2 drug delivery,3 sensors,4 gas sorption or storage5, magnetism,6 and as photocatalytic materials.7â€“9 These materials usually have 3D open frameworks. Of these, a variety of binodal network topologies based on mineral boracite, pyrite (FeS2), rutile (TiO2), Pt3O4, corundum (Al2O3), fluorite (CaF2), and PtS topologies have been realized.10 However, due to the limit of coordination numbers of metal centers and the steric hindrance of commonly used organic ligands, there is lack of highly-connected structures, such as (4,10)-, (3,12)-, and (4,12)-connected networks.11 Currently, considerable effort is devoted to enriching the structural library of highly connected MOFs. The utilization of the building block methodology to construct such frameworks represents a mainstream method. It is well documented that polynuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) have been proven to be a powerful strategy in the construction of MOFs with a high-connected framework structure.12

Taking the above into account, we chose 4,4â€²,4â€²â€²,4â€²â€²â€²-silanetetrayltetrabenzoic acid (H4L) (Scheme 1) and the salts of lanthanide metals as struts to construct MOFs, mainly based on the following considerations: (i) H4L ligand may show a variety of conformations and coordination modes to metal ions because of its four carboxylate groups, similar to other tetrapodal carboxylic ligands.13 (ii) It is a rigid ligand, which could link the metal ions or clusters into an extended network with high thermal stability. (iii) The lanthanide family of metal cations usually has high coordination number,14 likely leading to MOFs with highly-connected topology. Herein, we report the syntheses and crystal structures of three novel lanthanide coordination compounds, namely [NdL(H2O)][NH2(CH3)2]Â·(DMF)xÂ·(H2O)y (x â‰ˆ 2, y â‰ˆ 4) (1), [Eu4L3(H2O)6]Â·(DMF)yÂ·(H2O)x (x â‰ˆ 2, y â‰ˆ 4) (2) and [Er4L3(H2O)6]Â·(DMF)xÂ·(H2O)z (x â‰ˆ 2, z â‰ˆ 5) (3), respectively. Their structures have been determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction and further characterized by elemental analysis, and IR spectroscopy. The gas adsorption behavior of 1 has been investigated. In addition, the relationship between unusual topological structures of the resulting MOFs and coordination modes of ligands and metal clusters has been discussed.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

The title compounds were prepared solvothermally in an acidic system by using the rigid tetrapodal ligand H4L and different metal salts under the otherwise same conditions. Numerous conditions and metal sources were investigated. We found that the addition of appropriate amounts of HCl was necessary for the formation of the title compounds. Otherwise, clear solution or unknown powders were obtained. In addition, the crystals of the three compounds were obtained only at specified reaction temperatures.

Structure description

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with eight asymmetric units in one unit cell. Each asymmetric unit contains one single L ligand, one crystallographically independent Nd atom, one coordinating water molecule and one NH2(CH3)2+ counterion. The NH2(CH3)2+ was likely generated via either hydrolysis or decarbonylation of DMF under solvothermal conditions, which is not without precedent.19 The guest molecules in 1 could not be determined crystallographically. As shown in Fig. 1, the three-dimensional structure of 1 can be assembled from a secondary building unit (SBU) of dinuclear metalâ€“carboxylate cluster {Nd2(COOR)8}. Two neighboring Nd centers (Nd1 and Nd1A) are bridged through two carboxyl groups via chelating modes to form a dinuclear metalâ€“carboxylate cluster. Each dinuclear unit connects eight carboxylate groups from eight independent fully deprotonated L4âˆ’ ligands. The Nd atom is nine-coordinated by seven oxygen atoms from four chelating bidentate carboxylate groups of three crystallographically equivalent L ligands, one oxygen atom from a monodentate carboxylate group of another L ligand, and one oxygen atom from a coordinated H2O molecule. The Ndâ€“O distances range from 2.355(2) to 2.698(3) Ã…, which are similar to those found in other Nd complexes.20 The ligand H4L is fully deprotonated and coordinates via its four carboxylate groups to four dinuclear Ndâ€“carboxylate units in monodentate, chelation, bridging and chelating coordination modes (Fig. 1b), forming a complicated 3D open framework structure.

The structure of 1 shows small 1D channels with a window size of 4.3 Ã— 3.1 Ã…2 (taking account of the van der Waals radii) along the b axis (Fig. 2), in which the disordered solvent molecules reside. The solvent accessible volume is estimated by using the PLATON program18b to be about 39.5% of the total crystal volume.


Fig. 2 View of 3D coordination framework of 1, showing 1D channels along the b axis. The coordinated water and solvent molecules are omitted for clarity.

A better insight into the nature of 1 can be achieved by the application of the topological approach, i.e. reducing multidimensional structures to simple node-and-linker nets. As shown in Fig. 3a. The dinuclear {Nd2(COOR)8} SBU is described as a pentagonal dodecahedron and regarded as an eight-connected node. Each L ligand, which bonds to four {Nd2(COO)8} inorganic units, is regarded as a four-connected organic SBU (Fig. 3b). On the basis of this simplification, the structure of 1 is represented as a binodal (4,8)-connected net. Its SchlÃ¤fli symbol was calculated by using the TOPOS program21 as {41261284}{46}2 with eight-connected dinuclear {Nd2(COOR)8} and four-connected ligand L nodes in the ratio of 1:2. The net is best known as fluorite (CaF2) topology (Fig. 3c).


Fig. 3 Views of (a) 8-connecting {Nd2(COO)8}, (b) 4-connecting L building units, and (c) schematic representation of the flu topology in 1 with different nodes discriminated by colors. Hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity.

The single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies reveal that compounds 2 and 3 are isostructural and crystallize in the orthorhombic space group Cccm with four asymmetric units in one unit cell. As a representative example, only the crystal structure of 3 is described here in detail. As shown in Fig. 4, each asymmetric unit in 3 contains two crystallographically independent Er atoms (half-occupied Er1 and Er2), two crystallographically independent L ligands (one quarter-occupied L1 and half-occupied L2), and one and a half coordinating water molecules. The Er1 center is seven-coordinated with six oxygen atoms from six bidentate carboxylate groups of three L ligands, and one oxygen atom from a coordinated water molecule. The Er2 atom is eight-coordinated with six oxygen atoms from three L ligands, and two oxygen atom from two coordinated water molecules. An S-shaped tetranuclear SBU is formed by the linkage of Er1 and Er2 together with two other 2-fold axis symmetry-related Er1A and Er2A. Notably, such a tetranuclear Er(III) cluster is quite different from other known examples.22 It contains 7-, and 8-coordinated Er(III) ions which result from the various linking modes of L ligand. As also shown in Fig. 4, the ligand L exhibits two coordination modes in 3: the ligand L1 (Si1) is fully deprotonated and links to four Er1 atoms and four Er2 atoms in bidentate coordination modes. On the other hand, the ligand L2 (Si2) is also fully deprotonated and connects to four Er1 atoms and two Er2 atoms in a bidentate and chelating coordination mode, respectively.


Fig. 4 Views of (a) 12-connecting inorganic and (b and c) 4-connecting organic building units of 3, showing the local coordination environments. The H atoms are omitted for clarity.

The 3D framework of 3 is constructed by a 12-connecting tetranuclear Er(III) cluster and a 4-connecting L ligand. The ligand L coordinates via its four carboxylate groups to four tetranuclear Er(III) cluster in chelation and bridging coordination modes, while the tetranuclear Er(III) cluster is connected by twelve L ligands. This gives rise to a complicated 3D open framework structure. The structure shows small 1D channels with a window size of 3.9 Ã— 3.9 Ã…2 along the a axis, in which the disordered solvent molecules reside (Fig. 5). The solvent accessible volume is estimated by using the PLATON program18b to be about 48.7% of the total crystal volume.


Fig. 5 Packing view of 3, showing small channels along the a axis. The coordinated water and solvent molecules are omitted for clarity.

To fully understand the structure of 3, a topological analysis of the network has been performed with the TOPOS program21 by considering both the metal and the ligand as topological nodes. Apparently, the tetranuclear Er(III) motifs could be regarded as the SBUs, each of which is connected by twelve L ligands. On the other hand, each L ligand is linked to four tetranuclear Er(III) SBUs and the resulting 4-connected nodes have the same SchlÃ¤fli symbol of {46}. Notably, the vertex symbols of two independent L nodes are not equivalent, that is, (42.42.42.42.43.43) for type L1 (Si1) ligand, and (4.4.42.42.42.42) for type L2 (Si2). Each tetranuclear SBU is linked to twelve L nodes [4 type L1 (Si1) + 8 type L2 (Si2)]. Consequently, the 3D structure of 3 presents a novel (4,4,12)-connected 3-nodal network with a SchlÃ¤fli symbol of {430626810}{46}3 (Fig. 6).


Fig. 6 Schematic representation of the (4,4,12)-connected network in 3 with different nodes discriminated by colors (green and cyan, 4-connecting nodes; purple, 12-connecting nodes).

It has been demonstrated that the construction of highly-connected coordination frameworks by using polynuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) usually produces intriguing architectures with highly-connected topologies and excellent properties.12 In this paper, our work further illustrates that utilizing a polynuclear metal cluster of the lanthanide as an SBU is a rational and effective strategy for the design and construction of highly-connected MOFs with unusual structural topology. In compound 1, the binuclear Nd cluster motif can be regarded as the SBU, which is surrounded by eight L ligands, while each L ligand is connected to four binuclear Nd clusters and acts as a 4-connected node. As a consequence, the resulting 3D framework can be simplified as a (4,8)-connected net with flu topology. Although the fluorite topology is relatively common in minerals, its observation is rare in lanthanoid-based coordination polymers as 8-connecting cube nodes are required.23 In contrast, there are a handful of examples based on 8-connecting d-block metals, including polynuclear {Mn2}, {Co3}, {Mn4}, and {Cd4} units, with 4-connecting organic ligands derived from 5-carboxyl-1-carboxymethyl-3-oxidopyridinium, tetrakis[(4-carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]methane acid, trimellitic acid and tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)methane.24â€“27

In compounds 2 and 3, the tetranuclear Eu and Er cluster units serve as the 12-connecting nodes, while the tetracarboxylate L ligands represent the 4-connecting nodes. The resulting structure is an exceptional 3-D trinodal network consisting of alternating 4-connected and 12-connected nodes with a {430626810}{46}3 point symbol. To the best of our knowledge, compounds 2 and 3 represent the first examples of 3D lanthanoid-based metalâ€“organic coordination frameworks with highly (4,4,12)-connected networks based on S-shaped tetranuclear metal clusters.

Thermal properties

To investigate the thermal stability of compounds 1â€“3, the TG analyses were carried out (Fig. 7). Compound 1 displayed a weight loss of 25.4% at 25â€“300 Â°C, corresponding to the complete release of the free guest molecules as well as the coordinated solvent molecules (calcd 25.26%). On further heating, the framework began to decompose with the slow release of the organic L4âˆ’ ligand. Compounds 2 and 3 exhibited a similar TGA profile. The first weight loss occurred at 25â€“225 Â°C with weight losses of 16.0% for 2 (calcd 16.98%), and 18.2% (calcd 18.27%) for 3, respectively, contributed to the loss of coordinated water molecules and guest molecules. Further heating resulted in the release of the coordinated organic ligand and the collapse of the lattice structures.

In order to check if the solvent molecules in 1â€“3 can be removed and replaced by other similar molecules, the as-synthesized samples were immersed in CH2Cl2 for 36 h with the solvent refreshed every 12 h. After the solvent exchange, the TG analyses were carried out, which show the solvent molecules can be partly replaced (Fig. S4 in ESIâ€ ). In attempts to remove guest molecules in the framework of 1â€“3, the CH2Cl2-exchanged samples were then degassed at 100 Â°C under dynamic vacuum for 24 h. The thermal stabilities of compounds 1â€“3 after activation were then checked by X-ray powder diffraction. The results showed that main XRD peaks of the activated 1 matched those of the as-synthesized and the simulated ones on the basis of the single crystal structure (Fig. S1 in ESIâ€ ). However, the framework of the activated 2 had converted into an unknown phase, while that of 3 has been severely deteriorated as shown by the occurrence of peak broadening and peaks of amorphous phase.

Adsorption properties

The X-ray structure analyses showed that compounds 1â€“3 consist of solvent accessible voids of 39.5, 47.9, and 48.7% of their respective total crystal volume. We thus carried out gas adsorption measurements over three compounds. The results showed that only compound 1 exhibited permanent porosity, while compounds 2 and 3 showed negligible gas adsorption, which are consistent with the thermal stability studies. As shown in Fig. 8, N2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K for 1 reveals a type-I behavior, indicating the microporous nature. The Brunauerâ€“Emmettâ€“Teller (BET) and the Langmuir surface areas were calculated from the N2 adsorption isotherm to be 153 and 201 m2 gâˆ’1, respectively. The O2 adsorption isotherm at 77 K for 1 is also of type I (Fig. S5 in ESIâ€ ). The maximum O2 uptake is higher than that of N2, which is as expected.28
Fig. 8 Gas adsorption (solid symbols) and desorption (open symbols) isotherms of 1 for N2 at 77 K.

The H2 sorption measurements at 77 K were also carried out to check the hydrogen storage performance of 1. As shown in Fig. 9, the activated 1 can uptake 71 cm3 gâˆ’1 (0.64 wt%) of H2 at 77 K and 760 Torr. Because the H2 sorption isotherm of compound 1 is not fully saturated, a higher hydrogen sorption capacity may be expected under higher pressures. In addition, the hydrogen sorption isotherms display remarkable hysteresis between adsorption and desorption curves at 77 K. Such a hysteresis is often observed in porous MOFs and offers the possibility of developing new porous MOFs that can unload hydrogen at high pressures and store it at low pressures.29


Fig. 9 Gas adsorption (solid symbols) and desorption (open symbols) isotherms of 1 for (a) H2 at 77 K, (b) CO2 and (c) CH4 at 273 K.

To examine the gas storage capacity and selective adsorption behaviors of 1, CO2 and CH4 adsorption measurements have been carried out at 273 K. As shown in Fig. 9, compound 1 takes up large amounts of carbon dioxide (31 cm3 gâˆ’1, 1.38 mmol gâˆ’1), which are higher than that of methane (12 cm3 gâˆ’1, 0.53 mmol gâˆ’1) at 273 K and 760 Torr. This discrimination can be attributed to the differences in quadrupolar nature and kinetic diameters (kinetic diameter: CO2, 3.3 Ã…; CH4, 3.8 Ã…) between the two gas molecules.30 The large quadrupolar moment of CO2 induces strong interactions between gas molecules and the MOF, while small 1D channels with a window size of 4.3 Ã— 3.1 Ã…2 in 1 further limit the adsorption of CH4. To evaluate the separation ability, the dual-site Langmuirâ€“Freundlich model was applied to experimental isotherms, and the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) calculations were performed under equimolar mixture conditions.30 The IAST selectivities of 1 are presented in Fig. S6 (ESIâ€ ). It is shown that in the low-pressure region, the selectivity of CO2 over CH4 is large, âˆ¼5, while it drops as pressure increases. The selectivity is relatively low in comparison with other highly selective adsorbents such as zeolites and MOFs.30

Conclusions

In summary, three 3D MOFs based on 4,4â€²,4â€²â€²,4â€²â€²â€²-silanetetrayltetrabenzoic acid (H4L) and lanthanide metals have been hydrothermally synthesized and characterized. The three compounds possess highly-connected 3D frameworks with (4,8)-flu and (4,4,12) structure topology, built from a 8-connecting dinuclear neodymiumâ€“carboxylate cuboid block and a 12-connecting tetranuclear lanthanideâ€“carboxylate unit, respectively. The tetrahedral ligand (H4L) acts as a 4-connected tetrahedral node to extend structure dimension, leading to highly-connected frameworks. The successful construction of the three MOFs with rare topological networks reveals that using polynuclear lanthanide metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) could be an effective method in the construction of MOFs with a highly-connected network.
