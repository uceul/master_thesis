Diverse architectures and luminescence properties of two novel copper(I) coordination polymers assembled from 2,6-bis[3-(pyrid-4-yl)-1,2,4-triazolyl]pyridine ligands†

Jie Pan ab, Cai-Ping Liu a, Fei-Long Jiang a, Ming-Yan Wu a, Lian Chen a, Jin-Jie Qian ab, Kong-Zhao Su ab, Xiu-Yan Wan ab and Mao-Chun Hong *a
aKey Laboratory of Optoelectronic Materials Chemistry and Physics, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou 350002, China. E-mail: hmc@fjirsm.ac.cn; Fax: +86 591 83794946; Tel: +86 591 83792460
bUniversity of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, 100049, China

Received 28th November 2014 , Accepted 2nd January 2015

First published on 7th January 2015

Two attractive Cu(I) coordination polymers with unique structures, namely, {[Cu8(bptp)4]·6H2O} (1) and {[Cu5(bptp)2(CN)]·2H2O} (2), have been successfully synthesized based on a V-shaped multidentate N-containing ligand 2,6-bis[3-(pyrid-4-yl)-1,2,4-triazolyl]pyridine (H2bptp). The structural analysis reveals that complex 1 displays a Cu8 cluster-based infinite 1D ladder chain, in which each H2bptp ligand links four or five Cu(I) ions through nitrogen atoms of pyridine/triazole groups. Complex 2 features an unusual (3,4,5)-connected 3D topological network with the Schläfli symbol (42·54·63·7)(42·63·7)(5·6·8)(5·62·7·82), which has not been reported before. The structural and dimensional diversity of the two complexes indicates that H2bptp exhibits strong coordination ability and diverse coordination modes. It is worth mentioning that the Cu⋯Cu distances in 1 and 2 are shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii of copper(I) (2.80 Å), implying the metal–metal bonding interactions. What is more, the solid-state luminescence bands of 1 and 2 have also been investigated between 298 and 10 K. Interestingly, the low-energy emission bands of 1 and 2 exhibit yellow/orange-red luminescence, and their intensities increase gradually upon cooling.

Introduction

Currently, much attention has been focused on the assembly of coordination polymers (CPs) with intriguing structural motifs and encouraging potential applications.1 Among CP research fields, photo-functional transition-metal complexes have received increasing interest since they play a crucial role in chemical sensors, organic light-emitting devices (OLEDs), biological labeling and so on.2 As it is known, copper(I)-based complexes have become an important class of luminescent CPs, owing to their abundant resource, extensive availability, relatively low price and non-toxicity.3 Previous reports suggest that Cu(I) ions with d10 closed-shell electronic configurations possess high affinity for N donors and irregular coordination geometry with coordination numbers 2–5, which appear to be the key to the self-assembly of novel structural motifs.4 Moreover, they usually exhibit metallophilic interactions which can lead to the enhancement of photophysical properties as well as stabilization of metal complexes.

For the Cu(I)-polymer design, rigid multidentate N-heterocyclic molecules are excellent candidates for the construction of various coordination networks with intriguing structures and functional properties.5 Polypyridine ligands, as a crucial family of multidentate N-donor ligands, have been extensively used due to their versatile coordination conformations and strong coordination ability, allowing for various structural topologies.6 Nevertheless, the coordination chemistry of multi(pyridine–triazole) ligands, which integrate the features of both polypyridine and polytriazole, has been rarely investigated.7 To continue our research on the pyridine–triazole system, 2,6-bis[3-(pyrid-4-yl)-1,2,4-triazolyl]pyridine (H2bptp), featuring three pyridine rings connected by two triazole groups, captures our attention (Scheme 1). For this V-shaped organic ligand, there are several obvious characteristics: firstly, H2bptp can provide nine potential nitrogen donors and exhibit various structural patterns to yield diverse fascinating networks; secondly, five aromatic rings of H2bptp can form different dihedral angles through the rotation of Cpyridine–Ctriazole to satisfy the requirements of the coordination geometries of metal ions; thirdly, H2bptp can ligate metal centers closely, giving rise to multinuclear-based networks with unique architectures and remarkable properties. On the other hand, we could hardly predict the target structures for H2bptp since there are too many possible coordination fashions. To the best of our knowledge, CPs based on H2bptp are relatively limited.8 Furthermore, the use of H2bptp in the construction of Cu(I) coordination polymers is practically unexplored up to now, which is still a great challenge. Thus, further systematic investigation on the coordination chemistry of H2bptp is absolutely necessary.


Scheme 1 Structure of the H2bptp ligand used in this work.

Herein, two H2bptp-based Cu(I) coordination polymers, namely, {[Cu8(bptp)4]·6H2O} (1) and {[Cu5(bptp)2(CN)]·2H2O} (2), have been successfully synthesized under solvothermal conditions. The structures and topology analyses of complexes 1 and 2, along with the coordination modes of the H2bptp ligand, have been discussed in detail. In addition, the luminescence properties of the two complexes have also been investigated between 298 and 10 K to track their response to temperature.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial vendors and used without further purification. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected using a diffractometer (Rigaku DMAX2500) with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were carried out on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. IR spectra were recorded with KBr pellets on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum One FT-IR spectrometer in the range 400–4000 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a NETZSCH STA 449C instrument at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under nitrogen atmosphere. Emission and excitation spectra were recorded on an Edinburgh Instruments FLS920 spectrofluorometer. The pulse width of the picosecond pulsed diode laser used for time-resolved decay measurements is 65 ps. The solid-state diffuse reflectance UV-vis spectra were measured on a Perkin-Elmer Lambda 35 UV-vis spectrophotometer with an integration sphere.

Synthesis of {[Cu8(bptp)4]·6H2O} (1)

A mixture containing CuI (19 mg, 0.1 mmol), H2bptp (35 mg, 0.1 mmol), 4 mL of acetonitrile (MeCN) and 4 mL of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) was placed in a Teflon reactor heated at 150 °C for 3 days. After cooling to room temperature, yellow block crystals of 1 were obtained in 51% yield based on H2bptp. Anal. calcd for C76H56N36O6Cu8: C, 43.93%; H, 2.72%; N, 24.27%. Found: C, 43.89%; H, 2.74%; N, 24.30%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3413(s), 1617(s), 1578(m), 1402(m), 1384(w), 1093(w), 808(w), 749(w).

Synthesis of {[Cu5(bptp)2(CN)]·2H2O} (2)

A mixture of CuBr (29 mg, 0.2 mmol), H2bptp (35 mg, 0.1 mmol), 2 mL of ammonia and 8 mL of MeCN was placed in a Teflon reactor, which was heated at 150 °C for 3 days. Upon cooling to room temperature, yellow block crystals of 2 were collected in 64% yield based on H2bptp. Anal. calcd for C39H26N19O2Cu5: C, 42.18%; H, 2.36%; N, 23.97%. Found: C, 42.22%; H, 2.33%; N, 23.92%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3411(m), 1638(m), 1615(s), 1568(w), 1400(m), 1062(w), 846(w), 749(w).

X-Ray crystallography

Single crystal X-ray diffraction data for complexes 1 and 2 were recorded by a Rigaku Mercury CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromatic Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at 293 K. All of the structures were resolved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares fitting on F2 by SHELX-97.9 All non-hydrogen atoms except some solvent molecules were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. The positions of hydrogen atoms on the organic ligands were generated geometrically and refined using a riding model. In the structure of complex 1, the highly disordered solvent molecules were treated with the program PLATON/SQUEEZE, and the final chemical formula of 1 was calculated from SQUEEZE results combined with the TGA and elemental analyses data. In the case of 2, hydrogen atoms of free water molecules could not be determined. Basic information pertaining to crystal parameters and structure refinements is summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1 of the ESI.† CCDC 1034648 and 1034649 contain the supplementary crystallographic data (1 and 2, respectively) for this paper.

Result and discussion

Crystal structure of {[Cu8(bptp)4]·6H2O} (1)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in a triclinic crystal system with the space group P. As depicted in Fig. 1, there are eight crystallographically independent Cu(I) ions, four completely deprotonated bptp2− ligands and six guest water molecules per asymmetric unit.
Fig. 1 View of the asymmetric unit of complex 1, hydrogen atoms and guest water molecules are omitted for clarity.

If the close Cu⋯Cu contact is neglected, the Cu atoms are coordinated in two types of coordination geometries. For Cu1, Cu3, Cu7 and Cu8, they are all coordinated by three nitrogen atoms from bptp2− ligands, giving a CuN3 distorted planar triangular geometry with bond angles ranging from 62.02 to 160.21°. However, Cu2, Cu4, Cu5 and Cu6 are surrounded by two nitrogen atoms of distinct bptp2− N-donor ligands, displaying a linear geometry. Notably, the Cu3–N14 bond (2.455 Å) is slightly longer than other Cu–N bonds in 1, indicating that there may be only a weak interaction between Cu3 and N14 atoms.10 The unprecedented octanuclear copper cluster can be described as a [Cu6(bptp)4]2− anion core with two Cu(I) ions, bound together by triazole groups of bptp2− organic ligands, resulting in a [Cu8(bptp)4] secondary building unit (SBU). In detail, Cu1–Cu6 ions lie at the vertices of a distorted polyhedron, held together tightly by nitrogen atoms of four different bptp2− ligands. The Cu⋯Cu distances in the [Cu6(bptp)4]2− hexanuclear core are 2.905 Å (Cu1⋯Cu2), 3.024 Å (Cu1⋯Cu3), 3.039 Å (Cu2⋯Cu4), 2.547 Å (Cu3⋯Cu4), 2.926 Å (Cu3⋯Cu6), 2.993 Å (Cu4⋯Cu5) and 2.926 Å (Cu5⋯Cu6), which are close to the sum of the van der Waals radii (2.8 Å), and thus imply metal–metal bonding interactions.11 The further connectivity between Cu7, Cu8 and [Cu6(bptp)4]2− core gives rise to the [Cu8(bptp)4] SBU (Fig. 2a, Cu5⋯Cu7 = 3.533 Å, Cu6⋯Cu7 = 3.388 Å, Cu1⋯Cu8 = 3.493 Å and Cu2⋯Cu8 = 3.363 Å). Meanwhile, each SBU is connected to three equivalent ones through the pyridine/triazole nitrogen atoms of bptp2−, resulting in a Cu8 cluster-based infinite 1D ladder chain (Fig. 2b and c). Due to the coordination effect and the rigidity of bptp2−, strong π⋯π interactions between pyridine and triazole groups of adjacent chains are observed with a centroid–centroid distance of 3.468 Å, generating a 3D supramolecular framework (Fig. 3).


Fig. 2 (a) View of the Cu8 cluster in 1, bptp2− ligands are omitted for clarity; (b) 1D infinite chain structure; (c) simplification of the Cu8-based 1D chain of 1.

Fig. 3 The structure of the 3D supramolecular framework of complex 1.

Crystal structure of {[Cu5(bptp)2(CN)]·2H2O} (2)

When CuBr was utilized in place of CuI under appropriate reaction conditions, complex 2 was obtained. It is also solved in the triclinic space group P with five Cu(I) ions, a pair of fully deprotonated bptp2− ligands, one CN− anion and two free water molecules in the asymmetric unit. As shown in Fig. 4, Cu1 and Cu4 exhibit similar coordination environments, which are completed by three nitrogen atoms from separate bptp2− ligands, thus representing the CuN3 trigonal coordination sphere. Cu3 has a coordination environment similar to those of Cu1 and Cu4 except that one CN− nitrogen atom is coordinated instead of the bptp2− nitrogen atom. Cu5 also bears a distorted trigonal environment, formed by two nitrogen atoms of bptp2− ligands and one carbon atom provided by the CN− anion. While for the Cu2 center, it is bound by two nitrogen atoms of bptp2−, featuring a linear coordination geometry with a N–Cu–N angle of 167.40°.
Fig. 4 Coordination environment of the Cu(I) ions in 2 with hydrogen atoms and free water molecules omitted for clarity. Symmetry codes: (A) −x, 1 − y, 1 − z; (B) −1 + x, y, 1 + z; (C) −1 + x, y, z; (D) −1 − x, 1 − y, 2 − z; (E) −x, 2 − y, 1 − z.

In 2, the Cu–N bond distances vary from 1.870 to 2.315 Å, which lie in the range of other Cu(I) complexes.12 As displayed in Fig. 5a, Cu3, Cu5 and their own symmetry-related atoms are integrated by pyridine/triazole groups from bptp2− and CN− “linkers”, thus leading to the Cu–bptp 1D chain with a Cu3⋯Cu5 distance of 4.905 Å. Adjacent 1D chains are further connected together by Cu1 and nitrogen atoms of bptp2− producing a 2D network (Cu1⋯Cu3 = 4.643 Å, Fig. 5b). In addition, these non-interpenetrated 2D layers are extended to a fascinating 3D architecture bridged by Cu2, Cu4 and pyridine/triazole rings of bptp2− (Fig. 6). It is worth noting that Cu1 and Cu2 atoms in 2 have a distance of 2.701 Å which is comparable with the Cu⋯Cu separation found previously, indicating a strong metal–metal contact.


Fig. 5 (a) A drawing showing the Cu-bptp 1D chain in 2; (b) view of the 2D layer structure.

Fig. 6 The 3D framework of 2 after omitting the uncoordinated water molecules.

Better insight into such an intricate framework can be accessed by the topology method. As each CN− anion connects with Cu3 and Cu5, the entire section can be reduced to a 4-connected node. Furthermore, bptp2− links four or five Cu(I) ions and each Cu(I) ion connects three ligands in turn, accordingly the bptp2− and Cu(I) ions can be regarded as 5-, 4- and 3-connected nodes, respectively. As a result, the complicated structure of 2 is simplified to a novel (3,4,5)-connected 3D topological network with the Schläfli symbol (42·54·63·7)(42·63·7)(5·6·8)(5·62·7·82) (Fig. 7). As far as we know, this new topology has not been reported in the literature up to now.


Fig. 7 The (3,4,5)-connected topological net of 2.

The diverse coordination modes of H2bptp and structural discussion

H2bptp is a multidentate N-heterocyclic ligand, which has various coordination modes and different conformations. This makes H2bptp an excellent building block for the rational design and assembly of target complexes with unexpected structures and novel applications. From the structure descriptions above, we can see that H2bptp can display six types of distinct coordination fashions in this work, as displayed in Scheme 2, leading to the formation of diverse structural features. In complex 1, four H2bptp ligands are completely deprotonated and bridge/chelate four or five Cu(I) ions via modes I–IV, resulting in a Cu8 cluster-based infinite 1D ladder chain. The H2bptp ligands in complex 2, as hexadentate bridging/chelating ligands, coordinate to five or six metal cations through nitrogen atoms of pyridine/triazole groups (modes V and VI), producing a charming 3D network. Owing to the steric hindrance, the five aromatic rings of H2bptp are not coplanar. In 1 and 2, two terminal pyridine groups are either free or exhibit monodentate coordination, and they are twisted by 3.07–41.42° relative to the central pyridine ring to enhance the structural stability (3.07–40.00° for 1 and 4.44–41.42° for 2). These results suggest that the number of coordination sites and coordination modes of H2bptp N-donor ligands are decisive factors in shaping up the architecture of the resultant CPs. Obviously, H2bptp has an advantage over other N-donor ligands since it can modulate its conformations and coordination modes to fine-tune itself to meet the coordination demands of metal centers. So this kind of ligand deserves to be further investigated in the field of crystal engineering.
Scheme 2 The coordination modes of H2bptp ligands in complexes 1 and 2.

PXRD patterns and thermogravimetric analyses

To confirm whether the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk materials, PXRD experiments were carried out for complexes 1 and 2. As shown in Fig. S1 of the ESI,† the PXRD experimental and computer-simulated patterns match well, thus indicating the phase purity of each bulk sample.

The thermal stabilities of 1 and 2 were analyzed on crystalline samples from 30 to 1000 °C under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S2†). For 1, a weight loss of 5.22% (calcd. 5.20%) is observed in the temperate range of 35–325 °C, which is due to the loss of six lattice water molecules. The anhydrous sample remains stable up to 460 °C, from which, backbone decomposition follows. The TGA curve of 2 shows a weight loss of 3.21% (calcd. 3.24%) from room temperature to 115 °C, this can be assigned to the loss of two solvent water molecules. Upon further heating, the remaining framework starts to collapse.

Luminescence properties

The combination of metal centers and conjugated organic ligands in coordination polymers provides a possibility for the development of new types of photoluminescent materials with potential applications.13 It is well known that the copper(I) complexes with both discrete and multi-dimensional structures show excellent photophysical properties due to their structure- and metal-dependent emission.14 Bearing this mind, the luminescence properties of complexes 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state between 298 and 10 K to track their response to temperature. As shown in Fig. S3,† the UV-visible absorption spectra of complexes 1 and 2 show a broad band in the range 230 to 500 nm. At room temperature, excitation of the microcrystalline samples of 1 and 2 at 380 nm produces single, low-energy emission with a maximum at 623 nm for 1 (yellow luminescence, Fig. 8 and S4†) and 673 nm for 2 (orange-red luminescence). To further analyze the nature of such emission bands, the emission spectra of the free H2bptp ligand have also been explored. As depicted in Fig. S5,† upon excitation at 350 nm, H2bptp containing pyridine and triazole conjugated groups shows an emission maximum at 475 nm (blue luminescence), which is probably attributed to the π*–π transitions. As a result, we can presume that the emission bands of 1 and 2 may not be intraligand charge transition in nature since H2bptp has almost no contribution to the luminescence behaviors of the two complexes in the range of 600–700 nm. According to the previous reports on Cu(I) complexes, the emission bands of 1 and 2 are tentatively assigned to originate from the metal-to-ligand charge-transfer (MLCT) character.15 The temperature-dependent luminescence of 1 and 2 is revealed by immersing the samples in liquid nitrogen. The emission maximum in the solid state luminescence spectrum of 1 has no obvious shift with further cooling except for an increase of intensity. While for 2, when cooled to 10 K, the emission peak shows a slight blue shift of about 23 nm, and the intensity relatively strengthens. As far as we know, the increment of emission intensity under cryogenic conditions is due to the reduction of non-radiative decay processes.16
Fig. 8 (a) Emission spectra of 1 at varied temperatures; (b) emission spectra of 2.

A time-resolved luminescence study was further performed by monitoring the most intense emission of the respective samples at 298 K and 10 K. The decay curves can be fitted well as shown in Fig. 9, and detailed data are listed in Table S2.† The luminescence of some complexes may be affected by some factors such as the defects of crystals and so on during the luminescence measurement, which may be the reason for the bi-exponential behavior. At 298 K, the emission decay lifetime in the solid state is τ298 K = 0.2389 μs for 1 and τ298 K = 1.084 μs for 2. Lowering the temperature to 10 K, relatively longer lifetimes of 1 (τ10 K = 8.872 μs) and 2 (τ10 K = 12.13 μs) are observed, which are ascribed to the reduction of the nonradiative decay.17 In addition, the lifetime of complex 2 is longer than that of complex 1. This result is probably due to their different structures. The decreased amount of water molecules in 2 virtually decreases the nonradiative decay, thus increasing the emission lifetime.18 Moreover, the luminescence behavior of 2 may be associated with its relatively dense structure, the crystal density of 2 (1.644 g cm−3) is somewhat higher than that of 1 (1.616 g cm−3). From the structural point of view, the dihedral angles between the terminal pyridine groups and the central pyridine ring change from 3.07–40.00° for 1 and 4.44–41.42° for 2. The luminescence performance of 1 and 2 is sensitive to the detailed structure, like Cu–Cu interactions and packing modes. These observations suggest that complexes 1 and 2 can serve as candidates for potential photoactive materials.

Conclusions

In this study, the self-assembly of copper(I) halide salts with the pyridine–triazole ligand H2bptp under solvothermal reactions affords two charming copper(I) coordination polymers. The polydentate bridging/chelating H2bptp ligand adopts tetra-, penta- and hexadentate coordinated modes in complexes 1 and 2, giving rise to various connectivity patterns and remarkable structural diversity. Complex 1 bears a Cu8 cluster-based infinite 1D ladder chain, while 2 exhibits a fascinating 3D framework. The comparison of 1 and 2 clearly demonstrates that the multiple coordination sites and the rigid nature of the H2bptp ligand have crucial effects on the construction of the resulting architectures. These results indicate that this sort of N-heterocyclic ligand can effectively tune the final structural features and maneuver much more complicated and fascinating topologies, as well as further enrich crystal engineering. In addition, complexes 1 and 2 display intense yellow and orange-red luminescence in the solid state which originate from metal-to-ligand charge transfer, revealing the potential for application in light-emitting diode (LED) technology.
