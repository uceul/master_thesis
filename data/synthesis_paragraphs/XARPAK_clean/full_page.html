Synthetic control of coincidental formation of an N-heterocyclic carbene–copper(I) complex and imidazolium cations within metal–organic frameworks†

First published on 15th February 2017

By changing the concentration of copper salt (Cu(ClO4)2), a two-dimensional (2D) copper paddle-wheel-based metal–organic framework (MOF) containing an imidazolium unit (Im-MOF-1 with a framework formula of [CuII(L)(NMP)](ClO4), L = 1,3-bis(4-carboxyphenyl)imidazolium and NMP = N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone) and a 3D MOF (Im-MOF-2, CuII(L)(H2O)) with aqua-bridged Cu ions as inorganic infinite secondary building units were respectively prepared. With CuCl as a copper source, another square-grid 2D MOF (Im-MOF-3, CuII(LNHC–CuICl)(NMP), LNHC = a carbene form of L) was obtained; Im-MOF-3 is isostructural with Im-MOF-1 except that its organic linker has an N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) copper(I) complex (LNHC–CuICl). Density functional theory modeling calculations show that the NHC–CuCl complex in the organic linker of Im-MOF-3 retains the electronic properties of its molecular form, as evidenced by molecular orbital analysis.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are crystalline porous materials assembled with inorganic nodes and organic bridging ligands, providing well-defined pores in terms of sizes, shapes, and chemical environment.1 In addition, a particular functionality can be introduced on frameworks by various post-modification methods, which allows MOFs to exhibit enhanced properties compared with pristine or as-synthesized forms. Besides gas separation and storage applications, this approach to functionalize MOFs has been also adapted actively to develop potential heterogeneous catalysts that need well-defined and precisely located catalytic sites in pores.1 A representative and plausible strategy is to utilize organic linkers containing N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) precursors and their complexation with metal ions.2 Since NHCs are important ligands widely employed in organometallic catalysts, there has been great research interest in the design and synthesis of imidazolium- or NHC-containing MOFs to demonstrate their applications to heterogeneous catalysis,3 as well as gas adsorption.4 Thus far, six types (a to f in Scheme 1) of ligands containing NHC precursors have been used for synthesizing MOFs.
Scheme 1 Ligand classification according to the imidazolium types. Types: a, rigid (COOH);3ab, rigid (py);5c, flexible alkyl centre;3bd, rigid anthracene centre;6e, tethered;7f, fused.8

Two examples illustrate how the NHC precursor ligands are incorporated into MOFs and used to exhibit catalytic functions. When an imidazolium ligand (type a) is reacted with Zn(II) and Cu(I) ions in a reaction mixture, a three-dimensional porous MOF forms with Zn4O clusters as nodes and in situ generated NHCs joined by Cu(I) ions (type a); interestingly, this MOF shows catalytic hydroboration of CO2 although two NHCs are connected with Cu(I).3a Flexible bis-imidazolium linkers (type c) with Zn ions assembled to a metal–organic nanotube (MONT) that can catalyse C–C coupling reactions by the Pd–NHC sites generated through a post-modification reaction between MONTs and Pd(OAc)2.3b

Although there are some examples showing that NHC–metal complexes play a catalytic role in MOFs, it is not straightforward to achieve these results due to two challenging problems. First, MOFs having NHC precursors in their organic linkers may not be so stable during the deprotonation of imidazolium because of added bases. For example, conversion of benzimidazolium (type f) in IRMOF-76 with a MOF-5-type structure into NHC or its Pd complex formation was not successful presumably due to the instability of the framework in basic reaction media.8 The second issue is related to making porous MOFs with imidazolium-containing linkers. That is, after introducing NHC or NHC–metal complexes, MOFs should have a void space sufficient enough to accommodate reactants and diffuse out products. Therefore, it is still necessary to develop methods to make porous MOFs employing unconventional NHC-precursor linkers as demonstrated in controlling framework interpenetration using a biphenyl dicarboxylate-based linker appended by a methyl imidazolium ligand (type e).7

Our research goal is to construct porous MOFs assembled with transition metal ions and NHC ligands, and thus to develop a well-defined heterogeneous catalyst for efficient organic transformations. Here we report three porous MOFs obtained with Cu(I) or Cu(II) salts with a simple imidazolium ligand, type (a) in Scheme 1. One of the MOFs shows selective Cu(I) coordination at the N-heterocyclic carbene centre of two-dimensional metal–organic frameworks.

Experimental section

Syntheses of ligands and MOFs

N,N′-Bis(4-carboxyphenyl)ethylenediimine. Synthesis of [H2L]Cl was performed according to a literature procedure.9 4-Aminobenzoic acid (2.00 g, 14.6 mmol) and 40% in H2O glyoxal solution (1.06 mL, 6.56 mmol) were dissolved in methanol (5.00 mL). The mixture was stirred at ambient temperature for 24 h. The white precipitate was collected by filtration, washed with cold methanol, and dried in a vacuum. Yield: 1.88 g (87.0%). The white solid was used for the next step without further purification.
1,3-Bis(4-carboxyphenyl)imidazolium chloride ([H2L]Cl). N,N′-Bis(4-carboxyphenyl)ethylenediimine (1.50 g, 5.06 mmol) and paraformaldehyde (0.190 g, 6.33 mmol) were dissolved in THF (5.00 ml) followed by addition of 12 M HCl (0.629 ml, 7.59 mmol). The mixture was stirred at room temperature for 24 h. The precipitate was collected on a frit by filtration, washed with Et2O, and dried in a vacuum. Yield: 1.08 g (62.0%). 1H NMR spectrum (400 MHz, DMSO-d6) δ(ppm): 8.02–8.14 (m, 4H), 8.21–8.31 (m, 4H), 8.69 (d, J = 1.76 Hz, 2H), 10.55 (t, J = 1.63 Hz, 1H), 13.47 (br. s., 2H).
[CuII(L)(NMP)](ClO4)·(NMP)(p-xylene)0.5 (Im-MOF-1). Cu(ClO4)2·6H2O (140 mg, 0.378 mmol) and [H2L]Cl (45.0 mg, 0.131 mmol) were placed in a 20 ml vial and dissolved in 5.00 ml of NMP (NMP = N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone) and 10.0 ml of p-xylene. The vial was placed in an oven at 120 °C for 24 h. Green crystals were collected and washed with DMF and THF. Yield: 55.0 mg (67.0%). Elemental analysis (CHN) after the sample was fully dried at 90 °C for one day under dynamic vacuum: calc. for [(C22H23ClCuN3O9)·(C5H9NO)·(H2O)]: C 46.63, H 4.55, N 7.77; found (%): C 46.99, H 4.19, N 7.61.
[CuII(L)(H2O)](ClO4)·(DMF) (Im-MOF-2). Cu(ClO4)2·6H2O (560 mg, 1.51 mmol, 12.0 equiv.) and [H2L]Cl (45.0 mg, 0.131 mmol, 1.00 equiv.) were placed in a 20 ml vial and dissolved in 5.0 ml of NMP and 10.0 ml of xylene. The vial was placed in an oven at 120 °C for 24 h. Light blue crystals were collected and washed with DMF and THF. Yield: 43.5 mg (57.0%). Elemental analysis (CHN): calc. for [(C33H24Cl2Cu2N4O17)·(C5H9NO)2·(H2O)]: C 44.98, H 3.78, N 7.15; found (%): C 44.97, H 3.76, N 7.11. Although elemental analysis of the bulk Im-MOF-2 was satisfactory, we also observed a trace amount of Im-MOF-1 in the PXRD data of Im-MOF-2. For single crystal X-ray analysis, the crystals were kept for solvent exchange (NMP to DMF) in DMF for one week at room temperature.
[CuII(LNHC–CuICl)(NMP)]·(NMP)2·(H2O) (Im-MOF-3). CuCl (102 mg, 1.05 mmol) and [H2L]Cl (120 mg, 0.349 mmol) were placed in a 20 ml vial and dissolved in 10.0 ml of NMP. The vial was placed in an oven at 120 °C for 20 h. Green rectangular crystals were collected and washed with NMP and cyclohexane. Yield: 105 mg (43.0%). Elemental analysis (CHN): calc. for [(C22H21ClCu2N3O5)·(C5H9NO)·(H2O)2]: C 45.99, H 4.86, N 7.95; found (%): C 45.93, H 4.22, N 7.91.

Single crystal X-ray analysis

A crystal of Im-MOF-1 or Im-MOF-2 was picked up with Paratone oil and mounted on a CCD diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71075 Å) at 100 K, using Bruker APEX and SMART and equipped with a graphite crystal incident beam monochromator. The collected data were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects (SAINT),10 and multi-scan absorption corrections based on equivalent reflections were applied (SADABS).11 Crystal structures were elucidated by direct methods and subsequent full-matrix least-squares refinement on F2 using the SHELXTL program package.12 In the case of Im-MOF-2, some of the occluded solvent molecules at inversion centres could not be identified due to diffuse electron densities, which required PLATON SQUEEZE13 treatments at the final stages of refinement. A selected crystal of Im-MOF-3 was mounted using Paratone oil and its intensity data were collected with an ADSC Q210 CCD area detector diffractometer at 100 K using synchrotron radiation (λ = 0.62998 Å) at 2D-SMC with a silicon (111) double crystal monochromator (DCM)14 at Pohang Accelerator Laboratory (PAL). HKL3000sm15 was used for cell refinement and data reduction. All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and hydrogen atoms were added to their ideal positions.

DFT calculations of Im-MOF-3

Two model structures were computed for analysing the electronic structures of Im-MOF-3, mainly focusing on the NHC Cu(I) chloride complex (Scheme S1†). One had Cu(II) paddle-wheel clusters on each end of an NHC–CuCl ligand, and the other was an NHC–CuCl ligand terminated with methyl ester groups. All calculations were carried out with Gaussian 09 suite of programs16 using hybrid density functional methods B3PW91.17 The two model structures were optimized under standard convergence criteria with BS I (BS I is a combined basis set of 6-31G(d,p) and quasirelativistic pseudopotentials), which contains Stuttgart/Dresden (SDD) quasirelativistic pseudopotentials on Cu (18 core electrons) and Cl (10 core electrons) with their associated basis sets augmented by polarization functions (Cu: f, 3.525;18 Cl: d, 0.640 (ref. 19)) and 6-31G(d,p)20 on H, C, N, and O. All basis sets employed Cartesian basis functions (6d, 10f).

Results and discussion

Crystal structures of Im-MOF-1, -2 and -3

The three MOFs (termed Im-MOF-1, -2, and -3) were prepared from solvothermal reactions between [H2L]Cl with Cu(ClO4)2 or CuCl under slightly different reaction conditions as shown in Fig. 1. Their crystal structures were determined using single crystal X-ray diffraction methods. In detail, Im-MOF-1 was produced as green rectangular crystals when three equivalents of Cu(ClO4)2·6H2O were reacted with [H2L]Cl in NMP/p-xylene (1/2 in v/v) at 120 °C for 24 h (Fig. 1). Im-MOF-1 has Cu(II) paddle-wheel clusters as inorganic secondary building units (SBUs) in its two-dimensional (2D) framework with a square-grid topology. Since a fully deprotonated L has a negative charge of −1 (+1 from imidazolium, and −2 from two terminal carboxylate groups), with coordinated NMP molecules, the framework was formulated as [CuII(L)(NMP)]+. In the pore, both perchlorate anion and free guest molecules (1 NMP and 0.5 p-xylene per asymmetric unit) were accommodated, which resulted in the overall formula of Im-MOF-1 as [CuII(L)(NMP)](ClO4)·(NMP)·(p-xylene)0.5 where p-xylene sits on an inversion centre. A measured powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern matched well with the simulated one from the crystal structure, supporting that the bulk crystals were homogeneous.
Fig. 1 Reaction schemes, crystal pictures, and simplified framework structures of Im-MOF-1, -2, and -3, where the lines denote organic linkers; the in situ generated NHC–CuCl linker in Im-MOF-3 is displayed in a dotted box. Both Im-MOF-1 and -3 have Cu2 square paddle-wheels as inorganic SBUs exhibiting square-grid 2D networks. Im-MOF-2 has infinite inorganic SBUs along the channels provided by a three-dimensional framework.

In a monoclinic unit cell with a P21/c space group, the almost flat 2D networks of Im-MOF-1 are lying parallel to the crystallographic (102) plane whose inter-planar angles to (100) and (001) are respectively ca. 35 and 54°. This slanted stacking is due to the coordinated NMP molecules which are protruding into the rectangular void segments of neighbouring frameworks while maintaining van der Waals contacts. Viewed along the a-axis, the stacked layers form straight channels with seemingly rhomboidal openings where free solvent molecules and perchlorate ions are occluded. The rectangular segment has four equilateral edges of 17.578 Å defined by the centres of Cu(II) paddle-wheel clusters (Fig. 2 and S1†). Since the clusters are linked with bent L, where the angle is 142.2° with two carboxylate C atoms and the centroid of the imidazolium ring, the square segment becomes distorted to a parallelogram by a deviation of 9.9° from 90°: the centre-to-centre distances of two opposite paddle-wheels are respectively 22.8324(9) and 26.9115(1) Å. Therefore, a pair of two opposite linkers is bent toward the parallelogram centre while the other pair is directed outwards: the imidazolium centre-to-centre distances are respectively 12.712 and 23.096 Å. The perchlorate anions are located at the corner space of the segment so as to interact effectively with cationic imidazolium rings. The void space of [CuII(L)(NMP)](ClO4), that is, without the free NMP and p-xylene molecules, was calculated to be 37.3% using the PLATON SOLV program.13b


Fig. 2 A square segment in Im-MOF-1 with ClO4− ions (viewed along the a-axis) is illustrated with Cu (orange) and Cl (green) atoms as balls and C (gray), N (blue) and O (red) atoms as sticks. Guest molecules are not shown.

Im-MOF- 2 was obtained as blue elongated rectangular crystals when 12 equivalents of Cu(ClO4)2·6H2O was reacted with [H2L]Cl (Fig. 1). Interestingly, the increase in the amount of metal salt resulted in a three-dimensional (3D) cationic framework with a formula of [CuII(L)(ClO4)]·(DMF)(solvent)x, where the solvent could not be identified with the current X-ray data, and assumed water molecules considering the pore size. In Im-MOF-2, both square planar and octahedral Cu(II) centres are connected through bis-monodentate carboxylates to form linear infinite SBUs along the crystallographic a-axis. These infinite SBUs are located at (y, z) = (0.5, 0), (0, 0.5), (0.5, 1), and (1, 0.5) in a unit cell, and interconnected with L ligands to form straight channels running in the [100] direction. In detail, the rhomboidal opening of the channel is elongated to the b-axis or contracted in the c-axis direction with the same sides of 18.287 Å measured with the Cu(II) centres: the narrow and wide angles of the rhomboidal opening are 49 and 131°, respectively, and the shorter and longer diagonal distances defined by the four Cu(II) vertices are respectively 15.158 and 33.284 Å. The inner angle of the bent ligand L is 142.5° which is the same as that of Im-MOF-1. Similar to Im-MOF-1, perchlorate anions are tightly enclosed by positively-charged imidazolium rings of the linkers at two opposite corners of the channels, causing the openings to be more contracted: d(O6⋯C1) = 2.8367(3) and d(O8⋯N2) = 3.0545(4) Å, less than the sum of van der Waals radii. Free DMF molecules fill the remaining void space and interact with coordinated water molecules via hydrogen bonding as described below. The space of the narrow channels seems to be insufficient to include additional p-xylene, the co-solvent molecule. The void space in empty Im-MOF-2 was calculated to be 30.4%.13b

In the infinite SBUs, two independent Cu(II) ions (Cu1 and Cu2) are alternatively located in the chain with a Cu1⋯Cu2 distance of 3.3431(6) Å, half the a-axis length (Fig. 3). These metal ions are joined by ligand carboxylates; one of the terminal carboxylates is responsible for connecting the two types of Cu ions in a bis-monodentate fashion while the other is not bridging but just bound to Cu2 ions through an O3 atom. Cu1 at an inversion centre (0.5, 0, 0.5) has two trans-located water molecules as ligands with d(Cu1–O1W) = 2.4839(4) Å and two O atoms from different bridging carboxylates: Cu1–O3 = 1.9518(2) Å. At the other inversion centre (0, 0, 0.5), Cu2 has four O atoms, two of which are donated from two bridging carboxylates with d(Cu2–O1) = 1.944 Å and two from monodentate carboxylates with d(Cu2–O3) = 1.952 Å, respectively. The water molecules on Cu1 are engaged in hydrogen bonding with the monodentate carboxylate O atoms, which do not coordinate to Cu2: d(O1W⋯O4) = 2.5600(2) Å (Fig. S2 (c)†). The coordinated water molecule also forms a hydrogen bond with DMF: d(O1W⋯O1S) = 2.6006(3) Å. In addition, the water molecules at Cu2 are weakly bound to the nearest Cu1 atom at the axial positions while the carboxylate O atoms at the axial positions on Cu2 were too distant to form coordination bonds: d(Cu1–O1W) = 2.484 and d(Cu2⋯O3) = 2.8930(4) Å. Therefore, the Cu ions are bridged by the water molecules in the infinite SBU.

(b) A fragment of the infinite SBU showing the coordination environment around two types of Cu ions displayed as large balls. Guest molecules are not shown. Curiously, other Cu(II) salts such as CuCl2 or Cu(NO3)2 did not produce any solid products in the reactions with the ligand. By contrast, a solvothermal reaction between CuCl and [H2L]Cl in NMP yielded Im-MOF-3 as green block crystals. The Im-MOF-3 crystal structure resembles that of Im-MOF-1 in many aspects. Im-MOF-3 exhibits a 2D square-grid framework with also Cu(II) paddle-wheel clusters as inorganic SBUs with similar unit cell parameters and the same space group as those of Im-MOF-1 (Fig. 4 and S4†). However, the imidazolium ring in ligand L reacted with Cu(I) to give an NHC-complex (LNHC–CuICl) which forms a neutral framework with a formula of [CuII(LNHC–CuICl)(NMP)] with axially coordinated NMP. The Cu(I) atom links linearly the C atom (C1) in the NHC ring and Cl with <(C1NHC–Cu–Cl) = 176.4°. The bond lengths, d(Cl–CuI) = 2.106 and d(CuI–CNHC) = 1.886 Å, are comparable to those in other NHC–CuCl complexes.21 In a rectangular segment of the 2D square-grid network, two opposite CuCl units in the linkers were directed to the interior while the others are to other segments.


Fig. 4 A square segment in Im-MOF-3 (viewed along the a-axis) is illustrated with Cu and Cl atoms as balls and C, N and O atoms as sticks. Guest molecules are not shown. Cu (orange), Cl (green), C (gray), N (blue) and O (red).

It was tentatively concluded that Im-MOF-3 could be achieved only through the synthetic route involving aerobic oxidation of Cu(I) in the course of reaction with [H2L]Cl. The measured PXRD pattern of Im-MOF-3 also indicated that the partial oxidation of Cu(I) occurred homogeneously in the reaction mixture (Fig. S6 (c)†). When the same reaction was performed in dry and degassed NMP under an N2 atmosphere, any solid products were not produced. Indeed, other Cu(II) salts (Cu(ClO4)2, CuCl2 or Cu(NO3)2) did not give any crystalline materials with the [H2L]Cl ligand either. In fact, it is known that a similar MOF also has both Cu(I) NHC complexes and Cu(II) paddle-wheels.22 However, the MOF was constructed from Cu(II) ions, some of which were reduced to Cu(I) to give a Cu(I) NHC chloride complex, which is opposite to our case.

The 2D frameworks in Im-MOF-3 are also located parallel to the crystallographic (102) plane whose inter-planar angles to (100) and (001) are respectively 36.04 and 47.27°. Neighbouring layers interact through the coordinated and NMP molecules, as in Im-MOF-1. The pores in Im-MOF-3 contain NMP and H2O molecules. The water molecules are engaged in hydrogen bonding with NMP and Cl respectively with interatomic distances of d(O1W⋯O3S) = 2.8549(4) and d(O1W⋯Cl) = 3.4860(1) Å (Fig. S4 (a)†). Without the occluded solvent molecules, the void space provided by the neutral Im-MOF-3 was calculated to be 43.5% which is greater than that (37.3%) of Im-MOF-1.13b

Framework stability and gas sorption behaviors

Despite the similarity in framework topology, Im-MOF-3 had a less stable framework than Im-MOF-1. Im-MOF-1 was stable in air and many organic solvents including THF, MC, and MeOH as evidenced by the PXRD patterns while Im-MOF-3 was stable only in dry organic solvents, which is ascribed to the presence of the reactive Cu(I) NHC complexes. In addition, Im-MOF-3 decomposed at lower temperature than Im-MOF-1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) indicated that the occluded guest molecules in Im-MOF-1 could be removed up to 100 °C without framework decomposition (Fig. 5(a)). The variable temperature PXRD (VT-PXRD) patterns also showed that the Im-MOF-1 sample heated up to 100 °C showed a PXRD pattern almost the same as that of the as-prepared one (Fig. 5(b)). In contrast, Im-MOF-3 at 100 °C produced no noticeable peaks (Fig. 5(d)). For comparison, the VT-PXRD patterns of Im-MOF-2 measurements showed strong Bragg peaks even at 150 °C for Im-MOF-2 which has infinite SBUs and a 3D framework (Fig. 5(c)).
Fig. 5 (a) TGA curves measured under an N2 atmosphere at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1, and VT-PXRD patterns for (b) Im-MOF-1, (c) Im-MOF-2, and (d) Im-MOF-3.

Although the MOFs in this work showed marginal thermal stability, their permanent porosity was not secured under vacuum. A supercritical CO2 treatment was applied for vulnerable Im-MOF-3 to remove guest molecules and then N2 and CO2 gas adsorption studies were carried out up to 1 bar. The N2 adsorption/desorption isotherms measured at 77 K for Im-MOF-1 and -3 followed Type III curves, indicating that the frameworks do not maintain well-defined micropores under reduced pressure (Fig. S7(a) and S9(a)†): the adsorbed amounts of nitrogen were 47 cm3 g−1 at P/P0 = 0.96 and 250 cm3 g−1 at P/P0 = 0.99, respectively, for Im-MOF-1 and -3. The estimated Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas employing CO2 as a probe gas at 195 K were 124 and 79 m2 g−1 for Im-MOF-1 and Im-MOF-3, respectively (Fig. S7(c) and S9(c)†). However, these small surface areas are much lower considering the relatively large solvent-accessible void spaces near 40% predicted by the PLATON SOLV calculations. Im-MOF-2 also showed the same porosity properties as those of the 2D frameworks.

Electronic structures of Im-MOF-3

Since Cu(I) NHC molecular complexes can be efficient catalysts in various organic reactions,23 it was anticipated that Im-MOF-3 would exhibit a similar catalytic function due to the LNHC–CuCl units supported on the extended networks. In order to understand the electronic structure of the potential catalytic site in Im-MOF-3, density functional theory (DFT) calculations (B3PW91/6-31G(d,p)) were carried out with two model complexes designated as A and B. A was a methyl ester form of LNHC–CuCl and B was a fragment of Im-MOF-3, where LNHC–CuCl was coordinated to Cu(II) paddle-wheels (Scheme S1†). The DFT-calculated bond distances and angles of the model structures matched well with those of the crystal structure (Table S4†). Moreover, the molecular orbitals including the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the model complex A were retained in B. In B, HOMO and HOMO-1 were attributed to the Cu(II) paddle-wheels (Fig. 6 and S10†). However, the next five molecular orbitals, from HOMO-2 to HOMO-6 of B, were localized in the NHC–CuCl unit, specifically around the Cu–Cl moiety. These five molecular orbitals were almost identical to the HOMO to HOMO-4 of A, indicating that the Cu(I) NHC complex would play the same catalytic role even in the pores of Im-MOF-3.
Fig. 6 DFT computed frontier molecular orbitals of NHC–Cu(I)–Cl in two model complexes (A and B) of Im-MOF-3 after optimization at B3PW91/6-31G(d,p). The model complex A has methyl ester terminals and the model complex B has paddle-wheel terminals (see the ESI†).

In accordance with the calculation results, a preliminary experiment on borylation of 4-bromophenyl with Im-MOF-3 produced borylated biphenyl in 34% yield, implying that Im-MOF-3 facilitated the reaction.24 By contrast, Im-MOF-1 or CuCl did not afford the product. This observation suggests that Im-MOF-3 could have exerted a catalytic function in the reaction. However, since Im-MOF-3 crystals decomposed in the course of the reaction, it cannot be ruled out that some decomposed chemical species might be involved with the borylation reactions.

Conclusions

In this work, three Cu-MOFs could be prepared by adjusting the reaction conditions: two isostructural and two-dimensional MOFs (Im-MOF-1 and Im-MOF-3), and a three-dimensional MOF (Im-MOF-2). The in situ generation of the NHC–CuCl complex inside a Cu(II) paddle-wheel-based MOF (Im-MOF-3) was achieved by the reaction of CuCl and the imidazolium ligand as an NHC precursor. With different metal salts, an isostructural MOF (Im-MOF-1) containing an imidazolium unit could be obtained. DFT calculations on the frontier molecular orbitals of the NHC–CuCl unit could be preserved in Im-MOF-3.

We conducted a typical borylation reaction under a nitrogen atmosphere. 4-Bromobiphenyl (9.45 mg, 0.041 mmol), B2Pin2 (15.4 mg, 0.061 mmol) and NaOtBu (7.80 mg, 0.082 mmol) were suspended in 0.50 mL of THF in a 4 mL glass vial. Subsequently, 30 mol% of Im-MOF-3 was added to the reaction mixture which was stirred at room temperature for 48 h before filtration through a pad of Celite. A borylated biphenyl product was obtained in 34% yield, as confirmed by GC-MS analysis. For comparison, Im-MOF-1 was used for the borylation reaction under the same conditions, which resulted in a GC yield of only 1.2%. As a control experiment, when CuCl was used, the reaction did not afford the desired product. The borylation reaction requires a strong base such as NaOtBu25 which unfortunately destroyed the coordination networks and did not allow recycling tests.
