Highly selective sorption of CO2 and N2O and strong gas-framework interactions in a nickel(II) organic material†

Xiaoping Zhang , Wenjie Chen , Wei Shi * and Peng Cheng *
Department of Chemistry, Key Laboratory of Advanced Energy Materials Chemistry (MOE), State Key Laboratory of Elemento-Organic Chemistry, Collaborative Innovation Center of Chemical Science and Engineering, Nankai University, Tianjin 300071, P. R. China. E-mail: shiwei@nankai.edu.cn; pcheng@nankai.edu.cn; Fax: +86-22-23502458

Received 1st August 2016 , Accepted 19th September 2016

First published on 19th September 2016

A new robust microporous Ni-MOF with cylindrical channels based on an interesting 41 helix structure was fabricated by the reaction of biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic acid and nickel (II) acetate via a solvothermal method. Activated Ni-MOF exhibits excellent CO2 and N2O sorption capabilities as well as high separations of CO2 and N2O over N2/O2/CH4/H2/Ar/He at 273 K, 298 K and 313 K. Moreover, the framework shows high isosteric heats of adsorption of three main greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4 and N2O).

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted considerable research interest in the field of materials science. As an emerging class of inorganic–organic hybrid porous materials,1,2 MOFs not only have intriguing structures but also have potential applications such as in gas storage and separation, luminescence, sensing, ion-exchange and catalysis.3–10 Carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O) have been recognized as the three main greenhouse gases. The selective capture of CO2 from flue gas and natural gas has become the most urgent issue driven by the concerns of climate change.11 The adsorption of CO2 and CH4 have been well studied in MOFs recently, whereas the study of N2O adsorption in MOFs is still rarely documented. The concentration of N2O in the atmosphere increases steadily at a rate of about 0.2% per year, which not only causes the global warming effect but also contributes to the stratospheric ozone depletion.12,13 Removal of CO2, CH4, and N2O from air is an important separation process in energy production and environmental protection.14 The process to inhibit the formation or emission of the powerful greenhouse gas nitrous oxide (N2O) from soil has already been studied;15 however, the removal of N2O from air has been paid less attention. Moreover, although the applications of MOFs in gas storage and separation have been well studied recently, quantitative adsorption study of all the three major greenhouse gases has not been reported.

The development of high-performance MOFs with versatile sorption capacities still represents one of the greatest challenges for chemists. Their crystalline nature, high porosity, uniform pore size, extraordinary surface area and finely tunable pore surfaces lead to potential industrial applications for further study.16 Organic aromatic polycarboxylates have been extensively employed in the preparation of MOFs, because carboxyl groups can assume many types of bridging or chelating modes, beneficial for the variety of MOFs.17–23 Among this type of ligand, biphenyl-based bi-, tri-, and tetracarboxylates are particularly interesting, as different coordination conformations can be afforded depending on the dihedral angle between the free rotation of the phenyl rings.24–28 In this context, our interest was given to biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic (H4bptc) acid-based MOFs, as tetra-anionic ligands can increase the metal-to-ligand ratio, and the coordination chemistry of isophthalic acid groups is well known to build functional building blocks for a high density of active metal sites and gas uptake capacity.29 Recently, MOFs with H4bptc have attracted increasing interest.30–37 As a continuous study on the gas adsorption and separation of MOFs,38–43 particularly the paucity of quantitative research on all three main greenhouse gases in the same material, herein, we report a new MOF {[Ni(bptc)0.5(H2O)]·2H2O·DMF}n (Ni-MOF) with nanoscale cylindrical channels, which exhibits excellent performances in the CO2, CH4 and N2O storage at low pressure and highly selective sorption of CO2 and N2O over N2/O2/CH4/H2/Ar/He at 273 and 298 K.

Experimental section

General methods and materials

All the reagents and solvents were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were performed on a D/Max-2500. PXRD were performed on a Rigaku Ultima IV instrument with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å), with a scan speed of 10° min−1 in the range 2θ = 3–50°. FT-IR spectra were obtained with a Bruker Tensor 27 spectrometer on KBr disks. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a Delta Series TA-SDTQ600 analyzer in a nitrogen atmosphere from room temperature to 800 °C (10 °C min−1) using aluminum crucibles.

Synthesis of {[Ni(bptc)H2O]·2H2O·DMF}n (Ni-MOF)

A mixture of H4bptc (0.016 g, 0.05 mmol), Ni(CH3COO)2·4H2O (0.037 g, 0.15 mmol), LiOH·H2O (0.004 g, 0.1 mmol), HNO3 (100 μL, 1.6 mmol), 2.5 mL DMF and 1.5 mL water was placed into a 25 mL Teflon-lined Parr, heated to 140 °C for 3 days, and then cooled to room temperature for 2 days. Green strip crystals of Ni-MOF were obtained in 46% yield (based on Ni). Elemental analysis (%) found (calcd): C 37.70 (37.86), H 6.06 (5.62), N 4.6 (4.31). IR (KBr, cm−1): 3448 br, 1610 s, 1522 s, 1419 s, 1367 s, 790 m, 725 m, 661 m, 464 m.

X-ray crystallography

Single crystal X-ray diffraction measurements of Ni-MOF were recorded on an Oxford Supernova diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 129 K. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2 using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.44 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions or located in the difference Fourier map. The formula was identified by combining the single crystal structure, elemental and thermogravimetric analyses; the free electrons were assigned to be from one DMF and two H2O molecules. The crystallographic data and structural refinements are summarized in Table S1.†

Low-pressure sorption experiments

All gas sorption experiments were performed on a Quantachrome Autosorb-IQ2 automatic volumetric instrument. All gases used are of 99.999% purity. The CO2, N2O, N2, O2, CH4, H2, Ar and He sorption isotherms at 273, 298 and 313 K were measured in a temperature-controlled water bath in the pressure range from 10−3 to 1 bar. Gas sorption (CO2) isotherms at 195 K were measured with a slush bath of dry ice–acetone. Typically, about 200 mg of the as-synthesized sample was vigorously refluxed in a small amount of methanol for 3 days, and CH3OH was refreshed every day. The solid was collected by centrifugation, loaded into the sample cell, and then heated to 50 °C under dynamic vacuum for 12 hours before gas sorption measurements were made. Selectivity abilities of Ni-MOF towards different gases were based on the adsorption data of each single gas by IAST method.

Results and discussion

Ni-MOF was synthesized by the reaction of H4bptc with nickel acetate via a solvothermal method at 140 °C for 72 hours. The reaction generated essentially a quantitative yield to give a high quality crystalline product (Fig. S1†) that crystallized in the tetragonal space group I4122. The asymmetric unit consists of half a Ni(II) ion, half a μ2-O anion, and a quarter of the bptc4− (Fig. S2†). As shown in Fig. 1, each Ni(II) ion is six-coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four unique bptc4− ligands and two oxygen atoms from two water molecules to form a distorted octahedral geometry with Ni–O bond lengths in the range of 2.011(5)–2.093(6) Å. Oxygen atoms and bptc4− anions bridging Ni(II) ions formed a rare helical 41 chain with composition of {Ni2(μ2-H2O)2(COO)4}, which cooperated with carboxylate ligands to generate a microporous framework with cylindrical channels occupied by H2O and DMF molecules (Fig. S3 and S4†). The chains of corner-sharing NiO6 octahedra along the c axis are linked by carboxylate ligands to form a 1D cylindrical channel with diameter of 1.0 nm. The calculated free void is 1396.7 Å3 per unit and the free volume in fully desolvated Ni-MOF is 50.2% calculated by PLATON when the free water and DMF molecules are removed.45

TGA and XRD analysis

Based on EA and TGA (Fig. S5†), there should be two H2O and one DMF per Ni(II) ion inside the channel. The framework releases the guest DMF and H2O molecules gradually in the temperature range from 25 to ca. 400 °C without an obvious plateau. The phase purities of the bulk material, the CH3OH-exchanged samples and the crystals after gas sorption study were confirmed by PXRD (Fig. S6†). The results indicated that the framework was retained after removal of the free DMF and H2O molecules.

Gas adsorption and separation

Surface area and pore size distribution. An activated Ni-MOF sample was obtained by heating the methanol-exchanged Ni-MOF at 50 °C for 12 hours under ultrahigh vacuum (10−7 bar). The N2 adsorption is very low at 77 K (Fig. S7†). The highest adsorption capacity at P/P0 = 1.0 is 59.2 cm3 (STP) per g, which cannot reveal the true surface area or pore volume.46 Fortunately, CO2 adsorption at 195 K exhibits a type I isotherm with a steep uptake at low-pressure regions (Fig. S8†). The adsorption capacity at P/P0 = 1.0 is 125.8 cm3 (STP) per g (24.7 wt%) at 195 K, corresponding to 2.7 CO2 molecules per bptc4− ligand. The Langmuir and Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas calculated from the CO2 adsorption data (195 K) are 529.7 and 447.4 m2 g−1, respectively. Based on the adsorption isotherm of CO2 at 273 K and the NLDFT (non-local density functional theory) method, the pore volume was calculated as 0.094 cm3 g−1 and the pore diameter distribution is centered around 5.7 Å (Fig. S9†),47 which is consistent with the result calculated from the single crystal structure analysis if the van der Waals radius of the atoms in the channel walls are considered.48
CO2/N2 and CO2/CH4 separation. The adsorption capacity of CO2, N2, O2, CH4, H2, Ar and He was measured at 273, 298 and 313 K (Fig. 2a–c, respectively). The adsorption isotherms of CO2 show a gradual increase and reach the maximal amounts of 88.4 cm3 (STP) per g (17.4 wt%) at 273 K, 72.5 cm3 (STP) per g (14.2 wt%) at 298 K and 59.8 cm3 (STP) per g (11.8 wt%) at 313 K. It exhibits a significantly higher capacity for adsorption of CO2 compared to all the other gases tested under the same conditions. The uptake at room temperature is much higher than that for ZIF-8 (4.3 wt%), MOF-5 (8.5 wt%), MIL-53(Al) (10.6 wt%) and NOTT-140a (11.7 wt%) but still below the values of the MOF-74 series (19.8–27.5 wt%) and HKUST-1 (27 wt%), which have strong free metal sites.49–56 Notably, the excellent CO2 loading of 59.8 cm3 (STP) per g at 313 K (1 bar) is among the best values of CO2 uptake in MOFs.57–60 In contrast, the adsorption capacities of N2, O2, CH4, H2, Ar and He are extremely poor. The selectivity of CO2 adsorption from binary gas mixture can be evaluated by the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST), which is well recognized and applied to predict gas mixture separation behavior in porous materials.61,62 Based on the single gas adsorption isotherms and the IAST model, the selectivity of CO2 over N2 (15/85) for Ni-MOF (Fig. 3a and S10†) is 161 at 273 K, 57 at 298 K, and 76 at 313 K, which shows more outstanding selectivities than most of the reported MOFs under the same conditions even if the MOFs contain different multiple functional groups (Fig. 4a).63–72 In general, selectivity will decrease as the temperature is increased. However, it is noted that the CO2/N2 selectivity at 313 K is higher than that at 298 K, which may be attributed to a larger percentage reduction for N2 uptake than that for CO2 when the temperature is increased. The selectivities at 273 K, 298 K and 313 K show a slightly increasing tendency along with increasing pressure, which means that the cylindrical pore can enhance CO2 affinity more than that of N2 towards the pore surface. CO2 over CH4 (50/50) selectivities are 123 (273 K), 33 (298 K) and 14 (313 K), which are also significantly higher than most reported MOFs without or with functional groups (Fig. 3c and 4a).63–72 The selectivity at 273 K shows a significant increase, ranging from 32 at zero loading to 123 at 1 bar with the increasing pressure, whereas at 298 K, the selectivity has a slight increase and remains steady at 313 K. The high values of CO2 selectivity over CH4 could be rationalized by the strong interactions and the smaller radius of CO2. Several factors can affect the selectivity of CO2/N2 and CO2/CH4. First, the diameter of CO2 (3.3 Å) is smaller than N2 (3.64 Å) and CH4 (3.8 Å). Second, CO2 is a polar molecule, which may have stronger interactions with the channel of the framework. In addition, the 1H NMR spectrum showed that about a quarter of DMF molecule was residual in the asymmetric unit of the activated framework (Fig. S11†); the existing polar molecules (DMF) may also have strong interactions with CO2 and thus benefit the selectivity. Though the low activated temperature (50 °C under vacuum) cannot remove all the guest DMF molecules, increasing the activated temperature leads to a reduced sorption amount of CO2 and signified that the existence of guest DMF molecules also has a positive effect on CO2 capture. The high selectivity of Ni-MOF indicated that it could be an excellent candidate for the selective sorption of CO2 from natural gas. As the framework exhibits high, but nearly equivalent, sorption of both CO2 and N2O, it shows low selectivity of CO2/N2O (Fig. S12†). It may be ascribed to the fact that CO2 and N2O have similar structures and electronic formulae; moreover, the radius of N, O, and C is all close to each other, thus leading to similar interactions with the framework.

CO2 over O2/H2/Ar/He separation. There has been a lot of research on CO2 over N2 or CH4 separation, whereas the separation of CO2 over O2/H2/Ar/He, CH4 over N2/O2 or N2/O2 is rarely studied.73,74 Calculated by IAST, the selectivities for CO2/O2 (50/50) are 6981 (273 K) and 65 (298 K) at 1 bar (Fig. 3b). At 273 K, the selectivity presents a steady and fast increase with increasing pressure, which may also be attributed to the strong interactions between the polar CO2 molecules and the cavity surface. Compared with 273 K, the selectivity shows a rapid reduction when the temperature increases to 298 K. Along with the increase in the temperature, thermal shock of the CO2 molecules increases, which reduces the interactions between the gas molecules and the framework. For the other tested gases, all of them have quite weak interactions with the framework; therefore, they were affected less notably with temperature, thus leading to higher selectivity at 273 K. H2, Ar and He gases, at all the tested temperatures, show nearly zero uptake and hence achieve good separation. The sorption selectivities of CH4 over N2 (50/50) and O2 (50/50) are in the range of 2–16 at measured temperatures, whereas N2/O2 (50/50) separation ability is comparatively weak (Fig. 3g–i, respectively). Such selective sorption properties originate from the quite weak interactions of the framework with N2, O2, H2, Ar and He molecules due to their non-polar nature, as well as the thermodynamic equilibrium effect.75
CO2 and CH4 adsorption enthalpies. The coverage-dependent adsorption enthalpies (Qst) for CO2 and CH4 were calculated using the viral equation.76 The Qst at zero surface coverage for CO2 is 30.6 kJ mol−1 based on the adsorption data collected at 273, 298 and 313 K (Fig. 5 and S13†), which is comparable to many well-known MOFs and indicates the strong interaction strength of CO2 molecules with the framework (Fig. 4b).77–82 CH4 also shows a strong interaction strength with the framework with a Qst value of 22.3 kJ mol−1 based on the adsorption data at 273 and 298 K, which is higher than most adsorption enthalpies of CH4 on porous materials with or without polarizing functional groups or open metal sites (Fig. 4b and 5).77–82 Notably, in this crystal framework, there are no strong adsorption sites such as open metal sites or functional groups. The strong adsorption enthalpies for CO2 and CH4 may be ascribed to their interaction with the window sites, the inner surface and the shape of the hole. There are windows at about 4.12 Å × 6.53 Å in both a and b directions (Fig. S4†); the sizes of the windows fit the CO2 and CH4 molecules well; therefore, they may also have interactions with CO2 and CH4. Moreover, this high heat of adsorption was largely responsible for the high volumetric uptake of CO2 and CH4 gas molecules. The steady increase in the isosteric heat of adsorption at lower pressures may be due to the packing of guest CO2 or CH4 molecules and the narrow distribution of pore sizes; thus, the smaller pores may have stronger interactions with the packing of gas molecules than larger pores.83

N2O adsorption and separation. N2O adsorption has aroused considerable attention recently; however, the quantitative research is still rare. Ni-MOF performs high N2O sorption behavior with the uptakes of 77 cm3 (STP) per g (15.1 wt%) at 273 K, 63 cm3 (STP) per g (12.4 wt%) at 298 K and 45 cm3 (STP) per g (12.4 wt%) at 313 K (Fig. 2d). Similar to CO2 uptake, the N2O adsorption shows a steady increase with no saturation until the pressure is increased to 1 bar. The high volumetric of N2O uptake is slightly lower than the uptake of zeolite 5 Å and much higher than other reported MOF materials.14 The zero-coverage adsorption enthalpy of N2O is 26.6 kJ mol−1 based on the viral equation, which indicates the strong interaction between N2O and the framework (Fig. 5 and S13†). Similar to CO2 and CH4, the Qst of N2O also exhibits a steady increase along with the increase of the pressure, which may be caused by the close packing of N2O gas molecules with the inner surface of the channel and their interactions with the polar molecules in the hole of the framework. The N2O/N2, N2O/O2 and N2O/CH4 selectivities are 129 (53), 2046 (59) and 62 (28) at 273 K/1 bar (298 K/1 bar) (Fig. 3d–f and S14†), respectively. Compared with CO2 separation, the N2O selectivity exhibits a similar trend. The high separation may be attributed to the polarity of N2O and the smaller kinetic diameter than other non-polar molecules. These results suggest Ni-MOF is a fairly good candidate for N2O capture from the atmosphere because of the highly selective sorption ability of N2O from other gases even at low pressure.

Conclusions

In summary, we successfully synthesized a new Ni-MOF containing 1D cylindrical channels based on an interesting 41 helix structure. The activated Ni-MOF exhibits both high uptake capacity of CO2 and N2O and high adsorption selectivity of CO2 and N2O over N2, O2, CH4, H2, Ar and He at 273 K, 298 K and 313 K. The result illustrated the potentials of Ni-MOF in not only greenhouse gas capture but also selective gas separation and purification. Further studies will focus on the conversion of CO2 and N2O into other valuable chemicals within the channels of such types of MOF.
