Abstract

Two organically templated beryllium phosphite and phosphate, [H2tmpda][Be3(HPO3)4] (1) and [H2ampy][Be3(HPO4)4] (2) [tmpda = N,N,N′,N′‐tetramethyl‐1,3‐propanediamine, ampy = 3‐(aminomethyl)pyridine], were synthesized and structurally characterized. Compound 1 exhibits a (3,4)‐connected layered structure with eight‐ring windows, and it has a 44 grid topology by regarding the heptameric [Be3(HPO3)4] group as a 4‐connected node in the layer. Compound 2 possesses a different (3,4)‐connected three‐dimensional framework with multidirectional 8‐ring channels and zigzag 12‐ring channels. This 3D architecture shows a pcu topology when the heptameric [Be3(HPO4)4] groups are regarded as 6‐connected secondary building units. Crystal data: 1, monoclinic, P21/m, a = 8.0217(6) Å, b = 13.7969(5) Å, c = 8.6581(2) Å, β = 92.315(3)°, V = 957.45(8) Å3, Z = 2, R1 = 0.0761, wR2 = 0.1640; 2, monoclinic, P21/n, a = 7.5292(5) Å, b = 14.0999(8) Å, c = 16.5910(3) Å, β = 95.740(3)°, V = 1752.49(16) Å3, Z = 4, R1 = 0.0434, wR2 = 0.1017.

Introduction

Microporous materials have attracted increasing research interest due to their rich structural chemistry and widespread applications in catalysis, absorption, ion‐exchange, and separation, etc.1–6 Since the pioneering work reporting the crystalline aluminophosphate molecular sieves in 1982,7 considerable progress has been achieved in the exploitation of new non‐aluminosilicate‐based framework materials. Of these, metal phosphates have evidenced extraordinary expansion in terms of the structural and compositional diversities.8 A very wide range of transition metals and main group elements have been incorporated into the frameworks of these materials, in which some typical examples include Zn3(PO4)2(PO3OH)(H2DACH)·2H2O (denoted as ND‐1) with extra‐large 24‐ring channels,9 H3N(CH2)6NH3·Zn4(PO4)2·Zn4(HPO4)2·3H2O and [H2CHBMA][Zn2.5(PO4)(HPO3)2]·H2O (denoted as TJPU‐3) with 20‐ring channels,10,11 as well as several cases with helical channels or chiral frameworks.12–14 Using pseudo‐pyramidal phosphite [HPO32–] groups to substitute tetrahedral [PO43–] units in metal phosphate architectures recently has resulted in a new class of metal phosphites with great success. Compared with 4‐connected phosphate group, the presence of 3‐connected phosphite group can reduce the M–O–P connectivity and produce more “open” interrupted architectures. Some eye‐catching metal phosphites with large 18‐, 20‐, 24‐, 26‐, 28‐, 40‐, 56‐, 64‐ and 72‐ring channels have been successively synthesized and structurally characterized in this family.15–22

From the viewpoint of structural chemistry, the beryllium element is also an ideal structural building block for open‐framework structures because it is always tetrahedrally coordinated to oxygen atoms and its radius (0.27 Å) is similar to that of Si4+ (0.26 Å). Therefore, it is expected that a series of new Be‐containing open‐framework compounds with novel architectures would be realized via amine‐template‐directed routes under hydrothermal or solvothermal conditions. However, only a very limited number of organically templated Be‐P‐O solids have been reported. [H3N(CH2)2NH3]0.5[BePO4], for example, is the first organically templated beryllium phosphate with gismondine (GIS) zeolite topology,23 BePO4‐GME, BePO4‐GIS, and BePO4‐ABW are three large single‐crystals obtained from concentrated amine templates conditions,24 CBP‐CHA is the first molecular sieves with CoII ions encapsulated in channels,25 [H3N(CH2)3NH3][Be3(HPO4)4] and [C6N2H13][Be2(H2O)(PO4)(HPO4)]·H2O show interrupted 3D open‐framework structures,26,27 and Be2(Hea)(PO4)(HPO4) has a neutral inorganic‐organic hybrid framework.28 In the specific field of beryllophosphites, [H3N(CH2)3NH3]·Be3(HPO3)4 represents the first organically templated beryllium phosphite with 12‐ring channels,29 whereas BeHPO‐1 and SCU‐24 possess large 16‐ring and 24‐ring channels, respectively.30,31

With an aim toward synthesize interrupted open‐frameworks based on BeO4 tetrahedra and 3‐connected building units such as [HPO42–] and [HPO32–] groups we conducted our study on the hydrothermal synthesis in the beryllium phosphate and phosphite systems. Two new beryllium‐containing compounds, [H2tmpda][Be3(HPO3)4] (1) and [H2ampy][Be3(HPO4)4] (2) [tmpda = N,N,N′,N′‐tetramethyl‐1,3‐propanediamine and ampy = 3‐(aminomethyl)pyridine] were prepared in the presence of different organic templates. Both compounds exhibit interrupted (3,4)‐connected frameworks with varying dimensionality: 2D beryllium phosphite layer in 1, and 3D beryllium phosphate architecture in 2.

Results and Discussion

Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy

The IR spectra of compounds 1 and 2 were similar and showed typical peaks, with the exception of the characteristic band at 2366 cm–1 observed in 1, which is attributed to the stretching vibration of the P–H bonds in phosphite anions (Figure 1). The intense bands at 1116, 1078, 1039 cm–1 for 1, or 1135 and 1069 cm–1 for 2 are associated with stretching vibrations of P–O bonds, whereas the bands at 445–622 cm–1 for 1 and 461–616 cm–1 for 2 are associated with the bending vibrations of P–O bonds. Absorption at 782 and 713 cm–1 for 1 and 781 and 714 cm–1 for 2 can be attributed to Be–O vibration. The stretching vibration bands of –NH2 and –CH2 groups are observed at about 3123–2951 cm–1, and the bending bands of –NH2 and –CH2 are present at about 1431–1632 cm–1. In addition, the stretching vibration of the O–H bond is also observed at about 3438cm–1 for 1 and 3452 cm–1 for 2, respectively.

Crystal Structures

Single‐crystal structural analysis reveals that the asymmetric unit of compound 1 contains 17 non‐hydrogen atoms, 12 of which belong to the “host framework” (two Be, three P, and seven O atoms) and the remaining five from the guest species (Figure 2a). Atoms Be(1) and P(1) are located in the general positions, whereas Be(2), P(2), and P(3) atoms each occupy a special position with a site occupancy of 0.5, respectively. Both beryllium atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms, and each shares four oxygen atoms with adjacent phosphorus atoms. Thus, there are no terminal Be–O groups and Be–O–Be bonding. The Be–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.583(9) Å‐1.635(7) Å (av. 1.603 Å) and the O–Be–O angles are between 104.3(4)° and 113.7(4)° (av. 109.6°), in agreement with those of other open‐framework beryllium phosphites. The phosphorus atoms each makes three P–O–Be linkages with adjacent beryllium atoms, with the fourth vertex occupied by a terminal hydrogen atom. The P–O bond lengths vary from 1.476(4) to 1.512(3) Å (av. 1.494 Å), and the O–P–O angles span from 109.9(2) to 114.2(2)° (av. 112.9°). Assuming the usual valence of Be, P, O, and H to be +2, +3, –2, and +1 respectively, the composition of [Be3(HPO3)4] creates a net charge of –2, which is balanced by one diprotonated N,N,N′,N′‐tetramethyl‐1,3‐propanediamine cation per formula unit.

The inorganic framework of 1 is built up from strictly alternating BeO4 tetrahedra and HPO3 pseudopyramids through their vertices, giving rise to a (3,4)‐connected two‐dimensional architecture. Interestingly, the [Be3(HPO3)4] group presenting as a cyclic six‐membered ring constituted by the alternative connections of three BeO4 and three HPO3 with the one additional HPO3 group capping above the center of the 6‐MR (denoted as 6*1, Figure 2b) can act as the secondary building unit in the construction of the layer structure. As shown in Figure 2c, each 6*1 building block is linked to four neighboring units by sharing vertex atoms [i.e. O(1) and O(6)], generating a two‐dimensional architecture with eight‐ring apertures. Thus, the layer structure exhibits a 44 grid topology, if each [Be3(HPO3)4] group is regarded as a 4‐connected node. The 8‐ring window, composed of 4BeO4 tetrahedra and 4HPO3 pseudo pyramids, has a pore size of ca. 6.6 × 7.0 Å (calculated from the oxygen‐to‐oxygen distance across the window). It should be noted that this kind of layered structures were also observed in zinc phosphites, such as H2tmdp·Zn3(HPO3)4 (tmdp = 4,4'‐trimethylenedipiperidine)32 and (H2bpp)2·Zn6(HPO3)8 [bpp = 1,3‐bis(4‐pyridyl)propane].33 One main difference between the three compounds is that, in compound 1, the adjacent inorganic layers are stacked in an –AAAA– sequence (Figure 2d), whereas distinct –ABAB– and –ABCD– stacking modes have been observed in H2tmdp·Zn3(HPO3)4 and (H2bpp)2·Zn6(HPO3)8, respectively. The doubly protonated N,N,N′,N′‐tetramethyl‐1,3‐propanediamine cations are well ordered in the interlayer regions, compensating the negative charges and simultaneously interacting with the inorganic framework through N–H···O and C–H···O types hydrogen bonds. A void space analysis using the program PLATON indicates that these extra‐framework organic cations occupy 51.6 % of the unit cell volume.34

The asymmetric unit of 2 contains 31 non‐hydrogen atoms, of which three beryllium atoms and four phosphorus atoms are crystallographically independent (Figure 3a). All the beryllium atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms with Be–O bond lengths in the range of 1.576(4)–1.663(4) Å (av. 1.628 Å) and O–Be–O angles lying between 102.4(2)° and 117.4(2)° (av. 109.5°). Each Be atom is linked to four neighboring phosphorus atoms through Be–O–P linkages, with an average bond angle of 142.1°. The phosphorus atoms, however, each forms three P–O–Be linkages with adjacent beryllium atoms and possesses one P–O terminal bond. The P–O distances are in the range of 1.494(2)–1.589(2) Å (av. 1.534 Å), and the O–P–O bond angles are in the range of 104.8(2)–113.7(2)° (av. 109.4°). These geometrical parameters are in good agreement with those observed earlier. Bond valence sum values35 indicate that all the terminal P(1)–O(2), P(2)–O(8), P(3)–O(11), and P(4)–O(16) linkages with distances of 1.579(2), 1.577(2), 1.589(2), and 1.584(2) Å are terminal P–OH bonds, and this assignment also corresponds well with the proton positions observed near the oxygen atoms in the difference Fourier maps. Thus, considering the usual valence of Be, P, O, and H to be +2, +3, –2, and +1 respectively, the composition of [Be3(HPO4)4] creates a net charge of –2, which should be compensated by one diprotonated 3‐(aminomethyl)pyridine cation.

The framework structure of 2 is made up of strictly alternating BeO4 and HPO4 tetrahedral units, linked through their vertexes forming a (3,4)‐connected three‐dimensional network. The 3D architecture can also be conceptually built up from a heptameric [Be3(HPO4)4] group as the secondary building unit (SBU), as shown in Figure 3b. Similar to the heptameric [Be3(HPO3)4] building block in 1, it is also featured as a cyclic six‐membered ring containing three BeO4 and three PO3(OH) groups with one additional PO3(OH) group. However, this exocyclic PO3(OH) group in SBU of 2 only bridges two beryllium atoms of the cyclic six‐membered ring, distinct from the exocyclic HPO3 unit in SBU of 1 simultaneously bridging three beryllium atoms and capping above the center of the 6‐MR. Therefore, only six bridging oxygen sites exist in each [Be3(HPO3)4] building block in 1, while there are eight bridging oxygen sites (i.e. two O(4), two O(7), two O(10) and two O(15) atoms) in each [Be3(HPO4)4] building block in 2. As shown in Figure 3c, each [Be3(HPO4)4] group is firstly connected to four neighboring clusters by sharing six vertex atoms [i.e. O(4), O(10) and O(15)] to give rise to a similar two‐dimensional architecture with eight‐ring apertures, as found in compound 1 (Figure 3d). Adjacent inorganic layers are further stacked exactly one over the other along the [100] direction in an –ABAB– fashion, and covalently held together by the remaining O(7) bridging atoms to generate a three‐dimensional framework with regular eight‐ring apertures along the [101] and [110] directions (Figure 4a, b), and puckered 12‐ring channels along the [010] direction (Figure 4c, d). Distinct from the SBU in compound 1 only linking four neighboring groups to produce a two‐dimensional architecture, each SBU in 2 is linked to six neighbors to form a 3D open‐framework with pcu topology. It is also worthy to note that the present 12‐ring channels are not straight but propagate in a zigzag way. It was defined by six BeO4 and six PO3(OH) tetrahedral groups, with a diameter size of ca. 7.5 × 8.8 Å. The framework density of this structure measured by the number of polyhedra per 1000 Å3 is 15.9, which shows a considerable degree of openness comparable to that observed in many beryllium phosphate zeolites,[23–31,36–38] such as BePO4‐ABW (21.5), BePO4‐GIS (19.1), CBP‐CHA (18.6), BePO4‐GME (17.2), [C6N2H13][Be2(H2O)(PO4)(HPO4)]·H2O (17.1) and [H3N(CH2)3NH3][Be3(HPO4)4] (17.5), etc.

The guest templates are found perfectly arranged in the middle region of the 8‐ring channels and, as the counterion, balance the overall negative charges of the three‐dimensional architecture. Extensive O–H···O, N–H···O, and C–H···O types hydrogen bond interactions were observed in 2, and the shortest O/N···O distances are in the range of 2.558(3)–3.003(3) Å. A void space analysis using the program PLATON indicates that these extra‐framework organic cations occupy 36.2 % of the unit cell volume.

Thermal Properties

Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses of the powder samples of 1 and 2 were carried out in a nitrogen atmosphere to investigate their thermal stabilities. As shown in Figure 5, compound 1 remains stable up to 398 °C. On further heating, a sharp weight loss occurred in the range of 398–525 °C, which should correspond to the decomposition of organic template. However, the observed weight loss (23.46 %) was lower than the expected value (27.55 %). The lower reduction in this stage may be due to the partial retention of carbon in the solid residue (black in color). Similar thermal behavior was also observed in previously reported metal phosphite/phosphates.39,40. The remaining weight loss of 3.40 % between 580–746 °C should be attributed to the removal of carbon from the black solid residue. The total observed weight loss for the two steps compared well with that calculated on the basis of the above interpretation. For 2, the structure can be stable before 293 °C and then underwent similar two‐step weight loss, one sharp step followed by a tail process. The total weight loss of 28.17 % in the range 293–656 °C was in good agreement with the removal of the organic molecules and the dehydration of the hydroxyl groups in the product (calcd: 28.03 %). The remaining residue of each sample is amorphous after the calcination and their phases are unidentified.

Conclusions

Two new open‐framework beryllium phosphite and phosphate, [H2tmpda][Be3(HPO3)4] (1) and [H2ampy][Be3(HPO4)4] (2), were successfully synthesized in the presence of different amines as the structure directing agents. The alternating connections of BeO4 tetrahedra and 3‐connected units, i.e. HPO3 pseudopyramids in 1 and HPO4 groups in 2, give rise to different (3,4)‐connected inorganic frameworks. Interestingly, both compounds can be constructed from similar heptameric group as the secondary building unit: [Be3(HPO3)4] in 1 and [Be3(HPO4)4] in 2. By regarding the [Be3(HPO3)4] group as a 4‐connected node, the layer structure of 1 has a 44 grid topology, whereas the 3D framework of 2 shows a pcu topology when the[Be3(HPO3)4] units are regarded as 6‐connected secondary building units. The successful preparation of the two compounds exemplifies the possibility for constructing novel interrupted open‐framework solids with different topologies in the presence of different structure‐directing agents. Further work on this subject is in progress.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods: All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. The CHN analyses were carried out with an Elemental Vario EL III CHNOS elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were obtained from sample powder pelletized with KBr with an ABB Bomen MB 102 series FTIR spectrophotometer over a range 400–4000 cm–1. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed with a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA 851e analyzer in a nitrogen atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 from 40 to 800 °C.

Synthesis of [H2tmpda][Be3(HPO3)4] (1): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.175 g), H3PO3 (0.658 g), N,N,N',N'‐tetramethyl‐1,3‐propanediamine (0.83 mL) and H2O (4.50 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and heated at 160 °C for 8 d. The initial pH of the reaction mixture was found to be about 3. After cooling to room temperature, the resulting product, containing colorless crystals of 1, was recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (75.3 % yield based on beryllium). CHN elemental analysis confirmed its stoichiometry. [C7N2H20]2[Be3(HPO3)4] (479.19): calcd. C 17.54, H 4.21, N 5.84 %; found: C 17.36, H 4.08, N 5.69 %.

Synthesis of [H2ampy][Be3(HPO4)4] (2): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.170g), H3PO4 (0.30 mL), 3‐(aminomethyl)pyridine (0.20 mL), methanol (4.40 mL), and H2O (0.60 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and heated at 160 °C for 9 d. The initial pH of the reaction mixture was found to be about 5. After cooling to room temperature, the resulting product, containing colorless crystals of 2, was recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (46.7 % yield based on beryllium). CHN elemental analysis confirmed its stoichiometry. [C6N2H10][Be3(HPO4)4] (521.10): calcd. C 13.83, H 1.93, N 5.38 %; found: C 13.64, H 1.74, N 5.27 %.

Determination of Crystal Structure: Suitable colorless single crystals of 1 and 2 were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber with epoxy resin. Crystal structure determination by X‐ray diffraction was performed with a Siemens SMART CCD diffractometer with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) in the ω and φ scanning mode at room temperature. An empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.41 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full‐matrix least‐squares methods using the SHELXL program package.42 All non‐hydrogen framework atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms residing on the phosphorus were located by Fourier maps and the remaining hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically and refined in a riding model. The crystallographic data and structure refinements for 1 and 2 are given in Table 1. The final atomic coordinates for compounds 1 and 2 are listed in Table 2 and Table 3.
