New mimic of zeolite: heterometallic organic host framework accommodating inorganic cations†

Yu-Biao Chen , Yao Kang and Jian Zhang *
State Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, The Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, P. R. China. E-mail: zhj@fjirsm.ac.cn

Received (in Cambridge, UK) 23rd December 2009 , Accepted 19th February 2010

First published on 17th March 2010

Presented here is a heterometallic organic framework [Na2(H2O)10]n[CuZn4(μ3-OH)4(btec)2]n·2nH2O (1, btec = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate) that incorporates mixed Cu2+–Zn2+ oxide chains and accommodates Na+ cations as extraframework guest species; such a building mode is comparable to that of aluminosilicate zeolites where the porous framework combines two kinds of structural building units (such as [SiO4] and [AlO4]) and one kind of inorganic-templating cation.

During the last decade, lots of works have witnessed the rapid development from inorganic zeolites (mostly known as aluminosilicates or aluminophosphates) to crystalline microporous metal–organic framework materials which exhibit unique features on high Langmuir area and tunable structural compositions.1–7 While MOFs can adopt a variety of different structures, one of the strongest interests in the current MOF research is to mimic the structural features of zeolites.8–10 One common strategy for the creation of new zeolite-like materials is the synthesis of MOFs with 4-connected zeolite topologies, which has been realized in many zeolitic tetrahedral imidazolate frameworks.9,10

The typical aluminosilicate zeolites have a porous structure that can accommodate a wide variety of cations, such as Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+ and others.11 Thus, the zeolite synthesis strongly relies on the combination of two kinds of structural buiding units (such as [SiO4] and [AlO4]) and one kind of guest. The composition of aluminosilicate zeolites always has such three main parts and presents a clear tri-component system.

In comparison with the above inorganic zeolites, there have been much fewer examples of 3D porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) that show such similar tri-components system. Although some organically or inorganically templated zeolite-like MOFs are known to date,2c,d,8,10c their host frameworks are often based on homo-metallic building unit, and thus there are only two components (one structural building unit and one guest) in the structures. Obviously, a synthetic approach to fabricate a tri-component system like zeolites will help to significantly expand the structural diversity of MOFs, and also extend the route for MOFs to mimic inorganic zeolites.

Herein, we report a heterometallic organic framework [Na2(H2O)10]n[CuZn4(μ3-OH)4(btec)2]n·2nH2O (1, btec = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate) that incorporates mixed Cu(II)–Zn(II) oxide chains and accommodates Na+ cations as extraframework guest species. It is interesting to note that Cu and Zn, just like Al and Si in zeolites, are adjacent elements in the periodic table. They have similar atom size, coordination ability and X-ray atomic scattering factor, but small differences in bond distances and quite large differences in coordination geometries could result in distinct structures. Although the simple Cu-btec or Zn-btec system has been largely explored during the last decade,12 no mixed Cu–Zn-btec structures are known to date. To our knowledge, compound 1 reported here represents an extraordinary structure containing a heterometallic organic host framework and inorganic guests, which fully mimic the special tri-component system of zeolites.

The blue crystals of compound 1 were simply synthesized by mixing ZnCl2, Cu(NO3)2·3H2O, H4btec and Na2CO3 with the molar ratio of 4:1:1:3 under hydrothermal reaction conditions.‡ The structure of 1 was characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction,§ IR and elemental analyses. The phase purity was identified by X-ray powder diffraction. It is noteworthy that compound 1 could not be obtained under similar hydrothermal reaction conditions if Na2CO3 was replaced by other bases (K2CO3 or Li2CO3). Such results clearly indicate the structure directing role of the Na+ cation.

The outstanding structural feature of 1 is the microporous anionic host framework built from mixed Cu2+–Zn2+ oxide chains as well as its one-dimensional channels occupied by inorganic Na+ cations and guest water molecules. Compound 1 crystallizes in the space group C2/c, it is a tri-metallic system including Cu2+, Zn2+ and Na+ cations (Fig. 1a). Determining the Cu/Zn ratio is one of the most important aspects in the structural analysis of this compound. Elemental analyses (Cu and Zn) were measured by an electron probe microanalyzer (EPMA-810), the result showed the Cu/Zn ratio was 1:4. The similarity in the X-ray atomic scattering factor between Cu2+ and Zn2+ makes it difficult to distinguish Cu2+ and Zn2+ atomic sites in the crystal structure refinement. However, in some cases, it is possible to determine the distribution of Cu and Zn atoms by analyzing metal–oxygen (M–O) bond distances and coordination geometries.


Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environment in 1 with atom labelling (symmetry codes: a = 0.5 − x, 0.5 + y, 3.5 − z; b = x, 1 − y, −0.5 + z; c = 0.5 − x, 0.5 − y, 3 − z; d = 1 − x, 1 − y, 3 − z; e = 0.5 + x, 0.5 + y, z; f = 0.5 + x, 0.5 − y, 0.5 + z; g = 1 − x, y, 3.5 − z); (b) the mixed metal oxide chain {CuZn4O16}n of 1 along the c axis; the Zn centers are represented as polyhedra.

In the asymmetric unit of 1, four metal sites should be considered carefully to distinguish Cu2+ and Zn2+, the Na+ sites are easy to identify because of the significantly different atomic scattering factor. Fortunately, the four metal sites exhibit four different coordination geometries which help greatly with the analysis (Fig. 1a). Firstly, the tetrahedral site, which is coordinated by two O atoms (dM–O = 1.970(4) Å) from two different btec4− ligands and two μ3-OH groups (dM–O = 1.968(4) Å), is favorable for a Zn(II) center (Zn2), since a tetrahedron is not a Cu2+ center’s preferred geometry. The second metal center with distorted square pyramid, which is bonded by four O atoms (dM–O = 1.956(4)–2.316(4) Å) from four individual btec4− ligands and one μ3-OH (dM–O = 2.010(3) Å), is also a Zn2+ center (Zn1) by considering the bond distances. For the octahedral metal site which is coordinated by two O atoms (dM–O = 2.035(4) Å) from two individual btec4− ligands and four μ3-OH groups (dM–O = 2.023(3)–2.284(4) Å), the bond distances indicate a Zn2+ center (Zn3) may be more suitable. Thus, the remaining metal site with square planar geometry, which is surrounded by four μ3-O atoms (dM–O = 1.926(3)–1.998(3) Å), is assigned to a Cu(II) center (Cu1) because of the suitable bond distances and coordination geometry.

Now, the connectivity between the btec4− ligands and metal centers is also clear. The btec4− ligand acts as a μ9-bridge to link six Zn(II) centers and three Na(I) centers. Two independent Na(I) centers are coordinated by water molecules and attach to the btec4− ligand through the coordination with the carboxylate groups.

In the structure of 1, the [CuO4] squares and the [ZnO6] octahedra are connected to each other alternately in the edge-sharing mode along the c axis to form mixed metal oxide inorganic chain [CuZnO6]n (Fig. 1b). At the same time, one [ZnO4] tetrahedron attaches to one [ZnO6] octahedron in the edge-sharing mode and further bridges two [CuO4] squares through the corner-sharing mode, while each [ZnO5] square pyramid shares a common corner with one [ZnO6] octahedron and one [CuO4] square. The attachments of [ZnO4] tetrahedron and [ZnO5] square pyramid on the [CuZnO6]n chain generate a longer chain [CuZn4O16]n which also can be seen as the tetranuclear Zn clusters linked by the Cu centers.

It is interesting to note that the resulting inorganic mixed metal oxide chains are pillared by organic btec4− ligands into a hybrid 3D anionic framework [CuZn4(μ3-OH)4(btec)2]n2n− exhibiting 1D elliptical channels with the approximate dimension of 7.8 × 5.6 Å2 (Fig. 2). Such an anionic porous framework accommodates sodium ions and water molecules as guest species. The sodium ions not only play an important role in charge equilibrium but also act as special inorganic guests inside the pores.


Fig. 2 View down the c axis for 1, showing the 3D framework with 1D channels that are occupied by guest [Na(H2O)x]+ (x = 3 or 4).

To study the stability of 1, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) were performed. TGA experiments of 1 showed an 18.75% weight loss in a long range between 60 °C to 280 °C, corresponding to the loss of 12 water molecules (calculated value of 18.7%) (Fig. 3). Two water-losing stages are worthy of noting: (a) the first stage is 15.56% weight loss in the range between 60 °C to 210 °C, corresponding to the loss of 10 water molecules (calculated value of 15.58%) that comprises two free water molecules and eight weakly-coordinated water molecules (dNa–O = 2.367(5)–2.425(7) Å); (b) the second stage is a 3.14% weight loss in the range between 210 °C to 280 °C that can be attributed to the two strongly-coordinated water molecules (calculated value of 3.12%), (dNa–O = 2.179(19) and 2.185(11) Å). The DSC diagram of 1 shows two strong peaks in the site of 129.0 °C and 260.8 °C, which is consistent with the result of TGA experiment.


Fig. 3 TGA-DSC curve for heating 1 from 30 to 600 °C.

The X-ray powder diffraction (XPRD) determination shows that the skeleton of 1 is remaining after the first water-losing stage at 129 °C and it can be rehydrated at room temperature (Fig. S1†). Compared to the original crystals, the dehydrated solid obtained by heating crystals of 1 to 129 °C shows an almost identical XPRD pattern as far as the line positions are concerned but the intensity is weakened. After this dehydrated solid is immersed in water, the intensity of the lines can be partially recuperated but a few additional strong peaks appear. This suggests that the main framework of 1 is retained but maybe the hydrate Na+ ions in the channels are rearranged. When 1 was heated at 260 °C, some of the characteristic peaks disappeared, which illustrate that the framework is beginning to change.

Ion-exchange experiments were performed at room temperature by immersing the powder (about 50 mg) of 1 into water solution of LiCl or KCl (3 mL, 2.0 mol L−1) for 1 day. The products were filtered, washed with ethanol and then dried in air. Elemental analysis indicates that the Na+ cations in 1 can be fully exchanged by Li+ or K+ ions, respectively.

In summary, we report here a special tri-component system of a heterometallic organic host framework with inorganic cation guests that is comparable to aluminosilicate zeolites. The combination of Cu2+ and Zn2+ ions in the synthetic system contributes to the successful formation of such an unusual structural type. The results demonstrate that the rational assembly of mixed Cu/Zn metals with organic ligands is possible to generate new frameworks. Meanwhile, it goes beyond the common strategy for the creation of new zeolite-like MOFs that adopt 4-connected zeolite topologies; a new perspective from the consideration of structural composition is provided and demonstrated here for the first time.
