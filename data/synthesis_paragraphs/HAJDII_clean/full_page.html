Template-directed synthesis of a luminescent Tb-MOF material for highly selective Fe3+ and Al3+ ion detection and VOC vapor sensing†

Di-Ming Chen , Nan-Nan Zhang , Chun-Sen Liu * and Miao Du *
Henan Provincial Key Laboratory of Surface & Interface Science, Zhengzhou University of Light Industry, Zhengzhou 450002, P. R. China. E-mail: chunsenliu@zzuli.edu.cn; dumiao@zzuli.edu.cn

Received 12th December 2016 , Accepted 30th January 2017

First published on 30th January 2017

A flexible bidentate polyaromatic acid ligand 4,4′-oxybis(benzoic acid) (H2oba) was used for the construction of a novel luminescent Tb(III)-based metal–organic framework (MOF), namely {(Me2NH2)[Tb(OBA)2]·(Hatz)·(H2O)1.5}n, with 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (Hatz) as the template reagent. In the structure of the Tb-MOF, there co-exist single-stranded and double-stranded helical chains along the c axis, and the overall network shows a three-fold interpenetrated qtz net. This MOF is capable of selectively sensing Fe3+ and Al3+ ions in water via quenching the luminescence and tuning the emission ratio between the ligand-based and metal-based luminescence, respectively. More importantly, this MOF can realize fast detection for p-xylene (PX) vapor with a response time of less than 10 s and 40% fluorescence enhancement, while nitrobenzene (NB) vapor could lead to a quenching effect in 10 min with a 75% quenching efficiency. Remarkably, this is the first example of a multi-responsive luminescent Tb-MOF sensor for Fe3+ and Al3+ ions and VOC vapor detection. In connection to these, the probable sensing mechanisms were also discussed in this paper.

Introduction

Lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) that are built up from functional lanthanide ions and organic carboxylic ligands have attracted tremendous attention in recent years not only for their diversiform structures due to the variable coordination spheres of lanthanide ions, but also for their wide range of potential applications in many important domains especially in luminescent detection.1 The intrinsic luminescence features of lanthanides such as high color purity, large Stokes shift, long fluorescence lifetime and wide emission range, coupled with the unique advantages of MOFs, offer excellent prospects for the design of novel luminescent materials with enhanced desired functionalities and a high added value for specific targeted applications.2 Tb3+ ion is one of the most extensively used lanthanide ions for building luminescent MOFs because it can emit strong characteristic green light emission by energy transfer from the ligand to the lanthanide ion (also called “antennae effect”) when excited via UV radiation, and this emission can be effectively modulated by analyte–ligand interactions.3 In the past few years, fluorescent sensors based on luminescent Tb-MOFs have been widely investigated for the selective detection of metal ions or small organic molecules as a result of their ability to provide a simple, selective, and visible detection method.4

Iron and aluminum are ubiquitous metals in our living environment, and their cations also play important roles in biological systems and have a wide range of applications in industrial production. The Fe3+ ion influences a variety of vital cell functions such as muscle function, hemoglobin formation, and brain function. Both excess and deficiency from the normal permissible limit can induce various health hazards.5 On the other hand, the Al3+ ion is not a necessary microelement for the human body, and its concentration level has a direct impact on human health. However, the Al3+ ion existing in natural water and most plants can enter the human body through foods and drinking water. Many diseases can be caused by aluminum toxicity, such as bone softening and Alzheimer's disease. Therefore, the selective detection of Fe3+ and Al3+ ions is very important for human health.6

As the main sources of atmospheric pollution, volatile organic compounds have posed various threats to homeland security and human health risks.7 They are present in indoor/outdoor air, as a consequence of the release from chemical industries, cosmetics, building blocks, etc. Therefore, effective detection of VOCs is of great importance for both environmental and health issues. Although there are a number of luminescent MOFs showing great promise in sensing VOCs in solution, fluorescence sensing of trace solvent vapors is still challenging due to their low vapor pressures at room temperature, so the luminescence detection in the vapor phase is much more difficult than in the liquid form.8

In this work, we present a unique luminescent Tb-MOF material with single-stranded and double-stranded helical chains co-existing in the three-fold interpenetrated framework by using the 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (Hatz) as the directing reagent. Gas sorption studies show that this MOF demonstrates a preferred adsorption of C2H2 and CO2 over CH4 and N2. Furthermore, this MOF could be applied to sense Fe3+ and Al3+ ions in water via quenching the luminescence and turning the emission ratio between the ligand-based and metal-based luminescence, respectively. More importantly, this MOF can realize fast recognition of p-xylene (PX) vapor with a response time of less than 10 s and 40% fluorescence enhancement, while nitrobenzene (NB) vapor could lead to a quenching effect in 10 min with a 75% quenching efficiency. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example of a multi-responsive luminescent Tb-MOF sensor for Fe3+ and Al3+ ions and VOC vapor detection, especially for the distinguishing of p-xylene from ortho-xylene and meta-xylene via fluorescence enhancement.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All the chemicals are commercially available and were used as received. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Vario EL III Elementar analyzer. Thermal analysis (under a N2 atmosphere, a heating rate of 10 °C min−1) was performed on a Labsys NETZSCH TG 209 Setaram apparatus from room temperature to 800 °C. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were performed on the Rigaku model Ultima IV diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation. C2H2, CH4, N2 and CO2 sorption data were measured on a Micromeritics 3Flex surface area and pore size analyzer under ultrahigh vacuum in a clean system, with a diaphragm and turbo pumping system. Luminescence spectra were obtained on a Varian Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer.

Synthesis of {(Me2NH2)[Tb(OBA)2]·(Hatz)·(H2O)1.5}n (1)

A mixture of Tb(NO3)2·6H2O (0.046 g, 0.1 mmol), H2oba (0.026 g, 0.1 mmol), Hatz (0.042 g, 1 mmol), DMF (2 mL), and H2O (2 mL) was sealed in a 5 mL glass bottle and heated at 80 °C for 72 h under autogenous pressure. After cooling to room temperature, colorless block crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were obtained. The yield was 60% for 1 (based on H2oba ligand). Elemental analysis calcd (%) for 1 (C32H31N5O11.5Tb): C, 46.39; H, 3.77; N, 8.45; found: C 46.53, H 3.62, N 8.32. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for activated 1 (C32H28N5O10Tb): C, 47.95; H, 3.52; N, 8.74; found: C 48.02, H 3.36, N 8.91.

X-ray structure determination and structure refinement

The crystal data for 1 were collected on an Oxford Xcalibur Gemini Eos diffractometer with MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) with the ω-scan technique at 298 K. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined with the full matrix least-squares technique embedded in the SHELXTL crystallographic software package.9 The non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and the hydrogen atoms of the ligand were refined isotropically using a riding model. For the highly disordered nature of the guest solvents, they could not be well modeled in the refinement, so they were removed by using the SQUEEZE option of PLATON.10a The final chemical formula of 1 was derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. It is notable that the amount of disordered solvent deduced from the TGA and EA (one and a half water per formula unit, 15 electrons) results of 1 is not consistent with the amount of electrons deduced from the SQUEEZE output (34 electrons per formula unit). That is maybe due to the weak diffractions of these structurally disordered guest molecules in the porous structures.10b Details for structural analyses are summarized in Table S1 (ESI†).

Results and discussion

Crystal structure and topological analysis

The solvothermal reaction of the organic ligand H2oba and Hatz with Tb(NO3)3·6H2O in a mixed solvent of N,N-dimethyl-formamide (DMF) and H2O (1:1, v/v) afforded colorless block crystals of {(Me2NH2)[Tb(OBA)2]·(Hatz)·(H2O)1.5}n. During the synthesis process, we found the Hatz ligand played a vital role in the formation of the final product. The single-crystal X-ray study reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the chiral space group P6422 with a 3D channel-type framework. The asymmetric substituent group on the atz− ligand might be responsible for the formation of chiral elements in 1.11a The Flack parameter of −0.004(4) demonstrates the homochirality of the tested single crystal. Spontaneous resolution occurs here and the bulk sample is racemic because of the achiral precursors.11b The asymmetric unit of 1 contains half a Tb(III) ion, one oba2− ligand, half a lattice Hatz and half a Me2NH2 cation. The Tb(III) ion is eight-coordinated with eight O atoms from six oba2− ligands, representing a triangular dodecahedron geometry (Fig. 1a). The Tb–O bond distances range from 2.280(7) to 2.481(7) Å, which are comparable to those reported for the Tb-MOFs.4 Two symmetry-related Tb(III) ions are coupled in pairs by four carboxylate groups to afford a binuclear {Tb2} secondary building unit. The oba2− ligand acts as a μ3-bridge, in which one carboxylate group adopts a μ2-η1:η1-bridging mode while the other adopts a μ1-η1:η1-bridging mode. Viewed along the c axis, the structure features two types of channels: a large 1D hexagonal mesoporous channel with a diameter of 2.6 nm was formed by two interleaving right-handed and left-handed helices, which is further surrounded by six 1D triangular channels with a diameter of 0.48 nm shaped by right-handed helices (Fig. 1b). The potential voids are large enough to be filled via mutual interpenetration of two independent equivalent frameworks, generating a 3-fold interpenetrating 3D architecture with 1D circular channels (Fig. 1c). The Hatz ligands reside in the free voids of the channels as template molecules and interact with the framework O atoms through hydrogen bonds (N–H⋯O distance: 1.992 Å), which further form another two helical chains inside the molecular helix (Fig. S1, ESI†). The short hydrogen bond indicates that the Hatz ligands are tightly binding with the framework, which makes them difficult to be removed at room temperature. From the topological point of view, the binuclear Tb cluster could be considered as a four-connected node and the oba2− ligand could be viewed as a two-connected linker, then the overall structure of the Tb-MOF can be simplified as a Class Ia three-fold interpenetrated 4-connected qtz network with a point of (64·82). According to the literature, the qtz net is intrinsically chiral, which corresponds well with the chiral nature of this framework (Fig. 1d). PLATON calculations indicate that 1 consists of 24.6% void space to accommodate solvent molecules (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Pore environment characterization

The pore environment properties of the Tb-MOF were investigated by the gas sorption studies. Before the gas sorption experiments, the as-synthesized Tb-MOF samples were firstly soaked in CH2Cl2 for three days, and then activated under dynamic vacuum for 24 h at room temperature. The porosity of the desolvated Tb-MOF (denoted as 1a) was probed by the N2 sorption at 77 K. As shown in Fig. S5 (ESI†), 1a showed a type-III sorption behaviour with the maximum N2 uptake of 33 cm3 g−1, which indicates that the N2 molecules could not effectively diffuse into the pores of 1a, resulting in surface sorption.12 On the other hand, the C2H2, CO2 and CH4 sorption isotherms collected around room temperature imply that these gases could access into the porosity of 1a and the uptake amount followed the hierarchy: C2H2 > CO2 > CH4, whereas only neglectable N2 uptake could be observed (Fig. 2a). In addition, the isosteric heats (Qst) of adsorption of 1a to C2H2, CO2 and CH4 gases at low coverage, which reflect their binding energies, are 28.8, 24.7 and 17.5 kJ mol−1 respectively, as calculated based on the virial method (Fig. S6, ESI†). These values also follow the same trend as their uptake capacities. The higher Qst value of C2H2 compared with that of CO2 or CH4 might be attributed to the strong interactions between C2H2 and open triazolyl N active sites in the framework.13 In order to establish the feasibility of this purification, the Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST) was used to estimate the separation performance of 1a (Fig. 2b). Based on the single gas adsorption isotherms collected at room temperature, the calculated selectivity of C2H2/CH4 (50:50) and CO2/CH4 (50:50) at 298 K and 1 bar is 13 and 6, which is higher than many reported Ln-MOFs for CH4 purification such as [Yb(BPT)], Tb-BCB and so on.14

Fluorescence behavior and sensing properties

The solid-state luminescence properties of the as-synthesized Tb-MOF as well as the free H2oba ligand were explored at room temperature. Upon excitation at 310 nm, the Tb-MOF shows emission peaks at 488, 541, 585, and 619 nm, which could be assigned to the 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, and 3) transitions of Tb3+ ions. While the ligand shows a broad emission peak at 386 nm, which could be attributed to the ligand-based π* to π transitions (Fig. 3a).15 In addition, it is worth noting that the ligand-centered broad emission is not observed in the solid-state luminescence emission spectrum of the Tb-MOF, indicating the occurrence of the “antenna effect”: the oba2− ligand acts as the antenna to adsorb the energy from light, then it transfers energy to the Tb3+ ion, leading to a bright blue characteristic color which can be readily observed by the naked eye. In light of the excellent luminescence properties of the Tb-MOF, we examine its potential for sensing of metal ions in water. The as-synthesized Tb-MOF samples were ground and dispersed in 1.8 mL aqueous solutions, then 0.2 mL aqueous solutions containing 10−2 M different metal ions (K+, Cd2+, Co2+, Cr3+, Cu2+, Fe3+, Mg2+, Mn2+, Ni2+, Al3+ and Pb2+) were added, respectively, and the solution was mixed via an ultrasonic facility to form a suspension. To ensure the consistency of experimental conditions, the solution was mixed for 30 min before the collection time of fluorescence data. The luminescent spectra and corresponding emission spectra were recorded and are compared in Fig. 3b. The emission spectra show that the various metal ions display markedly different effects on the luminescence of Tb-MOF, particularly for Fe3+ ions and Al3+ ions, whose varying extents could be easily distinguished. The incorporation of Fe3+ ions into the Tb-MOF system could drastically quench the emission of Tb3+, whereas the emission of the ligand could be greatly enhanced after the addition of the Al3+ ion. Other metal ions only show a little or mild effect (for Co2+ and Cu2+ ions) on the luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF. Such a character might be useful for the detection of targeted metal ions such as Al3+ or Fe3+ ions.16 Meanwhile, the competition experiments have been carried out to evaluate the efficiency of 1 towards Fe3+ and Al3+ ions by recording its luminescence intensity in the presence of equal concentrations of the interference metal ions such as Co2+ and Cu2+ ions (0.5 mM:0.5 mM). As shown in Fig. S7 (ESI†), the presence of Co2+ ions or Cu2+ ions could not disturb the sensing process, implying its high selectivity. To validate the high sensitivity of the Tb-MOF for the detection of Fe3+ and Al3+ ions, we measured the fluorescence responses of the Tb-MOF in the presence of different concentrations of Fe3+ or Al3+ ions. The as-synthesized Tb-MOF solid samples were ground and immersed in water by adding different concentrations of Fe3+ ions or Al3+ ions, and then their luminescence spectra were recorded. The emission intensity of the Tb-MOF suspension was quenched steadily with increasing Fe3+ concentration from 1 to 100 μM, and was totally quenched after the addition of 10 mM Fe3+ ions (Fig. 4a). In comparison, the ligand-based luminescence was enhanced accordingly with increasing Al3+ concentration from 0.5 to 10 mM coupled with the decrease of the emission of Tb3+ (Fig. 4b). As a result, when irradiated under UV light, the Fe3+ ion can completely quench the emission of the Tb-MOF system and results in a dark color, while the Al3+ ion could induce a blue-colored luminescence which could be clearly seen by the naked eye. In addition, we also collected the response rate of the fluorescence intensity. As shown in Fig. S8 (ESI†), the fluorescence intensity of the Tb-MOF rapidly reduced as soon as 0.2 mL of Fe3+ aqueous solution were incorporated, and there is a little further decrease with the growth of time. This response is much faster than that in many MOFs reported for Fe3+ ion sensing (minutes to hours).17 As for the Al3+ ion, the fluorescence intensity varied gradually with the time passed, and became steady within 20 min (Fig. S9, ESI†). The different response time for the Fe3+ ion and the Al3+ ion might imply their different sensing mechanism.

To further quantitatively evaluate the quenching effect of the Fe3+ ion, the quenching effect constant (KSV) is calculated using the Stern−Volmer (SV) equation: I0/I = 1 + KSV[M]. The I0 and I are the luminescence intensity of the 5D4 → 7F5 transition of Tb3+ at 544 nm before and after the addition of the analyte, and [M] is the concentration of the analyte. It is assumed that the decrease in luminescence intensity is proportional to the concentration of the metal ion. The Stern–Volmer plot for the Fe3+ ion exhibited a good linear correlation at low concentrations (0–100 μM) and the value of KSV is estimated to be 3.4 × 104 M−1, which reveals a strong quenching effect on the Tb-MOF luminescence (Fig. S10, ESI†).18 Furthermore, the decrease of the luminescence intensity was still clearly observed when the concentration of Fe3+ is 1 μM, indicating that the detection limit of the Tb-MOF Fe3+ probe can reach 10−6 M. According to the literature, most reported MOFs for the Fe3+ detection can detect Fe3+ with concentrations from 10−3 to 10−5 M, and the present results indicate that the Tb-MOF could behave as a highly sensitive luminescence sensor for the detection of Fe3+ in aqueous solution.19 To check the recyclability for the Fe3+ ion sensing, the solid compound after Fe3+ detection was collected from the solution through centrifugation and washed with water several times, and then the luminescence intensity was recorded (Fig. S11, ESI†). The similar luminescence intensity of the Tb3+ emission peak clearly showed the excellent recyclability for the Tb-MOF. In fact, the Tb-MOF could be used at least four times without losing its initial luminescence intensity. The low detection concentration and the high quenching constant for Fe3+ reveal that the Tb-MOF is an excellent sensor for the sensitive and selective detection of Fe3+. The underlying mechanism of luminescence quenching by Fe3+ ions could be analysed from the following aspects. Firstly, the PXRD pattern for the Tb-MOF after Fe3+ detection was consistent with the as-synthesized samples, so the framework collapse could be ruled out (Fig. S3, ESI†). Secondly, the quick response time as well as the recoverable character implies that the luminescence quenching should not be attributed to the cation exchange, and this was further supported by the ICP analysis. Thirdly, the competitive energy absorption was another possible reason for the quenching phenomena.20 If the excitation spectrum of the fluorophore (donor) has a certain degree of overlap with the absorption band of the analyte (acceptor), there might be a competitive energy absorption progress between the oba2− ligand and the Fe3+ solution. Therefore, the UV-vis absorption spectra for Fe3+ and other tested metal ions in aqueous solution were investigated, and the corresponding spectrum is plotted in Fig. S12 (ESI†). An inappreciable spectral overlap between the absorption spectrum of Fe3+ aqueous solution and the excitation peaks of the Tb-MOF was observed, implying that the energy of the excited light can be strongly absorbed by Fe3+ ions, which reduces the efficiency of energy transfer from the ligand to the lanthanide ions, and ultimately leads to the luminescence quenching.20 While the absorption spectra of other metal ions show no such overlap with the excitation peaks of the Tb-MOF. In addition, the fluorescence lifetime of the Tb-MOF before and after the addition of Fe3+ ions has been measured. The average lifetime remained unchanged even after the addition of Fe3+ ions, suggesting that the quenching process follows a static mechanism and there is no energy transfer process in the Tb-MOF-Fe3+ system (Fig. S13, ESI†).

For the Al3+ detection on the Tb-MOF, there are two emission bands: the ligand-based emission and the Tb3+ ion emission. The intensity ratio of these two emissions can be applied as the reference parameter in ratiometric luminescence Al3+ sensing, allowing self-calibrating measurement of the Al3+ in a concentration from 0.5 to 10 mM based on the emission spectra. Compared with the traditional absolute emission intensity method, such a self-referencing emission intensity has generated stable luminescent signals for decoding Al3+ ions, which has great potential for practical applications.21 As shown in Fig. S14 (ESI†), the calibration curve approximately follows a linear relationship between the IL/ITb ratio (IL is the emission intensity for the oba2− ligand and ITb represents the emission intensity of the Tb3+ ion) and the Al3+ concentration with a correlation coefficient R2 = 0.994 which demonstrates that the Tb-MOF might be an excellent luminescent Al3+sensor within the Al3+ concentration ranging from 0.5 to 10 mM. It should be pointed out that when the Al3+ concentration reaches 100 mM, the suspension of the Tb-MOF turned clear, indicating the entire decomposition of the Tb-MOF at such an Al3+ concentration. Furthermore, recyclability experiments at 10 mM Al3+ concentration showed that although the luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF could be recovered and the framework integrity could be kept, the obtained sample mass was far less than the one used in the detection test. Combining these observations with the above experimental results, we can conclude that the Al3+ ion can concentration-dependently collapse the crystal structure of the Tb-MOF to Tb3+ and the oba2− ligand, which were then dissolved in the aqueous solution, leading to a quenching effect of Tb3+ ions and increasing emission of the oba2− ligand.

The remarkable sensing performances of 1 toward different metal ions arouse our enormous interest in detecting trace volatile organic compounds (VOCs) in the vapor state, especially for the toxic aromatic compounds. To achieve this purpose, a home-made setup was fabricated by placing a MOF-coated thin paper into the cuvette, followed by adding one drop of organic compound (such as benzene, toluene, bromobenzene, nitrobenzene, chlorobenzene, para-xylene, ortho-xylene, meta-xylene, acetone, methanol and CH2Cl2) into the cuvette (Fig. 4a). The cuvette was then capped to form a hermetic environment and then the time-dependent emission spectrum was recorded. Notably, the Tb-MOF shows significantly different luminescence responses toward the vaporized analytes: it exhibits remarkable fluorescence enhancement in para-xylene, but obvious fluorescence quenching for nitrobenzene. As shown in Fig. 5b, the time-dependent emission spectrum shows that the response rate of the Tb-MOF for para-xylene vapor is 5 s with a 40% fluorescence enhancement, and the increased fluorescence intensity is stable within 5 min without an obvious change; and the fluorescence intensity of the Tb-MOF exposed to nitrobenzene drops to the half of its original value within 3 min, which reaches a quenching percentage of more than 75% in 10 min (Fig. 5c). Other VOC vapors show no or a little effect on the luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF (Fig. S15, ESI†). For instance, the emission intensity of the Tb-MOF did not show an obvious increase in the first 2 min upon exposure to toluene vapor, and showed a little increase in 3 min without further changing in 5 min. This luminescent enhancement might be attributed to the higher swelling pressure of toluene than that of para-xylene. Whereas acetone could result in a little quenching effect in 5 min, and this quenching effect is much weaker than that of NB. Notably, ortho-xylene and meta-xylene, which only have subtle differences with para-xylene, show little effect on the luminescence intensity of the Tb-MOF. These experimental results indicate that the Tb-MOF could behave as an excellent sensor for the detection of PX and NB vapor through luminescence enhancement and quenching. To further understand such experimental results, the sensing mechanism should be carefully discussed. According to the N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K, the pore diameter of the Tb-MOF should be less than 0.36 nm, which indicates that neither NB nor PX could enter the pore of the Tb-MOF. So the fluorescence quenching and enhancing observed may be attributed to the photoinduced donor–acceptor electron-transfer between the antenna ligand and analyte molecules which are adsorbed on the surface of the MOF particles. According to the literature, the antenna ligand firstly adsorbs energy from light, and then the electron transfers from the ground state (HOMO) to the singlet state (LUMO). The energy levels of the antenna ligand H2oba were calculated using the DFT theory, and the results are shown in Fig. 5d, which indicates that PX has a higher LUMO energy level and NB exhibits a lower LUMO energy level than that of the H2oba ligand. When irradiated under UV light, the antenna ligand firstly adsorbs energy and then transfers electrons from the HOMO to the LUMO. For PX, it has a high-lying p*-type LUMO orbital that is above the singlet state energy of the ligand, so the exited electrons of PX are easily transferred to the LUMO of the ligand, resulting in fluorescence enhancement. Similarly, NB has a lower LUMO energy level than the singlet state energy of the ligand, so the ligand could act as an electron donor and excited electrons will be transferred from the ground state of the ligand to the LUMO of the NB, giving rise to the fluorescence quenching effect.22

Conclusions

In summary, a novel luminescent Tb-MOF material has been successfully prepared by using the Hatz molecule as the template reagent, which exhibits helical channels and a three-fold interpenetrated qtz net. This material could serve as a multi-functional luminescent sensor, capable of selectively detecting Al3+ and Fe3+ ions in water via different luminescent responsive behaviours, discriminating p-xylene vapor among its homologues, as well as sensing highly toxic nitrobenzene vapor. This work demonstrates that luminescent MOFs may be template-directedly synthesized to serve as practical multi-responsive sensors for the detection of targeted metal ions in water and VOCs in vapor.
