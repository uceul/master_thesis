Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 24, October 2012, Pages 50-54

A novel fluorinated metal-organic framework with rhombic channels

Author links open overlay panelDong-PingLiQiLiuFengLiuJingLiPing-HuaChenXiao-YanJiaoYong-XiuLi
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2012.07.048Get rights and content

Abstract

A novel fluorinated metal-organic framework, namely [Zn3(Int)2F4]·CH3OH·H2O (1) (Int = isonicotinate), has been synthesized under solvothermal conditions, and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, infrared (IR) spectroscopy, elemental analysis, solid state fluorescent spectra, thermogravimetry analysis and gas adsorption studies. The unusual F− ions pillared structure of 1 exhibits a porous three-dimensional network with a pcu topology.

Graphic abstract

The Zn2+ ions are first interconnected by Int ligands to form positive 2D skeletons ([Zn3(Int)2]4+), which were further pillared by negative F− ions to generate permanent 3D porous network with rhombic channels.

Highlights

► Fluorinated metal-organic complex constructed by rod-shaped SBUs. ► The unusual F− ions pillared structure of 1 exhibits a porous three-dimensional network with a pcu topology. ► Complex 1 exhibits permanent porosity. ► The adsorption properties and fluorescent properties of 1 have been investigated.

Owing to their interesting structural topologies and potential applications in gas storage and separation, catalysis, and ion exchange, microporous metal-organic frameworks (MMOFs), as a new generation of porous material, have attracted tremendous attention in recent years [1]. Apparently, the design and synthesis of MMOFs with desired porosity and stability are crucial to their adsorptive properties [2]. Yaghi and coauthors have provided an effective route to obtain novel robust MMOFs without interpenetration by incorporating infinite rod-shaped metal carboxylate secondary building units (SBUs) into the structure with appropriate length of carboxyl ligands [3]. Thence, many MMOFs based on rod-shaped SBUs have been produced with intriguing properties [4]. However, MMOFs involving rod-shaped SBUs constructed by ligands without carboxyl groups still remain largely unexplored.

Recent studies have shown that hydrogen and carbon dioxide storage in MMOFs can be enhanced by ways of introducing open metal sites, functionalizing ligands, cation exchange or utilizing catenation [5]. Of particular promise is the possibility of synthesizing fluorinated metal-organic frameworks (FMOFs) with microporous channels using fluorine functionalized organic ligands [6]. The first example of FMOF constructed by silver(I) and 3,5-bis-(trifluoromethyl)-1,2,4-triazolate with superior volumetric gas uptake and “breathing effect” has been demonstrated by Omary and coauthors [6a,6b]. Such FMOFs have also exhibited unique properties such as enhanced thermal stability and adsorption, superacidity, low surface energy and surface tension. Another significant observation is that a novel class of “acceptor type” graphite intercalation compounds (GICs) containing naked fluoride anions can show an enhanced hydrogen adsorption enthalpy which indicates an unusually strong interaction between H2 and F− anion [7]. Theoretical calculation shows that such strong interaction was attributed to the donation of electron density from F− anions to the antibonding σ⁎ orbital of H2 molecules [8]. Though fluorinated materials have shown intriguing adsorption for hydrogen molecules, there are only a few reports of FMOFs containing perfluorinated carboxylates as such ligands are more acidic, less soluble and stable in common organic solvents. Moreover, FMOFs with permanent porosity built up from F− anions are yet unreported.

Herein, we report the synthesis, structure and gas sorption properties of a novel FMOFs [Zn3(Int)2F4]·CH3OH·H2O (1). Complex 1 represents the first example of porous FMOFs with 1D permanent channel derived from F− anions pillared SBUs.

Complex 1 was prepared by solvothermal reaction of ZnF2·4H2O and isonicotinic acid in a mixed isobutanol and methanol solvent [9]. X-ray crystallography study reveals that complex 1 crystallizes in monoclinic space group of C2/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half Zn centers, one Int ligand, two F− anions, one half of solvated water molecule and one half of methanol molecule. As illustrated in Fig. 1, Zn1 atom is coordinated to four F− ions, one pyridine nitrogen atom and one carboxylate oxygen atom from two distinct Int ligands; while Zn2 atom to four F− ions, two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two distinct Int ligands. The Zn atoms show slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry with different coordination units. The Zn1 and Zn2 atoms are interconnected by two F− anions and a μ2-bridging carboxyl group of Int ligand, and the Zn1∙∙∙Zn2 distance is 3.080 Å. The Zn–O (2.035 and 2.046 Å) and Zn–N (2.084 Å) bond lengths in 1 are slightly longer than those in other zinc- isonicotinate complexes [10]. The Zn–F bond lengths range from 1.994 to 2.239 Å.

Interestingly, the adjacent Zn centers are further interconnected by μ3-bridging F− anions to form 1D chains running along the c-axis (Fig. 2). Each F− anion links one Zn1 and two Zn2 atoms, and exhibits two types of configurations in the chain. The F1 anion sits in the junction of a “T” type conformation, while the F2 anion takes up a vertex of pyramid. The Zn2–F1–Zn2 and Zn2–F2–Zn2 angles are 169.90 and 100.05°, respectively. It is noticeable that the rod-shaped chain can be viewed as edge-losing quadrangular prisms stacked in a staggered conformation. The gaps in the quadrangular prism are occupied by lattice water molecules (Fig. 2a). These μ3-bridging F− anions in the rod-shaped chain form a unique edge-sharing octahedral polyhedron strip (Fig. 2b). The rod-shaped SBUs are further linked through the Zn2 atoms by exo-tridentate Int ligands along four directions to generate a 3D metal-organic framework with 1D rhombic channels viewed along the c-axis (Fig. 2c, d).

Further insight into the structure of 1, the 3D porous network can also be viewed as interlaced positive 2D skeletons ([Zn3(Int)2]4+) (Fig. 3) pillared by negative F− ions (Fig. 2d). Given the Zn2···Zn1···Zn2 trinuclear cluster as one collided node, the 2D layer in the ab plane shows a (4, 4) topology (Fig. S1). On the basis of this simplification and regarding F− ions as linkers, the resulting structure of 1 is a six-connected net with a Schläfli symbol (412·63) (Fig. S2).

To our knowledge, complex 1 represents the first example of porous metal-organic framework with 1D channels pillared by F− anions. These rhombic channels show a size of 6.4 × 8.2 Å in diameter considering the van der walls radii, and are partly occupied by methanol and water molecules (Figs. 4 and S3). There are versatile strong hydrogen-bonding interactions formed by guest methanol molecules and the guest water molecules and the F− anions in the 1D channels of complex. The d(D∙∙∙A) distances of hydrogen bonds arrange from 2.000 to 2.747 Å. Upon removal of guest molecules, the effective free volume of 1 calculated by PLATON analysis (635.9 Å of the 1996.7 Å unit cell volume) is 31.8% per unit cell volume [11].

To confirm the phase purity of compound 1, the as-synthesized samples were characterized by Powder X-ray diffraction pattern (PXRD) at room temperature. The PXRD of 1 is in agreement with the simulated one from the single-crystal X-ray data (Fig. 5). It indicates that the crystal structure is truly representative of the bulk material. The thermal property of complex 1 was examined by the method of thermogravimetric analysis under nitrogen atmosphere. As explicated in Fig. 6, compound 1 shows a weight loss of 8.80% in the range of 30–150 °C, which corresponds to the loss of lattice water and methanol molecules (calculated 8.84%). A plateau exists between 150 and 300 °C, followed by the decomposition of organic components (observed 48.00%, calculated 48.05%). The remaining weight of 43.23% was assumed to be the final product of ZnO (calculated 43.11%). The porous framework of 1 remains unchanged after the removal of guest molecules, which was also verified by PXRD (Fig. 5c).

As the pore size of 1 is higher than the kinetic diameter of N2(3.6Ǻ) and H2(2.89Ǻ), the compound may serve as potential candidate of gas storage/separation material. Hence, the adsorption measurements of 1 for N2 and H2 were performed at 77 K. However, the desolvated complex 1 shows very poor N2 and H2 adsorption at 77 K (Fig. S4). Further research on the CO2 adsorption of 1 was also performed at 273 k. However, no favorable CO2 adsorption isotherm of 1 was observed (Fig. S5). The possible reason for the poor adsorption properties of 1 may be attributed to the limited interaction among the gas molecules and the F− anions as there are only parts of F− anions exposed in cavities.

The excitation and emission spectra of 1 in the solid sate at room temperature were characterized and shown in Fig. 7. Excitation of the solid state sample of 1 at λex = 330 nm produces very strong blue fluorescence with an emission at 437 nm. Contrastively, the free isonicotinic acid ligand displays very weak emission at 433 nm under the same excitation at 330 nm (Fig. S6). The enhanced fluorescence efficiency of 1 is attributed to the coordination of the Int ligand and Zn(II) ions, which effectively increases the rigidity of the Int ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless thermal vibrations [12]. The high luminescence in blue light region indicates that 1 may be a good candidate for blue fluorescent materials.

In summary, we have successfully synthesized a novel 3D metal-organic framework, [Zn3(Int)2F4]·CH3OH·H2O (1), which exhibits a pcu topology with 1D rhombic channels. For an unusual F− ions pillared MMOFs, complex 1 provides an example which may facilitate the exploration of new types of FMOFs with potential gas adsorption/separation properties. The fluorescent characterization of 1 indicates that the compound is an excellent candidate for fluorescent materials.
