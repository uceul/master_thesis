The structural diversity, band gap energy and photoluminescence properties of thiophenedicarboxylate based coordination polymers†

Prabu M.‡ , K. S. Asha‡ , Mekhola Sinha , Anamika Poduval and Sukhendu Mandal *
School of Chemistry, Indian Institute of Science Education and Research Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, 695016 India. E-mail: sukhendu@iisertvm.ac.in; Web: http://iisertvm.ac.in/~sukhendum Fax: +91 471 2597 438

Received 24th September 2015 , Accepted 1st December 2015

First published on 2nd December 2015

A series of 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid (TDC) ligand based coordination polymers were solvothermally synthesized, namely, 1, [Mg3(OH)2(H2O)4(TDC)2]; 2, [NH2(CH3)2]2[Mg3(TDC)4]; 3, [Mg2(TDC)2(DMF)2]; 4, [Zn2(TDC)2(bpe)](solvent)X; and 5, [(CH3)2NH2][In(TDC)], where DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide and bpe = 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane. Compound 1 was synthesized in a MeOH–H2O solvent mixture and it has a two dimensional layered structure. Compound 2 was obtained using a DMF–H2O mixture and it has a three dimensional structure. It contains Mg3O16 trimeric units which are linked by TDC anions to form the three-dimensional connectivity. Compounds 3 and 5 were synthesized using only DMF as a solvent and compound 4 was synthesized using a DMF–MeOH mixture. Compound 3 also has a three-dimensional structure where Mg4O20 clusters are linked through the TDC anions. Compounds 4 and 5 have three-dimensional structures with a 2-fold interpenetration. All the compounds are characterized well through powder X-ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy, photoluminescence spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis. Interestingly compound 5 crystallizes in a chiral space group which has been confirmed by solid state circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy. We have measured the optical band gap energy for all the compounds and the results showed that the band gap energy varies from 3.44 eV to 3.86 eV. This variation in the band gap energy may be due to the difference in structural arrangement.

Introduction

Enormous attempts have been made for the synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs) with a new structure with novel topology and potential applications in catalysis, gas/solvent storage and separation, magnetism, ion exchange, luminescence, etc.1–5 The controlled synthesis of MOFs or PCPs for target materials is still a great challenge, as several factors are associated with their synthesis. It has been proved that several factors like solvent,6 pH,7 counter anion,8 concentration9 and temperature10 play a crucial role for the determination of the overall structure. Thus, control over solvothermally obtained products is very difficult. For the synthesis of target materials, it is very important to understand the role of the above said factors. The temperature and solvents are the important factors, which govern the overall structure of the materials. The pioneering work by Cheetham et al.10e and Natarajan et al.10g,h has shown how thermal desolvation and subsequent changes in the coordination environment led to different dimensionalities. Due to the variation in their physical and chemical properties, different solvents influence the crystal growth rate, and the crystal morphology.6 Solvents also play an important role via solute–solvent interactions towards the synthesis of the hierarchy of structures. Cheng and co-workers have shown how the volume ratio of H2O in the DMF–H2O mixture influenced the shapes and sizes of the crystals.11 Recently we have studied the modulation of crystal growth and morphology by tuning the ratio of solvent in the solvent mixture during the reaction in Cd based MOFs.12

In recent times, interest has been directed towards the incorporation of s-block metals as a component of these extended networks instead of transition metals. This is because the construction of MOFs from smaller s-block elements, like Li and Mg, is appealing as they have good potential to form lightweight materials.13–20 The Mg2+ ion offers a dual advantage of using a lightweight metal and a metal ion that behaves in similar ways to the well described three-dimensional coordination networks of Zn2+. Both of these metal ions, Zn2+ and Mg2+, have comparable ionic sizes (0.72 pm for Mg2+; 0.74 pm for Zn2+), and have similar affinity towards rigid aromatic carboxylate linkers. Both are commonly observed to adopt an octahedral geometry.21

It has been reported that indium based frameworks exhibit interesting structural features. The 8-coordination mode of the indium metal ion with four equivalent carboxylate ligands gave rise to topologically interesting structures.22

Rigid aromatic linkers impart structural strength and are frequently chosen as building blocks for the structures requiring high thermal stability.23–25 The ligand 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid (TDC) being an aromatic rigid molecule ensures stability of the network. The sulphur atom of the thiophene ring contains lone pairs of electrons which can be easily delocalised within the thiophene ring. This type of ligand exhibits unique physical and chemical properties due to good charge-transfer abilities.

Here we report five polymeric structures based on 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid. Among these compounds, 1–3 were synthesized with Mg metal ion while compound 4 was based on Zn metal ion and compound 5 was synthesized with In metal ion. Compound 1, [Mg3(OH)2(H2O)4(TDC)2], has a two-dimensional structure where Mg–O–Mg chains are pillared by TDC anions. Compound 2, [NH2(CH3)2]2[Mg3(TDC)4], has a three-dimensional structure, where Mg3O16 trimers are linked through TDC anions. Compound 3, [Mg2(TDC)2(DMF)2], also has a three-dimensional structure where Mg4O20 tetramers are linked through TDC anions. Compound 4, [Zn2(TDC)2(bpe)](solvent)X, has a three-dimensional structure where ZnO4N units are linked to form the paddle-wheel unit which are linked through TDC anions to form the layered structure. Further, these layers are pillared by bpe molecules to form a 2-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional structure. Compound 5, [(CH3)2NH2][In(TDC)], was crystallized in the chiral space group (P4322). InO8 polyhedral units are linked through TDC anions to form the 2-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional structure. All the compounds are well characterized through single crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction, TGA, PL spectroscopy and IR spectroscopy. The solid-state CD shows that compound 5 is not enantiopure but a case of twinning by inversion. We have also measured the optical band gap energy for all the compounds. The results underscored the effect of the structural variation in the band gap energy.

Results and discussion

The effect of solvent

We have synthesized five compounds. Among these, compounds 1 and 2 were formed by subtle variation of solvents. We have explored the effects of solvents in more detail for these two compounds. It was observed that the pH of the reaction has substantial effects on the formation of compounds 1 and 2, respectively. Compound 1 can only form under basic conditions with a mixture of MeOH–H2O solvents, while compound 2 is formed under non-basic conditions using a DMF–H2O mixture. Under basic conditions, instead of 2, a well-known Mg-formate, namely [Mg(HCOO)3][(CH3)2NH2], was formed.26 It was reported that the formamide or acetamide-type solvents like DMF and DMA decompose to produce HCOO− or [(CH3)2NH2]+ with or without the existence of H2O at a temperature around 100 °C or higher under solvothermal conditions and this was further confirmed in the present case by the synthesis of compound 2. The combination of H2O with a solvent of low boiling point (MeOH) was essential to obtain the crystals of 1 which might help in decreasing the viscosity of the reaction mixture.

Structure description

Compound 1, [Mg3(OH)2(H2O)4(TDC)2]. Single crystal structure determination reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with the space group P21/c (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit contains two Mg atoms, one TDC, one bridging OH group and two coordinated water molecules. One type of Mg atom adopts an octahedral geometry with four oxygen atoms from TDC anions and two from OH groups, while the other type of Mg atom has two oxygen atoms from TDC anions, two from coordinated water molecules and two from OH groups. The Mg–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.049–2.186 Å (av. 2.1241 Å) (Table S2†). The Mg(2)O6 octahedral units are linked through edges to form the dimeric unit and these dimers are bridged through corners of the Mg(1)O6 units to form a one-dimensional chain with Mg–O–Mg connectivity (Fig. 1a). These one dimensional chains are connected by TDC anions to form a layered structure (Fig. 1b). Topological analysis using the TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a 2 nodal 7,12-c net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {314·418·518·614·72}{38·48·55}2 (td10 = 4775) (see the ESI†).27a

Compound 2, [NH2(CH3)2]2[Mg3(TDC)4]. Compound 2 crystallizes in the P space group (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit contains one and a half Mg atoms, two TDC anions, and one dimethyl ammonium cation. The Mg atoms adopt a distorted octahedral geometry with six oxygen atoms from TDC anions. The Mg–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.998–2.121 Å (av. 2.0825 Å) (Table S3†). The distorted MgO6 octahedral units are linked through corners to form the Mg3O16 trimeric unit (Fig. S1†). These trimeric units are connected by one type of TDC anion to form the layered architecture (Fig. 2a). These layered structures are pillared by another TDC anion to complete the three-dimensional connectivity (Fig. 2b). Compound 2 has an anionic framework whose charge is balanced by dimethyl ammonium cations, which are formed from DMF solvent during the course of reaction. Topological analysis using the TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a 2 nodal 11,12-c net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {315·433·57}2{318·436·511·6} (td10 = 4160) (see the ESI†).27a

Compound 3, [Mg(TDC)(DMF)]n (where n = 2). Compound 3 crystallizes in the P21/n space group (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit contains two Mg atoms, two TDC ligands, and two coordinated DMF molecules. Both Mg(1) and Mg(2) adopt a distorted octahedral geometry with five oxygen atoms of the TDC anion and one oxygen atom of the DMF molecule. The Mg(2)O6 octahedral units are linked through edges to form a dimer. The dimeric unit is flanked in both sides by Mg(1)O6 octahedra to form the Mg4O20 tetramer (Fig. 3a). These tetramers are linked through TDC anions to form the three-dimensional structure (Fig. 3b). The Mg–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.986–2.323 Å (av. 2.0907 Å) (Table S4†). Topological analysis using the TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a 2 nodal 12,14-c net with stoichiometry (12-c)(14-c); 2-nodal net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {323·439·54}{325·455·511} (td10 = 5400) (see the ESI†).27a
Fig. 3 (a) The one-dimensional chain present in compound 3. (b) The one-dimensional chains are linked through TDC anions to form the three-dimensional structure along the ac-plane in 3. DMF molecules are not shown for clarity.
Compound 4, [Zn2(TDC)2(bpe)](solvent)X. Compound 4 crystallizes in the C2/c space group (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit contains two Zn atoms, two TDC ligands, one 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane ligand and disordered solvent molecules. Both the Zn atoms adopt a distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry with four carboxylate oxygen atoms and one nitrogen atom from the bpe molecule. The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.02–2.069 Å (av. 2.035 Å) (Table S5†). Two distorted ZnO4N units are linked through a carboxylate moiety to form the paddle-wheel type building unit. These paddle-wheel units are linked through TDC anions to form the layered structure with a sql-type topology (Fig. 4a).27b The layered structure is pillared by bpe molecules to form a 2-fold interpenetrated three-dimensional structure with a fsc-type topology (Fig. 4b and c).27b Topological analysis using the TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with a 10-c uninodal net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {312·428·55} (td10 = 2721) (see the ESI†).27a
Fig. 4 (a) The layered architecture in compound 4. (b) The layered structures are pillared by the bpe molecule to form the three-dimensional structure. (c) Schematic representation of the 2-fold interpenetration in compound 4.
Compound 5, [(CH3)2NH2][In(TDC)]. Compound 5 crystallizes in the P4322 space group (Table S1†). The structure determination of 5 shows that it is not enantiopure but a case of twinning by inversion with a Flack parameter value of 0.48.28 The asymmetric unit contains half of In metal ion, one TDC ligand and half molecule of a disordered dimethyl ammonium cation. An indium ion is coordinated with eight oxygen atoms to form an InO8 unit. The In–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.216–2.375 Å (av. 2.2877 Å) (Table S6†). The eight coordinated indium centres act as nodes, which are connected by four TDC ligands and can be seen as a four-fold connected node, and every TDC ligand is a two-fold connected node (Fig. 5a). Each indium center in 5 is linked to the other indium centers by μ2 TDC bridges to generate a two-fold interpenetrated 3D framework with two different types of 1D channels of ca. 13.89 × 13.89 Å2 and 10.28 × 10.28 Å2 (excluding the van der Waals radii of the atoms) along the c axis (Fig. 5b–d). Between these two channels, one of the channels is occupied by dimethyl ammonium cations and the other is empty. In(III) carboxylate based MOFs have the tendency to create anionic frameworks.22 In order to neutralize these anionic structures, different types of cations get trapped inside the system. In the present case we have observed that the anionic framework is neutralized by the dimethyl ammonium cation. This cation is heavily disordered. A structure with a different ligand that closely resembles this framework was reported in the literature.22e Topological analysis using the TOPOS software identified that the structure has a dia-c4 topological network with a 4-c uninodal net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {66} (td10 = 981) (see the ESI†).27a,c
Fig. 5 (a) The eight coordinated In metal ion in compound 5. (b) Three dimensional coordination network of compound 5 along the ab-plane with two types of channels along the c axis. (c) and (d) The schematic representation of the 2-fold interpenetration of compound 5.

X-ray diffraction and thermal studies

The powder X-ray diffraction studies were done for the bulk sample of all the compounds in order to confirm their phase purity. The PXRD patterns obtained experimentally were matched with the simulated patterns, which was generated based on the structures determined using the single crystal X-ray diffraction for compounds 1–5 (Fig. S2–S6†). This indicated the phase purity of the compounds. The TGA graph showed that the structures are stable up to 300 °C (Fig. S7–S11†). The weight loss visible at around 200 °C for compounds 1 and 2 is due to the loss of coordinated water molecules and hydroxyl molecules. In compound 3, the weight loss at 220 °C is due to the loss of coordinated DMF molecules. In the case of compounds 4 and 5, the weight loss at 150 °C is due to loss of solvent molecules and dimethyl ammonium cations, respectively. The remaining weight loss at above 300 °C for all the compounds is due to the loss of aromatic moieties which leads to the breakdown of the framework. Since compounds 1 and 3 contain coordinated solvent molecules, we have activated these compounds. In the case of compound 1, the weight loss due to coordinated water molecules is around 250 °C. At 250 °C, compound 1 lost its structural integrity (Fig. S12†). In the case of compound 3, the TGA data shows that the compound loses its coordinated DMF up to 350 °C. We have carried out the PXRD at different temperatures which reflected that the compound loses its structural integrity after 250 °C (Fig. S13†).

Band gap energy

The diffuse reflectance spectra of the powder samples of compounds 1–5 were collected in order to measure the optical band gap energy (Ebg). The optical band gap energy was confirmed as the intersection point between the energy axis and the line extrapolated from the linear portion of the absorption edge in a plot of Kubelka–Munk function vs. energy. The band gap values are observed to be 3.82 eV, 3.44 eV, 3.86 eV, 3.81 eV and 3.67 eV for compounds 1–5, respectively (Fig. 6 and Table 1). Structural analysis of 1–5 revealed that the metal nodes are well isolated and connected by organic molecular linkers. CP or MOF based structures generally exhibit very small dispersion and flat valence band (VB) and conduction band (CB). Theoretical calculation on the electronic structural analyses on different types of MOF structures shows that the electronic states of the atom associated with the metal nodes (in this case O (oxygen atoms) and C (carbon) atoms) in the linker determine the band gap energy (Ebg).29,30 Generally, atoms associated with the metal nodes contributed towards the conduction band (CB) and the C atoms of the linker towards the valence band (VB).28 We have measured the indirect band gap energy for this case. The band gap energy varies from 3.44 eV to 3.86 eV. This small variation in the band gap energy for these series of structures may be due to subtle differences in their structural arrangement.

Circular dichroism (CD). The Flack parameter (0.48) of compound 5 exhibits that the bulk crystal material contains a mixture of enantiomers. In order to further evaluate the spontaneous resolution process, the absolute configuration was determined by solid-state circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy using KBr pellet methods. The spectrum shows negative peaks at 203 nm, 218 nm and 238 nm, and positive peaks at 229 nm, 248 nm and 266 nm (Fig. S14†). The region chosen for CD was from 200 nm to 400 nm because compound 5 absorption starts from 340 nm. These results show that 5 is not enantiopure but a case of twinning by inversion. This confirms the spontaneous resolution process.

Luminescence properties

The solid-state luminescence properties of compounds 1–5 as well as the free ligand TDC have been investigated at room temperature. Free ligand TDC displays a main emission band centred at 380 nm on excitation at 300 nm (Fig. 7). This emission peak may be attributed to the π* → π or π* → n transitions within the ligand. We have observed emission bands at 427, 410, 437, 402, and 450 nm for compounds 1–5, respectively, on excitation at 340 nm (Fig. 7). These emission bands were neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature and could be assigned to a combination of the charge transfer process and intraligand emission states (π*–n or π–π*), because similar emissions are observed for the free ligand.

Experimental section

Materials. All reagents and solvents were of reagent-grade quality. Mg(NO3)2·6H2O, Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, InCl3, 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylic acid (TDC), 1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)ethane (bpe) and solvents were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and used as received without further purification. The IR spectra were recorded from prepared KBr pellets, using an IR prestige-21 (Shimadzu) spectrometer. The TGA was performed on an SDT Q600 (Shimadzu) analyser. Powder X-ray diffraction data were collected using an X'pert PRO (PANalytics) powder diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα radiation (1.5405 Å). The CD spectra were obtained on a circular dichroism spectrometer [J815] at room temperature.
Synthesis. Compound 1: Mg(NO3)2·6H2O (1.0 mmol) was dissolved in 5 ml of distilled water. 2,5-Thiophenedicarboxylic acid (TDC, 9.2 mmol) was dissolved in 5 ml of methanol separately. Also the pH was adjusted to 8 using 4 M NaOH then both solutions were mixed together in a 20 mL glass vial. The reaction mixture was heated at 100 °C for 14 days. Colourless crystals were obtained. The crystals were washed with DMF and dried in air for further use.

Compound 2: Mg(NO3)2·6H2O (1.0 mmol) and TDC (9.2 mmol) were dissolved in 10 ml of solvent (DMF–H2O) with a 1:1 ratio, sonicated for 5 minutes then transferred into a Parr Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (23 mL). Then the vessel was sealed and heated at 140 °C for five days. Colourless crystals were obtained. The crystal were washed with DMF and dried in air for further use.

Compound 3: Mg(NO3)2·6H2O (0.5 mmol) and TDC (1.5 mmol) were dissolved in DMF (10 mL), sonicated for 5 minutes then transferred into a 20 mL glass vial. Then the glass vial was heated at 130 °C for three days. Colourless rectangle-shaped crystals were obtained. The crystals were washed with DMF and dried in air for further use.

Compound 4: Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.10 mmol), TDC (0.10 mmol) and bpe (0.10 mmol) were dissolved in 10 ml of solvent (DMF–CH3OH) with a 1:1 ratio, sonicated for 5 minutes then transferred into a Parr Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (23 mL). Then the vessel was sealed and heated at 130 °C for three days. Colourless block-shaped crystals were obtained. The crystals were washed with DMF and dried in air for further use.

Compound 5: InCl3 (0.10 mmol) and TDC (0.10 mmol) were dissolved in 5 mL of DMF, sonicated for 5 minutes then transferred into a 20 mL glass vial then the glass vial was heated at 120 °C for three days. Brown colour block-shaped crystals were obtained. The crystals were washed with DMF and dried in air for further use.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction

For single crystal XRD measurement, a suitable crystal for all the compounds was carefully selected under a polarizing microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber. The single crystal diffraction data were collected on a Bruker AXS Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at 298 K. The X-ray generator was operated at 50 kV and 35 mA using MoKα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. The data were collected with a ω scan width of 0.3°. A total of 606 frames was collected in three different settings of φ (0°, 90°, 180°) keeping the sample to-detector distance fixed at 6.03 cm and the detector position (2θ) fixed at −25°.

The data were reduced using SAINTPLUS31 and an empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.32 The crystal structure was determined by direct methods using SHELXS97 and refined using SHELXL97 present in the SHELXTL V6.14 (ref. 33) package. All hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions during the final step of the refinement process. For the final refinement, the hydrogen atoms were placed in geometrically ideal positions and refined using the riding mode. The last cycles of the refinement included the atomic positions, anisotropic thermal parameters for all the non-hydrogen atoms, and isotropic thermal parameters for all the hydrogen atoms. Full-matrix-least-squares structure refinement against F2 was carried out using the WINGX34 package of programs. The crystallographic parameters for compounds 1–5 are provided in Table S1.† CCDC 1418262, 1418263, 1426841, 1426837 and 1426842 for compounds 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5, respectively.

Optical band gap measurements

The optical absorption spectra of all the compounds were collected on a Shimadzu UV/vis/NIR spectrophotometer. The room temperature diffuse reflectance spectra were measured using BaSO4 as a reference material and converted to a Kubelka–Munk function, from which the band gap of the compound was estimated.35,36 The samples were prepared in the form of thin layers on glass slides. Typically, a glass slide of dimension 16–60 mm was rinsed with ethanol and dried in air. A thin layer of glue was then applied to the lower half of the slide. After that, well-ground powder sample was sprinkled evenly onto the glued surface of the slide. The extra sample was removed by gentle tapping and putting the face of the slide down. By this process, a thin layer of sample could be formed on the glass surface. The films were subsequently sandwiched between two quartz plates and edge sealed by paraffin tape. Spectra were obtained using a UV-vis spectrometer.

We have measured the optical band gaps of all these materials using diffuse reflectance spectroscopy and the Kubelka–Munk model. This two-flux model, which considers only diffuse light, is used to determine the absorption coefficient from a surface that both scatters and absorbs incident radiation. Using the above equation, the band gap of a material can be obtained by extrapolating to zero with the linear fit to a plot of (khν)1/nvs. hν. By this method, we have measured the lowest electronic transition between valence and conduction bands via absorption, which reflects the optical band gap of the material.

IR spectra

The IR spectra for all the compounds were recorded using KBr pellets, on a IR prestige-21 (Shimadzu) spectrometer. We have observed all the characteristic peaks for carboxylate moieties, dimethyl formamide solvent, coordinated water molecules and dimethyl ammonium cation (Fig. S15–19†).

Conclusions

We have synthesized a series of 2,5-thiophenedicarboxylate ligand based coordination polymers by using solvothermal methods. All the compounds are well characterized through single crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction, TGA, PL spectroscopy and IR spectroscopy. We have explored the role of solvent and pH for the formation of compounds 1 and 2, respectively. Compound 1 has a two-dimensional structure whereas compounds 2–5 are three-dimensional in nature. The presence of the Mg–O–Mg bond in 1, Mg3O16 cluster in 2 and Mg4O20 cluster in 3 is noteworthy. Interestingly, compounds 4 and 5 exhibit a 2-fold interpenetration. Compound 5 crystallizes in the chiral space group P4322. Chirality of compound 5 (Flack parameter = 0.48) was studied by solid-state circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy and the results show that compound 5 is not enantiopure but a case of twinning by inversion and confirming the spontaneous resolution process. We have measured the optical band gap energy for all the compounds and the results showed the small variation in the band gap energy due to the subtle difference in their structural arrangement.
