Microporous and Mesoporous Materials

Volume 97, Issues 1–3, 29 December 2006, Pages 132-140

Novel open-framework fluorinated indium phosphate with 14-ring intersecting channels: Assembled from 6∗1 and racemic pillared secondary building units

Author links open overlay panelChaoChenabYunlingLiuaQianrongFangaLiLiuaJarrod F.EubankcNingZhangbShuzheGongaWenqinPanga
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.micromeso.2006.08.010Get rights and content

Abstract

Templated by 4,4′-bipyridine (bipy), a new indium phosphate with an In/P ratio of 9/14 has been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. The compound, In9(HPO4)14(H2O)6F3 · (C10N2H9)3 · (H3O) · (H2O)2, crystallizes in the trigonal system with the space group P-31c (No. 163) having cell parameters a = 13.9306(5) Å, c = 24.0435(12) Å, V = 4040.8(3) Å, and Z = 2 with R1 = 0.0508. The overall structure consists of two types of secondary building units (SBUs): SBU-1, 6∗1, with a formula of [In3(HPO4)4(H2O)3], constructs into a 4.6.12-net layer; and SBU-2, [In3(HPO4)6F3], features two [3.3.3] propellane-like chiral motifs with Δ and Λ configurations, forming an unprecedented racemic “pillar” unit. The layers are stacked in an ABAB sequence, penetrated by the pillar units perpendicularly, forming a 3-D pillared layer structure with a rectangular 14-ring channel system in two dimensions (along the [1 0 0] and [0 1 0] directions). Organic amine and fluorine ions play key roles in directing the formation of the structure, and the compound presents the same solid fluorescence properties as the bipy molecules attributing to the intraligand π–π∗ transition.

1. Introduction

The ability of SiO4 units to act as building blocks for the construction of zeolites with small or large cavities has been well-known and studied extensively for many years [1]. Acting in a similar fashion, metal phosphates, involving not only tetrahedral but also pentahedral and octahedral coordinations for the metals, present additional fascinating structural features and potential applications in porous chemistry and catalysis [2]. Frequent use of hydro/solvothermal techniques and the influence of organic amines on inorganic frameworks promote the formation and structural diversity of metal phosphates [3]. Additionally, the presence of fluoride as a mineralizer during hydrothermal syntheses has a pronounced effect on the microporous phases produced, and may even result in the incorporation of fluoride into the inorganic framework [4]. Using these approaches, a rich structural chemistry has already been established for group 13 phosphates of Al [5] and Ga [6], and work is only recently emerging on the indium phosphate family. Thus far, there are three-dimensional (3-D) open-framework indium phosphates (InPOs) with In/P ratios of 1/2 [7], [8], 4/7 [9], 2/3 [10], 6.8/8 [11], 1/1 [12], [13], 9/8 [14], 5/4 [15] and 2-D layered structures with In/P ratios of 1/3 [16], 1/2 [17], [18], [19], 1/1 [15]. Sometimes rigid octahedral coordination of indium results in interesting chiral features in indium phosphates [20]. Recently, we also reported two layered indium phosphates with chiral motifs templated by a metal complex [19] and an achiral amine [8], respectively.

For describing and analyzing existing topological structures, the concept of secondary building units (SBUs) was introduced into the microporous solid area [21]. Many periodic microporous structures can be regarded as being composed of SBUs, simple elementary units, assembled in one, two or three dimensions. More importantly, the “Scale Chemistry” of SBUs was proposed, in which larger and more unique SBUs would create larger pores and special structures [22]. Férey et al. has illustrated this idea by analyzing a series of metal phosphate structures, and has pointed out that the creation of structures with large and special SBUs commonly requires the use of large, multifunction amines with weak protonation [23]. Furthermore, in situ NMR studies and ex situ investigations clearly show the existence of reactive species (called prenucleation building units or PNBUs) in the reaction solution whose structures are quite close to the SBUs existing in the solid [24]. These observations give a potential approach to design and synthesize large-pore and special-structure compounds.

In the last 10 years, we have delved into the synthesis and structural study of group 13 phosphates, and numerous alumino- [25], gallium- [26] and indium- [8], [12], [14], [15] phosphates were reported. Based on the synthetic strategy of larger pores and novel microporous materials with special SBUs that imply enhanced specific surfaces, more possibilities for storage of varied chemical species and fascinating structural features, we choose 4,4′-bipyridine, large, rigid and weak protonated, as the templating reagent, In(III) as the metal source, and hydrofluoric acid as a mineralizer and connector between the metal atoms. As a result, we successfully synthesized an indium phosphate, In9(HPO4)14(H2O)6F3 · (C10N2H9)3 · (H3O) · (H2O)2, which contains two special types of SBUs, constructing into a 3-D open-framework structure with rectangular, intersecting 14-ring channels. In this paper, we introduce the synthesis, structure, and photoluminescence property of the compound.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis

The title compound was prepared hydrothermally from a mixture of indium nitrate (In(NO3)3 · 4.5H2O, Shanghai Reagent Plant, 99.5%), phosphoric acid (Beijing Chemical Plant, 85 wt%), 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-bipy, Aldrich), hydrofluoric acid (Beijing Chemical plant, 40%) and distilled water with the molar ratio of In(NO3)3 · 4.5H2O:3.0 H3PO4:2.0-4,4′-bipy:7.0HF:555H2O. Typically, 0.35 g indium nitrate was first dispersed into 9 ml of H2O with stirring, and then 0.18 ml of H3PO4 and 0.3 ml HF were added successively with stirring, to form a clear solution. Finally, 0.34 g of 4,4′-bipy was added dropwise under vigorous stirring. The white gel thus formed was stirred until homogeneous. The final mixture was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 8 days under autogenous pressure. The product (about 90% yield based on In source) consisting of colorless block-shaped crystals was separated by sonication, further washed with distilled water and dried at room temperature.

2.2. Characterization

Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were collected on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). The step size was 0.02° and the count time was 2 s. The elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 LSII C.H.N. analyzer. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analyses were carried out on a Perkin–Elmer Optima 3300 DV ICP instrument. F− content was determined using a fluoride ion-selective electrode. A Perkin–Elmer DTA 1700 differential thermal analyzer was used to obtain the differential thermal analyses (DTA), and a Perkin–Elmer TGA 7 thermogravimetric analyzer was used to obtain thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves in an atmospheric environment with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Fluorescent spectra were measured on a Perkin–Elmer LS 55 luminescence spectrometer, equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp.

2.3. Determination of crystal structures

A suitable single-crystal was selected for single-crystal XRD analysis. The data were collected on a Bruker Smart CCD diffractometer [λ (Mo Kα) = 0.71073 Å] at 20 ± 2 °C. A total of 23,381 reflections were collected, with 3040 unique reflections, respectively (Rint = 0.0658). Data processing was accomplished with the Process-Auto processing program. The structure of the title compound was solved by direct methods and refined by a full-matrix least-squares approach on F2 using SHELXTL 5.1 software package [27]. The In and P atoms were first located, then the O, F, C and N atoms were found in the difference Fourier maps. Two lattice waters were also found in the final diference Fourier maps but the hydrogen atoms could not be located. For charge balance, the 4,4′-bipy must be monoprotonated and additional proton should be distributed over one lattice water with SOF (site occupancy of) 1/6. The hydrogen atoms of the framework and bipy rings were initially located from difference Fourier maps and, for the final refinement, were placed geometrically. Finally, the non-hydrogen atoms were all refined anisotropically. The detailed crystallographic data of the compound is listed in Table 1. CCDC reference numbers CCDC 299105.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the structure

The final atomic coordinates with isotropic temperature factors, selected bond lengths, and bond angles are listed in Table 2, Table 3, respectively. The atomic labeling scheme of the framework atoms is shown in Fig. 1. Each asymmetric unit contains three crystallographically unique In atoms, which are located in three distinct sites with point symmetries C1, S6 and C3, and three P atoms, residing in three distinct sites with point symmetries C3, C1 and C1, respectively. Each In atom is octahedrally coordinated, but the coordination environments are different. For In(1), it shares all but one oxygen atom with adjacent P atoms (In–Oav = 2.113 Å). The exception is O(8), which is assigned as an terminal –OH2 group, as indicated by the rather longer In–O distance (In(1)–O(8) = 2.272(5) Å). Similar assignment has been observed in some InPOs compounds with structural water [12], [28]. In(2), which sits on the threefold reversion symmetry axis, connects with six P(2)O4 tetrahedra via six bridging O(4) atoms, respectively. The In(2)–O(4) distance is expected, 2.146(4) Å, and the bond angles are in the range from 87.31(16)° to 180.00(1)°. Except for sharing three bridging oxygen atoms with adjacent P atoms, In(3) coordinates three bridging F atoms with another In(3) atom to form a face sharing dimmer. The F atoms are located on the twofold symmetry axis in cis positions, with the In–F bond distance of 2.158(3) Å and F–In–F angles of 73.25(15)°. This face sharing linkage is rare in the group 13 phosphates, and only several vanadium phosphates [29] and polymolybdated cluster [30] systems have exhibited a similar phenomenon previously. All the coordination tetrahedra around the P atoms have some deformation because of the –OH group (P(1)–Oav = 1.503 Å, P(2)–Oav = 1.530 Å and P(3)–Oav = 1.515 Å), with the fourth coordination site in each case corresponding to a terminal P–OH group, as shown by the longer P–O distance (P(1)–O(7) = 1.568(8) Å, P(2)–O(2) = 1.563(5) Å and P(3)–O(9) = 1.581(5) Å). Those resulting bond lengths and bond angles are all close to those observed previously in indium phosphates, and bond-valence calculations for all atoms within the framework atoms are listed in Table 3 and clearly confirm the valence assignment [31].

As shown in Fig. 2(a) and (c), the InO5(H2O), InO6 octahedra and PO3(OH) tetrahedra based on vertex linked and the unique InO3F3 octahedra based on face sharing, give rise to a novel 3-D framework structure, which may be understood as a series of layers stacked in an ABAB sequence perpendicularly penetrated by unprecedented “pillar” units. From Fig. 2(d), we can see the layer consists of a central circular 12-membered ring surrounded by a series of 4-membered rings, of which there are two distinct types. One set of these rings consists of P atoms, which constitute part of the wall of a 12-membered ring, whilst in the other a HPO4 group “caps” a 6-membered ring and alternates above and below the plane of the 12-membered ring. Notably, the layer contains a unique SBU (denoted SBU-1), 6∗1 (Fig. 2(e)), only observed in three classical zeolite-related topologies, AFS, BPH and MEI [32]. SBU-1 is constructed from alternating InO5(H2O) octahedra and PO3(OH) tetrahedra in which all of the InO5(H2O) groups and three PO3(OH) groups are shared, to form a 6-membered ring, where another PO3(OH) tetrahedron caps the 6MR. Then, each SBU, respectively, shares three pairs of vertices with three others in one plane to construct a 2-D 4.6.12-net along the [1 0 0] direction. Layers with the same connectivity have been observed previously in some AlPOs compounds [33]. However, the Al species of those layers are all tetrahedral coordination and share all their vertices with three of the PO4 tetrahedra, the remaining vertex of which being a P=O group. The phosphate groups of the title compound leave a –OH as a terminal group, and the corresponding In atoms are all octahedral coordination, sharing four sixth vertices with adjacent P atoms of the layer, leaving a terminal water and sharing a bridging oxygen with the “pillar” units penetrating through the layers, to generate a 3-D framework structure. The pillar unit, as shown in Fig. 2(f) and (g), may also be understood in terms of another SBU (SBU-2), the enneamer, with formula of [In3(HPO4)6F3]. The SBU-2 consists of a central In(1)O6 and a pair of In(3)O3F3 octahedra, which are connected to each other by three HP(3)O4 tetrahedra, respectively. Thus, interestingly, the SBU-2 is composed of two [3.3.3] propellane-like chiral motifs sharing a common In(1) atom. The difference in the two motifs is their configurations. One twists in the right-hand direction along the symmetric axis (the axis of In(3)⋯In(2)⋯In(3)), denoted as Δ configuration, and the other twists in the left-hand direction, denoted as Λ configuration (Fig. 2(h)). They are a pair of enantiomers in such that the structure topology is achiral. Then, the adjacent SBU-2 units link together through three bridging F atoms along the threefold symmetry axis, forming the unprecedented pillared structure. It is worthwhile to note that the three F atoms bridging two In(3) atoms leads to the formation of octahedral face sharing dimer for In octahedra (Fig. 2(i)). To our best knowledge, there are many indiumphosphate structures with corner sharing, edge sharing linkage fashions for the metal polyhedra, whereas the face sharing linkage fashion of the title compound has never been reported. The pillared unit perpendicularly penetrates through the 12-MRs of the layer, anchoring the lamella via P(3) tetrahedra of the pillar unit (Fig. 2(b)). Thus, a novel pillared layered structure consisting of a rectangular channel system with 14-ring apertures in two dimensions (paralleling to the [1 0 0] and [0 1 0] directions) is formed. The terminal P–OH and In–OH2 groups of the framework protrude into the channel. The monoprotonated 4,4′-bipy and water molecules are located at the center of the 14-ring windows and the intersecting tunnels. In such way, there are extensive H-bond interactions between the framework atoms and the guest atoms, with the NH⋯O and OH⋯O separation of 1.866–2.677 Å, performing the certain function of neutralizing the negative charges of the inorganic framework and stabilizing the structure.

The feature of [3.3.3] propellane-like chiral motifs present in the structure is very interesting. Yu et al. reported similar motifs in several cobalt complex-templated metal phosphates with layered structures, and the forming of the motifs was explained by chirality transfer through the hydrogen bonds between the framework and chiral metal complexes [34]. However, for the title compound, the formation of the chiral motifs are templated by achiral, rigid 4,4′-bipy. We also have recently reported an indiumphosphate with similar chiral motifs, which was templated by an achiral long-chained amine (trithylenetetramine) [8]. Although the arrangement fashion of the chiral motifs is different, the guests of two compounds are both achiral amines, and thus the formation of chiral motifs could not be explained by chirality transfer through the H-bonds, without doubt. We assumed that the nature of the rigid octahedral indium atoms and their special linkage fashion between indium octahedra and phosphorous tetrahedra might result in the formation of chiral motifs. Further research of the formation mechanism is proceeding.

The other most remarkable feature is the low value of the framework density (FD) of the title compound topology. In comparison to 14.6 T, 14.6 T and 14.7 T per 1000 Å3 of AFS, BPH and MEI topology with 6∗1 SBUs [32], the extended FD value of the title compound is 11.4 T per 1000 Å3 (T = octahedrally coordinated and tetrahedrally coordinated atoms, that is In or P). The lower value should be attributed to the InO6 octahedra and the interrupted structure (the terminal –OH and –OH2 protruding into the channels), especially the intersecting 14-ring channels and large pores of the framework. Assuming the guest molecules can be removed from the channels, the amount of potential-free volume and its accessibility were calculated by the Free Volume module of the Cerius 2 package [35], which both apply the Connolly method [36]. Forcing a standard molecule (a radius of 1.4 Å) to enter the unit cell from the outside, the free volume and the calculated accessible volume of channels for the title compound were obtained and estimated to be about 47.9% and 19.1% of the volume of the unit cell, respectively, higher than those of typical indium phosphate compounds (see Table S1 of the supporting information). The surface enclosing the void volume of the title compound is about 750.81 Å2/unit cell.

3.2. Synthesis and characterization

It is noted that fluorines seemed to play key roles in the synthesis of the title compound. Without additional HF of the preparative mixture, we failed to synthesize the title compound, and only an organic–inorganic hybrid compound employing 4,4′-bipy as a ligand could be obtained [17]. Chippindale et al. [9] reported a pillared layer structural compound in a non-fluorine system, the layer of which is similar to that of the title compound. However, compared to the pillar of the former being simple InO6 octahedra, the fluorine atoms in the title compound occupy the bridging sites, allowing the indium polyhedra to connect together through face sharing linkage, to create unusual chiral motifs and In2O6F3 subunits to pillar the layers. So, the additional fluorine atoms not only create a novel stoichiometry (In/P = 9/14), but also play key roles in the unusual framework and channel architectures formed for the compound.

Surprisingly, the 4,4′-bipy molecule serves as a structural direct reagent (SDA) for the compound. Commonly, 4,4′-bipy is always directly bonded to the inorganic skeletal backbone as a pillar, as in In4(4,4′-bipy)3(HPO4)4(H2PO4)4] · 4H2O [17], or as a ligand linking both a metal and inorganic framework to form a organic–inorganic hybrid material, as in [Cu-4,4′-bipy]](VO2)(PO4) [37]. Up to now, only a 1-D chained aluminophosphate [38], two 2-D layered phosphovandium structures [39], and some polymolybdated clusters [40] intercalated by 4,4′-bipy have been reported, whereas 3-D framework metalphosphates, like the title compound, with uncoordinated 4,4′-bipy residing in the channels is first described here. The possible reason may be that the F/In ratio in the reaction mixture is greater than unity (Section 2) and the fluorine atoms are all coordinated to a surplus of In sites, preventing 4,4′-bipy from coordinating to the metals. So, the large, rigid, as well as the lower charge density, monoprotonated 4,4′-bipy has to fill in the channels and forms the large pore structure as a template. It scatters in the center of the channel and the windows of the 14-rings with the two pyridyl rings twisted at an angle of 37.7°. The title compound presents the same solid fluorescence property as the 4,4′-bipy, attributing to the intraligand π–π∗ transition.

The ICP analysis for the title compound gives rise to an In/P of 9/14 with a novel stoichiometry. The elemental analysis and fluoride ion-selective electrode analysis indicate that the contents of C, N, F are 11.92, 2.99 and 2.01 wt%, respectively, in good agreement with the values (11.74, 2.74 and 1.86 wt%) based on the single-crystal structure formula In9(HPO4)14(H2O)6F3 · (C10N2H9)3 · (H3O) · (H2O)2.

The thermogravimetric analysis (Fig. 3) shows that the first mass loss of 1.90% below 200 °C is due to removal of lattice water molecules (calcd: 1.79%). On further heating, a two-step weigh loss was observed. The initial weight loss between 200 °C and 700 °C should correspond to the departure of 4,4′-bipy ligands. However, the observed weight loss (12.80%) was much lower than the expected value (calcd: 15.37%). The lower reduction in this stage was due to the retention of carbon in the solid residue (black in colour). The same phenomenon was observed in a GaPOs compound [41]. The second step, occurring between 750 °C and 950 °C with a weight loss of 14.50%, should be attributed to the removal of hydroxyls, structural water, HF, and carbon from the black solid residue. The total observed weight loss (27.50%) for three steps compared well with that calculated on the basis of the above interpretation (calcd: 26.74%). The XRD patterns of the post-residue corresponded to dense InPO4 (crystobalite form and JCPDS: 08-0052) together with an amorphous phase. The DTA curve exhibits four exothermic peaks corresponding to those behaviors.

4. Conclusion

The compound described here, with its unique rectangular 14-ring channel structure, is the first indium phosphate with stoichiometry of In/P = 9/14. The influences of 4,4′-bipy and fluorine ions suggest that the large, rigid, as well as lower charge density, organic amine and fluorine play significant roles for the formation of a compound with large pores and special structure. The nature of rigid octahedral indium atoms and the special linkage fashion between indium octahedra and phosphorous tetrahedra could lead to the chiral motifs of the structure, giving a facile approach to obtain chiral microporous materials. Further research concerning this subject is proceeding.
