Structural motif-dependent magnetic diversity observed in three-dimensional tetrazolyl-based MMOFs: synthesis, structures and magnetism†

Zhong-Yi Liu, Hong-Ai Zou, Zhao-Jun Hou, En-Cui Yang* and Xiao-Jun Zhao*
Key Laboratory of Inorganic-Organic Hybrid Functional Material Chemistry, Ministry of Education, Tianjin Key Laboratory of Structure and Performance for Functional Molecules, College of Chemistry, Tianjin Normal University, Tianjin 300387, P. R. China. E-mail: encui_yang@163.com; xiaojun_zhao15@163.com; Fax: +86-22-23766556

Received 11th August 2013, Accepted 2nd September 2013

First published on 2nd September 2013

Three extended three-dimensional (3D) magnetic metal–organic frameworks (MMOFs), {[Mn3(H2O)2(atz)4Cl2]·2CH3OH}n (1), {[Mn2(H2O)(atz)2(ip)]·C2H5OH}n (2) and {[Mn2(H2O)2(atz)(btc)]·3H2O·0.7CH3OH}n (3) (atz− = 5-aminotetrazolate, ip2− = isophthalate and btc3− = 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate), were respectively obtained by varying the coligands with different numbers of functionalities and molecular topology, and structurally and magnetically characterized. Complex 1 exhibits an eight-connected 36·418·53·6 topological network with linear MnII3 subunits periodically linked by ditopic atz− connectors. Complex 2 possesses a pillared-layer framework constructed from undulated {Mn2(atz)2} layers and bidirectional ip2− spacers. By contrast, 3 consists of bent one-dimensional (1D) {Mn(atz)} ribbons, which are crosslinked through the carboxylate groups of tripodal btc3− connectors to generate a 3D architecture. Structural analysis reveals that the interesting motifs of 1–3 and their diverse linkages are significantly dominated by the cooperate coordination of the mixed ligands to the octahedral MnII ion. Magnetically, 1 displays ferrimagnetic behavior resulting from the periodic arrangement of the net moment in the MnII3 subunit, which is scarcely observed in the homometallic azolate systems. Complex 2 features unusual coexistence of spin-canted antiferromagnetic behavior and field-induced spin-flop transition due to the asymmetric magnetic superexchange within the two-dimensional (2D) {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer. By contrast, only antiferromagnetic ordering is observed in the 1D {Mn(atz)} ribbon of 3. These interesting results suggest that the diverse structural motifs by the cooperate coordination of octahedral MnII ion with the N-rich tetrazolyl group can more significantly direct the magnetic behaviors and could be hopefully utilized upon the construction of novel MMOFs.

Introduction

Magnetic metal–organic frameworks (MMOFs) constructed from transition-metal ions and organic bridging mediators have always attracted intense interest and thus witnessed a rapid development due to their fascinating structural diversity and potential applications in information storage and quantum computing.1,2 In contrast to those typically linear magnetic mediators, five-membered N-heterocyclic azoles (such as imidazole, triazole and tetrazole) and their abundant derivatives are becoming one of promising candidates resulting from their short –NN– and three-atom –NCN– superexchange pathways for favorable antiferromagnetic couplings.3–15 In particular, with the increased numbers of endocyclic N donors, the selective coordination of the monocyclic azolate with spin carriers becomes more flexible and can show unexpected behavior,16–24 which can essentially increase the dimensionality of the resulting architecture for long-range magnetic ordering,5,6,16 induce the distorted triangular/tetrahedral magnetic lattice for spin frustration,25,26 as well as generate asymmetric magnetic pathways for spin canting.9,10,27,28 Moreover, with the aid of secondary mediators bearing abundant bonding characteristics and spin carriers with different magnetic nature (single-ion magnetic anisotropy, spin–orbit coupling, and spin-crossover transition), the structures and the resulting magnetism of the azolyl-based MMOFs can be skillfully fabricated by manipulating the preparation methods and conditions.7,29–31 For example, a series of butterfly-shaped/square MII4 and triangular/linear MII3 core (M = Cu, Mn, Co)-based three-dimensional (3D) frameworks with different inter-core linkages have been generated by introducing aromatic polycarboxylate coligands,27,29,30,32 in which the strength of the antiferromagnetic interactions synergically transmitted by the mixed azolyl and carboxylate heterobridges can be enhanced up to 206 cm−1. More interestingly, field-induced multiple-step magnetic phenomena such as spin-canting and spin-flop, metamagnetic and spin-glass, as well as metamagnetic and spin-flop behavior can be coexist in the same system by triggering the spin moment and heterobridges.6,9,28,33 Additionally, the coordinative side group of the mixed mediators in the ternary metal ion–azolate–carboxylate systems can be further utilized to generate aesthetically pleasing building units and variably polytopic connectors, leading to novel high-dimensional superstructures and interesting magnetic behavior.34 Continuing our interest along this line,5,9,22,24,31,35 in the present paper, 5-amino-1H-tetrazole (Hatz) and two coligands with different numbers of functionalities and molecular topology (isophthalic acid (H2ip) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3btc)) were selected as mixed heterobridges to self-assemble with anisotropic MnII ion. As a result, three 3D magnetic samples, an eight-connected 36·418·53·6 network with linear MnII3 cluster extended by ditopic atz− linkers, a pillared-layer framework constructed from undulate {Mn2(atz)2} layers and ip2− support, as well as a one-dimensional (1D) ribbon-based 3D architecture, were hydrothermally obtained and structurally and magnetically characterized. Magnetically, complex 1 exhibits ferrimagnetic behavior below 3.8 K resulting from the ordered alignment of the net moment by the MnII3 subunit in the 3D space and thus becomes an unusual magnetic entity in the azolate-containing homometallic systems. Complex 2 features uncommon coexistence of canted antiferromagnetic behavior below 2.7 K and field-induced spin-flop transition at 15.0 kOe due to the asymmetric magnetic superexchange within the wave-like {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer. By contrast, 3 features typically antiferromagnetic ordering mediated by double heterobridges. From the viewpoint of magnetostructural relationships, these interesting structural subunits in the 3D tetrazolate-based frameworks generated by the N-rich tetrazolate and isotropic spin carriers can more significantly direct the magnetic behaviors, and can thus be considered in the design and exploration of the novel MMOFs.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All initial reagents were commercially purchased (Hatz, H2ip and H3btc were from Acros and other analytical-grade reagents were from Tianjin Chemical Reagent Factory) and used without further purification. Doubly deionized water was used for the conventional synthesis. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were carried out with a CE-440 (Leeman-Laboratories) analyzer. Fourier transform (FT) IR spectra (KBr pellets) were taken on an Avatar-370 (Nicolet) spectrometer in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were performed on a Shimadzu simultaneous DTG-60A compositional analysis instrument from room temperature to 800 °C in a nitrogen atmosphere at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were obtained using a Bruker D8 ADVANCE diffractometer at 40 kV and 40 mA for Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å), with a scan speed of 0.1 s per step and a step size of 0.01° in 2θ. The simulated PXRD patterns were calculated using single-crystal X-ray diffraction data and processed by the free Mercury v1.4 program provided by the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center. Magnetic susceptibilities were acquired on a Quantum Design (SQUID) magnetometer MPMS-XL-7 with phase-pure crystalline samples. Diamagnetic corrections were calculated using Pascal's constants. The experimental correction for the sample holder was also applied.

X-Ray data collection and structure determination

Diffraction intensities for 1–3 were collected on a Bruker APEX-II QUAZAR diffractometer equipped with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation with a radiation wavelength of 0.71073 Å by using the φ–ω scan technique at 296 K for 1 and at 173 K for 2 and 3. There was no evidence of crystal decay during data collection. Semiempirical multiscan absorption corrections were applied by SADABS36 and the program SAINT37 was used for integration of the diffraction profiles. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXL-97 programs.38 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-H atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were geometrically generated. H atoms of the water molecule were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. The coordinated water molecule (O4) in 3 is disordered with the site occupancy refined to 0.65 and 0.35 for O4 and O4′, respectively. The site occupancy of the lattice methanol and one free water molecule (O6) is 0.35 and 0.5, respectively. No hydrogen atoms for the split water molecule were located. Results and discussion

Syntheses and FT-IR spectra

Complexes 1–3 were hydrothermally synthesized by use of high temperature of the reaction mixture (170 °C for both 1 and 2, and 190 °C for 3). The pH value of the reaction mixture, especially for the latter two complexes with di- and tricarboxylate-coligands, was carefully controlled by triethylamine. In the IR spectra, broad bands centered around 3400 cm−1 in 1–3 corresponding to the characteristic vibrations of O–H and N–H, suggesting the presence of water molecules and amino group of N-heterocyclic ligands. Compared with the free organic acid, an absence of a strong absorption at 1692 ± 1 cm−1 in 2 and 3 suggests the full deprotonation of the carboxylate-containing coligands. The characteristic bands for the asymmetric (νas) and symmetric (νs) stretching vibrations of the carboxylate group are observed at 1637, 1619, and 1437, 1388 cm−1 for 2 and at 1625, 1575, 1562, and 1442, 1375 cm−1 for 3. The separations (Δν = νas − νs = 182 cm−1 for 2 and 120 cm−1 for 3) are smaller than 200 cm−1, revealing a bidentate binding mode of the carboxylate group in 2 and 3.39 Thus, the IR spectra of the three complexes are in good agreement with those from single-crystal structural determinations.

Crystal structures of 1–3

{[Mn3(H2O)2(atz)4Cl2]·2CH3OH}n (1). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/n space group, exhibiting an eight-connected 36·418·53·6 topological framework with linear MnII3 clusters periodically extended by anionic atz− ligands. As shown in Fig. 1a, the MnII3 cluster in 1 is a dimer of two crystallographically asymmetric units composed of one-and-a-half crystallographically independent MnII ions, two unique atz− ligands with the same binding fashion, one bridging Cl− anion, and one terminally coordinated water molecule. Being locating at an inversion center, Mn1 ion is hexa-coordinated by two axial Cl− anions and four equatorial N atoms from two pairs of tetrazolyl groups of deprotonated atz− ligands, building axially elongated octahedral coordination surroundings (Table 2). In contrast, Mn2 has a distorted N4OCl octahedron defined by four different tetrazolyl groups of atz− ligands, one coordinated water molecule, and one bridging Cl− anion. The bond lengths of Mn–N, Mn–O and Mn–Cl are between 2.183(2) and 2.5758(8) Å (see Table 2), comparable with typical values in Mn(II)-containing complexes with azolyl and carboxylate ligands.27,31,34

The central Mn1 site aggregates two centrosymmetric Mn2 and Mn2A ions by a pair of μ-Cl− anions, leading to a linear MnII3 subunit with an intermetallic separation of 3.7505(5) Å. Both unique atz− anions in 1 adopt the uniform μ3-N1,N2,N4-binding mode to reinforce the adjacent MnII ions within the linear {Mn3Cl2} subunit through the N1 and N2 positions and also to coordinate with an octahedral MnII site belonging to adjacent subunits through the N4 donor, extending the trinuclear subunits into an infinite 3D framework of 1 (Fig. 1b). Topologically, each {Mn3Cl2} subunit in 1 is surrounded by eight neighbors through eight atz− connectors and can thus be considered as an eight-connected node. Instead, each atz− mediator in 1 holds two separate trinuclear subunits, behaving as a ditopic connector. Thus, the overall 3D architecture of 1 can be simplified as an eight-connected 36·418·53·6 topological structure (Fig. 1b). Notably, the nearest inter-subunit MnII⋯MnII distance is 6.2770(6) Å, considerably longer than that within the linear {Mn3Cl2} subunit and falls in the magnetically interacting range.

{[Mn2(H2O)(atz)2(ip)]·C2H5OH}n (2). Complex 2 features a 3D pillared-layer framework assembled from undulated {Mn2(atz)2} layers and doubly deprotonated ip2− pillars. The fundamental structural unit of 2 consists of two crystallographically unique MnII octahedra, two atz− ligands bearing two different coordination modes, one fully deprotonated ip2− anion, one terminally coordinated water, and one free ethanol molecule. As shown in Fig. 2a, both the unique MnII ions are in distorted octahedral coordination geometry. Mn1 is surrounded by three N donors belonging to three separate atz− ligands and three O atoms from one aqua ligand and two different carboxylate groups of two separate ip2− anions. Instead, the pseudo-octahedral Mn2 site is completed by four equatorial N donors from four symmetry-related atz− ligands and two axial carboxylate O atoms belonging to two different ip2− anions. The Mn–O and Mn–N distances fall in the normal range of 2.123(3)–2.323(3) Å (Table 3), and are comparable to reported values.4,6,31 Notably, two equatorial planes of the unique MnII octahedra form a dihedral angle of 37.912(7)°.

As illustrated in Fig. 2b, the anionic atz− ligands in 2 adopt tridentate μ3-N1,N2,N4- and quadridentate μ4-N1,N2,N3,N4-binding modes to coordinate with MnII ions, generating an undulate {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer in the crystallographic ab-plane. Alternatively, the wavy sublayer of 2 can also be considered as a μ4-N1,N2,N3,N4-atz− aggregated 1D broad ribbon interlinked by μ3-N1,N2,N4-atz− anions. Notably, the broad ribbon of 2 contains a two-fold screw axis symmetry and can potentially induce an asymmetric magnetic exchange for spin canting.6 Furthermore, each carboxylate group of the ip2− coligand affords a bidentate bridging mode to link two intralayer MnII ions and the adjacent layers are joined together by the phenyl backbone of ip2− anions. As a result, a 3D pillared-layer framework is generated with the nearest interlayer MnII⋯MnII distance of ca. 11.3 Å (Fig. 2c). Apparently, the undulate {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer becomes an important magnetic lattice of 2 due to the long interlayer distance, in which adjacent MnII ions are tightly enhanced by three-fold heterobridges (one μ-syn,syn-COO−, and two –NN– moieties of atz− ligands, Fig. S1†)

{[Mn2(H2O)2(atz)(btc)]·3H2O·0.7CH3OH}n (3). Complex 3 has a robust 3D framework with bent {Mn(atz)} ribbons crosslinked by tritopic btc3− ligands. The asymmetric unit of 3 contains one unique MnII ion, half an atz− ligand with two-fold rotation axis symmetry, half a btc3− anion bearing another two-fold rotation axis symmetry, one coordinated aqua ligand, 0.35 free methanol, as well as one-and-a-half lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 3a, the crystallographically sole MnII ion in 3 is hexa-coordinated in a N2O4 donor set defined by two separate atz− ligands, three carboxylate groups from three individual btc3− anions, and one terminally coordinated water molecule, displaying highly pseudo-octahedral coordination geometry. The Mn–N and Mn–O bond lengths are between 2.134(7) and 2.268(18) Å (see Table 4).

Symmetry codes: A = x − 0.5, 0.5 − y, 0.5 + z; B = 0.5 − x, −y, z; C = x, 0.5 − y, 1.5 − z; D = x, 0.5 − y, 0.5 − z; E = 0.5 − x, 1 − y, z). (b) 1D ribbon of 3 linked by atz− anion. (c) 3D framework of 3 extended by btc3− ligands.

The atz− ligand in 3 presents a μ4-N1,N2,N3,N4-binding mode to link four symmetry-related MnII ions into an infinite 1D ribbon running along the crystallographic b-axis (Fig. 3b). Notably, the 1D ribbon of 3 contains two-fold rotation axis symmetry although the linkage of the spin carriers and the atz− ligand is the same as that in 2, which can significantly induce the different magnetic behavior between 2 and 3. The separate ribbons of 3 are extended into a 3D framework by the carboxylate groups of fully deprotonated btc3− anion coordination with spin carriers from adjacent ribbons (Fig. 3c), in which the three carboxylate groups of btc3− anion are consistently in the bidentate bridging μ-syn,syn-COO− form and also contribute to the superexchange pathway of the spin carriers for favorable antiferromagnetic couplings (Fig. S2†). The inter-ribbon distance is ca. 8.0 Å, which is almost beyond the magnetically interacting range.

TGA and PXRD of 1–3

TGA experiments were conducted between room temperature and 800 °C in order to explore the compositional stability of 1–3 (Fig. S3†). The first weight-loss stage of 1 ranged from room temperature to 220 °C, corresponding to the removal of two free methanol and two coordinated water molecules (obs.: 13.8%, calc: 14.9%). The remaining substance did not lose any weight until 300 °C. The second obvious weight loss process was observed between 300 and 504 °C, ascribed to the decomposition of atz− and Cl− anions. The final residue of 1 was a mixture of MnO and MnO2 with a molar ratio of 1:2 (obs.: 35.5%, calc.: 36.4%). The TG curve of 2 revealed the first weight-loss process for the consecutive release of free ethanol and coordinated water molecules between 97 and 310 °C (obs.: 13.2%, calc.: 12.7%), and the extended pillared-layer structure of 2 was broken once the temperature was higher than 370 °C. The lattice water and methanol molecules in 3 were continuously released from room temperature to 112 °C (obs.: 14.7%, calc.: 14.9%). The bound aqua ligands were removed between 150 and 253 °C (obs.: 6.0%, calc.: 7.0%). The collapse of the 3D framework of 3 occurred above 371 °C, and was not completely finished at the highest temperature. The structural consistency and phase purity of the bulk products of 1–3 have also been evidenced by comparison of the experimental and computer-simulated PXRD patterns (Fig. S4†).

Magnetic properties

Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities for the crystalline samples of 1–3 were measured under an applied direct-current (dc) field of 1.0 kOe. As shown in Fig. 4a, the χMT value per MnII3 subunit of 1 is 12.96 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K, being close to the spin-only value (13.13 cm3 K mol−1) expected for three magnetically isolated MnII ions with S = 5/2 and g = 2.0. The χMT value decreases continuously with decreasing temperature and reaches a minimum value of 4.79 cm3 K mol−1 at 12.0 K, revealing that significant antiferromagnetic interactions transmitted by μ-Cl− and –NN– moieties of the atz− ligand occur in the nearest neighbors of 1. In fact, the bond angle Mn1–Cl1–Mn2 (93.80°) is larger than 90°, implying that the μ-Cl− bridges transfer ferromagnetic coupling within the MnII3 subunit.40 Furthermore, the ferromagnetic couplings by μ-Cl− mediators can be overcompensated by strong antiferromagnetic interactions through the –NN– moiety of the atz− ligand, and essentially contributes to the overall antiferromagnetic superexchange in 1. Upon further cooling, the χMT product abruptly increases to a maximum value of 17.64 cm3 K mol−1 at 3.5 K, and finally decreases to 14.19 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. Furthermore, the χMT value of 1 is field-dependent at low temperature (Fig. 4a, inset). The steep increase of χMT below 4.0 K under low external fields suggests an onset of a spontaneous magnetization state due to the ferrimagnetism or the spin canting of the antiferromagnetically coupled MnII ions. The final drop may be attributed to a saturation effect. The curve of χM−1vs. T above 20.0 K follows the Curie–Weiss law with Curie constant C = 14.23 cm3 K mol−1 and Weiss constant θ = −29.33 K (Fig. S5†).

(b) Field-dependent magnetization for 1 at 2.0 K and Brillouin function for S = 5/2. Inset: the hysteresis loop. (c) Real and imaginary ac magnetic susceptibilities in a zero applied dc field with an oscillating field of 3.5 Oe at different frequencies for 1.

Field-dependent magnetization of 1 performed at 2.0 K (Fig. 4b) showed a high gradient increase between 0 and 20.0 kOe and became saturated at 30.0 kOe. The saturated magnetization for per MnII3 subunit of 1 was 5.21Nβ at 70.0 kOe, being in agreement with an uncompensated MnII ion with S = 5/2 (5.00Nβ) per MnII3 unit. From the viewpoint of magnetostructural correlations, the uncompensated magnetic moment of 1 results from the antiferromagnetically coupled MnII ions within the linear MnII3 subunit, which causes the spontaneous magnetization at low temperature. The field dependence of the magnetization does not follow a Brillouin curve and shows a very small hysteresis loop at 2.0 K (Fig. 4b, inset). The residual magnetization and coercive field are close to zero, suggesting that 1 is a typically soft magnet because of the isotropic configuration of the spin carriers and highly ordered structure of 1.

A clear bifurcation of the field-cooled (FC)/zero-field-cooled (ZFC) curves of 1 at 3.8 K (Fig. S6†) suggests the occurrence of a long-range ferrimagnetic ordering below this temperature, which can also be confirmed by the alternating-current (ac) magnetic susceptibility measurement carried out in a zero applied dc field with a 3.5 Oe oscillating field at frequencies of 10, 100 and 997 Hz (Fig. 4c). Both the in-phase (χ′) and out-of-phase (χ′′) signals exhibit a peak at 3.8 K, indicating that the Néel temperature (TN) of 1 is 3.8 K. Hence, complex 1 is an unusual example of a 3D homometallic ferrimagnet. To the best of our knowledge, homometallic ferrimagnets with tetrazolate-based mixed ligands have been scarcely obtained,41,42 although a series of polyzolate-based antiferromagnets with weak spontaneous magnetization were previously reported.6,9,10,43,44 Additionally, the spin topology of 1 is also different from the previously reported spin-frustrated ferrimagnet [Mn8(atz)4(μ-OH)4(SO4)4(H2O)4]41 and the alternating AF–AF–FM chain in [Co3(OH)2(bdt)2(bpp)2(H2O)2]n (H2bdt = 5,5′-(1,4-phenylene)bis(1H-tetrazole), bpp = 1,3-bi(4-pyridyl)propane).42

Complex 2 displays both field- and temperature-dependent magnetic phenomena, suggesting the coexistence of spin-canted antiferromagnetic behavior at low temperature and the field-induced spin-flop transition at higher field. The curve of χM−1vs. T of 2 obeys the Curie–Weiss law in the whole temperature range, with C = 8.98 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −14.82 K (Fig. S7†). The negative θ value suggests dominant antiferromagnetic interactions are mediated in the nearest neighbors of 2 by mixed atz−–COO− heterobridges. As shown in Fig. 5a, the χMT value of 2 is 8.79 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K, consistent with the theoretical value (8.75 cm3 K mol−1) for two magnetically uncoupled MnII ions with S = 5/2 and g = 2.0. The χMT value of 2 decreases continuously with lowering temperature and reaches a value of 1.59 cm3 K mol−1 at 3.0 K. Upon further cooling, the χMT product increases slightly to a maximum value at 2.5 K and finally drops to 1.79 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. The behavior suggests a possible canted antiferromagnetism at low temperature, in which a predominantly antiferromagnetic phase possesses a small spontaneous magnetization due to a small deviation from a strictly antiparallel arrangement of the spins. The gradual decrease of χMT below 2.5 K is ascribed to a saturation effect or interlayer antiferromagnetic interactions. More interestingly, the plots of χMT vs. T are field-dependent below 3.0 K (Fig. 5b), consistent with spin canting at low temperatures and spin-flop at high field. Upon exposure to a magnetic filed lower than 2.0 kOe, the susceptibility increases as the external field decreases below 3.0 K, consistent with canted antiferromagnetic behavior. At 2.0 kOe, the canted antiferromagnetic interaction was dominated by a strong external field with the emergence of a broad peak around 2.5 K. Remarkably, when the strength of the external field further exceeds 10.0 kOe, the disappearance of the peak at ca. 2.5 K in the χM–T plot at a higher applied field suggests the occurrence of a field-induced spin-flop transition.45


Fig. 5 (a) Temperature dependence of χMT for 2. (b) Plots of χMvs. T at various dc fields for 2. (c) Field-dependent magnetization of 2 measured at 2.0 K (inset: the hysteresis loop). (d) Temperature dependence of real (χ′) and imaginary (χ′′) ac magnetic susceptibilities in a zero applied dc field with an oscillating field of 3.5 Oe at different frequencies for 2.

The coexistence of the spin-canted antiferromagnetism and the field-induced spin-flop transition of 2 can also be rationalized by the field-dependent magnetization measured at 2.0 K. As shown in Fig. 5c, a relatively rapid increase of the magnetization was observed at an external magnetic field lower than 0.6 kOe. Then, the magnetization increases almost linearly to the highest field measured at 70.0 kOe. The magnetization value is 1.10Nβ at the highest field, far below the saturation value (10.0Nβ) expected for two MnII ions, being in accordance with an antiferromagnet. The critical field for the spin-flop transition appears at about 15.0 kOe, as determined by the dM/dH derivative curve (Fig. S8†). At 2.0 K, sample 2 possesses a magnetic hysteresis loop with a coercive field of 80.0 Oe and a remnant magnetization (Mr) of 0.03Nβ. Thus, the canting angle φ is calculated to be about 0.17° based on the equation φ = tan−1(Mr/Ms).46

The divergence of the ZFC and FC magnetic susceptibility curves of 2 at ca. 2.7 K (Fig. S9†) confirms the onset of a weak ferromagnetic state as a result of spin canting in the present magnetic system. The value of TN is further determined as 2.7 K from the emergence of the maximum of χ′ and χ′′ measured at zero external magnetic field and a 3.5 Oe oscillating field at frequencies of 10, 100 and 997 Hz (Fig. 5d). No frequency dependence was observed, excluding the possibility of spin glass behavior of 2.

Structurally, the wave-like {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer of 2 is essentially responsible for the interesting magnetism of 2. The anisotropic Mn1 and Mn2 ions in 2 locate respectively at two orthogonal twofold screw axis parallel to the ab plane through μ-N1,N3-bridges. Thus, antisymmetric magnetic exchanges exist within the 2D magnetic layer, which are significantly responsible for the canted antiferromagnetism of 2 with isotropic spin carriers.30,47 By contrast, the field-induced spin-flop transition is due to the presence of distorted octahedral MnII ions. The spin carriers in the distorted octahedral field generate a zero-field splitting at low temperature,48–50 which causes weak single-ion anisotropy of the MnII ions and is responsible for the spin-flop transition of 2.

Complex 3 exhibits a typically antiferromagnetic behavior transmitted in the individual 1D {Mn(atz)} ribbon of 3. As shown in Fig. 6, the χMT product for 3 is 4.44 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K, being in accordance with the expected value (4.38 cm3 K mol−1) for magnetically non-interacting MnII ions with S = 5/2 and g = 2.0. The χMT value of 3 decreases continuously as the temperature decreases and reaches a value of 0.26 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. The appearance of a round peak in the χMvs. T curve indicates an antiferromagnetic ordering. The TN value is 3.0 K determined from the sharp peak of d(χMT)/dT. The temperature dependence of χM−1 obeys the Curie–Weiss law in the temperature range 20 to 300 K with C = 4.74 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −19.55 K (Fig. S10†). These features are indicative of antiferromagnetic couplings between the MnII ions mediated by the mixed μ-COO− and –NN– moieties of heterobridges in 3. No spontaneous magnetization can be observed, as concluded from the isothermal magnetization of 3 (Fig. S11†). The significant reason is that the {Mn(atz)} ribbon only contains a 2-fold rotation axis symmetry and cannot induce asymmetric magnetic exchange, although the 1D ribbon of 3 shares an analogous motif with one part of the {Mn2(atz)2} sheet in 2.

Conclusion

In summary, three extended MMOFs with antiferromagnetically coupled linear MnII3 subunit, wave-like {Mn2(atz)2} sublayer containing asymmetric superexchange pathways, and local {Mn(atz)} ribbon were created by varying the accessorial coligands in the coligand-assisted MnII-tetrazolate self-assembly systems. More significantly, regulated by the differently local motifs, these homometallic complexes exhibit unusual ferrimagnetic behavior, coexistence of spin-canting and spin-flop transition, as well as typically antiferromagnetic ordering. These interesting magnetostructural correlations suggest that the structural motifs constructed by the N-rich tetrazolate and spin carriers in the 3D extended frameworks can significantly influence the magnetic behaviors.
