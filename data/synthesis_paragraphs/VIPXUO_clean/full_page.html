Metal–organic frameworks constructed from mixed-ligand 1,2,3,4-tetra-(4-pyridyl)-butane and benzene-polycarboxylate acids: syntheses, structures and physical properties†‡

Jian-Guo Lin , Shuang-Quan Zang , Zhen-Fang Tian , Yi-Zhi Li , Yan-Yan Xu , Hui-Zhen Zhu and Qing-Jin Meng *
Coordination Chemistry Institute, State Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing University, Nanjing, P. R. China 210093. E-mail: mengqj@nju.edu.cn; Fax: +86-25-83314502; Tel: +86-25-83597266

Received 4th June 2007 , Accepted 12th July 2007

First published on 30th July 2007

Three novel coordination polymers, [Mn(TPB)0.5(IPA)(H2O)2]n (1), [Zn1.5(TPB)0.5(TMA)(H2O)·(H2O)]n (2), and [Co(TPB)0.5(PMA)0.5(H2O)2·(H2O)]n (3) (TPB = 1,2,3,4-tetra-(4-pyridyl)-butane; H2IPA = isophthalic acid; H3TMA = trimesic acid; and H4PMA = pyromellitic acid), have been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions and structurally characterized. Complex 1 features a 2D infinite layer framework consisting of bi-manganese clusters, which is further stacked along the [11] direction via strong interlayer π⋯π interactions to form a 3D supramolecular architecture. Complex 2 has a 3D complicated framework comprised of two kinds of infinite 1D chains. The 3D framework of complex 3 possesses three topologically nonequivalent 4-connected nodes synchronously and exhibits an unprecedented (64·102)(42·84)(4·65)2 topology. The magnetic susceptibility data of complex 1 show strong antiferromagnetic interactions in the dinuclear Mn(II) units. Complex 2 exhibits strong fluorescent emissions in the solid state at room temperature and may be suitable as a candidate of blue-fluorescent material.

Introduction

Over the past few years, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have received a wide range of attention in the field of supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering, owing to their intriguing aesthetic structures and topological features as well as their potential applications in host–guest chemistry, magnetic materials, ion exchange, optical devices, etc.1–3

Compared to linear 4,4′-bipyridine and 4,4′-bipyridine-like N,N′-donor ligands,4–7 the four N-donors ligand 1,2,3,4-tetra-(4-pyridyl)-butane (TPB) (Chart 1) is less frequently used in the assembly of metal–organic frameworks. However, TPB may act as a promising ligand because of its moderate length, flexibility and several discrete metal-binding sites, which can probably be used for obtaining unexpected, unpredictable and interesting structures. On the other hand, the multi-carboxylate ligands with complicated configurations, such as isophthalic acid (H2IPA), trimesic acid (H3TMA) and pyromellitic acid (H4PMA) (see Chart 1), are also good candidates of ligands in making high-dimensional networks. With the aim of preparing novel materials with interesting structural topologies and physicochemical properties, we chose TPB bearing multifunctional groups along with benzene-polycarboxylic acids (H2IPA, H3TMA or H4PMA) to elaborate new metal coordination polymers. In the present paper, three high-dimensional coordination polymeric complexes, [Mn(TPB)0.5(IPA)(H2O)2]n (1), [Zn1.5(TPB)0.5(TMA)(H2O)·(H2O)]n (2), and [Co(TPB)0.5(PMA)0.5(H2O)2·(H2O)]n (3), are reported.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All analytical reagents were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. 2,3,4,5-Tetra-(4-pyridyl)-thiophene was synthesized as reported8 and then reductively desulfurated to get 1,2,3,4-tetra-(4-pyridyl)-butane.9 C, H, and N microanalyses were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. The IR spectra were recorded on KBr discs on a Bruker Vector 22 spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Thermogravimetric analyses were performed on a simultaneous SDT 2960 thermal analyzer under flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 between ambient temperature and 720 °C. Magnetic susceptibility data on the powder samples were collected over the temperature range of 1.8–300 K by using a Quantum Design MPMS7 superconducting quantum interference device magnetometer. Luminescence spectra for the solid sample were recorded with a Hitachi 850 fluorescence spectrophotometer.

X-Ray crystallography

Single crystals of approximate dimensions for complexes 1–3 were used for structural determinations on a Bruker SMART APEX CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature using the ω-scan technique. Lorentz polarization and absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXS-9710 and SHELXL-97 programs.11 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. Analytical expressions of neutral-atom scattering factors were employed, and anomalous dispersion corrections were incorporated. The crystallographic data and selected bond lengths for 1–3 are listed in Tables 1 and 2. More details on crystallographic data for the structures reported in this paper have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre with CCDC reference numbers 646586–646588 for 1–3.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of 1

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis shows that complex 1 crystallizes in the P21/n space group and has a 2D structure. The Mn1 atom exhibits a slightly distorted octahedral geometry where the angles of the coordinated N and O atoms subtended at the center Mn(II) atom vary from 82.04 to 175.36°. As shown in Fig. 1a, two oxygen atoms (O1, O2#1) from different IPA anions, N1 from TPB and one coordinated aqua molecule (O6) occupy the equatorial positions; N2#2 from the TPB and O5 of a coordinated aqua molecule occupy the axial sites of the Mn(II) ion. In 1, the Mn–O bond lengths range between 2.1026(15) and 2.3242(16) Å, while the Mn–N bond lengths vary from 2.2601(17) to 2.2817(17) Å, which are in the normal ranges.12
Fig. 1 (a) Perspective view of the coordination environment of the Mn ion in complex 1 (#1 −x, 1 − y, 1 − z; #2 1 − x, 1 − y, −z); (b) polyhedral representation of the 2D MOF of 1 (the guest water molecules and all hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity), one binuclear unit is highlighted in a magenta color; (c) π–π interactions between 2D layers.

A polyhedral representation of this compound is displayed in Fig. 1b. The green polyhedra correspond to the manganese ions coordinated to two nitrogen atoms of TPB and two oxygen atoms of IPA. The Mn(II) atoms related by a centered symmetry are bridged by the carboxylate groups of two IPA anions into a binuclear unit with the Mn⋯Mn separation of 4.712 Å. The TPB ligand is used as a four-connector to join four manganese ions from four different dinuclear manganese units to form a 2D layer structure. Here, the dihedral angle between the two planes of the pyridyl rings is 67.473° and the long and short distances between the adjacent N donors in the TPB are 8.4066 and 6.2884 Å, respectively (see Fig. S1 of the ESI†). Furthermore, the 2D planes were stacked along the [11] direction by the π⋯π interactions. The interacting phenyl rings at different sheets are offset, with a centroid–centroid contact of ca. 3.881 Å.13 (Fig. 1c)

Crystal structure of 2

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis shows that complex 2 crystallizes in the C2/c space group and has a complicated 3D network. As depicted in Fig. 2a and Fig. S2 of the ESI,† there exist two types of coordination environments around the zinc ions in the crystal structure: Zn1 has a distorted trigonal bipyramidal coordination sphere which is defined by one TPB nitrogen atom and one carboxylate oxygen occupying the apical positions, while the trigonal basal plane is completed by two carboxylate oxygen atoms and one aqua molecule; the Zn2 center adopts a distorted octahedral coordination environment which is surrounded by four carboxylate oxygens, as well as two nitrogen donors from different TPB ligands.
Fig. 2 (a) A perspective view of the coordination environments of the Zn ions in complex 2 (#1 x, 2 − y, 0.5 + z; #2 −x, y, −0.5 − z; #3 0.5 − x, −0.5 + y, 0.5 − z; #4 −0.5 + x, −0.5 + y, −1 + z); (b) view of the 1D channel formed by zinc centers and TMA anions along the c-axis; (c) overall view of the 3D hybrid architecture (TPB ligands are colored as yellow and the channels are shown in different colors); (d) view of connected modes of TPB and zinc centers (Zn1 and Zn2).

In 2, zinc centers (Zn1 and Zn2) are firstly bridged by the TMA anions to form 1D channels along the c-axis (Fig. 2b). Each TMA anion coordinates to three Zn(II) atoms. Two carboxylate groups of the TMA coordinate to the Zn(II) atoms via chelating bidentate modes, while the third is a monodentated one. Then, the adjacent but independent 1D channels are crossly connected by the “second” ligand of TPB molecules to afford a complicated 3D framework (Fig. 2c). The detailed connected process is depicted in Fig. S3 of the ESI.† Here, TPB acts as the tetradentate ligands to bridge the channels along the c-axis and the [212] direction, respectively. It should be noted that the N2 atoms of TPB ligands coordinate to the Zn2 atoms of different channels to form a 1D chain along the c-axis (see Fig. 2d). The shortest Zn2⋯Zn2 separation is 15.18 Å in the chain. The dihedral angle between the two planes of the pyridyl rings of TPB is 53.014°, the long and short distances between the adjacent N atoms of the TPB are 8.7057 and 5.5820 Å respectively.

Crystal structure of 3

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis shows that complex 3 crystallizes in the P2(1)/n space group and has a 3D MOF constructed from tetradentate ligands TPB and PMA. As depicted in Fig. 3a, the Co(II) ion is in a distorted octahedral coordination sphere, which is defined by one TPB nitrogen donor and one aqua oxygen atom occupying the axial positions. While the equatorial positions are furnished by one TPB nitrogen atom, two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two different PMA ligands and one aqua oxygen atom.
Fig. 3 (a) Perspective view of the coordination environment of the Co ion in complex 3 (#1 −1 + x, y, z; #2 −0.5 − x, −0.5 + y, 1.5 − z); (b) view of the 4 + 4 grids of {Co4PMA2}n along the bc plane; (c) overall view of the 3D structure; (d) scheme representation of the 3D framework.

All carboxylic groups of the organic ligand H4PMA in 3 are deprotonated, as shown in the IR spectrum, where no absorption peak around 1710 cm−1 for a protonated carboxylic group is observed. Two kinds of carboxylate groups (O1–C1–O2 and O3–C4–O4) on each PMA ligand are twisted out of the plane of the phenyl ring. The dihedral angles between the carboxylato groups and the phenyl ring are 72.1 and 21.9°, respectively. If we neglect the TPB ligands, a grid structure along the ab plane (Fig. 3b) is observed in 3: each PMA ligand links four Co(II) ions and each Co(II) ion attaches to two PMA ligands to form a 2D layer, which consists of 4 + 4 grids14 with a size of 10.95 × 11.75 Å (defined by the separation between two opposite Co centers); the phenyl rings stand at the corners while the Co(II) atoms stand in the sides of the grids. These layers are further linked by the cross-arranged TPB bridges to give an overall complicated 3D network (Fig. 3c). Notably, the dihedral angle between the planes of the pyridyl rings of TPB is 52.936°, the long and short distances between the adjacent N atoms of the TPB are 8.7126 and 5.8459 Å, respectively, which is just appropriate to coordinate to the two adjacent Co centers of the grid (Co⋯Co distance is 11.75 Å). Another two N-donor atoms of TPB link two Co centers of the neighboring layer with a separation between the two layers of 8.69 Å. As can be seen from Table 2, the coordinated distances of Co–N are 2.138(3) and 2.145(3) Å, respectively, which are comparable to the literature values.15

Here, the Co centers can be considered as nodes, both TPB and PMA ligands are 4-connected nodes. The framework can be assigned as a very unusual network with a Schläfli symbol of (64·102)(42·84)(4·65)2 (see Fig. 3d). Traditionally, the frameworks constructed from four-connected tetrahedral nodes display diamond or diamond-related nets.16 In complex 3, there exist three topologically nonequivalent 4-connected points with unusual configurations. We are unaware of a precedent despite the large number of 4-connected networks reported in the literature. As far as we can know, the structure of 3 represents the first example in a class of coordination framework that exhibits a topology of (64·102)(42·84)(4·65)2.

Thermal analyses

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) has been used to check the phase purity of the bulky samples in the solid state. For complexes 1–3, the measured PXRD patterns closely match the simulated patterns generated from the results of single-crystal diffraction data (see ESI,† Fig. S4), indicative of pure products. Complexes 1, 2, and 3 are stable at ambient conditions, and thermogravimetric experiments were performed to explore their thermal stabilities. As depicted in Fig. S5 of the ESI,† the weight loss of 7.67% from 176 to 300 °C (calcd 8.21%) of 1 corresponds to the loss of two coordinated water molecules per formula and consecutive decompositions suggest the total destruction of the framework. For 2, the first weight loss occurs in the range 92 to 117 °C, which is attributed to the departure of both the lattice and coordinated water molecules (obsd 5.21%, calcd 5.15%), followed by a plateau region ranging from 117 to 360 °C, and consecutive decompositions suggest the total destruction of the framework. The framework is very stable in the long temperature range (between 117 and 360 °C) after removing coordinated water molecules and free water molecules, which may originate from the intertwist of the two kinds of ligands to form the dense 3D structure. For 3, the consecutive decompositions below 334 °C are assigned to the liberation of free and coordinated water molecules (obsd 12.44%, calcd 12.81%). Then the consecutive decompositions also suggest the total destruction of the framework

Magnetic properties

The magnetic properties of 1 were investigated over the temperature range 1.8–300.0 K. The χMT value at 300 K is 9.10 cm3 K mol−1, which is close to the expected value (8.75 cm3 K mol−1) of two uncorrelated spins of MnII ions (S = 5/2, g = 2.0). As T decreases, the χMT decreases continuously to a value of 0.867 cm3 K mol−1 at 1.8 K indicating that there is a dominant intermetallic antiferromagnetic interaction in complex 1. The χM value increases continuously on cooling to reach a maximum value of 0.5295 cm3 mol−1 at 5.0 K and decreases to a value of 0.4832 cm3 mol−1 at 1.8 K as shown in Fig. 4. The plot of 1/χMversusT for 1 is consistent with the Curie–Weiss law in the temperature range of 7–300 K, with C = 9.36 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −7.49 K. The large negative θ value indicates that there exist extremely strong antiferromagnetic interactions between the Mn(II) centers. From the structure information, the shortest Mn⋯Mn distances are 4.71 Å bridged through the syn–syn bridging fashion of two carboxylates, the observed antiferromagnetic interaction possibly arises from the magnetic superexchange through the carboxylate bridges.17 The phenomenon we have found for complex 1 is in good agreement with that found in other similar carboxylate bridged Mn(II) complexes.18

Luminescent properties

The luminescent spectra of 2 in the solid state at room temperature were investigated, as depicted in Fig. 5. Upon excitation at ca. 386 nm, a strong blue fluorescent emission band at ca. 436 nm was observed in complex 2. A similar emission band at about 430 nm has also been observed previously for other zinc–organic polymer compounds19 with either photoluminescent or nonphotoluminescent ligands. These emissions are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature since the Zn2+ ions are difficult to oxidize or to reduce due to their d10 configuration, which can probably be assigned to the intraligand (π–π*) fluorescent emission because similar emissions are observed at 387 nm for the free H3TMA ligand.20 Compared with the emissions of the free ligands, the enhancement of those in complex 2 may be ascribed to the increase of the ligand conformational rigidity, which is due to their coordination to Zn(II) ions resulting in a decrease in the nonradiative decay of intraligand excited states.21 The complex may be one potential candidate of the thermally stable and solvent-resistant blue fluorescent materials.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully isolated three new coordination polymers constructed from TPB and benzene-polycarboxylate acids. All complexes have been characterized by elemental analysis, FT-IR spectra, thermal analysis, and single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies. Interestingly, TPB acts as a perfect ligand in the self-assembly because of its moderate length, flexibility and four discrete metal-binding sites. As can be seen, TPB is centrosymmetrical and has four N-donor atoms in a plane. The dihedral angles between the planes of the pyridyl rings of the TPB ligands decrease in complexes 1–3 (67.473° (1), 53.014° (2), and 52.936° (3), respectively). The short distances between two N donors of one TPB ligand are decreased in the order of 6.2884 Å (1) > 6.0322 Å (2) > 5.8459 Å (3), while the long distances increase in the order of 8.4066 Å (1) < 8.4769 Å (2) < 8.7126 Å (3). The self-adjusted angles and lengths of the tetradentate ligand do play a dominant role in the construction of these crystalline materials. The new design idea depicted in this paper may be a promising technique for the construction of other intriguing organic–inorganic hybrid coordination polymers, and further efforts on this perspective are underway.
