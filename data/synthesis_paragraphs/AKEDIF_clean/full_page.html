    Abstract

    The degree of interpenetration is known to influence the gas sorption, catalytic, magnetic and nonlinear optical properties, chirality, and sensing of various molecules but not the solid-state [2 + 2] photocycloaddition reaction. In our previous studies of a solvothermal reaction using dimethylacetamide (DMA) as one of the solvents, a photoreactive 6-fold interpenetrated metal–organic framework with dia topology, [Zn(bpeb)(bdc)] (1) [bpeb = 1,4-bis[2-(4′-pyridyl)ethenyl]benzene; bdc = 1,4-benzenecarboxylate], was isolated. Because of the slip-stacked alignment of a dipyridyl ligand with two conjugated olefin bonds, the [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction occurs under UV light leading to the formation of an organic polymer ligand fused with a coordination polymer, 2. On the contrary, under similar conditions when diethylformamide was used instead of DMA, a 5-fold interpenetrated structure, 3, with the same dia topology was obtained in this work. This has been found to be photostable as also predicted from the analysis of the solid state structure. All the solids show interesting photoluminescence properties, and the emissions were preferentially quenched by curcumin to make these materials as potentially useful sensing agents.

    Synopsis

    Two interpenetrated dia metal−organic frameworks exhibit contrast photoreactivities due to different alignments of adjacent olefin bonds of the spacer ligands but show similar photoluminescence and sensing properties toward small molecules.

    Interpenetration is a common occurrence observed in metal–organic framework (MOF) structures and coordination polymers (CPs).(1-5) This unpredictable phenomenon can greatly modify the pore shape and size, surface area, and hence their gas, catalytic, optical, and magnetic properties.(4-6) For a given spacer ligand and metal ion, the degree of interpenetration can be varied by experimental conditions such as temperature, concentration, and nature of solvents. Zaworotko et al., for example, found that high temperature and concentration favor a 2-fold interpenetrated pillared-layer structure, [Cd(4,4′-bpy) (bdc)] (4,4′-bpy = 4,4′-bipyridyl and bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate), whereas low temperature and concentration induce its non-interpenetrated form.(7)
    The interpenetration can be varied from 3- to 5-fold by varying the amide based solvents used in solvothermal synthesis such as diethylformamide (DEF), dimethylformamide (DMF), and formamide (FAM) in the diamondoid MOF.(8) Of these 3- and 4-fold structures show gas uptake properties, while the 5-fold interpenetrated structure has been found to be nonporous. Liquid-phase epitaxial growth on a two-dimensional (2D) template organic surface has been successfully used to prevent interpenetration,(9) whereas the sonochemical method can control the catenation.(10) The influence of the degree of interpenetration on gas sorption properties,(4-6) chirality,(11, 12) nonlinear optical (NLO) properties,(13-15) and sensing of ammonia(16) and nitrobenzene(17) have been explored before.
    Photoreactivity in organic polymorphs may be traced back to Schmidt’s classical papers on cinnamic acids,(18, 19) while the solid-state polymerization of one of the two different forms of distyrylpyridine via the [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction by Hasegawa(20) highlighted the importance of the relative packing of the adjacent organic molecules in controlling the solid state reactions. Recently two “polymorphs” of a MOF which differ only by the nature of guest solvents in the channels show two different photoreactivities. One with methanol in the channel was found to be photoinert, whereas the channel occupied by water was photoreactive and underwent photopolymerization via a [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction.(21) Further, fluorine substitution at the backbone of the ligand was found to change its photoreactivity, while the crystal packing was little affected in an isostructural metal complex containing the ligand with no fluorine substitution.(22)
    The photoreactivity and sensing properties have been investigated in two distortional supramolecular isomers.(23) The relative orientation of the olefin pairs in a conjugated spacer ligand (bpeb = 1,4-bis[2-(4′-pyridyl)ethenyl]benzene, Scheme 1) with an “out-of-phase” orientation responsible for this type of isomer also found to control the photoreactivity between them. Such orientations between the olefin bonds can also occur in different interpenetrated structures. Surprisingly, the influence of interpenetration on the solid-state photoreactivity has not been investigated probably due to the lack of suitable systems for investigation.
    By changing the solvent to DEF in the solvothermal synthesis, we obtained a 5-fold interpenetration, which is different from the previously known photoreactive MOF with 6-fold interpenetrated diamondoid structure obtained from DMA solvent.(24) This new structure provides an opportunity to investigate and compare the solid-state photoreactivity arising from [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction as well as the sensing of some commonly available pharmaceutical drugs and nitro-aromatic compounds. The results are discussed below.

    As mentioned earlier, we have reported a 6-fold interpenetrated diamondoid MOF, [Zn(bpeb)(bdc)] (1), obtained from the solvothermal reaction of Zn(NO3)2·4H2O, H2bdc, and bpeb (Scheme 1) in a mixture of DMA, dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), and water at 100 °C (Figure 1a).(24) This has been found to undergo solid-state photopolymerization via [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction to form a metal–organopolymeric framework (MOPF) structure, 2, due to a slip-stacked arrangement of adjacent bpeb ligands (Figure 1b).
    Under similar experimental conditions when DEF was used instead of DMA, pale yellow block crystals of [Zn(bpeb)(bdc)]·2DEF (3) suitable for single crystal X-ray diffraction (SC-XRD) studies were obtained. SC-XRD studies revealed that 3 crystallized in the monoclinic space group C2/c with Z = 4. The asymmetric unit contains half of the formula unit, in which Zn1 sits on the crystallographic 2-fold axis. The Zn1 with a highly distorted tetrahedral geometry (101.95(5)–121.1(6)°) is coordinated to two N atoms of the bpeb spacer ligands (Zn1–N1 2.034(1) Å) and two O atoms of the bdc ligands (Zn1–O1 1.961(1) Å) (Figure 2a). The Zn1···O2 distance, 2.733(5) Å, can be considered as nonbonded. The Zn(bdc) zigzag chains are propagated along the c-axis, while Zn(bpeb) zigzag chains are orthogonal to it. The crystallographic inversion centers in the middle of bdc and bpeb ligands generate a 3D infinite framework with dia topology (Figure 2c). Despite 5-fold interpenetration, 3 has large porous channels parallel to the b-axis with a calculated solvent accessible void of 1669.2 Å3, which is 45.3% of the total volume.(25, 26) The voids are occupied by the guest DEF molecules (Figure S6). Unfortunately 3 has not been found to be suitable for gas sorption studies as the desolvated solid did not yield similar powder XRD patterns.
    The structure of 3 is lesser interpenetrated than 1 probably due to the presence of bulkier DEF solvent as compared to DMA in 1. This is also reflected in the more ideal tetrahedral N–Zn–N angle of 109.85(8)° as compared to 93.1(3)° in 1 (Figure S7). Such angular distortions have been found to be associated with the degree and nature of interpenetration before.(7, 8, 15, 16) In the diamondoid structures containing asymmetric spacer ligands, the odd number of interpenetration is expected to provide non-centrosymmetric crystals showing nonlinear optical properties properties.(14-16) However, due to symmetric spacer ligands employed in 3, the compound crystallized in the centrosymmetric space group C2/c.
    Further, the relative orientations of the adjacent bpeb ligands in 3 is different from 1 where slip-stacked alignment of the bpeb ligands makes 1 not only photoreactive, but also the [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction led to the formation of organic polymeric ligand inside the MOF in a single-crystal-to-single-crystal (SCSC) manner as illustrated in Figure 1b. In 3, the bpeb ligands are aligned in a face-to-face manner, but the distance between the adjacent C═C bond pairs (5.89 Å) are too far away to satisfy the Schmidt’s criteria to be photoreactive (Figure 2d).(18, 27) Indeed, 3 was experimentally found to be photoinactive under UV light (Figure S9a). The degree of interpenetration is reduced from six in 1 to five in 3 to accommodate bulkier DEF, and this has altered the relative orientations and distances between the adjacent bpeb ligands to transform photoreactive crystals of 1 to photoinert crystals of 3.
    It was thought that if the guest DEF molecules are removed from 3, it might cause the relative positions of the bpeb ligands to be shifted so that the olefin pairs become closer (Figure 2d). Such molecular movements and subsequent reorientations of the ligands due to the perturbation caused by the loss of the guest molecules have been amply demonstrated in CPs and MOFs before.(21, 29-36) In order to test this hypothesis, crystals of 3 were soaked in ethanol to exchange with DEF molecules in 3. The removal of DEF was confirmed by 1H NMR data (Figure S9a,b), but the isolated pale yellow solid (denoted as 4) lost single crystallinity. As a result, the solid-state structure of 4 could not be determined unequivocally, but the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern has changed in this solvent exchange process (Figure S2).
    The photoreactivity of 4 was examined under UV light for 2 days, and the 1H NMR spectra of the irradiated 4 (denoted as 5) showed that ∼67% of the olefin peaks at 7.66 and 8.05 ppm in bpeb have disappeared. The resultant formation of the cyclobutane ring was confirmed by the appearance of the peak at 4.65 ppm (Figure S9c). From these 1H NMR data, it may be speculated that partial sliding of bpeb ligands indeed occurred after the exchange of the bulky DEF ligands, and the bpeb ligands come closer to undergo photoreactivity. Such realignments of bpeb spacer ligands can give rise to partial photoproducts as noted before.(28)
    Since a number of MOFs shows interesting photoluminescent (PL) properties,(37-41) some of them have been used to sense and detect the guest molecules including nitro-containing explosives, carcinogens, pharmaceuticals, and toxic chemicals.(42-49) Previously, structurally similar MOFs exhibit different sensing of several nitro compounds and pharamaceuticals.(21, 23) Since 1 and 3 have dia topology with two different interpenetrations, here we have investigated the PL and sensing properties of 1–3 (Figures S12–S16).
    The solid-state PL spectra of 1–5 upon excitation at 360 nm show different λmax at 473 (1),(24) 444 (2),(24) 495 (3), 480 (4), and 472 (5) nm probably due to the presence of different guest solvent molecules in the lattice.(45-48) However, when they were dispersed in DMF, they exhibit emission in the green region (λmax = 420–430 nm) due to exchange of the solvents by DMF (Figure S11). Influence of guests on the emissive property has been well-documented.(37-49)
    Some of the commonly available pharmaceutical drug molecules and nitro aromatic compounds have been used to study their ability to quench the photoemission of 1–3. Of the 18 different pharmaceutical compounds tested, only curcumin exhibits a remarkable ability to reduce the PL intensities of 1–3 drastically, whereas no significant intensity change was observed upon addition of other pharmaceuticals tested. The results for some selected pharmaceutical compounds are shown in Figure 3. In a typical titration experiment shown in Figure 4, stepwise addition of curcumin to the dispersed DMF solution of 3 resulted in the 413 nm emission to decrease gradually, whereas the ∼500 nm emission gradually increased to give an isosbestic point at 500 nm.(49, 50) The observed results might be associated with the interaction between 3 and curcumin molecule via hydrogen bonds.(51) Similar results were also obtained for 4 and 5 (Figures S12f and S13f). Accordingly, 3 is able to detect the presence of curcumin up to the 2 ppm level in the dispersed DMF solution (Figure S14s). Surprisingly, there is not much difference in their detection ability that can be attributed due to the difference in the degree of interpenetration or other structural variations among 1–3.
    The detection ability of 1–3 has been investigated for 12 aromatic nitro compounds (see Supporting Information). The intensity of the PL emission of 1–3 can be drastically reduced by the nitro compounds such as 2,4-dintrophenol, 2,4-nitrophenylhydrazine, and 2,4-dinitroanaline, while to some extent by 4-chloro-7-nitrobenzofurazan, 2,5-dintrophenol, 4-nitrophenylhydrazine, p-benzoquinone, and 4-chloro-7-nitro-2,1,3-benzoxadiazole. However, 1–3 show sensing behavior similar to all the analytes examined. The difference in the quenching ability of 4-chloro-7-nitro-2,1,3-benzoxadiazole is in the order, 2 > 3 > 1 despite the very low quenching ability of this analyte. The exact reason for this behavior is yet to be established.

    The solid-state [2 + 2] cycloaddition reaction between a pair of olefin bonds in organic molecules, metal complexes, coordination polymers, and MOFs depends on the crystal packing. Schmidt’s work on cinnamic acid highlights the influence of polymorphs on the photoreactivity.(18, 27) Supramolecular isomers have also been found to control such photoreactivity.(23) In this work two similar MOFs with dia topology have 5- and 6-fold interpenetrations. The presence of double bonds in the spacer ligand provided a rare opportunity to investigate the influence of the degree of interpenetration on their photoreactivity. The 6-fold interpenetrated 1 was found to undergo [2 + 2] cycloaddition leading to polymerization.(24) On the contrary, the face-to-face aligned bpeb ligands are too far apart to be photoreactive in the 5-fold interpenetrated 3. The removal of DEF guest molecule in 3 brought the olefin pairs partially closely making it photoreactive. Unfortunately, the structures 4 and 5 are not known. The three compounds 1–3 are photoluminescent in the solid state and exhibit the same behavior in DMF dispersed solution. The quenching of the emissions of 1–3 was investigated by various commonly available pharmaceutical drugs and several nitro aromatic compounds. Preferential quenching of PL emissions especially by curcumin makes these materials as potentially useful sensing agents.

    General

    All chemicals were purchased from commercial sources and used as received. All solvents used were of reagent grade. The bpeb ligand was synthesized by the reported procedure.(52, 53) Elemental analyses were carried out on a LECO CHNS-932 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses were recorded in a TA Instruments TGA-Q50 thermogravimetric analyzer. Samples were heated at a constant rate of 5 °C min–1 from room temperature to 650 °C and at a continuous flow nitrogen atmosphere. The FT-IR spectra were recorded using Thermo Fisher Scientific Nicolet iS 10 FT-IR spectrometer with KBr pellets.

    Preparation of [Zn(bpeb)(bdc)]·1.4DEF (3)

    A mixture of bpeb (20.1 mg, 0.070 mmol), H2bdc (11.6 mg, 0.070 mmol), and Zn(NO3)2·4H2O (18.4 mg, 0.070 mmol) dissolved in DEF (3 mL), H2O (1 mL), and DMSO (0.5 mL) was placed in a 10 mL glass tube, and 3–4 drops of 0.1 M NaOH was added. The tube was sealed and kept at 110 °C for 48 h, followed by cooling to room temperature over 8 h. Yellow block-shaped crystals of 1 suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained. Yield: 72%. Anal.     X-ray Crystallographic Analysis

    Single crystals suitable for data collection were chosen under an optical microscope, mounted on glass fiber, and frozen under a stream of cryogenic nitrogen gas before data collection. Intensity data were collected on a Bruker SMART APEX II ULTRA diffractometer attached with a CCD detector and graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. The cell parameters for the compounds were obtained from a least-squares refinement of the spot (from 36 collected frames). Data collection, data reduction, and semiempirical absorption correction were carried out using the software package of APEX2.(54) All of the calculations for the structure determination were carried out using the SHELXTL package.(55) Relevant crystal data collection and refinement data for the crystal structure of 3 is summarized in Table S1.
