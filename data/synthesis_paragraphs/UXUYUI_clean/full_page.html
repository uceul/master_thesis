A zeolitic porous lithium–organic framework constructed from cubane clusters†

Xiang Zhao a, Tao Wu a, Shou-Tian Zheng b, Le Wang a, Xianhui Bu b and Pingyun Feng *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, University of California, Riverside, CA 92521, USA. E-mail: pingyun.feng@ucr.edu; Fax: +1 951-827-4713; Tel: +1 951-827-2042
bDepartment of Chemistry and Biochemistry, California State University, 1250 Bellflower Blvd., Long Beach, CA 90840, USA. E-mail: xbu@csulb.edu; Fax: +1 562-985-8557; Tel: +1 562-985-4843

Received 2nd March 2011 , Accepted 14th March 2011

First published on 7th April 2011

Reported here is a lithium cubane based zeolitic framework possessing a multi-dimensional channel system. The unique design strategy of adopting the ditopic ligand 4-pyridinol leads to a rigid porous framework with high thermal stability. It has a BET surface area of 440.3 m2 g−1 and a H2 uptake capacity of 108.7 cm3 g−1 at 77 K.

Porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been extensively studied for potential applications in gas storage.1–5 Compared to other gas storage materials such as carbon nanotubes,6 activated carbons,7 and microporous polymers,8 MOFs have some unique advantages including modular synthesis, abundant choice of metal ions and clusters, numerous organic ligands, and great structural diversity. Very recently, some efforts have been made on increasing the gravimetric uptake capacity by introducing lightweight main group ions such as Li+, Be2+, Mg2+, B3+ and Al3+, instead of heavier transition metal ions.9–13 Most of these frameworks, including recently discovered Li-containing porous materials (e.g., lithium isonicotinate, BIFs with Li and B), were built by using carboxylates and azole type ligands.9,13

Metal clusters have often been used as secondary building units (SBUs) for the rational design of porous networks.14 In zeolite chemistry, the double-four-ring unit (D4R) is a very interesting SBU and is present in a number of zeolite topologies such as ACO, AFY, and commercially important LTA.15 In MOF chemistry, these cluster units are also highly desirable because not only do they help to generate a highly open framework architecture, they also contribute to the high framework stability.

It's long been known that lithium, together with aryloxide, can form various types of metal clusters in which lithium and oxygen atoms are both four-coordinated.16 Among these lithium clusters, Li4(OAr)4 cubane has been well studied and can be considered as an example of the double four ring (D4R). So far, only one three-dimensional framework topology (dia net) containing this SBU has been obtained with no gas sorption property reported.16 In the aforementioned structure, the naphthol only acts as a cluster-forming ligand using its –OH group and does not have an additional functional group for crosslinking with adjacent clusters. Even if Li4(OAr)4 clusters can be further linked into a polymeric framework with a different ligand, the resulting structure would unlikely be porous because the large dangling naphthyl groups can severely block the pore access and reduce or totally eliminate the pore space.

Herein we report a 3D porous framework built from a different type of Li4(OR)4 cubane clusters by replacing the terminating ligand naphthol with the ditopic ligand 4-pyridinol (also called 4-hydroxypyridine), whose additional metal binding site (the pyridine N) helps create connectivity to adjacent cubane clusters (Fig. 1). Because 4-pyridinol serves the dual roles (i.e., cluster formation and intercluster crosslinking), this strategy eliminates the pore-blocking effect of the naphthyl group, making it feasible to introduce the porosity (Fig. 2).


Fig. 1 Schematic illustration of the self-assembly process from (a) molecular species to (b) cubane clusters and finally to (c) the 3D framework (purple: Li; red: O; blue: N; grey: C).

Fig. 2 Representation of the 3D channels along the c-axis (purple: Li; red: O; blue: N; grey: C).

The compound Li4(C5H4NO)4‡ (or Li4(OPy)4 for short) was obtained by addition of t-BuOLi (1.0 M solution in hexane) to a methanol solution of equimolar 4-pyridinol, followed by solvent evaporation at 120 °C for about 24 hours.§ Colorless block-shaped crystals were obtained at the bottom of the loosely sealed glass vial (Fig. S2, ESI†). The purity of the sample was supported by the powder X-ray diffraction pattern, which matches well with the simulated pattern (Fig. 3).


Fig. 3 Simulated (black) and experimental X-ray diffraction patterns for the as-synthesized sample (blue) and samples after thermal treatment at different temperatures: 300 °C (cyan), 400 °C (green), 500 °C (yellow), 520 °C (orange) and 550 °C (red).

The structure, which was determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, can be regarded as a microporous 3D framework constructed by interconnected Li4(OPy)4 cubane clusters through N–Li bonding. All lithium atoms are bonded with three oxygen atoms and one nitrogen atom from four different linkers. Each cluster is then connected to eight nearest neighbours (Fig. 1), leading to a 3D network. The Li4(OPy)4 unit is a structural analogue of the isolated Li4(OPh)4L4 (L is usually a terminating ligand such as THF) cluster. The Li–O bond lengths determined by X-ray diffraction are between 1.953(01)–1.969(89) Å, and the Li–N bond length is 1.985(01) Å.

If each Li4O4 cubane is treated as a secondary building unit (an 8-connected node), the resulting topology can be understood as a bcu (body centered cubic) net.17 Alternatively, since all lithium and oxygen atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated in this structure, they can be all considered as tetrahedral sites similar to those in zeolites. The resulting framework topology adopts the ACO zeotype (Fig. S1, ESI†), originally found in aluminium cobalt phosphate.18

Fig. 2 illustrates the framework structure viewed along the (001) direction and shows the 8-ring channels. Due to the highly symmetrical structure, similar pore channels can also be observed from the (100) and (010) directions (Fig. S3, ESI†). The free volume accessible to solvents in this compound is 34.1% as calculated with the PLATON program,19 which is very high for framework structures with only lithium as the framework nodes (Fig. S4, ESI†).

The compound exhibits a very high thermal stability under the N2 atmosphere. Thermogravimetric analysis of the compound indicates that solvent loss (∼13%) occurs in the range 60–120 °C (Fig. S5, ESI†), which suggests a possible formula of Li4(C5H4NO)4·1.9CH3OH. No further weight loss is observed until approximately 540 °C, after which the compound starts to lose more weight. To further examine the thermal stability of the framework, the sample was heated at different temperatures under N2 gas flow, after which powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded. The XRD patterns reveal that the structure remains unchanged until at least 520 °C (Fig. 3). Such a thermal stability is very high among metal–organic frameworks. To our knowledge, it is in fact the highest among porous MOFs containing monovalent cations as framework nodes. The high thermal stability may be attributed to the cluster feature of the building block and the eightfold connection of each cluster to its adjacent clusters, which leads to a very rigid framework.

Gas sorption measurements (N2, H2 and CO2) were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2010 surface area and pore size analyzer. The sample was degassed at 180 °C overnight prior to the measurement. The N2adsorption/desorption curves obtained at 77 K exhibit type-I isotherm characteristics, which proves the permanent porosity of the compound (Fig. 4). The isotherm also shows a significant H4 hysteresis loop between the 0.4–1.0 P/Po range. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller and Langmuir surface areas are 440.3 m2 g−1 and 632.5 m2 g−1, respectively. The pore size analysis obtained by the Horvath–Kawazoe method showed a uniform and narrow pore size distribution around 11 Å when the cylindrical pore geometry was used for calculation (Fig. S6, ESI†).


Fig. 4 Gas sorption isotherms of Li4(OPy)4 for (a) N2 at 77 K, P/Po is the ratio of gas pressure (P) to saturated pressure (Po), with Po = 772 Torr, and for (b) H2 at 77 K and CO2 at 273 K.

Hydrogen adsorption studies gave a gravimetric uptake of 108.7 cm3 g−1 (or 0.96 wt%) and a volumetric uptake of 9.83 g L−1 (calculated crystal density: 1.013 g cm−3) at 760 Torr and 77 K (Fig. 4). This is, to our knowledge, the best hydrogen uptake capacity for an all-lithium based framework. The CO2 adsorption isotherms were also investigated. At 760 Torr and 273 K, the volume of CO2 adsorbed by the compound reached 35.9 cm3 g−1 (1.61 mmol g−1).

In conclusion, we have described the synthesis, structural and gas sorption properties of a lithium cubane based 3D framework material, in which the double four ring (D4R) SBU units lead to the formation of a zeolitic ACO topology. The compound exhibits a high thermal stability up to at least 520 °C without any structural change. In addition, it can reversibly adsorb N2, H2 and CO2. Although the uptake capacity for H2 and CO2 is moderate when considering all microporous materials, it is impressive considering few lithium based porous materials are currently known. In addition to the cubane-type clusters, lithium and aryloxides can form other clusters such as hexamer and octamer, which are analogues of SBUs in zeolites such as the double six-ring (D6R) and aww cage. Therefore our work reported here demonstrates a great potential to synthesize porous frameworks with other zeolitic topologies based on lithium aryloxide clusters, which will hopefully lead to further improvement of the gas sorption properties and possible applications such as gas storage and separation.
