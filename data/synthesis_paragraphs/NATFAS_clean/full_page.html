An electron-transfer photochromic crystalline MOF accompanying photoswitchable luminescence in a host–guest system†

Yu-Shuang Liu a, Yu-Hui Luo ab, Li Li a and Hong Zhang *a
aInstitute of Polyoxometalate Chemistry, Department of Chemistry, Northeast Normal University, Changchun, Jilin 130024, PR China. E-mail: zhangh@nenu.edu.cn
bDepartment of Chemical Engineering, Huaihai Institute of Technology, Lianyungang 222005, Jiangsu, PR China

Received 5th January 2017 , Accepted 21st February 2017

First published on 22nd February 2017

A new electron transfer type photoactive host–guest supramolecule was constructed by introducing (CH3)2NH2+ cations to the MOF framework. The resulting compound 1 exhibits reversible photochromic property without using photochromic components, resulting from photoinduced electron-transfer between the electron-rich anionic framework and the electron-deficient guest ions. In addition, a photoluminescence “on/off switch” occurs during the coloration–decoloration process. The raw materials are non-poisonous and harmless, hence compound 1 may be more cost-effective, clean, and harmless to the heath than existing photochromic materials.

Introduction

Photochromic materials have the ability to tune their photochemical or photophysical properties with light, and are considered to be excellent candidates for information storage,1 photoprint,2 photomasks,3 solar energy conversion,4 smart windows,5 and so forth. A main area of research in this field is focused on exploiting new materials with better photochromic behaviors or various switchable functions, such as luminescence,6 electric conductivity,7 magnetism,8 catalysis,9 and gas adsorption.10 There is growing interest in photochromic inorganic–organic hybrid materials, which can combine the advantages of inorganic and organic components and have the ability to apply synergistic effects between inorganic and organic components. Particular areas of significance in constructing electron-transfer photochromic compounds are fabrication of an array of electron donor (D) and acceptor (A) units and the mechanism of the photoinduced electron transfer (PET) process. Viologen cations (V2+, 1,1′-disubstituted-4,4′-bipyridinium) have attracted great attention because of their excellent electron-accepting ability and promising properties such as light-induced color change,11 and therefore, viologens have been used to prepare electron-transfer photochromic materials. Photochromic viologen-based MOF materials have been reported, built via a combination of a MOF host framework and methyl viologen guest molecule.12,6b However, the main ingredient of the herbicides, methyl viologen, has intrinsic high toxicity and is harmful to the body.

Herein, we report a new ET photochromic MOF material {[(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn3(BTC)2]}n (1, H4BTC = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid), containing self-assembly electron donor–acceptor complexes. The photoresponsive complex is constructed from the [Zn3(BTC)2]2− anionic host framework and (CH3)2NH2+ guest cations rather than traditional photochromic components. New families of photochromic species with non-photochromic components may offer opportunities to obtain diverse photochromic materials. Compared with viologen-based photochromic materials, compound 1 appears to be more cost-effective, clean, and nontoxic. Accompanying the photoinduced electron transfer and energy transfer processes, its reversible photochromism is coupled with switchable photoluminescence.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents and solvents purchased commercially were used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed using a PerkinElmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were measured on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer with KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Rigaku Dmax 2000 X-ray diffractometer with graphite monochromatized Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.15418 nm) and 2θ ranging from 5 to 50° at room temperature. The UV-Vis absorption was measured with a Cary 500 UV-Vis-NIR spectrophotometer. Fluorescence spectra were obtained with a FLSP920 fluorescence spectrometer. The ESR spectra were recorded at X-band frequency (9.867 GHz) on a Bruker ELEXSYS E500 spectrometer. XPS measurements were performed with a VG Scientific ESCALAB 250 instrument. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed with a PerkinElmer thermal analyzer under nitrogen at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1.

Synthesis of compound {[(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn3(BTC)2] }n (1)

The reaction mixture containing Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (0.0438 g, 0.2 mmol), H4BTC (0.05 g, 0.2 mmol), water (1 mL), DMF (2 mL), and ethanol (2 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon reactor autoclave and heated to 85 °C for 3 days. After cooling down to room temperature, the resulting single crystals of 1 were collected and washed with water, and then dried in air (31.54 mg, 40% yield based on H4BTC). Anal. X-ray crystallographic analysis

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurement for compound 1 was carried out using an Oxford Diffraction Gemini R Ultra diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. Absorption correction was performed using the multi-scan technique. The structure was solved by the direct method followed by the difference Fourier method and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 program. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and hydrogen atoms on organic ligands were located and included at their calculated positions. The crystal data and structure refinements of 1 are summarized in Table S1.† Selected bond lengths and angles for compound 1 are listed in Table S2,† while hydrogen-bonding interactions in 1 are listed in Table S3.† CCDC 1504825 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis demonstrated that compound 1 possesses an anionic framework [Zn3(BTC)2]2− with disordered (CH3)2NH2+ cations residing in the channels to achieve the charge balance, the (CH3)2NH2+ cations resulting from in situ decomposition of DMF.13 As illustrated in Fig. 1a, each BTC4− ligand bridges six Zn atoms, with two O atoms not joining the coordination. The structure of compound 1 contains three crystallographically independent Zn(II) ions. Zn1 is coordinated by four O atoms (O1, O11, O13, O16) from four individual BTC4− anions, resulting in a distorted tetrahedral geometry. Zn2 shows a five-coordinated trigonal bipyramidal geometry. The five coordinated O atoms come from five individual BTC4− anions (O3, O4, O10, O14, O15). Zn3 is coordinated by four O atoms and shows distorted tetrahedral geometry. The four O atoms around Zn3 come from four individual BTC4− anions (O5, O6, O7, O9). The Zn–O bonds, ranging from 1.922(3) to 2.096(4) Å, are unexceptional (Table S2†).
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environment around the BTC4− ligand in 1. (b) 1D Zn(II)-carboxylate chain along the a axis in 1. (c) 3D anionic framework structure of 1. (d) Views of the layer with encapsulated (CH3)2NH2+ cations and hydrogen bonding interactions.

Through the bridging of the carboxylic groups, Zn1 and Zn3 ions are connected together, forming a 1D chain along the [0 0 1] direction (Fig. 1b). Further linkage of the chains and the bridging BTC4− anion generate a 3D framework (Fig. 1c). The anionic framework of 1 contains 1-D channels of approximately 14.593 × 6.231 Å, which encapsulate the (CH3)2NH2+ cations (Fig. 1d). The void volume of 1 is 25% of the cell volume (calculated by PLATON). Each (CH3)2NH2+ cation connects the anionic framework through electrostatic attractions and N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds.

Photochromism

Compound 1(1a) showed a rapid color change from pale yellow to dark blue (1b) in response to sunlight or a Xenon lamp (Fig. 2a). The time-dependent UV-Vis spectra of 1 (Fig. 2a) are related to the photochromic behavior on irradiation with a 300 W Xenon lamp. Compared with the initial sample, three new absorption bands centered at 397 nm, 610 nm, and 750 nm appear, and their intensities rise with increasing irradiation time. These spectral features presumably arise from formation of the (CH3)2NH2˙ radical14 through photoinduced electron transfer transition, which is indeed substantiated by ESR studies. ESR studies show that there was no ESR signal before irradiation (1a), but present a single line signal with g = 2.0034 after irradiation (1b) (Fig. 2b), close to that of a free electron (2.0023). This indicates that the paramagnetic center of the colored sample should be a radical and that (CH3)2NH2+ cations are reduced to (CH3)2NH2˙ radicals. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements further demonstrate this conclusion.
Fig. 2 (a) Time-dependent UV-Vis spectra of 1 on Xe light irradiation. The inset shows photographic images of the photochromic effects. (b) ESR spectra of 1 during the photochromic processes. Note that the small signals around g = 2.00 for 1a and decolored samples were caused by unexpected light during sample handling and ESR measurements.

As shown in Fig. 3, the core-level spectra of both Zn 2p and C 1s show no significant changes before and after irradiation. However, the core-level spectra of O 1s and N 1s change obviously after irradiation. The O 1s binding energy of 1b is 0.5 eV higher than that of 1a, meaning that the O atoms of carboxylate groups lose electrons as electron donors. A prominent shift towards a lower N 1s binding energy position occurs after irradiation because electrons are received as electron acceptors. Thus, the radicals are likely to originate from O → (CH3)2NH2+ electron transfer. The unchanged PXRD and IR data during the reversible process (Fig. S1 and S2†) further demonstrate that the photochromism is not caused by photoinduced isomerization or photolysis. This process is likely to originate from photoinduced electron-transfer15 between the electron-rich anionic framework and the electron-deficient (CH3)2NH2+ ions (Fig. 4).

Fig. 4 Energy transfer and electron transfer in the Zn-MOF.

The O atoms in the carboxylate groups are assumed to be electron donors based on the literature.16 In compound 1, the distance between carboxylate oxygen atoms and (CH3)2NH2+ nitrogen atoms is 2.914(16) Å, 2.948(13) Å, and 3.019(10) Å, respectively (Table S3†), which is much shorter than the threshold distance (3.5 Å) observed for light activated D–A systems, providing a path for the electron transfer.17 The reversible photochromism of compound 1 is attributed to the following: (1) short contact between the electron acceptor and electron donor; and (2) the presence of N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds. The photochromic transformation is reversible and the colored photoproduct can reverse to yellow on heating at 80 °C for 1 h in air because of charge recombination.18 However, reverse transformation of 1b to 1a does not occur when heating is carried out under vacuum, which illustrates that color recovery is significantly influenced by oxygen. This radical signal almost disappeared after decoloration (Fig. 2b), reflecting the reversible essence of photochromism. The coloration–decoloration process can be repeated at least five times.

Luminescence

In compound 1, a fluorescence change accompanies the photochromic process. As shown in Fig. 5, compound 1 exhibits a green-light maximum emission at 505 nm (λex = 380 nm). The chromaticity coordinate of 1 is (0.22, 0.43) (Fig. 6a). Coupled with the photochromic process, the luminescence intensity of 1 decreases gradually with increasing length of time of irradiation with a Xe lamp, dropping to approximately 12% of the original value after illumination for 15 min. This phenomenon results from spectral overlap between the emission band of the luminescent components and the absorption band of the colored photoproduct, with intermolecular energy transfer from the excited luminescent center to the (CH3)2NH2˙ radical responsible for the strong fluorescence quenching (Fig. S3†).19 The fluorescence intensity is recovered to its initial state when the blue crystals of 1 turn into the yellow form (Fig. 6b), demonstrating that such switch is reversible without obvious loss of luminescence intensity. Compound 1 belongs to the luminescence-photochromic switch system.

Conclusions

In summary, a new crystalline electron transfer type photoactive complex was prepared, which consists of a [Zn3(BTC)2]2− anionic host framework and (CH3)2NH2+ guest cations. Compound 1 is photoactive and can perform a rapid color change in response to ultraviolet visible light. The coloration–decoloration process is reversible by alternating photoirradiation and heat treatment, while the structure is unchanged. It is noted that the photochromic process originates from PET between the electron-rich anionic framework and the electron-deficient guest cations, rather than using photochromic components. The O atoms in the carboxylate groups play a key role as electron donors in the electron transfer process of compound 1. In addition, compound 1 shows photoregulated luminescence switching, which can be modulated by ultraviolet visible light irradiation. Thus, this work provides an effective and harmless to health strategy for constructing new families of photochromic species with non-photochromic components.
