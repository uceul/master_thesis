Selective gas adsorption within a five-connected porous metal–organic framework†

Ming Xue ab, Zhangjing Zhang b, Shengchang Xiang b, Zhao Jin a, Chengdu Liang c, Guang-Shan Zhu *a, Shi-Lun Qiu *a and Banglin Chen *b
aState Key Laboratory of Inorganic Synthesis & Preparative Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, China 130023. E-mail: zhugs@mail.jlu.edu.cn; sgiu@mail.jlu.edu.cn
bDepartment of Chemistry, University of Texas at San Antonio, San Antonio, TX 78249. E-mail: banglin.chen@utsa.edu
cCenter for Nanophase Materials Sciences, Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Oak Ridge, Tennessee 37831

Received 4th January 2010 , Accepted 19th February 2010

First published on 19th March 2010

A microporous metal–organic framework, Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)41 (L = 2,5-dichloro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate and DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide) of a rare five-connected (44)(66) topology was synthesized and structurally characterized. The de-solvated phase is thermally stable up to 350 °C, thus activation of this new MOF material at different temperatures of 150 and 300 °C under high vacuum formed two microporous materials 1a and 1b, respectively, exhibiting selective gas adsorption behavior with respect to H2/N2 and O2/N2, and CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2.

Introduction

The emergence of porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) over the past two decades has not only enriched the structural database of inorganic–organic hybrid solids of diverse topologies, but also led to the realization of some very promising porous materials for gas storage, separation, sensing and catalysis.1–15 The richness of such MOF structures is attributed to the readily available metal ions and/or metal-containing clusters, and abundant organic linkers of diverse geometries. For example, the reactions of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O with H2BDC (H2BDC = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid) at slightly different conditions produced binuclear Zn2(COO)4 based four-connected 2D framework Zn2(BDC)2 (MOF-2), trinuclear Zn3(COO)6 based six-connected 3D framework Zn3(BDC)3 (MOF-3), and tetranuclear Zn4O(COO)6 based cubic six-connected 3D framework Zn4O(BDC)3 (MOF-5), respectively, the very first examples of MOF materials exhibiting permanent porosity;16 while the reaction of Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O with H2o-Br-BDC (H2o-Br-BDC = ortho-bromo-1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid) formed a binuclear Cu2(COO)4 based four-connected 3D framework Cu2(o-Br-BDC)2 (MOF-101) of NbO topology.17 Among the diverse MOFs, five-connected MOFs are comparatively rare, partly because there cannot be a 5-connected net with all edges equivalent (it would require the presence of 5-fold symmetry axes which are not possible in two- or three-dimensional periodic structures) as rationalized by O'Keeffe and Yaghi and co-workers.18 To employ a chloro-substituted BDC derivative, 2,5-dichloro-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (L), herein we report a rare example of five-connected MOF Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)4 (1) of (44)(66) topology.19 The activation of this new MOF material at different temperature of 150 and 300 °C under high vacuum formed two microporous materials 1a and 1b, respectively, exhibiting selective gas adsorption behavior with respect to H2/N2 and O2/N2, and CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All reagents and solvents employed were commercially available and used as supplied without further purification. TGA data were obtained on a TGA G500 V5.3 Build 171 instrument with a heating rate of 5 K/min under N2 atmosphere. Powder XRD patterns were obtained with a Bruker D8 discover powder diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation. The routine power was 2000 W (50 kV, 40 mA).

Synthesis of 1

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.18 g, 0.6 mmol), H2L (0.144 g, 0.6 mmol) was suspended in DMF (60 mL), and heated in a vial (100 mL) at 100 °C for 24 h. The colorless block shaped crystals formed was collected, washed with DMF, and dried in the air. Elemental analysis: Calcd. for Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)4 (C32H34N4O15Cl5Zn3): C, 35.33; H, 3.15; N, 5.15; Found: C, 35.06; H, 3.12; N, 4.75.

Single-crystal X-ray crystallography

Intensity data for 1 were collected using Bruker X8 APEX II diffractometer (Mo radiation) in a cold nitrogen stream. Data collection and reduction were done using Bruker Apex2 software package. Structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least squares using SHELXL97. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. We employed PLATON/SQUEEZE to calculate the diffraction contribution of the solvent molecules and, thereby, to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities.42 Crystal data for MOF 1: Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)4, monoclinic, space group C2/c, a = 44.7915(25), b = 12.3416(7), c = 20.7246(10) Å, α = 90°, β = 114.539(2)°, γ = 90°, V = 10421.8(10) Å3, Z = 8, Dc = 1.096 g cm−3, μ = 1.665 mm−1, T = 243(2) K, R1 [I > 2σ(I)] = 0.0616, wR2 (all data) = 0.1368, GOF = 1.041.

Gas sorption measurements

Micrometrics ASAP 2020 was used to measure gas adsorption. In order to remove guest solvent molecules in the framework, the fresh as-synthesized 1 was filtered and vacuumed at temperature 150 °C overnight to form 1a, and at temperature 300 °C for 8 h to form 1b, respectively. A sample of 60.0–100.0 mg was used for sorption measurement, maintained at 77 K with liquid nitrogen and at 273 K with water-ice bath, respectively.

Results and discussion

1 was synthesized by the solvothermal reaction of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O with H2L in DMF at 100 °C for 24 h as block-shaped crystals. It was formulated as Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)4 by elemental microanalysis and single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies, and the phase purity of the bulk material was independently confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA).

X-Ray crystallography reveals that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system, space group C2/c as a three-dimensional net composed of trinuclear Zn3(OH)(COO)5 SBUs. As shown in Fig. 1a, one of the Zn ions is four coordinated with three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different L ligands and the μ3–OH at the center of the cluster, whereas other two Zn ions are six coordinated each with two terminal DMF molecules. These trinuclear SBUs are bridged by L ligands to form a rare five-connected net with its Schläfli symbol of (44)(66) (Fig. 1b). Viewed along the b and c axis, respectively, there exist 2D intersected irregular channels of approximately 4 × 5 Å2 and 3 × 4 Å2 filled with the terminal DMF molecules (Fig. 1c–d).


Fig. 1 Single X-ray crystal structure of 1 indicating (a) the Zn3(OH)(COO)5 SBU and coordination environments, (b) the five-connected framework of (44)(66) topology, and views along (c) b axis and (d) c axis demonstrating the micropores of about 4 × 5 Å2 and 3 × 4 Å2, respectively, filled with solvent molecules (Zn, polyhedron, hydrogen atoms are omitted for the clarity).

TGA studies indicate that MOF 1 gradually loses the solvent molecules between 100 °C and 250 °C, and the new phase can be thermally stable up to 350 °C (Fig. 2a). The weight loss of 27.3 wt% determined from TGA matches with the calculated weight loss of four DMF molecules (26.9 wt%) from MOF 1 Zn3(OH)(L)2.5(DMF)4, so the completely de-solvated MOF 1 might be expected to be Zn3(OH)(L)2.5. The temperature-dependent powder X-ray diffraction was further examined to check the framework change and stability of the new phase. As shown in Fig. 2b, PXRD of activated MOF 1 at 50 °C is almost the same as that of the as-synthesized MOF 1, indicating that the framework basically remains the same. The activated MOF 1 at 50 °C under high vacuum overnight exhibits no gas uptake to both hydrogen and nitrogen because the micropores are still filled with terminal DMF molecules which cannot be removed under such activation condition. However, PXRD of activated MOF 1 at 150 °C is completely different from that of the as-synthesized MOF 1, which means that a new phase was formed once the guest molecules are removed. The PXRD further indicates that such a new phase is highly thermal stable and keeps crystalline state even at 350 °C.


Fig. 2 (a) TGA plot and (b) temperature-dependent PXRD of the as-synthesized MOF 1.

Encouraged by the high thermal stability and the potential to construct small micropores for gas separation and purification, MOF 1 was carefully examined and activated at different temperatures for gas adsorption of H2, O2, N2, CO2 and CH4. Heating the as-synthesized MOF 1 at 150 °C under high vacuum overnight, the resulting activated MOF 1a of new phase exhibits very interesting selective adsorption of H2 and O2 over N2. The adsorption of these small gases exhibits typical I isotherm, which is characteristic of microporous MOFs (Fig. 3a). The BET and Langmuir surface areas based on N2 adsorption isotherm are 41 and 65 m2 g−1, respectively. The gas uptake of 1a for H2, O2 and N2 is 35.0, 25.5 and 14.7 cm3 g−1 at P/P0 of about 0.8, respectively, underlying the potential of 1a for selective separation of H2 and O2 over N2. Such preferential hydrogen and oxygen uptake over nitrogen might be attributed to size-exclusive effect in which some micropores are accessible to hydrogen and oxygen, but not to nitrogen because of their differential kinetic diameters of 2.8, 3.46 and 3.64 Å, respectively.201a also exhibits selective adsorption of CO2 over CH4 and N2 in which its CO2 uptake is 3.2 times higher than CH4 uptake, and 14.3 times higher than N2 uptake at 800 Torr, based on their single adsorption isotherms at 273 K (Fig. 3b). The preferred CO2 adsorption of microporous MOFs over CH4 and N2 is quite typical because of the stronger interactions between porous MOFs and CO2.21,22 It needs to be mentioned that, although kinetic diameter of CH4 (3.8 Å) is larger than that of N2 (3.64 Å), 1a still takes certain amount of CH4 at 273 K. This is because of the so-called “kinetically controlled flexibility” in which the micropores within such flexible MOFs are temperature dependent.2 The micropores within 1a at 273 K are larger than those at 77 K which allow their uptake of CH4. There are several examples of such flexible MOFs in the literature exhibiting selective gas adsorption.23–25


Fig. 3 Gas adsorption isotherms of 1a at (a) 77 K and (b) 273 K.

As the de-solvated MOF 1 is highly thermally stable, we thus activated MOF 1 at 300 °C under high vacuum for 8 h to completely lose the terminal solvent molecules and thus to form the more porous phase MOF 1b. As expected, 1b takes up much more nitrogen (97.5 cm3 g−1) than 1a at 77 K because of the complete release of the solvent molecules. The BET and Langmuir surface area of 1b are 248 and 419 m2 g−1, respectively, based on the nitrogen adsorption isotherm (Fig. 4a). The hydrogen storage capacity of 1a is 85.5 cm3 g−1 (0.77 wt%) at 77 K and 1 atm, which is moderate compared with other porous MOFs. Of the most importance is the increased storage capacity for CO2, accordingly 1b takes up 36.2 cm3 g−1 (7.1 wt% at STP) at 760 Torr which is more than twice as 1a does (16.8 cm3 g−1). Such higher storage capacity is practically important for porous MOFs to be efficiently utilized for the CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 separation. It needs to be mentioned that the increased uptake of 1b basically does not affect its selectivity for CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 separation much in which its CO2 uptake is 3.3 times higher than CH4 uptake, and 11.3 times higher than N2 uptake based on their single adsorption isotherms at 273 K and 800 Torr (Fig. 4b), which are higher than those of MOF-508.21 As revealed in MOF-508 for the separation of binary and ternary CO2/CH4/N2 mixtures, the separation selectivity of such MOF materials for CO2/CH4/N2 mixtures might be even higher than those calculated from single adsorption isotherms, thus 1b is expected to be even more efficient for the separation of CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 from their CO2/CH4/N2 mixtures than MOF-508. Our studies indicate that different activation profiles can be efficiently utilized to tune the porosity and thus to direct gas separation. The tunable and flexible nature of porous MOFs together with the ability to implement specific functional sites within MOFs highlight the great promise of this new type of porous materials for gas separation.21–40 Recently there has been extensive work on developing new organic linkers and thus producing porous MOFs for their separation functions. Our work reported here reminds us once again of the richness of MOF chemistry and the power of simple commercially available organic linkers for the construction of useful porous MOF materials. Apparently, the richness and structure diversity of such porous MOFs are attributed to the variety of metal-containing clusters or secondary building units (SBUs) which are readily available as the potential nodes for the self-assembly of MOF materials, as recently reviewed by Yaghi and co-workers.41 To realize the suitable reaction conditions for the in situ formation of SBUs of different geometries, it is expected more porous MOFs from some simple organic linkers will be emerging for the gas separation in the near future.
