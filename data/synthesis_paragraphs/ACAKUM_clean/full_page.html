Structures and properties of lanthanide metal–organic frameworks based on a 1,2,3-triazole-containing tetracarboxylate ligand†

Libo Sun , Yi Li , Zhiqiang Liang *, Jihong Yu and Ruren Xu
State Key Lab of Inorganic Synthesis and Preparative Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, 130012, P. R. China. E-mail: liangzq@jlu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-431-85168609; Tel: +86-431-85168609

Received 30th July 2012 , Accepted 24th August 2012

First published on 28th August 2012

A family of 3D lanthanide metal–organic frameworks, [La(HTADIP)(H2O)4]·xH2O (2 < x < 2.5) (1), [Eu(HTADIP)(H2O)4]·H2O (2), [Tb(HTADIP)(H2O)3]·2H2O (3), and [Er(HTADIP)(H2O)3]·2H2O (4) (H4TADIP = 5,5′-(1H-1,2,3-triazole-1,4-diyl)diisophthalic acid), have been hydrothermally synthesized and characterized. The unsymmetrical 1,2,3-triazole-containing tetracarboxylate ligand was synthesized through the copper-catalyzed azide–alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction of diethyl-5-azidoisophthalate (M1) and diethyl-5-ethynylisophthalate (M2). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses for 1–4 reveal that they are isostructural. These compounds crystallize in the monoclinic P21/c space group with the rutile topology. The luminescent properties of compounds 2 and 3 were investigated and characteristic Eu(III) and Tb(III) emissions are observed. Compound 3 exhibits selective luminescent sensing behavior for Cu2+ ions in aqueous solution. The magnetic properties of compounds 3 and 4 were studied.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted considerable attention because of their promising applications in catalysis,1 gas storage or capture,2 luminescence,3 and magnetism.4 Presently, great efforts have been paid in the synthesis of MOFs based on novel rigid ligands.5 One of the most important reasons is that, by simple selection of different kinds of ligands, the chemical and physical properties of MOFs can be adjusted.1a,c,d,6 Such an idea inspires chemists to design novel ligands in the construction of diverse MOFs. Transition metal-catalyzed organic reactions, such as Suzuki, Heck and Sonogashira coupling reactions, have emerged as a powerful tool to design and synthesize novel ligands.5b By using these ligands, a large number of 3D MOFs with fascinating structures and properties have been constructed.7 In recent years, the copper-catalyzed azide–alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction has become one of the most useful and efficient methods for synthesis of 1,2,3-triazoles, and has been widely applied in the fields of biology, medical diagnosis, supramolecular chemistry and polymer chemistry.8 These have stimulated the exploration of the CuAAC reaction in the post-modification of MOFs,9 but it is rare to construct MOFs based on the ligands synthesized by the CuAAC reaction.10

Lanthanide metals display larger coordination spheres and more flexible coordination geometries, which make it difficult to control the synthesis of lanthanide complexes with desired topology, but do provide an opportunity to construct unusual frameworks with interesting magnetic and luminescent properties.3,11 Recent progress has shown the development of lanthanide MOFs as luminescent sensors for chemical species (metals, solvent) due to their unique luminescent properties.12 For example, Cheng and co-workers reported a series of 3d–4f heterometallic MOFs: {[Ln-(PDA)3Mn1.5(H2O)3]·3.25H2O}∞ (PDA = pyridine-2,6-dicarboxylic acid, Ln = Eu, Tb), and found that they can be used as luminescent probes for Zn2+.12a Chen and co-workers reported a Tb-MOF: Tb(BTC)·G (MOF-76: BTC = benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylate, G = guest solvent), which exhibits high-sensitivity sensing properties for fluoride ions.12c

Recently, we and others have focused on the design and synthesis of novel ligands based on 5-ethynylisophthalic acid, which has a terminal alkynyl group.13,14 Through homocoupling reaction and Sonogashira coupling reaction of its simple ester derivatives followed by hydrolysis reaction, many ligands based on 5-ethynylisophthalic acid were synthesized and used to construct MOFs with excellent properties.7b,13a,14 Diethyl-5-ethynylisophthalate (M2) is an ideal candidate for the CuAAC reaction to synthesize 1,2,3-triazole-containing multi-carboxylate ligands. In this work, we designed and synthesized a novel tetracarboxylate ligand, 5,5′-(1H-1,2,3-triazole-1,4-diyl)diisophthalic acid (H4TADIP), through the CuAAC reaction of diethyl 5-azidoisophthalate (M1) and diethyl-5-ethynylisophthalate (M2) followed by hydrolysis reaction. There are several reasons for designing this ligand and exploring its application in the synthesis of MOFs: (1) CuAAC reaction is easily handled but rarely used to synthesize ligands for the construction of MOFs; (2) the 1,2,3-triazole is a nitrogen-rich linker, and MOFs containing 1,2,3-triazole may show interesting properties such as magnetism15 and adsorption;10a,16 (3) there are two different isophthalic acids (N-linked isophthalic acid named N-IP and C-linked isophthalic acid named C-IP, Fig. S2a†) within H4TADIP and this unsymmetrical ligand may be used to construct MOFs with novel structures and interesting properties. Herein, we present a family of lanthanide MOFs based on the H4TADIP ligand. These compounds are three-dimensional and isostructural frameworks with small differences in the coordinated terminal and lattice water molecules. The luminescent properties of 2 and 3 were investigated and the magnetic properties of 3 and 4 were studied. Compound 3 exhibits good selective luminescent sensing behavior to Cu2+ ions in aqueous solution.

Experimental section

Materials and physical characterizations

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed under air condition from room temperature to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 on Perkin-Elmer TGA-7 thermogravimetric analyzer. Element analyses (C, H and N) were carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. The infrared (IR) spectra were recorded from 400 to 4000 cm−1 on a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer by using KBr pellets. The UV-vis diffuse reflectance spectra were recorded with a Shimadzu 3600 UV-vis-NIR spectrophotometer equipped with an integrating sphere diffuse reflectance accessory, while BaSO4 was used as a reference. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D-Max 2550 diffractometer working with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) over the 2θ range of 4–40° at room temperature. Photoluminescent spectra were measured on a RF-5301 PC spectrometer. Temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility data were recorded on a Quantum-Design MPMS-XL SQUID magnetometer under an applied field of 1 kOe over the temperature range of 2–300 K.

Diethyl-5-azidoisophthalate (M1) was synthesized according to the literature procedure17 and used immediately without further purification. Diethyl-5-ethynylisophthalate (M2) was synthesized according to the literature procedures.13b,18

Metal ion sensing experiments

Compound 3 (2 mg) was added to the aqueous solutions (3 mL) of M(NO3)x (M = K, Co, Ni, Zn, In, Cd; 0.01 M) or Cu(NO3)2 (0.0001, 0.001 and 0.01 M). After stirring for 0.5 h, the emission spectra of these suspensions with stirring were recorded (excited at 331 nm).

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements were carried out on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID diffractometer for compounds 1–3 and a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer for compound 4, with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at 293 K. Data processing was accomplished with the SAINT processing program.19 The structure of these compounds were solved by the direct method and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares with the SHELEX-97 program.20 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters (expect for O1W in 2, and O2W in 3 and 4). The hydrogen atoms on the aromatic rings were placed geometrically. The hydrogen atoms on the uncoordinated carboxylic acid were readily located from the Fourier difference map (except for water molecules where hydrogen atoms were not placed). Results and discussion

Synthesis of H4TADIP

As depicted in Scheme 1, diethyl-5-azidoisophthalate (M1) and diethyl-5-(ethynyl)isophthalate (M2) were used as the key building blocks for the synthesis of ligand H4TADIP. M1 was synthesized by the azidation reaction of diethyl-5-aminoisophthalate and used without further purification. M2 was synthesized from diethyl-5-aminoisophthalate by using iodination/Sonogashira coupling/desilylation reactions. CuAAC reaction of M1 and M2 followed by hydrolysis yielded the desired tetracarboxylate ligand H4TADIP.
Scheme 1 Reaction conditions: (a) NaNO2, HCl (6 M), NaN3, 0–5 °C; (b) CuSO4·5H2O, Na-ascorbate, H2O–tert-BuOH, room temperature; (c) H2O–THF, KOH, 80 °C, overnight.

Crystal structures

Single-crystal X-ray analyses reveal that compounds 1–4 are isostructural, although their coordinated and lattice water molecules are different. The structure of compound 1 is described here representatively. Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. As shown in Fig. S1,† the asymmetric unit contains one crystallographically independent La(III) ion, one HTADIP3− anion and four coordinated terminal H2O (four coordinated water molecules for Eu; three for Tb and Er). The La(III) ion is nonacoordinated with five oxygen atoms (O1–O4, O10) from four ligand anions and four oxygen atoms (O7–O9, O11) from the H2O molecules (La–O = 2.425(2)–2.676(2) Å) (Fig. 1). In compound 1, the carboxylate groups of HTADIP3+ display three different coordination modes (Fig. S2a†): bridging bismonodentate mode (O(1)–C(2)–O(2)), chelating bidentate mode (O(3)–C(1)–O(4)), and monodentate mode (O(10)–C(21)–O(12)). The remaining carboxylic acid of N-IP participates in the formation of hydrogen bond and a weak O(5)⋯H(20)–O(12) interaction can be found (Fig. S3†). These coordination modes in tetracarboxylate ligands have also been reported by Liu's group in the synthesis of 2D lanthanide MOFs based on 5,5′-(1,2-ethynediyl)bis(1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid).13c
Fig. 1 The coordination environment of La in compound 1 presented by thermal ellipsoids drawn at the 30% probability level. The hydrogen atoms and lattice water molecules are omitted for clarity.

In the crystal structure of compound 1, the two neighboring La(III) ions are bridged by the carboxylate group of N-IP to form a binuclear La unit with a La⋯La distance of 5.738 Å. This binuclear building unit is linked by the carboxylate groups of C-IP resulting in a zigzag layer along the bc plane (Fig. 2a and 2b). The N-IP moieties are further linked to the C-IP moieties of the neighboring layers by the 1,2,3-triazole group to form the 3D framework (Fig. 2c).


Fig. 2 (a) View of the bc plane (top). (b) View of the bc plane (side). (c) Schematic representation of the 3D framework. Color scheme: carbon, grey and yellow; oxygen, red; nitrogen, blue; lanthanide, green.

In order to better understand this framework, topological analysis was performed. The binuclear La unit coordinated to six ligands could be considered as a six-connected node (Fig. S2a†) and the HTADIP3+ ligand could be viewed as a three-connected node (Fig. S2b†). The assembly of these two nodes results in a 3,6-connected rtl network (Fig. S2c†) with the point symbol of (4·62)2(42·610·83).

Thermogravimetric analyses

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) of compounds 1–4 were performed under air conditions from 30 to 800 °C. The TGA curves of these compounds are similar. As shown in Fig. S6,† compound 1 exhibits a total weight loss of 75.64% (calcd: 74.62–74.91%), which corresponds to the loss of water molecules and decomposition of organic ligand. Similarly, compounds 2–4 exhibit a total weight loss of 72.43% (calcd: 72.35%), 72.04% (calcd: 71.57%) and 70.39% (calcd: 71.44%), respectively.

Luminescent properties and sensing behaviors

Solid-state luminescent spectra of compounds 2 and 3 were recorded at room temperature, as compared with the free ligand (Fig. 3 and S8†). When excited at 333 nm, H4TADIP exhibits a broad emission band at 472 nm (Fig. S8†). The emission spectrum of compound 2 (excited at 340 nm) is originated from two emission groups: (1) the broad emission band from 400 to 570 nm is assigned to the organic ligand; (2) the sharp emission bands from 570 to 710 nm are characteristic for the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) of Eu(III) ions.21 The strong emission at 618 nm in the red region is attributed to the electric dipole induced 5D0 → 7F2 transition, which is the so-called hypersensitive transition and is responsible for the strong red emission of compound 2. The medium emission at 591 nm corresponding to the 5D0 → 7F1 transition, and the 5D0 → 7F0, 5D0 → 7F3 and 5D0 → 7F4 transitions are too weak to be observed. The intensity of 5D0 → 7F2 is stronger than that of 5D0 → 7F1, indicating that the Eu(III) ion is not located at the inversion center and the symmetry of the Eu(III) ion is low, which is in agreement with the crystal structure.22 This is also supported by the appearance of the 5D0 → 7F0 transition, which is forbidden in a symmetry field. As shown in Fig. 3b, compound 3 exhibits characteristic Tb(III) emissions (excited at 342 nm), resulting from the 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6–2) transitions. The emission bands at 490, 544, 584, 621 nm arise from the 5D4 → 7F6, 5D4 → 7F5, 5D4 → 7F4 and 5D4 → 7F3 transitions, respectively. The band at 646 nm is too weak to be observed.
Fig. 3 Solid-state emission spectra of compounds 2 (a) and 3 (b) at room temperature.

In order to study the selectivity of luminescent sensing for metal ions, compound 3 was immersed in aqueous solutions of different M(NO3)x (M = K+, Co2+, Ni2+, Zn2+, In3+, Cd2+, Cu2+; 0.01 M). As shown in Fig. 4b, no significant change was observed for K+ and slight luminescence quenching was found for Co2+, Ni2+, Zn2+, In3+, and Cd2+ ions. Notably, the luminescent intensity of compound 3 was selectively quenched by Cu2+ ions. The luminescent spectra of a variety amount of Cu2+ are presented in Fig. 4a and characteristic Tb3+ transitions are exhibited. When the amount of Cu2+ increases, the intensity of the spectra decreases. It is remarkable that compound 3 shows high sensitivity for Cu2+ in aqueous solution. The selectivity and sensitivity of luminescence quenching of compound 3 is probably induced by the highly selective coordination ability of the uncoordinated carboxylic acid and triazole group in HTADIP3+.10b,12d,23,24

Magnetic properties

The temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility measurements of compounds 3 and 4 have been performed on powder samples in the range of 2–300 K under a field of 1 kOe. For compound 3, the observed value of χMT per Tb unit is 12.32 cm3 K mol−1 at room temperature (Fig. 5a), which is slightly higher than the value expected for a free Tb(III) ion (11.28 cm3 K mol−1).11b,25 Upon cooling, the value of χMT increases up to a maximum of 12.90 cm3 K mol−1 at ca. 50 K. This magnetic behavior is indicative of the presence of possible weak ferromagnetic coupled interactions between the adjacent two Tb(III) ions bridged by carboxylate at high temperature. Then the value of χMT abruptly decreases to reach a minimum of 10.01 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. This indicates the presence of possible antiferromagnetic interactions within compound 3 at low temperature. The plot of χM−1vs. T over the temperature range 50–300 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law [χ = C/(T − θ)] with C = 12.20 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = 4.28 K. For compound 4 (Fig. 5b), χMT is 12.31 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K, which is slightly higher than the theoretical value for a free Er(III) ion (11.48 cm3 K mol−1).11b Upon cooling, the χMT value decreases gradually and continuously to a value of 5.61 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, this feature is possibly the result of intramolecular antiferromagnetic interactions dominating the magnetic properties in compound 4. Fitting of the χM−1vs. T results in the C = 12.77 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −10.02 K in the range of 20–300 K. The lowering of χMT with decreasing temperature and the negative value of θ may be due to the thermal depopulation of the Stark levels and/or possible the result of intramolecular antiferromagnetic interactions in compound 4.26

Conclusions

In summary, we present here a novel 1,2,3-triazole-containing tetracarboxylate ligand H4TADIP synthesized through the CuAAC reaction. A family of isostructural lanthanide metal–organic frameworks based on this asymmetric ligand have been successfully synthesized and characterized. In these compounds, each binuclear building unit can be regarded as a 6-connected node and the ligand can be considered as a 3-connected node. The assembly of these two nodes results in a 3,6-connected rtl network. The luminescent properties of compounds 2 and 3 exhibit characteristic Eu(III) and Tb(III) emissions. Magnetic behaviors for compounds 3 and 4 differ in different temperature regions. Compound 3 exhibits selective luminescent sensing for Cu2+ ions in aqueous solution. This work may provide helpful information in the construction of MOFs based on novel ligands prepared from the CuAAC reaction. The H4TADIP ligand can potentially be used in the construction of other metal-based MOFs, and such work is currently ongoing in our laboratory.
