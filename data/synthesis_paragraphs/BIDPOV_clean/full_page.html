A highly porous 4,4-paddlewheel-connected NbO-type metal–organic framework with a large gas-uptake capacity†

Zhaoxu Wang a, Baishu Zheng *a, Heting Liu a, Pinggui Yi a, Xiaofang Li a, Xianyong Yu *a and Ruirui Yun *b
aKey Laboratory of Theoretical Chemistry and Molecular Simulation of Ministry of Education, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Hunan University of Science and Technology, Xiangtan 411201, People's Republic of China. E-mail: zbaishu@163.com; yu_xianyong@163.com
bCollege of Chemistry and Materials Science, Anhui Normal University, Wuhu 241000, People's Republic of China. E-mail: yunruirui@gmail.com

Received 14th March 2013 , Accepted 6th June 2013

First published on 11th June 2013

A highly porous 4,4-paddlewheel-connected NbO-type metal–organic framework (HNUST-2, HNUST represents Hunan University of Science and Technology) has been designed and synthesized by self-assembling [Cu2(COO)4] SBUs with a nanosized tetracarboxylate ligand prolonged by alkyne groups, 5,5′-(naphthalene-1,4-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic acid (H4NDED). HNUST-2 exhibits high structural stability, a porous non-interpenetration framework with open metal sites and excellent gas-uptake capacity. This MOF material possesses a high BET surface area of 2366 m2 g−1, a large unsaturated excess and total H2 uptake of 4.57 wt% and 5. 20 wt% at 20 bar and 77 K, respectively. Meanwhile, HNUST-2 also exhibits an excellent adsorption capacity for CO2 (18.07 mmol g−1 at 20 bar and 298 K) and CH4 (85.6 cm3 cm−3 at 20 bar and 298 K) with a high selectivity for CO2 over N2 (22.9) and CH4 (4.9) at 298 K.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are newly emerging porous materials for potentially strategic storage and separation of hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide in clean energy applications.1 Compared with traditional porous materials (zeolites, active carbon, etc.), MOFs not only possess chemically-tunable structures and functional pores, but also exhibit high surface areas and large pore volumes that are crucial to determine the maximum gas-adsorption capacity at high pressure.2 To obtain highly porous MOF materials, two major design strategies have been pursued and proved to be effective: (i) judicious choice of large bridging ligands and/or highly connected secondary building units (SBUs);3 and (ii) utilization of metal–organic polyhedra (MOPs) with both high connectivity and inherent cavity as supermolecular building blocks (SBBs) to assemble extended 3D porous frameworks.4 For example, along these strategies, Hupp and coworkers recently synthesized two landmark MOF materials known as NU-109 and NU-110,5 which display the highest experimental Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas for any porous materials reported to date (∼7000 m2 g−1).

The 4,4-paddlewheel-connected NbO type porous MOFs, which are usually constructed from square planar copper paddlewheel [Cu2(COO)4] SBUs and 4-connected tetracarboxylate linkers, are highly attractive due to their high surface areas, appropriate pore sizes and open copper sites suitable for gas adsorption.6 We are interested in the design and synthesis of porous MOFs from large multidentate carboxylate linkers and [Cu2(COO)4] SBUs,7 because such an approach may facilitate the generation of high surface area MOFs with open copper sites. Indeed, by self-assembling [Cu2(COO)4] SBUs with two nanosized rectangular tetracarboxylate linkers bridged by –CONH– and –CC– bonds, we recently obtained two highly porous NbO type MOFs exhibiting excellent gas uptake.7d,e To expand our investigations in this area, herein we present a new isoreticular NbO type MOF, HNUST-2 (HNUST = Hunan University of Science and Technology), designed from a nanosized rigid alkyne-bridging tetracarboxylate linker [5,5′-(naphthalene-1,4-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic acid = H4NDED] and [Cu2(COO)4] SBUs. HNUST-2 showed a high BET surface area of 2366 m2 g−1 and a large unsaturated total hydrogen-storage capacity of 52.0 mg g−1 at 77 K and 20 bar. Additionally, this MOF material also demonstrated high and selective CO2 capture at 298 K (Scheme 1).

Experimental section

General information

All commercially available chemicals and solvents are of reagent grade and, unless otherwise noted, were used as received without further purification. The organic linker 5,5′-(naphthalene-1,4-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic acid (H4NDED) was prepared according to the literature procedure.8 Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 240 analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed under an N2 atmosphere (100 mL min−1) with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 using a 2960 SDT thermogravimetric analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) at room temperature with a routine power of 1600 W (40 kV, 40 mA) at a scan speed of 5° min−1 and a step size of 0.02 in 2θ. Simulation of the PXRD spectrum was carried out by the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury program.
Synthesis of [Cu2(NDED)(H2O)2]·xS (S = solvent guest molecule), HNUST-2. 5,5′-(Naphthalene-1,4-diylbis(ethyne-2,1-diyl))diisophthalic acid, H4NDED (12.6 mg, 0.025 mmol) and Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (25 mg, 0.1 mmol) in 2 mL DMF (DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide) with 15 μL concentrated HNO3 were stirred for a few minutes in air and the clear solution was transferred into an autoclave Teflon-lined stainless vessel (20 mL). The vessel was sealed and heated to 65 °C for 2 days and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C hour−1. Pale green block crystals of HNUST-2 were filtered and washed with DMF. Yield was 12 mg (∼75% based on the ligand).

Single crystal X-ray study

An appropriate single-crystal of HNUST-2 was sealed in a capillary and mounted on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at 298 K with a graphite-monochromatic Mo/Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the ω-scan technique. The CCD data were integrated and scaled using the Bruker-SAINT software package. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques based on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 program.9 All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic parameters while H atoms were placed in calculated positions with isotropic displacement parameters set to 1.2 × Ueq of the attached atom. The hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecules could not be located. Because the guest solvent molecules are highly disordered and impossible to refine using conventional discrete-atom models, the SQUEEZE subroutine of the PLATON software suite10 was applied to remove the scattering from the highly disordered solvent molecules, and sets of solvent-free diffraction intensities were produced. In the structure, the naphthalene ring moiety of the NDED ligand was disordered into two positions. Structural information of HNUST-2 was deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center (CCDC reference number 926773). Crystallographic data for HNUST-2: C15H6CuO5, Fw = 329.74, trigonal, R, a = b = 18.6777(3) Å, c = 54.443 (2) Å, α = β = 90°, γ = 120 °, V = 16448.2(7) Å3, Z = 18, T = 296 (2) K, μ = 0.604 mm−1, 33865 reflections were collected (8396 were unique) for 1.96° < θ < 27.48°, R(int) = 0.0530, R1 = 0.0483, wR2 = 0.1427 [I > 2σ(I)], R1 = 0.0754, wR2 = 0.1469 (all data), GOF = 1.079.

Sample activation

A solvent-exchanged sample was prepared by immersing the as-synthesized HNUST-2 in anhydrous acetone for 3 days to remove the nonvolatile solvates, the solvent was decanted every 8 h, and fresh acetone was added. The completely-activated sample was obtained by heating the solvent-exchanged sample at 373 K under a dynamic high vacuum for 20 h. During this time, the pale-blue sample changed to a deep-purple color, indicating the presence of unsaturated Cu(II) sites. A similar color change upon activation was observed for other frameworks constructed from Cu(II)-paddlewheel SBUs.11 Anal. Calcd for activated HNUST-2 (C15H6CuO4, Fw = 312.96 g mol−1): C 57.42, H 1.93, N 0; found: C 57.54, H 2.11, N 0. The completely-activated samples were moisture sensitive and a few minutes of exposure to air could change the sample's color back to pale green.

Gas adsorption experiments

In the gas sorption measurements, all of the gases used are of 99.999% purity. Low-pressure (up to 1 bar) nitrogen (N2, at 77 K), hydrogen (H2, at 77 and 87 K), carbon dioxide (CO2, at 273 and 298 K), and methane (CH4, at 273 and 298 K) sorption experiments were carried out on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 M + C surface area analyzer. For all low-pressure isotherms, warm and cold free space correction measurements were performed using ultra-high-purity He gas (UHP grade 5.0, 99.999% purity). A part of the N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K in the P/P0 range 0.001–0.1 was fitted to the BET equation to estimate the BET surface area and the Langmuir surface area calculation was performed using all data points. The pore size distribution (PSD) was obtained from the DFT model in the Micromeritics ASAP2020 software package (assuming split pore geometry) based on the N2 sorption isotherm. High-pressure adsorption isotherms of H2 (at 77 K), CO2 (at 273 and 298 K), CH4 (at 273 and 298 K) and N2 (at 273 and 298 K) were measured using an IGA-003 gravimetric adsorption instrument (Hiden-Isochema, UK) over a pressure range of 0–20 bar. For high-pressure measurements, the completely-activated sample mass was monitored until equilibrium was reached (within 25 min) at each pressure. The total high-pressure gas uptake was calculated by: Ntotal = Nexcess + ρbulkVpore, where ρbulk equals the density of compressed gases at the measured temperature and Vpore was the pore volume of the sample that was obtained from the N2 isotherm at 77 K.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

Solvothermal reaction of H4NDED and Cu(NO3)2·3H2O in DMF–HNO3 (DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide) at 65 °C for 48 h afforded block-shaped pale-green crystals of HNUST-2 with the framework formula [Cu2(NDED)(H2O)2]n in high yields (about 75% based on the ligand). The single crystal X-ray structural studies reveal that HNUST-2 crystallizes in the rhombohedral space R with a = b = 18.6777(3) Å and c = 54.443(2) Å. This is different from the prototype framework NOTT-109,6i which crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I4/mmm. Additionally, in contrast to NOTT-109, the NDED ligand in the structure of HNUST-2 is almost in a plane and can be attributed to the shrinkage of tension after ligand extension by incorporating two triple CC bonds into the original 5,5′-(naphthalene-1,4-diyl)diisophthalic acid6i organic backbone. Similarly, HNUST-2 also adopts the dicopper paddlewheel clusters [Cu2(CO2)4] as secondary building units (SBUs) (Fig. 1 and 2), and the copper atoms become coordinatively unsaturated upon axial aqua ligand removal. The [Cu2(CO2)4] SBUs are further bridged by the four-connected rectangular organic building block NDED to give rise to an expanded 3D (4, 4)-connected structure with NbO type topology, the same as other reported MOFs assembled with tetratopic linkers and 4-connected SBUs.6
Fig. 1 (a) Nanosized alkyne-bridging tetracarboxylate linker NDED and [Cu2(COO)4] paddlewheel SBU; (b, c) the shuttle shaped and spherical cage; (d) schematic representation of the framework in HNUST-2 viewed through the [1 0 0] direction. Cu, blue-green; C, black; O, red. Water molecules and H atoms have been omitted for clarity.

Fig. 2 View of HNUST-2 showing the arrangement of the two types of cages (the shuttle shaped and spherical cages shown in green and yellow, respectively) through the extended crystal lattice.

A careful examination of the crystal structure reveals that the framework of HNUST-2 can be viewed as the packing and combination of two types of metal–ligand cages (Fig. 1 and 2). The first type consists of six NDED ligands connecting twelve paddlewheel SBUs to form a shuttle-shaped pore with an inner sphere diameter of ca. 1.1 nm (after considering the van der Waals radii of the atoms) (Fig. 1), in which each ligand uses all of its four carboxylate groups to support the cavity. The second type, which is a spherical-like pore about 1.6 nm in diameter, is formed from twelve NDED ligands and six paddlewheel SBUs. In this type, each ligand utilizes only two of its four carboxylate groups (Fig. 1) to support the cavity and the other two carboxylate groups are utilized to link adjacent cavities. In each cavity, one-half of the 12 NDED ligands join adjacent SBUs through carboxylate groups from the same phenyl ring and the other half uses carboxylates from the opposite ends of the ligand for the same purpose. These two types of cavities are connected to each other by sharing three paddlewheel SBUs between two adjacent ones of different types, resulting in a highly porous 3D framework (Fig. 2). The solvent accessible volume in the dehydrated structure is 73.4% as calculated from PLATON/SOLV.10

Framework stability

To study the framework stability of HNUST-2, we performed thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) studies. The TGA curve (Fig. S1, ESI†) of acetone-exchanged HNUST-2 reveals a weight loss of about 29.1% in the temperature range of 25–300 °C, which can be attributed to the release of free acetone molecules and the coordinated H2O, and sharp weight losses were observed owing to decomposition of the framework upon further heating above 300 °C, indicating good stability of the framework. The robustness of the framework for HNUST-2 has been further examined by PXRD experiments, as shown in Fig. 3, the similarity of the simulated PXRD pattern from single-crystal data to those for both as-synthesized and activated samples demonstrates that the single crystal is representative of the pure bulk sample, and the framework is robust and its crystallinity can be retained after the removal of guest molecules.
Fig. 3 The PXRD patterns of MOF material under various conditions: the simulated PXRD pattern obtained from the X-ray crystallographic data, as-synthesized and activated for HNUST-2, respectively.

Permanent porosity

Encouraged by the 3D porous structure, the high thermal stability and structural rigidity, we determined the permanent porosity of the desolvated HNUST-2. To obtain the fully desolvated form, the as-synthesized HNUST-2 was first repeatedly soaked and decanted in anhydrous acetone and then vacuum-dried at 100 °C overnight. A color change from pale-green (in DMF) to pale-blue (in acetone) to deep purple (activated) was observed, typical of dicopper(II)-paddlewheel frameworks in which open copper sites were generated.11 The N2 sorption for activated HNUST-2 at 77 K exhibits a reversible type-I isotherm as shown in Fig. 4, suggesting the microporous nature of the framework,13 which is coincidental with the crystal structure. HNUST-2 can uptake large amounts of N2 (ca. 630 cm3 g−1) and the surface area calculated from the Langmuir equation is about 2746 m2 g−1, and the corresponding BET surface area is 2366 m2 g−1. Notably, this BET value is smaller than that reported for the prototype NJU-Bai12-ac (3038 m2 g−1),7e but is much larger than that of the prototype NOTT-109 (∼1718 m2 g−1)6i and comparable to the values reported for other MOFs such as MOF-253 (2160 m2 g−1),14 SNU-50 (2300 m2 g−1),6l NOTT-105 (2387 m2 g−1)6i and PCN-46 (2500 m2 g−1),6k thus making HNUST-2 one among the high surface area MOF series reported to date.15 The inset to Fig. 4 shows the differential pore volume distributions as functions of pore width calculated by the DFT model in the Micromeritics ASAP2020 software package (assuming split pore geometry), which suggests that HNUST-2 has a narrow pore size distribution of around 1.0–1.18 nm. This agrees well with the pore size obtained from a single crystal structure. The total pore volume of HNUST-2 calculated from the maximum amount of N2 adsorbed at 77 K reaches up to 0.97 cm3 g−1.
Fig. 4 The N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K (filled circles, adsorption; empty circles, desorption) and pore size distribution of HNUST-2.

Gas storage properties

The high surface area, large pore volume and open copper sites within the framework of HNUST-2 prompted us to study its gas-uptake capacity, especially that for H2, CH4 and CO2. Hydrogen, with a high heat of combustion per mass unit (146 kJ g−1) and zero emission, has long been considered as an ideal energy source candidate in the future.16 However, the lack of an effective storage method hinders its application. The US Department of Energy (DOE) recently reset the gravimetric and volumetric storage targets for onboard H2 storage for the year 2017 (5.5 wt%, 40 g L−1).17 MOF-based H2 storage has attracted remarkable attention recently because of its fast kinetics and favorable thermodynamics in H2 adsorption and release.18 To evaluate the H2 storage performance of HNUST-2, H2 sorption isotherms were measured at cryogenic temperature under both low and high pressures. As shown in Fig. 5, HNUST-2 can reversibly adsorb 7.27 and 4.75 mmol g−1 of H2 at 77 K and 87 K up to 1 bar, which match up about 1.45 and 0.95 wt% storage capacity [wt% = 100(mass of adsorbed gas)/(mass of MOF)]. This value is lower than the corresponding value of the prototype NOTT-109 (2.33 wt% at 77 K) and other isostructural MOFs6 probably owing to the larger pore size. Under a range of high pressures, the unsaturated excess gravimetric H2 uptake reaches up to 4.57 wt% at 20 bar and 77 K, considerably larger than that of NOTT-109 (<4.15 wt%) and most of the other NbO type MOFs under the same conditions.6 Notably, this H2 storage capacity is far from saturated, which means that its uptake can be further maximized at a higher pressure range (restricted by the maximum pressure of the instrument, the H2 sorption isotherm was recorded only from 0 to 20 bar in this work). Taking into consideration the gaseous H2 compressed within the framework void, the total gravimetric H2 uptake can reach as high as 5.20 wt% at 20 bar and 77 K. Calculated from the crystal density of the activated form (0.570 g cm−3), HNUST-2 has an excess and total volumetric H2 uptake of 27.6 and 30.3 g L−1 at 20 bar, respectively. Based on the virial method,12 the H2 isosteric adsorption enthalpy of HNUST-2 is about 6.23 kJ mol−1 (Fig. 6) at zero coverage and steadily decreased to 4.5 kJ mol−1 at medium loading, indicating a moderate H2–framework interaction and comparable to those of other NbO type MOFs constructed from long linkers (mainly in the range of 5.5–7.2,15 respectively).6

Methane gas is another alternative on-board fuel that has attracted much interest. Like hydrogen, however, it also lacks an effective storage method. The DEO target for on-board CH4 storage is based on volumetric capacity of 180 v(STP)/v(STP) under 35 bar and near ambient temperature, where STP represents standard temperature and pressure; T = 273 K, P = 101.325 kPa. Among the various porous materials studied, MOFs offer great promise for CH4 storage and several MOF compounds have been reported to exhibit exceptional CH4 uptake that surpasses the DOE standard (180 cm3 cm−3) at room temperature and 35 bar.6c,h,19 To monitor the CH4 storage capacity of HNUST-2, gravimetric CH4 adsorption isotherms have been also recorded up to 20 bar at both 273 and 298 K (Fig. 7). Both isotherms show typical type-I behavior and fast kinetics. Restricted by the maximum pressure of the instrument here, we are unable to measure the sample at a higher pressure range. At 20 bar, the unsaturated CH4 uptake of HNUST-2 reaches 15.0 wt% and 10.7 wt% at 273 and 298 K, respectively, corresponding to volumetric (v/v) uptakes of 119.7 and 85.6 cm3(STP)cm−3, which are comparable to other MOF materials at higher pressure at 298 K (PCN-68,3c 99 cm3(STP)cm−3 at 35 bar; NOTT-119,20 97.8 cm3(STP)cm−3 at 66 bar; MOF-205,3d 93 cm3(STP)cm−3 at 35 bar; DUT-13,21 71 cm3(STP)cm−3 at 35 bar).


Fig. 7 Excess CH4 isotherms of HNUST-2 at 0–20 bar, where squares and circles represent the isotherms at 273 and 298 K, respectively (filled and open symbols indicate adsorption and desorption, respectively).

Economical and efficient CO2 capture and storage (CCS), a process involving the capture of carbon dioxide from air and sequesering it underground, has been proposed as a feasible way to control the atmospheric CO2 concentration and has attracted tremendous attention due to the escalated global warming.22 Using porous materials to capture CO2 based on the sorption mechanism will be an energy-conserving alternative approach. The study of MOF-based CO2 capture is growing dramatically because MOFs can store greater amounts of CO2 than other classes of porous materials and their chemically-adjustable organic and inorganic moieties can be carefully pre-designed to be suitable for molecular recognition of CO2.1h,23 To explore the potential of HNUST-2 as CO2 storage/seperation material, high-pressure gravimetric CO2 and N2 adsorption isotherms have been also measured at 273 and 298 K from 0 to 20 bar and are shown in Fig. 8. More interestingly, HNUST-2 shows a completely reversible, high CO2 adsorption at near ambient temperature. A large unsaturation excess CO2 uptake of 95.0 wt% and 79.5 wt% (∼21.6 and 18.1 mmol g−1) is observed at 273 and 298 K under 20 bar, respectively. This CO2 storage capacity at 298 K is below those of MOFs with an exceptionally high surface area reported at higher pressures (e.g. MOF-210,3d 54.5 mmol g−1 at 50 bar; MOF-200, 55.4 mmol g−1 at 50 bar; NU-100,24 46.4 mmol g−1 at 40 bar; MIL-101,3b 40.0 mmol g−1 at 50 bar), but still comparable to most of the other high CO2-uptake MOFs reported at higher pressures such as MIL-10025 (18.0 mmol g−1 at 30 bar), IFMOF-33a (18.7 mmol g−1 at 35 bar) and Mg-MOF-7426 (15.7 mmol g−1 at 36 bar). Notably, based on the total capacity, a container filled with HNUST-2 can store about 12 times the amount of CO2 in an empty container at 20 bar and 298 K, further suggesting that HNUST-2 is a good CO2 adsorbent.


Fig. 8 High-pressure excess CO2 and N2 isotherms of HNUST-2 collected at 273 and 298 K (filled and open symbols indicate adsorption and desorption, respectively).

Notably, in sharp contrast to CO2, HNUST-2 can only absorb limited amounts of CH4 and N2 (∼11.6 wt% and 8.28 wt% for N2 uptake-capacity at 273 and 298 K under 20 bar, respectively), indicating that HNUST-2 has the ability to selectively adsorb CO2 over CH4 and N2 (Fig. 7 and 8). This behaviour can be attributed to the large quadrupolar moment of CO2 (1.34 × 10−39 C m−2) and the higher polarizability of CH4 (26 × 10−25 cm3) vs. N2 (17.6 × 10−25 cm3),27 inducing different gas–framework interactions and gas adsorption enthalpies. Indeed, as shown in Fig. 9, HNUST-2 exhibits a stronger CO2 binding affinity than CH4 and N2 throughout the adsorption process. The ratios of initial slopes of gas adsorption isotherms at room temperature are used to estimate the adsorption selectivity,28 and the CO2/CH4 and CO2/N2 selectivity is thus calculated to be 4.9 and 22.9 respectively (Fig. S2, ESI†), much higher than the corresponding value of MOF-177 (4.4 and 17.5)29 and most other MOF materials (mainly in the range of 3–10 and 10–50,23a respectively).


Fig. 9 Isosteric heats of CO2, CH4 and N2 adsorption for HNUST-2 calculated from the adsorption at 298 K and 273 K through the virial method.

Conclusion

In summary, by incorporating a nanosized rigid alkyne-bridging tetracarboxylate ligand within a network of NbO-type topology, we have successfully generated a highly porous (4,4)-paddlewheel-connected MOF (HNUST-2), which shows a high BET surface area of 2366 m2 g−1, a large excess and total H2 uptake of 4.57 wt% and 5. 20 wt% at 20 bar and 77 K, respectively. Meanwhile, HNUST-2 also exhibits an excellent uptake capacity for CO2 (79.5 wt% at 20 bar) and CH4 (10.7 wt%, 85.6 cm3 cm−3 at 20 bar) with high selectivity of CO2/CH4 (4.9) and CO2/N2 (22.9) at 298 K. We believe that the structural characteristics of HNUST-2, including the microporous structure, high solvent-accessible volume, large surface area, and non-interpenetration framework with open metal sites, together are responsible for the high performance of the gas adsorption. Further studies will focus on exploring other highly-connected SBUs to address MOFs with a high surface area and incorporating other functionalities onto a linker for gas applications.
