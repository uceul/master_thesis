Abstract

The heterometallic CuII‐BaII coordination polymer, namely [CuBa(tdc)2(H2O)(DMF)]n (1) (H2tdc = 2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylic acid, DMF = N,N′‐dimethylformamide), was solvothermally synthesized by the reaction of H2tdc, CuCl2·2H2O, and Ba(NO3)2. Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 features a 3D intricate framework with the 1D channels occupied by the coordinated solvent molecules. After removing the coordinated solvent molecules, the desolvated samples of 1a exhibit high capacity for light hydrocarbons.

1 Introduction

Metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) are of current research interests not only owing to their intriguing structural topologies but also owing to their potential applications as functional solid‐state materials.1–4 Of particular interest is to create porous MOFs with high stability and high surface area, which can be used as potential materials for the storage and separation of light hydrocarbons owing to the tunable microenvironment of the pore and surface.5–7 To date, numerous porous MOFs materials, such as MOF‐177, MOF‐74, FIR‐3 and so on,8–10 have been investigated for the storage and purification of light hydrocarbons.11–13 However, the construction of three‐dimensional porous frameworks with high thermal stabilities remains a great challenge to chemists owing to the occurrence of the interpenetration phenomenon in the crystallization process. Therefore, avoiding interpenetration phenomenon is significant for the construction of porous structures. According to previously reported documents, one effective way to build porous MOFs is that employing polycarboxylate ligands to bridge or chelate metal ions into rod‐shaped metal‐carboxylate subunits, which have intrinsic rigidity and geometrics that can effectively avoid interpenetration due to the intrinsic packing arrangement of such rods in the crystal structure.14 Another method is to use appropriate solvent molecules (such as DMF, DMA, ethyleneurea and its derivatives) as terminal ligand, which can be easily removed by heating to generate the porosity.15 Up to now, many porous MOFs are mainly based on single metal ions and organic ligands, heterometallic porous MOFs with high thermal stabilities are relatively rarely reported.16–18

Based on the above consideration, in this work, we selected an organic carboxylate ligand (2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylic acid) to react with CuII and BaII ions, successfully obtaining a new heterometallic compound, namely [CuBa(tdc)2(H2O)(DMF)]n (1) (H2tdc = 2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylic acid, DMF = N,N′‐dimethylformamide). Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis revealed that compound 1 features a 3D framework based on rod‐shaped heterometallic CuII‐BaII‐carboxylate chain. Remarkably, the coordinated solvent molecules can be removed by heating treatment, generating a porous framework of 1a. The porous framework of 1a exhibits high capacities for light hydrocarbons at room temperature.

2 Experimental Section

2.1 Materials and Methods

All the starting materials and reagents used in this work were obtained commercially and used without further purification. Element analyses (C, H, and N) were determined with an elemental Vario EL III analyzer. TGA analyses were carried out with a NETSCHZ STA‐449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 K·min–1 in a nitrogen atmosphere. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded with a PANalytical X′Pert Pro powder diffractometer with Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) with a step size of 0.05°. Gas adsorption measurement was performed in the ASAP (Accelerated Surface Area and Porosimetry) 2020 System. Single crystal X‐ray diffraction was carried out with an Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer.

2.2 Synthesis of [CuBa(tdc)2(H2O)(DMF)]n (1)

A mixture of CuCl2·2H2O (0.1 mmol, 0.017 g), Ba(NO3)2 (0.1 mmol, 0.026 g), H2tdc (0.1 mmol, 0.017 g), and DMF (3 mL) was sealed in a small vial, which was kept at 100 °C for 60 h and finally cooled to the room temperature slowly. Green block crystals yield in 35 % based on H2tdc. C15H13BaCuNO10S2 (632.26): calcd. C 28.47; N 2.21; H 2.06 %; found: C 28.42; N 2.32; H 2.11 %.

2.3 X‐ray Crystallography

Single crystal X‐ray structure analysis of 1 was performed with an Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer (Mo‐Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å, graphite monochromator) at 293(2) K. Empirical absorption corrections were applied to the data using the SADABS program.19 The structure was solved by the direct method and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares on F2 using the SHELXL‐97 program.20 All of the non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and the hydrogen atoms attached to carbon atoms were located at their ideal positions. Experimental details for the structure determination are presented in Table 1.

3 Result and Discussion

3.1 Structural Description of Compound 1

Single crystal X‐ray structural analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic P212121 space group with an asymmetric unit containing one CuII ion, one BaII ion, two tdc2– ligands, one coordinated water molecule, and one coordinated DMF molecule. As shown in Figure 1a, the Cu1 ion is four‐coordinate and exhibits a quadrilateral coordination arrangement with four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different tdc2– ligands. The Ba1 ion is coordinated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from five different tdc2– ligands, one coordinated water molecule, and one coordinated DMF molecule. The Cu–O distances are in the range of 1.947(4)–2.013(4) Å, and the Ba–O distances range from 2.620(4) to 2.844(4) Å. Selected bond lengths and angles for compounds 1 are listed in Table 2. The tdc2– ligands adopt two different coordination modes: one bridges four metal ions in the μ4‐O, O′, O′′, O′′′ mode and the other bridges five metal ions in the μ5‐O, O, O′, O′′, O′′′ mode. It is interesting that CuII ions and BaII ions are bridged into a rod‐shaped heterometallic inorganic chain by the carboxylate groups extending along crystallographic a axis (Figure 1b). The inorganic chains are further interconnected by the tdc2– ligands into a 3D neutral framework (Figure 1c). Viewing along crystallographic a axis, the coordinated DMF and water molecules are filled in the 1D channels. After removal of the coordinated DMF and water molecules, the framework of 1 contains very large 1D opened channels with the solvent accessible volumes of 47.7 % of the unit cell volume as calculated by PLATON (Figure 1d).

3.2 PXRD Patterns and Thermal Analysis

Powder X‐ray diffraction experiment was performed on bulk samples to confirm the phase purity of compound 1. As shown in Figure S1a (Supporting Information), the diffraction peaks of the simulated and experimental patterns match well, supporting the phase purity of the compound 1. In addition, the thermal stability of compound 1 was also investigated via thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S1b, Supporting Information). From the TGA curve of 1, we found that the first weight loss of 14.12 % occurs in the temperature range of 150–223 °C, which corresponds to the departure of one coordinated water molecule and one coordinated DMF molecule per unit cell (calcd: 14.39 %). Then, a plateau continues to 338 °C, indicating the desolvated samples can be stable up to 338 °C. Variable‐temperature powder X‐ray diffraction further confirms that compound 1 can maintain its structure to 338 °C (Figure S1a). Upon heating above 338 °C, dramatically weight loss occurs owing to the decomposition of the organic ligand.

3.3 Gas Adsorption Properties

To demonstrate the permanent porosity of the desolvated compound 1, gas adsorption measurements were performed with a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area and pore size analyzer. For the gas sorption studies, the fresh compound 1 was activated at 250 °C for 8 h under high vacuum to obtain the desolvated sample of 1a. The PXRD patterns of 1 before and after removing the coordinated solvent molecules confirm that the desolvated samples maintain the original structure of compound 1 (Figure S2a, Supporting Information). The N2 sorption isotherm of 1a at 77 K shows a type‐I behavior and N2 uptake of 161.2 cm3·g–1, giving the BET and Langmuir surface areas of 575.2 and 768.6 m2·g–1, respectively (Figure 2a). In addition, we also investigated the sorption behaviors of the light hydrocarbons at room temperature. As shown in Figure 2b, 1a exhibits notable adsorption capacities of C3H8 (121.6 cm3·g–1), C2H6 (123.1 cm3·g–1), C2H4 (123.8 cm3·g–1), and CH4 (36.2 cm3·g–1), which are higher than those for UTSA‐35a and UTSA‐36a reported by Chen et al.21,22 This high adsorption capacities for light hydrogen carbons reveals that 1a can be used as a good candidate for storage of light hydrocarbons at room temperature.

4 Conclusions

The stable heterometallic CuII‐BaII coordination polymer based on 2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylate was solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. Compound 1 features a 3D framework with the 1D channels occupied by the coordinated DMF and water molecules. After removing the coordinated solvent molecules, the desolvated samples exhibit high adsorption capacities for light hydrocarbons.
