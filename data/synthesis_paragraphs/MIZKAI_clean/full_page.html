Two microporous metal–organic frameworks with different topologies constructed from linear trinuclear M3(COO)n secondary building units†

Xiao-Feng Wang ab, Yue-Biao Zhang a, Xiao-Ning Cheng a and Xiao-Ming Chen *a
aMOE Key Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, 510275, China. E-mail: cxm@mail.sysu.edu.cn
bSchool of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanhua University, Hengyang, 421001, China

Received 1st November 2007 , Accepted 18th February 2008

First published on 10th March 2008

Two new microporous metal–organic frameworks, [Zn3(NH2BDC)3(H2O)2]·5DMF (1) and (Me2NH2)2[Cd3(NH2BDC)4]·2DMF·2.5 H2O (2) (NH2BDC = 2-amino-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate), based on linear trinuclear secondary building units of Zn3(COO)6 or Cd3(COO)8 that have different connected numbers, have been synthesized and characterized, which exhibit usual pcu and rare hex-like topologies, respectively. Both 1 and 2 are three-dimensional porous frameworks, having solvent accessible volumes of ca. 53 and 39%, respectively. There are unremovable guest Me2NH2+ cations clogged in the channels of 2. In contrast, being of good thermo-stability, 1 has a Langmuir surface area about 570 m2 g−1 and type I isotherm behaviour for nitrogen gas sorption at 77 K, being typical of microporous material.

Introduction

In the last decade, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have received considerable attention, owing to their potential applications in separations,1 heterogeneous catalysis,2drug delivery,3 magnetism,4 nonlinear optics,5 luminescence6 and gas storage.7 So far, a large number of porous MOFs have been obtained by using the strategy of secondary building units (SBUs). In fact, a variety of representative SBUs have been adopted to construct porous and related metal carboxylate MOFs, such as the tetranuclear Zn4O(COO)8 which was an octahedral “basic zinc acetate” cluster, the square “paddle wheel” dimeric M2(COO)4 (M = Cu,9,10aZn,2b,10a,bCo,10c or Cd),10b the octahedrally dinuclear Ln2(COO)6,11 and the trigonal prismatic oxo-centered trimeric M3O(COO)6 (M = Cr,12 In13 or Zn)2b as well as Fe3O(COO)6 and its derivative Fe2O(COO)3(SO4)3.14 It has been well-known that a change of the nature of SBU may lead to a drastic change in the structures of MOFs, while the selection of organic linkers also allows chemists to multiply the number and to tune functionality of the MOFs.

The versatile linear trinuclear M3(COO)6 SBU has been utilised for generating a number of metal-carboxylate MOFs with M = Zn(II), Cd(II), Mg(II) or mixed transition-metal ion(II), which were categorized as octahedral and triangular SBUs.15,16 However, many of these MOFs have two-dimensional (2-D) frameworks6,16,17 in which both terminal metal ions adopt a four/five-coordination geometry, where one or two coordination sites are occupied by terminal solvent molecules. If the coordination character of metal ion is different, the terminal solvent ligand might be replaced by bridging ligands, so that the SBU should be changed from a six-connected one to a higher eight-connected one, thus the topologies of frameworks will be different.

Herein, we report the synthesis, crystal structures of two new 3-D microporous MOFs: [Zn3(NH2BDC)3(H2O)2]·5DMF (1) and (Me2NH2)2[Cd3(NH2BDC)4]·2DMF·2.5 H2O (2) (NH2BDC = 2-amino-1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, and DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide). The linear trinuclear SBUs of the two compounds are different. The Zn3(COO)6 in 1 is in an octahedral geometry compared with the dodecahedral Cd3(COO)8 in 2. Both MOFs in 1 and 2 are 3-D networks with different topologies and considerable solvent accessible area. Among them, 1 features the well-known pcu topology while 2 has a rare hex-like 36·414·57·6 net.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemicals were commercially purchased and used without further purification. The C, H and N elemental analyses were carried out with a Vario EL elemental analyzer. Thermo-gravimetric (TG) analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer TGS-2 thermogravimetric analyzer in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max 2200 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). The adsorption isotherms of N2 were measured by using BELmax 00027 adsorption equipment (BEL Japan).

Preparation of [Zn3(NH2BDC)3(H2O)2]·5DMF (1)

A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6 H2O (0.0595 g, 0.2 mmol) and H2NH2BDC (0.0362 g, 0.2 mmol) was dissolved in DMF/H2O (10 ml, 2:1), then Et3N (0.05 ml) was added. The mixture was stirred for 2 h in air and then filtrated. The yellow filtrate was kept at room temperature for two weeks, yellow block crystals of 1 were obtained (35% yield based on Zn). This compound was stable in air without crystal collapsing and insoluble in common organic solvents such as methanol, ethanol, dichloromethane, chloroform and DMSO. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C39H54Zn3N8O19: C, 41.27; H, 4.80; N, 9.87. Found: C, 41.33; H, 4.74; N, 9.81%.

Preparation of (Me2NH2)2[Cd3(NH2BDC)4]·2DMF·2.5 H2O (2)

Cd(NO3)2·6 H2O (0.0617 g, 0.2 mmol) and H2NH2BDC (0.0362 g, 0.2 mmol) was dissolved in DMF (10 ml), Et3N (0.05 ml) was then added. The mixture was stirred for 30 min in air, and then sealed in a 15-mL Teflon-lined reactor and kept at 160 °C for 120 h. The autoclave was cooled over a period of 12 h at a rate of 10 °C h−1 to room temperature. Yellow hexagonal crystals of 2 were collected by filtration, washed with water, and dried in air (yield 70%, based on Cd). This complex was stable in air without crystal collapsing and insoluble in common organic solvents such as methanol, ethanol, dichloromethane, chloroform and DMSO. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C42H56Cd3N8O20.5: C, 37.13; H, 4.26; N, 8.45. Found: C, 37.18; H, 4.21; N, 8.39%.

X-Ray crystallography

All diffraction data were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. All intensity data were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects (SAINT), and empirical absorption corrections based on equivalent reflections were applied (SADABS). The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2 with SHELXTL program package.18 For all structures, the refinements revealed two-fold disorder of the amino group of ligands. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters except for the disordered guest molecules and the amino group in 2. Conformationally disordered ligands were treated with split-atom models. The disordered guest molecules were subjected to geometric restraints during the refinements. The crystallographic details and selected bond lengths and bond angels are provided in Tables 1 and 2. CCDC reference numbers 666002 and 666003 for 1 and 2, respectively. Synthesis

Although we successfully prepared the 3-D MOF of 1 using Zn(II) ions with a conventional evaporation method, 1-D chain compound was obtained by a similar method by using Cd(II) ions. This fact may be ascribed to the different coordination behaviour of Zn(II) and Cd(II) ions, since the terminal Zn(II) ions adopt tetrahedral geometries while Cd(II) prefers a high coordination number than four. Fortunately, we were then able to prepare a Cd(II) 3-D MOF (2) by using a solvothermal reaction, in which a similar trinuclear cluster is found (see Fig. S2 in the ESI).† In the trinuclear cluster in 2, the terminal Cd(II) ions are each ligated by a chelate carboxylate end rather than an aqua ligand in 1, thus each terminal Cd(II) ion is hexa-coordinated.

Crystal structures

Single crystal X-ray analysis shows that 1 is a 3-D MOF composed of the SBUs of Zn3(COO)6. This SBU contains a pseudo centrosymmetrical, linear array of three Zn(II) atoms, being similar to that reported very recently.6 The asymmetric unit consists of three Zn(II) ions. The central Zn(II) atom is octahedrally coordinated by six oxygen atoms from six NH2BDC carboxylate groups, while each of the two terminal Zn(II) atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three carboxylate groups and one aqua ligand (Fig. 1, see also Fig. S1 in the ESI for complete coordination structural unit).†
Fig. 1 Schematic view of the SBU (zinc, green; oxygen, red; carbon, grey), node and the pcu topology in 1.

The lengths of Zn-O bonds of the central Zn2 atom (2.251(9)–2.278(7) Å) are obviously longer than those of the terminal Zn(II) atoms (1.930(6)–2.028(9) and 1.929(6)–2.035(7) Å for Zn1 and Zn3, respectively, see Table 2). For the terminal, highly distorted tetrahedrally Zn(II) atoms, the longest Zn–O bond is associated with the aqua ligand. All the three NH2BDC ligands adopt a bis-bidentate bridging mode, in which the amino group exhibits a 2-fold disorder. The bridging NH2BDC ligands link the SBUs into a 3-D porous framework, which is similar to MOF-3 with different SBUs.15 The square and triangular channels (see Fig. 2) of 1 are filled with guest DMF molecules. The solvent accessible volume in 1 is estimated with PLATON19 to be ca. 53% of the full crystal volume. The net of 1 has a well-known pcu topology (Schläfli model: 412·63) when considering the linear trinuclear SBU as a six-connected node. It is worth noting that, when the four terminal ethanol ligands in the SBU of MOF-3 were replaced by other solvent molecules, such as DEF and DMA, as well as the coordination geometries of terminal zinc(II) ions were transferred from six-coordinate to five- or four-coordinate, the 3-D frameworks of MOF-3 analogues were changed into 2-D frameworks.16,17a,b In contrast, when using NH2BDC in place of BDC as the bridging ligands, together with water molecules as the terminal ligands, 1 features a 3-D framework. This fact may be attributed to the spatial hindrance of the amino group in NH2BDC, which link the adjacent SBUs alternatively at the head and end positions, or in the head-to-end fashion (see Fig. S6 in the ESI),† being different from the head-to-head fashion found in the related 2-D frameworks documented previously.16,17a,b This fact implies that the modification of the bridging ligands may have effect on controlling the overall structures of the coordination networks.


Fig. 2 Space-filling views of channels in 1 (a) parallel to 1 0 −1 plane; (b) along the c-axis. Colour coding: zinc, green; oxygen red; carbon, grey; nitrogen, blue; hydrogen, white).

Compound 2 also has a 3-D porous framework, featuring crystallographically centrosymmetric, linear trinuclear SBUs that are dissimilar to those found in 1 (Fig. 3, see also Fig. S2 in the ESI for complete coordination structural unit).† However, each terminal Cd(II) atom is now ligated by a chelate carboxylate group, and the structural unit becomes Cd3(COO)8. The central Cd2 atom is located at a crystallographic inversion center, adopting an octahedral coordination geometry with the Cd–O distances ranging from 2.183(5) to 2.367(4) Å (see Table 2). Unlike the terminal tetrahedral Zn(II) atoms in 1, each terminal Cd(II) atom assumes a highly distorted octahedral coordination geometry with the Cd–O distances ranging from 2.2055(5) to 2.456(14) Å (see Table 2), where six oxygen atoms are from four carboxylate groups: two chelate and two bridging ones. The eight NH2BDC ligands in the SBU exhibit three coordination modes: four bridging bis-bidentate, two chelating/bridging bis-bidentate, and two chelating bis-bidentate. Among them, the terminal carboxylate groups were 2-fold disordered. If the chelating bis-bidentate carboxylate groups at the terminal Cd(II) ions of the SBUs are neglected, these trinuclear SBUs are connected by the NH2BDC bridges into a 2-D net (the blue layer in Fig. 2) having a 36 tessellated 2-D sheet (see Fig. S7 in the ESI),† which is similar to those of the Zn(II), Mg(II) or hetero-trinuclear carboxylate MOFs.16,17 However, the chelating bis-bidentate NH2BDC bridges in 2 link at the terminal positions of the SBUs in adjacent tessellated layers, thus pillar the layers into a 3-D MOF with two-directional, intersecting channels (Fig. 4). These channels are filled with the Me2NH2+ cations, DMF and lattice water molecules. The Me2NH2+ cations are generated by the decomposition of DMF. The channel volume in 2 is estimated with PLATON19 to be ca. 52% of the full crystal volume. Taking each linear trinuclear SBU as one node, the net has a rare hex-like topology20 (Schläfli model: 36·414·57·6) having uninodal eight-connected nodes. It should be noted that half number of the pillars adopt a vertical fashion while the others adopt slanted fashion to the tessellated 2-D sheets (red sticks in Fig. 3). In contrast, all related pillars adopt the vertical fashion in a regular hex net.20

Thermal stability

Thermogravimetirc analysis (TGA) of 1 (see Fig. S3 in the ESI)† shows a weight loss of 32.2 wt% that is attributed to the loss of 5 DMF molecules per formula unit (calcd. 32.2 wt%), and no further weight loss during 250–350 °C. This fact suggests that the framework of 1 may serve as a robust porous material.

The TGA profile of 2 (see Fig. S4 in the ESI)† exhibits the first weight loss of 14.3 wt% between 30 and 240 °C, corresponding to the loss of the 2.5 H2O and 2 DMF molecules per formula unit (14.4 wt%, calcd.), and then the loss of 2 Me2NH neutral molecules per formula unit between 240 to 310 °C (found 6.5 wt%, calcd. 6.7 wt%). However, after removal of the Me2NH molecules, the 3-D framework becomes protonated and thus unstable. Consequently, there was another abrupt weight loss between 310 to 330 °C (found 13.3%, calcd. 13.5 wt%), corresponding to the loss of one H2NH2BDC molecule per formula unit, which led to the collapse of the 3-D framework.

Gas sorption property

Prior to gas sorption experiments, the guest solvent molecules were removed by thermal activation at temperatures of 150 and 180 °C under vacuum over 20 h. The PXRD patterns of 1 (see Fig. S5 in the ESI)† after removal of the guest and absorption were very similar to that of the as-synthesised sample, although remarkably weakened intensities were observed. This fact also indicates that 1 has a very good framework robustness. The porosity and surface areas of 1 were estimated by nitrogen gas sorption isotherms at 77 K, as shown in Fig. 5. The N2 gas sorption reached near-saturation at low relative pressures (P/Po < 0.1) and thereafter increases very slowly up to the pressure of 1 atm. These are typical of a Type I isotherm behaviour for a microporous material. There is no significant hysteresis between sorption and desorption traces. With an approximation based on a monolayer condensation of adsorbed N2 molecules on a uniform surface, the sorption isotherms were fitted to a Langmuir equation and gave surface areas of 570 and 550 m2 g−1 (BET surface area was 530 and 510 m2 g−1) for 180 or 150 °C, respectively. The apparent surface area of 1 is comparable to those of most open inorganic zeolites, but is remarkably larger than that of structurally analogous MOF-3 (140 m2 g−1), which may be attributed to the replacement of four large terminal EtOH molecules in MOF-3 by the two smaller water molecules in 1 per formula unit.
Fig. 5 Nitrogen gas sorption/desorption isotherm at 77 K for 1 after degassing over 20 h at different activated temperatures. P/P0 is the ratio of gas pressure (P) to saturation pressure (P0 = 750 mmHg). V is the adsorbed volume.

Unfortunately, despite 2 holding an apparent solvent accessible volume ratio of ca. 39% (estimated by PLATON considering only the solvent molecules), it has no significant adsorption for N2 gas upon desolvation by long-time thermal activation under vacuum or after exchanging the higher boiling guest (DMF and H2O) with lower boiling molecule (MeOH and CH2Cl2) by soaking before degassing at room temperature under vacuum.21 This fact indicates that the channels are clogged by the large, unremovable Me2NH2+ cations.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully obtained two new microporous MOFs containing different linear trinuclear metal carboxylate cores of Zn3(COO)6 and Cd3(COO)8 as the SBUs under different reaction conditions, which feature 3-D pcu and hex-like frameworks, respectively, and have considerable solvent accessible volumes. Compound 1 has a reasonably large Langmuir surface area, while a larger surface area may be obtained with a higher activating temperature.
