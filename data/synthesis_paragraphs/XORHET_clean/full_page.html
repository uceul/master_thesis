Structural diversity of five new bitriazole-based complexes: luminescence, sorption, and magnetic properties†

Li-Yun Du , Hui Wang , Ge Liu , Dong Xie , Fu-Sheng Guo , Lei Hou * and Yao-Yu Wang
Key Laboratory of Synthetic and Natural Functional Molecule Chemistry of the Ministry of Education, Shaanxi Key Laboratory of Physico-Inorganic Chemistry, College of Chemistry & Materials Science, Northwest University, Xi'an 710069, P. R. China. E-mail: lhou2009@nwu.edu.cn

Received 11th October 2014 , Accepted 5th November 2014

First published on 5th November 2014

Employing an uninvestigated eight N-donor bitriazole ligand, 5′-(pyrazin-2-yl)-2H,4′H-3,3′-bi(1,2,4-triazole) (H2pzbtz), five complexes, {[Zn2(pzbtz)(SO4)(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1), {[Cd(pzbtz)]·3H2O}n (2), [Zn3(Hpzbtz)2(SO4)2(H2O)6] (3), [Co(Hpzbtz)2(H2O)2] (4) and {[Co2(pzbtz)2(H2O)2]·4H2O}n (5), have been solvothermally synthesised by altering the reaction solvents and temperatures. Complex 1 shows a three-dimensional (3D) framework with a very rare (3,5)-connected fsc-3,5-Cmce-2 topology. Complex 2 exhibits a (4,4)-connected 3D pts framework with 1D channels. Complexes 3 and 4 are discrete trinuclear and mononuclear molecules, respectively. Complex 5 presents a chiral 3D framework based on a novel trinuclear Co3(tr)4 cluster and features an unusual six-connected lcy topology. In the five complexes, H2pzbtz show a very variety of geometrical configurations and coordination modes. Due to the planarity of H2pzbtz, complexes 1–3 display strong photoluminescence; in particular, 1 features highly selective luminescent sensing for the Cu2+ ion and nitrobenzene molecule. In addition, 2 shows highly selective adsorption for CO2 over CH4, and 5 exhibits antiferromagnetic exchange interactions among the Co2+ ions.

Introduction

Increasing attention has recently been paid to coordination polymers (CPs) due to their intriguing architectures and potential applications as materials for catalysis,1 separation,2 drug delivery,3 sensors,4 and in electronic5 and magnetic devices.6 Although much effort has focused on the design and synthesis of CPs with novel structures and functional properties, it still remains a challenge to fabricate desired CPs due to the effects of many experimental factors such as pH,7 solvent,8 and temperature.9 Meanwhile, because the structures of CPs are closely related to the coordination geometries of metal centres and binding fashions of organic linkers, it is necessary to design appropriate organic ligands to synthesise diverse and novel structures by changing metal centres and/or tuning the external experimental parameters.

1,2,4-Triazole (Htz) attracts considerable interest from chemists because it combines the coordination geometries of pyrazole and imidazole. In particular, the convenient modification at the 3- and/or 5-positions makes Htz an excellent linker for the assembly of CPs. In this regard, many works have investigated the coordination of Htz derivatives with N-containing substituent groups such as 2-pyridyl and 2-pyrazinyl at the 3- and/or 5-positions, revealing various coordination modes with a wide range of transition metal ions.10 Based on these results, it would be a more appealing project to investigate the coordination chemistry of bi-1,2,4-triazole derivative systems due to their more changeable geometric configurations and coordination modes. However, bi-1,2,4-triazole derivative-based complexes have only been sporadically studied.11 A good example is the ligand H2pbt = 5′-(pyridin-2-yl)-2H,4′H-3,3′-bi(1,2,4-triazole), which possesses one 2-pyridyl group and seven N-donor atoms and exhibits multiform coordination modes with Mn2+, Co2+, Cu2+, and Cd2+ ions, generating versatile frameworks. In these complexes, 2-pyridyl serves as a chelating site with one central triazole N atom.11b–f Therefore, upon incorporating the 2-pyrazinyl group into bi-1,2,4-triazole, a new ligand with more coordination sites is accessible; however, related studies have not been conducted.

Herein, 5′-(pyrazin-2-yl)-2H,4′H-3,3′-bi(1,2,4-triazole) (H2pzbtz), an uninvestigated bi-1,2,4-triazole derivative that contains one 2-pyrazinyl group, is designed because the multipoint N-coordinated atoms including three potential bidentate chelating sites and two monodentate coordination sites can build versatile structures. In particular, three rings in H2pzbtz can rotate around the interplanar C–C bonds, which not only generates four different configurations (cis–cis, cis–trans, trans–cis and trans–trans; Scheme 1), but also gives rise to diverse structures. In this study, for the first time, we functionalised H2pzbtz with different metal ions by varying the reaction temperature and solvent to yield five new complexes: {[Zn2(pzbtz)(SO4)(H2O)2]·2H2O}n (1), {[Cd(pzbtz)]·3H2O}n (2), [Zn3(Hpzbtz)2(SO4)2(H2O)6] (3), [Co(Hpzbtz)2(H2O)2] (4), and {[Co2(pzbtz)2(H2O)2]·4H2O}n (5). As expected, H2pzbtz in 1–5 shows various geometrical configurations and coordination modes. The structures and luminescence, gas sorption, and magnetic properties of 1–5 were investigated in detail.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

All solvents and starting materials were purchased commercially and were used as received. Infrared spectra (IR) were obtained using KBr discs on a Nicolet Avatar 360 FTIR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were determined with a PerkinElmer 2400C Element Analyzer. Photoluminescence analyses were performed on a Hitachi F4500 luminescence spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in an N2 stream using a Netzsch TG209F3 instrument at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray powder diffractometer (Cu Kα, 1.5418 Å). Sorption isotherms were measured using an ASAP 2020 M adsorption instrument. Samples were activated by heating under vacuum to remove solvent molecules prior to the measurements. Magnetic measurements were carried out on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL-7 SQUID magnetometer.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

The formation of CPs is closely related to the reaction temperatures, solvents, metal ions, counter anions and so on.14 The same reactants in different solvents or at different temperatures can form diverse structures, providing direct evidence for the structural influence of these external factors. Complexes 1–5 failed to form at room temperature due to the insolubility of H2pzbtz in common organic solvents at room temperature. In contrast, 1–5 were successfully synthesised by solvothermal reactions (Scheme 2). 1 and 3 were respectively prepared at 145 °C in mixed H2O–CH3CN (7:2) and H2O–ethanol (5:5) solvents. The different products mainly result from the different solubilities of ZnSO4 and H2pzbtz in H2O–CH3CN and H2O–ethanol solvents as well as the different polarities of the solvents. Similarly, the different solubilities of H2pzbtz in water at different temperatures also induce the different products 4 (105 °C) and 5 (160 °C); the high temperature facilitates H2pzbtz to act as a dianionic ligand to form the 3D framework of 5, while at relatively low temperature, the monoanionic Hpzbtz generates discrete mononuclear 4. The formation of different products (4 and 5) at different temperatures implies that their formations are thermodynamically controlled.

Crystal structure of 1

Complex 1 crystallises in the monoclinic space group P21/c, and the asymmetric unit consists of two independent Zn2+ ions, one pzbtz, one SO42− anion, two water ligands, and two lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, all Zn2+ ions are six-coordinated with slightly distorted octahedral geometries. Zn1 is surrounded by four N atoms of three pzbtz, one O atom of SO42−, and one water ligand. Zn2 is coordinated by three N atoms of two pzbtz, one O atom of SO42−, and two water molecules. One SO42− bridges two Zn2+ ions, and pzbtz is almost planar and exhibits a cis–cis geometric configuration (Scheme 1a). Each pzbtz links five Zn2+ ions and features a trans coordination mode for the two chelating sites formed between the central triazolyl ring and the two outer rings. Along the a axis, the adjacent Zn1 and Zn2 are alternately connected by the triazolyl of pzbtz to form 1D helical chains with opposite chiralities (Fig. 1b). The chains are further extended by pzbtz to yield a 3D framework. In the framework, the 1D channel is formed along the 101 direction (Fig. 2b), wherein the wall is surrounded by 18-membered metallamacrocycles (Fig. 2a). The channels are occupied by SO42− anions, leading to small voids of 18.5%13 after the removal of all water molecules as well as a narrow window of sizes of about 4.1 × 2.8 Å2 (excluding van der Waals radii of the atoms). Topologically, pzbtz can be simplified as a 5-connected node, while Zn1 and Zn2 atoms can be simplified as 3- and 2-connected nodes, respectively; thus, the framework can be designated as a very rare binodal (3,5)-connected fsc-3,5-Cmce-2 topology with point symbol (4·62)(4·66·83) by TOPOS.15 Notably, this topology has only been observed in three different types of known coordination polymers.16

Crystal structure of 2

Complex 2 crystallises in the monoclinic space group P21/c and exhibits a 3D framework with 1D channels. The asymmetric unit contains one Cd2+ ion and one pzbtz. As shown in Fig. 3a, the Cd2+ ion displays a distorted octahedral coordination geometry involving six N atoms; four of them are provided by two pzbtz in chelating fashion, and the remaining two (N2B, N8C) are from two monodentate pzbtz. In 2, pzbtz displays a trans–trans geometric configuration (Scheme 1d) with a trans coordination mode for its two chelating sites, differing from the situation of pzbtz in 1. Interestingly, the interlinkages of the four pzbtz, eight Cd2+ ions, two pyrazinyl and two triazolyl units of the four different pzbtz produce a cage (Fig. 3b). The neighbouring cages are further interlinked to form a 3D framework in which the 1D channels are formed along the c axis with voids of 31.3%13 and window sizes of about 4.0 × 3.9 Å2 (Fig. 3c). Topologically, each Cd2+ ion in 2 is a distorted four-connected tetrahedral node linked by four pzbtz, while each pzbtz links four Cd2+ ions and can be simplified as a square-planar four-connected node. Thereby, the framework of 2 can be rationalised as a binodal (4,4)-connected pts net with the same point symbols 4284 (Fig. 3d), but with the different long vertex symbols 4·4·82·82·88·88 and 4·4·87·87·87·87 for pzbtz and Cd2+, respectively.17

Crystal structure of 3

Complex 3 crystallises in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit contains 1.5 Zn2+ ions, one Hpzbtz, one coordinated SO42− anion, and three water ligands. As shown in Fig. 4, 3 is a discrete trinuclear molecule with a 21 axis threading Zn1 and O1w atoms. In the trimer, the central Zn1 atom locates on the two-fold axis and displays an octahedral coordination geometry defined by four N atoms of two Hpzbtz in the chelating modes and two axial water O atoms. The outer Zn2 atom is trigonal-bipyramidally (τ = 0.625)18 coordinated by two chelating N atoms of one Hpzbtz, one O atom of SO42− anion, and two water O atoms. One pyrazinyl N (N7) and one water O (O3w) occupy the axial sites of the coordination sphere. In 3, Hpzbtz shows a trans–trans geometric configuration (Scheme 1d) with a trans coordination mode for the two chelating sites. In the crystal packing, the trimer forms multiple intra- and inter-molecular O–H⋯O, O–H⋯N and N–H⋯O hydrogen-bonding interactions (Table S2†) due to the existence of strong hydrogen-bonding donors/acceptors such as SO42−, water, the uncoordinated pyrazinyl N8 atom and the outer triazolate N1 and N2 atoms of Hpzbtz, affording a 3D supramolecular framework (Fig. S1†).
Fig. 4 The coordination environment of Zn2+ ion in 3; symmetry codes: A: −x, −y, −z.

Crystal structure of 4

Complex 4 crystallises in the monoclinic space group C2/c and contains 0.5 Co2+ ion, one Hpzbtz, and one coordinated water in the asymmetric unit. The entire molecule is a mononuclear structure with a symmetric centre at the Co2+ ion. As shown in Fig. 5, the Co2+ ion is octahedrally coordinated by two pairs of chelating N atoms of two Hpzbtz and two axial water O atoms. In 4, Hpzbtz displays a cis–cis geometric configuration (Scheme 1a). In one molecule, each water forms two intermolecular O–H⋯N hydrogen-bonding interactions with the triazolyl N6 and N3 atoms of Hpzbtz, while the uncoordinated pyrazinyl N8 atom forms an intermolecular C–H⋯N interaction with the –CH group in the outer triazolyl of Hpzbtz, giving rise to a 2D layer parallel to the ab plane (Fig. 6a). Along the c axis, the neighbouring layers were stabilised by interlayer N–H⋯N interactions between –NH (N2) groups and N4 atoms of different Hpzbtz, yielding a 3D supramolecular framework (Fig. 6b).

Crystal structure of 5

Complex 5 crystallises in the tetragonal space group P43212 and displays a homochiral 3D framework based on trinuclear clusters. The flack value is 0.034(16). The asymmetric unit contains three independent Co2+ ions, two pzbtz, and two water ligands (Fig. 7a). Both Co2 and Co3 atoms locate on the cell ab plane with 50% site occupancies. All Co2+ ions adopt slightly distorted octahedral geometries: Co1 is coordinated by five N atoms of three pzbtz and one water O atom; Co2 is surrounded by six N atoms of four pzbtz, four of which are from two pzbtz (N9, N12, N9C, N12C) and adopt the chelating fashion; and Co3 is ligated by four N atoms from two chelating pzbtz (N14, N15, N14D, N15D) and two water ligands. In 5, pzbtz displays a cis–trans geometric configuration (Scheme 1b) with a trans coordination mode for the two chelating sites. Interestingly, two Co1 and one Co2 are bridged by four outer triazolyls of four pzbtz to form a trinuclear Co3(tr)4 cluster (Fig. 7b), which is previously undocumented according to the latest CCDC research (version 5.35). Each cluster in 5 is further connected with four Co1 and two Co3 atoms through six pzbtz, affording a 3D framework (Fig. 8). Along the c axis, Co1 atoms are bridged by N1-containing pzbtz to form homochiral left-handed 41 helical chains with helical pitches of 17.503 Å, equalling the c-axis length (Fig. 8a); thus, the framework is chiral.
Fig. 7 (a) The coordination environment of Co2+ ion in 5; symmetry codes: A: 3/2 − x, −1/2 + y, 1/4 + z; B: x − 1/2, 3/2 − y, 1/4 − z; C: x, y, −z; D: 2 − x, 2 − y, 1/2 − z. (b) The linking environment of the trinuclear cluster.

Fig. 8 (a) The left-handed 41 helical chains, (b) 3D framework along the c axis, and (c) lcy net of 5.

Topologically, each cluster in 5 can be simplified as a six-connected triangular prism node (Fig. S2a†), and both pzbtz and Co3 can be viewed as two-connected nodes; therefore, the overall framework features a six-connected lcy topology with point symbol 335963 (Fig. 8c). Although the six-connected nets frequently occur in CPs, lcy is rarely reported because it requires the uncommon triangular prism node. Compared to other well-known six-connected uninodal nets such as pcu (41263), roa (446108), and rob (48668) nets, which are based on four-membered rings, the most striking feature of the lcy topology is the presence of three-membered and five-membered rings. The design and synthesis of lcy topology is still considered to be an interesting challenge.19 In 5, seven trinuclear nodes form a cage consisting of one triangular face and three pentagonal faces (Fig. S2b†). The cage mainly accommodates the pyrazinyl rings of pzbtz; therefore, the framework possesses an almost closed porous system and is nonporous for gas molecules such as N2, H2 and CO2 despite the presence of 35.8% accessible free voids.13

Coordination feature of H2pzbtz

In complexes 1–5, H2pzbtz displays various coordination features, which plays a crucial role in the diverse structures of 1–5. In 1 and 4, the H2pzbtz have the same cis–cis geometric configuration (Scheme 3a and d), but with the bi-chelating and mono-chelating coordination modes as anions with charges of −2 and −1, respectively. As a result, the higher dimensional 3D framework for 1 and the mononuclear structure for 4 are formed. In contrast, in 2 and 3, H2pzbtz exhibits the same trans–trans geometric configuration and the same trans mode for the two chelating coordination sites (Scheme 3b and c). However, the additional coordination of other triazolyl (N2) and pyrazinyl (N8) N atoms leads to the higher dimensional 3D framework in 2, while the discrete trinuclear molecule is formed in 3. Meanwhile, although H2pzbtz in 2 and 5 possess the same trans coordination mode for the two chelating sites (Scheme 3b and e), they display different trans–trans and cis–trans geometric configurations, respectively; this difference is mainly responsible for the different 3D patterns of 2 and 5. In addition, in 1–5, the H2pzbtz are anionic linkers with charges of −2 and −1; the former forms 3D frameworks in 1, 2 and 5, while the latter produces zero-dimensional molecules in 3 and 4. Therefore, it is a practicable strategy to construct various H2pzbtz-based metal–organic frameworks by controlling the solvent and pH values of the reaction media.

PXRD and thermal analyses

The good matches between the experimental PXRD patterns of complexes 1–5 and the simulated ones from the single crystal structures confirm the phase purity (Fig. S3†). TGA was performed in N2 atmosphere on polycrystalline samples of 1–5 (Fig. S4†). TGA shows that 1 releases two water ligands and two water guests below 320 °C with a total weight loss of 14.1% (calcd 14.1%); it then exhibits a plateau within 320–400 °C. Beyond 400 °C, an abrupt weight loss indicates the departure of pzbtz. 2 shows two weight loss steps from 30–85 °C (6.9%) and 160–210 °C (6.7%), corresponding to the removal of three lattice water molecules (calcd 14.3%). A stable plateau follows until 520 °C; this temperature is very high and is close to ZIF-8,20 but it greatly surpasses the reported values for most MOFs (below 400–450 °C). Complex 3 loses 12.5% weight before 205 °C, corresponding to the release of six water ligands (calcd 11.7%); no further weight loss is observed before 360 °C, and the framework then begins to decompose above this temperature. 4 possesses a slow 7.7% weight loss from 30–240 °C, corresponding to the removal of two coordinated water molecules (calcd 6.9%); subsequently, no obvious weight loss is observed until the framework decomposes at 300 °C. For 5, the preliminary 16.9% weight loss from 30–230 °C is ascribed to the escape of all water molecules (calcd 16.6%), and no further weight loss occurs before the framework decomposition at 400 °C. The TGA results clearly verify that the high-dimensional 3D frameworks of 1, 2 and 5 have higher decomposition temperatures than the zero-dimensional molecules 3 and 4. This can be attributed to the more coordinated N atoms and the more negative charges of H2pzbtz in 1, 2 and 5, inducing rigid frameworks in these molecules.

Sorption properties

The excellent thermal stability of complex 2 encourages us to evaluate its porosity. The desolvated 2 was obtained by heating 2 at 140 °C for 3 h followed by heating at 175 °C for 2 h under vacuum. PXRD confirms the structural intactness of 2 after desolvation. The CO2 and CH4 sorption isotherms were measured at 195 K. As shown in Fig. 9, the desolvated 2 displays a reversible type-I sorption isotherm for CO2 with an uptake of 71.5 cm3 (STP) g−1 at 760 Torr and a Langmuir surface area of 360 m2 g−1. However, the CH4 sorption isotherm reaches only 19.1 cm3 (STP) g−1 and displays an obvious hysteresis, which probably results from the hindered escape of CH4 in the narrow channels of 2 due to approximate sizes of CH4 (3.8 × 4.1 × 3.9 Å3) and the channel (4.0 × 3.9 Å2).21 The significant selectivity for CO2 over CH4 can be attributed to the smaller kinetic diameter of CO2 (3.30 Å) compared to CH4 (3.80 Å); therefore, CO2 is able to diffuse into the pore more easily. Meanwhile, the large quadrupole moment of CO2 (−4.30 B) compared to CH4 (nonpolar) facilitates CO2⋯framework interactions. The adsorption enthalpies (Qst) of 2 for CO2 were calculated from the virial method by fitting adsorption isotherms at 273 and 293 K (Fig. S5 and S6†). The Qst gradually increases from 23.9 kJ mol−1 at zero loading to 24.9 kJ mol−1 at 1 atm; the zero-loading Qst (23.9 kJ mol−1) is comparable with those of some MOFs containing acylamide or amine groups.4a,22 The increasing trend of Qst with the pressure of CO2 in 2 could be attributed to the intermolecular CO2⋯CO2 interactions in the narrow channels, encouraging the CO2 molecules to be closer together.23
Fig. 9 Gas sorption isotherms of 2 for CO2 and CH4 at 195 K. The inset shows the CO2 sorption isotherms at 273 and 293 K.

In addition, gas adsorption measurements were carried out for 1 and 5; however, no gas adsorption was observed for N2, H2, and CO2, possibly due to the narrow channels filled by SO42− groups in 1 and the closed porous system in 5.

Luminescent properties

H2pzbtz in complexes 1–5 shows good planarity and can functionalise as a luminescent source due to the highly conjugated system. The solid state luminescent properties of free H2pzbtz and 1–3 (Fig. 10) were detected at room temperature. H2pzbtz shows an emission band at 420–520 nm with a peak at 450 nm (λex = 360 nm). 1–3 exhibit bright luminescence under UV radiation (Fig. 10) as well as the strong luminescence with a maximum emission at 461 nm (λex = 360 nm), 414 nm (λex = 372 nm), and 417 nm (λex = 360 nm), respectively, matching well with their luminescent colours under UV radiation. These emissions can be assigned to the intraligand π*→π and π*→n transitions in H2pzbtz. In addition to the effect of the different metal ions in 1–3, their luminescent differences can be mainly ascribed to the different coordination features of H2pzbtz. In 2 and 3, the H2pzbtz exhibit the same trans–trans geometric configuration and the same trans mode in the two chelating coordination sites; thus, they possess similar emission energies. In contrast, the cis–cis geometric configuration and cis chelating coordination mode of H2pzbtz in 1 lead to an obvious red shift in the emission band compared to those of 2 and 3. The lifetime curves reveal double exponential decays for H2pzbtz, 2 and 3 and a single exponential decay for 1 (Fig. S7†). The lifetimes are 1.29 and 6.28 ns for H2pzbtz, 6.52 ns for 1, 2.35 and 4.66 ns for 2, and 1.55 and 3.59 ns for 3.
Fig. 10 Solid-state emission spectra of H2pzbtz and 1–3 at room temperature. The insets are snapshots of 1–3 under a UV lamp.

The very bright luminescent colour of complex 1 inspires us to explore it as a potential luminescent sensor for organic solvent molecules and metal ions. 1 was immersed in methanol, ethanol, n-propanol, DMF, CH3CN, acetone, THF, CH2Cl2, DMSO, CHCl3, ethylacetate, benzene, methylbenzene, chlorobenzene, bromobenzene and nitrobenzene (NB) to test the emulsions of these complexes after ultrasonic agitation for 30 min. As shown in Fig. 11a and b, the luminescent intensity of 1 is dependent upon the different solvent molecules. Interestingly, the luminescence of 1 was completely quenched by NB. To further evaluate the sensing ability of 1 toward NB, the desired amounts of NB were titrated gradually into the emulsion of 1 in DMF (5 mg 1 in 5 mL DMF). As shown in Fig. 12a, the luminescent intensity of the emulsion gradually decreases with the addition of NB. This quenching behaviour might be due to the electron transfer from the triazolyl and pyrazinyl rings of H2pzbtz in 1 to electron-deficient NB and the interactions between the framework and NB molecules.4g,24

Fig. 12 (a) Emission spectra of 1 in DMF with different amounts of NB (insets: colour changes of 1 in DMF after the addition of NB (0.73 mM) under UV light and the plot of intensity versus NB concentration); (b) emission spectra of 1 in DMF with Cu(NO3)2 at different concentrations and the colour changes of 1 in DMF after adding Cu2+ (10−2 M) under UV light; and (c) emission spectra of 1 in DMF with different amounts of Cu2+ (insets: colour changes of 1 in DMF after adding Cu2+ (7.0 mM) under UV light and the plot of intensity versus Cu2+ concentration).

Meanwhile, the potential of 1 as a luminescent sensor for metal ions was also investigated. 1 (5 mg) was immersed in DMF solution containing 10−2 M of different nitrate cations (KNO3, NaNO3, Ca(NO3)2, Mg(NO3)2, Zn(NO3)2, Co(NO3)2, Ni(NO3)2, Mn(NO3)2, Cu(NO3)2, Cd(NO3)2, Al(NO3)3, Eu(NO3)3, Tb(NO3)3). As shown in Fig. 11c and d, all metal ions decrease the luminescent intensity of 1; in particular, Cu2+ exhibits significant quenching effects compared to the other ions, indicating the highly selective sensing of Cu2+ by 1. A sensitive sensor must provide both qualitative and quantitative detection of the target molecule. The quenching effect in 1 was further examined in different Cu(NO3)2 solutions with concentrations ranging from 10−6 to 10−2 M. As illustrated in Fig. 12b, the luminescent intensity of 1 is almost completely quenched at a Cu (NO3)2 concentration of 10−2 M. Furthermore, the titration experiment verifies that the increased amount of Cu2+ ion causes a gradual decrease in the luminescent intensity of 1 (Fig. 12c). Cd2+, Zn2+, alkali, and alkaline-earth metal ions possessing closed-shell electron configurations (filled d10 or p6 configurations) have trivial effects on the luminescent intensity, whereas the other metal ions with odd electron configurations (Mn2+, Ni2+, Co2+ and Cu2+) often produce different degrees of luminescent intensity quenching.25 However, the highly selective luminescent quenching effect for only Cu2+ ion observed in 1 has rarely been reported.26 This unique quenching effect is possibly attributed to the interactions between Cu2+ ion and the uncoordinated N atoms of H2pzbtz in 1; this provides a significant passageway for electrons to transfer from 1 to Cu2+ ion, which is an excellent electron acceptor, inducing the luminescent quenching.27

The luminescent intensity at 450 nm versus the volume ratio of Cu2+ ion or NB can be fit well by a single exponential function (Fig. 12a and c, inset), establishing a diffusion-controlled process for the Cu2+ ion and NB luminescent quenching.28 These highly selective luminescent quenching phenomena suggest that 1 can serve as an excellent luminescent sensor to identify NB molecule and Cu2+ ion.

Magnetic properties

Considering the formation of the uncommon trinuclear Co3(tz)4 cluster in complex 5, the variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility (χM) of 5 was examined in the 2–300 K temperature range under an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe. As shown in Fig. 13, the χMT value for one Co2+ ion at 300 K is 2.86 cm3 K mol−1. This value greatly surpasses that for one spin-only S = 3/2 Co2+ system (1.875 cm3 K mol−1), implying a significant orbital contribution from the high-spin Co2+ ion. As the temperature decreases from room temperature, the χMT values of 5 continuously decline to 0.22 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K, indicating that antiferromagnetic coupling between Co2+ ions is dominant.
Fig. 13 Plots of χM and χMT vs. T for 5. Red lines correspond to the best fit according to the equation.30

In 5, the Co2+ ions are linked by two types of bridges. Compared to the magnetic exchange pathway of Co–N–N–Co, the Co⋯Co magnetic interactions produced by the N–C–N bridge are smaller but cannot be ignored; thus, it is difficult to analyse the detailed magnetic interactions in the complicated 3D system. Herein, the Curie–Weiss law is adopted, and the temperature dependence of the reciprocal susceptibilities (χM−1) above 25 K can be fit to the model with θ = −27.74 K and C = 3.12 cm3 mol−1 K (Fig. S8†). Although the value of θ is slightly overestimated due to the contribution of the anisotropy of the octahedral Co2+ centres, the negative sign of the Weiss constant and the near zero value of χMT at low temperature confirm the presence of antiferromagnetic interactions between Co2+ ions, similar to other Co2+ cluster-based frameworks.29

To estimate the intensity of antiferromagnetic exchange interactions (J), the magnetic data of 5 from 10 to 300 K were fit by a simple phenomenological equation, χMT = A × exp(−E1/kT) + B × exp(−E2/kT),30 where A + B equals the high-temperature Curie constant and E1 and E2 represent the “activation energies” corresponding to the spin–orbit coupling and antiferromagnetic exchange interactions, respectively. The least-squares analysis gave the following parameters: A + B = 3.14 cm3 K mol−1 (A = 1.28 cm3 K mol−1, B = 1.86 cm3 K mol−1), which is consistent with the Curie constant (C = 3.12 cm3 K mol−1); E1/k = 68.75 K (E1/k on the order of +100 K) for the effect of spin–orbit coupling; and E2/k = 6.32 K for antiferromagnetic exchange interactions, which corresponds to J = −12.64 K based on the approximation of χmT = exp(J/2kT). The moderate J value is comparable with the reported values for some Co2+-containing species31 and demonstrates the moderate antiferromagnetic interactions between the Co2+ ions.

Conclusions

In summary, five new complexes were assembled using a multiple N-donor planar H2pzbtz ligand. In 1–5, we successfully captured three types of geometric configurations of H2pzbtz, including cis–cis in 1 and 4, trans–trans in 2 and 3, and cis–trans in 5, as well as different cis or trans coordination modes for its two chelating sites. These features lead to versatile structures from the discrete 0D molecules of 3 and 4 to the 3D frameworks of 1, 2 and 5. 1 shows an uncommon (3,5)-connected fsc-3,5-Cmce-2 topology, 2 is a binodal (4,4)-connected pts net, and 5 displays a rare 6-connected lcy topology based on the novel trinuclear Co3(tr)4 cluster. Meanwhile, 1–3 exhibit strong luminescence with different emission energies resulting from the various coordination features of H2pzbtz. Most importantly, 1 shows highly efficient luminescent sensing for Cu2+ ion and NB. 2 reveals very high thermal stability and significantly selective adsorption for CO2 over CH4. 5 displays antiferromagnetic interactions. This study provides a less-investigated assembly method for novel CPs by employing multipoint N-coordinated bitriazole systems. Studies on the fabrication of various CPs such as sorption and magnet materials by utilising more functionalised bitriazole derivatives are underway.
