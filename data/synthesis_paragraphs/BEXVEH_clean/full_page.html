Coordination polymers of flexible poly-carboxylic acids with metal ions. IV. Syntheses, structures, and magnetic properties of polymeric networks of 5-(3,5)-(dicarboxybenzyloxy)isophthalic acid with Cd(II), Cu(II), Co(II) and Mn(II) ions†

Ranjan Patra , Hatem M. Titi and Israel Goldberg *
School of Chemistry, Sackler Faculty of Exact Sciences, Tel-Aviv University, Ramat-Aviv, 69978 Tel-Aviv, Israel. E-mail: ranjanpatra06@gmail.com; goldberg@post.tau.ac.il

Received 11th December 2012 , Accepted 8th February 2013

First published on 11th February 2013

Four new framework solids consisting of coordination polymers [Cd2(L)(H2O)2(DMA)]n (1), [Cu2(L)(H2O)2]n (2), [Co2(L)(H2O)5]n (3) and [Mn2(L)(H2O)5]n (4) (H4L = 5-(3,5-dicarboxybenzyloxy)isophthalic acid; DMA = dimethyl acetamide) have been synthesized under hydro(solvo)thermal conditions and characterized by X-ray crystallography. In all cases, the metal-ion connectors consist of di-nuclear clusters. The fully de-protonated flexible tetradentate organic linker assumes a flat conformation with laterally oriented carboxylate functions in 1 and 2, but a twisted conformation with one carboxyphenyl residue rotated by nearly 90° with respect to the other in the isomorphous structures of 3 and 4. The resulting polymeric metal–organic lattices in 1 and 2 are perforated by wide (accounting for more than 50% of the crystal volume) solvent-accessible channel voids. In 3 and 4 the organic linker is characterized by tetrahedral tetra-carboxylate functionality, inducing the formation of open polymeric architectures with diamondoid geometries which interweave into one another in the crystal to fill most of the empty space. The remaining voids are filled by molecules of water. Thermal analysis (TGA and DTA) features of 1–4, nitrogen gas sorption behavior of 2 and solid-state variable temperature magnetic susceptibility data of 3 and 4 are reported as well.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are receiving considerable attention in the field of solid state materials1 owing to their intriguing structural diversity and wide applications in gas storage and separation, catalysis, drug delivery, luminescence, and magnetism.1–4 Synthesis of coordination polymers with desired magnetic properties is extremely important with an ever growing number of useful applications. A significant number of coordination polymers exhibiting ferromagnetic,5 antiferromagnetic,6 spin canting,7 metamagnetism,8 single chain magnets9 and spin glass behavior10 have been observed. Coordination complexes with rigid polycarboxylic acids which provide reliable coordination modes, such as benzene-tricarboxylic acid and benzenetetracarboxylic acid, have been widely studied.11 The rigidity of the organic linker is particularly beneficial for the generation of porosity because it cannot permit structural distortion of the framework.12 On the other hand, some conformational freedom when imparted to the ligand used in the assembly process may offer various possibilities for the release of the steric strain imposed by the metal–ligand association and perhaps even more facile supramolecular syntheses.13

Following our earlier efforts of MOFs synthesis with the relatively rigid porphyrin tetracarboxylic acids,14 we have also recently engaged in a parallel avenue of crystal engineering investigations with more flexible tetracarboxylate scaffolds.15 This involves creating non-rigid ligands with four diverging tetracarboxylic acid groups by appending two dicarboxylic acid aromatic species (e.g., the isophthalate moieties) through a flexible spacer entity. In the above context, the present study focuses on the 5-(3,5)-(dicarboxybenzyloxy)isophthalic acid ligand (H4L, Scheme 1) and its crystalline coordination polymers with cadmium(II) [Cd2(L)(H2O)2(DMA)]n·S (S = intra-lattice solvent) (1), copper(II) [Cu2(L)(H2O)2]n·S (2), cobalt(II) [Co2(L)(H2O)5]n·S (3) and manganese(II) [Mn2(L)(H2O)5]n·S (4), as well as their magnetic properties.

Experimental

All the chemicals used for the syntheses were commercially available reagents of analytical grade and were used without further purification. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the 4000–400 cm−1 range on a Nicolet 5DX spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were taken on a Perkin-Elmer Pyris l (30–600 °C, 5 °C min−1, flowing N2(g)). The nitrogen sorption isotherm was measured using the Quantachrome Nova 1001 volumetric adsorption equipment. The magnetic data were collected on a Quantum Design MPMS SQUID-XL-5 magnetometer using crushed single-crystal samples. Magnetic data were corrected for the diamagnetic contribution calculated from Pascal constants16 and the background of the sample holder.

Synthesis of H4L

Diethyl 5-hydroxy isophthalate (240 mg, 1.1 mmol) was dissolved in 20 ml of acetonitrile and then anhydrous K2CO3 was added. The mixture was heated to reflux after diethyl 5-bromomethyl isophthalate (352 mg, 1 mmol) was added. The whole reaction mixture was refluxed for 6 h and then the solvent was removed and water was added to yield a white solid (yield 356 mg, 75%). The second step is to dissolve the isolated ester (2.36 g, 5 mmol) and NaOH (0.6 g, 15 mmol) in 20 mL of ethanol–water (4:1). The reaction mixture was refluxed for 4 h at 80 °C, after which the solvent was removed under reduced pressure. 10 mL of water was added into it and the solution was acidified (pH = 2) with a dilute HCl solution. The obtained white solid product H4L was filtered and washed with water until it was free from acid. Yield: 62%. FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3286 (bs, γO–H), 2987 (mb, γC–H), 1602 (s, γCO asymmetric), 1542 (s, γCC), 1360 (m, γCO symmetric), 1261 (s, γC–O), 1034 (w), 1024 (s), 907 (w), 776 (s), 721 (w), 594 (m); 1H-NMR (DMSO-d6): 8.51 (2H, s), 8.34 (4H, s), 8.18 (2H, s), 7.84 (4H, s), 5.48 (2H, s).

Synthesis of compound 1

A mixture of H4L (7.2 mg, 0.02 mmol) and CdCl2·2½H2O (8.8 mg, 0.04 mmol) was dissolved in 6 mL of dimethyl acetamide (DMA). The mixture was heated for 48 h in a bath-reactor at 120 °C and after cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals were obtained, separated by filtration, washed with water and DMA and then dried in air (yield 45% based on the metal salt). FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3290 (bs, γO–H), 2987 (mb, γC–H), 1602 (s, γCO asymmetric), 1543 (s, γCC), 1360 (m, γCO symmetric), 1261 (s, γC–O), 1034 (w), 1024 (s), 907 (w), 776 (s), 721 (w), 594 (m), 437 (w).

Synthesis of compound 2

A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·2½H2O (11.6 mg, 0.05 mmol) and H4L (7.2 mg, 0.02 mmol) was dissolved in 8 ml of DMA–water (1:1). Then, 0.5 ml of 12(N) HNO3 was added. The mixture was heated for 48 h in a bath-reactor at 80 °C and after cooling to room temperature, green crystals were obtained, separated by filtration, washed with water and DMA and then dried in air. The yield was 52% based on the metal salt. FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3369 (br, γO–H), 2988 (mb, γC–H), 1587 (s, γCO asymmetric), 1454 (s, γCC), 1366 (m, γCO symmetric), 1261 (s, γC–O), 1019 (w), 776 (s), 731 (w), 593 (m), 475 (w).

Synthesis of compound 3

A mixture of H4L (7.2 mg, 0.02 mmol) and Co(NO3)2·6H2O (11.6 mg, 0.04 mmol) was dissolved in 10 mL of DMA–water (1:1). The mixture was heated for 24 h in a bath-reactor at a temperature of 120 °C and after cooling to room temperature, pink crystals were obtained, separated by filtration, washed with water and DMA and then dried in air. The yield was approximately 51% based on the metal salt. FT-IR (KBr, cm−1): 3285 (bs, γO–H), 2984 (s, γC–H), 1542 (s, γCO asymmetric), 1446 (s, γCC), 1366 (m, γCO symmetric), 1261 (s, γC–O), 1039 (w), 916 (w), 773 (s), 708 (w), 592 (m), 484 (w).

Synthesis of compound 4

A mixture of H4L (7.2 mg, 0.02 mmol) and MnClO4·6H2O (14.2 mg, 0.04 mmol) was dissolved in 10 mL of DMA–water (1:1). The mixture was heated for 24 h in a bath-reactor at 120 °C and after cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals were obtained, separated by filtration, washed with water and DMA and then dried in air. The yield was approximately 49% based on the metal salt. Crystal structure determinations

The X-ray measurements [Nonius-KappaCCD (1, 2 and 4) and Bruker-ApexDuo (3) diffractometers, Mo–Kα radiation] were carried out at ca. 110(2) K on crystals coated with a thin layer of amorphous oil to minimize crystal deterioration, possible structural disorder and related thermal motion effects, and to optimise the precision of the structural results. These structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares (SIR-97 and SHELXL-97).17,18 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms were located in idealized/calculated positions and were refined using a riding model. Some of those involved in hydrogen bonds were located directly in the difference-Fourier maps but were not refined. Compounds 1–4 were found to contain disordered crystallization solvent (DMA and water) within the intra-lattice voids. The solvent species could be clearly recognized in the difference electron-density maps, but could not be reliably modelled by discrete atoms due to their severe disorder in the interstitial voids. Correspondingly, the contribution of the disordered solvent moieties was subtracted from the diffraction pattern by the SQUEEZE procedure and PLATON software.19 However, the crystallographic refinements of the metal–organic frameworks converged at acceptable R-values, providing a reliable description of the coordination schemes. Another source of disorder involves the central C–O bond. In the resolved structural models of 1–4, molecules of the ligand are located on centers of inversion, revealing a twofold disorder of this bond.

The uniform identity of the formed crystal lattices in a given reaction were confirmed in each case by spectroscopic data as well as by the uniform morphology of the crystals in the bulk, repeated measurements of the unit-cell dimensions from different single crystallites and powder X-ray diffraction data (for 3 and 4; Fig. S1 and S2 in the ESI†).

FT-IR spectra

The FT-IR spectra of compounds 1–4 are displayed in the ESI† (Fig. S3 to S6). They show broad absorption at 3200–3500 cm−1, which indicates the presence of coordinated water molecules. The strong band at 1360–1650 cm−1 of all the compounds is characteristic of the carbonyl groups. The absorption at 1261 cm−1 is attributed to the C–O–C stretching vibration of the Cphenyl–O–C groups. In the 490–420 cm−1 range, there are some medium and weak bands that could be assigned to the γ(M–O) stretching vibrations.

Results and discussion

Parts I–III of this series have been reported in ref. 15a–c. The principal conformational degree of freedom of ligand L investigated here involves possible rotation of the two peripheral carboxyphenyl rings about the central Cphenyl–O–C–Cphenyl fragment of the ligand. The terminal carboxylate functions may also adapt a range of orientations with respect to the respective aryl residues they are bound to, although co-planarity of these moieties is prevalent as it optimizes π-electron delocalization. In 1, the two ligand species of the asymmetric unit are located on centers of crystallographic inversion and their two aromatic fragments are coplanar. The four carboxylate groups are coplanar with the corresponding aryl rings, diverging in lateral directions. Every flat organic linker coordinates to four different metallic nodes. The inorganic connectors take the form of dinuclear Cd2(COO−)4(H2O)2(DMA) clusters, each one of them associating with four different ligands (Fig. 1). The Cd-ions are six-coordinate, one of them binding to all four ligands while the other to only two of the ligands, two molecules of water and a molecule of DMA. The Cd–O coordination distances are within 2.211(3)–2.523(3) Å and the Cd⋯Cd distance with each cluster is 3.518(2) Å.
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination pattern around the di-nuclear Cd2 connecting node in 1. The four different organic linkers are denoted by L. (b) Metal–ligand connectivity scheme of the two independent ligands in the asymmetric unit of 1. Note the diverse coordination modes of the ligands to the two metal ions: chelating bi-dentate, μ2-η1-η1-bridging and μ2-η2-η1-bridging.15a Only one orientation of the twofold disordered ligands is shown.

In the vicinity of a given di-nuclear connector in 1, two of the ligands are aligned on one plane, while the other two lie on a perpendicular plane. Such normal alignment of the organic moieties along with the 4:4 node-linker coordination stoichiometry gives rise to the formation of a square-grid three-dimensional metal–organic framework (Fig. 2a). It is perforated by wide intra-lattice channel voids along the a- and c-axes of the crystal. The former are filled by the metal-coordinated DMA ligands that protrude into them, while the latter are accommodated by disordered molecules of non-coordinated DMA solvent. The distance between the centers of the ligands that line the walls is approximately 10.5 Å wall-to-wall and the corresponding solvent-accessible void in this lattice is nearly 55% (assessed by PLATON,19 given that the Cd-coordinated DMA is part of the framework) of the crystal volume. The topology of this framework solid has been analyzed with the TOPOS (v. 4.0) software, reducing the complex structure to a simpler node-and-linker net.20 It can be best represented as a complex (3,4,6,8)-connected 4-nodal net, having a Schlafli symbol of {43}2{44.62}2{46.88.10}{48.64.815.10} (Fig. 2b).


Fig. 2 (a) The actual crystal structure of 1 viewed approximately down the c-axis and showing the solvent-accessible channels in the structure. The Cd-ions are denoted by spheres and the ligands by the wireframe. (b) Simplified view (approximately down the a-axis of the crystal) of the framework architecture in 1, showing channel voids which accommodate the metal-coordinated DMA moiety. The blue lines represent the organic linkers and the purple centers denote the metal ions.

The crystal structure of 2 is considerably more complex, yet it is characterized by a more symmetrical coordination scheme. The asymmetric unit consists of four molecules of the ligand (two of them located in general positions and the other two on inversion centers) and six copper(II) ions. The organic linker is nearly planar, as in the preceding example, and the three crystallographically independent connecting nodes are of the well documented Cu2(COO−)4(H2O)2 paddle-wheel type. The inner metal⋯metal distances within the di-nuclear clusters are 2.6551(5), 2.6556(5) and 2.6979(5) Å. Each such Cu2 pair is coordinated to four carboxylate functions of four different ligands at Cu–Ocarboxylate distances within 1.911–2.018(2) Å, as well as to two molecules of water at 2.167–2.239(2) Å. The organic linkers are aligned parallel to either the (1 1 0) or the (1 −1 0) plane of the crystal, each of them coordinating through their carboxylate end groups to four different di-nuclear metal-ion connectors.

The rigid three-dimensional coordination framework thus formed (Fig. 3) is characterized by open interstitial voids, which provide the driving force for the inclusion of the solvent species within them. The PLATON-calculated solvent accessible void is relatively large in 2, nearly 65% of the crystal volume (slightly less if all the H-atoms of the metal-coordinated water molecules would be included in the final structural model). The topology of the empty framework in 2 can be represented as a complex 5,8-c 2-nodal net with a stoichiometry of (5-c)2(8-c), having the Schlafli symbol: {34.46}2{34.616.88}.


Fig. 3 (a) The paddle-wheel-type inter-coordination synthon in 2. (b) Crystal structure of the open metal–organic framework in 2. The Cu-ions are denoted by spheres. For clarity, the paddle-wheel patterns at some metallic nodes are schematically depicted by dotted lines without displaying the associated organic linkers in full. Only one orientation of the ligands centered on inversion is shown.

Compounds 3 and 4 are isomorphous and thus only the Co-derivative will be discussed here in more detail. As opposed to the earlier two examples, the observed conformation of the organic linker in 3 and 4 is characterized by the nearly perpendicular orientation of the carboxyphenyl rings. This imparts a pseudo-tetrahedral functionality to the ligand as its four carboxylate functions now point in tetrahedral directions. Coordination of the multi-dentate ligand with either the cobalt or manganese ions in these structures also results in the formation of three-dimensional polymeric assemblies. The framework connector units in this case are also di-nuclear. They are represented by the (Co/Mn)2(COO−)4(H2O)5 cluster, wherein two of the carboxylates attach to the metal centers in a monodentate fashion and the two other in a chelating bidentate manner (Fig. 4). The octahedral coordination sphere around each metal ion is supplemented by two molecules of water and another water moiety which bridges between the two metal ions. In 3, the Co–O distances are within 1.995–2.159(3) Å and in 4, the corresponding Mn–O distances are 2.072–2.279(2) Å. The ligand species and the bridging water molecule are located on axes of twofold rotation.


Fig. 4 Coordination pattern around the dinuclear metallic node and the organic linker in 3. The Co-ions are denoted by larger spheres, while the metal-coordinated molecules of water are depicted by smaller spheres. A single orientation of the disordered ligand is shown.

Inspection of the crystal structure reveals that the coordination of the four carboxyphenyls around the Co2 metal center can also be characterized by a pseudo tetrahedral geometry. The tetrahedral functionality of both the organic linker as well as the inorganic connector leads to the formation of an open diamondoid framework which extends throughout the crystal (Fig. 5a). The large voids within the thus formed frameworks are partly filled by the interpenetration of two symmetry-related nets (Fig. 5b) and partly by molecules of non-coordinated (disordered) solvent. Due to the interpenetration, the solvent accessible voids in 3 and 4 are considerably smaller (29% and 30% of the crystal volume, respectively) than in the previous examples (Fig. 6). The topology of the resulting structure can be formally characterized as a (2,4,6)-connected 3-nodal net with the Schlafli symbol {42.610.83}{43.62.8}2{4}.

Thermal analysis

TGA and DTA analysis of the complexes (see Fig. S7–S10 for compounds 1–4 in the ESI†) were performed under a N2 atmosphere by varying the temperature to about 600 °C at heating rate of 5 °C min−1. 1 exhibits weight loss of 3.6% near 76 °C due to the release of the uncoordinated water molecules. A further weight loss of 14.3% in the 100–320 °C range accounts for the removal of the coordinated water and DMA molecules (calcd.: 13.9%). Decomposition of this material occurs above 360 °C. The TGA curve of compound 2 also exhibits weight loss of 12.9% around 43 °C, corresponding to the escape of the uncoordinated water molecules. At the temperature range of 150–185 °C, a further 19.75% weight loss corresponds to the desorption of the coordinated water species (calcd.: 18.85%). Decomposition of 2 occurs above 318 °C. The TGA curves of compounds 3 and 4 within 100–150 °C show a weight loss of 15.3% and 16.5%, respectively, which corresponds to the release of three coordinated water molecules (the corresponding calculated values are 14.8% and 15.6%). Decomposition of these interwoven frameworks was observed only above 500 °C.

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibility of compound 3 was measured in the temperature range of 5–300 K under a 500 Oe field (Fig. 7a). The measured XMT value at 300 K is 4.94 cm3 K mol−1, which is higher than the spin-only value of 3.75 cm3 K mol−1 for two isolated Co(II) ions with a spin value 3/2 and Landé g value of 2. This result is consistent with the well-documented orbital angular momentum contribution of the octahedral Co(II) ion with a 4T1g ground state at high temperature.21,22 On decreasing the temperature, the XMT value decreases smoothly to a value of 4.40 cm3 K mol−1 at 100 K, then reaches a minimum of 2.02 cm3 K mol−1 at 5 K. The XM−1vs. T plot gives a straight line, indicating the paramagnetic behavior of the complex (see Fig. S11 in the ESI†). The fitting of the data obeys the Curie–Weiss law, resulting in a Curie constant C = 1.12 cm3 K mol−1 and Weiss constant θ = −48.5 K. The large negative Weiss constant value indicates the presence of anti-ferromagnetic coupling between the Co(II) ions, although the spin–orbit coupling and anisotropy can also contribute to this value. The individual Co(II) ions in 3 are in a distorted octahedral geometry and the Co–Owater–Co angle is 109.8(8)°. It has been generally observed in similar clusters that such large-angle geometry gives rise to a negative value for the magnetic exchange interaction.23 The negative θ value indicates dominating anti-ferromagnetic interactions between the Co(II) ions via the Co/O–C–O(carboxylate)/Co and Co–O(oxo)–Co pathways.23 Although the Co⋯Co distances are very small (3.542 Å), there are no ferromagnetic interactions observed between these metal centers. Weak ferromagnetic interactions have been reported in the literature for cases with smaller Co–O–Co angles within 96–99°.24
Fig. 7 Plots of the temperature dependence of XMT and XM for compounds (a) 3 and (b) 4.

The magnetic susceptibility of compound 4 was measured in the temperature range of 5–300 K under a 500 Oe field. The plots of XMT and XMvs. T are shown in Fig. 7b. The experimental XMT value at 300 K is about 7.84 cm3 K mol−1, slightly lower than the spin-only value of 8.76 cm3 K mol−1 for two magnetically isolated high-spin Mn(II) ions with a spin value of 5/2 and Landé g value of 2.21,22 The XMT vs. T plot decreases slowly as temperature goes down and at temperatures below 50 K, the decrease in the XMT product is more pronounced. Upon cooling down to 5 K, the value is 2.83 cm3 K mol−1. The inverse susceptibility plot as a function of temperature (see Fig. S12 in the ESI†) is linear above 80 K, following the Curie–Weiss law with a Weiss constant of θ = −87.5 K. The high negative Weiss constant suggests that predominantly antiferromagnetic interactions exist between the two Mn center through oxo and carboxylate bridges, as observed previously in a similar context.25 Both Mn(II) ions are in a distorted octahedral geometry and the water-bridged dinuclear cluster is characterized by a Mn1–O–Mn2 angle of 109.8(8)°.

To characterize the low-temperature behaviors of compound 3 and compound 4, the temperature dependencies of the field-cooled (FC) and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization were performed under a 500 Oe field. As shown in Fig. 8, on warming the samples from 5 K, the FC curves are consistent with the ZFC curves and the magnetizations increase continuously on decreasing the temperature. No maxima were observed for both the complexes. These observations indicate the absence of any long-range magnetic ordering in the two complexes.

Gas absorption study

The permanent porosity of guest-free (activated) Cu-complex 2 was confirmed by sample gas adsorption measurements. Based on TGA analyses (vide supra), the crystal sample of 2 was dried under a high vacuum at 100 °C for 24 h to remove the lattice-included solvent. The dehydrated sample of 2 was placed in a quartz tube to measure the nitrogen adsorption and its adsorption–desorption behavior is presented in Fig. 9. The type-I isotherm26 (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry classification) observed for the N2 adsorption at 77 K confirms the micro-porosity of the activated Cu-complex. The process of N2 adsorption–desorption showed very good reversibility without hysteresis. Approximately 83.12 mL of N2 was adsorbed per 1.0 g of the de-solvated sample at 1 atm. The calculated BET surface area of 2 for nitrogen sorption is relatively small, 232.07 m2 g−1, in relation to what might have been expected for the open-framework crystal structure of 2, wherein the solvent accessible space is about 65% of the crystal volume. Compounds 3 and 4 showed limited porosity and negligible sorption features towards di-nitrogen and were not analyzed any further.

Concluding remarks

This study explores the attractive coordination chemistry of the flexible H4L tetra-acid ligand and in particular, the utility of this organic linker in the crystal engineering of three-dimensional coordination polymers. It involves the synthesis, detailed crystallographic characterization and measurements of the magnetic properties of four new framework solids obtained to this end. The flexible nature of the title ligand allows for diverse coordination modes to the metal ion connectors by adjusting the shape of the ligand to the spatial coordination requirements of the di-nuclear inorganic centers. The presence of multiple diverging carboxylic acid functions facilitates the supramolecular reaction simultaneously in different directions and their easy de-protonation in a suitable reaction environment accounts for the charge balance without the need to incorporate foreign counter ions into the polymeric lattice. Two conformational modes of the ligand were observed in the analyzed products. The flat conformation in 1 and 2, with the four carboxylate functions arranged in a pseudo-square-planar manner, which gives rise to metal–ligand frameworks perforated by wide channels (lined by the flat ligand) propagating through the structure in nearly perpendicular directions. The rotated conformation in 3 and 4, where one carboxyphenyl residue is nearly perpendicular to the other and the four carboxylate functions are oriented in tetrahedral directions. The latter conformer is expressed in reactions with di-nuclear metal centers adopting matching tetrahedral coordination geometry. In all the resulting materials, solvent molecules from the reaction mixture (water, DMA) were found to supplement (in an unpredictable manner) the coordination requirements of the given metal ion connectors. The particular solvent mixture was used not only to solubilize the reacting components but also to induce the formation of di(multi)-nuclear metal centers,27 leading to successful formulations of open three-dimensional metal–organic frameworks in the present study.

It is of particular interest to relate the porous structure of compound 2 to the metal–organic frameworks involving similar di-nuclear Cu2 paddle-wheel connectors and the closely related organic linkers trans-stilbene-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate and 5,5′-methylene-di-isophthalate (known respectively as PCN-11 and PCN-12).28a,b The organic linkers in PCN-11 and in 2 have a nearly identical planar shape and functionality and are located on centers of crystallographic inversion. In both cases, the resulting frameworks are characterized by a considerable porosity and cuboctahedral/cube-like solvent/gas accessible intra-lattice cages. The latter are slightly larger in PCN-11 than in 2 and reveal considerably higher porosity of di-nitrogen, possibly due to the higher rigidity and stricter inversion symmetry of the stilbene derivative. PCN-11 crystallized in a rhombohedral space group as opposed to the somewhat more condensed monoclinic intermolecular arrangement in 2.28a According to this comparison, the added flexibility in the present ligand (in relation to the rigid stilbene linker in PCN-11) negatively affected the gas-sorption capacity of the porous coordination network in 2. The PCN-12 materials involved the 5,5′-methylene diisophthalate linker in two non-planar conformations, which were found to be interlinked into porous 3D framework structures through similar dinuclear-Cu2 paddle-wheel synthons.28b In the Cs conformer of that ligand, the two benzene rings are perpendicular to each other, as in 3 and 4, while in the C2v variant the diisophthalate is bent at the central methylene C-atom, with the two phenyl rings facing one another with a dihedral angle of about 110° between their planes. The two polymorphs of PCN-12 and compound 2 exhibit the conformational diversity of the diisophthalate organic linkers, which when combined with the dinuclear-Cu2 paddle-wheel connectors, results in metal–organic frameworks of different architectures. After completion of the present work, additional results on the structural versatility of coordination polymers constructed from the tetracarboxylate ligand L have been reported.29

Following the above findings by others and us, one may tentatively conclude that the careful design of flexible tetra-acid linkers is an attractive avenue to consider in crystal engineering efforts of hybrid organic–inorganic MOF materials and their potential applications.
