Dynamic microporous indium(III)-4,4′-oxybis(benzoate) framework with high selectivity for the adsorption of CO2 over N2†

Yan-Xi Tan , Fei Wang , Yao Kang and Jian Zhang *
State Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, The Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, P. R. China. E-mail: zhj@fjirsm.ac.cn

Received 27th September 2010 , Accepted 13th October 2010

First published on 10th November 2010

Presented here is a dynamic microporous indium(III)-4,4′-oxybis(benzoate) framework material constructed from dual flexible building units, showing high selectivity for the adsorption of CO2 over N2.

Three-dimensional (3D) metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with well-defined pores have been paid great attention recently owing to their potential applications in catalysis, gas storage, chemical separation, and ion exchange.1–9 The design of microporous frameworks is mainly dependent on the selection of suitable organic ligands and inorganic secondary building units (SBUs).1a,4 One kind of famous microporous framework materials is the MIL-53 system developed by Férey and co-workers,2a–c whose frameworks show an abnormal “breathing effect” toward different guest species. The structure of MIL-53 interconnects the infinite trans chains of corner-sharing MO4(OH)2 (M = Al3+ or Cr3+ or In3+) octahedra by terephthalate ligands, creating 1D rhombic-shaped tunnels.2 Here, terephthalate is a rigid linear ligand and the “breathing effect” of the MIL-53 framework is mostly related to the flexible linkage between the metal centers and the carboxylate groups. Further exploration indicated that such dynamic framework materials with specific polar groups, e.g. –OH, –COOH or –NH2, have outstanding selective adsorption capacities of CO2 and may help to resolve the critical issue in many technological processes, for instance the capture of CO2 in postcombustion flue gases from coal-fired power stations.10

In this work, we are particularly interested in introducing a flexible organic ligand to replace terephthalate in MIL-53 and further adjust the “breathing effect” from both ligand and metal coordination approaches. As a typical flexible V-shaped dicarboxylate ligand, 4,4′-oxybis(benzoic acid) (=H2obb) was selected into the self-assembly with indium salts under different synthetic conditions. The use of In3+ ion (not Al3+ or Cr3+) for the reaction is because the synthesis can be performed at a lower temperature and suitable single crystals can be obtained easily. Although the coordination chemistry of the obb ligand has been widely explored,11,12 only one anionic CdSO4-type In-obb framework synthesized ionothermally was known to date.12 A further investigation on such a self-assembly system under solvothermal conditions leads to a distinct neutral In-obb framework material [In2(OH)2(obb)2]·2DMF (1) which has the very similar one-dimensional inorganic substructures presented in MIL-53. The combination of flexible inorganic backbones and flexible ligands in the structure of 1 results in an interesting “breathing effect” towards different guest species. Remarkably, this dynamic material has high selectivity for the adsorption of CO2 over N2 at 0 °C and ambient pressure, showing that it is a potential candidate for the capture of carbon dioxide from flue gas.13

Colorless crystals of 1 were synthesized by mixing H2obb, In(NO3)3·xH2O and tetrapropylammonium bromide in DMF solvent at 120 °C for 2 days.‡Single-crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that 1 crystallized in the space groupFddd.§ The structure of 1 features infinite chains of corner-sharing InO6 octahedra linked by the V-shaped obb ligands. Although it adopts the similar chain-like building blocks as MIL-53, the connectivity of such infinite chains in 1 is totally different from that in MIL-53.2 The V-shaped coordination of the obb ligand plays a dominant role in the linkage of the chains. Each obb ligand bridges two perpendicular chains and the dihedral angle in the obb ligand is 64.9° (Fig. 1a). Meanwhile, such two perpendicular chains are four-fold fixed by four obb ligands, generating an unusual large cage. The cage is irregular, but a ball of radius of 3.5 Å can be fully filled into it. The perpendicular assembly between the chains in 1 makes up another special rod-and-spacer building motif different from MIL-53 where each chain is only linked to four adjacent chains. Each chain in 1 acts as an inter-connector of countless parallel chains from two separated planes (Fig. 1b). This also results in the formation of open channels with dimensions of approximately 5.7 × 5.8 Å2 along the (110) direction (Fig. 1c).


Fig. 1 (a) The connectivity between two perpendicular chains in 1, showing the generated void illustrated by a yellow ball; (b) schematic representation of the 3D framework of 1; (c) one segment with the 1D channel filled by guest DMF molecules in the 3D framework of 1.

What is particularly noteworthy is the large void in the framework with a pore volume ratio of 47.7%, excluding guest DMF molecules, as calculated by PLATON.14 The free spaces in the framework are occupied by the structural disordered DMF guest molecules, equal to two DMF guest molecules per In2(OH)2(obb)2 unit, as evidenced by thermogravimetric (TG) analysis data. The TG curve of 1 shows a weight loss of 22.9% from 20 to 250 °C, corresponding to the release of DMF guest molecules (expected 22.0%) (Fig. S5†). No weight loss was observed between 250 and 350 °C, suggesting that no chemical decomposition occurred between the desolvating and ligand-releasing temperatures. These results also prove that DMF guest molecules can be completely removed.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) experiments were further performed to investigate the stability of 1 upon removal of guest molecules according to the TG analysis. In the PXRD pattern of the guest-free sample, the peak of [111] shifts to high 2θ values compared to that of the as-synthesized material, which is consistent with the shrinking of pore size after removal of all the guest molecules. Interestingly, the structural change associated with guest removal is fully reversible. The PXRD pattern of the guest-free microcrystalline sample after soaking in DMF for 1 day is exactly the same as that for the as-synthesized material; therefore the host framework shrinks back upon guest inclusion (Fig. 2h).


Fig. 2 XRPD patterns of simulated 1 (a), as-synthesized 1 (b), guest-free of 1 (c), dried crystals 1 immersed in water (d), methanol (e), ethanol (f), propanol (g), DMF (h), benzene (i), and cyclohexane (j).

The results encourage us to further check steric hindrance inclusion properties of the guest-free sample for other guest molecules with different sizes and to examine if the framework can reversibly expand the window size upon the above process. The crystalline sample of 1 was dried under vacuum at 180 °C for 7 h to give a desolvated form 1-ht confirmed by IR analysis (Fig. S7†), and then it was immersed in water (H2O), methanol (MeOH), ethanol (EtOH), propanol (PrOH), benzene (C6H6) and cyclohexane (C6H12), respectively, for 1 day. Comparison of PXRD patterns between the guest-free sample, the as-synthesized material and the immersed products show three distinct states. Since the peak shift directly reflects the structural change, the main [111] peak is identified to explain the different structural stages (Fig. 2 and S8†). Firstly, the uptake of MeOH or H2O in 1-ht made the [111] peak shift from 7.3° to 6.9°, leading to a middle stage between 1 and 1-ht (Fig. 2d–e). Secondly, the uptake of EtOH, PrOH, or DMF in 1-ht almost recovered the host framework of 1 according to their similar PXRD patterns (Fig. 2f–g). Thirdly, no change in XRPD patterns are observed for 1-ht after immersing in both C6H6 and C6H12 (Fig. 2i–j). From the above guest uptake experiments, the dynamic characterization of the host framework is confirmed. It is interesting to note that different guests can control the recovery degree of the origin framework in 1. To clearly illustrate such a special feature, Fig. 3 is presented. The shrunken hollow pore in 1-ht becomes larger and larger with the uptake of H2O, MeOH, EtOH and PrOH, respectively. The shift of the [111] peak of different inclusion samples (1-ht: 7.3°; 1-H2O: 6.9°; 1-MeOH: 6.9°; 1-EtOH: 6.7°; 1-PrOH: 6.6°; 1-DMF: 6.6°) clearly demonstrated this trend. For the non-polar solvent molecules, C6H6 and C6H12, they may not enter the pores in 1-ht because of the small size of the pore or repulsion from the hydrophilic framework.


Fig. 3 Schematic representation of the “breathing pore” in 1-ht.

The permanent porosity of 1-ht was established by reversible gas sorption experiments using N2 and CO2 at 77 K and 195 K, respectively, which all show a typical type I behavior characterized by a plateau reached at low relative pressure (Fig. 4a–b). The maximum N2 and CO2 uptake at 1 atm for 1-ht are 128 and 83.4 cm3 g−1 (STP), respectively. N2 adsorption data gives a BET surface area of 354.1 m2 g−1 and a Langmuir surface area of 518.5 m2 g−1. Two types of pores exist in 1-ht. A large portion of these pores, which fall in the range of ultramicropores (pore diameter <7 Å), have a pore diameter less than 5.5 Å and a small portion of the pores have a pore diameter about 6.8 Å (Fig. S9†). The appearance of different pore sizes is due to the flexible property of the obb ligand which maybe spread out in the presence of a strong sorbate-sorbent interaction.15 On the other hand, the micropore filling of gas is described well by the Dudinin-Radushkevich (D-R) equation (Fig. S10–11†). The isosteric heat of adsorption qst,=1/e of 1-ht for N2 and CO2 are 12.23 and 31.03 kJ mol−1, respectively.


Fig. 4 Gas adsorption isotherms for 1-ht: (a) N2 at 77 K, (b) CO2 at 195 K, (c) CO2 at 273 K and (d) N2 at 273 K.

To demonstrate the potential of this dynamic framework material in CO2/N2 gas separation, the adsorption isotherms of CO2 and N2 for 1-ht at 0 °C were measured (Fig. 4c and d, respectively). The uptake value of CO2 was 39.3 cm3 g−1, which corresponds to 77.2 mg g−1, whereas N2 was hardly adsorbed at all (1 cm3 g−1 or 1.2 mg g−1). Although the CO2 adsorption capacity at 1 atm is lower than that of some other recently reported MOFs,16 the material showed an excellent ideal CO2/N2 adsorption selectivity of 64 at STP (calculated by weight percentage). To our knowledge, MOFs with such a high CO2/N2 adsorption selectivity are rarely reported.16a

This apparent selectivity for CO2 is presumably attributed to the small pores which restrict the adsorption of gases and give rise to a diffusion barrier. For CO2, this diffusion barrier can be overcome by extremely favourable host–guest interactions. From the consideration of the structural feature of 1, such gas-adsorption selectivity is also closely associated with the combination of an interrupted, cage-based structure and the strong chemical interactions of CO2 with corner-sharing hydroxy groups in the pores. The presence of the –OH groups provide opportunities for the formation of O(CO2)⋯H(–OH) hydrogen bonds to yield an enhanced lone pair polarization of the CO2 electron density,10a which also highlights the impact of the specific pore geometry in the structure of 1.

In summary, following the construction strategy of MIL-53, the replacement of the rigid terephthalate ligands to the flexible obb ligands leads to another unprecedented dynamic microporous framework material with dual flexible elements (inorganic backbone and ligand). The material exhibited an interesting breathing effect to different guest species. What is most important is this material has high selectivity in the adsorption of CO2 over N2 and thus shows great potential for gas-separation applications.
