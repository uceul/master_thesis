Synthesis and stabilization of a hypothetical porous framework based on a classic flexible metal carboxylate cluster†

Yong-Sheng Wei , Jian-Qiang Shen , Pei-Qin Liao , Wei Xue , Jie-Peng Zhang * and Xiao-Ming Chen
MOE Key Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry, School of Chemistry & Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou 510275, P. R. China. E-mail: zhangjp7@mail.sysu.edu.cn

Received 7th September 2015 , Accepted 4th November 2015

First published on 4th November 2015

By using pyridyl derivatives 4-cyanopyridine (4-pyCN) or nicotinate (na−) as terminal ligands (LT) to decorate the flexible trigonal-prismatic trinuclear metal carboxylate M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 clusters, a theoretically predicted uninodal 6-connected flu-e network for [M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(bdc)3(LT)3] (H2bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid) has been realized in three new porous coordination frameworks. The flu-e topology is isomeric to the uninodal 6-connected acs (MIL-88) and tetranodal 6-connected mtn-e (MIL-101) ones observed in the classic metal carboxylate framework materials, but comprises of unique cubic cages which require exceptional conformation for the trinuclear clusters. The weak coordinating 4-pyCN terminal ligands tend to leave the clusters during thermal activation, leading to framework distortion and reduction of the long-range order of the flu-e network, which can only be restored by 4-pyCN instead of other guest molecules. On the other hand, the carboxylate ends of the adjacent na− ligands coordinate with additional metal ions to crosslink the coordination networks as new binodal 4,9-connected networks with remarkably enhanced thermal/chemical stability and porosity.

Introduction

Porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been studied extensively because of their highly designable framework and pore structures, as well as their interesting flexibility for external stimuli.1–7 One of the most successful strategies for constructing PCPs with the desired structures/topologies is to use rigid metal carboxylate clusters with well-defined geometries as secondary building units (SBUs) to reduce structural uncertainty during self-assembly and crystallization.7–14 The diversified conformations of soft or flexible building blocks (organic ligands or inorganic clusters) are generally used for generation of framework flexibility instead of framework design.15

The oxo or hydroxo centered trinuclear metal carboxylate cluster M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 (LT = terminal ligand) has been widely used as flexible 6-connected SBUs with shape-changeable trigonal-prismatic geometries, in which the pointing directions of carboxylate groups (described by the included angle θ between three adjacent clusters) can be largely varied (Fig. 1a).16,17 Unlike the rigid octahedral cluster Zn4(μ4-O)(RCOO)6, combination of these trinuclear clusters with linear dicarboxylate linkers can produce a variety of isomeric 6-connected metal carboxylate frameworks with a general formula of [M3(μ3-O)(ldc)3X(H2O)2] (ldc2− = linear dicarboxylate, X− = small anion such as Cl−, OH−, etc.), including the highly flexible MIL-88 series with a simple uninodal 6-connected acs topology (Fig. 1b)2–5,18 and more complicated MIL-101 consisting of vertex sharing tetrahedral cages (trinodal zeolitic MTN topology considering the tetrahedral cages as 4-connected nodes or tetranodal 6-connected mtn-e topology considering the trinuclear SBUs as 6-connected nodes) (Fig. 1c).19–24


Fig. 1 Representative topological structures (all 6-connected) for a combination of trigonal-prismatic M3(μ3-O/OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 clusters and linear dicarboxylates. (a) Definition of the conformation of the M3(μ3-O/OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 clusters in these networks. The (b) acs, (c) mtn-e, and (d) flu-e (three types of cages in the hypothetical crystal structure constructed by bdc2− are shown) topologies.

Besides MIL-88 and MIL-101, other highly porous and symmetric open frameworks based on the same M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 SBU and linear dicarboxylate linker have also been designed/predicted (Fig. 1d).25,26 Herein, we report the synthesis and properties of such a hypothetical network structure possessing a uninodal 6-connected flu-e topology, and show that its thermal/chemical stability can be drastically enhanced by a metalloligand decoration strategy.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

All the reagents were commercially available and used without further purification. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were performed with a VG Scientific ESCALAB 250 instrument. Elemental analyses (EA) were conducted using an Elementar Vario EL analyzer, using solvent exchanged (CH2Cl2 for 1, and CH3OH for 2 and 3) samples. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Bruker TENSOR 27 FT-IR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region with KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a Bruker D8-Advance diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation and a LynxEye detector. Thermogravimetry (TG) analyses were performed on a TA Q50 thermogravimetric analyzer under nitrogen at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. CO2 sorption experiments were carried out on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020M physisorption analyzer. Prior to the gas adsorption measurements, the samples were first solvent exchanged similar to that for EA, and then activated for 12 h at 100 °C under vacuum.

Synthesis

[NiII2NiIII(μ3-OH)(bdc)3(4-pyCN)3]·guest (MCF-35, 1, H2bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid, 4-pyCN = 4-cyanopyridine). A mixture of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (1.740 g, 6.0 mmol), H2bdc (0.996 g, 6.0 mmol), 4-pyCN (1.872 g, 18.0 mmol), and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA, 60.0 mL) was stirred for 30 min in air, then transferred into a 100 mL vial and sealed with a screw cap, which was heated in an oven at 120 °C for 48 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, giving green cubic crystals (yield 30%). EA calc. (%) for C46H59Cl8N6Ni3O26 ([Ni3(OH)(C8H4O4)3(C6H4N2)3]·13H2O·4CH2Cl2): C, 35.15; H, 3.78; N, 5.35; found: C 34.81, H 3.38, N 5.63.
[{NiII2NiIII(μ3-OH)(bdc)3}4{NiII2(na)4(H2O)2}3]·guest (MCF-36, 2, Hna = nicotinic acid). A mixture of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (1.740 g, 6.0 mmol), H2bdc (0.996 g, 6.0 mmol), Hna (0.738 g, 6.0 mmol), HBF4 (1.2 mL), and DMA (60.0 mL) was stirred for 30 min in air, then transferred into a 100 mL vial and sealed with a screw cap, which was heated in an oven at 140 °C for 48 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, giving green cubic crystals (yield 60%). EA calc. (%) for C198H242N12Ni18O117 ([{Ni3(OH)(C8H4O4)3}4{Ni2(C6H4NO2)4(H2O)2}3]·5H2O·30CH3OH): C, 41.62; H, 4.20; N, 2.94; found: C 41.81, H 4.47, N 3.14.
[{FeIII3(μ3-O)(bdc)3}4{FeIII(na)4(H2O)}3]X·guest (MCF-37, 3). A mixture of FeCl2·4H2O (1.193 g, 6.0 mmol), H2bdc (0.996 g, 6.0 mmol), Hna (0.738 g, 6.0 mmol), HBF4 (50 wt% in H2O, 0.4 mL), and DMA (60.0 mL) was stirred for 30 min in air, then transferred into a 100 mL vial and sealed with a screw cap, which was heated in an oven at 140 °C for 48 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, giving dark brown crystals (yield 50%). EA calc. (%) for C218H248BF4Fe15N22O107 ([{Fe3O(C8H4O4)3}4{Fe(C6H4NO2)4(H2O)}3]BF4·10C4H9NO·10CH3OH·8H2O): C, 45.04; H, 4.30; N, 5.30; found: C, 45.33, H, 4.47, N, 5.03. To determine the valence state of Fe, the as-synthesized crystals of 3 (10.0 mg) were digested by H2SO4 (1.0 mL, 1.0 mol L−1), to which an aqueous solution of K3[Fe(CN)6] (10.0 mL, 0.1 mol L−1) was added and stirred for 20 min. The reaction was carried out under N2 protection.

Single-crystal X-ray analysis

Diffraction data were collected on an Oxford Gemini-S Ultra CCD-detector or a Rigaku XtaLAB P300DS-detector diffractometer (Cu Kα). All structures were solved by the direct method and refined with the full-matrix least-squares technique on F2 by the SHELXTL-2014 software package. All the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically. The PLATON SQUEEZE treatment was applied because all guest solvent molecules are extremely disordered and cannot be modeled. Detailed structure determination parameters and crystallographic data are given in Table S1.†

Results and discussion

Synthesis and structure

A solvothermal reaction of Ni(NO3)2, H2bdc, and 4-pyCN in DMA afforded green cubic crystals of [NiII2NiIII(μ3-OH)(bdc)3(4-pyCN)3]·guest (MCF-35, 1), whose structure was characterized by not only single-crystal X-ray diffraction (Table S1 and Fig. S1a†) but also X-ray photoelectron and infrared spectroscopy (Fig. S2 and S3†). Addition of a small amount of HBF4 can effectively improve the crystal quality, but this leads to the formation of impurities. The crystal structure of 1 represents a highly symmetric (Fmm) uninodal 6-connected flu-e network built up by the trigonal-prismatic Ni3(μ3-OH)(RCOO)6(4-pyCN)3 clusters and the linear bdc2− ligands, which is identical to that of the theoretical structure predicted by Férey et al.25 except that the terminal coordination sites are coordinated by the pyridyl ends of 4-pyCN molecules (Fig. 2a). The 6-connected flu-e network can be regarded as alternatively vertex sharing tetrahedra and cubes, which encloses three kinds of cages with different shapes and sizes. The smallest one (S-cage) is the tetrahedron defined by 4 SBUs and 6 bdc2− ligands, just the same as in MIL-101. The medium one (M-cage) is a cube defined by 8 SBUs and 12 bdc2− linkers. Finally, the largest one (L-cage) is a rhombicuboctahedron formed by 24 clusters and 48 bdc2− linkers. Because all cyano groups of the coordinated 4-pyCN molecules point toward the center of the L-cage, its free diameter is reduced from the predicted value of 25 Å to 11 Å in 1. For the M- and S-cages, their free diameters are consistent with the predicted values of 13 and 7 Å, respectively. The solvent accessible volume of 1 was calculated as 65% or 79% considering the retention or removal of the coordinated 4-pyCN molecules, respectively.
Fig. 2 Local coordination structures of 4-pyCN, Ni2(na)4(H2O)2 and Fe(na)4(H2O) terminal ligands and their distributions on the inner walls of L-cages in (a) 1, (b) 2, and (c) 3, respectively.

Interestingly, when 4-pyCN was replaced by Hna in the reaction mixture, cubic green crystals of [{NiII2NiIII(μ3-OH)(bdc)3}4{NiII2(na)4(H2O)2}3]·guest (MCF-36, 2, Table S1 and Fig. S1b†) were obtained. Compound 2 crystallizes in the same space group (Fmm) and possesses the same [Ni3(μ3-OH)(bdc)3] backbone as 1. However, the terminal coordination sites of the Ni3 clusters in 2 are coordinated by the pyridyl ends of the na− ligands. Further, the carboxylate ends of each four adjacent na− ligands gather together and coordinate with two Ni(II) ions to form a classical paddle-wheel Ni2(RCOO)4(H2O)2 cluster (Fig. 2b). If the Ni2 clusters are considered as 4-connected nodes, 2 can be simplified as a new binodal 4,9-connected network with a point symbol of {34·42}3{39·49·515·63}4.27 Because each four adjacent 4-pyCN molecules are replaced by a {Ni2(na)4(H2O)2} metalloligand, the free diameters of the L-cages are reduced from 11 Å to 9.5 Å. The solvent accessible volume of 2 is slightly decreased to about 62%, assuming that all lattice and coordinated solvent molecules were removed.

We also tried to synthesize the Fe(III) analogue of 1, but failed by using various Fe(III) salts in different synthetic methods. Fortunately, the solvothermal reaction of FeCl2, H2bdc, and Hna in DMA afforded cubic dark brown crystals of [{FeIII3(μ3-O)(bdc)3}4{FeIII(na)4(H2O)}3]X·guest (MCF-37, 3, Table S1 and Fig. S1c†). No colour change was observed by reaction of the acid-digested as-synthesized crystals with K3[Fe(CN)6] (see the Experimental section), confirming that 3 is solely composed of Fe(III). Compound 3 possesses the same space group (Fmm) and network connectivity, as well as virtually identical pore size and solvent accessible void to 2. Nevertheless, only one Fe(III) ion was coordinated by the four carboxylate ends of adjacent na− ligands in the {Fe(na)4(H2O)}− metalloligand (Fig. 2c), which can be attributed to the small radius of the high-valence cation Fe(III) (Fig. S4†).

Stability and porosity

TG showed no plateau for the as-synthesized 1, which can be attributed to the gradual removal of the high boiling point lattice solvent molecules and the weakly coordinated 4-pyCN molecules (Fig. S5†). A TG plateau between ca. 120–220 °C was observed for the CH2Cl2 exchanged sample, but a small weight loss of ca. 2.7% was still observed in this temperature range. Guest-free 1, obtained by different activation methods, such as CH2Cl2 exchange and subsequent heating at 120 °C, showed significantly weakened and broadened PXRD peaks (Fig. S6†). Infrared spectra showed that guest-free 1 contains less 4-pyCN than the as-synthesized 1 (Fig. S3†). The instability of the 4-pyCN coordination can be ascribed to the weak basicity of its pyridyl end (strong electron withdrawing effect of the cyano group) and its charge neutrality. Immersing guest-free 1 in DMA, even at 120 °C for 1 day, cannot recover the sharp PXRD pattern of the as-synthesized 1 (Fig. S6†), implying partial and permanent framework collapse after guest removal. However, when guest-free 1 was immersed in a DMA solution of 4-pyCN, the PXRD pattern returned sharp and identical to that of the as-synthesized 1 (Fig. S6†). The necessity of 4-pyCN for restoring the sample's high crystallinity confirmed that some of the terminal 4-pyCN ligand are removed along with the lattice guest molecules during thermal activation, and the guest-free coordination network distorts and loses some long-range order but may retain the network connectivity of the as-synthesized form.

TG curves of the as-synthesized 2 and 3 are similar to that of the as-synthesized 1, which can be ascribed to the high boiling point DMA molecules. Nevertheless, after solvent exchange by methanol, 2 and 3 showed obvious TG plateaus up to 300 °C (Fig. S5†). Variable-temperature PXRD measurements further confirmed that the desolvated samples of 2 and 3 can be stable up to 300 °C (Fig. S7†), being much more rigid and stable than 1. More interestingly, 3 has excellent chemical stability in water even under acidic (pH = 1) and basic (pH = 13) conditions despite its high porosity (Fig. S8†), which is similar to some other Fe(III)-based PCPs.19,20,22,28

To evaluate the porosity of guest-free 1–3, CO2 sorption isotherms were measured at 195 K (Fig. 3). Guest-free 1 showed a very small saturation uptake of 80 cm3 (STP) g−1, corresponding to a pore volume of 0.143 cm3 g−1. On the contrary, guest-free 2 and 3 showed large saturation uptake of 304 and 358 cm3 (STP) g−1, corresponding to pore volumes of 0.543 and 0.639 cm3 g−1, respectively. For comparison, the theoretical values empirically calculated from the crystal structures of 1–3 are 0.877/1.07 (4-pyCN retained/removed), 0.678, and 0.725 cm3 g−1, respectively. Further, the BET/Langmuir surface areas of 1–3 were calculated, based on the measured CO2 isotherms, to be 327/447, 1368/1516, and 1539/1829 m2 g−1, respectively. The very low CO2 uptake of guest-free 1 is consistent with its poor crystallinity.

The role of cluster flexibility

To explain the successful syntheses and different stabilities of 1–3, we compared their structures with MIL-88 and MIL-101 and found that the conformation distortion of the trinuclear SBUs plays a crucial role (Fig. 1). Obviously, MIL-101 consisting of vertex sharing tetrahedra has θ values of about 60°. For the flexible MIL-88 series, θ can change from 51 to 81° from the most close to the most open form, respectively.2–5 The fact that the trinuclear cluster cannot be further opened by guest inclusion indicates that the conformation with θ > 81° is highly energetically unfavorable. However, as required by the tetrahedral and cubic cages of the flu-e topology, θ should be not only 60° but also 90°, the latter of which has not been observed for such types of 6-connected PCPs. On the other hand, we recently demonstrated that such trinuclear SBUs can be decorated by pyridyl type ligands on the three monodentate terminal sites located on the equatorial plane, which not only extends the cluster as 9-connected SBUs, but also can push the cluster to conformations with θ > 81°, because the pyridyl ligands connect different parts of the metal carboxylate framework.6,29–31

As shown in Fig. 2b and c, the 4-connected metalloligands in 2 and 3 obviously serve as rigid scaffolds to support and stabilize the cubic cage with θ = 90°. Obviously, four adjacent monodentate 4-pyCN ligands in 1 (Fig. 2a) cannot produce a rigid pushing effect to the metal carboxylate backbone as powerful as the 4-connected metalloligands. Nevertheless, the highly electronegative cyano groups of the adjacent 4-pyCN ligands possess a relatively close separation of 4.7 Å, which can have non-ignorable repulsing effects similar to a multidentate ligand. The lattice solvent molecules residing in the center of the L-cage may also prevent the 24 4-pyCN ligands from getting closer to each other at the cyano ends. Therefore, it can be understood that the highly porous flu-e coordination network in 1 tends to distort and lose some long-range order during thermal activation (Fig. S3, S5 and S6†). On the other hand, in 2 and 3, the 4-connected metalloligand can be hardly separated from the whole coordination networks, giving rise to drastically improved stabilities.

Conclusions

In summary, three porous coordination frameworks possessing a hypothetical flu-e [M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(bdc)3(LT)3] backbone have been constructed by adding suitable pyridyl derivatives as terminal ligands for the M3(μ3-O/μ3-OH)(RCOO)6(LT)3 clusters. The terminal ligands play a structure directing role for the cubic cages in this topological structure, by forcing the flexible clusters to adopt an energetically unstable conformation. The weak coordinated monodentate ligand 4-pyCN tends to leave the cluster during thermal activation, leading to framework distortion and partial loss of long-range order, which highlights the structure directing role of the terminal ligands. When suitable bifunctional terminal ligands were used, the flu-e network can be strengthened by crosslinking with additional metal ions, forming new binodal 4,9-connected coordination networks with drastically improved thermal/chemical stability and porosity. These results should be instructive for further exploration of highly porous coordination frameworks.
