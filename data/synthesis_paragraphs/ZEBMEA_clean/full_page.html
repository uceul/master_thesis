    Abstract

    Reaction of the pseudotetrahedral tetracarboxylic acid proligand tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)silane (H4L) with salts of various lanthanoid metals has afforded three new lanthanoid coordination polymers: {[H3O]2[Ce2(L)2(H2O)2]·2DMF·2H2O}∞ (1), {[Eu3L2(NO3)(DMF)4(H2O)]·0.5DMF·6H2O}∞ (2), and {Eu(HL)(DMF)2(H2O)]·DMF·2.5H2O}∞ (3) (DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide), which have been structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compounds 1 and 2 are both noninterpenetrated three-dimensional networks that display the rare fluorite (CaF2) topology. In compound 1, the fully deprotonated L4– ligands act as pseudotetrahedral 4-connecting nodes and dinuclear cerium-carboxylate building blocks act as 8-connecting nodes, while in compound 2 the L4– ligands and trinuclear europium-carboxylate units are 4- and 8-connecting, respectively. In contrast, compound 3 exhibits a two-dimensional layered structure with triply deprotonated HL3– ligands acting as 3-connecting units, linking single europium centers within each layer. Gas sorption studies of 2 show a high affinity of the pretreated microcrystalline solid for carbon dioxide gas.

    Synopsis

    Three new lanthanoid-based coordination polymers have resulted from the use of a pseudotetrahedral tetracarboxylic acid proligand, which facilititates the in situ formation of carboxylate-bridged polymetallic aggregates that can act as high connectivity nodes. Two of the synthesized compounds display the rare fluorite topology, with gas sorption studies of one of these showing a high affinity for carbon dioxide gas.

    The chemical and physical properties intrinsic to trivalent lanthanoid ions render lanthanoid-based coordination polymers (Ln-CPs) an important source of new functional materials.(1) The luminescence, magnetic, and Lewis acid catalytic behavior of the constituent lanthanoid metal centers can potentially be harnessed in such porous materials to afford coordination polymers for future application as luminescence-based sensors, light emitting materials, heterogeneous catalysts, materials for gas separation, and porous magnetic materials.(2-4) Moreover, novel structural features can arise in CPs based on lanthanoid metals, as opposed to d-block metals, because of the particular coordinative characteristics of the lanthanoid(III) ions, which will inevitably influence the physical properties displayed by Ln-CPs.
    A key advance in the design of highly connected topological nets is the use of polynuclear metal complexes as nodes rather than single metal centers. These polynuclear aggregates are typically robust moieties with well-defined and divergent binding sites that can be synthesized in situ or preformed. Well known examples include the basic zinc acetate tetramer {Zn4O(O2CR)6} and oxo-centered chromium(III) trimer {Cr3O(O2CR)6(py)3}+, which serve as octahedral and trigonal (or trigonal prismatic) nodes, respectively.(5) The typically higher coordination number of f-block metal centers conveys promise for accessing nodes of higher connectivity for the construction of novel CPs by employing lanthanoid ions within polynuclear aggregates. In this regard Schröder, Champness, and co-workers have demonstrated that single lanthanoid ions can serve as nodes with a high connectivity within coordination polymers.(6, 7) The tendency of lanthanoid(III) ions to form di- and trinuclear complexes with bridging carboxylate ligands provides encouragement for the in situ formation of coordination clusters as high connectivity nodes.(8, 9) Examples of Ln-CPs that comprise dinuclear lanthanoid building blocks that have formed in situ include [Ce2L′2(DMF)2]·5DMF·6H2O (H3L′ = N,N′,N″-tris(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,3,5-benzenetricarboxamide,(10) [Ln2(MEL)(μ-H2O)2(H2O)2] (Ln = Er, Tm; MEL = mellitic acid,(11) [Sc2(succ)2.5(OH)] (succ = succinate),(12) and [Er2(dms)3(H2O)] (dms = 2,2-dimethyl succinate).(13)
    The majority of reported Ln-CPs, including those listed above, employ polycarboxylate ligands as linkers, making use of the strong affinity of the relatively large and strongly Lewis acidic lanthanoid(III) ions for binding to carboxylate ligands, with the chelating binding mode particularly favored. Most of these have been prepared from commercially available polycarboxylate ligands. While the use of dicarboxylates as 2-connecting linkers is common for Ln-CPs, there are also examples with tricarboxylates (1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate and biphenyl-3,4′,5-tricarboxylate), a tetracarboxylate (1,1′-butadiynebenzene-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate), and even the hexacarboxylate mellitate.(11, 14-16) In addition, the Ln-CP [Ce2L′2(DMF)2]·5DMF·6H2O incorporates a tricarboxylate-monoamido ligand as a 4-connecting node.(10) However, there are no reports to date of the use of pseudotetrahedral tetracarboxylate ligands in Ln-CPs. In contrast, the tetracarboxylate ligands [E(p-C6H4CO2)4]4– (E = C, Si, or Ge) have been employed to synthesize CPs based on d-block metals, some of which have displayed previously unobserved structures.(17-23) These include CPs with PtS, fluorite (CaF2), and diamondoid topologies constructed from mono-, di-, tri-, and tetranuclear transition metal-based nodes. The combination of the intrinsically nonplanar ligands [E(p-C6H4CO2)4]4– with high-connectivity polylanthanoid-based building blocks offers considerable promise for the formation of highly connected three-dimensional networks. Herein, we report the results of our exploration into the use of the tetrakis(4-carboxyphenyl)silane Si(p-C6H4CO2H)4 (H4L) proligand for the construction of new Ln-CPs.

    General Methods

    All chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and Frontier Scientific as reagent grade or higher. The ligand H4L was synthesized as described previously under an atmosphere of dry N2 using freshly distilled and degassed solvents dried over sodium wire.(24) The syntheses of all lanthanoid compounds were performed under aerobic conditions using reagents as received.

    A solution of Eu(NO3)3·5H2O (19 mg, 45 μmol) and H4L (23 mg, 45 μmol) in a mixture of DMF (4 mL) and H2O (1.3 mL) in a capped 12 mL glass vial was heated to 80 °C in a sand bath for 24 h, affording a mixture of small parallelogram-shaped and larger rod-shaped crystals. The small parallelogram-shaped crystals that cocrystallized with 3 had a different unit cell but did not diffract sufficiently well to allow structural determination.

    Synthesis of Compounds 1–3

    Mixed solvent systems are commonly employed for the formation of Ln-CPs with carboxylate based ligands. Typically, the use of multiple solvents is required to impart a degree of control over the reaction process and prevent the rapid formation of insoluble amorphous polymeric materials. Our initial exploration involved heating a solution of Eu(NO3)3 with an equimolar amount of H4L in a mixture of DMF, ethanol, and water in sealed reaction vessels. Prolonged heating of the initially clear reaction mixture at 80 °C for 24 h, followed by cooling to room temperature, afforded a mixture of small parallelogram-shaped plates and larger plates of similar morphology. Single crystal X-ray diffraction studies allowed identification of these crystals as compounds 2 and 3, respectively. Optimization of the reaction conditions to yield pure samples of each compound was attempted by varying the reaction temperature, solvent system, reagent concentrations, and stoichiometry, finally arriving at a distinct synthetic method for 2; however, 3 could not be obtained as a pure bulk material. Replacement of europium with cerium in the procedure used to synthesize compound 3 affords compound 1 instead. This method involves the reaction of the lanthanoid nitrate with equimolar amounts of proligand in a DMF/H2O solvent mixture (method 1). Increasing the lanthanoid to proligand ratio to 4:1 and including ethanol in the solvent mix (method 2) affords a pure sample of compound 2 when europium is used, although the substitution of other lanthanoid ions gives rise to a mixture of crystalline and amorphous material. Interestingly, both triply deprotonated (Si(C6H4CO2)3(C6H4CO2H))3– (HL3–) and fully deprotonated (Si(C6H4CO2)4)4– (L4–) forms of the ligand are obtained under these reaction conditions. Finally, it is important to note the importance of DMF for the synthesis of all three compounds, which contain coordinated DMF. The omission of DMF from the synthesis, or its replacement with other coordinating solvents such as DMSO, invariably leads to the formation of materials unsuitable for single-crystal X-ray diffraction experiments.
    Although it was possible to access sufficient quantities of analytically pure 2 for powder X-ray diffraction, thermogravimetric analysis, and gas sorption studies (see below), the lower synthetic yield of 1 prevented similar bulk measurements for this compound. In addition, while single crystals of 3 could be manually separated for X-ray diffraction, it was not possible to obtain an analytically pure bulk sample.

    Crystal Structures of Compounds 1–3

    Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c with a crystallographically unique cerium center, a coordinated water ligand and a single L4– ligand residing in the asymmetric unit. The solvation of the compound could not be determined crystallographically. The three-dimensional structure of 1 can be considered to be assembled from dinuclear Ce-carboxylate units (Figure 1a), with each dinuclear unit containing eight carboxylate groups from eight independent fully deprotonated L4– ligands. Bond valence sum calculations indicate that the cerium centers are present as cerium(III) ions. Each cerium center is 9-coordinate, with approximate tricapped trigonal prismatic coordination geometry. Bridging of the cerium centers within the dinuclear unit occurs through two μ2 oxygen atoms, each originating from two separate carboxylates (Figure 2). These μ2–η2:η1 carboxylates each also chelate one of the two crystallographically equivalent cerium ions. Each cerium is involved in further coordination to three more carboxylates, two via chelation and one in a monodentate fashion. To the best of our knowledge, there are no reports of discrete dinuclear lanthanoid–carboxylate complexes with the same structure as the dinuclear unit evident in 1; however, similar motifs are known. The closest example is the cerium acetate complex [Ce2(OAc)8]2– where a chelating acetate group takes the place of the monodentate carboxylic acid in 1.(25) A similar dinuclear lanthanoid building block is evident in the octanuclear mixed metal complex [Cu4Ln4(OAc)12(ampdH)8(OH2)2] (Ln = Gd or Tb; ampdH2 = 2-amino-2-methyl-1,3-propanediol).(26) We propose that the cation providing charge balance for the negatively charged framework is the hydronium ion, H3O+, which is not detectable by X-ray diffraction. This assignment is supported by neutron diffraction studies on an anionic yttrium oxalate network, which clearly showed that the countercation was the hydronium ion hydrogen bonded to water molecules.(27)
    The combination of 8-connecting dinuclear cerium units and 4-connecting L4– ligands gives rise to the fluorite (CaF2) topology observed in 1 (Figure 3a). The fluorite net may be described as a face-centered cubic arrangement of 8-connecting centers (Ca2+ ions in CaF2) with 4-connecting centers occupying all the tetrahedral sites (F– ions in CaF2); within the face-centered cubic unit cell of fluorite, a rhombic dodecahedron unit is clearly apparent (Figure 1c). In the case of 1, dinuclear cerium units serve as the 8-connecting nodes, while the tetracarboxylate L4– ligands represent the 4-connecting nodes. The irregular geometry of the dinuclear cerium-carboxylate aggregate results in significant distortions from the ideal cubic network observed for fluorite; nevertheless, if the dinuclear cerium building blocks are represented by a single 8-connected center and the pseudotetrahedral ligands by a 4-connecting center, then a distorted rhombic dodecahedral unit is apparent (Figure 1a).
    Although the fluorite topology is relatively common in minerals, its observation is rare in coordination polymers as 8-connecting nodes are required. While there are no other reports to date of Ln-CPs displaying the fluorite topology, there are a handful of examples based on d-block metals, including species with polymetallic {Mn2}, {Co3}, {Mn4}, and {Cu4} units acting as 8-connecting nodes, with 4-connecting organic ligands derived from 5-carboxyl-1-carboxymethyl-3-oxidopyridinium, tetrakis[(4-carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]methane acid, trimellitic acid and tetrakis(4-tetrazolyl phenyl) methane.(28-31)
    The solvent accessible voids in compound 1 were calculated by removing bulk and coordinated solvent molecules resulting in a value of 14.1%, using a spherical probe with radius of 1.4 Å.(32) This solvent accessible volume is in the form of irregular channels that run along the crystallographic b axis; unfortunately, the yield of 1 has thus far prohibited any investigation into the permanent porosity of this compound.
    Compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1̅ with half of a trinuclear europium coordination cluster, with two coordinated DMF ligands and an equally disordered nitrate and water ligand residing in the asymmetric unit. Additionally, a quarter DMF and three water molecules are located as solvent in the channels of 2. Compound 2 also displays the fluorite topology (Figure 1b); however, the 8-connecting nodes in 2 are trinuclear europium-carboxylate units, while the 4-connecting nodes represent the tetracarboxylate ligand L4– ligand.
    The 8-coordinate center Eu1a is chelated by two carboxylates and two monodentate DMF ligands, displaying distorted dodecahedral coordination geometry. Metal center Eu1a is separated from Eu2 by a distance of 4.99 Å through two bridging carboxylates, which bind in different modes (Z,E–μ2–η1:η1 and Z,Z–μ2–η1:η1). The 8-coordinate center Eu2 instead displays a distorted dodecahedral coordination geometry, with chelation of a nitrate ligand, a water ligand, and the carboxylate ligands from L4–. Center Eu2 is separated from Eu1b by a distance of 4.10 Å through three carboxylate functional groups. One of these ligands simply bridges the europium centers, whereas the other two carboxylates (μ2–η2:η1) chelate to Eu1b while also connecting to Eu2. Center Eu1b, like Eu1a, has two monodentate DMF ligands but has an additional filled coordination site bringing its coordination number to nine, with it displaying a distorted tricapped trigonal prismatic coordination environment. Crystallographic disorder is observed within the trinuclear coordination cluster, due to the presence of an inversion center in close proximity to Eu2. The structure of the trinuclear Eu-carboxylate 2 is, to the best of our knowledge, unprecedented in lanthanoid carboxylate chemistry.
    Compound 2 also displays the fluorite topology observed for 1, again with significant distortion from the atomic arrangement of Ca2+ and F– ions observed in fluorite itself, owing to the irregular geometry of the trinuclear species. The solvent accessible voids of compound 2 were calculated to occupy 13.5% of the volume using a spherical probe with radius of 1.4 Å.(32) This value was calculated for the solvent free structure, including removal of the coordinated DMF and water ligands, and is in agreement with experimental thermogravimetric analysis data for the sample used for gas sorption experiments (see later). The solvent accessible volume is in the form of irregular channels that run perpendicular to the crystallographic a axis (Figure 3b). Powder X-ray diffraction studies of an air-dried bulk sample of 2 afforded a diffractogram (Figure S1 in the Supporting Information), which shows good agreement with the pattern calculated from the single crystal data, indicating structural retention in the bulk sample upon air drying. Thermogravimetric analysis on the air-dried sample (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information) shows a 23% mass loss in the range 100–350 °C, consistent with the complete loss of solvent of crystallization and the coordinated DMF and water ligands. Further heating leads to an additional mass loss at 450 °C, corresponding to decomposition.
    Compound 3 displays a very different structure to the three-dimensional networks evident in 1 and 2, with 3 comprising two-dimensional layers constructed from single europium centers rather than multinuclear aggregates (Figure 4). Each europium center has two monodentate DMF ligands and a monodentate water ligand and is chelated by three carboxylates from three independent triply deprotonated HL3– ligands. This gives rise to an undulating 6,3 network of alternating 3-connected europium centers and 3-connected HL3– ligands. The 6-membered rings within this network, formed by three europium centers, three silicon centers, and the benzoate bridges between them, adopt a chair-like conformation similar to that displayed by cyclohexane. The two-dimensional networks of 3 stack along the crystallographic b direction, interdigitiating in pairs, with the cavity at the center of the 6-membered rings hosting the carboxylic acid belonging to the protonated arm of HL3– from the interdigitating layer above or below. The carboxylic acid is involved in several hydrogen bonds to the formyl hydrogen of the DMF and a carboxylate oxygen coordinated to the europium center. Also located in this cavity are an additional two disordered DMF solvent molecules. Interestingly, there are further channels between the layers running parallel to the crystallographic c axis, which solely contain water molecules. This observation is of note as it indicates solvent selectivity during the crystallization process. Further exploration of this observation has thus far been hampered by our inability to isolate a pure bulk material.

    Gas Sorption Properties of Compound 2

    The solvent filled channels apparent in the structure of compound 2 and the accessibility of reasonable quantities of pure bulk sample prompted an investigation of its gas sorption properties. Thermogravimetric analysis of 2 indicated complete loss of solvent of crystallization and coordinated water and DMF ligands after heating to 335 °C. Following the removal of solvent under vacuum at 335 °C (608 K), a CO2 isotherm was measured at 258 K (Figure 5). The measurement indicates an uptake of 74 cm3(STP)/g at approximately 1 atm, which corresponds to 5.1 mol (CO2)/mol (2) or 14 wt %. At approximately 19 atm, an uptake of 120 cm3(STP)/g (8.3 mol (CO2)/mol (2)) or 23 wt % of CO2 is observed. At 273 K, 2 provided an uptake of 11.5 wt % at 1 atm and 22 wt % at approximately 30 atm. For comparison at 273 K and 1 atm, the Ln-CP Dy(BTC) (H3BTC = 1,3,5-benzenetricarbocylic acid) shows an uptake of 27.2 wt %.(33)
    Heat of CO2 sorption measurements were calculated by fitting a virial-type thermal adsorption equation to the 258 and 273 K data (Table S1 in the Supporting Information). The measurements show that 2 has a CO2 sorption enthalpy of −41 kJ/mol at zero loading. This value falls to −32 kJ/mol as the sorption approaches maximum loading.
