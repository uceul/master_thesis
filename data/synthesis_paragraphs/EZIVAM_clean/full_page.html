White-light emission by selectively encapsulating single lanthanide metal ions into alkaline earth metal-organic coordination polymers

Abstract

An effective method to obtain white-light emitters by encapsulating a single lanthanide metal ion into an alkaline earth metal-organic coordination polymers is presented. Two novel complexes SrL(H2O) and BaL(DMF) (L = 1,1â€²,1â€²â€²-(benzene-1,3,5-triyl)tripiperidine-4-carboxylic acid) were synthesized, and lanthanide metal ions were encapsulated by coordination with their active sites. The CIE coordinates of the modified complexes are tuned along two separate trajectories by regulating the content of either Eu3+ or Tb3+. The trajectories of the complexes containing Eu3+ pass through the white region in the CIE diagram. Different from the traditional idea of overlaying three emission colors, white-light emission of the modified complexes is explored by encapsulating only Eu3+ into the blue-light emission coordination polymers to decrease the number of unmanageable variables. Luminescence measurements show that the complexes can specifically identify Fe3+ with almost complete emission quenching, which is not observed in the presence of other metal ions.

1. Introduction

Metal-organic coordination polymers with a three-dimensional ordered microporous structure have been elaborated in fruitful researches for gas storage [1], [1](a), [1](b), separation [2], catalysis [3], [3](a), [3](b), drug delivery [4], biomedical imaging [5], [5](a), [5](b), magnetism [6] and luminescence performance [7], [7](a), [7](b). Accounting for about ten percent of all reported coordination polymers [8], luminescent coordination polymers have aroused tremendous interest because of their potential applications in displays [9], optical devices [10], sensing [11](a), [11](b), [11](c), [11](d), [11] and lighting [12], [12](a), [12](b), [12](c). Owing to the fact that the luminescence of coordination polymers can be subversively influenced by the guest, some luminescent coordination polymers have been used as sensors to identify target molecules and ions [13](g), [13](h), [13], [13](a), [13](b), [13](c), [13](d), [13](e), [13](f), while others have been modified to optimize their luminescence performance [14](a), [14](b), [14].

Most reported luminescent coordination polymers contain lanthanide [15], [15](a), [15](b), [15](c) or transition metals [16]; alkaline earth metals are inactive because of their poor coordination ability. However, alkaline earth metals may be used in luminescent coordination polymers and become more economical substitutes when coordinated with a specific luminescent ligand. This ligand should possess active sites that can interact with guests and influence the luminescence performance, and also play vital roles in postsynthetic modification (PSM) to expand the functionality of coordination polymers.

Since coordination polymers with PSM were first reported by Robson [17], modified coordination polymers for many potential applications have been produced [18](d), [18](e), [18], [18](a), [18](b), [18](c). The PSM of alkaline earth luminescent coordination polymers for functional properties is still relatively unexplored. Coordination polymers must be sufficiently stable during a chemical transformation, dense and porous to allow access to the interior of the lattice. In addition, most have to provide an available active site to interact with the guest.

The luminescence of coordination polymers can be affected by the guest, which makes them attractive for sensing. Simultaneously, to optimize luminescence properties including white-light emission, some luminescent coordination polymers with more than one lanthanide metal ion were prepared. However, coordination polymers based on alkaline earth ions with multiple functions of sensing, tunable color and white-light emission have not been studied.

White-light emission coordination polymers have attracted considerable interest recently [19]. It has been suggested that white-light emission could be realized by adjusting the molar ratios of Tb3+ and Eu3+ within blue-light emission coordination polymers based on the three-primary colors principle [20](a), [20](b), [20]. However, this strategy introduced more variables, which made color tuning difficult, especially when energy transfer occurred between Tb3+ and Eu3+ [21](a), [21]. Therefore, it is important to decrease the number of variabilities and determine the possibility of white-light emission by incorporating a single Ln3+, which requires a ligand with flexible luminescent properties.

Herein, we report two stable microporous luminescent coordination polymers composed of alkaline earth metal ions (Sr2+ and Ba2+) and a Ï€-conjugated ligand with heterocyclic rings and carboxylate groups. Remarkably, multiple active sites in the pores of the coordination polymers can interact with guest metal ions, allowing the luminescence of the host framework to be tuned. The luminescence mechanisms are and demonstrated by the photoluminescence measurements, diffuse reflectance ultravioletâ€“visible (DR UVâ€“Vis) analysis and electrospray mass spectrometry (ESIâ€“MS) analysis.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthetic processes

To synthesize the coordination polymers, in addition to adjusting the solvent (the DMF was added for the protonation of the ligand), temperature and filling degree, we used sulfates, nitrates and chlorides as the metal source in the reaction system. However, good quality crystals were not obtained because of the rapid release of metal ions, which limited formation of single crystals. The white precipitates formed using these metal salts were microcrystals of the target products. To slow the release of metal ions, carbonate was used as a starting material to synthesize 1 and 2. Fortunately, the low solubility of the carbonate salts slowed the release of metal ions to give single crystals.

3.2. Crystal structure descriptions

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that 1 and 2 crystallized in the orthorhombic space group Pbca. Their framework structures are isostructural, the asymmetric unit of them contains one crystallographically independent Sr (Ba) cation, one HBTPCA2âˆ’ anion and one coordination H2O (DMF) molecule. The coordinated solvent molecules of alkaline earth metals are different, and the IR spectra confirmed the absence or presence of a peak at 1679 cmâ€“1 in 1 or 2, which is attributable to the CO (DMF) bond, as shown in Fig. S1.

The Sr2+ ions of 1 are each nonacoordinate and situated in a coordination sphere composed of eight O atoms from six different BTPCA ligands and one coordinated water molecule; while the Ba2+ ions of 2 are formed by eight O atoms from six different BTPCA ligands and one coordinated DMF molecule (Fig. 1a and b). The Srâ€“O bond lengths are in the range of 2.501(4)â€“2.907(4) Ã…, and the Baâ€“O bond lengths are in the range of 2.7055(18)â€“3.009(18) Ã…, which are in accordance with those previously reported [25](c), [25](d), [25], [25](a), [25](b). As shown in Fig. 1c, except the uncoordinated carboxylic groups, the ligand based on semirigid C3-symmetric tricarboxylic acid binds to a total of six alkaline earth metal centers.

On the basis of the coordination mode of ligand, the adjacent alkaline earth metal ions are linked by O atom of the first carboxylate group into 1D chain structure. This 1D chain is further interconnected with each other via the second carboxylate group along the b axis giving a 3D framework with three channels in 1 (Fig. 2a). The coordinated water molecules are found directing into the inner of the triangular-similar channel, the uncoordinated carboxylic groups are directing into the parallelogram-similar channel, which is adjacent to another parallelogram-similar channel without an uncoordinated carboxylic group. Similarly, 1D Ba2+ carboxylate chains of 2 are connecting to ligand molecules to give rise to a 3D framework structure as for 1 (Fig. 2b), and the triangular-similar channel of 2 along the a axis is filled by two coordinated DMF molecules. From the topological viewpoint with the SchlÃ¤fli symbol {49Â·66}2, the network structures of 1 and 2 can be viewed as 3D 6,6-connected frameworks based on the Sr2+/Ba2+ cation as a six-connected node, and each ligand as six-connected node (Fig. 3 and Fig. S2).

It is worth noting that all of the Lewis basic pyridyl sites are directed into the inner of channel in 1 and 2, and the number of Lewis basic sites per unit cell is twenty-four, in addition, there are eight incompletely coordinated carboxylic groups in each unit cell. Taking 1 as an example, the solvent accessible volume of 1 is about 11.9% as determined by using PLATON [26]. As shown in Fig. 4, the typical type-I N2 adsorption and desorption isotherms at 77 K reveal the microporosity of 1 with the Langmuir surface area 128.5 m2 gâˆ’1, by contrast, the hysteretic curves in CO2 desorption isotherms at 273 K reveals the hostâ€“guest interactions between the Lewis basic active sites and CO2 molecules.

3.3. The thermal stability and porosity

The thermal stability and dense porosity are promising to introduce guest molecule into coordination polymers. To determine whether the frameworks could be retained upon heating or not, 1 and 2 were observed from the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves in the 25â€“800 Â°C range under N2 atmosphere (Fig. S3, Fig. 4). For 1, before 200 Â°C, it was found that the weight loss 3.80% (calculated at 3.18%) corresponds to the loss of coordinated water molecule; and the further weight loss of 77.58% between 300 and 550 Â°C corresponded to the decomposition of the framework, finally SrO was produced at 18.62% of the original weight (calculated at 18.30%). For 2, from room temperature up to 300 Â°C, the lost 11.05% (calculated at 10.90%) of its weight corresponded to the loss of coordinated DMF molecules, the further weight loss of 66.72% between 300 and 550 Â°C corresponded to the decomposition of the framework, eventually BaO was occurred at 22.23% of the original weight (calculated at 22.85%). The experiment results showed that the frameworks can be stable up to at least 200 Â°C, which is high enough to sustain sample treatments.

3.4. Tunable colors and white-light emission

The excitation spectra of 1 and 2 showed that both of them consisted of a broad band from 250 to 400 nm with a maximum excitation peak at about 365 nm, which was ascribed to the Ï€â€“Ï€* electronic transition of the ligand (Fig. S5). While the emission spectrum of 1 and 2 showed major peak at 445 nm and 452 nm, respectively, which were assigned to the intramolecular energy transfer (Fig. S6). Compared with the pure ligand emission with a major peak at 438 nm, the emissions of both 1 and 2 are more stronger, and red-shifted by about 7 nm and 14 nm, respectively. The enhancement of intensity and the red shift of the emission for 1 and 2 are attributed to the increased rigidity of the ligands upon coordination with the alkaline earth metal ions. Generally, the increase of the ligand rigidity contributed to reducing the loss of energy otherwise occurring via radiationless decay of the intra-ligand emission excited state [27].

The complexes were reacted with Tb3+ (green-light emission) and Eu3+ (red-light emission) to modulate their colors; 1âŠƒTb and 1âŠƒEu were prepared with various concentrations of LnCl3 as described in the Experimental Section (Table S1). The ligand, 1 and 2 emitted in the blue region according to their CIE color coordinates with the maximum exciting wavelength at 365 nm. In order to standardize the method for color measurement and expression, the CIE (Commission Internationale de I'Ã©clairage) recommended the CIE-RGB colorimetric system in 1931. In addition, the method of CIE chromaticity calculation, the interface of the control is provided and it is easy to be called to calculate the chromaticity coordinates and display the color in different chromatic spaces. To optimize the luminescence performance of 1âŠƒTb and 1âŠƒEu, the excitation wavelength of the complexes was adjusted to 394 nm, where the emission intensity of the ligand was weakened slightly (Fig. S7), which can be further associated with the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT).

As shown in Fig 5, at the beginning of the encapsulation reaction, the Eu3+-free sample exhibited the emission of 1. As the Eu3+ concentration was increased, the characteristic peaks of Eu3+ started to appear and the corresponding CIE coordinates changed only marginally. When Eu3+ was extended, narrow-band emission peaks were displayed, which were attributed to the parity forbidden 5D0 â†’ 7FJ (J = 0â€“4) transitions: 590 nm (5D0-7F1) for magnetic dipole transition, 618 nm (5D0-7F2), 650 nm (5D0-7F3), and 695 nm (5D0-7F4) for electric dipole transitions, respectively. Interestingly, when the amount of encapsulated Eu3+ reached 1.12%, which was confirmed by inductively coupled plasma measurements, the CIE coordinates moved to the red region. As expected, the intensity of Eu3+ emission increased in proportion to the amount of encapsulated Eu3+. However, the emission intensity of the host framework gradually decreased as the content of Eu3+ increased. Similar experimental results were found when Eu3+ was replaced by Tb3+ (Fig. 6). Powder X-ray diffractions (PXRD) for Eu/Tb loading samples are shown in Fig. S8.

Tb3+ and Eu3+ are typically used in coordination polymers because of their excellent luminescence characteristics as well as similar charge and ionic radii. For blue-light emission coordination polymers, it seems a traditional way to obtain white-light emission coordination polymers by encapsulating Tb3+ and Eu3+ based on the three-primary colors principle, although it is more difficult to control the molar ratio of Tb3+ and Eu3+, especially when there existed energy conversion between them.

Herein, we aimed to minimize the number of variable components to obtain white-light emission by incorporating a single Ln3+. This is a practical approach for our coordination polymers: systematic modulation of 1âŠƒTb (Table S1), as shown in Fig. 6, allowed emission color tuning from blue to green. The trajectories of the CIE coordinates were almost straight lines, revealing the region of color tuning.

In contrast, the trajectories of 1âŠƒEu moved gradually from blue to red through the white region. Overall, these results suggest that the white-light emission coordination polymers can be designed by encapsulating only Eu3+ within the obtained range of appropriate concentration.

As a foregone conclusion, all efforts to adjust the amount of Tb3+ in the coordination polymers failed to generate white-light emission. However, by carefully controlling the amount of Eu3+ within the obtained range of appropriate concentration, white-light emission (Fig. S9) was achieved and final CIE coordinates of point L (0.3301, 0.3523) in the white region were obtained, which was close to the international pure white-light CIE coordinates (0.333, 0.333).

3.5. Luminescence sensing

An important structural feature of 1 and 2 are the active sites within the pores that allow recognition of metal ions to achieve sensing, transition metal ions were therefore encapsulated in 1 and 2. The colors of the obtained microcrystalline solids were consistent with their metal ions. The photographs of 1, 2, 1âŠƒM and 2âŠƒM under visible light and UV irradiation were illustrated in Figs. S10 and S11, and PXRD of them are shown in Fig. S12 and S13.

The luminescence intensity of 1 at 445 nm did not decrease noticeably when 1 was incorporated with Sn2+, Cu2+, Co2+, Mn2+, Cr3+, Ni2+, Zn2+ and Pb2+, respectively (Fig. S14). Conversely, encapsulation of Fe3+almost completely quenched the luminescence from 1 (about 98%), revealing the ability of 1 to sense Fe3+ (Fig. 7).

As a ubiquitous species in the environment, sensing Fe3+ is an attractive research subject [28]. Fe3+ is indispensable for production of many materials, so it is often found as a contaminant [29]. Therefore, it is important to monitor Fe3+ in a selective and sensitive manner. Further study showed that the emission intensities of both 1 and 2 decreased gradually upon addition of increasing concentrations of Fe3+ (Fig. S15). When the molar ratio of Fe3+ to 1 reached 4:1, which was the equivalence point of complexation, the luminescence from 1 was almost completely quenched, and further increasing the Fe3+ content did not lead to any discernible change of luminescence. Repeated experiments showed the good accuracy and reproducibility of this sensing reaction. Similar sensing activity was also displayed by 2 (Figs. S16 and S17). The content of active sites and crystal structure of 1 and 2 suggested that Fe3+ coordinated with three N atoms from triazinyl and an oxygen atom from an uncoordinated carboxyl group.

To explore the mechanism of luminescence quenching of 1âŠƒFe, we evaluated the interaction between active sites and guest ions. ESIâ€“MS studies of 1âŠƒFe (Fe3+ = 1 Ã— 10âˆ’3 mol Lâˆ’1) were performed in aqueous solutions. The experiment data showed that aqueous solutions of 1âŠƒFe can be identified in the positive ion mode (Fig. S18). With the exhaustive analysis, the signals at m/z = 303.82, 310.80 and 369.42 unambiguously corresponds to the segments of [Sr(BTPCA) (H2O)Fe3Cl5]3+, [Sr(BTPCA) (H2O)Fe]2+ and [Sr(BTPCA) (H2O)Fe2Cl3]2+, respectively. The signal 463.21 was allocated to H4BTPCA+. The existence of these segments suggested that the trivalent Fe3+ do interact with the triazinyl N atoms or uncoordinated carboxyl groups of the host framework, as described in Fig. S19.

In addition, 1âŠƒM and 2âŠƒM were studied by diffuse reflectance ultravioletâ€“visible (DR UVâ€“Vis) spectra. As shown in Figs. S20 and Table S2, the changes of absorption bands location and intensity further confirmed the interaction between metal ions and active sites.

The absorption spectra clearly corresponded to the dâ€“d transitions of the transition metal ions Mn+ (M = Co2+, Cr3+, Ni2+, Fe3+, and Cu2+) from 400 to 700 nm in the visible wavelength region. We believe that the strong interaction between the active sites and trivalent Fe3+ perturbed the electronic structure of the ligand, enhancing the efficiency of energy transfer from the ligand to Fe3+, which led to the luminescence quenching of 1âŠƒFe.

4. Conclusions

Two porous, stable alkaline earth metal-organic coordination polymers with multiple active sites were synthesized using a Ï€-conjugated ligand. The interaction between the host framework and guest metal ions influenced the luminescence of the host frameworks, revealing an efficient way to modulate the emission of white-light emission coordination polymers by encapsulation of red-emitting Eu3+. The polymers also highlighted their promise as sensors for recognizing Fe3+. These prototype luminescent coordination polymers represent a tunable platform to synthesize practical solid-state luminescent materials.
