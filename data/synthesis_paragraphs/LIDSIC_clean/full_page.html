3D CoII, NiII, CoII-doped NiII noninterpenetrated ladder-polymers: structural and magnetic studies†

Huijun Li , Yi Han , Xiaofeng Lv , Shanshan Du , Hongwei Hou * and Yaoting Fan
The College of Chemistry and Molecular Engineering, Zhengzhou University, Henan 450052, P. R. China. E-mail: houhongw@zzu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-371-67761744; Tel: +86-371-67761744

Received 11th January 2013 , Accepted 4th March 2013

First published on 5th March 2013

Two isomorphous 3D noninterpenetrated CoII, NiII polymers with secondary building units (SBUs) of 1D ladder-shaped chains have been studied through their structures and magnetic properties. To systematically gain insight into the influence of metal ions on the magnetic behaviors, intermediate CoII-doped NiII polymers have been successfully synthesized. All of these complexes display noninterpenetrated 3D ladder structures and possess usual 3,6-connected ant nets. There are also large pores in the structures (49.1%). The magnetic results show that all of them display weak antiferromagnetic interactions. Such a strategy may have advantages for the generation of more interesting and adjustable magnetic materials.

Introduction

Currently, the design and synthesis of novel magnetic materials with transition-metals and inorganic/organic bridge ligands have been rapidly enhanced in order to better understand or discover fundamental and new magnetic phenomena in molecular materials.1,2 Central metal ions, as one of the most important factors influencing the magnetic behaviors, govern the exchange coupling interaction with the bridges.3 Therefore, it is necessary to create a platform for magnetic materials to understand the metal ion–magnetic behavior relationship. In previous reports, isomorphous polymers (M and M′ polymers) have been favorable candidates because they may exhibit a drastic change in their magnetic properties.4 To systematically gain insight into the above relation, we consider that intermediate M–M′ polymers should be introduced to better understand the influence of metal ions on the magnetic behaviors, which may enhance the transmitting coupling interactions between the metal ions. However, challenges still remain in the synthesis of isomorphous polymers and intermediate M–M′ polymers due to the variability and complexity in coordination chemistry. During our repeated attempts, we finally chose the T-shaped pyridine–carboxylic acid 5-(pyridin-4-yl)isophthalic (H2pyip) and successfully obtained two novel 3D noninterpenetrated ladder-shaped isomorphous polymers with SBUs of 1D ladder-shaped chains,5 which are linked by pyip2− ligands and μ-H2O groups: [Co4(μ-H2O)2(pyip)4(H2O)4] (1) and [Ni2(μ-H2O)(pyip)2(H2O)2] (2). To systematically gain insight into the metal ion–magnetic behavior relationship, we also synthesized a CoII-doped NiII polymer [Co0.94Ni1.06(μ-H2O)(pyip)2(H2O)2] (3). All of these complexes display three-dimensional frameworks. There are 1D ladder chains in the noninterpenetrated 3D structures. The three polymers present 3,6-connected ant net with Schläfli symbol of (426)4(44628810)2. The pores occupy 49.1% of the framework. Magnetic studies show that they all display weak antiferromagnetic magnetic couplings.

Experimental section

General details

All the solvents and starting materials for the synthesis were purchased commercially and were used as received. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu IR435 spectrometer as KBr disks (4000–400 cm−1). Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out on a FLASH EA 1112 elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were recorded using Cu–Kα1 radiation on a Bruker Advanced diffractometer. Atomic absorption spectrometer analysis (AAS) was carried out on a Hitachi Z-8000 spectrometer. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities were measured using a MPMS-7 SQUID magnetometer. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all constituent atoms.

Preparation of crystalline [Co4(μ-H2O)2(pyip)4(H2O)4] (1)

A mixture of 5-(pyridin-4-yl)isophthalic (H2pyip, 0.030 g, 0.05 mmol) and Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0437 g, 0.15 mmol) in N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF) (3 mL), ethanol (3 mL) and water (2 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (12 mL), heated at 80 °C for 72 h and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. The resulting pink prism-like crystals of 1 were isolated by washing with DMF–ethanol and dried in vacuo. The yield was 30.2% based on H2pyip. Anal. calc. for C52H36Co4N4O22: C, 47.87; H, 2.78; N, 4.29. Found: C, 47.79; H, 2.83; N, 4.24%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3419 m, 1619 s, 1577 m, 1439 m, 1370 s, 1297 w, 831 w, 780 w, 707 w, 643 w.

Preparation of crystalline [Ni2(μ-H2O)(pyip)2(H2O)2] (2)

Pale-green prism-like crystals were obtained under conditions similar to those for 1 only with Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0436 g, 0.15 mmol) instead of Co(NO3)2·6H2O. The yield was 60.6% based on H2pyip. Anal. calc. for C26H20Ni2N2O11: C, 47.87; H, 2.78; N, 4.29. Found: C, 48.04; H, 2.59; N, 4.14%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3408 m, 1633 s, 1582 m, 1439 m, 1374 s, 1296 w, 831 w, 779 w,771 w, 646 w.

Preparation of crystalline [Co0.94Ni1.06(μ-H2O)(pyip)2(H2O)2] (3)

Brown prism-like crystals were obtained under conditions similar to those for 1 only with Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0218 g, 0.075 mmol) and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0218 g, 0.075 mmol) instead of Co(NO3)2·6H2O. The yield was 58.3% based on H2pyip. AAS analysis of 3 gave the Co and Ni contents as 48.642 mg g−1 and 56.080 mg g−1, respectively, indicating the molar ratio of Co:Ni is about 0.87:1. Anal. calc. for C26H20Co0.94Ni1.06N2O11: C, 47.74; H, 3.08; N, 4.28%. Found: C, 48.10; H, 3.26; N, 4.21%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3417 m, 1655 s, 1438 m, 1373 m.

Single crystal X-ray crystallography

The diffraction data of 1 was collected with a CrysAlis CCD, Oxford Diffraction Ltd., with an imaging-plate detector diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo–Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. The crystallographic data for the compounds 2 and 3 was collected on a Bruker Smart area-detector diffractometer with Mo–Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were carried out utilizing the SADABS routine.6 The structure was solved by direct methods with the SHELXS-97 program7 using full-matrix least-squares8 procedures based on F2 values. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, with the hydrogen atoms added to their geometrically ideal positions and refined isotropically except for the hydrogen atoms on the coordinated water molecules, which were located with the OLEX2 program. The disorder of the terminal pyridyl ring of the pyip ligand was modelled by allowing two alternative positions for C27/C27′ C28/C28′ C30/C30′ C30/C31′ and C40/C40′ C41/C41′ C43/C43′ C44/C44′ of 1, C1/C1′, C2/C2′, C4/C4′ and C5/C5′ in 2 and C10/C10′ C11/C11′ C12/C12′ C13/C13′ C14/C14′ and N1/N1′ of 3, with occupancies of 0.50, respectively. Due to the presence of large cavities in the structure, the crystals of 1–3 scattered weakly and only low-angle data could be detected. Thus, the SQUEEZE routine9 of PLATON10 was applied to remove the contributions to the scattering from the solvent molecules (Table 1).

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structure

Polymers 1 and 2 were prepared by a solvothermal reaction of M(NO3)2·6H2O with H2pyip in DMF–EtOH–H2O solvent at 80 °C for 72 h. The two polymers possess the same structural motifs. However, according to the single-crystal structure analyses, 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P , whereas 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. Only the structure of 1 is discussed in this article. 1 displays a (3,6)-connected 3D noninterpenetrated ladder polymer built from binuclear cobalt clusters and pyip2− ligands. The asymmetric unit contains four independent Co atoms, each of which is octahedrally coordinated by three carboxylate O atoms, one O atom of the μ-H2O group, one pendant pyridyl N atom from pyip and one terminal H2O molecule. There are two different pyip ligands. Each pyip2− coordinates with four Co atoms and the carboxylate groups in each pyip2− adopt a similar distorted syn–syn conformation. The two pyip2− arrange parallel to each other. It is different that one pyip (N1) coordinates with Co1, Co3 and Co4, while another bridges Co1, Co2 and Co4. The two adjacent Co ions are bonded together to generate a binuclear cobalt cluster {Co2(μ-H2O)(CO2)2} with Co1⋯Co2 and Co3⋯Co4 distance of 3.564 Å and 3.557 Å, (Ni1⋯Ni1A is 3.498 Å in 2) and Co–O–Co angles in pairs, 106.43° and 106.83° (Ni–O–Ni is 110.43° in 2). Interestingly, two Co atoms in 1 are linked via two pyip2− ligands, forming a repeating square-like [Co2(C12N2O2)] unit with dimensions of 8.5 × 5.5 Å (Fig. 1). The units are further interconnected to form 1D ladder-shaped chains, respectively, extending along the crystallographic a and c axis, which result in the formation of a series of parallel ladder chains in which pyip2− ligands are used as the rails and rungs in the ladder motifs. It is noted that the CoII ions (Co1, Co2, and Co3, Co4) in the binuclear cobalt clusters are located in double ladder chains extending along the a and c axis (Fig. 2a), which are connected by μ-H2O groups with an inclination of 67°, whereas isophthalic acid fragments of the pyip2− ligands and μ-H2O groups bridge the parallel chains, with the distance being about 4 Å (Fig. 2b). Viewed along the a and c axis, the 1D ladder-shaped chains are shown in Fig. 2c and 2d. Consequently, all the ladders are linked together by pyip2− ligands and μ-H2O groups resulting in the formation of a fascinating noninterpenetrated 3D ladder polymer (Fig. 3b). Ladder-shaped chains are commonly observed in 1D complexes and the catenation of 1D molecular ladders either in inclined or parallel modes have been shown to generate higher dimensional polymers, such as 1D → 2D and 1D → 3D. However, the ones in 1, linked by organic ligands and inorganic groups creating a noninterpenetrated 3D ladder polymer, are scarce.

The overall framework of 1 can be described as a 3D topology 3,6-connected ant net with a Schläfli symbol of (426)4(44628810)2,11 upon considering pyip and the binuclear cobalt cluster {Co2(μ-H2O)(CO2)2} as 3- and 6-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. 3a). The 3D framework aligns along the axis shown in Fig. 2 to form 1D square-like channels with diameters of ∼11 Å (considering the van der Waals radii). Remarkably, the size of the voids estimated by PLATON12 is 49.1% of the total volume. Notably, the pore surfaces of these channels are mainly surrounded by phenyl rings of pyip, furnishing a mostly hydrophobic channel system.

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility of 1 in the form of χMT versus T is displayed in Fig. 4. The value of χMT at 300 K is 4.8 cm3 K mol−1, which is greater than the expected value of 3.75 cm3 K mol−1 for two isolated high-spin Co(II) ions (g = 2 and S = 3/2). This is a common phenomenon for Co(II) centers because of their strong spin–orbit coupling interaction.13 As the temperature cools, χMT continuously decreases from 4.6 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K to 3.2 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, indicating antiferromagnetic coupling between the Co ions. The molar magnetic susceptibility over the full temperature range obeys the Curie–Weiss law of χM = C/(T − θ) with θ = −5.4 K and C = 4.95 cm3 K mol−1 in the range of 300–2 K. The coupling between Co1⋯Co2 is assumed to be equal to that between the Co3⋯Co4 centers because of their nearly equal Co⋯Co separations (3.564 and 3.557 Å) and similar bridging pathways. The CoII located in the octahedral environment is in a high spin state (t2g)5(eg)2. In this case, the orbital contribution to the magnetic moment cannot be neglected in the fitting procedure.14 Thus, in order to obtain an estimate of the strength of the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction, the following simple phenomenological equation could be used:15
 
χMT = Aexp(−E1/kT) + Bexp(−E2/kT) (1)
to give obtained values of A + B = 5.01 cm3 K mol−1 and −E1/k = −42.8 K (the effect of spin–orbit coupling). As for the value found for the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction, it is very weak indeed, corresponding to J = −0.58 K.

For 2, the experimental χMT value is 1.91 cm3 K mol−1 per Ni2 unit (Fig. 4), which is slightly lower than the expected spin-only value (χMT = 2.00 cm3 K mol−1, S = 1, assuming g = 2) for two high-spin Ni(II) atoms in each Ni2 unit per formula. Upon cooling, χMT steadily decreases from 300 to 2 K. The molar magnetic susceptibility over the full temperature range obeys the Curie–Weiss law of χM = C/(T − θ), with θ = −4.54 K and C = 1.90 cm3 K mol−1 in the range of 300–2 K. From the structural point, we can use the classical spin model and the spin Hamiltonian, which is H = −2JS1S2. An exact matrix diagonalization by using MAPACK allowed us to calculate the J and g magnetic parameters. An excellent fit was obtained with J = −0.81 K and g = 2, with an agreement factor R = 6.12 × 10−4.

In order to investigate the influence of heterometallic substitution on the magnetic behavior, we decided to introduce an intermediate Co–Ni polymer. Principally, there are two strategies for the synthesis of heteronuclear coordination polymers: cation-exchange and one-pot reactions.16 Previous efforts of our group made us believe that central metal ion exchange can serve as an effective method to modify the properties of crystalline materials by varying central metal ions.16a Unfortunately, we did not obtain the desired exchange products, which may be attributed to the similar coordination ability of the in-going metal ions and central ones. Hence, we employed pyip to assemble Co and Ni ions in the same condition as the preparations of 1 and 2 and successfully synthesized the intermediate CoII-doped NiII coordination polymer. Fig. 5 shows a photograph of the crystal samples of 1–3 and the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns for 1–3 are also shown in Fig. 6. The patterns of 3 are consistent with those of 1 and 2, which indicates that polymers 1, 2 and 3 are isomorphous. 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, as does 2. The distance of M1⋯M1A is 3.523 Å and M–O–M is 109.25° in each dimer system of 3. The magnetic behavior of 3 in the form of χMT versus T plots is shown in Fig. 4. The data between 300–2 K were fitted by the Curie–Weiss law to give constants of θ = −2.8 K and C = 2.82 cm3 K mol−1, respectively. As the temperature cools, χMT continuously decreases from 2.77 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K to 1.89 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, indicating the presence of very weak antiferromagnetic interactions between the M(II) ions. Although the ratio of Co to Ni in 3 is close to one, there are at least three kinds of magnetic interactions within the dimer: Co⋯Co, Ni⋯Ni and Co⋯Ni, and these interactions should be considered. However, we cannot define the ratios of all the components. We attempted to analyse the magnetic behavior by using the model: 25% Co dimer, 25% Ni dimer, 50% mixed dimer because that's what is likely really going on in terms of a statistical distribution. However, the fitting result is not very good.

Magneto-structural correlation studies

Extensive work on carboxylate bridges transferring magnetism has shown that the exchange coupling is strongly antiferromagnetic with a syn–syn conformation, very weakly so (possibly ferromagnetic) with a syn–anti conformation and weak-to-medium antiferromagnetic with an anti–anti conformation.17 Looking at the structure of complex 1, the magnetic interactions are transmitted through the carboxylate bridges along (–OCO–Co). The syn–syn geometry of the carboxylate favors strong antiferromagnetic interactions. However, the syn–syn conformation here is much distorted, which could not induce very effective magnetic orbital overlap, so weak antiferromagnetic interactions are observed in these polymers. Magnetic orbital analysis also supports overall antiferromagnetic interactions between the NiII ions. The magnetic orbitals are composed of NiII dx2–y2 and dz2 ligand orbitals. The considerable contribution of the μ2-O bridges is responsible for the overlap between the dz2 orbitals, which consequently induces antiferromagnetic interactions. The θ values of complex 3 indicate that the magnetic exchange interactions within the Ni–Co mixed crystals are different from homometallic 1 and 2. Although the three complexes present antiferromagnetic interactions, the strength of the magnetic couplings is somewhat different, which confirms that distinctive paramagnetic ions almost have different contributions for transmitting coupling interactions. Our results demonstrate that magnetic properties can be adjusted by changing the relative ratios of metals. This work may open a new avenue for the preparation of magnetic materials with adjustable magnetic behaviors.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we have successfully synthesized and characterized three unprecedented isomorphous 3D noninterpenetrated CoII, NiII and CoII-doped NiII ladder-polymers. Although the magnetic results indicate that the three polymers show antiferromagnetic interactions, the approach provided in this work is useful for systematically studying the metal ion–magnetic behavior relationship. Such a strategy may have advantages for the generation of more interesting and adjustable magnetic materials.
