Porous carbon-coated ZnO nanoparticles derived from low carbon content formic acid-based Zn(II) metal-organic frameworks towards long cycle lithium-ion anode material

Abstract

Single-C formic acid-based metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are used to construct novel ZnO@porous carbon matrix nanocomposites by controlled pyrolysis. In the constructed nanocomposites, the porous carbon matrices act as a confined support to prevent agglomeration of the ZnO nanoparticles and create a rapid electron conductive network. Meanwhile, the well-defined, continuous porous structured MOFs provide a large specific surface area, which increases the contact of electrolyte-electrode and improves the penetration of electrolyte. Especially, the reasonable choice of formic acid-based MOFs construct the low carbon content composite, which contribute to the high energy density and long cycle life. The constructed nanocomposites show stable, ultrahigh rate lithium ion storage properties of 650 mAh gâˆ’1 at charge/discharge rate of 1 C even after 200 cycles.

1. Introduction

Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have successfully captured the portable electronics market over the past decades [1], [2]. However, extensive efforts have to be made to improve the electrochemical behavior of anode materials of LIBs to master the forthcoming markets of energy storage for renewable sources, hybrid electric vehicle and electric vehicles [3], [4]. As a representative example of the new-style anode materials, metal oxides can potentially be substituted for graphite (âˆ¼370 mAh gâˆ’1) to meet the demand for high capacities [5], [6], [7], [8]. Nevertheless, the practical application of most metal oxides suffers from their pulverization problem caused by the huge volume change during lithiation/delithiation processes, which would result in the loss of electrical conductivity and consequently a rapid capacity fading in the cycling [9], [10], [11]. To overcome this problem, several methods have been developed, including the preparation of nanocomposites with an inactive matrix, such as carbon, which mechanically buffers against volume changes [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. Many previous results prove that increasing the porosity could be an effective way to improve the lithium storage properties of the materials, as the void space provided by these pores can buffer the volume variation during the charge-discharge cycles, leading to enhanced capacity [17], [18]. Based on the above analysis, new strategy in the field of synthesis porous nanocomposites with suitable structural parameters is believed to hold the key to further breakthroughs in LIBs applications.

As compared to other precursors, MOFs offer distinct properties because of their microstructure and porosity, which provide a unique opportunity to develop a new class of highly tailorable functional nanomaterials with promising applications [19], [20], [21], [22]. However, the carbon content in the MOFs derived composite is very important to the electrochemical performance. The high carbon content will increase the rate capability obviously, lead to bad effect to the cycle performance (owing to the side reaction between carbons with electrolyte), and decrease the total energy density of the ZnO composite due to the low specific capacity of carbon (compared to ZnO) [23], [24], [25]. At present, the carbon content in the ZnO composite derived from MOFs is more than 30 wt%. Hence, the development of low carbon content (âˆ¼20 wt%) ZnO composite is of great importance.

In this work, low carbon content ZnO composite derived from MOFs was synthesized through a controlled pyrolysis of formic-based MOFs (Scheme 1, low carbon content ligand, the molecular structure of {[Zn3(HCOO)6]Â·DMF}n is shown in Fig. S1). In this composite, ZnO nanoparticles are well-dispersed and embedded in hierarchically porous carbon matrices. The porous carbon matrices not only creates a conductive network that provides a rapid electron channels to ZnO nanoparticles, but also acts as a confined support to prevent agglomeration and growth of the ZnO nanoparticles. With the reasonable choice of organic ligand (formic acid with one C in the molecule), the carbon content in the composite is 20 wt%. The tailor-fit microstructures enabled the display of exceptionally high Li storage performances. The composite shows a remarkable high-rate performance and a stable cycle performance of 650 mAh gâˆ’1 at 1 C even after 200 cycles. On the basis of our extensive literature study, the long term cycling stability prior to this study is extremly rare [26], [27], [28], [29]. Thus, the proposed method provides new effective ways to fabricate high-performance anode materials based on ZnO@porous carbon matrix.

The slurry was pressed onto a cleaned copper foil by a doctor-balding method and dried at 80 Â°C for 10 hours under vacuum. Coin cells (CR2025) were assembled in an argon-filled glove-box (Mbraun, H2O < 0.1 ppm, O2 < 1 ppm) using metallic lithium as the counter/reference electrode, porous polypropylene films (two pieces, Celgard 2400) as the separator and LiPF6 (1 M) in ethylene carbonate, diethyl carbonate and ethylmethyl carbonate (1:1:1 in vol) as an electrolyte. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were performed using a PARSTAT 2273 advanced electrochemical system over the frequency range between 1 MHz and 100 mHz with the amplitude of an ac signal of 10 mV.

3. Results and discussion

The X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the ZnO@porous carbon is shown in Fig. 1, which can be indexed to the standard ZnO. This suggests that the ZnO with high purity is obtained by the pyrolysis of MOFs. The corresponding ZnO peaks exhibited line broadening due to the small size of ZnO particles [30].

The structural reorganization of the MOFs to ZnO@porous carbon, synthesized via a controlled pyrolysis reaction to obtain the initial state of the transformation, was investigated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The microparticles with cracked surfaces (Fig. S2) were developed during decomposition of the organic species. The morphology of ZnO@porous carbon was characterized by well-defined cubic crystals 200â€“300 Î¼m in width (Fig. 2a). Further evidence for the uniformly distribution of Zn, O, and C components in the ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite is identified by the elemental mapping. As shown in Fig. 2bâ€“d, the elemental mapping images of Zn and O clearly demonstrate the ZnO nanoparticles are homogeneous distribution in the 3D porous conductive carbon matrix, which insures intimate contact between the two components. Meanwhile, the elemental mapping image of C indicates that the carbon coating layer provides an effective electron conductive paths in the ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite.

TEM images reveal that the cubic microparticles consist of ZnO nanoparticles and amorphous carbon (Fig. 3a). A magnifying TEM image, detected from the ZnO nanoparticles (labeled by red circles) with a size approximately 3 nm is uniformly embedded in the conductive matrix composed of the amorphous carbon (Fig. 3b). The lattice fringe spacing of 0.26 nm correspond to the (002) plane of ZnO crystal, which clearly confirmed that the ZnO nanoparticles is highly crystalline (Fig. 3c). The selected-area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (Fig. 3d) displays a typical hexagonal wurtzite crystal structure of ZnO, indicating that the obtained carbon is nearly amorphous and lacks long-range order.

For direct comparison to ZnO@porous carbon, Fig. 4a and b display typical SEM and TEM images of bare ZnO nanoparticles, respectively. What merits our primary concern, without the confinement by the porous carbon, the bare ZnO nanoparticles tend to aggregate. This result demonstrates that porous carbon plays a crucial role in the formation of well-dispersed ZnO nanoparticles [31].

Raman spectroscopy is a forceful mean to investigate the modification of carbon-based material. The Raman spectra of ZnO@porous carbon and bare ZnO are shown in Fig. 5. The two distinct bands at 1320 and 1590 cmâˆ’1 can be assigned to the typical D and G bands of amorphous carbon, respectively. The D-band is caused by edges or structural defects which can break the selection rule and symmetry; the G-band is related to the vibration mode in graphite-like carbon [32]. The peak at around 445 cmâˆ’1 corresponds to E2 (high) mode, which is the characteristic peak of the hexagonal wurtzite phase [33]. The intensity of this peak is remarkably reduced in ZnO@porous carbon compared to that in bare ZnO due possibly to the interaction between ZnO nanoparticles and amorphous carbon. The peak at 590 cmâˆ’1 is assigned to E1 (LO) mode associated with the structural defects in ZnO. The bands located at near 328 and 1145 cmâˆ’1 are due to the multiple-phonon scattering processes [34]. The Raman spectrum of the sample confirms the coexistence of ZnO nanoparticles and amorphous carbon.

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) determines a mass ratio of the two materials in the composites [35]. The amount of carbon within the composite is estimated to be 20.1 wt% by TGA test under air atmosphere as shown in Fig. S3. This amount is in good agreement with carbon content in our precursor, i. e. the as-prepared MOFs. It is worth mentioning, the appropriate carbon content in the composite is very important to the electrochemical performance. High carbon content will lead to lower energy and bad cycle performance due to the side reaction between carbon and electrolyte; low carbon content canâ€™t provide good rate performance.

The Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) specific surface area and pore size distribution of the ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite were measured by nitrogen isothermal adsorption (Fig. S4) [36], [37]. The BET specific surface area of the hybrid is 118 m2 gâˆ’1, which can provide more reaction sites and is beneficial for electrolyte access. The sample exhibits a pore size distribution centered at 30 nm. Combined with the observation of a considerable increase of nitrogen adsorption at low pressure, this material exhibits a hierarchical pore system consisted of micro- and mesopores. This unique mesoporosity between the ZnO and carbon matrix may promote the diffusion of electrolyte ions, suggesting that the nanocomposite may show a large specific capacity and good high-rate performance.

The electrochemical performance of the ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite was evaluated by a discharge/charge test [38], [39], [40]. In Fig. 6, the discharge capacity during the first cycle achieved 1370 mAh gâˆ’1 at 0.2 C. During discharging, the profiles show sloped reaction plateau at 1.2 V and a very obvious long plateau located at about 0.5 V, which can be attributed to the reduction of Zn2+ to Zn0 and LixZn, and the formation of a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film caused by the decomposition of electrolyte [41]. During the first charge process, the five plateaus, located at 0.28, 0.36, 0.55, 0.68 and 1.23 V are ascribed to the multi-step dealloying process of LixZn alloy.

To highlight the rate capability of ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite, the rate cycling performance is shown in Fig. 7 [42], [43]. The nanocomposite presents excellent rate cycling performance. The ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite exhibits prominent rate capability with discharge capacities of 970 mAh gâˆ’1 at 0.1 C, 800 mAh gâˆ’1 at 0.5 C, 660 mAh gâˆ’1 at 1 C, and 570 mAh gâˆ’1 at 2 C. Notably, a discharge capacity of 375 mAh gâˆ’1 can still be obtained at a ultrahigh rate of 5 C. When the charge rate is returned to 0.1 C after 50 cycles, the reversible capacity of the nanocomposite reaches 955 mAh gâˆ’1. The greatly enhanced rate performance of the nanocomposite can be attributed to the tiny ZnO nanocrystallites, conductive carbon network and unique mesoporous structure, resulting in better electronic and ionic conduction throughout the electrode.

Whatâ€™s more, the nanocomposite shows an excellent reversible capacity [44], [45]. The cycling performance of ZnO@porous carbon at 1 C for 200 cycles is shown in Fig. 8. The first discharge and charge capacities are 1180 and 915 mAh gâˆ’1, respectively, with an initial coulombic efficiency of 77.5%. As observed, there is a large irreversible capacity ratio (22.5%) due to the irreversible conversion reaction, for example, electrolyte decomposition, formation of SEI layer, which are common for most anode materials. The discharge capacity of ZnO@porous carbon drops slightly after the first 10 cycles, and then gradually increases and keeps the capacity of 650 mAh gâˆ’1 even after the 200th cycles. The excellent capacity retention can be attributing to the stable carbon matrix which acts as a confined support to prevent agglomeration and growth of the ZnO nanoparticles.

To explain the brilliant electrochemical performance of ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite, EIS was applied before cycling and after 200 variable-rate cycles [46]. As shown in Fig. 9, the high-frequency semicircle corresponds to the charge-transfer resistance (Rct), the inclined line can be attributed to the diffusion process of lithium ion within the electrode. The values of Rct show slight decrement after 50 cycles, suggesting that the nanocomposite owns high electron conductivity and a fast charge-transfer reaction for Li+ insertion/extraction [47], [48].

The superior performance was determined by the unique microstructure of the ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite. Firstly, the ZnO@porous carbon electrodes own well-developed mesoporous derived from the unique MOFs structure, which can facilitate the rapid Li+ transfer. The large specific surface area supplies an adequate electrode/electrolyte contact region resulting in a reduction in the current density of unit surface area and an enhancement in the charge/discharge rate. Secondly, the agglomeration and growth of the ZnO nanoparticles are intensively suppressed during cycling process because of the confinement effect of the carbon matrix. Moreover, the carbon matrix can provide steady support to the ZnO nanoparticles. This tough micro-structure will definitely strengthen the cycling steadiness of the nanocomposite. Finally, ZnO nanoparticles are embedded in the carbon matrix, constructing an elegant conductive network that supplies better electron conductivity and efficient charge transport. All these above would determinately promote the Li+ and electron transfer in the nanocomposite electrodes, resulting in greatly enhanced lithium storage properties.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a reasonable design and controlled pyrolysis of low carbon content formic acid-based MOFs for the synthesis of ZnO@porous carbon nanocomposite has been realized. The well-defined, continuous channels derived from the porous structure of the MOFs provide a large specific surface area that increases the electrolyte-electrode contact area, ensuring that the electrolyte can easily penetrate the mesoporous. Whatâ€™s more, the agglomeration and growth of the ZnO nanoparticles are intensively suppressed during cycling process because of the confinement effect of the appropriate carbon content matrix. As expected, the materials exhibit promising Li storage properties, high specific charge capacities (âˆ¼970 mAh gâˆ’1 at 0.1 C), stable cyclability (âˆ¼200 cycles with almost 100% capacity retention after the initial 10 cycle), and good performance rate (âˆ¼375 mAh gâˆ’1 at 5 C). This unique synthesis of LIBs anode materials using MOFs paves a new way for the application of MOFs system. It will potentially be expanded to other high-capacity metal oxide anode material systems that undergo a large volume expansion during an electrochemical reaction.
