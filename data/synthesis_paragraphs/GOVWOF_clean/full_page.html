Introduction

Organic dyes, as important industrial products, have been widely used for the dyeing of silk, cotton, linen and Orlon fiber, and in the paper-making industry.1 They also, however, have many drawbacks, including being toxic contaminants,2 and have generated much pollution.3 It is thus of vital importance to solve the problem of pollution caused by organic dyes. Thus far, several approaches, such as coagulation, membrane filtration, adsorption, photocatalysis and advanced oxidation, have been developed to treat organic dye wastes.4 Among them, adsorption is considered to be one of the best technologies because of its effectiveness, efficiency and low cost.5 In this regard, the well-known adsorbents, such as activated carbons, zeolites and polymeric materials, have often been utilized to remove organic dye pollutants.6 These adsorbents can readily adsorb various mixed organic dyes, but are generally poor at selectively removing the targeted organic dye wastes.6 It would be desirable to be able to re-use remaining organic dyes if they could be selectively separated from the mixed organic dye wastes by the adsorbents. It is thus necessary for this purpose to develop highly selective adsorption for the separation of organic dye wastes.

Porous metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) are multifunctional materials with intriguing topological structures and tunable pore sizes,7â€“9 and are thus good candidates for selective separation of organic dye wastes. As potential organic dye adsorbents, however, porous MOFs have received much less attention than the well-known activated carbons and zeolites.10,11 As far as we know, only a few examples of porous MOFs being used for organic dye adsorption have been reported to date.11 Among these examples, only one positively charged MOF, [In3O(pba)3(bpdc)1.5]Â·(NO3) (pba = 4-(pyridine-4-yl)benzoate and bpdc = 4,4â€²-biphenyldicarboxylate), can completely separate two types of dyes through selective adsorption of the anionic organic dye orange G.11a Moreover, a negatively charged MOF that can completely separate mixed cationic organic dyes through highly selective adsorption has not yet been reported. Thus, it is highly desirable to develop new MOFs with negatively charged frameworks for highly selective cation exchange and separation (Scheme 1).

In this work, a new microporous negatively charged MOF, [(C2H5)2NH2]2[Mn6(L)(OH)2(H2O)6]Â·4DEF (1) (H12L = 5,5â€²,5â€²â€²,5â€²â€²â€²,5â€²â€²â€²â€²,5â€²â€²â€²â€²â€²-[1,2,3,4,5,6-phenylhexamethoxyl]hexaisophthalic acid and DEF = N,Nâ€²-diethylformamide), was successfully synthesized and structurally characterized. MOF 1 exhibits highly selective adsorption and separation of the cationic dye methylene blue (MB+) in DEF solution through an ion-exchange process. This work represents the first example of a negatively charged MOF completely separating mixed cationic dyes through a highly selective adsorption process.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of MOF 1

The pale yellow block-shaped crystals of 1 were synthesized with H12L and MnCl2Â·4H2O under solvothermal conditions. The SQUEEZE routine of PLATON was applied to remove the contributions to the scattering from the guests of 1, and its molecular formula was calculated and confirmed by the thermogravimetric and elemental analytical data.13 Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that 1 crystallizes in the trigonal space group Pm1. As shown in Fig. 1a, the asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half Mn(II) ions, a quarter of an L anion, a half of a hydroxyl ion, one and a half water molecules, one DEF molecule, and a half of a [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cation. The [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cation is generated from the decomposed DEF, and balances the negative charge of the framework. The three manganese ions, termed Mn1, Mn2 and Mn3, are bridged by one Î¼3-OH group to form a planar trinuclear Mn3(Î¼3-OH) cluster with C3 symmetry (Fig. S1aâ€ ). Each Mn3(Î¼3-OH) cluster is encapsulated by six carboxylate groups and three water molecules to yield a trigonal prismatic Mn3(Î¼3-OH)(COO)6(H2O)3 secondary building unit (SBU).

Organic dye adsorption and separation

During the MOF adsorption process, the charge and size of organic dyes are the two most important factors.11a Therefore, two sets of different experiments were carried out. First, the effect of organic dye charge on its ability to be absorbed by 1 was investigated. For this study, five organic dyes with different types of charges, methylene blue (MB+), Sudan I (SD0), solvent orange 7 (SO0), acid orange A (AOâˆ’) and methyl orange (MOâˆ’), were selected (Table 1 and Fig. S3â€ ). Typically, the fresh crystalline samples of 1 were put in DEF solutions of MB+, SD0, SO0, AOâˆ’ and MOâˆ’, respectively (Fig. S3â€ ). The content of dye in solution was monitored by UV-Vis absorption spectroscopy. As illustrated in Fig. S4â€“S7,â€  the concentrations of SD0, SO0, AOâˆ’ and MOâˆ’ in DEF solutions were nearly constant with time, but the concentration of MB+ in DEF solution drastically decreased (Fig. 3). After 96 hours, ca. 67.5% of MB+ was absorbed by crystalline samples of 1, with the color of the solution changing from blue to light blue (Fig. 3). To further demonstrate the selective adsorption of MB+, the crystalline samples of 1 were soaked in mixed dyes MB+/SD0, MB+/SO0, MB+/AOâˆ’ and MB+/MOâˆ’ solvents, respectively. The same phenomenon was observed as above: only MB+ was absorbed by 1 with the solution colors varying from green to jade green and the crystalline sample changing from pale yellow to blue (Fig. 4). It can be inferred that an ion-exchange process occurred between the free [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cations in the channels of MOF 1 and MB+ cations in the DEF solution. However, the [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cations cannot be exchanged by neutral or negatively charged dyes. In other words, the crystalline samples of 1 are only able to selectively absorb the positively charged dyes through the ion-exchange process.

The effect of the size of the organic dye on its ability to be absorbed by 1 was also studied. The three candidate organic dyes MB+, rhodamine 6G (R6G+) and butyl rhodamine B (BRB+) possess the same positive charge, but different molecular sizes (Table 1 and Fig. S3â€ ). When crystalline samples of 1 were respectively immersed in DEF solution of R6G+ and BRB+, almost no R6G+ and BRB+ cations were absorbed by 1 (Fig. S8 and S9â€ ). Once the crystalline samples of 1 were put in the mixed MB+/R6G+ and MB+/BRB+ solutions, the characteristic MB+ cation peaks decreased drastically. Meanwhile, the characteristic R6G+ and BRB+ peaks also decreased, albeit slightly, which is attributed to the interference of MB+ with these peaks (Fig. 5). The MB+ also can absorb a certain amount of incident light at 534 nm and 564 nm, and this absorption resulted in a slight decrease of the characteristic R6G+ and BRB+ peak with decreasing concentration of MB+. In addition, the colors of the mixed MB+/R6G+ and MB+/BRB+ solution changed significantly from dark blue to purple, and the crystalline samples changed from being pale yellow to becoming blue. The result shows that although MB+, R6G+ and BRB+ possess the same positive charge, their different sizes influenced how they were absorbed by 1. In other words, the slightly large sizes of R6G+ and BRB+ are the main reason that ion exchange could not occur with the free [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cation.

To further confirm that the selective dye adsorption is an ion-exchange process, a comparable organic dye release experiment was performed. Typically, the MB+-absorbed crystalline samples of MB+@1 were soaked in fresh DEF and NaCl-containing DEF solution, respectively. As shown in Fig. 6 and 7, after 144 hours, MB+ cations were apparently released in NaCl-containing DEF solution, while only a trace amount of MB+ was released in fresh DEF solution. Moreover, the release of MB+ in NaCl-containing DEF solution was triggered by Na+ ions. The absorption peaks of MB+ in NaCl-containing DEF solution increased drastically, indicating that the amount of MB+ released in NaCl-containing DEF solution is much more than the amount released in fresh DEF solution. The above result further confirmed that the selective adsorption of MB+ is an ion-exchange process. In addition, the PXRD of MB+@1 and MB+-released MB+@1 showed the same patterns as compound 1, indicating the stability of crystalline sample 1 (Fig. S10â€ ).

The above experimental results reveal that the selective adsorption of MB+ is an ion-exchange process. For the anionic MOF 1, only organic dyes with positive charges are likely to enter the channels and exchange with the [(C2H5)2NH2]+ cations. Nevertheless, this ion-exchange behavior is also highly size-dependent for positively charged organic dyes such as MB+, R6G+ and BRB+. Therefore, the channel diameter and positively charged dye size can be used for the analysis of dye accessibility. For this purpose, geometrical optimization of the studied dyes without any symmetry constraint was carried out by using the B3LYP16 exchangeâ€“correlation functional as implemented in the Gaussian 09 computational chemistry program (Fig. 8).17 The B3LYP functional is a combination of Becke's three-parameter hybrid exchange functional16 and the Leeâ€“Yangâ€“Parr18 correlation functional. Basis sets of 6-31G(d) for C, N, O, S, and H atoms were applied. Harmonic vibrational frequency calculations were used to confirm that the optimized structures were minima, as characterized by positive vibrational frequencies. The measured dimensions of organic dye molecules from the calculation are shown in Table 1. For the positively charged dye MB+, its two relatively short molecular dimensions along the x and y directions are 4.59 and 8.01 Ã…, respectively, which are much smaller than the diameter of channel B for MOF 1 (14.4 Ã…). As a result, the access of dye MB+ into the channel of MOF 1 is allowed. Relative to the dye MB+, R6G+ and BRB+ possess relatively larger sizes. As illustrated in Table 1, their molecular lengths along the y and z directions are much larger than the diameter of channel B (14.4 Ã…). That is, only one dimension along the x direction (10.89 Ã… for R6G+ and 12.68 Ã… for BRB+) is slightly shorter than the diameter of channel B. Thus, R6G+ and BRB+ were indeed denied access by channel B due to their relatively larger y and z dimensions.

Conclusions

In summary, we have synthesized a new microporous negatively charged MOF 1 by employing multidentate carboxylate ligands and MnCl2Â·4H2O under solvothermal condition. UV-Vis spectroscopy analysis showed that 1 exhibits a remarkable capability to highly selectively adsorb and separate organic dyes. This selective adsorption and separation process is capable of size- or charge-selective ion exchange of organic dyes, and this selectivity can be controlled by the structural characteristics of MOF 1. This work also provides the basis for additional applications in size- and charge-selective ion chromatography.
