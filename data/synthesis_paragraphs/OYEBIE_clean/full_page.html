Abstract

A new coordination polymer, [Zn(dpe)(bdc)]⋅4H2O (ZndB; dpe=1,2‐bis(4‐pyridyl)ethane, bdc2−=dianion of benzenedicarboxylic acid), which possesses a 3D metal–organic framework (MOF) has been synthesized and structurally characterized. This 3D MOF is constructed by the assembly of helical channels filled with guest water molecules in both inner and outer regions of the channel. The resulting network also creates a 2D water layer containing hydrogen‐bonded (H2O)16 rings as the basic building units. Thermogravimetric and powder X‐ray diffraction measurements of ZndB revealed a two‐step weight loss of water molecules with a reversible water adsorption/desorption process in the inner channel for the first stage, and irreversible water desorption in the outer channel for the second stage. This spongelike property is manifested by the excimer emission originating from interaction between dpe (π*) and the other dpe (π) of the proximal helical channel, which is highly sensitive to the environmental perturbation. Powder X‐ray analyses reveal that the dehydration process induces the readjustment of dpe π–π stacking distance/orientation, which results in dramatic luminescence changes from dim pale blue (λem≈470 nm) upon hydration to bright white‐light generation (broad, λem≈500–550 nm) upon water depletion, accompanied by a ≈100‐fold increase in the emission intensity.

Introduction

Porous coordination polymers or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) containing guest molecules provide a unique opportunity for developing advanced functional materials and for studying fundamental nanomolecular assemblies in confined spaces.1 In particular, microporous MOFs have received extensive attention because of their potential applications in gas storage2 and separation.3 It is now well established that guest molecules may also play a key role by acting as an essential support to the porosity of the host lattice MOFs.4, 5 Such a framework may require formation of clusters or aggregation of the guest molecules to sustain the scaffold. In this approach, solvent molecules, especially water, commonly occupy a substantial portion of the unit cell volume of crystals even without direct involvement in the coordination. Accordingly, the coordination network and the lattice solvent may appear to have a symbiotic relationship.6 Discrete water clusters including trimers,7 hexamers,8 octamers,8b, 9 decamers,10 (H2O)14 water clusters,9b and 1D infinite water chains11 have frequently been found in coordination polymers. Conversely, reports regarding 2D water/ice layers containing large water rings in the MOF are scant. Thus, understanding how the clusters link together12a as building units to form a 2D network12b and the exploration of its perhaps unprecedented properties remain highly interesting and challenging.

What is more, the luminescence properties of MOFs have attracted much attention within the last ten years because of their potential applications in optoelectronics.13 In the relevant approach, on the one hand, rigid multi‐carboxylate ligands have been extensively employed to link metal ions and produce high‐dimensional frameworks.14 On the other hand, the flexible dipyridyl ligand and its derivatives can be used as an ancillary ligand, together with the carboxylate ligand, to meet the requirement of coordination geometries of metal ions in the assembly process.15 By combining these O‐donor and N‐donor ligands with transition‐metal ions, for example, ZnII, which possess d10 configurations, the resulting MOFs can exhibit appealing structures as well as photoluminescence properties.14c, 16a, 17 For instance, by using 5‐hydroxyisophthalic acid, Zn(NO3)2⋅6H2O, and flexible dipyridyl ligands with different spacers, Li et al.18 reported elegant interpenetrating diamondoid networks, the emission of which was assigned as ligand‐to‐metal charge transfer (LMCT). Wang et al.19 reported four new coordination polymers through hydrothermal self‐assembly with biphenyl‐3,3′,4,4′‐tetracarboxylic acid, ZnII, and bipyridyl ligands as the molecular building blocks. The complexes display luminescence properties resulting from intraligand fluorescence (π*→π transition). In yet another approach, Jin and co‐workers20 reported the syntheses and structures of [Zn2(bpy)(cca)2]n⋅0.5nH2O (bpy=4,4′‐bipyridine; H2CCA=2‐carboxycinnamic acid) and [Zn2(bpe)(cca)]n⋅0.5nH2O (bpe=1,2‐bis(4‐pyridyl)ethane). The associated luminescence properties are attributed to the π*→n transition. Having reviewed the literature we noticed that the structure of the coordination polymer framework may be greatly affected by the solvent system; that is, the solvent molecules are involved in the network as the guest molecules or coordinating ligands.21 The associated emission properties may accordingly be influenced by the incorporation of guests, thereby perhaps leading to emission spectral shifts, intensity changes, or even new types of emission as a result of excimer or exciplex formation.22 Extensive progress has been made in the development of various luminescent MOFs, among which several elegant works have reported on the guest‐ and/or guest‐morphology‐dependent luminescence.23 For example, Li et al.23d as well as Li and co‐workers23e recently reported that guest molecules induced changes in fluorescence. In addition, some purely organic hydrogen‐bonded frameworks exhibit similar behavior.23c Unfortunately, detailed mechanistic studies on the guest‐ and/or guest‐morphology‐dependent luminescence are rather few.23c In particular, to our knowledge, the detailed mechanism regarding reversible changes of luminescence properties and their correlation with respect to the structural alternation upon host–guest recognition has not yet been fully comprehended.

Herein, we report the exploration of a new coordination polymer, [Zn(dpe)(bdc)]⋅4H2O (ZndB; dpe=1,2‐bis(4‐pyridyl)ethane, bdc2−=dianion of benzenedicarboxylic acid). ZndB reveals a 3D MOF through the connectivity of ZnII ions with bis‐monodentate gauche‐dpe and bdc2− ligands. The resulting network creates a very distinctive 2D water layer containing hydrogen‐bonded (H2O)16 rings as the basic building units. Thermogravimetric (TG) and in situ powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) clearly evidenced a two‐stage weight loss of water molecules incorporating 1) a reversible water adsorption/desorption process of inner‐channel water and 2) irreversible water desorption on the outer channel. Both steps are accompanied by subtle structural alteration that causes dramatic changes of the luminescence properties in both spectrum and intensity. Details of structural characterization and structure‐versus‐luminescence properties are elaborated in the following sections.

Results and Discussion

Structural descriptions: ZndB was synthesized by hydrothermal reaction of Zn(ClO4)⋅6H2O with dpe and Na2bdc in H2O solution at 120 °C. After a reaction period of 72 h, ZndB could be isolated as colorless block‐shaped crystals. X‐ray structure determination (see Experimental Section and Table S1 in the Supporting Information) reveals that ZndB possesses a 3D MOF assembled by helical channels fully filled with guest water molecules. As shown in Figure 1, the basic building unit of ZndB is composed of a four‐coordinate ZnII center bonded with two nitrogen donors of two dpe ligands and two oxygen donors of two bdc2− ligands, and is configured in a tetrahedral geometry. Crucial bond distances and angles around the ZnII ions are listed in Table S2 (Supporting Information). The dpe ligand adopts a bis‐monodentate bridging mode with a gauche conformation connecting the ZnII centers, thereby constructing 1D helical chains (see Figure 2 a). The adjacent helical chains are then mutually interlinked through the connection between ZnII centers and bdc2− ligands that adopt a bis‐monodentate bridging mode to complete a 3D MOF (Figure 2 b), which is different from the structural isomerism of [Zn(dpe)(bdc)] with a fivefold interpenetrating 3D diamondoid MOF bridged by bis‐monodentate bdc2− and anti‐dpe ligands.16b The π–π interactions of sandwich‐type pyridyl–benzene–pyridyl of dpe–bdc–dpe (dotted circle in Figure 2 b) and pyridyl–pyridyl rings24 of dpe (dashed circle in Figure 2 b), which have the ring centroid distances of 3.685 and 3.968 Å (see also Figure 8), respectively, provide extra stabilization energy onto this 3D network.

Among the four lattice water molecules present in the asymmetric unit, two are located inside the ZnII–dpe helical channels, whereas the other two are outside. The former two water molecules (O(5) and O(8), Figure 3 a) are hydrogen‐bonded, producing a 1D helical polymeric water chain inside the helical 1D (ZnII–dpe) chains. The neighboring 1D helical water chains are then mutually interlinked with the other two water molecules outside the helical channels (O(6) and O(7)), thereby constructing a puckered four‐membered (H2O)4 ring (Figure 3 a) through hydrogen‐bond association. Such an interplay creates a 2D (H2O)16 networked layer (Figure 3 b, i.e., L16(6)). The O–O distances of hydrogen bonds between water molecules are in the range of 2.60 to 3.07 Å. Related hydrogen‐bond parameters such as mutual distances and angles are listed in Table S3 of the Supporting Information. Meanwhile, the synergistic interactions through the hydrogen bonding between the water molecules (O(7)) and uncoordinated oxygen atoms (O(2) and O(4)) of bdc2− ligands (see Figure 1) render the O–O distances of 2.76 and 2.72 Å, respectively, thus providing another key stabilization energy to hold the aggregation of a 2D water layer in the 3D MOF. In other words, the distinctive (H2O)16 hydrogen‐bonded ring structure is grown and stabilized within the 3D framework of ZndB, and vice versa, which also acts as the base substrate for further stabilization of the as‐prepared MOF. By using (H2O)16 as the subunit, as shown in Figure 3 b, the assembly of an elegant 2D‐like water channel within the 3D MOF is thus established. A movie with different rotational views for the 3D MOF of Figure 2 is provided in the Supporting Information. Despite the fact that a manageable subset of over 37 000 crystal structures, including at least one molecule of water per unit cell in the Cambridge structural database,25 has been developed to classify water aggregates,26 and some 2D water layers12a,12b containing large (>12‐membered) water rings have been observed in the solid state, we emphasize herein that ZndB represents an intriguing example with the encapsulation of 2D hydrogen‐bonded water layers within a 3D MOF.

Thermogravimetric and in situ powder X‐ray diffraction analyses: The thermal stability of a microporous MOF associated with solvent or gaseous desorption/adsorption properties may play a key role for the potential application in chemical storage.12c As for the ZndB MOF, calculated by PLATON,27a the total water‐accessible volume of this 3D framework in the unit cell is 1014.9 Å3, which accounts for 24.1 % of the total cell volume. The results of TG measurement (see Figure 4) reveal a two‐stage weight loss (total ca. 15.2 %) for ZndB in the temperature ranges from 30 to 86 °C (stage 1) and 195 to 250 °C (stage 2), which correspond to the total loss of four lattice water molecules (14.8 %) located in these 2D channels. The nitrogen gas adsorption isotherm was measured after H2O molecules were evacuated at 474 K. The isotherm obtained with N2 gas at 77 K reveals a typical Type‐I adsorption curve as defined by the IUPAC classification scheme (see Figure S2, Supporting Information). The BET surface area calculated from the nitrogen gas adsorption data is 19 m2 g−1 for ZndB. The pore diameter is thus deduced to be about 7 Å by Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) analysis,27e which is thus less than the dynamic diameter of N2. More importantly, the first stage has proven to be a reversible process, as evidenced by the plot of desorption/adsorption processes as a function of time and temperature shown in Figure 5 a, in which temperature variations (up to 120 °C) have been repeated for five cycles with nearly full recovery in weight. Conversely, stage 2 is more likely to be an irreversible process. When water is completely removed after reaching 250 °C, no more weight can be recovered during the cooling process.

To gain the structural changes in depth upon water desorption, in situ synchrotron PXRD patterns of ZndB were collected continuously from 25 to 650 °C, and the results at some specific temperatures are shown in Figure 5 b. Comparisons of crystal data between the single‐crystal and powder diffraction measurements are listed in Table S4 (Supporting Information). As indicated by the length of the three axes (see Table S4), the unit cell parameters of the powder sample are slightly larger than those of the single‐crystal sample. Interestingly, although the unit cell volume for powder samples measured at room temperature and 120 °C showed negligible change, subtlety was observed in each dimension. The unit cell dimensions of a and c axes at 120 °C became shorter than those at room temperature, whereas the b axis was slightly longer. Note that in this approach, a single‐crystal diffraction structure of ZndB was used as the initial model for Rietveld refinement of powder diffraction data. Moreover, the single‐crystal coordinates for ZndB were considered as a rigid body in Rietveld refinement except that of water in the channel. The four water molecules (O(5)–O(8)) were then adjusted through their positions and occupancies with fixed thermo parameters during the Rietveld refinement for powder diffraction data at 120 °C. Upon optimization, the occupancies for final Rietveld refinement were 0.38(2), 0.53(2), 0.76(2), and 0.13(2) for O(5), O(6), O(7), and O(8), respectively. It is thus clear that O(5) and O(8), which are located in the helical channel, are to be evacuated first during the in situ thermal treatment at 120 °C. The zeolitic water molecules, O(5)–O(8), are sensitive to low‐angle powder diffraction as demonstrated in Figure S3 (see Supporting Information). It is obvious that the PXRD pattern (Figure S3c) with the O(5), O(6), O(7), and O(8) occupancies of 0, 1, 1, and 0, respectively, matches well with the PXRD pattern (Figure S3a) at 120 °C.

Thus, with high‐quality powder patterns such as those obtained by synchrotron in situ powder diffraction, one can trace the change of zeolitic water in powders.27b–27d The in situ XRD results lead us to conclude that the dehydration process of the ZndB MOF invokes the removal of water molecules (O(5) and O(8)) inside the helical channels (stage 1, 25–130 °C), followed by the loss of water molecules outside the helical channels (stage 2, up to 250 °C). The compound clearly decomposes above 320 °C and, depending upon the heating rate, perhaps even earlier at a slower heating rate. The PXRD between 360 and 450 °C shows evidence of the continuous decomposition. Upon holding the sample at 450 °C for 5 min, as evidenced by the PXRD pattern (2θ=21.078°), the MOFs of ZndB are decomposed to form hexagonal ZnO. The powder pattern was indexed using a Dicvol program, the result of which corresponds to an anhydrous phase. We thus tentatively propose that the collapse of the ZndB crystalline structure23j might consequently seal the water channels and hence prohibits water transfer, thereby resulting in the irreversibility of the second stage of water sorption. It is also noteworthy that maintenance of reversibility of guest molecules, however, has been reported even after destruction of the MOF.23k,23l Thus, full decipherment of the actual mechanism to account for second‐stage irreversibility is still pending resolution.

Luminescence properties: Perhaps the most striking results in this study should be ascribed to the dramatic water‐ and hence MOF‐dependent luminescence properties. Taking the latent luminescence of d10 metal complexes into account (see below), the luminescence properties of both free ligands (dpe and Na2bdc) and the ZndB MOF were then investigated. In serendipity, we discovered that the photoluminescence of ZndB, in view of both spectral features and intensity, exhibited remarkable water‐content dependence that intimately correlates with the two‐stage hydration process described above. Figure 6 reveals the solid‐state absorption and single‐crystal emission for the ZndB MOF at room temperature. For a fair comparison, the solid‐state luminescence spectra of the free ligands (dpe and Na2bdc) also served as references. All luminescence spectra were acquired with a confocal microscope equipped with various laser excitation sources (see Experimental Section for details). Upon excitation at, for example, 325 nm, the water‐saturated ZndB exhibits a moderate, blue emission band maximized at ≈470 nm. The lifetime of ≈3.1 ns ensures its origin from a single manifold, that is, the fluorescence (see also Figure S4, Supporting Information). Moreover, the same emission spectral features were resolved throughout different probing areas of the crystals, thus ruling out the possibility that the emission originates from any crystal defects. For the control, the photoluminescence of the ligand Na2bdc single crystal exhibits a weak fluorescence maximized at 419 nm, consistent with that of previous reports.23h, 28a–28c As for the dpe crystal, very weak emission could be barely resolved with a peak wavelength at ≈470 nm. Morsali and co‐workers as well as other groups have reported similar (470 nm) weak emission in the solid and assigned it to the dpe excimer‐like emission.28e,28f Note that the fluorescence signal of dpe also reveals a shoulder around 400 nm, which is assigned to the emission from monomer.28g However, this normal emission band disappears in ZndB mainly due to the different and periodic arrangement of dpe–dpe.

Although the fluorescence of the ZndB crystal is clearly authentic, its origin is rather intriguing and deserves to be further explored. Because no emission could be detected when ZndB dissolves in common solvents, the fluorescence of ZndB, in part, seems to originate from the associated crystal packing. Prior to the analyses of ZndB emission, we first suspected that the emission properties of ZndB may strongly correlate with water‐ and thus temperature‐dependent transformation of ZndB. Indeed, the results of temperature‐dependent spectroscopy shown in Figures 7 and 8 are remarkable. Upon increasing the temperature from RT to ≈86 °C (see T1 in Figure 7), which corresponds to the first stage of desorption involving inner‐channel water molecules (see above), the fluorescence peak wavelength is gradually redshifted from 470 (RT) to 510 nm (86 °C). Such changes are also accompanied by a ≈30‐fold increase of the emission intensity. Also, the fluorescence intensity increases upon excitation at 325 nm, which is correlated with the increase of the 325 nm absorbance at RT, 85, and 200 °C (see Figure S5, Supporting Information). In this temperature range, the emission in terms of spectral features and intensity reveals a full reversal process upon achieving a heating–cooling cycle, the result of which is in good correlation with the reversible water adsorption/desorption process observed in TG measurements (see above). Upon further heating from 86 to 200 °C, which corresponds to the second stage of removal of outer‐channel water molecules, the emission peak wavelength then further shifts to 550 nm with an increase of intensity to ≈100‐fold (cf. RT, Figures 7 and 8). At this stage, the emission could no longer be traced back reversibly upon cooling. The results are also consistent with those of the TG and PXRD analyses (see above).

The correlation between water content and luminescence properties reaffirms the emission to be associated with water contents that harness the crystal packing. We thus carefully examined any possible and subtle changes of intermolecular distances between ligand chromophores during the temperature‐dependent measurements. Figure 8 reveals a closer look at interrelated channels on the 3D ZndB MOF at RT and 120 °C. Two locations regarding salient changes of ligand interactions can be pinpointed. First of all, there seems to be a sandwich‐type, that is, dpe(pyridyl)–bdc(benzene)–dpe(pyridyl), π–π interaction, which has the ring centroid distance of 3.69 Å at room temperature. Second, there is a π–π interaction of two dpe moieties in between two adjacent channels, with a ring centroid distance of 3.97 Å at room temperature. Removing two inner‐channel water molecules at 120 °C causes the centroid distance of 3.69 Å to be elongated to 3.82 Å. Conversely, the interchannel distance between two nearest dpe moieties is only slightly shortened from 3.97(1) to 3.94(1) Å. Moreover, it has been reported that the adjacent pyridyl rings in dpe with a distance between the ring centroids of 5.561(2) Å should have a decent intermolecular π–π stacking interaction.28g Thus, the distance of 3.97 Å between the adjacent pyridyl rings in ZndB implies even greater interaction, which leads to the increase of excimer formation and hence further bathochromic‐shifted emission. The broad, redshifted, and long‐lived (see Figure S4, Supporting Information, and below) spectral feature is indicative of an excimer emission.28h This, together with the temperature‐dependent PXRD analyses of ZndB and the weak dpe excimer‐like emission reported in a dpe single crystal,28f,28g leads us to propose that the emission in the water‐saturated ZndB crystal more plausibly originates from types of dpe(π*)–dpe(π) excimer‐like interaction between two proximal interchain dpe moieties. The emission spectrum is then gradually redshifted due to the closer proximity, that is, enhancement of the π stacking, between nearby interchain dpe moieties upon removal of water. Moreover, this should be accompanied by an increase of emission intensity due to the closer packing and hence the environmental rigidity that is greatly amplified in the ZndB 3D MOF. An additional enhancement of emission intensity, although pending resolution, may in part be due to an increase of the exciton–phonon coupling at high temperature.28

Although no single crystal can be obtained at the second stage (>200 °C) upon removing the outer‐channel water, as indicated by shrinkage of the crystal axes in PXRD analyses, further closer packing and hence spectral redshift as well as increase of emission intensity are expected. Nevertheless, from another point of view, one may suspect that the increase of emission intensity upon the removal of water may, in part, be due to the diminishing of water quenching. In other words, the existence of water molecules may play a role to increase the nonradiative deactivation and hence quench the emission. Careful analyses of the ZndB single crystal at both RT and 120 °C, however, indicate that water molecules are far away from the designated dpe moiety by an average of >4.12 and >5.87 Å for inner‐ and outer‐channel water, respectively. Thus, the role of water molecules in quenching the emission should be discarded. Also, nonpolar solvent, for example hexane, or less polar solvent such as ethanol (cf. water), were added to the 3D MOF while the temperature was maintained at 100 °C. As a result, the emission intensity remained unchanged, thus implying that the solvent polarity plays a very minor role in the observed photophysical properties.

Supplementary support for the above viewpoints is provided by computational approaches. The large ZndB MOF makes the ab initio approach formidable. Alternatively, based on the crystal structure determined by X‐ray analyses at RT, we truncated the ZndB MOF into a tetramer configuration such that the interaction remained intact under a 3D‐like configuration. To gain more insight into the detail of the electronic transition, time‐dependent DFT calculations with the B3LYP functional and 6‐31G(d)/LANL2DZ basis set29 based on this truncated tetrameric crystal structure were then performed. In this approach, the first 15 lower‐lying singlet states were calculated. Figure 9 depicts the molecular frontier orbitals involved in the lowest‐lying, that is, S0→S1, transition. The calculation wavelength (≈355 nm) of the first transition approaches the absorbance onset of the experimental results. Evidently, the transition is mainly imparted by the interligand charge transfer from the nonbonding orbital mixed with π bonding of the carbonyl group of bdc2− (nonbonding n mixed with π, HOMO) to the pyridine part of dpe (π*, LUMO+1, and LUMO+3). Due to the short dpe–dpe distance, it is then plausible that the π* orbital of dpe then interacts with respect to the dpe moiety of the proximal channel (see Figure 8) through the pre‐existing dpe–dpe π stacking (see above), which results in an excimer‐like emission. Having closer packing, through the shortening in dpe–dpe distance upon waver removal, the energy of excimer formation should be stabilized, thereby resulting in a bathochromic shift and intensity increase of the emission.

Conclusion

Several results from this study can be summarized. 1) A 3D MOF of [Zn(dpe)(bdc)]⋅4H2O (ZndB) has been synthesized, which is constructed by the assembly of helical channels fully filled with guest water molecules in both inner and outer regions of the channel. 2) This 3D ZndB MOF exhibits an interesting guest structure, an all‐hydrogen‐bonded (H2O)16 2D cooperative assembly, in which the inner (outer) water adsorption/desorption is reversible (irreversible). 3) The spongelike hydration behavior is accompanied by remarkable luminescence changes, which achieve nearly white‐light generation and ≈100‐fold increase in intensity upon water depletion. The emission is ascribed to an interchain dpe(π*)–dpe(π) excimer‐like transition. These results exemplify a model in which the structural constraints acting on the orientation of water by its surroundings and vice versa can be very significant. Future extension of this type of infinite coordination polymer should be versatile, among which studies of the dynamics of water storage and an on (dry)/off (hydrated) luminescent water sensor are possible. We thus anticipate that the results should spark a broad spectrum of interest from both fundamental and application aspects.

Synthesis of [Zn(dpe)(bdc)]⋅4H2O (ZndB): A mixture of dpe (0.0184 g, 0.1 mmol), Zn(ClO4)⋅6H2O (0.0372 g, 0.1 mmol), Na2bdc (0.0210 g, 0.1 mmol), and deionized water (6 mL) was sealed in a Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), which was heated at 120 °C for 72 h, then cooled slowly to room temperature. Colorless crystals of ZndB were obtained after standing for several days. Yield: 0.0253 g (52.23 %); IR (KBr pellet): =3376(m), 3369(w), 1620(s), 1591(vs), 1431(s), 1228(m), 749(s) cm−1; elemental analysis calcd (%) for C20H24N2O8Zn (Mr=485.78): C 49.35, H 5.28, N 6.39; found: C 49.54, H 5.27, N 6.42.

Crystallographic data collection and refinement of ZndB: Single‐crystal structure analysis was performed on a Siemens SMART diffractometer incorporating a charge‐coupled device (CCD) detector with Mo radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) at room temperature. A preliminary orientation matrix and unit cell parameters were determined from three runs of 15 frames each, and each frame corresponded to a 0.3° scan in 10 s, followed by spot integration and least‐squares refinement. Data collections were measured by using ω scans of 0.3° per frame for 20 s until a complete hemisphere had been collected. Cell parameters were retrieved with SMART30 software and refined with SAINT31 on all observed reflections. Data reduction was performed with the SAINT31 software and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. Absorption corrections were applied with the program SADABS.32 Direct phase determination and subsequent difference Fourier map synthesis yielded the positions of all non‐hydrogen atoms, which were subject to anisotropic refinements. All hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically (CH=0.93) with the exception of the hydrogen atoms of the crystallized water molecules, which were located in the difference Fourier map with the corresponding positions and isotropic displacement parameters being refined. The final full‐matrix and least‐squares refinements on F2 were applied for all observed reflections [I>2σ(I)]. All calculations were performed with the SHELXTL‐PC V 5.03 software package.33 Crystallographic data, details of data collection, and structure refinements of compound ZndB are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).

CCDC‐722441 http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/cgi‐bin/catreq.cgi (ZndB) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.

Thermogravimetric analysis of ZndB: TG analysis of ZndB was performed on a computer‐controlled Perkin–Elmer 7 Series/UNIX TGA7 analyzer. Single‐phase powder samples of ZndB (1.530 mg) were loaded into alumina pans and heated with a ramp rate of 3 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C under a nitrogen atmosphere.

In situ X‐ray powder diffraction of ZndB: The PXRD pattern of ZndB was recorded at the BL01C2 beamline of the National Synchrotron Radiation Research Center (NSRRC) in Taiwan. The ring of the NSRRC was operated at an energy of 1.5 GeV with typical current 300 mA with top‐up injection mode. The wavelength of the incident X‐rays was 1.0332 Å (12.0 keV), delivered from the superconducting wavelength‐shifting magnet and a Si(111) double‐crystal monochromator. The diffraction pattern was recorded with a Mar345 imaging‐plate detector approximately 300 mm from the sample and typical exposure duration of 5 min. The pixel size of Mar345 was 100 μm. The resolution was about 0.02° in 2θ. The 1D powder diffraction profile was converted with the program FIT2D and cake‐type integration. The diffraction angles were calibrated according to the Bragg positions of Ag‐Behenate and Si powder (NBS640b) standards. PXRD for ZndB in situ was performed at BL01C2 of the NSRRC from 25 to 650 °C with a heating rate of approximately 4 °C min−1. The powder sample was sealed in a quartz capillary (1.0 mm) and heated with a stream of hot air; each PXRD pattern in situ was exposed for about 3 min. The crystal structures of ZndB at room temperature and 120 °C were refined by the Rietveld Method of the GSAS program (see Figure S1, Supporting Information). The complex was refined together as a rigid body and water molecules were refined in both positions and occupancies but their thermal parameters were kept at reasonable values to avoid high correlation between thermal parameters and occupancies. The synchrotron powder diffraction patterns were detected by imaging plate (Mar345). There was no slit or fly path between sample and detector. Air scattering and amorphous scattering contribute to high background on the imaging plate, which reduces the R factor according to the formula of Rp and wRp as well as goodness of fit (GOF). Number of total reflections 1623, 18 structural parameters, RBragg=0.0715 for 120 °C data.

Spectral measurement of ZndB: UV/Vis diffuse reflectance spectra of ZndB crystals were obtained with a Hitachi U‐3900H spectrophotometer equipped with an integrating‐sphere accessory (Al2O3 was used as a reference). To investigate the differences in photoluminescence of ZndB at different temperatures due to the variation of topologies, ZndB was measured with the confocal mode of a MonoVista confocal Raman microscope system (Princeton Instruments/Acton) employing a Linkam THMS 600 hot stage (Linkam Scientific Instruments, United Kingdom) equipped with a video camera (QImage 5.0 RTV), in which the optics were specially made to allow both UV (up to 280 nm) excitation and emission. The microscope (Olympus BX51) had an upright‐style frame in which all optics were modified to allow the passage of deep‐UV to infrared light. Simultaneously, a video camera (QImage 5.0 RTV) was used to capture images. In this approach, a 325 nm laser line (He–Cd laser, KIMMON IK3301R‐G) was used as an excitation source throughout the measurements. A set of neutral density filters was placed in the beam path to attenuate the laser power. The photoluminescence was separated from the scattering light of the excitation pulse by an edge filter with a cutoff wavelength of 325 nm (333AELP, Omega Optical). Subsequently, the luminescence was collected by an optical assembly to the entrance slit of a polychromator (blazed at 500 nm), which was coupled to a sensitive CCD detector (Princeton Instruments, PI‐MAX). The CCD was operated in shutter mode and the measurements were performed with 500 ms exposure time. All spectra were accumulated over an average of 20 scans. Typical‐lifetime studies were performed with an Edinburgh FL 920 photon‐counting system with a hydrogen‐filled or nitrogen lamp as the excitation source. Data were analyzed by using the nonlinear least‐squares procedure in combination with an iterative convolution method. The emission decays were analyzed by the sum of exponential functions, which allowed partial removal of the instrument time broadening and provided a temporal resolution of approximately 300 ps. The decay curve of the lifetime of ZndB at room temperature is available in the Supporting Information.
