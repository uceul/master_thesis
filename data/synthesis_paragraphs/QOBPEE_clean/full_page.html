Abstract

Four beryllium hydrogen phosphates, Be2(Hea)(PO4)(HPO4) (1), Him·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2a), Hpy·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2b), and Hma·Be1.5(HPO4)2·0.5H2O (3) in which ea = ethanolamine, im = imidazole, py = pyridine, and ma = methylamine, were synthesized under solvothermal conditions. All compounds have open‐framework structures with (3,4)‐connected networks. Compound 1 features a neutral inorganic–organic hybrid framework with an interrupted zeolitic GIS topology. The dual role of the amine molecules as charge‐balancing agents and coordinating agents to the beryllium atoms is noteworthy. Compounds 2a and 2b are isostructural, and they have an AFI‐related framework with elliptical 12‐ring channels. Compound 3 has a 3D structure with 4.82 layers pillared by corner‐sharing four‐ring chains. It represents the first example of an open‐framework beryllium hydrogen phosphate with 16‐ring channels.

Abstract

Solvothermal crystallization in the presence of different organic structure‐directing agents results in the formation of three kinds of beryllium hydrogen phosphate frameworks that have (3,4)‐connected networks with 10‐ring, 12‐ring, and 16‐ring channels, respectively.

Introduction

Open‐framework inorganic solids have been extensively studied owing to their potential applications in catalysis, ion exchange, and separation.1 Since the discovery of aluminophosphate molecular sieves in 1982, great efforts have been devoted to the synthesis of new open‐framework metal phosphates by replacing aluminum with other main‐group elements and transition metals.2 Beryllium is an ideal zeotype framework building element because of its tetrahedral coordination geometry and because it has properties similar to those of aluminum according to the diagonal relationship.3 The ultralight nature of beryllium and the strong Be–O covalent bond indicate that Be‐based compounds may possess low‐density frameworks with enhanced thermal and chemical stability. Over the past years, a number of artificial beryllium phosphates with 4‐connected zeolitic frameworks, such as ABW, ANA, CHA, FAU, GIS, GME, MER, and SOD, have been prepared and structurally characterized.4 The largest pore sizes of these compounds are delimited by 12 tetrahedra.

It has been demonstrated that the incorporation of 3‐connected oxoanions (e.g., HPO42– unit) into open‐framework networks favors for the formation of extra‐large pore structures such as cloverite and JDF‐20.5 By combining BeO4 tetrahedra with 3‐connected oxoanions, it is possible to create extra‐large pore apertures. An illustrative example is the (3,4)‐connected beryllium phosphite SCU‐24 containing 24‐ring channels with a very low density.6 In our efforts to explore new (3,4)‐connected frameworks in beryllium hydrogen phosphate systems, four open‐framework compounds, Be2(Hea)(PO4)(HPO4) (1), Him·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2a), Hpy·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2b), and Hma·Be1.5(HPO4)2·0.5H2O (3) in which ea = ethanolamine, im = imidazole, py = pyridine, and ma = methylamine, were prepared in the presence of different organic cations. Structural analyses revealed that these compounds have 3D structures and that they are constructed from strictly alternating Be‐centered tetrahedra and P‐centered tetrahedra. As far as we know, the presence of Be–amine linkages in 1 and the large 16‐ring channels in 3 are observed for the first time in beryllium hydrogen phosphate chemistry. Herein, we report their syntheses, characterization, and crystal structures.

Results and Discussion

Colorless crystals of 1 were obtained by heating a mixture of BeSO4·4H2O, H3PO4, ea, H2O, and di(ethylene glycol) at 180 °C for 6 d. The powder X‐ray diffraction pattern of the as‐synthesized compound was in good agreement with the simulated one on the basis of single‐crystal data, which was indicative of the phase purity of the as‐synthesized compound. The difference in the reflection intensities between the simulated and experimental patterns was due to variations in the crystal orientation for the powder sample. Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis revealed that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/c (no. 14) space group. There are two unique Be atoms and two unique P atoms in the asymmetric unit, and all of them are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms. The Be–OP bond lengths are in the 1.597(4)–1.646(4) Å region, and the Be–Oea bond length is 1.696(4) Å. The P–OBe bond lengths are in the 1.509(2)–1.538(2) Å range, and the P–OH bond length is 1.581(2) Å. The compound has a 3D structure, constructed from strictly alternating Be‐centered tetrahedra and P‐centered tetrahedra, as shown in Figure 1 (a).

Compound 1 has a (3,4)‐connected framework containing chainlike building blocks, as shown in Figure 1 (b). If the adjacent 3‐connected Be atoms and P atoms in the chainlike SBUs are connected with each other (Figure 1, c), double crankshaft‐like chains will form, and the (3,4)‐connected framework of 1 can be converted into a 4‐connected gismondine (zeolite code GIS) topology. Viewed along the [100] and [001] directions, compound 1 exhibits 8‐ring and 10‐ring channels, respectively. It should be noted that the largest pore size in the GIS topology is the 8‐membered ring. The occurrence of irregular 10‐ring pore in 1 is due to the interruption of the Be–O–P linkage in the GIS network. The framework density (FD, measured by the number of tetrahedra per 1000 Å3) is 19.4 for 1, which is comparable to the value of 19.2 for the beryllium phosphate with zeolitic GIS topology.7

A striking structural feature of 1 is the presence of Be–ea connections. Prior to this work, the organic cations were found to be located in the free voids of the beryllium phosphate framework. They interact with the inorganic framework through hydrogen‐bonding and electrostatic interactions. The dual role of ea molecule as a charge‐balancing agent and as a coordinating agent to the beryllium atoms in 1 is noteworthy. This compound is a rare example of a beryllium hydrogen phosphate with an inorganic–organic hybrid framework.

Compound 1 is unusual in that the Be/P ratio is 1. It was demonstrated that a (3,4)‐connected framework with alternating 3‐connected and 4‐connected nodes always has the composition of A3B4 (A = 3‐connected nodes, B = 4‐connected nodes).8 For those beryllium phosphites with (3,4)‐connected frameworks, the beryllium atoms act as 4‐connected nodes and the phosphorus atoms serve as 3‐connected nodes. As a result, they have a Be/P ratio of 3:4.9 In the case of 1, the Be/P ratio deviates from 3:4 because both the beryllium atoms and the phosphorus atoms can act as 3‐connected and 4‐connected nodes.

By using cyclic structure‐directing agents (i.e., im and py) under similar solvothermal conditions, colorless crystals of 2a and 2b were obtained. The phase purity of the two compounds was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction. Notably, the two compounds are isostructural to the known open‐framework berylloarsenate BAF1011.10 The structure has a (3,4)‐connected network related to zeolitic AFI topology. Viewed along the [010] direction, the structure displays elliptical 12‐ring channels, as shown in Figure 2. The diameter of the 12‐ring window is about 8.2 × 13.9 Å2, which was calculated from the distance between two oxygen atoms across the window. The monoprotonated organic cations orderly reside in the 12‐ring channels, which occupy approximately 33 % of the unit cell volume (calculated by using the PLATON program).11

The ionic size of P5+ (0.17 Å) is smaller than that of As5+ (0.335 Å).12 As a result, 2b exhibits a smaller pore size and a smaller unit cell volume than BAF1011. The FD value of 2b is 18.2, which is a little larger than the value of 17.3 for BAF1011. However, the density of 2b (2.072 g cm–3) is only 80 % of that of BAF1011 (2.593 g cm–3), and this demonstrates the advantage of light elements in the synthesis of low‐density frameworks.

Colorless crystals of 3 were obtained as a pure phase by using ma as the structure‐directing agent. It crystallizes in the orthorhombic Pccn (no. 56) space group. The asymmetric unit contains two unique Be atoms and two unique P atoms, which are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms. The Be–O bond lengths are in the 1.612(4)–1.630(4) Å range, and the P–OBe bond lengths are between 1.506(2) and 1.525(2) Å. The longer P–OH bond lengths are in the range from 1.568(2) to 1.585(2) Å.

The compound has a 3D inorganic framework that is constructed from two distinct building blocks, a 4.82 layered network and a corner‐sharing 4‐ring chain, as shown in Figure 3. The 4.82 layers are parallel to the ac plane, which are further pillared by corner‐sharing 4‐ring chains to form a (3,4)‐connected framework with 16‐ring channels. The P–OH groups point towards the center of the 16‐ring window and reduce the free void of 3.

The monoprotonated ma cations are ordered in the structure and occupy the corner positions of the 16‐ring windows. They interact with the host framework through extensive hydrogen bonding, for which the shortest N···O distances are in the range from 2.967(2) to 3.164(2) Å. Besides organic cations, water molecules are located in the centers of the 16‐ring windows. A void space analysis performed by using the PLATON program indicated that these extra‐framework species occupy 24.0 % of the unit cell volume.

Thermogravimetric analysis was performed in a flow of N2 to study the thermal stabilities of the as‐synthesized compounds. The structures of 1, 2a, and 2b remained stable up to 310, 200, and 230 °C, respectively. Upon further heating, their frameworks started to decompose. For 3, a weight loss of 3.60 % was observed in the 90–220 °C temperature region, which was due to the departure of water molecules occluded in the 16‐ring channels (expected: 3.65 %). The weight loss of 22.01 % between 260 and 600 °C was caused by the decomposition of the organic cations and the dehydration process involving the HPO4 groups.

The fluorescent spectra of the as‐synthesized compounds were measured in the solid state at room temperature. For 1, no detectable luminescence was observed in the measured region under the experimental conditions. Upon excitation at 299 (for 2a), 328 (for 2b), and 294 nm (for 3), a strong luminescence with a peak maximum at 370 (for 2a), 377 (for 2b), and 368 nm (for 3) was observed (Figure 4). Compared to bimetallic phosphates with emission in the 402–436 nm region, these emission bands are dramatically blueshifted.13

Conclusions

Four new open‐framework beryllium hydrogen phosphates were synthesized under solvothermal conditions in the presence of different organic structure‐directing agents. These compounds display three kinds of (3,4)‐connected structures with 10‐ring, 12‐ring, and 16‐ring channels. This work takes a step towards the synthesis of new beryllium hydrogen phosphate derivatives with extra‐large pores. The replacement of methylamine with longer monoamines of the formula CH3(CH2)nNH2 (n ≥ 1) under similar synthetic conditions may favor for the formation of extra‐large pores. Further work on this subject is in progress.

Experimental Section

General Methods: Powder X‐ray diffraction data were obtained by using a Rigaku D/MAX‐rA diffractometer with Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). CHN analyses were performed with a Euro EA3000 analyzer. IR spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded with a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer. The thermogravimetric analyses were performed by using a Netzsch STA 449c analyzer in a flow of N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min–1. The fluorescent spectra were measured with a PerkinElmer LS 55 luminescence spectrometer equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp.

Syntheses and Initial Characterization

Be2(Hea)(PO4)(HPO4) (1): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.089 g), H3PO4 (0.129 g, 85 wt.‐%), ethanolamine (0.061 g), H2O (2.0 mL), and di(ethylene glycol) (4.0 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon‐lined steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 6 d. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals of 1 were recovered by filtration, which were washed with distilled water and dried in air (44.3 % yield based on beryllium). C2H9Be2NO9P2 (271.07): calcd. C 8.86, H 3.35, N 5.17; found C 8.75, H 3.28, N 5.12. IR (KBr pellets): = 3170 (s), 1620 (m), 1490 (m), 1400(m), 1130 (vs), 1050 (vs), 777 (s), 671 (s), 528 (s) cm–1.

Him·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2a): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.089 g), H3PO4 (0.115 g, 85 wt.‐%), imidazole (0.034 g), water (0.9 mL), and ethylene glycol (4.0 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon‐lined steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 8 d. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals of 2a were recovered by filtration, which were washed with distilled water and dried in air (89.8 % yield based on beryllium). C3H9Be3N2O13P3 (401.07): calcd. C 8.98, H 2.26, N 6.98; found C 9.05, H 2.21, N 6.87. IR (KBr pellets): = 3150 (s), 2990 (w), 2870 (w), 1640 (m), 1390 (m), 1140 (vs), 723 (s), 490 (s) cm–1.

Hpy·Be3(OH)(HPO4)3 (2b): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.089 g), H3PO4 (0.144 g, 85 wt.‐%), pyridine (0.101 g), H2O (1.0 mL), and isopropyl alcohol (4.0 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon‐lined steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 6 d. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals of 2b were recovered by filtration, which were washed with distilled water and dried in air (82.0 % yield based on beryllium). C5H10Be3NO13P3 (412.09): calcd. C 14.57, H 2.45, N 3.40; found C 14.36, H 2.37, N 3.46. IR (KBr pellets): = 3450 (m), 3110 (m), 2890 (w), 1640 (m), 1490 (m), 1140 (vs), 754 (s), 679 (s), 492 (s) cm–1.

Hma·Be1.5(HPO4)2·0.5H2O (3): A mixture of BeSO4·4H2O (0.089 g), H3PO4 (0.144 g, 85 wt.‐%), methylamine (0.105 mL, 40 wt.‐%), H2O (1.6 mL), and isopropyl alcohol (4.0 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon‐lined steel autoclave and heated at 180 °C for 9 d. After cooling to room temperature, colorless crystals of 3 were recovered by filtration, which were washed with distilled water and dried in air (98.8 % yield based on beryllium). CH9Be1.5NO8.5P2 (246.55): calcd. C 4.87, H 3.68, N 5.68; found C 4.73, H 3.55, N 5.54. IR (KBr pellets): = 3480 (m), 3260 (m), 1620 (m), 1520 (m), 1090 (vs), 955 (s), 721 (s), 528 (s) cm–1.

The final wR2 (all data) = 0.0752, final R1 = 0.0304. Crystal data for 2a: C3H9Be3N2O13P3: M = 401.6, monoclinic, space group C2/c (no. 15), a = 25.0986(9) Å, b = 4.8666(2), c = 24.0359(9) Å, β = 117.200(5)°, V = 2611.20(17) Å3, Z = 8, Dcalcd. = 2.040 g cm–3, μ = 0.534 mm–1, 5251 reflections measured, 2297 unique (Rint = 0.0293). The final wR2 (all data) = 0.1129, final R1 = 0.0421.
