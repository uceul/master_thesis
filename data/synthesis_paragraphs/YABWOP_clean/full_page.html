Inorganica Chimica Acta

Volume 378, Issue 1, 30 November 2011, Pages 250-256

Hydrothermal syntheses and structures of vanadium oxyfluorides templated by organic and metal organic cations

Author links open overlay panelTiffany M.SmithaMichaelTichenoraJose M.VargasbCharles J.O’ConnorbJonZubietaa
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ica.2011.09.001Get rights and content

Abstract

The hydrothermal reactions of V2O5, HF and an organodiphosphonic acid, in the presence of appropriate templating organoammonium or metal–organic complex cations provided three new oxyfluorovanadate compounds. The V(IV) species [H3N(CH2)2NH2(CH2)2NH2(CH2)2NH3][V3O3F2(H2O){O3PCH2PO3}2]·2H2O (1·2H2O) exhibits a three-dimensional anionic framework constructed from {VO(O3PCH2PO3)}n2n− chains and {VF2O4} octahedra. The molecular structure of [N(CH2CH2NH3)3]2[NH4][V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2]·2H2O (2·2H2O) is characterized by the presence of unique {V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2}7− clusters. The bimetallic phase [{Cu(ophen)}VOF{HO3P(CH2)5PO3}] (3) is one-dimensional with {Cu2V2O2F2(HO3PR)2(O3PR)2} cluster building blocks.

1. Introduction

The ubiquity of inorganic oxides, together with their vast compositional range and structural versatility [1], have generated widespread interest in their rational design and applications [2]. Hydrothermal reaction conditions provide a practical method for the preparation of inorganic oxide materials, as well as organic–inorganic hybrid composites, where the inorganic substructure is modified by the introduction of organic components as charge-compensating units and/or as conventional ligands [3], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Since acidic conditions are often required for the preparation of such metal oxide-based hybrids, hydrofluoric acid has been conventionally used to effect mineralization and to induce crystallization, although fluoride is not incorporated into the products [12]. However, judicious modification of reaction conditions allows incorporation of fluoride to produce oxyfluorinated materials, a phenomenon initially observed for the aluminum and gallium phosphates and demonstrated to provide even more varied coordination types for this class of materials [13], [14]. Fluoride rich hydrothermal methods have also been exploited in the preparation of a variety of transition metal fluorophosphates [15], and fluoride incorporation into oxovanadium and oxomolybdenum organophosphates has been shown to result in unusual structural complexity [16], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22], [23]. For example, the family of oxyfluorovanadates of the general type {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+/VxOyFzn−/{O3P(CH2)nPO3}4− exhibits a range of V/P/O/F building blocks such as embedded clusters, chains and networks and even three-dimensional frameworks.

Upon incorporation into a metal oxide framework, fluoride effectively substitutes for oxide in the metal coordination sphere, resulting in changes in both the charge of the M-oxyfluoride subunit and the composition of the material. The structural consequences are most apparent in the different bridging modes adopted by the fluoride and the modified linkage requirements of [MOxFy] polyhedra in comparison to [MOx+y] polyhedra of the parent material. In the specific case of vanadium oxyfluorides, we have noted a pronounced tendency for the products to incorporate reduced metal sites, that is, V(IV) and even V(III) in preference to V(V). This latter observation may reflect the electron-withdrawing character of the fluoride anion stabilizing the lower formal oxidation state.

Metal oxyfluorides also exhibit useful physical properties such as magnetism [24], catalysis [25] and non-linear optical behavior [26]. Detailed studies of the vanadium fluorides and oxyfluorides by Lightfoot demonstrate a complex structural chemistry, including oligomeric, chain and ladder building blocks [27], [28], [29], [30], [31], [32], [33], [34]. However, as noted by us and others [35], [36], [37], the complexity of the hydrothermal reaction domain can provide unusual, unprecedented and even inconceivable structures, suggesting that many metal oxyfluoride structures remain to be discovered. Factors such as variations in temperature, pH, reactant stoichiometry, the presence or absence of organic cations, and even the fill volume have been shown to influence the product outcome.

In the course of our own investigations of the hydrothermal chemistry of vanadium oxides in the presence of HF, a number of novel vanadium oxyfluoride phases have been discovered. In this contribution, we report the structural chemistry of a number of vanadium oxyfluoride materials with organophosphonate ligands and different charge-compensating cations: the three-dimensional (H4teta)[V3O3F2(H2O)(O3PCH2PO3)2]·2H2O (1·2H2O), the molecular anionic cluster of (NH4)(H3ntea)2[V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2]·2H2O (2·2H2O), and the one-dimensional, bimetallic phase [{Cu(o-phen)}VOF {HO3P(CH2)5PO3}] (3), where ntea = nitrilotris(ethylamine), teta = trisethylenetetramine, and o-phen = 1,10-phenanthroline.

2. Experimental

2.1. General considerations

All chemicals were used as obtained without further purification with the exception of 1,5-pentylene diphosphonic acid which was synthesized by the literature method [38]. Vanadium(V) oxide (99%), copper(II) acetate monohydrate (98%), triethylenetetramine (97%), 1,10-phenanthroline (99%) and hydrofluoric acid (48 wt.% in H2O) were all purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Methylene diphosphonic acid (97%) and ethylene glycol (99%) were purchased from Alfa Aesar. Tris(2-aminoethyl)amine (98%) was purchased from Pressure Chemical Company. All syntheses were carried out in 23-mL poly(tetrafluoroethylene)-lined stainless steel containers under autogeneous pressure. The pH of the solutions were measured prior to and after heating using pHydrion vivid 1-11® pH paper. Water was distilled above 3.0 MΩ in-house using a Barnstead Model 525 Biopure Distilled Water Center.

2.5. X-ray crystallography

Structural measurements for 1–3 were performed on a Bruker-AXS SMART-CCD diffractometer at a low temperature (90 K) using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ[Mo Kα] = 0.71073 Å) [39]. The data were corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects and absorption using sadabs [40]. The structures were solved by direct methods. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. After all of the non-hydrogen atoms were located, the models were refined against F2, initially using isotropic and later anisotropic thermal displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms were introduced in calculated positions and refined isotropically. Neutral atom scattering coefficients and anomalous dispersion corrections were taken from the International Tables, volume C. All calculations were performed using shelxtl crystallographic software packages [41].

Compound 1 was refined in the acentric space group C2. Attempts to refine in the alternative acentric space group Cm were unsuccessful. Refinement in the centrosymmetric space group C2/m produced unacceptably large residuals, indicating that the choice of the acentric space group is correct. The vanadium sites of the {VO(O3PCH2PO3)}n2n− chains (V3 and V4) are disordered about the basal plane of organophosphonate oxygen donors, along the V–Oterminal bond axes with relative populations of ca. 0.9 and 0.1 for the Vn and VnA sites (n = 3, 4), respectively.

Compound 2 exhibited disordered ammonium cation and water of crystallization sites. The consequence of these partially occupied sites is to produce a number of apparent close contacts.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses and infrared spectroscopy

The compounds were synthesized using conventional hydrothermal/solvothermal methods. Hydrothermal synthesis, in combination with the use of organic ligands and cations as structure-directing components, has been extensively explored for the preparation of organic–inorganic hybrid materials [42], [8], [43], [44], [45], [46], [47], [48]. While HF is generally introduced into hydrothermal reactions as a mineralizer to promote solubility and crystal growth and not as a component of the final product, fluorinated products may be prepared through the simple expedient of increasing the concentration of HF in the reaction medium. In this study, fluoride to metal ratios of ca. 6:1–20:1 were required for fluoride uptake into the products.

All structures exhibit an IR band between 965 and 983 cm−1 attributed to ν(VO) for the terminal vanadium oxo-groups. For compound 1, which contains water of crystallization, there is a corresponding band at 3091 cm−1, that is absent in compounds 2 and 3 which do not contain water molecules of crystallization. Compound 2 has IR bands at ca. 3300 cm−1 attributed to ν(N–H) of the ammonium countercation. Compounds 1–3 exhibit common features in the 1000–1200 cm−1 range, assigned to the phosphonate group [49].

3.2. Crystal structures

As shown in Fig. 1, the structure of (H4teta)[V3O3F2(H2O)(O3PCH2PO3)2]·2H2O (1·2H2O) consists of an anionic {V3O3F2(H2O)(O3PCH2PO3)2}n4n− three-dimensional framework, with the (H4teta)4+ cations occupying the void cavities of the framework. The oxofluorovanadium–phosphonate substructure is constructed from {V2O4(O3PCH2PO3)}n2n− chains running alternately along the [−1 −1 0] and [−1 1 0] directions, linked through {VO(H2O)F2(Ophosphonate)2} polyhedra. The chain substructure, shown in Fig. 1(b), consists of V(IV) square pyramids linked through η4,μ2-phosphonate ligands. The basal plane of the {VO5} polyhedra is defined by four phosphonate oxygen donors, two from each of two methylene diphosphonate ligands, while the apical position is occupied by the terminal oxo-group. The direction of the V–O(oxo) vector alternates along the chain axis. Each methylene diphosphonate ligand bridges two vanadium sites and forms two six-membered chelate rings {V–O–P–C–P–O–}, a common motif in the oxovanadium–methylene disphosphonate structural chemistry. One of the remaining P–O groups is used to link the chain to the bridging {VF2O4} units, while the other {PO3} terminus projects a pendant P–O group into an intraframework void. The P–O distance of 1.503(3) Å for this pendant group is consistent with deprotonated phosphonate oxygen atom. The ligand is thus present in the fully deprotonated form {O3PCH2PO3}4−, consistent with charge balance considerations.

As noted previously, the chains are linked through {VF2O4} distorted octahedra. These V(IV) sites exhibit a terminal oxo-group with a short VO distance of 1.605(4) Å, an aqua ligand trans to the oxo-group, two trans-fluoride ligands, and phosphonate oxygen donors from two adjacent oxofluorovanadium–phosphonate chains.

The (H4teta)4+ cations and water molecules of crystallization occupy intraframework cavities generated by the corner-sharing of vanadium and phosphorus polyhedra to produce twenty polyhedral rings of the type {V–O–P–O–}10, each constructed from four {VF2O4} and six {VO5} polyhedra, linked through {CPO3} tetrahedra of ten methylene diphosphonate groups. The dimensions of the cavities are approximately 6.0 × 23.0 Å with two constrictions of 3.0 Å in width resulting from the projection of the pendant {PO}group and the aqua ligand of the {VF2O4} polyhedra into the cavity.

When the trisethylene(tetraamine) (teta) reactant for the preparation of 1 is replaced by the isomeric nitrilotris(ethyleneamine) (ntea), the molecular species (NH4)(H3ntea)2[V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2]·2H2O (2·2H2O) is obtained. The structure of 2 consists of discrete {V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2}7− molecular anions and charge-compensating (NH4)+ and {N(CH2CH2NH3}3+ cations. As shown in Fig. 2, the vanadium oxyfluoro-cluster is a linear trinuclear array of corner-sharing vanadium octahedra, linked through bridging fluoride and methylene diphosphonate ligands. While the central vanadium site is in the +3 oxidation state, the terminal vanadium sites of the cluster are in the +4 oxidation state, as confirmed by valence sum calculations [50]. The V(III) center adopts the trans {VO4F2} geometry through bonding to two fluoride groups that bridge to the peripheral V(IV) sites and two phosphonate oxygen donors from each of two methylene diphosphonate ligands. The crystallographically equivalent V(IV) sites adopt facial {VO3F3} geometries, bonding to the bridging fluoride, two terminal fluoride ligands, a terminal oxo-group with a short V–O distance of 1.609(2) Å, and two oxygen donors from an organodiphosphonate ligand in a six-membered {V–O–P–C–P–O–} chelate ring.

Each methylene diphosphonate ligand bridges a terminal V(IV) site to the central V(III) site through two oxygen donors from each phosphorus terminus. Consequently, each methylene diphosphonate ligand projects one pendant, uncoordinated oxygen atom from each {PO3} terminus. The short P–O distances of 1.497(2) and 1.529(2) Å for the uncoordinated groups are consistent with deprotonated phosphonate oxygen atom. Pendant, protonated {P–OH} groups generally exhibit P–O distances in the order of 1.57–1.60 Å. The assignment of the ligand as the tetranegative {O3PCH2PO3}4− is consistent with charge balancing requirements and the assigned vanadium oxidation states. Furthermore, the final electron density maps in the crystal structure study failed to reveal residual electron density in a region consistent with protonation of these sites.

The final structure [{Cu(o-phen)}VOF{HO3P(CH2)5PO3}] (3) demonstrates the consequences of introducing a longer tether length into the diphosphonate ligand and of the presence of a complex cation component. As shown in Fig. 3, the structure of 3 is one-dimensional and consists of bimetallic clusters {Cu2(o-phen)2V2O2F2(O3PR)2(HO3PR)2} linked through the pentyl chains of the diphosphonate ligand.

The V(IV) sites exhibit distorted octahedral geometry with a terminal oxo-group, a bridging fluoride trans to the oxo-group, a second bridging fluoride cis to this first fluoride, and oxygen donors from three diphosphonate ligands. The two vanadium sites of each cluster form a binuclear unit edge-sharing through the two bridging fluoride atoms.

The fluoride ligands adopt the μ3-bridging mode through bonding to the vanadium sites and one copper site of the cluster. The Cu(II) sites exhibit {CuFO2N2} square pyramidal geometry, with the basal plane defined by the nitrogen donors of the o-phen ligand and two phosphonate oxygen donors and the apical position occupied by the fluoride.

Each diphosphonate ligand bridges two clusters, bonding to one copper and two vanadium sites at one {PO3} terminus and to a single vanadium and copper at the other terminus. Consequently, one oxygen at the latter terminus is pendant, and in this case, clearly protonated as evidenced by the {P–OH} distance of 1.583(4) Å and the appearance of a peak at a position consistent with protonation on the final electron density map.

3.3. Structural observations

While molecular vanadium oxyfluorides [51] and oxovandium organophosphonates [52] are now well-documented, the structural chemistry of molecular oxyfluorovanadium–organophosphonate compounds, {VxOyFz(O3PR)n}, remains relatively unexplored. Compound 2 is a unique example of a trinuclear cluster type, although the tetranuclear oxyfluorovanadates [NBu4n]2[V4O6F(O3PPh3)4] have been previously described [53].

On the other hand, the structural consequences of fluoride incorporation into pillared layer and three-dimensional oxyfluorovanadium–organophosphonate materials has been extensively investigated [23]. These latter materials are characterized by {VxOyFz(H2O)a(O3PR)b}nm− layers or {VxOyFz(H2O)a(O3PR)b}nm− three-dimensional frameworks, as noted in Table 3. In no case do they share structural building blocks with compound 1 of this study. This observation reflects in part the use of methylene diphosphonate in the design of 1, rather than the longer carbon tethers of the diphosphonates {O3P(CH2)nPO3}4− (n = 2–5) of the previous study. The ligands with tethers of two or greater possess the spatial extension to bridge to different metal or cluster sites. In contrast, methylene-diphosphonate is constrained by the short tether length to adopt chelation through oxygen donors at either phosphorus terminus as the preferred ligation mode. However, the {VO(O3PCH2PO3)}n2n− chain of 1 is identical to that observed in [H2N(CH2)4NH2][VO(O3PCH2PO3)] [54], suggesting that such chain structures may be persistent building blocks in the structural chemistry.

The structural chemistry of oxyfluorovanadium–organophosphonates with Cu(II)-ligand cationic components has also been investigated, although not in great detail. 3.4. Magnetism

The temperature dependent magnetic data for 1–3 were recorded at magnetic field of H = 1000 Oe in the temperature range of T = 2–300 K, in field cooling mode (FC) using a Quantum Design MPMS-XL-7 SQUID magnetometer. In all cases the dependence of the magnetic susceptibility on temperature followed the Curie–Weiss law, as shown for compound 1 in Fig. 4.

A temperature-independent-paramagnetism (TIP) was added to the equations to account for the baseline correction. The final expression used for the susceptibility was the following:χ=χh+TIP=Ng2μB2S(S+1)3kB[T-θ]+TIPwhich as a function of the effective magnetic moment takes the form:χ=χh+TIP=μeff28[T-θ]+TIP,where μeff2=g2S(S+1).

The best fit for compound 2 gives μeff = (3.34 ± 0.40), θ = (4.4 ± 0.5) K, and TIP = 0.0006 cm3/mol. The room temperature value of μeff of ca. 3.34 μβ/molecule is lower than the theoretical spin only value of 3.74 μβ/molecule for a system of two V(IV)-d1 and one V(III)-d2 sites in the absence of coupling. The θ value of 4.4 ± 0.5 K suggests the presence of weak antiferromagnetic coupling. The structure of 2 reveals a distinct canting of the central V(III) octahedron with respect to the peripheral V(IV) octahedral, suggesting that the overlap of the magnetic orbitals of the vanadium sites is poor.

The Curie–Weiss fit for compound 1 gives values of μeff = (3.01 ± 0.31), θ = (0.52 ± 0.05) K, and TIP = 0.0013 cm3/mol, while the theoretical spin only value for a system of three V(IV)-d1 sites in the absence of coupling is 3.00 μβ/molecule. For compound 3, the best fit gives μeff = 2.71 ± 0.31 μβ, θ = (3.22 ± 0.05) K, and TIP = −0.0008 cm3/mol. The theoretical spin only value for a system of one V(IV)-d1 and one Cu(II)-d9 sites in the absence of coupling is 2.45 μβ/molecule.

4. Conclusions

Hydrothermal methods have been used to prepare three novel vanadium oxyfluorides, incorporating diphosphonate coligands and organic or metal organic cationic templates. The complexity of the hydrothermal reaction domain is reflected in the structural variety observed for materials prepared in this fashion. In this case, molecular, 1-D and 3-D structures have been obtained. The molecular species [N(CH2CH2NH3)3]2[NH4][V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2] (2) exhibits an unprecedented trinuclear anion {V3O2F6(O3PCH2PO3)2}7−, suggesting that polyoxyfluorometalates (POFMs) may have an expansive structural chemistry. The 3-D structure of [H3N(CH2)2NH2(CH2)2NH2(CH2)2NH3][V3O3F2(H2O){O3PCH2PO3}2]·2H2O (1·2H2O) is also unique for the organically templated oxyfluorovanadium–phosphonates, reflecting the structural consequences of the large diammonium cation and of the spatially restrictive methylene-diphosphonate ligand. The 1-D structure of [{Cu(ophen)}VOF{HO3P(CH2)5PO3}] (3) is also unique, a not unexpected observation in view of the incorporation of {Cu(ophen)}2+ as a building block in place of {Cu2(bisterpy)}4+. The fundamental conclusion to be drawn from these results is that there remain huge numbers of oxyfluoro-phosphonate solids and molecular POFMs with unusual, unprecedented and inconceivable structures that remain to be discovered.
