Construction of structural diversity and fine-tuned porosity in acylamide MOFs by a synthetic approach†

Wan-Ting Yao , Ming-Biao Luo , Xue-Feng Feng *, Pan-Pan Meng , Le-Le Gong and Feng Luo *
School of Biology, Chemistry and Material Science, East China University of Technology, Nanchang, Jiangxi 344000, China. E-mail: xffeng@ecit.cn; ecitluofeng@163.com

Received (in Montpellier, France) 28th September 2015 , Accepted 30th November 2015

First published on 2nd December 2015

Four new metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), namely, Zn(L1)(AzDC)·(DMF)3 (1), Zn(L2) (AzDC)·(DMF)0.5(H2O) (2), Zn2(AzDC)2(L3)·(DMF)(H2O)0.5 (3), and Zn(AzDC)(L3)·(DMF) (4), (L1 = N1,N4-di(pyridin-4-yl)terephthalamide, L2 = N1,N4-di(pyridin-3-yl)terephthalamide, L3 = N4,N4′-di(pyridin-4-yl)-[1,1′-biphenyl]-4,4′-dicarboxamide, H2AzDC = 4,4′-(diazene-1,2-diyl)dibenzoic acid, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide) were prepared by the solvothermal reaction of Zn(NO3)2, H2AzDC, and acylamide ligands. Polymers 1–3 show the common layer-pillared pcu-type net with four- or two-fold interpenetration, while polymer 4 displays an unprecedented four-connected net with the 638210 topology symbol. Note that the difference in the pillar of L1 for 1 and L2 for 2, related to the position isomers, caused significant alterations, including a reduction in the interpenetration number from four to two, enhanced potential porosity from 47.1% to 66.6%, and a change in the pore configuration from a smaller triangle window to a larger quadrangle window. Furthermore, controlling the volume of the reaction solvent, such as 5 mL for 3 and 15 mL for 4, also resulted in significant alterations, including a change in the structural diversity from a four-fold interpenetrating pcu net to a two-fold interpenetrating 638210 net, enhanced potential porosity from 49.1% to 57.2%, and a change in the pore configuration from a smaller rectangle window to a larger quadrangle window. Most importantly, all these polymers display highly selective adsorption of CO2 over other gases, such as N2, CH4, CO, and O2, most likely due to the free standing acylamide groups the polymers contain, which afford unique affinity towards CO2.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has drawn a large amount of attention since the 1960s, owing to their diverse architectures and range of potential applications in many fields, such as luminescence, catalysis, gas absorption, separation, ion exchange and magnetism.1–3 However, the rational design and controlled synthesis of MOFs are still challenging topics, as their formations are based on the interplay of starting materials, pH, template, reaction temperature, solvent, reagent concentration, and metal-to-ligand ratio.4–7 There is no doubt that the rational synthesis of such materials is unpredictable, and further systematic studies where diversified conditions are controlled are important and of great interest. For example, many efforts have been made to control the interpenetration of identical nets to enhance porosity. Yaghi and co-workers have shown how interpenetration can be controlled by the high dilution of solution.8 By taking full advantage of template reagents, a so-called template effect has been developed by Kitagawa, Zhu, Lin and co-workers to control interpenetration and modulate porosity.9 To determine the dominating factor controlling the interpenetration of nets, a systematic investigation on synthetic conditions was explored by Zaworotko and co-workers, revealing that at least in their case, interpenetration was controlled by both temperature and concentration.10 Likewise, Xu and co-workers found that interpenetration and continuous porosity were controlled by both temperature and concentration among three isomers, where the isomer formed was governed by the reaction temperature and the concentration of the solution.11 Moreover, other elegant methods have also been developed for such purposes, such as the liquid-phase epitaxial growth reported by Shekhah and co-workers12 and the rational design of organic ligands as attested by Hupp and co-workers.13 In addition, cases such as solvent-induced supramolecular isomers, pH-caused supramolecular isomers, pH dependent crystallization of chirality, and temperature-controlled structural diversity have also been observed.14 In this work, we report some interesting results in this category, as well as the synthesis, structure, and characterization of four new MOFs in detail.

Experimental section

Synthesis

Synthesis of Zn(L1)(AzDC)·(DMF)3 (1). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2 (0.2 mmol), H2AzDC (0.2 mmol), and L1 (0.2 mmol) in DMF (5 mL) was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 115 °C for 3 days; the reaction system was then cooled to room temperature. Red crystals were obtained. Yield: 65% based on Zn. Elemental analysis (%): calc.: C 54.83, H 4.27, N 12.79. Found: C 54.64, H 4.57, N 12.58.
Zn(L2)(AzDC)·(DMF)0.5(H2O) (2). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2 (0.2 mmol), H2AzDC (0.2 mmol), and L2 (0.2 mmol) in DMF (5 mL) was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 115 °C for 3 days; the reaction system was then cooled to room temperature. Red crystals were obtained. Yield: 73% based on Zn. Elemental analysis (%): calc.: C 55.86, H 3.60, N 11.54. Found: C 53.55, H 3.42, N 11.37.
Zn2(AzDC)2(L3)·(DMF)(H2O)0.5 (3). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.2 mmol), H2AzDC (0.2 mmol) and L (0.2 mmol) in DMF (5 ml) was sealed in a Teflon reactor, heated at 115 °C for 3 days, and then cooled to room temperature at 3 °C h−1. Subsequently, red block crystals were obtained in 70% yield based on Zn. Element analysis (%) for 3: calc.: C 58.42, H 3.37, N 10.66; found: C 58.29, H 3.51, N 10.43.
Zn(AzDC)(L3)·(DMF) (4). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.2 mmol), H2AzDC (0.2 mmol) and L (0.2 mmol) in DMF (15 ml) in a ratio of 1:1:1 was sealed in a Teflon reactor, heated at 115 °C for 3 days, and then cooled to room temperature at 3 °C h−1. Subsequently, orange slice crystals were obtained in 50% yield based on Zn. Element analysis (%) for 4: calc.: C 62.65, H 3.77, N 11.31; found: C 62.45, H 3.54, N 11.13.

Materials and general methods

All chemicals and solvents used in the syntheses were of analytical grade and used without further purification. The acylamide ligands were synthesized according to a literature method. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a TGA Q500 thermal analysis system. All TGA experiments were performed under a N2 atmosphere from 40 to 800 °C at a rate of 5 °C min−1. Data were analyzed using the TA Universal Analysis software package. X-ray powder diffraction data were collected using a Bruker AXS D8 Discover powder diffractometer at 40 kV, 40 mA for Cu Kα, (λ = 1.5406 Å). The simulated powder patterns were calculated using Mercury 1.4. The purity of the bulk products was determined by comparison of the simulated and experimental PXRD patterns. The as-synthesized sample (weight of about 100 mg) was degassed in the sample tube and dried for 24 h at 200 °C prior to measurements. Ultrahigh-purity-grade (>99.999%) N2, CO, O2, CO2, and CH4 gases were used in the adsorption measurements. To maintain the experimental temperatures, liquid nitrogen (77 K) and a temperature-programmed water bath (273 and 293 K) were used respectively.

X-ray crystallography

Unit cell measurements and intensity data were collected at 113 K on a Bruker-AXS SMART Breeze CCD diffractometer using graphite monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The data reduction included a correction for Lorentz and polarization effects, with an applied multi-scan absorption correction (SADABS). The crystal structure was solved and refined using the SHELXTL program suite. Direct methods yielded all non-hydrogen atoms, which were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. All hydrogen atom positions were calculated geometrically and were riding on their respective atoms. The solvent molecules were treated with the Platon Squeeze program. The CCDC numbers are 1409106 and 1409107 for polymers 1 and 2, and 1414037 and 1414038 for polymers 3 and 4.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures

The coordination modes of the carboxylate ligands and the conformations of the acylamide ligands in polymers 1 to 4 are shown in Scheme 1.
Scheme 1 Schematic description of the coordination modes of AzDC2− carboxylate ligands and the conformations of acylamide ligands in polymers 1 to 4. The trans conformation in polymers 1, 3, and 4 means that the two CO units of the acylamide ligands are located on opposite sides, while the cis conformation in polymers 3 and 4 means that the two CO units of the acylamide ligands are located on the same side. As for the cis–cis conformation in polymer 2, each cis means that the pyridine nitrogen atom and the CO unit of acylamide are located on the same side.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c space group. The Zn(II) ions show a common paddle-wheel and a Zn2(O2C)4 subunit; coordination sites on the axis orientation are occupied by L1 nitrogen atoms. The Zn2(O2C)4 subunits through the carboxylate ligands (AzDC2−) generate a 2D layer, which is then further supported by long nitrogen ligands (L1). The span between two adjacent layers is close to 2 nm, while the size of the 44 layer is ca. 17.2 × 17.2 Å2, indicative of a large, potentially porous framework. However, this underwent further four-fold interpenetration, thus only affording a smaller, trigonal channel along the c axis and 47.1% solvent-accessible volume (Fig. 1d).15


Fig. 1 A structural comparison between 1 and 2 involving the (a) and (e) paddle-wheel, Zn2(O2C)4 subunit plus the pillars of the position isomers, (b) and (f) the smallest box, (c) and (g) the interpenetration of identical boxes, and (d) and (h) the pore configurations of the boxes with an aperture of 3.5 Å for 1 and 10.3 Å for 2. The face at which the arrowhead is pointing represents the contractive face in 2.

By contrast, when the L2 position isomer is used, although this also generates the paddle-wheel, the Zn2(O2C)4 subunit and finally the layer-pillared framework, several distinct characteristics are observed in the aspects of interpenetration, pore structure, and potential porosity. In contrast to polymer 1, the degree of interpenetration is effectively reduced, only giving two-fold interpenetration in polymer 2. Corresponding to this is a large quadrangular channel along the c axis and an enhanced solvent-accessible volume of 66.6% (Fig. 1h).15

To better understand this feature, we selected the smallest cuboid box to perform a careful analysis (Fig. 1b and f). The 44 grid of the layer in both 1 and 2 is constructed by Zn2(O2C)4 subunits and AzDC2− linkers (Fig. 1a and e), thus resulting in a window of ca. 17.2 × 17.2 Å2. However, slight differences were found in the aspects of distortion between the two phenyl groups of the AzDC2− ligand. In 1 this is not observed, whereas in 2, partial AzDC2− ligands undergo this distortion, giving a dihedral angle of ca. 25°. However, we do not think this factor could cause the significant changes in interpenetration, pore structure, and potential porosity between 1 and 2. We then focused on the pillars of the position isomers. Fig. 1 clearly depicts the differences in the configuration of the smallest box (Fig. 1b and f), where polymer 1 shows a regular box with a length of 2 nm (approximately equal to the length of the L1 ligand) and an angle of approximately 90°, whereas a slantwise box is observed in polymer 2, accompanied by a decrease in length (1.7 nm) and a significant incline with an angle of 56°. This directly causes one of the faces of this slantwise box to become cabined and less accessible. Therefore, we think this could be responsible for the decrease of the interpenetration number for polymer 2 when a position isomer is utilized as the pillar.

As shown in Fig. 2, the single X-ray crystal structure reveals that 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, and its asymmetric unit includes two crystallography-independent Zn(II) ions, two L3 ligands and four AzDC2− ligands. Each Zn(II) site is five-coordinated in a regular pyramidal geometry by four oxygen atoms (Zn1–O4/2.039(8), Zn1–O6/2.030(8), Zn1–O8/2.030(7), Zn1–O10/2.042(8), Zn2–O3/1.982(9), Zn2–O5/1.947(11), Zn2–O7/2.009(10), Zn2–O9/1.991(11) Å) from four AzDC2− ligands and one nitrogen atom (Zn1–N1/1.915(8), Zn2–N4/2.006(15) Å) from one L3 ligand. The AzDC2− ligand adopts the bi(bidentate) mode and takes the μ4:η1:η1:η1:η1 coordination mode, while the L3 ligand connects to two Zn(II) ions by its pyridyl nitrogen atom. Typically, binuclear Zn(II) ions are combined together by four carboxyl groups from four AzDC2− ligands, generating the common paddle-wheel {Zn2(O2C)4} unit. Within the ab plane, through the AzDC2− connectors, these {Zn2(O2C)4} units form a 2D layer. This is further extended by L3 ligands to generate an overall 3D layer-pillared net. Generally speaking, this kind of net could be simplified to a six-connected pcu net, based on the consideration of each {Zn2(O2C)4} unit acting as a six-connecting node. Note that regardless of four-fold interpenetration, a considerable solvent-accessible volume, equal to 49.1% of the cell volume, estimated by Platon, is observed in 3.15


Fig. 2 (a) The symmetrical unit of 3 and the coordination environment of Zn(II) within the unit. The hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity. Color code: Zn/blue-green, C/green, N/blue, O/red; (b) view of the 2D network of 3; (c) the porous framework of 3 with an aperture of 3.8 Å; (d) schematic description of the six-connected pcu net with four-fold interpenetration; (e) view of the interpenetrating mode in the four-fold interpenetrating net (each color represents one identical net).

The framework of 4 is very different from that of 3, and the asymmetric unit includes two crystallography-independent Zn(II) ions, three L3 ligands and four AzDC2− ligands. As shown in Fig. 3, both Zn1 and Zn2 are four-coordinated with distorted tetrahedral geometry finished by oxygen atoms (Zn1–O8/1.986(12), Zn1–O9/1.947(8), Zn2–O4/1.901(11), Zn2–O6/1.946(12) Å) from the AzDC2− ligands and nitrogen atoms (Zn1–N8/2.033(14), Zn1–N11/2.016(6), Zn2–N1/1.997(12), Zn2–N5/1.997(17) Å) from the L ligands. The AzDC2− ligand adopts the bi(monodentate) mode and takes the μ2:η1:η0:η1:η0 coordination mode. L3 ligands connect to Zn1 and Zn2, forming a 1D chain. Furthermore, the combination of 1D chains and AzDC2− ligands creates an overall 3D framework, allowing for two-fold interpenetration. However, a considerable solvent-accessible void is observed as estimated by Platon, giving 57.2% of the cell volume.15 Topology analysis using the Topos40 program gives an unprecedented 4-connected topology net with the 638210 symbol.16


Fig. 3 (a) The symmetrical unit of 4 and the coordination surrounding the Zn(II) ion. Color code: Zn/blue-green, C/green, N/blue, O/red. (b) View of the 1D chain structure; (c) the porous framework in 4, with an aperture of 6.4 Å; (d) the 4-connected 638210 topology net with two-fold interpenetration.

Thermostability and gas adsorption

The thermostability of the compounds was explored by TG studies at 30 to 800 °C (Fig. 4). For 1, the first weight loss before 200 °C is 18.2%, equal to three DMF molecules (18.6%), whereas for 2, the first weight loss should be the removal of half a molecule of DMF plus one water molecule (exp. 10.0%, calc. 10.6%) before 300 °C. Sample 3 shows the first weight loss of 5.3% (calc. 5.3%), observed in the temperature range of 30–250 °C, due to the removal of guest molecules: one DMF molecule and half a molecule of H2O. Sample 4 loses 10.0% (calc. 10.2%) at the first step from 30 to 250 °C, which is equivalent to four DMF molecules.
Fig. 4 The TG plots of polymers 1 to 4 for the as-synthesized samples and after activation at 150 °C.

Before carrying out gas adsorption, the as-synthesized samples of the compounds were pretreated by immersing them in CH3OH for three days and then degassing them under vacuum at 150 °C for 24 h. TG was further used to trace the degassing process. It is clear that in the activated samples of 1 to 4, the solvent molecules were completely removed after the degassing treatment. The thermostability of the MOF skeletons of the compounds was estimated by PXRD investigations. As shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†), we can conclude that the fresh samples of the compounds can maintain a crystalline state. This can be also retained in polymers 1 and 4, even after activation at 150 °C; however, polymers 2 and 3 seem to greatly lose their crystalline states, and polymer 2 even attained a somewhat amorphous state.

Furthermore, the porosity of the compounds was investigated by gas adsorption studies. Both the 77 K and 293 K N2 adsorption results for polymers 1 and 2 suggest almost no N2 adsorption, mainly because of the formation of an amorphous state after activation in polymer 2 (as evidenced by the PXRD investigations) and the aperture of 3.5 Å in polymer 1, which is smaller than the diameter of an N2 molecule (3.64 Å) (Fig. S2, ESI†).

However, both 1 and 2 can absolutely adsorb CO2 at 293 K, relative to broad gases such as N2, CH4, O2, and CO. For polymer 1, the CO2 adsorption amount at 1 atm is 35 cm3 g−1, which is almost 5.6 and 19.6 times higher than the CH4 and N2 uptakes, respectively (Fig. 5). To estimate the CO2 adsorption selectivity, we carried out the theory calculation based on the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) and these single-component gas adsorption isotherms.17 The results are shown in Fig. 6, and are indicative of greater CO2 adsorption selectivity over N2, O2, and CO. For polymer 2, the CO2 adsorption amount (3.9 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm and 293 K) is very small, but exceeds that of other gases such as N2, CH4, O2, and CO, also suggesting selective CO2 adsorption. However, due to the smaller adsorption amount of these gases, similar theory calculations based on IAST did not yield reasonable values. Generally speaking, CO2 selective adsorption is highly related to two factors: matching aperture and special adsorption point.17 Therefore, the selective adsorption of CO2 in 1 is reasonable, as it meets the above criteria in that the aperture of 3.5 Å in polymer 1 is smaller than the CO2 diameter of 3.3 Å, and extensive, free-standing acylamide groups were observed. However, for 2, the factor of the matching aperture is excluded due to framework collapse after activation, and the smaller CO2 adsorption should be surface adsorption due to the interactions between CO2 molecules and the acylamide groups.


Fig. 5 Gas adsorption isotherms of 1/top and 2/bottom for CO2, N2, O2, and CO at 293 K.

Fig. 6 The calculated selective CO2 adsorption for polymer 1. Bulk gas composition: CO2:CH4 = 40:60 (typical composition of biogas) and CO2:N2 = 10:90 (typical composition of flue gas), CO2:CO = CO2:O2 = 35:65.

As for polymers 3 and 4, they exhibited typical type I N2 adsorption behavior at 77 K, as shown in Fig. 7 and 8, suggesting Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas of 69.5 m2 g−1 for 3 and 1382.8 m2 g−1 for 4. Single-component gas adsorption isotherms of CO2, CO, O2, CH4 and N2 were also measured at 293 K from 0 to 1 atm. Sample 3 exhibits a CO2 uptake capability of 25.56 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm, whereas the adsorption of CH4, CO, N2 and O2 were all less than 6.6 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm. By contrast, sample 4 demonstrated a higher CO2 uptake capability of 43.2 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm, and the adsorption of CH4, CO, N2 and O2 were all below 12.6 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm. These results indicate selective adsorption of CO2 in polymers 3 and 4. Furthermore, based on these adsorption isotherms, we estimated the selectivities for CO2/CH4, CO2/CO, CO2/N2, and CO2/O2 using ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST). These results are shown in Fig. 7 and 8. It is clear that polymer 3 shows high CO2 adsorption selectivity over N2 and CO and moderate CO2 adsorption selectivity over O2 and CH4, whereas polymer 4 shows high CO2 adsorption selectivity over N2, CO, and O2, and moderate CO2 adsorption selectivity over CH4. These results are also comparable with those observed in some benchmark MOFs for CO2 selective adsorption.18

Bulk gas composition: CO2:CH4 = 40:60 (typical composition of biogas) and CO2:N2 = 10:90 (typical composition of flue gas), CO2:COCO2:O2 = 35:65.

Bulk gas composition: CO2:CH4 = 40:60 (typical composition of biogas) and CO2:N2 = 10:90 (typical composition of flue gas), CO2:COCO2:O2 = 35:65.

Conclusions

In this work, we report the synthesis, structure, and gas adsorption of four new porous MOFs in detail. These MOFs were prepared by means of a mixed ligand approach, viz. azo carboxylate ligand plus a long acylamide-pyridyl ligand. For polymers 1 and 2, we explored the effects of position isomers of the acylamide-pyridyl ligand on the resulting structure and porosity, and found that this method is effective to control the interpenetration number of the net and consequently the porosity. Moreover, for polymers 3 and 4, the effect of the amount of solvent on the structure and porosity was investigated, indicating that this method enables the facile generation of structure diversity and enhanced porosity. Most importantly, all these polymers display various degrees of selective adsorption towards CO2, suggesting potential applications in CO2 capture and separation.
