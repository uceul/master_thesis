Volume 194, August 2014, Pages 126-131

One novel three-dimensional framework constructed from infrequent μ–O metal chains with alternating antiferromagnetic/ferromagnetic interactions

Author links open overlay panelHaiyanLiBingAnYanBaiJunliWangHuiPanDongbinDang
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.synthmet.2014.04.024Get rights and content

Highlights

•

One novel 3D polymer constructed from infrequent μ–O cobalt chains has been synthesized.

•

The polymer has been characterized by IR, XRPD, TG and elemental analysis.

•

X-ray single-crystal structure analysis and discussion for the polymer.

•

Magnetic property analysis of 1 indicates an intrachain alternating antiferromagnetic/ferromagnetic coupling.

Abstract

One novel metal–organic framework of formula {[Co2(1,4-NDC)2(4,4′-dpdo)(H2O)]·3H2O}n 1 (1,4-H2NDC = 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate acid and 4,4′-dpdo = 4,4′-dipyridyl-N,N′-dioxide) has been synthesized, and structurally and magnetically characterized. X-ray single crystal structure shows that polymer 1 exhibits a 3D metal–organic framework containing alternating metal chains with two kinds of triple bridge (two carboxylate plus μ–4,4-dpdo or μ–H2O). The metal centers exhibit somewhat distorted octahedral surroundings with two opposite vertexes shared with the adjacent in the chain. Each alternating chain segment is cross-linked to four other different chains through four 1,4-NDC2− ligands to form the 3D structure. The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility of 1 was investigated in the temperature range of 1.8–300 K. The magnetic coupling behavior between metal atoms can be estimated with the model developed by Fisher for an alternating chain of classical spins. The result indicates that it could be considered as an alternating chain with –AF–FM–AF–FM– interaction.

1. Introduction

The field of molecular magnets has been of considerable interest for decades, aimed at elucidating fundamental magnetic phenomena, unveiling magnetostructural correlations and constructing new magnetic materials with potential applications [1], [2], [3], [4]. The crystal engineering and magnetism of molecule-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with low or high dimensional structures have been the most powerful tool to access such systems [5], [6], [7]. One-dimensional magnetic complexes, especially showing single-chain magnetic properties, are currently of ongoing interest for the design and synthesis of new molecule/chain-based magnets [8], [9], [10]. As a purely 1D magnetic system does not exhibit long-range order at finite temperature, the interchain interactions are crucial in this kind of low-dimensional systems, having profound effects on the cooperative magnetic properties of the material [11]. To rationally design magnetic molecular assemblies, it is important to select the appropriate bridging ligands to effectively transmit the exchange interactions between the spin carriers together with the aspects of diverse topologies.

The general approach is to use short bridging groups to connect paramagnetic metal centers and to transmit magnetic coupling. The bridges thus far used to hold paramagnetic metal ions in close proximity in order to mediate sufficiently strong magnetic coupling are mainly restricted to monoatomic (oxygen), diatomic (cyanide) and triatomic (azide) [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. Carboxylate are among the most extensively investigated bridges owing to their structural and magnetic versatility: (1) they can act as connectors to bind two or more metal ions with various bridging modes, such as μ–O, syn–syn, syn–anti and anti–anti, so that they can efficiently mediate either ferromagnetic (FM) or antiferromagnetic (AFM) coupling, which depends mainly upon the bridging mode and the metal ion; (2) they can easily form extended MOFs with diverse topologies that have the benefit of long-range magnetic correlation; (3) in addition, the coordination versatility also makes them compatible with different organic auxiliary ligands, which could be used to tune and control the structure and magnetic properties as they are often sensitive to the change of organic components [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22]. Among the examples on the combination of carboxylate and pyridyl-N-oxide reported recently [23], [24], [25], 4,4′-dipyridyl-N,N′-dioxide (4,4′-dpdo) presents some novel features as a rigid organic ligand, for instance, as a longer spacer to construct MOFs with large cavities or channels, as an excellent donor possessing different coordinated hapticity and conducing to form hydrogen bonds [26], [27]. Importantly, it is a good candidate for assembly of magnetic materials which also can offer a variety of magnetic interactions due to its versatile bridging modes. For instance, when the NiO(N-Oxide)Ni bridging angle is less than 97° ferromagnetic coupling occurs, whereas the coupling becomes antiferromagnetic at larger bridging angles [28]. Hence, magnetic exchange interactions efficiently transmitted and propagated by these multiple bridges between paramagnetic centers allow the preparation of interesting magnetic materials [29], [30], [31].

By literature, the considerable successes in the field of polymers with interchain magnetic coupling were investigated, and the magnetic transmission mainly relies on the carboxylate or simultaneous carboxylate and azide systems [18], [22]. However, MOFs containing magnetic performance with ligands bearing carboxylate and pyridyl-N-oxide coordinative groups have been limited, especially 4,4′-dpdo coordination systems are comparatively scarce [32]. On the other hand, there is reviving interest in cobalt(II) ion because they are attractive spin carriers for this purpose owing to their large single-ion zero-field splitting [33]. With regards to abovementioned reasons, we report the structure and magnetic property of one 3D metal–organic framework {[Co2(1,4-NDC)2(4,4′-dpdo)(H2O)]·3H2O}n 1 (1,4-H2NDC = 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate acid) which consists of real 1D inorganic chains. Remarkably, magnetic property measurement and its theoretical investigation indicate that the structure of 1 exhibits an intrachain alternating antiferromagnetic (AF)/ferromagnetic (FM) coupling.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and physical measurements

All reagents were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H and N) were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analytical instrument. IR spectra were recorded in KBr pellets with a Nicolet 170 SXFT-IR spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. X-ray powder diffraction patterns were recorded on a D/max-γ A rotating anode X-ray diffractometer with Cu sealed tube (λ = 1.54178 Å). Magnetic susceptibility data on crushed single crystals were collected over the temperature range 1.8–300.0 K using a Quantum Design MPMS-5S super-conducting quantum interference device (SQUID) magnetometer. The thermogravimetric analyses were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer-7 thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from 25 to 800 °C.

2.2. Preparation of polymer 1

The polymer was synthesized by hydrothermal methods. A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.3 mmol), 1,4-naphthalenedicarboxylate acid (1,4-H2NDC) (0.1 mmol), 4,4′-bipyridine-N,N′-dioxide (4,4′-dpdo) hydrate (0.1 mmol), NaOH (0.2 mmol) and distilled water (15 mL) was stirred for 1 h. The mixture was then transferred to a 25 mL Teflon-lined steel autoclave and kept at 160 °C for 5 days under autogenous pressure. After the mixture had been slowly cooled down to room temperature, red block crystals were obtained and washed with distilled water. Yield: ca. 72% (based on 1,4-H2NDC). Anal. 2.3. Crystallographic studies

X-ray single crystal diffraction data for polymer 1 were collected on a Bruker Apex-II CCD detector using graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using SMART and SAINT programs. Routine Lorentz and polarization corrections were applied. The structure was solved by direct methods of SHELXS-97 [34] and refined by full-matrix least-squares method using the SHELXL-97 program package [35]. All of the non-hydrogen atoms except the disordered solvent molecules were refined with anisotropic thermal displacement coefficients. Hydrogen atoms were assigned to calculated positions using a riding model with appropriately fixed isotropic thermal parameters. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Description of the crystal structure of compound 1

Single-crystal X-ray structure analysis reveals that the 3D framework crystallizes in a monoclinic crystal system and the asymmetric unit comprises two cobalt(II) metal ions, two deprotonated 1,4-NDC2− ligands, one 4,4′-dpdo ligand, one coordination water molecule and three lattice water molecules of crystallization (Fig. 1).

In the case of 1, the two crystallographically distinct cobalt cations, Co(1) and Co(2), are bound to six coordinated oxygen atoms to form a distorted octahedral geometry. The axis positions of both Co(1) and Co(2) are O2 and O1W. The equatorial plane is occupied by O(4), O(6A), O(8B) and O(10C) of four 1,4-NDC2− ligands, and that of Co(2) by O(3), O(7), O(9C) and O(5E) also originates from four ligands, respectively. (Symmetry codes: A, 1/2 + x, −1/2 + y, z; B, x, 1 − y, −1/2 + z; C, −1/2 + x, 1/2 − y, −1/2 + z; D, x, 1 − y, 1/2 + z; E, 1/2 + x, 3/2 − y, 1/2 + z.) The deviations of Co atom from the mean plane formed by the four equatorial atoms are both 0.003 Å for Co(1) and Co(2). The maximum deviation of O(10C) below the mean plane among all the equatorial atoms is 0.107 Å and that in Co(2) coordination environment is O(3) and O(9C) above the mean plane with 0.021 Å. If marked the two deprotonated 1,4-NDC2− ligands as NDC-1 and NDC-2, both the equatorial plane oxygen donors of each Co center are from two NDC-1 and two NDC-2: for Co(1), O(4) and O(6A) belong to NDC-1, and O(8B) and O(10C) belong to two NDC-2; while for Co(2), O(3), O(5E) and O(7), O(9C) come from two different NDC-1 and NDC-2, respectively. Meanwhile, all carboxyl groups of the two deprotonated 1,4-NDC2− ligands adopt μ2–η1:η1 to connect Co(1) and Co(2) (Scheme S1). O(2) and O(1W), the opposite corner-sharing oxygen atoms in the axial positions, originate from one 4,4′-dpdo ligand with the μ–4,4 fashion and one water molecule, respectively. CoO6 octahedra are in the alternating corner-sharing fashion to generate a [Co(1)O(2)Co(2)O(1W)]n alternating collinear inorganic chain running parallel to the c axis (Fig. 2). The CoO bond lengths are in the range of 2.052(5)–2.193(3) Å for Co(1)O and 2.031(5)–2.208(3) Å for Co(2)O. The angles in the bridge parts are 117.3(2)° for Co(1)O(2)Co(2) and 111.1(1)° for Co(1)O(1W)Co(2) and the distances between the neighboring Co centers alternate between 3.58 Å and 3.63 Å within a chain. The two kinds of Co⋯Co⋯Co angle are 175.3° and 170.8°, respectively, indicating that the chain is almost collinear.

In ab plane orientation, each chain segment is cross-linked to four different chain segments through four 1,4-NDC2− ligands of two types with the dihedral angle between the two benzene rings within a ligand of 3.7° ([C(11)C(20)]) or 6.9° ([C(23)C(32)]) (Fig. 3). By the stretch of infinite inorganic chains, the ab plane cross-linkers prolongate along c axis to shape a 3D network and the separations of adjacent interchains are all more than 10 Å. That is to say, each 1,4-NDC2− unit respectively binds four Co(II) ions of two alternating inorganic chains through a μ4–κ1, κ1, κ1, κ1 carboxylate bridge to form a 3D framework with the arrangement of the I1O1 fashion, which is decorated with dpdo of μ–4,4 bridging mode and dpdo has no contribution to construct the 3D framework (Fig. 4). To the best of our knowledge, such a μ–4,4 bridging mode of dpdo was rarely documented for dn metal systems. In addition, there are three hydrogen bonds O(2W)H⋯O(1), O(3W)H⋯O(7) and C(7)H⋯O(3W) between 4,4′-dpdo and 1,4-NDC2− ligands and lattice water molecules in solid state.

Topologically, if the pink bonds represent 1,4-NDC2− ligands, the nodes represent [Co(1)O(2)Co(2)] segments and the turquoise bonds represent the bridge of O(1W), each node connects adjacent six nodes (Fig. S4). Hence, the overall framework of two polymers can both be represented as six-connected net with a short Schläfli symbol of (412.63).

3.2. X-ray powder diffraction

To confirm the phase purity and to examine the crystallinity of bulk samples, the X-ray powder diffraction pattern of 1 was recorded. As shown in Fig. 5, the peak positions of simulated and experimental patterns are in good agreement with each other, demonstrating the phase purity of the products. The dissimilarities in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples.

3.3. Magnetic property

As shown in Fig. 6, the χMT value of 1 at room temperature is 8.96 cm3 mol−1 K (χM is the molar magnetic susceptibility per two Co(II) ions), much larger than the spin-only value of a Co(II)2 unit (3.75 cm3 mol−1 K for S = 3/2 and g = 2) and suggesting the significant orbital contribution typical for the 4T1g ground term of high-spin Co(II) in an octahedral surrounding. As the temperature decreases, the χMT decreases smoothly to a rounded minimum of 5.62 cm3 mol−1 K at about 45 K, then rises abruptly to a sharp maximum of 17.07 cm3 mol−1 K at about 15 K, and finally drops rapidly upon further cooling to 2 K. Such a complex temperature-dependent behavior is the consequence of the interplay and competition of several concurrent effects, including the single-ion effects (first-order spin–orbital coupling and ligand-field distortion of Co(II) sites) and intrachain magnetic interactions between Co(II) ions. The χ−1 versus T plot shows no straight line which can be concerned to the possibility of the diamagnetic contribution. Concretely, the χ−1 versus T plot above 100 K follows the Curie–Weiss law with C = 11.20 cm3 mol−1 K (for two cobalt ions) and θ = −75.4 K. The C value is in the usual range of two octahedral high-spin Co(II) ions corresponding to g = 3.09, and the large negative value of the Weiss constant can be due to significant spin–orbit coupling of the octahedral Co(II) ions and the depopulating of the higher Kramers doublets and to the presence of antiferromagnetic interaction [5].

According to the structural characteristics, there are two types of intrachain magnetic exchange pathways corresponding to different linkages, J1 and J2 through the μ2–O(2) bridge and the μ2–O(1W) bridge with alternate arrangement (Scheme 1). To evaluate the magnetic interactions of the 1D alternating chain structures roughly, the alternating 1D Heisenberg Fisher model (Eq. (1)) based on the Hamiltonian (Eq. (2)) was used to fit the data incorporating λ as a correction item which takes care of both the orbital contribution due to the presence of spin–orbit coupling in the high-spin Co(II) system and the interchain antiferromagnetic interaction (this model can be reduced to the uniform chain if J1 = J2) [36]:(1)χchain=Ng2β2S(S+1)3k(T−λ)1+u1+u2+u1u21−u1u2(2)Hˆ=−J1∑NS2iS2i+1−J2∑NS2i+1S2i+2where u is the well-known Langevin function defined as u = coth[JiS(S + 1)/kT] − kT/[JiS(S + 1)] (i = 1, 2) with S = 3/2 (J > 0 for ferromagnetic and J < 0 for antiferromagnetic coupling). The best least-squares fit of the experimental data χMT above 50 K to the above expressions led to J1/kB = −51.7 K, J2/kB = 61.5 K, g = 2.95, λ = 28.8 K and R = 5.1 × 10−6, where R = Σ(χMTcald − χMTobsd)2/(χMTobsd)2 (Fig. S5). Depending on the alternation parameter α (α = J2/|J1|), when α < 1, an antiferromagnetic-like behavior is observed; when α > 1, the ferromagnetic exchange J2 is the leading parameter which can be taken as the signature of an alternating AF–F chain with the dominant ferromagnetic interactions. The simulation indicates that the magnetic properties should be attributed to the competition between ferromagnetic interaction and antiferromagnetic coupling along the chain.

To investigate the ferromagnetic-like behavior, where the χMT–T curves rise abruptly at the low temperature, the noncritical-scaling theory with the following simple phenomenological Eq. (3) proposed by Rueff et al. was used to fit the experimental data from 30 K to 80 K [37], [38]:(3)χMT=Aexp−E1kT+Bexp−E2kTHere A + B equals the Curie constant for two Co(II) ions per formula and E1, E2 represent the “activation energies” corresponding to single-ion effects (the spin–orbit coupling and site distortion) and the magnetic exchange interactions, respectively. E1 > 0 indicates the antiferromagnetic interaction within the chain, which is responsible for the initial high temperature decay of χMT. The low-temperature behavior, increasing of χMT below 45 K, is denoted by the second term with E2 < 0 which is ferromagnetic-like. The best fit gives A = 8.03, B = 0.32, E1/k = 32.16 cm−1 and E2/k = −75.14 cm−1, which suggest the coexistence of ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions and the dominant exchange to be ferromagnetic (Fig. S6). This approach allows one to have a rough estimation of the strength of the magnetic exchange interactions. Good results have been reported in 1D and 2D Co(II) complexes [39], [40].

To further investigate the phase transformation at low temperature, the field-cooled (FC) and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization measurements were performed at 20 Oe in the 2–30 K (Fig. 7). The ZFC curve increases as the temperature rises from 2 K, reaches the maximum at 22 K, and then decreases quickly, which reveals the antiferromagnetic intrinsic state. As the sample cools, the FC curve increase abruptly with decreasing temperature, and diverges with the ZFC curve below the critical temperature Tc = 22 K, which indicates a possible transition from a paramagnetic state to either a long-range ordered, a spin-glass or a superparamagnetic state [41]. At very low temperature, the FC/ZFC curves undergo a slight increase which could be due to the presence of paramagnetic contribution arising from defects in the crystal structure [42].

The magnetizations measured at 2 K show hysteresis loops with a small coercive field of 502 Oe and a remnant magnetization of 0.2 Nβ indicating a soft-magnetic behavior (Fig. 8). The magnetization M versus H plots at 2 K (per two Co(II) ions) is also shown in Fig. 8 The M values increase as the H rises and until at 50 kOe it shows an unsaturation value of the magnetization (Ms) of 3.86 Nβ, that is 1.93 Nβ per Co(II) ion. This value, which is smaller than the theoretical saturation magnetization of Co(II) ion (Ms = 3 Nβ expected for spin-only Co(II) species), may due to the coexistence of ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interaction [39].

3.4. Thermogravimetric analysis

Thermo gravimetric experiment was conducted to study the thermal stability of polymer 1, which is an important parameter of inorganic–organic hybrid materials. The TG properties were measured under air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from 25 to 800 °C (Fig. S7). The weight loss of 8.93% before the temperature of 215 °C is agree well with to the release of coordinated and crystallized H2O molecules (calcd. 8.94%). Polymer 1 continues loss from the decomposition of skeleton even at the upper limit of measurement range.

4. Conclusion

In summary, we report the synthesis, structural characterization and magnetic property of one novel 3D coordination polymer containing the alternating 1D corner-shared octahedral metal chains with two kinds of triple bridge (two carboxylate plus μ–4,4 dpdo or μ–H2O). In addition, magnetic behavior investigation of 1 indicates alternating antiferromagnetic–ferromagnetic (AF–F) spin chains and theoretical results afford a satisfactory agreement with the experimental observations. Therefore, the assembly of these kinds of polymers is tunable and controllable, and it is good examples and gives a new avenue for the magnetic studies on higher dimensional polymers with alternating AF–F chains.
