Slow relaxation of magnetization in 3D-MOFs based on dysprosium dinuclear entities bridged by dicarboxylic linkers†

Itziar Oyarzabal a, Belén Fernández b, Javier Cepeda a, Santiago Gómez-Ruiz c, Antonio J. Calahorro b, José M. Seco *a and Antonio Rodríguez-Diéguez *b

Three novel metal–organic-frameworks (MOFs) based on dysprosium as the metal and dicarboxylic ligands have been solvothermally synthesized with the aim of studying and modulating their magnetic properties according to the variation of the distances between metal centers. These materials display intense photoluminescence properties in the solid state at room temperature. In addition, a very interesting property of compound 1 is that it exhibits slow relaxation of magnetization with an activation energy barrier of 32 K. Magneto-structural correlations have been analyzed.

Introduction

In recent years, dysprosium ions have been widely employed as fascinating magnetic centers to construct single-molecule magnets (SMMs) due to their large magnetic moment and significant single-ion anisotropy derived from spin−orbit coupling and the crystal field effect.1 Because of this increasing interest, recently, different types of materials have been built based on this metal ion with the aim of studying the slow relaxation of magnetization and improving the magnetic properties. Some of these materials are metal–organic frameworks (MOFs). Research on these crystalline coordination polymers has been very productive, and various applications have been widely explored.2 MOFs are obtained by the self-assembly of metal ions or clusters with appropriate bridging organic ligands. The study of lanthanide-based MOFs has evolved enormously in areas such as luminescence,3 gas adsorption,4 optical storage5 and magnetism.6 However, although the synthesis of Dy-MOFs is not an extensively explored field by far, our research group and others are focusing on this point with the aim of analyzing slow relaxation of the magnetization. The great advantage offered by MOFs is that dysprosium ions can be positioned at desired distances due to the length of the linkers used. Therefore, there is a great interest in the design and preparation of new MOFs based on dysprosium ions and different ligands having interesting properties.

In recent years, we have designed and synthesized single-molecule magnets based on dysprosium, showing interesting effective energy barriers7 and, more recently, we have designed a new family of lanthanide 3D-MOFs consisting of the 9,10-anthracenedicarboxylic linker (ant2−) that exhibits slow relaxation of magnetization.8 This sort of dicarboxylic ligands,9 have been successfully used in the preparation of various carboxylate complexes due to their rich variety of coordination modes. For this reason, we decided to explore and prepare dysprosium-based MOFs using different dicarboxylic linkers (Scheme 1), and study their luminescence and magnetic properties. As Dy-based dinuclear compounds have shown interesting SMM properties,10 we focused our research on MOFs based on dinuclear entities. In addition, we decided to introduce small changes in used ligands to try to establish a relationship between the distances among the metal centers, the coordination sphere and the variation of the values of activation barriers. In particular, 2,5-diaminoterephthalic acid ((NH2)2-H2bdc) has been used alone or together with the 9,10-anthracenedicarboxylic linker since it exhibits similar coordination modes but shorter distances among dinuclear units. We also made use of 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2cyim) to obtain a MOF with big channels due to the presence of a cyanide group.


Scheme 1 Different ligands used to construct MOFs: a) 2,5-diaminoterephthalic acid; b) 9,10-anthracenedicarboxylic acid and c) 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid.

Taking into account our previous reports in the field, we report the synthesis, structure, luminescence and magnetic properties of three novel 3D-MOFs based on the three above mentioned dicarboxylic linkers.

Given their extended aromaticities, these linkers are good candidates for enhancing emissive properties, which are in principle tunable by coordination to dysprosium with different chemical environments. These organic ligands can act as sensitizers, absorbing and transferring the energy efficiently to lanthanide ions (intramolecular energy transfer), which results in the improvement of luminescence intensity. Herein, we report the synthesis, structure, porosity, luminescence and magnetic properties of three 3D-MOFs, {[Dy((NH2)2-bdc)1.5(DMF)2]·DMF·H2O}n (1), {[Dy(ant)((NH2)2-bdc)0.5(DMF)2]·DMF·H2O}n (2) and {[Dy2(cyim)3(DMF)4]·DMF}n (3), demonstrating the potential of these dicarboxylic linkers to construct new MOFs with interesting properties as multifunctional materials.

Results and discussion

The hydrothermal reactions of the corresponding dicarboxylic ligands with different dysprosium salts in dimethylformamide (10 ml) at 95 °C for 24–48 h produced prismatic crystals of these different 3D-MOFs. The crystal structures of the compounds were determined using single crystal X-ray diffraction.

Description of the structure

Compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The 3D-MOF structure is composed of Dy(III) dimers connected by oxygen atoms pertaining to the carboxylate groups of six different 2,5-diaminoterephthalic ligands with different coordination modes (bis-bridging intradinuclear mode for A and C ligands and bis-chelating interdinuclear mode for B). This MOF can be described by dysprosium dinuclear units (Fig. 1) bridged by six different L2− linkers, two of them with the bis-chelating coordination mode and four with the bis-bridging mode.
Fig. 1 Dinuclear entity formed by two dysprosium atoms and six dicarboxylic linkers. Hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green. Only the ligand donor atoms of the asymmetric unit are labelled.

The distortion of the DyO8 coordination polyhedron is induced mainly by the angle O1B-Dy-O2B of 54.50(7)° generated by the carboxylate group in the chelate mode. The Dy(III) atom is coordinated to six oxygen atoms pertaining to five different dianionic ligands and two oxygen atoms (O1D and O1E) belonging to two DMF coordinated molecules. These dinuclear units are bridged by linkers generating a three-dimensional network (Fig. 2) in which the amino groups are oriented to the channels along the c axis. These channels are occupied by crystallization DMF molecules.


Fig. 2 View down the c axis of the channels in compound 1. Hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. Color code N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green.

The Dy–Ocarb bond distances are in the range 2.2365(19)–2.3840(19) Å, whereas the Dy–ODMF distances are 2.316(2) and 2.4299(19) Å. Inside the dimers, Dy atoms are connected by four different dianionic ligands generating a three-dimensional coordination polymer with an intradinuclear Dy⋯Dy distance of 4.294 Å and interdinuclear Dy⋯Dy distances through ligands in the range 11.056–11.972 Å.

Compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The 3D-MOF structure is composed of Dy(III) dimers connected by carboxylate groups of two ant2− (A) and 2,5-diaminoterephthalic linkers (C) that show a bis-bridging intradinuclear coordination mode (Fig. 3).


Fig. 3 Dinuclear entity formed by two dysprosium atoms, four ant2− ligands, two 2,5-diaminoterephthalic linkers and four DMF coordination molecules. Hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green.

In addition to the bis-bridging ligands, the dysprosium atoms within these dinuclear entities are further bridged by two ant2− linkers showing a bis-chelating interdinuclear coordination mode (B). The junction of dinuclear units generates a three-dimensional network (Fig. 4) with channels along the c axis with crystallization DMF molecules.


Fig. 4 View along the c axis of the channels in compound 2. DMF solvent molecules and hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. Color code N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green.

The distortion of the DyO8 coordination polyhedron is mainly induced by the angle O1B-Dy-O2B of 53.7(2)° generated by the carboxylate group in the chelate mode. The Dy(III) atom is coordinated to six oxygen atoms pertaining to five different dianionic ligands and two oxygen atoms (O1M and O1N) belonging to two DMF coordinated molecules. These dinuclear units are bridged by linkers generating a three-dimensional network (Fig. 4) in which the amino groups from 2,5-diaminoterephthalic linkers are oriented to the channels along the c axis. The Dy–Ocarb bond distances are in the range 2.275(8)–2.461(6) Å whereas the Dy–ODMF distances are 2.348(6) and 2.417(7) Å. Inside the dimers, Dy atoms are connected by two ant2− ligands and two different 2,5-diaminoterephthalic linkers generating a three-dimensional coordination polymer with an intradinuclear Dy⋯Dy distance of 4.415 Å and interdinuclear Dy⋯Dy distances in the range 10.767–11.971 Å.

Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c. The 3D-MOF structure is composed of Dy(III) dimers connected through carboxylate groups of different 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic ligands showing a bis-bridging intradinuclear coordination mode (A ligand) or a bridging-chelating interdinuclear mode (B and C). The asymmetric unit (Fig. 5) of this material is composed of two dysprosium atoms, three dianionic ligands, four coordination DMF molecules and a crystallization DMF molecule. These dinuclear units are bridged by different linkers generating a three-dimensional network (Fig. 6) with big channels along the c axis that are occupied by crystallization DMF molecules. The two dysprosium atoms are crystallographically independent, both with DyO8 coordination polyhedra.


Fig. 5 Dinuclear entities of 3 in which the asymmetric unit is formed by two dysprosium atoms, three linkers and four coordination DMF molecules. Solvent and hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green.

Fig. 6 View down the c axis of the channels in compound 3. Hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. Color code N = blue, O = red, C = grey, Dy = green.

The Dy1 atom has a coordination environment formed by six oxygen atoms pertaining to five different 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic linkers and the last two oxygen atoms (O1L and O1P) are two DMF coordinated molecules. On the other hand, the Dy2 atom has a similar coordination environment with six oxygen atoms of five different dianionic linkers and two oxygen atoms (O1M and O1N) of two DMF coordinated molecules. In these polyhedra, we must highlight the small angles O31C-Dy1-O32C and O31B-Dy2-O32B of 54.02(6)° and 53.42(6), respectively, generated by the carboxylate groups in the chelating mode. The Dy–Ocarb bond distances are in the range 2.2350(19)–2.5644(19) Å whereas Dy–ODMF distances are between 2.3703(19) and 2.4252(19) Å. Within the dimers, the intradinuclear Dy⋯Dy distances are 4.422 and 4.202 Å for Dy1⋯Dy1 and Dy2⋯Dy2, respectively. On the other hand, the interdinuclear Dy⋯Dy distances are in the range 7.488–10.649 Å.

Due to the large channels which show these structures in solid state, gas adsorption analysis of N2 at 77 K on the activated sample of three MOFs was conducted, although they revealed no appreciable porosity in all cases.

Luminescence studies

The conjugated π-systems of the aromatic rings are interesting in fluorescent materials.11 For this reason, we have studied the luminescence properties of 1–3 (Fig. 7) as well as those of the free ligands at room temperature. As seen in bibliography, the (NH2)2-H2bdc ligand shows a main emission band at 450 nm when excited at λexc = 265 nm.11c Compound 1 exhibits a broad band at 453 nm upon excitation at λexc = 320 nm which is in agreement with another coordination compound with the same ligand.11c The H2ant ligand displays moderate luminescence in the solid state at λem = 518 nm upon excitation at λexc = 444 nm.8 Upon excitation at λexc = 380 nm, 2 exhibits intense luminescent emissions at λem = 440 nm. Similar values have been reported by us.8 Moreover, to the best of our knowledge, luminescence properties of 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid have not been explored. This ligand displays moderate luminescence in the solid state at λem = 360 and 372 nm and a small band at 481 nm upon excitation at λexc = 320 nm (Fig. S1†). Interestingly, compound 3 exhibits the same bands at 361, 376 and 480 nm, so the luminescence properties do not vary much after the coordination of the ligand to metal. On the other hand, the emission spectra were measured in the 550–700 nm range after excitation at the most intense emission wavelength of the ligand and the resulting two major peaks could be assigned to the 4F9/2/6H13/2 and 4F9/2/6H11/2 transitions. These peaks are in accordance with different Dy-MOF published in bibliography.

Magnetic properties

The magnetic properties of compounds 1–3 were measured in the 2–300 K temperature range under an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe and are given as the temperature dependence of the χMT product (χM being the molar susceptibility per dinuclear unit) in Fig. 8.
Fig. 8 Temperature dependence of the χMT product for compounds 1 (top), 2 (middle) and 3 (bottom) in the presence of an external magnetic field of 1000 Oe. Insets: M vs. H plots at 2 K.

The room temperature χMT values of complexes 1–3 are 28.18, 28.16 and 28.14 cm3 mol−1 K, respectively, which are in good agreement with the expected theoretical value for two independent Dy(III) ions in the free ion approximation (28.34 cm3 mol−1 K). The three compounds show a similar magnetic behavior, with a decrease in χMT product when decreasing the temperature, first slowly down to 100 K and then in a more abrupt way to reach the values of 21.35, 21.48 and 20.94 cm3 mol−1 K at 2 K, respectively for 1–3. The observed behavior is due to a combination of the thermal depopulation of the mj sublevels of the 6H15/2 ground state of the Dy(III) ions, which is splitted by the effects of the crystal field, and possible antiferromagnetic interactions between Dy(III) ions. The weak coupling constants cannot be extracted from these data due to the intrinsic magnetic properties of the Dy(III) ions.

The field dependence of the molar magnetization at 2 K for 1–3 (Fig. 8, insets) show a relatively rapid increase at low fields, followed by a very slow increase at high fields to reach values of 11.8, 11.2 and 11.6 NμB at 5 T. These values are much lower than the theoretical saturation value for a couple of Dy(III) ions of 20 NμB, which is due to the presence of magnetic anisotropy, crystal-field effects and/or low-lying excited states.

In order to know whether these complexes exhibit slow relaxation of the magnetization or not, dynamic alternating-current (ac) magnetic measurements were performed as a function of both temperature and frequency. Despite the expected large anisotropy of the Dy(III) ions, these complexes only showed a very weak frequency dependency of the out-of-phase signal (χM′′) under zero external dc field (Fig. S2†), which could be attributed to the presence of fast relaxation of the magnetization via a QTM mechanism. At zero dc field, dipole–dipole and hyperfine interactions induce the mixing of the two Kramers ground states of the Dy(III) ions, thus allowing the quantum tunneling dynamics of the magnetization.

When the ac measurements were performed in the presence of a small external dc field of 1000 Oe to fully or partly suppress the QTM relaxation process, compound 1 showed typical SMM behavior, while compounds 2 and 3 continued showing only a slight frequency dependency of the out-of-phase signal (Fig. 9 and Fig. S3†).


Fig. 9 Temperature dependence of the in-phase χM′ (top) and out-of-phase χM′′ (middle) components of the ac susceptibility for compound 1 under an external field of 1000 Oe. Inset: Arrhenius plot for the relaxation times of 1. Bottom: Cole-Cole plots under 1000 Oe external field for 1. Solid lines represent the best fits to the generalized Debye model.

The Cole–Cole plot for 1 (Fig. 9, bottom) shows semicircular shapes that can be fitted using the generalized Debye model, affording α values in the 0.12 (3 K)–0.02 (5.4 K) range (the α parameter determines the width of the distribution of relaxation times, so that α = 1 corresponds to an infinitely wide distribution of relaxation times, while α = 0 represents a relaxation with a single time constant). The obtained values suggest that more than one relaxation process operates at low temperatures, QTM relaxation mode among others, which has not been fully eliminated after the application of the external field. In fact, the in-phase and out-of-phase susceptibility signals measured at the highest frequencies do not go to zero below the maxima, which corroborates the existence of fast QTM (Fig. 9).

The high temperature relaxation times extracted from the frequency-dependent susceptibility data (Fig. 10) follow an Arrhenius data, affording an effective energy barrier for the reversal of the magnetization of 32 K with τ0 = 6.17·10−8 s (Fig. 9, inset). As expected, the Arrhenius plots constructed from the temperatures and frequencies of the maxima observed for the χM′′ signals also lead to the same results. The experimental relaxation times deviate slightly from linearity at the lowest temperatures, which indicates the presence of multiple relaxation processes. In view of this, we tried to fit the experimental data to the following equation, which considers that the spin–lattice relaxation takes place through Orbach and QTM processes:

τ−1 = τQTM−1 + τ0exp(−Ueff/kBT)

However, no good fit of the data was obtained, which may be due to the existence of several relaxation processes, including Orbach and QTM and probably Raman and activated processes in the studied temperature range.


Fig. 10 Variable-temperature frequency dependence of the χM′′ signal under an applied external field of 1000 Oe for compound 1.

Magneto-structural considerations

The differences in the SMM behavior of complexes 1–3 can be explained by taking into account the deviation of the coordination sphere of the Dy(III) ions from an ideal geometry, as this deviation introduces transverse field components, favoring the QTM and decreasing the Ueff.12 SHAPE measurements13 carried out in the three compounds (Fig. 11 and Table S2†) show that compound 1 possesses the least distorted DyO8 coordination sphere and therefore, this compound should exhibit the highest Ueff value, which is in agreement with the observed behavior. In addition, square antiprism coordination geometries, where the donor atoms are located above and below the equatorial plane, favor the presence of easy-axis anisotropy on the Dy(III) compounds, and therefore, the presence of SMM behavior.7b
Fig. 11 DyO8 coordination environments for compounds 1 (a), 2 (b) and 3 (c and d) with the CshM values (SHAPE measurement results) obtained for the closest geometries. Two different DyO8 coordination environments are found in complex 3. SAPR-8 = square antiprism; BTPR-8 = biaugmented trigonal prism and TDD-8 = triangular dodecahedron. Color code: O = red, Dy = turquoise.

On the other hand, the strength of the magnetic exchange interactions is also a decisive factor in determining the SMM properties.14 In fact, the {[Dy2(FDA)3(DMF)2]·1.5DMF}n (FDA = furan-2,5-dicarboxylic acid) compound found in bibliography,12a shows biaugmented trigonal prism DyO8 coordination spheres with slightly higher distortions (CshM = 1.579) compared to those observed in compound 2, but the magnetic exchange interaction is ferromagnetic and as a consequence, this compound exhibits a higher effective energy barrier of 41.8 K.

By studying the structural factors that may affect the magnetic behavior of 1–3, it is observed that compound 1 possesses the shortest Dy⋯Dy intradinuclear distance and the largest interdinuclear Dy⋯Dy distances. It should be noted that other factors, such as the angles of the bridging fragments or the direction of the anisotropy axis of the Dy(III) ions also influence the magnetic exchange interactions. However, due to the variety of bridging topologies found in carboxylate-bridged compounds, we decided to analyze only the connection between distances and effective energy barriers, comparing these results with similar compounds found in bibliography. The {[Dy(ant)1.5(DMF)2]·(DMF)}n compound previously reported by us8 exhibits even shorter intradinuclear distances (3.996 Å) with a largest interdinuclear distance of 14.442 Å and an effective energy barrier of 52 K (the DyO9 coordination spheres of this compound are close to muffin ideal geometry, with CShM = 1.675). The other analyzed material is {[Dy2(PA)3(H2O)3(DMF)]·(DMF)2·(H2O)}n (Table 1).15 These largest interdinuclear distances are measured from the centroids calculated in the dinuclear entities. Analyzing these results, it could be said that among other factors, short Dy⋯Dy intradinuclear distances together with large interdinuclear distances seem to improve the SMM properties in carboxylate quadruple bridged compounds.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we have synthesized three novel metal–organic frameworks based on dysprosium and dicarboxylic ligands with the aim to study their magnetic properties and modifying them by variation of the distances among metal centers. There are very few examples of 3D-MOF based on dysprosium that can be described as dinuclear entities. These materials display intense photoluminescence properties in the solid state at room temperature. What is more interesting is that compound 1 exhibits slow relaxation of magnetization with an activation energy barrier of 32 K. Magneto-structural considerations have been analysed concluding that short Dy⋯Dy intradinuclear distances together with large interdinuclear distances seem to improve the SMM properties in carboxylate quadruple bridged compounds.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All reagents were obtained from commercial sources and used as received. Elemental (C, H, and N) analyses were performed on a Leco CHNS-932 microanalyzer. The IR spectra of the powdered samples were recorded in the 400–4000 cm−1 region on a Nicolet 6700 FTIR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets.

Crystallographic refinement and structure solution

Prismatic crystals for 1–3 were mounted on a glass fibre and used for data collection on a Bruker D8 Venture with a Photon detector equipped with graphite monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The data reduction was performed with the APEX2 software16 and corrected for absorption using SADABS.17 The crystal structures were solved by direct methods using the SIR97 program18 and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 including all reflections using anisotropic displacement parameters by means of the WINGX crystallographic package.19 Generally, anisotropic temperature factors were assigned to all atoms except for hydrogen atoms, which are riding their parent atoms with an isotropic temperature factor arbitrarily chosen as 1.2 times that of the respective parent.

It should be noted that all crystals undergo degradation when they are removed from the mother liquor which has a high impact on the quality of the data. Several crystals of compounds were measured and the structures were solved from the best data we were able to collect. Attempts to solve disorder problems with DMF crystallization molecules failed in compounds 1–3. Instead, a new set of F2 (hkl) values with the contribution from the solvent molecule withdrawn were obtained by the SQUEEZE procedure implemented in PLATON-94.20 Therefore, chemical formulae have been estimated on the basis of the electron density and the voids. Final R(F), wR(F2) and goodness of fit agreement factors, details on the data collection and analysis can be found in Table 2. CCDC numbers are 1451703–1451705.

Magnetic measurements

Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility (2–300 K) under an applied field of 1000 Oe and magnetization measurements at 2 K and different magnetic fields (0–50 kOe) were carried out with a PPMS (Physical Property Measurement System) – Quantum Design Model 6000 magnetometer for compounds 1 and 3 and with a Quantum Design SQUID MPMS XL-5 device for compound 2. Alternating current magnetic measurements were performed in the same devices, with an oscillating ac field of 3.5 Oe in all cases. The experimental susceptibilities were corrected for the sample holder and diamagnetism of the constituent atoms by using Pascal's tables.

Luminescence measurements

A Varian Cary-Eclipse Fluorescence Spectrofluorimeter was used to obtain the fluorescence spectra. The spectrofluorimeter was equipped with a xenon discharge lamp (peak power equivalent to 75 kW), Czerny–Turner monochromators, R-928 photomultiplier tube which is red sensitive (even 900 nm) with manual or automatic voltage controlled using the Cary Eclipse software for Windows 95/98/NT system. The photomultiplier detector voltage was 700 V and the instrument excitation and emission slits were set at 5 and 5 nm, respectively.

Adsorption analysis

N2 adsorption isotherms were undertaken at 77 K using a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 instrument. Samples were activated at 393 K for 8 hours previous to the adsorption measurement.

Syntheses of compounds

Synthesis of {[Dy((NH2)2-bdc)1.5(DMF)2]·DMF·H2O}n (1). Compound 1 was obtained by solvothermal methods through the following procedure: 2 ml solution of DMF containing 0.05 mmol of ligand 2,5-diaminoterephthalic acid (9 mg) is prepared in a vial of 8 ml of volume. To this solution, 2 ml of DMF containing 0.05 mmol of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O (22.8 mg) is added. The reaction mixture was heated in a closed vial at 95 °C in an oven for 24 h. Pale yellow crystals were obtained. Yield: 31% based on Dy(III) ion. Elemental analysis of C39H55Dy2N11O18 (1), calcd: C 40.10, H 3.53, N 8.91; found: C 39.95, H 3.72, N 8.54.
Synthesis of {[Dy(ant)((NH2)2-bdc)0.5(DMF)2]·DMF·H2O}n (2). Compound 2 was obtained by solvothermal methods through the following procedure: 2 ml solution of DMF containing 0.0166 mmol of the ligand 9,10-anthracenedicarboxylic acid (4.5 mg) and 0.025 mmol of ligand 2,5-diaminoterephthalic acid (4.5 mg) are prepared in a vial of 8 ml of volume. To this solution, 2 ml of DMF containing 0.05 mmol of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O (22.8 mg) is added. The reaction mixture was heated in a closed vial at 95 °C in an oven for 24 h. Pale yellow crystals were obtained. Yield: 45% based on Dy(III) ion. Elemental analysis of C55H57Dy2N6O18 (2), calcd: C 46.68, H 4.06, N 5.94; found: C 46.32, H 3.98, N 5.13.
Synthesis of {[Dy2(cyim)3(DMF)4]·DMF}n (3). Compound 3 was obtained by soft solvothermal methods: 0.1 mmol (45.6 mg) of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O was dissolved in 2 ml of DMF and added to a vial of 8 ml volume. To this solution 0.1 mmol of 5-cyano-1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (19.1 mg) is added in 2 ml of DMF. The reaction mixture was heated in a closed vial at 95 °C in an oven for 24 h. Pale yellow crystals were obtained. Yield: 29% based on Dy(III) ion. Elemental analysis of C42H44Dy2N8O17 (3), calcd: C 40.10, H 3.53, N 8.91; found: C 39.86, H 3.24, N 9.02.
