Various crystal structures based on 4,4′-(diethynylanthracene-9,10-diyl) dibenzoic acid: from 0D dimer to 3D net framework†

Fan Yu*a, Yu-Min Zhanga, Yan-Hua Guoa, Ai-Hua Lia, Guo-Xian Yua and Bao Li*b
aHubei Key Laboratory of Industrial Fume & Dust Pollution Control, Jianghan University, Wuhan, 430056, PR China. E-mail: yufan0714@163.com
bCollege of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, Hubei 430074, PR China. E-mail: libao@hust.edu.cn

Received 3rd June 2013, Accepted 13th August 2013

First published on 14th August 2013

Three coordination polymers, {[Co2(L)2(H2O)8]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (1), {[Cd2(L)2(H2O)5]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (2), and {[Eu(L)1.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)·(DMF)2}n (3) (H2L = 4,4′-(diethynylanthracene-9,10-diyl) dibenzoic acid) based on a new dicarboxylate ligand have been synthesized and structurally characterized, in which the di-carboxylate ligand could act as multi-dentate bridging linker with different connection modes to connect various metal ions. Complex 1 exhibits an oligomer structure containing a mono-dentate dicarboxylate ligand and di-nuclear cobalt joint. Complex 2 is a double-chained 1D wire based on a bidentate-chelating/bridging ligand and mono-Cd ion. In contrast, complex 3 possesses a 3D open framework based on an infinite Eu-(μ2-OOC) chain and bridging ligands. In terms of the distinction between the three crystal structures, it is concluded that the coordination number and mode of metal ions and ligands play important roles in constructing the final crystal structure. In addition, the thermal stabilities and photoluminescence behavior of 1–3 and the gas sorption of 3 are further discussed in relative detail.

Introduction

Microporous coordination polymers (CPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have received considerable attention due to their intriguing topologies and potential applications as functional materials, such as luminescence, sorption and separation, catalysis, etc.1–3 Commonly, the construction of MOFs is highly influenced by several puzzling structure-directing factors such as the coordination geometry of the metal ions, configuration and coordination mode of the organic linkers, solvent, pH value and the temperature, etc.4–7 In these factors, the careful pre-design and selection of the organic ligands plays an important role in constructing the presumed topological structure and specific functional materials. Up to now, most of the ligands utilized in constructing MOFs are mainly derived from the readily available hexa-membered aromatic organic scaffolds, especially such as benzene and triazine.8 The incorporating of the organic scaffolds tends to avoid the interpenetration of the porous structure and easily brings up functional sites to improve the properties of the MOF materials. In contrast, the bridged linkers derived from larger conjugated-aromatic scaffolds, such as anthracene or pyrene, are rarely reported.9,10 The scaffolds such as anthracene or pyrene usually possess specific photo-luminescent properties, which would be easily incorporated into the resulted products to give luminescent coordination materials.9,10 For example, several derivates with different sizes derived from the anthracene ring have been utilized in assembling versatile coordination materials that exhibited excellent gas absorption and photo-luminescent properties (Scheme 1).
Scheme 1 The different ligands based on the anthracene scaffold.

It is also well-known that the length of the organic linkers has a significant effect on the coordination mode of the ligand, which further determines the final topological structure.11 For example, isostructural cubic topologies with different size of pores could be constructed by elongating the linear di-carboxylate ligands with an equivalent coordination geometry, which is very essential for developing the highly-porous MOF materials.12 Inserting another phenyl ring into the linkers is seen as one common strategy for enlarging the molecular-accessible surface and building high-porosity materials.12 Recently, utilizing triple-bond alkyne spacers to elongate the linkers and subsequently assemble various coordination structures has become a flourishing field to investigate the interaction of the alkyne functional group and small guest molecules.13 With all the above considerations in mind, we intend to incorporate a new di-carboxylate ligand based on the anthracene ring by inserting a triple-bond alkyne into the organic spacer (Scheme 1) followed by these considerations: (1) incorporating the alkyne tends to not only elongate the size of the linker, but also increase the conjugated degree of the scaffold, which is essential for the porous-luminescent MOF materials; (2) structural diversity would be exhibited due to the change of the length and non-covalent interactions such as π⋯π interactions; (3) a series of metal ions with different diameters were used to react with the long di-carboxylate ligand to observe the various coordination modes. Herein, we report the synthesis and characteristics of three novel coordination polymers, {[Co2(L)2(H2O)8]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (1), {[Cd2(L)2(H2O)5]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (2), and {[Eu(L)1.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)·(DMF)2}n (3) (H2L = 4,4′-(diethynylanthracene-9,10-diyl) dibenzoic acid ) based on a new dicarboxylate ligand, in which the long linker exhibits different coordination modes with different metal ions.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of 1–3

Compounds 1–3 were prepared under solvothermal conditions by heating a mixture of the corresponding nitrate salt and H2L at 80 °C for several days. All compounds were obtained as red blocks, and their crystal data are listed in Table 1. In the IR spectra of compounds 1–3, the broad peaks near ca. 3400 cm−1 indicate the presence of water molecules. The IR spectra also show characteristic absorption bands mainly attributed to the asymmetric (νasca. 1600 cm−1) and symmetric (νsca. 1385 cm−1) stretching vibrations of the carboxylate groups. No band in the region 1690–1730 cm−1 indicates the complete deprotonation of the carboxylate groups,16 and is consistent with the result of the X-ray diffraction analysis.

Crystal structure of 1

The single-crystal X-ray study of 1 exhibits an oligomer structure constructed by the connection of dinuclear cobalt units and linear di-carboxylate ligands. The complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, and the asymmetric unit contains one complete Co ion, one fully-deprotonated ligand, four coordinated aqua molecules and solvent molecules as water and DMF molecules (Fig. 1). Each cobalt center adopts a slightly distorted octahedral geometry and is bound by six oxygen atoms (five from coordinated aqua molecules and one from mono-dentate carboxyl). Two of the coordinated aqua molecules act as the μ2-bridge and combine two crystallographically symmetric cobalt centers together to form the di-nuclear cobalt unit. The Co–O bond lengths range from 2.016(2) and 2.164(1) Å, corresponding to the typical values in Co(II)–oxygen complexes.14 The di-valence of the Co ion could also be verified by calculating the bond valence sum, as 2.08.15 The di-nuclear metal nodes are further capped by two mono-dentate ligands that are fully de-protonated to assemble the oligomer. The other deprotonated carboxyl was stabilized by the coordinated aqua molecules via hydrogen bonds (Fig. S1†). Each di-carboxylate ligand is interconnected with two others via strong π⋯π interactions (3.626(2) and 3.515(1) Å) along the b-axis direction to form the whole 3D supramolecular structure, shown in Fig. 2.

Crystal structure of 2

Substituting the cobalt ions by cadmium ions gives a double-chained 1D wire structure constructed by the connection of mononuclear Cd ions and bridged di-carboxylate ligands. The complex 2 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pnma, and the asymmetric unit contains two Cd ions located on special positions with half the multiplicity of the general position, one fully-deprotonated ligand, five coordinated aqua molecules and solvent molecules, as water and DMF molecules (Fig. 3). All of the Cd ions adopt a hepta-coordinated mode, and the coordination spheres could be seen as a slightly distorted pentagonal bipyramid, whose apical positions are occupied by aqua molecules, but their equatorial coordination environments are filled with two chelating carboxylate groups and one coordinated water molecule (O4w for Cd1 and O5w for Cd2). In addition, the O4W molecule acts as the μ2-bridge to connect Cd1 and Cd2 together and form the di-nuclear joint. The Cd–O bond lengths range from 2.179(1) and 2.437(2) Å, corresponding to the typical values in Cd(II)–oxygen complexes.16 The di-valence of the Cd ion could also be verified by calculating the bond valence sum as 2.06.15 The di-nuclear metal nodes are further interlinkered by four fully de-protonated di-carboxylate ligands in chelating mode to assemble the double-chained 1D zigzag wires with a strong π⋯π interaction of 3.837(3) Å, shown in Fig. 4. Each 1D wire is further interconnected with adjacent ones via weak interactions, such as π⋯π interactions (4.374(1) Å), along the c-axis direction to form the stable crystal structure.

Fig. 4 Partial perspective of the π⋯π interaction in one chain and between the adjacent double-chained 1D structures of 2 along the crystallographic c-axis direction.

Crystal structure of 3

Interestingly, a distinct 3D crystal structure with 1D channels was constructed by replacing the transitional ions by lanthanide ions. The complex 3 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and the asymmetric unit contains one complete Eu ion, one and half fully-deprotonated ligands and three coordinated aqua molecules (Fig. 5). Each Eu3+ center is bound by eight oxygen atoms, five from five different carboxylate groups of distinct di-carboxylate ligands and three from the coordinated water molecules. The Eu–O bond lengths range from 2.363(2) to 2.527(3) Å, corresponding to the typical values in Eu(III)–carboxylate complexes.17 The tri-valence of the Eu ion could also be verified by calculating the bond valence sum as 2.93.15 Thus, the coordination polyhedron around the central Eu3+ can be visualized as a distorted square antiprism coordination environment formed by four syn-syn μ2 carboxyl groups, one monodentate carboxyl group and three water molecules. Two of the syn-syn μ2 carboxyl groups further interconnect adjacent Eu(III) ions to form a 1D metal–carboxylate chain, which is decorated by bridging di-carboxylate ligands to form the 3D crystal structure along with 1D channels along the a-axis direction (Fig. 6). In addition, the adjacent di-carboxylate ligands interconnect with each other via π⋯π interactions of 3.570(2) and 3.765(3) Å along the a-axis direction (Fig. 7). Calculated using the PLATON routine, the solvent accessible volume in the dehydrated structure of 3 is about 36.3%.

Comparison of the crystal structures of 1–3

Utilizing the same dicarboxylate ligand, reacted with different metal ions, diverse crystal structures were assembled under similar solvothermal reaction conditions. In 1–3, all the dicarboxylate ligands had been deprotonated, but connected different numbers of metal ions (from 1 to 3) with different connection modes (mono-dentate for 1, chelating for 2 and syn-syn bridging/mono-dentate for 3), as shown in Scheme 2. It is obvious that the coordination number and size of the metal ions must play an important role in constructing the diverse crystal structures. The different metal ions tended to assemble various metallic joints possessing different connection modes and numbers, leading to the diverse structures. In addition, along with the increasing size and coordination number of the metal ions, the dicarboxylate ligands would connect more metal ions to form the 3D dimensional structure.
Scheme 2 The coordination modes of the dicarboxylate ligand used in this work.

Luminescent properties of 1–3

The fluorescent emissions properties of H6L1 and 1–3 in the solid state at room temperature were investigated (Fig. 8). The free ligand exhibits the strongest emission peak at 614 nm (λex = 552 nm), which could be presumably attributed to the π–π* transitions.18 The emission spectrum of 1 also shows one strong peak at 619 nm (λex = 326 nm), which may originate from the luminescent properties of the organic linker. The photo-luminescent properties of 2 and 3 show an emission peak at 613 and 614 nm, which might be also ascribed to the features of the organic linker. For 3, no typical luminescent peaks of Eu ions were observed, which might be caused by the strong ligand peak at around 615 nm. That there were no obvious special luminescent peaks of the larger conjugated π-systems of the anthracene ring might be caused by the strong π⋯π interactions in the coordination structures of 1–3.
Fig. 8 The solid-state emission spectra of the ligand and compounds 1–3 at room temperature.

Gas sorption properties of 3

In the MOFs constructed by the linear di-carboxylate ligand, the highly porous and stable framework make 3 a good candidate for gas storage. To check the permanent porosities of 3, N2 sorption isotherms at 77 K were collected for the desolvated samples obtained by soaking 3 in DMF, MeOH, and methylene chloride and then vacuum-drying at room temperature and at 120 °C overnight, respectively. As shown in Fig. 9, 3displays typical Type-I adsorption isotherms with no significant hysteresis between sorption and desorption traces, corresponding to the other ones of micro-porous materials. The sample of 3 adsorbs 140 cm3 g−1 of N2 at 77 K, and the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and Langmuir surface areas are 455 m2 g−1 and 696 m2 g−1, respectively. Low-pressure H2 uptakes of desolvated samples of 3 were continuously determined using volumetric gas adsorption measurements. The adsorption isotherms were fully reversible, and exhibit approximate 115 cm3 g−1 or 1.01 wt% H2 uptake under the conditions of 77 K and 1 bar (Fig. 9). A predicted saturation of 2.12 wt% H2 uptake is given by a fit of the Langmuir–Freundlich equation to the H2 uptake data.19 In addition, the desolvated sample at room temperature also shows CO2 uptake with an absorption amount of 62 cm3 g−1. The polar interactions of the channels caused by the coordinated aqua molecules and functional groups such as alkyne or anthracene tend to retain the gas molecules. The XRD pattern of bulk crystalline samples after the adsorption experiments is similar to the one simulated from the single-crystal structure of 3 (Fig. S2†), which further supported the sustained porosity and stable framework. In terms of these results, the modulation of organic ligands is a very facile way to construct porous architectures.
Fig. 9 The sorption isotherms of N2 and H2 at 77 K and CO2 at 273 K for 3.

Conclusions

A series of coordination polymers based on a new dicarboxylate ligand have been synthesized and structurally characterized. These complexes show diverse structures and dimensionalities from 0D di-nuclear (1) to 1D double-chained wire (2) to 3D porous framework (3). The diversities of the structures obviously indicate that a judicious choice of the metal ions could be an effective way to result in variable SBUs with different geometries, coordinative abilities, and sizes, so as to affect the structural formation of complexes. Additionally, complex 3 also displays modest gas absorption, which exhibits the possibility of the construction of functional frameworks with the ability of catching small molecules by utilizing the derivations based on the scaffold of anthracene.

Experimental section

All reagents were purchased from commercial sources and were used without further purification. FT–IR spectra were recorded as KBr pellets with an Equinox 55 FT–IR spectrophotometer (4000–400 cm–1). Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed under N2 atmosphere (100 mL min−1) with a heating rate of 4 °C min−1 between ambient temperature and 500 °C using a Pyris1 thermogravimetric analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected over the 2θ range 5–60° using an X'Pert PRO automated diffractometer at room temperature, with a step size of 0.02° in 2θ angle. Preparation conditions of 4,4′-(diethynylanthracene-9,10-diyl) dibenzoic acid (H2L) was according to literature methods,20 and the detailed synthesis procedure is listed in ESI.†

Preparation of {[Co2(L)2(H2O)8]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (1)

A solution of Co(NO3)2·3H2O (0.030 g ) in 1 mL of 2 mol L−1 HNO3 was mixed with ligand (H2L) (0.045 g ) in 10 mL of DMF/5 mL of CH3CN. The mixture was sealed in a Pyrex tube and heated to 80°C for 2 d. The red block-shaped crystals obtained were filtered and washed with DMF. The yield of the reaction was ca. 41% based on the ligand. Anal. calcd for C70H66Co2N2O20: H 4.84%, C 61.23%, N 2.04%; found: H 4.02%, C 60.85%, N 2.89%. IR: 3440, 1606, 1529, 1387, 1173, 1095, 1020, 861, 784, 760.

Preparation of {[Cd2(L)2(H2O)5]·(H2O)2·(DMF)2}n (2)

A solution of Cd(NO3)3·7H2O (0.030 g) in 1 mL of 2 mol L−1 HNO3 was mixed with ligand (H2L) (0.010 g ) in 10 mL of DMF/3 mL of H2O. The mixture was sealed in a Pyrex tube and heated to 100°C for 4 d. The red block-shaped crystals obtained were filtered and washed with DMF. The yield of the reaction was ca. 36% based on the ligand. Anal. calcd for C70H60Cd2N2O17: H 4.24%, C 58.96%, N 1.96%; found: H 4.02%, C 58.82%, N 2.53%. IR: 3441, 1591, 1466, 1384, 1163, 1072, 1017, 857, 760.

Preparation of {[Eu(L)1.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)·(DMF)2}n (3)

A solution of Eu(NO3)3·3H2O (0.030 g) in 1 mL of 2 mol L−1 HNO3 was mixed with ligand (H2L) (0.015 g ) in 10 mL of DMF/5 mL of H2O. The mixture was sealed in a Pyrex tube and heated to 85 °C for 4 d. The red block-shaped crystals obtained were filtered and washed with DMF. The yield of the reaction was ca. 36% based on the ligand. Anal. calcd for C54H46EuO12N2: H 4.35%, C 60.79%, N 2.63%; found: H 3.95%, C 59.875%, N 3.14%. IR: 3429, 1607, 1466, 1384, 1170, 1072, 1018, 857, 760.

Low pressure gas sorption measurements

Low-pressure N2 adsorption measurements (up to 1 bar) were performed on Micromeritics ASAP 2020 M+C surface area and pore size analyzer. About 200 mg of methanol solvent-exchanged samples were activated at 90 °C for 12 h by using the “outgas” function of the surface area analyzer. Helium was used for the estimation of the dead volume, assuming that it is not adsorbed at any of the studied temperatures. To provide high accuracy and precision in determining P/P0, the saturation pressure P0 was measured throughout the N2 analyses by means of a dedicated saturation pressure transducer, which allowed us to monitor the vapor pressure for each data point. A part of the N2 sorption isotherm in the P/P0 range 0. 01–0.1 was fitted to the BET equation to estimate the BET surface area and the Langmuir surface area calculation was performed using all data points. The pore size distribution (PSD) was obtained from the DFT model in the Micromeritics ASAP2020 software package (assuming slit pore geometry) based on the N2 sorption at 77 K.

X-Ray structure determinations

Suitable single crystals were selected and mounted onto the end of a thin glass fiber. X-Ray intensity data were measured on a Bruker SMART APEX CCDbased diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.710 73 Å) at 293 K.21 The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least squares using the SHELXTL crystallographic software package.4 Anisotropic displacement parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. Hydrogen atoms residing on parent carbon atoms of L were generated geometrically, while those on the solvent molecules were omitted. In compounds 1–3, because of disorder, the thermal parameters of disordered atoms and Ow atoms were restrained. In addition, due to the highly disordered solvent molecules in compounds 3, the PLATON/SQUEEZE routine was employed to calculate the diffraction contribution from the solvent molecules, and thereby to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities and the zero value of Rint. The large Rint values for 1 and 2 might be caused by the poor quality of data due to the crystal quality and small size. The final formulas were derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analyses data.
