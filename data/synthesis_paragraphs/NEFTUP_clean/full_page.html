Abstract

A single‐chain magnet (SCM) with 1D EO‐azide‐cobalt chains has been previously synthesized from a solvothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2, NaN3, and 1,4‐bis(imidazol‐1‐yl)benzene (bib). Herein the template HNO3 is introduced into the above reaction system and another new coordination polymer (1) is obtained, which exhibits an α‐Po framework with 1D EE‐azide‐cobalt chains. The magnetic property of 1 has been well studied, and the results show spin‐canted antiferromagnetism and slow relaxation of magnetization.

Abstract

A nitrate‐templated 3D coordination polymer with a 1D well‐isolated EE‐azide‐cobalt chain has been obtained by a solvothermal reaction. The magnetic studies of the title compound reveal spin‐canted antiferromagnetism, hysteresis loop, and slow magnetic relaxation.

Introduction

Single‐chain magnets (SCMs), exhibiting slow relaxation of magnetization and quantum tunneling, has currently attracted extensive attention, since they have great potential applications in high‐density information storage devices, quantum computing devices, and molecular spintronics.1 According to Glauber's theory, the key point for achievement of SCMs is to synthesize 1D chains with the features of large uniaxial type magnetic anisotropy and strong intrachain and negligible interchain magnetic interactions.2 Although the rapid growth in the field of molecule magnetism has witnessed dozens of SCMs, successful construction of these molecular magnets is still a great challenge, because it is difficult to experimentally form the perfect 1D architecture.3

Because of its various bridging modes and strong magnetic coupling, azide is widely employed in the construction of molecular magnets.4 Furthermore, a suitable auxiliary ligand, such as rodlike or bulky‐angle ligands, is mostly utilized to diminish interchain interaction and prevent 3D magnetic ordering, which would contribute to the SCM character.5 As a linear long backbone to isolate the magnetic units, rigid bis(imidazole) ligands are good candidates for use in molecular magnets, and we previously reported a SCM with 1D EO‐azide‐cobalt chains by the solvothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2, NaN3, and 1,4‐bis(imidazol‐1‐yl)benzene (bib).6

From the point of view of magnetism, there are three effective strategies for the construction of SCMs: ferromagnetic (FO), ferrimagnetic (FI), and weak ferromagnetic (WF) patterns.2a,6 It is well known that EO‐azide chains are often ferromagnetic, and another basic mode EE‐azide usually is involved in antiferromagnetic (AFM) coupling.7 We then considered whether EE‐azide‐cobalt chains would lead to SCMs. As a result of our ongoing effort on molecular magnets, we herein successfully synthesized a novel coordination polymer {[Co(N3)(bib)2](NO3)}∞ (1) by introducing a template into the reaction system of Co(NO3)2, NaN3, and bib, which, as expected, resulted in 1D EE‐azide‐cobalt chains with slow magnetic relaxation.

Results and Discussion

Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in a monoclinic space group C2/c with one heteroleptic CoII node located at an inversion center. As shown in Figure 1a, each CoII ion lies in an octahedral coordination environment, which is equatorially coordinated by four N atoms from individual bib ligands with Co–N distances of 2.159(2) and 2.140(2) Å and axially occupied by two EE‐azide nitrogen atoms with a Co–N distance of 2.157(2) Å. All these distances are in good agreement with those typically observed (Table S1).8

The EE‐azide ion, acting as a bidentate ligand, bridges the CoII ions to form a 1D chain [Co(N3)]∞ with an intrachain Co···Co distance of 5.92 Å (Figure 1b). It should also be noted that the M–N–N angle associated with the end‐to‐end azide is 127.63(16)° for Co1–N5–N6, which is in accord with the reported values for typical EE‐azide metal complexes.9 The azide bridge links two neighboring Co centers in a trans mode, and the metal–azide–metal torsion angle, another magnetically important geometrical parameter, falls into the normal range for AFM coupling [42.08(14)°].7 In addition, two nearest basal least‐square planes CoN4 that contain two adjacent CoII ions associated with four equatorial nitrogen atoms of the bib ligand are not parallel and form a dihedral angle of 29.88(8)°, much smaller than those of the relevant 1D chains.7a,10

The bidentate bib ligand takes on a trans‐coordinated conformation to connect CoII ions into a 2D (4,4) layer [Co(bib)2]∞ (Figure 1c), which is further perpendicularly pillared by the linear azide‐cobalt chains to form a 3D pillar–layer framework (Figure 1d). If the bib molecule and azide ion are both considered as linkers and the CoII ion is viewed as a node, then the overall topology can be described as a 6‐connected α‐Po framework with a Schäfli symbol of 412·63 (Figure S1). Because of the large (4,4) lattice with a dimension of 13.35 × 13.35 Å, which is also the interchain Co···Co separation, the pcu framework exhibits a 1D quasi‐square channel along the c axis. However, the void space in the aperture is occupied by the NO3– ion, which destroys the porosity (Figure S2).

The NO3– ion not only balances the charge of the positive α‐Po framework, but also behaves as a template when considering the special position along the C2 axis. We previously reported a 2D coordination polymer 2 with a 1D EO‐azide‐cobalt chain, which is synthesized from the solvothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2, NaN3, and bib. Owing to the strong coordination affinity of N3–, the NO3– ion is squeezed out from the coordination sphere of the CoII ion, and 2 exhibits a non‐nitrate architecture. However, upon addition of a large amount of NO3– ions (in this system, the ratio of nnitrate/nazide is about 40:1), the high concentration of NO3– ions results in the construction of a novel coordination polymer 1, which is very different from 2 with respect to the 1D azide‐cobalt chain. The structural diversities arise as a result of the concentration of nitrate, and the NO3– ion plays a template role in the self‐assembly.

The IR spectrum of 1 exhibits a strong absorption band at ca. 1347 cm–1, characteristic of the free NO3– ion,11 which is consistent with the crystal structure as described above. The chemical formula is also confirmed by elemental analysis.

Before magnetic measurements, the phase purity of the sample was confirmed by a comparison of the experimental and simulated powder X‐ray diffraction patterns (Figure S3). The magnetic susceptibility (χ) was measured on a powder sample under 1 kOe in the range 2–300 K (Figure 2). The χmT value per CoII at 300 K is about 2.80 cm3 K mol–1, much larger than the spin‐only value for a magnetic CoII ion with S = 3/2 (1.88 cm3 K mol–1), as expected for CoII systems with a significant contribution from the unquenched orbital momentum in the octahedral field (the 4T1g state). As the temperature is lowered, the χmT product decreases slightly to a minimum value of 0.75 cm3 K mol–1 at 12 K, which indicates AFM interactions within the CoII ions. Upon further cooling, the χmT value increases rapidly until it reaches a maximum value of 2.59 cm3 K mol–1 at 3 K, after which it sharply decreases. The final decrease in the value of χmT may be attributed to the saturation effect, the zero‐field splitting of the CoII ion, or the presence of interchain AFM interactions. The high‐temperature behavior is expected for ferrimagnetic chains and/or compounds containing orbitally degenerated ions, whereas the low‐temperature upsurge indicates ferrimagnetism or spin canting. The data above 50 K follows the Curie–Weiss law with C = 3.46 cm3 K mol–1 and θ = –74.79 K (Figure S4). The negative θ value indicates AFM coupling between metal centers, and the C value falls in the usual range expected for CoII systems.12

According to the structure of compound 1, the system can magnetically be treated as an infinite uniform chain in which magnetic coupling is mediated through the EE‐azide bridges. The interchain magnetic interaction through the organic ligand bib is so weak that this coupling may be negligible in the present system. Consequently, to estimate the strength of the AFM exchange interactions (J) within the 1D azide‐cobalt chain, a noncritical scaling theory with the following phenomenological equation was used to fit the experimental data from 300 to 12 K:13

χmT = A·exp(–E1/kT) + B·exp(–E2/kT)

where A + B merely equals the high‐temperature Curie constant [approximately 3.4 cm3 K mol–1 for the octahedral CoII ion] and E1 and E2 represent the “activation energies” corresponding to the spin‐orbit coupling and the AFM exchange interaction, respectively. The best fit gives an agreement factor R of 4.45 × 10–4 {R = Σ[(χM)obs – (χM)calc]2/Σ[(χM)obs]2}, and the fit parameters obtained with this procedure are: A + B = 3.40 cm3 K mol–1, which is consistent with that obtained from the Curie–Weiss law in the high‐temperature range (C ≈ 3.46 cm3 K mol–1); E1/k = 85.41 K for the effect of the spin‐orbit coupling (E1/k in the order of +100 K); E2/k = 3.82 K found for the AFM exchange interaction corresponding to J = –7.64 K, according to the approximation of χmT = exp(J/2kT).

The negative value of J could further confirm the AFM interactions between the metallic centers. By considering the long pathway through the bib ligands, the AFM interactions should mainly be attributed to the magnetic coupling mediated by the EE‐N3 bridge. The EE‐coordination mode is very common in metal azide compounds, and it generally induces AFM interactions between the metal centers unless M–Nazide–Nazide and M–N3–M torsion angles are very large (the latter is more important and always larger than 45°).7a,14 For this complex, the Co–Nazide–Nazide angles is 127.63(16)° and the Co–N3–Co torsion angle is 42.08(14)°; both fall in the normal AFM range.

Below 12 K, an abrupt increase is observed in the χmT vs. T diagram, which can be attributed to spin‐canted antiferromagnetism.15 It is well known that spin canting may arise from two mechanisms: single‐ion magnetic anisotropy and antisymmetric magnetic exchange, which excludes the inversion center between adjacent spins.16 Obviously in 1, because of the centrosymmetric character, only the first factor should be responsible for the canting phenomenon, which is in accord with the strong anisotropic feature of the CoII ion.

The field dependences of magnetization (M) have been determined at 1.8 K in the range 0–70 kOe (Figure 3). The value of M rapidly increases at low fields, and then increases linearly as the field strength is increased until a value of 0.65 Nβ at 70 kOe is reached, which is much lower than expected (2–3 Nβ per CoII ion). This suggests that spin canting could exist in this system. Upon demagnetization, a small hysteresis loop is also observed with a coercitive field of 30 G and a remnant magnetization of 0.03 Nβ.

To examine the long‐range magnetic phase transition, the temperature dependency of alternating‐current (ac) magnetic susceptibility was collected at an ac field of 3.5 Oe with different oscillating frequencies. Interestingly, a frequency‐dependent out‐of‐phase signal (χ″) is observed (non‐zero values), which indicates the onset of slow magnetization (Figure 4). However, the frequency‐dependent peaks of the ac susceptibility signals are invisible in the measured temperature region (>1.8 K), which likely appear beyond the operating limit of our SQUID (Figure S5); nevertheless, after careful inspection, it is found that χ″ peaks shift toward high temperature following the oscillating frequency.

Up until now, there are dozens of azide‐cobalt SCMs, and most belong to the EO‐azide mode as a result of the strong intrachain FO coupling.4a,5a,6,17 Compounds with the EE‐azide mode could also be used to construct SCMs because it can efficiently transmit WF coupling, and these SCMs have been reported later and are relatively rare.2a,7b,10,18 However, the number of these SCMs is increasing rapidly, and it is believed that more WF SCMs will appear because it is easy to induce non‐collinearity of the anisotropy axes because of the fact that anisotropy or asymmetry is always found in molecular magnetism.

Conclusions

In summary, we have prepared and characterized a nitrate‐templated coordination polymer, which exhibits a pillar‐layer α‐Po framework with a 1D EE‐azide‐cobalt chain. The frequency dependence of ac signals and magnetization hysteresis loop suggest SCM‐like behavior for this complex. The researches enrich our knowledge of SCMs and confirm the potential application of rod‐like spacer in the construction of molecular magnetic material.

Experimental Section

Synthesis of 1 (Solvothermal Method): A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.12 mmol, 35 mg), NaN3 (0.2 mmol, 13 mg), and bib (0.1 mmol, 21 mg) was suspended in the solvent mixture (H2O/CH3OH = 3:4, 12 mL). After addition of nitrate acid solution (0.5 mL of a 68 % solution), the suspension was heated in a Teflon‐lined autoclave (25 mL) at 140 °C for 2 d. After the autoclave was cooled to room temperature at 10 °C h–1, pink block crystals suitable for single‐crystal X‐ray crystallographic analysis were collected in ca. a 10 % yield (based on bib).
