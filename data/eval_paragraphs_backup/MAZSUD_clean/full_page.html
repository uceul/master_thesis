Abstract

Breathing effect: Using two triazole homologues (1H‐benzotriazole and 1,2,3‐1H‐triazole), two isoreticular microporous Zn–benzenedicarboxylate frameworks 1 and 2 with reverse dynamic features are presented, in which different sized triazole ligands effectively control the state of breathing of two flexible frameworks (see figure).

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with various structures have been object of tremendous attention during the past decades because of their potential applications in catalysis, such as gas storage and separation and sensing.1–6 Of particular interest is that some of these MOFs exhibit structural flexibility and unusual dynamic behaviors, which are typically known in the MIL‐53 family.1 The ‘‘breathing effect′’ of the MIL‐53 framework arises from solvent removal, subsequent pore filling with gas/vapor molecules, or from temperature variation. Up to date, there are several other reports describing structural breathing and gate‐opening processes in MOFs.7–9 Some flexible MOFs are good for hysteretic sorption or stepwise gas uptake.9 The dynamic responses include either expansion or shrinkage of the pores, and each state is mainly dependent on the presence or absence of the guest molecules and changes in temperature. Other efforts on effectively controlling the expanded phase or shrunken phase are rarely explored.

In this work, we present a new idea for this purpose by employing a couple of triazole ligands, 1H‐benzotriazole (btz) and 1,2,3‐1H‐triazole (tz) (Scheme 1). We occasionally found that such two homologues with different sizes can effectively tune the pore size or pore geometry into two reverse states. Two isoreticular zinc‐1,4‐benzenedicarboxylate (bdc) frameworks with btz or tz as co‐ligands, namely Zn3(btz)2(bdc)2⋅x(DMA) (1, DMA=N,N‐dimethylacetamide) and Zn3(tz)2(bdc)2⋅x(DMF) (2, DMF=N,N‐dimethylformamide), are successfully synthesized under solvothermal conditions and reported here. Both compounds have the same structural topology and contain (4,4) Zn‐bdc layers doubly pillared by the triazole ligands. Despite the fact that they are constructed from dual rigid organic ligands, an interesting “breathing effect” similar to the one found in MIL‐53 is observed for both of them. Remarkably, compound 1 with big btz ligands is at an expanding stage and tends to shrink after solvent removal, whereas compound 2 with small tz ligands is in a shrinking phase and exhibits unusual stepwise gas uptake behavior.

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction reveal that compounds 1 and 2 crystallize in different space groups (C2 m for 1 and P for 2), although they have very similar structural features. Both structures consist of (4,4) Zn‐bdc layers which are further doubly connected by btz in 1 or tz in 2 to form neutral three‐dimensional (3D) frameworks. In the structure of 1, there are two independent Zn centers with distinct coordination geometries. One Zn center is octahedrally coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four bdc ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two btz ligands, whereas another Zn center is tetrahedrally coordinated by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two bdc ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two btz ligands. The octahedral Zn center is located at a special symmetry site, and it is closely surrounded by two symmetry‐related tetrahedral Zn centers, generating a trinuclear unit Zn3(COO−)4 with the shortest Zn⋅⋅⋅Zn distance being 3.588 Å. Each trinuclear Zn3(COO−)4 unit is connected to four adjacent units by the μ4‐bdc ligands, leading to a (4,4) Zn‐bdc layer parallel to ab plane (Figure 1 a). Each btz ligand adopts all three N donors to link one octahedral Zn center and two tetrahedral Zn centers. The trinuclear Zn3(COO−)4 units are doubly bridged by the btz ligands into a chain along the c axis (Figure 1 b). Combining both connectivity of Zn‐bdc and Zn‐btz, a 3D neutral framework with large quadrangular channels along the c axis is formed (Figure 1 c). Each channel is occupied by structurally disordered solvent DMA molecules. Interestingly, the benzene rings of btz ligands almost separate each channel into two triangular parts. The total solvent‐accessible volume for 1 is estimated to be 48.8 % using the PLATON program.10

The detailed structure of 2 is very similar to that of 1 except small tz ligand instead of large btz ligand (Figure 1 d–f). Compared to 1, it is expected for 2 to have much large solvent‐accessible volume. However, the total solvent‐accessible volume for 2 is only 50.0 % and a little higher than that for 1. The reason is closely associated with the shrinking structure of 2 as shown in Figure 1 f. It is obvious that the pore aperture in 1 and 2 are totally different (Figure 2). The free pore apertures (taking into account the van der Waals radius of the atoms) for 1 and 2 are approximately 13.5×8.5 Å and 14.5×5.0 Å, respectively. Here, the benzene rings of btz ligands effectively support expanded pores in 1, whereas 2 with small tz ligands shrinks the pores.

Although the detailed coordination connectivity in both compounds 1 and 2 is very different from the structure of MIL‐53,1a the dominating structural feature for them is somewhat similar to MIL‐53. Firstly, the inorganic M‐OH (M=Al3+ or Cr3+) chain in MIL‐53 is replaced by a Zn–tz chain. Secondly, the chains are further linked by the bdc ligands into a 3D framework with 1D channels. But the Zn‐bdc connectivity in MIL‐53 is three‐dimensional, and that in 1 or 2 is only two‐dimensional and further pillared by double triazole ligands. Apart from these close structural features with MIL‐53, both compounds also possess an interesting “breathing effect”.

To attain a more complete evacuation and keep the structural integrality, DMA and DMF were exchanged with dichloromethane (CH2Cl2) by soaking crystals of 1 and 2 in CH2Cl2 overnight, followed by evacuation at room temperature for 24 h. The dichloromethane‐exchanged and activated compounds were characterized by thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) and powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements to assure full activation was achieved. In the PXRD pattern of activated samples, several peaks shift and change, which are consistent with the flexibility presented by both frameworks (Figure 3).

To confirm the permanent microporosity and breathing effect, gas‐adsorption measurements (N2, CO2, and H2) of 1 and 2 were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface‐area and pore‐size analyzer. The CH2Cl2 activated samples of 1 and 2 were degassed at 60 °C until no weight change prior to the measurements, respectively. The N2 adsorption/desorption studies revealed that both compounds have reversible type‐I isotherms, indicating that they are microporous. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller and Langmuir surface areas for 1 are 210.8 and 280.6 m2 g−1, respectively (Figure 4), and those for 2 are 801.2 and 1123.8 m2 g−1 (Figure 5), respectively. In compound 1, a single data point at relative pressure 0.01 gives a micropore volume of 0.085 cm3g−1 by the Horvath–Kawazoe equation, whereas relative pressure 0.012 gives a micropore volume of 0.024 cm3g−1 for 2. Such big different pore volumes at low pressure are maybe related with the flexible nature of both frameworks.

Compared to the normal N2 sorption isotherm of 1, what the N2 isotherm of 2 shows is that there is a step in the isotherm. Interestingly, the adsorption of 2 is very limited below the pressure 0.012 bar, but at that point there is an abrupt increase from 15 cm3g−1 to 150 cm3g−1. To our knowledge, such sharp jump in N2 uptake has rarely been observed before.7, 8

Intriguing dynamic adsorption behavior is presented in the low pressure CO2 isotherm of 2 recorded at 273 K and 298 K, respectively (Figure 6). The CO2 isotherm at 273 K shows a sudden increase at P=0.31 bar and exhibits a large hysteresis. Such step and hysteresis in the isotherm is due to structural changes during adsorption.8b, 8d The atypical CO2 adsorption behavior at 273 K encouraged us to further measure its CO2 isotherm at room temperature. Notably, the adsorption step and hysteresis move to higher absolute pressure (P=0.68 bar) with increasing temperature. Looking first at only the 273 K isotherm, it is difficult to confirm if the jump in uptake is an effect of pore opening or a true step in the isotherm. As we expected that the isotherm measured at room temperature also clearly displays an inflection in the step. The results of the CO2 adsorption experiments at two different temperatures suggest that solid solutions of 2 exhibit a range of adsorption profile with gate‐open effect.7c In the isotherms that display the step and hysteresis, the hysteresis loop is nearly closed to rejoin the adsorption branch; all of the adsorbate molecules can be removed from the framework. In contrast, the CO2 sorption of 1 at 273 K shows reversible type‐I isotherm, and no gate‐opening process is observed (Figure 4). The CO2 uptake value of 1 at 273 K is 50 cm3 g−1, which is lower than that of 2 (57 cm3 g−1) at 273 K.

The dynamic framework behavior responsible for the stepped CO2 and N2 adsorption could conceivably take any of several forms, including reversible: 1) framework collapse, 2) pillar rotation, and 3) pore expanding or shrinking. Indeed, powder X‐ray diffraction of samples 1 and 2 after finishing gas adsorption are consistent with those for activated 1 and 2 (Figure 3). So the TGA and powder X‐ray diffraction of samples enables us to rule out framework collapse as an important contributor. The fact that btz acts not only as pillar linker but also linker of trinuclear Zn3(btz)2 building block likewise strongly argue against 2. We believe that pore expanding is a very important contributor resulting in dynamic framework behavior of 2. Most probably the increased thermal energy of both the framework and guest molecule leading to pore expanding allows CO2 and N2 to open the gate more easily.

The H2 sorption isotherms at 77 and 87 K for both 1 and 2 were also investigated (Figure S3 and 4 in the Supporting Information). However, such gated gas adsorption is not observed in the H2 isotherms of 2. The H2 uptake value of 2 at 77 K is 70 cm3 g−1, which is lower than that of 1 (105 cm3 g−1). These results might illustrate that small H2 molecules are difficult to bring about pore expansion for 2 at 1 atm.

In summary, by employment of two triazole homologues (btz and tz), two isoreticular microporous Zn‐bdc frameworks 1 and 2 with reverse dynamic features were successfully prepared. Different sized btz and tz ligands effectively controlled the state of breathing of two frameworks. 1 with btz ligands is ready to shrink, and 2 with tz ligands is ready to expand. The step and hysteresis in CO2 sorption of 2 further reveal interesting dynamic behavior and breathing effect of this framework.

Experimental Section

Synthesis of Zn3(btz)2(bdc)2⋅x(DMA) (1): A mixture of Zn(NO3)2⋅6H2O (0.1600 g, 0.55 mmol), pyrazine (0.0150 g, 0.19 mmol), 1H‐benzotriazole (0.0800 g, 0.67 mmol), 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid (0.1000 g, 0.60 mmol), methanol (2.5 mL), and N,N‐dimethylacetamide (7.5 mL) in a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel was heated at 120 °C for 5 days, and then cooled to room temperature. Colorless transparent crystals were thus obtained (70 % yield based on zinc).

Synthesis of Zn3(tz)2(bdc)2⋅x(DMF) (2): A mixture of Zn(NO3)2⋅6H2O (0.0800 g, 0.27 mmol), pyrazine(0.0150 g 0.19 mmol), 1,2,3–1H‐triazole (0.0400 g, 0.58 mmol), 1,4‐benzenedicarboxylic acid (0.0500 g 0.30 mmol), N,N‐dimethylformamide (7.5 mL) in a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel was heated at 120 °C for 5 days, and then cooled to room temperature. Colorless transparent crystals were thus obtained (83 % yield based on zinc).

Crystal data for 1: C28H16N6O11Zn3, M=808.58, monoclinic, a=14.6607(6) Å, b=17.0094(6) Å, c=9.2069(4) Å, α=90.00°, β=100.892(4)°, γ=90.00°, V=2254.56(16) Å3, T=293(2) K, space group C2m, Z=2, 6933 reflections measured, 1956 independent reflections (Rint=0.1338). The goodness of fit on F2 was 1.170.
