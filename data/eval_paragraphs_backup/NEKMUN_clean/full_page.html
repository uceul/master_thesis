Four novel coordination polymers of transition metals built using a semi rigid oxygen donor ligand: Crystal structures, novel topology and emission studies

Author links open overlay panelRupaliMishraaMusheerAhmadbMadhav RamTripathiaRay J.Butcherc
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.poly.2012.10.045Get rights and content

Abstract

Four new coordination polymers namely, [Cd3(L1)2(DMA)4]n (1), [Mn(HL1)(H2O)3]n (2), [Mn(H2L2)]n (3) and {[Co2(HL2)(L2)]·(Et4N)·(1.5H2O)}n (4) have been synthesized with semi-rigid ether bridged tricarboxylic acid ligands H3L1 and H3L2 [H3L1 = 5-(4-carboxy-phenoxy)-isophthalic acid and H3L2 = 5-(2-carboxy-phenoxy)-isophthalic acid] with various transition metal ions using hydro(solvo)thermal techniques. All the coordination polymers have been structurally characterized by IR spectroscopy, elemental analysis, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and X-ray single crystallography. Single crystal X-ray studies show that these coordination polymers contain homometallic clusters varying from dimeric to trimeric units except 2 which is a linear monomeric. On reacting para-isomer of the ligand H3L1 with metal ions, 2D and 3D supramolecular structures have been obtained while with the ortho-isomer H3L2 dimeric 2D and 3D framework were obtained. Topological analysis reveals that 1 and 3 are binodal (3,6)-connected kgd net, 2 is also a binodal (3,3)-connected honey-comb type hcb net, while 4 shows unprecedented (3,3,6)-connected pkb5 topological trinodal-network. Interestingly, 3 and 4 exhibits unusual coordination modes involving one of carbonyl O atom of an acid functional group. Moreover, in 4 ethereal O atom is also coordinated to one of the metal center. All complexes show high thermal stability, thus losing coordinated and lattice solvent molecules in the range, ∼100–200 °C. Upon excitation at 325 nm, compounds 1–4 exhibit solid-state luminescences at room temperature.

Graphical abstract

Four novel coordination polymers have been synthesized hydro(solvo)thermally using an ether bridged tricarboxylic acid ligand. Complex 1 is a 3D coordination polymer composed of trinuclear Cd units and complex 2 is a linear monomeric structure, while complexes 3–4 generated dimeric units. Solid-state luminescent behavior of the complexes 1–4 have been achieved at room temperature.

1. Introduction

Coordination polymers (CPs) have been a subject of immense current interest in research activities in many laboratories due to their diverse properties and highly tunable construction. CPs are potential candidates for investigating numerous uses including gas adsorption [1], [2], [3], molecular sieves [4], [5], ion exchange [6], [7], heterogeneous catalysis [8], [9], host–guest induced separation [10], [11] magnetic materials [12], [13], [14], luminescence materials [15], [16], [17] and so on. The design possibilities of CPs depends upon the organic connector and the coordination tendencies of metal ions which gives rise to an innumerable number of coordination polymeric structures quite often endowed with novel structural features as well as the intriguing nature of molecular connectivities and topologies.

A variety of multi-carboxylate ligands are often selected as multifunctional organic linkers because of their abundant coordination modes to metal ions, allowing different type of structural topologies and because of their ability to act as H-bond acceptors and donors, depending on the number of deprotonated carboxylic groups to assemble supramolecular structures [18], [19], [20], [21]. Carboxylate groups can take part in coordination as bridging ligands, and they usually have the tendency to form metal–carboxylate clusters, such as dinuclear, trinuclear, and even higher nuclear clusters. Having various bridging modes between metal ions, the carboxylate group has been widely used in the synthesis of CPs [22], [23], [24], [25], [26], [27], [28]. The high thermal stability of polycaboxylate complexes enables removal of solvent molecules from the lattice without destruction of the original framework [29], [30]. A vast majority of CPs with structural variations are known with both transition metal ions and carboxylate donor ligands connected to rigid aromatic groups. Recently, non-rigid ligands are usually the typical building reactants in the multidimensional networks. The conformational freedom nature of the flexible ligand may provide more possibility for the construction of unusual topologies and structures [31].

Herein, we report the synthesis and crystal structural studies of four coordination polymers, [Cd3(L1)2(DMA)4]n (1), [Mn(HL1)(H2O)3]n (2), [Mn(H2L2)]n (3) and {[Co2(HL2)(L2)]·(Et4N)·(1.5H2O)}n (4) with semi rigid ether bridged tricarboxylic acid, H3L1 and H3L2 ligands (Scheme 1) that incorporates flexibility in the construction of co-ordination polymers due to the rotation of single C–O bond. Solid-state photoluminescence behaviors were achieved at room temperature.

2. Experimental

2.1. General methods and materials

The transition metal salts Cd(NO3)2·6H2O, Mn(OAc)2·4H2O, MnCl2·4H2O, CoCl2·6H2O, 5-hydroxyisophthalic acid, 2-fluorobenzonitrile and 4-fluorobenzonitrile were purchased from Aldrich, USA and used as received. All solvents, N,N′-dimethylacetamide(DMA), Tetra-ethyl ammonium hydroxide(TEAH), sodium hydroxide and K2CO3 were procured from S.D. Fine Chemicals, India. All the solvents were purified prior to use following standard methods. Infrared spectra were recorded on Perkin-Elmer Model 1320 spectrometer (KBr disk, 400–4000 cm−1). 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a JEOL-ECX 500 FT (500 and 125 MHz respectively) instrument in DMSO-d6 with Me4Si as the internal standard. ESI-mass spectra were recorded on a WATERS Q-TOF Premier mass spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were recorded on Mettler Toledo Star System (heating rate of 5 °C/min). Microanalyses for the compounds were obtained using a CE-440 elemental analyzer (Exeter Analytical Inc.). Solid-state photo-excitation and emission spectra were recorded using double UV–Vis spectrophotometer (shimadzu, UV-2450) and Jobin Yvon Horiba Fluorolog-3 spectrofluorimeter at room temperature. Powder X-ray diffraction (Cu Kα radiation, scan rate 3 °C/min, 293 K) was performed on a Bruker D8 Advance Series 2 powder X-ray diffractometer.

2.2. Synthesis

2.2.1. Synthesis of 5-(4-carboxy-phenoxy)-isophthalic acid (H3L1)

The ligand was synthesized following an earlier reported [32] procedure with slight modifications. 5-Hydroxy-isophthalic acid diethyl ester (4 g, 16.8 mmol), dry K2CO3 (2.14 g, 24.12 mmol) was mixed in a 50 mL round-bottom flask under an inert atmosphere and then 10 mL of dry DMF was added to it. The mixture was stirred for a while at 80 °C followed by addition of 4-fluoro-benzonitrile (2.4 g, 18.4 mmol) and the resulting mixture was stirred for 24 h in an oil-bath at 80 °C. The resulting solution was poured into ice-cold water (150 mL) whereupon a white solid precipitated that was collected by filtration and dried in air. This white solid was hydrolysed by refluxing in aqueous ethanol containing 6(N) NaOH solution (100 mL). Finally, the resulting solution was acidified with 6(N) HCl solution to obtain a white precipitate. After keeping it overnight in the freezer, it was collected by filtration and dried at 80 °C. Yield: 1.9 g (80%). This compound did not melt up to 300 °C. 3. Results and discussion

The semi-rigid tripodal ligands, H3L1 and H3L2 have been designed to provide rotational liberty for the aromatic carboxylate that can facilitate bridging of transition metal ions. The coordination polymers, once isolated are found to be air-stable and also insoluble in common organic solvents as well as in water. The IR spectra of 1–4 show strong absorption bands between ∼1450 and 1627 cm−1, attributable to coordinated carboxylate groups (See Figs. S9–S12, Supporting information). The broad peak centering in the region, 3205–3395 cm−1 indicates [40], [41] the presence of coordinated water molecules in complex 2. A broad hump in 2 and 3 clearly, indicates the presence of free carboxylic acid groups.

3.1. Crystal structure of [Cd3(L1)2(DMA)4]n 1

Compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P1¯ with the asymmetric unit consisting of two crystallographically independent Cd(II) ions (one having full occupancy and second one with half occupancy), one L13− ligand and two metal coordinated N,N′-dimethylacetamide (DMA) molecules. Three carboxylate groups of the L13− ligand bind the metal ions as μ6-η1:η1:η1:η1:η2:η1 modes, both chelating and bridging fashion (Fig. 1a).

Cd1 shows distorted octahedral CdO6 coordination from six different carboxylate of L13− in bridging fashion (Cd1–O = 2.224(6)–2.287(6) Å. The Cd2 has a penta-coordinated distorted trigonal bipyramidal CdO5 coordination (Cd2–O = 2.213(6)–2.290(6) Å) from three bridging carboxylate O and two oxygen atoms (Cd–O = 2.262(7)–2.287(6) Å) from two N,N’-dimethylacetamide(DMA) (Fig. 2a). One of the carboxylate O occupying the axial position while two carboxylate O and two N,N′-dimethylacetamide oxygen sitting on equatorial plane. The five coordinate geometry about the Cd(II) ion is intermediate between a trigonal bipyramid (ideal tau = 1) and a square pyramid (ideal tau = 0) with a tau factor of 0.90. Therefore, the geometry about Cd(II) center is closer to a trigonal bipyramidal structure than a square based pyramid [42]. All the Cd–O bond distances are within the range reported for octahedral Cd(II) complexes [43], [44].

Interestingly, complex 1 consists of linear trimetallic cluster in which Cd1 sits on center of symmetry. The Cd⋯Cd distance in the trimeric unit is found to be 3.586 Å (Fig. 3b). These trimeric units extend to 2D framework via bridging carboxylate groups of L13− ligands (Fig. 3a). Furthermore, the intricate H-bonding leads to an overall 3D architecture as given in Fig. 4.

Topology analysis of 1 using the topos software [45], [46], [47] indicates that it can be described as a binodal (3,6)-connected kgd topology (Fig. 5), in which each Cd(II) ion is considered as a 6-connected node while each L13− ligand as 3-connected linker. The point symbol for this network is {43}2{46.66.83}.

3.1.1. Crystal structure of [Mn(HL1)(H2O)3]n 2

Compound 2 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pna21 with the asymmetric unit consists of one Mn(II) ion, one L13- ligand and three coordinated water molecules. The carboxylate groups of the HL12− ligand bind the metal ions as μ3-η1:η0:η1:η1:η0:η0 modes, in bridging fashion (Fig. 1b). Each Mn(II) ion shows distorted octahedral MnO6 coordination from three carboxylate O (Mn1–O = 2.083(2)–2.216(2) Å) from three independent HL12− ligands, three O (Mn1–O = 2.185(2)–2.229(2) Å) (Fig. 2b) from coordinated water molecules. All the Mn–O bond distances are within the range reported for octahedral Mn(II) complexes [48], [49], [50].

The zig-zag supramolecular framework (Fig. 6) is mainly stabilized by H⋯O interactions (2.529(5)–2.686(5) Å) originating from the H-atoms of coordinated water molecules and O atom of free carboxylic acid group. Furthermore, the weak and multipoint π⋯π interactions and strong H-bonding leads to an overall 3D architecture as given in Fig. 7. Selected hydrogen bond distances (Å) and bond angles (°) in 2 are summarized Table S2 in Supporting information.

Topologically, each Mn(II) ion is considered as a 3-connected node while each HL12− ligand as 3-connected linker. On the basis of this simplification, 2 is having a 2-dimensional non-interpenetrated (3,3)-connected hcb net (Fig. 8). The point symbol for this network is {63}.

3.1.2. Crystal structure of [Mn(H2L2)]n (3)

Complex 3 crystallizes in a monoclinic system with space group P21/n. Asymmetric unit of 3 consist of one Mn(II) ion and one H2L2− ligand. Mn(II) ion is situated at the crystallographic inversion center. The carboxylate groups of the H2L2− ligand bind the metal ions as μ3-η1:η0:η1:η1:η0:η0 modes, in bridging fashion (Fig. 1c). Mn1 shows distorted octahedral MnO6 coordination from six different carboxylate of H2L2− ligand in bridging fashion (Mn–O = 2.148(2)–2.2466(16) Å (Fig. 2c). The H2L2− ligand shows unusual kind of coordination mode involving one of carbonyl (–CO) O atom while one of the acid (–COOH) group remain free as shown in Fig. 1c. The linear dimeric Mn(II) unit, which is further extended by the H2L2− ligand to give an overall 2D coordination polymer (Fig. 9). The Mn⋯Mn distance in the dimeric unit is found to be 4.791 Å. The 2D architecture further, give rise to an overall 3D supramolecular framework (Fig. 10) involving very strong H-bonding interactions between the free acidic group (H⋯O = 1.844 Å) of the ligands. All the Mn–O bond distances are within the range reported for octahedral Mn(II) complexes [48], [49], [50].

Topologically, each Mn(II) ion is considered as a 6-connected node while each H2L2− ligand as 3-connected linker. On the basis of this simplification, 3 is having a 3D non-interpenetrated (3,6)-connected kgd net (Fig. 11). The point symbol for this network is {43}2{46.66.83}.

3.1.3. Crystal structure of {[Co2(HL2)(L2)]·(Et4N)·(1.5H2O)}n 4

Complex 4 crystallizes in a monoclinic system with space group P121/c with the asymmetric unit consists of two crystallographically independent Co(II) ions, one L23− ligand, one HL22− ligand, and one tetra-ethylammonium cation and one and half water molecules in lattice space. The carboxylate groups of the ligand shows two type of coordination modes. The carboxylate groups of the H1L22− ligand bind the metal ions as μ4-η1:η1:η1:η0:η1:η0 modes, in bridging fashion only (Fig. 1d and e) while L23− ligand, the carboxylate groups bind the metal ions as μ6-η1:η0:η1:η1:η1:η2 modes, in both bridging as well as chelating fashion. Crystallographically, it is observed that the ethereal O atom of the ligand L23− also binds with one of the metal ion. Co1 ion shows distorted octahedral CoO6 coordination from six carboxylate O (Co1–O, 2.007(3)–2.319(3) Å from both HL22− and L23− ligands. Co2 ion also exhibits distorted octahedral CoO6 coordination from six different carboxylate O (Co1–O, 2.036(3)–2.307(3) Å from both HL22− and L23− ligands (Fig. 2d). All the Co–O bond distances are within the range as reported for cobalt based coordination polymers [51].

Interestingly, in case of HL22− ligand one of carbonyl (–CO) O atom coordinated to the metal ions as shown in Fig. 1e. The Co⋯Co distance in the dimeric unit is found to be 3.320 Å. The dimeric unit in complex 4 is extended by both L23− and HL22− ligands to give a 3D supramolecular framework as depicted in Fig. 12.

Topologically, each Co(II) ion is considered as a 6-connected node, while both H1L22− and L23− ligands as 3-connected linker. On the basis of this simplification, 4 is having a 3D non-interpenetrated unprecedented (3,3,6)-connected pkb5 net (Fig. 13). The point symbol for this network is {4.6.8}{42.6}{43.64.88}.

3.2. Photoluminescence studies

Coordination polymers especially those with d10 transition metal centers are immensely studied nowadays for photoluminescent properties and also for its potential applications in chemical sensors, photochemistry, as well as electroluminescent display [52], [53], [54]. Metal coordination frameworks have been reported to exhibit their high thermal stability and the ability to tune the emission wavelength as well as intensity of the free-organic compounds after coordination with metals. The photoluminescent behaviors of compound 1–4 and organic ligands H3L1 and H3L2 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature, and the results are depicted in Fig. 14. Irradiation of crystalline compounds of 1–4 with ultraviolet light (λex = 325 nm) at room temperature resulted in broad unsymmetrical emission as a pair of bands at 432, 423, 436 and 418 nm and also accompanied by other bands at 403, 391, 397 and 400 nm, respectively. The pronounced variations in the luminescent spectra, particularly in the intensity of emission maxima of 1–4 may be due to differences in their compositions, crystal packing and nature of metal in clusters. However, the emission at lower wavelength could be due to intra-ligand interactions as the profile of the emission band is quite similar to that of the free organic ligands, while the longer wavelength bands probably originate from ligand-to-metal charge transfer. In case of 1 and 4, possibility of cluster-centered transitions cannot be ruled out because of the metal⋯metal separation in the cluster is less then ∼3.6 Å. Compared with the ligands, the complexes 1–4 exhibit enhancement in luminescent intensity. Usually, upon complexation with metal, enhancement in photolumniscent intensities have been observed as the metal cluster effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiation-less decay [55]. An obvious enhancement in the fluorescent intensity is observed for 1, it could be attributed to the presence of d10 transition metal centers. But, in case of 2–4 the quenching of photolumniscent intensity have been observed; possibly be due to the presence of paramagnetic metal clusters. Moreover, in complex 2 the quenching of photolumniscent emission is very pronounced due to presence of some coordinated aqua molecules [56].

3.3. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

Thermal analyses of the complexes 1–4 were carried out in N2 atmosphere at the rate of 5 °C per minute. Complexes 1–4 exhibit different type of weight loss pattern in thermograms (see Supporting information). TGA of compound 1 shows a weight loss of ∼27.92% (expected = 27.8%) within the temperature range 120–140 °C that corresponds to the loss of two coordinated DMA molecules and the mass remained constant up to 420 °C beyond it starts to decompose. On the other hand, TGA of complex 2 exhibits a gradual weight loss of ∼13.20% (expected = 13.25%) within the temperature range 100–130 °C due to loss of three coordinated water molecules, the weight remained constant up to 400 °C. Complex 3 shows no weight loss up to 410 °C and started to decompose after this temperature. Complex 4 shows a loss of ∼18.00% (expected = 18.1%) due to removal of lattice molecules, and the mass remained intact up to 410 °C. (See Figs. S13–S16, Supporting information).

3.4. PXRD analysis

Powder X-ray diffraction patterns of 1–4 are in excellent agreement with the simulated patterns. It exhibits that the crystal structures are truly representative of the bulk phase. The differences in intensity could be attributed to different orientations of the powder samples (Supporting information, S17–S20).

4. Conclusion

In summary, we have synthesized four different coordination polymers of Cd(II), Mn(II) and Co(II) ions having different metal-nuclearity using a semi-rigid bent ether-bridged tricarboxylate ligand under hydro(solvo)thermal conditions. The para-isomer of the ligand give rise to two different coordination polymers with Cd(II) and Mn(II) ions, former is a 2D linear trinuclear metal cluster while later is a 3D mononuclear structure. Similarly, the ortho-isomer also give rise to two different Mn(II) and Co(II)-based supramolecular framework having binuclear metal clusters. Interestingly, the complex 4 exhibits a novel trinodal pkb5 topological net while 1–3 give rise to binodal kgd and honey-comb hcb type network. Solid-state emission studies of the complexes 1–4 have been achieved at room temperature. Such type of cluster containing coordination polymers with transition metal and lanthanides ions are under investigation with this and other ditopic pyridine-based coligands in our laboratory.
