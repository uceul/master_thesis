pH-dependent two novel 3D polynuclear cobalt(II) cluster-based metal–organic frameworks constructed from a tri-pyridyl-bis-amide and a polycarboxylate: assembly, structures and properties†

Xiuli Wang *, Jingjing Huang , Lianli Liu , Guocheng Liu , Hongyan Lin , Juwen Zhang , Naili Chen and Yun Qu
Department of Chemistry, Bohai University, Liaoning Province Silicon Materials Engineering Technology Research Centre, Jinzhou, 121000, P. R. China. E-mail: wangxiuli@bhu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-416-3400158; Tel: +86-416-3400158

Received 1st April 2013 , Accepted 23rd May 2013

First published on 23rd May 2013

The hydrothermal reaction of CoCl2 with N,N′-bis(pyridine-3-yl)pyridine-3,5-dicarboxamide (L) and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTC) mixed ligands affords two novel polynuclear cobalt cluster-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), [Co3(L)2(BTC)2(H2O)2]·6H2O (1) and {[Co2(L)(BTC)(OH)]·2.5H2O}2 (2). Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the CoII ions in complex 1 are connected by BTC anions with a four-coordinated mode to form a two dimensional (2D) undulating layer based on dinuclear [Co2(COO)4] units, which is extended into a 3D (3, 4, 4, 5)-connected MOF by the L ligand with μ3-bridging coordination mode. In complex 2, the BTC ligand displays a six-coordinated mode to connect CoII ions into a 2D corrugated layer derived from tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2(COO)6] motifs, and the layers are further extended into a 3D (3, 8)-connected tfz-d MOF via μ2-bridging L ligands. The title complexes exhibit a (42·63·85)2(42·63·8)2(6·82)2(62·84) topology for 1 and a (43)2(46·618·84) topology for 2. The pH value, as well as the various coordination modes of the tri-pyridyl-bis-amide L and BTC ligands, play a crucial role in the construction of the diverse topological structures of 1 and 2. Complex 1 exhibits excellent photocatalytic activity for dye degradation under UV light. In addition, the electrochemical behaviour and magnetic and fluorescent properties of the two complexes have also been investigated.

Introduction

The growing interest in the crystal engineering of polynuclear metal cluster-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) is propelled by their intriguing topological structures as well as the potential application as functional materials in luminescence, gas adsorption, magnetism, catalysis and so on.1,2 Up to now, four main approaches have been developed for the construction of polymetallic clusters-based MOFs. (a) The combination of triazole/tetrazole or their derivatives with metal ions to form metal-clusters is one of the effective strategies.3 (b) The azido anion was selected to construct polynuclear metal clusters due to its versatile binding modes.4 (c) Halogen atoms act as linkages to connect the metal ions.5 (d) The metal ions are connected by carboxylates or hydroxyl groups to form polynuclear clusters, which have been extensively investigated in recent years.6

As is known, the rational design and selection of suitable organic ligands is a crucial factor for the preparation of multinuclear cluster-based MOFs. In this work, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTC) has been selected to construct polynuclear clusters according to the fourth approach, because the star-like molecule shows a strong coordination ability and can adopt various coordination modes in building MOFs.7 On the other hand, various N-containing ligands, such as bipyridyl-based,8 bis(triazole)-based,9 and phenanthroline derivatives,10 in the metal-carboxylates system play an important role in the construction of the final topological structures. Recently, the bis-pyridyl-bis-amide ligands, as a kind of neutral N-donor and O-donor ligand, have attracted great attention in metal-carboxylate systems due to their abundant coordination sites and H-bonding sites.11 Several groups reported a series of MOFs based on the flexible bis-pyridyl-bis-amide ligands with –(CH2)n– as a spacer.12 Our group have also investigated some flexible and semi-rigid bis-pyridyl-bis-amide based complexes.13

To further expand our research, in this work we designed and synthesized a “V”-type tri-pyridyl-bis-amide ligand N,N′-bis(pyridine-3-yl)pyridine-3,5-dicarboxamide (L) with the potential for various coordination modes (Chart 1). Comparing with bis-pyridyl-bis-amide ligands, the L ligand not only possesses amide groups to improve the flexibility of the whole ligand and provide hydrogen bonding sites, but also has an extra N-donor in the semi-rigid spacer to increase the coordination sites. To the best of our knowledge, a few groups have only investigated the key role of some inorganic anions (such as ClO4−, NO3−, SCN−, Cl−, Br−, I−) in the assembly of the L ligand with different metal salts.14 Very recently, our group reported the first examples of combining organic carboxylates with the L ligand, and obtained a series of Zn/Cd coordination polymers.15 In order to further investigate the effect of the central metal atoms on the construction of high-dimensional polynuclear cluster-based MOFs based on the L ligand, in this work, CoII was selected as the central metal, H3BTC was chosen aiming at constructing polynucler subunits, and L was used as a linker, aiming at building novel high dimensional MOFs. As a result, two polymorphous MOFs, namely [Co3(L)2(BTC)2(H2O)2]·6H2O (1) and {[Co2(L)(BTC)(OH)]·2.5H2O}2 (2) were obtained by one-pot hydrothermal synthesis, which is a fruitful research technique to study the polymorphism.16 In addition, the electrochemical behavior, photocatalysis, fluorescence and magnetic properties of the complexes were investigated.

Experimental section

Materials and characterization

All the solvents and reagents for synthesis were commercially available and used without further purification. Ligand L was prepared according to the literature method.14b FT-IR spectra (KBr pellets) were performed on a Magna FT-IR 560 Spectrometer and the elemental analyses of C, H and N were made on a Perkin-Elmer 240C Elemental Analyzer. Thermogravimetric data for the title compounds were collected on a Pyris Diamond thermal analyzer under N2 atmosphere (heating rate 10 °C min−1). The photoluminescent properties were carried out using a HITACHI F-4500 Fluorescence Spectrophotometer. The electrochemical experiments were measured on a CHI 440 Electrochemical Quartz Crystal Microbalance. The complex 1 bulk-modified carbon paste electrode (CPE) was used as a working electrode. An SCE and a platinum wire were used as reference and auxiliary electrodes, respectively. Magnetic data were collected on a Quantum Design MPMSXL7 (SQUID) magnetometer. The diamagnetic corrections were made by using Pascal's constants. UV-vis absorption spectra were obtained using a SP-1900 UV-vis spectrophotometer.

Preparation of the complexes 1–2

A mixture containing CoCl2·6H2O (0.0476 g, 0.2 mmol), L (0.0 32 g, 0.1 mmol) and H3BTC (0.032 g, 0.15 mmol) was dissolved in 8 mL of H2O. The pH value was adjusted to about 7 with 0.1 M NaOH solution. The resulting mixture was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon lined stainless steel container and then heated at 120 °C for 6 d. After the autoclave was cooled to room temperature, two kinds of crystals with different colors, pink for 1 and purple for 2, were obtained. Approximate yields: ca. 25% for 1 and 15% for 2 (based on Co).

Anal. calcd for C52H48Co3N10O24 (1): C 45.46, H 3.52, N 10.20%. Found: C 44.14, H 3.67, N 10.06%. IR (KBr, cm−1) for 1: 3359s, 3082w, 1618s, 1587w, 1539s, 1433m, 1375s, 1276w, 1209w, 893m, 817w, 732m, 686m, 655w.

Anal. calcd for C52H46Co4N10O23 (2): C 44.15, H 3.28, N 9.90%. Found: C 42.67, H 3.51, N 9.43%. IR (KBr,cm−1) for 2: 3419s, 3084w, 1678m, 1618s, 1544m, 1429m, 1373m, 1209w, 893w, 817m, 721m, 686m, 655w.

X-Ray crystallographic study

The single crystal X-ray diffraction data of the two complexes were collected on a Bruker Smart 1000 CCD area detector diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å for 1–2) at room temperature. The crystal structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL crystallographic software package and refined by the full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELXTL.17 All nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, the hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically. The atom O7 in 1 is disordered over two adjacent sites. The O3W and O5W for 1 were present with partial occupancy. The O2W, O3W and O4W for 2 were present with partial occupancy. Results and discussion

Syntheses of complexes 1 and 2

When the mixture of CoCl2·6H2O (0.2 mmol), L (0.1 mmol), H3BTC (0.15 mmol) and 8 mL of H2O with the pH value of about 7 was heated at 120 °C for 6 days, two kinds of crystals with different colors, pink for 1 and purple for 2, were obtained. (Fig. S1, ESI†). In order to further determine the formation conditions of 1 and 2, we carried out a series of synthesis experiments by tuning the pH values of the medium. If the pH value was adjusted to between 5 to 6, all the products afforded were the pink crystals 1. If the pH value was adjusted to between 6 to 8, both pink crystals 1 and purple crystals 2 were obtained and the yield of 2 gradually increases with pH increasing from 6 to 8, while the yield of 1 gradually decreases. Unfortunately, when the pH value is above 8, there are no crystals obtained. Moreover, when the ratio of the initial reactants, the reaction temperature and the reaction time were adjusted under the pH values from 5 to 8, crystals were also obtained. So, the above experimental results indicate that the pH value plays a crucial role in the formation of 1 and 2.

Description of crystal structures

[Co3(L)2(BTC)2·(H2O)2]·6H2O (1). Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As shown in Fig. 1a, two crystallographically independent CoII ions exhibit different octahedral coordination modes. Co1 is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two L ligands and four oxygen atoms from three BTC anions, whereas Co2 is on an inversion centre and surrounded by two L nitrogen atoms, two carboxyl oxygen atoms and two water molecules. The Co–N bond distances are 2.146(4) (Co1–N5), 2.181(3) (Co1–N3#4) Å and 2.178(4) Å (Co2–N1, Co2–N1#6), and the Co–O bond distances are in the range of 2.015(3)–2.248(3) Å (Table S1, ESI†), which are in the normal range of those observed in cobalt complexes.18
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environments for CoII ions in complex 1 (at the 30% probability level, #1 x − 1, y, z; #2 − x − 1, −y, −z + 3; #3 −x, −y, −z + 3; #4 x, y − 1, z; #6 −x, −y + 1, −z + 2; #7 x, y + 1, z). (b) The 2D sheet constructed from CoII ions and BTC anions containing two kinds of loops. (c) Schematic representation of the 3D (3,4,4,5)-connected topology of complex 1.

The ligand L adopts a μ3-bridging coordination mode (via the ligation of nitrogen atoms in pyridyl rings) (Chart 1c). The CoII ions are bridged by the L ligands to generate a 1D ladder structure, which contains Co4(L)4 50-membered rings with the dimensions of ca. 22.68 × 11.21 Å2 (Fig. S2a, ESI†). Three carboxyl groups of a BTC anion adopt three distinct coordination modes: μ1-η1:η0-monodentate mode, μ1-η1:η1-bidentate chelating mode and μ2-η1:η1-bidentate bridging fashion (Chart 1a), thus the BTC exhibits a four-coordinated mode. Each Co1 ion is connected by three BTC ligands and each BTC anion bridges four CoII ions to form a 2D puckered sheet [Co3(BTC)2]n (Fig. 1b), which is similar to those observed for {[Ni3(btc)2(bib)3(H2O)2]·(CH3OH)(H2O)4}n (bib = 1,4-bis(imidazolyl)benzene).16a There are two kinds of Co-BTC loops in the 2D layer. One is a 16-membered ring of Co2(BTC)2 with the dimensions of ca. 7.37 × 7.79 Å2, the other is a 32-membered ring of Co4(BTC)4 with the dimensions of ca. 15.58 × 10.04 Å2 (Fig. S3a, ESI†). If the BTC ligand is considered as a 4-connected node, the Co1 center is viewed as a 3-connected node and the two-connected Co2 ion is defined as a linker, then the 2D layer [Co3(BTC)2]n can be described as a simplified binodal (3,4) network. The 2D layers are further linked by the “V”-type L ligands to afford an extended 3D pillar-layered framework (Fig. S4a, ESI†). Topological analysis is carried out as stated above to get insight into the structure of 1. The L ligand is viewed as a 3-connected node. The Co1 ion is also connected with two L ligands besides three BTC anions, which is considered as a 5-connected node. The Co2 center can be regarded as a 4-connected node, which is coordinated by two L ligands and two BTC anions. Therefore, the 3D structure can be classified as a (3,4,4,5)-connected topology with a Schläfli symbol of (42·63·85)2(42·63·8)2(6·82)2(62·84) (Fig. 1c).19

{[Co2(L)(BTC)(OH)]·2.5H2O}2 (2). Complex 2 also crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically independent CoII ions, one BTC anion, one L ligand, one hydroxyl group and two and a half crystalline water molecules. As depicted in Fig. 2a, the CoII ions exhibit two types of coordination modes. The Co1 is six-coordinated by one nitrogen atom from an L ligand, three oxygen atoms of three BTC anions and two μ3-OH oxygen atoms in an octahedral geometry. The Co(1)–N(1) bond distance is 2.156(2) Å, the Co–O bond distances are in the range of 2.0800(19)–2.1420(19) Å. The Co2 ion shows a {CoO4N} trigonal bipyramidal geometry, which is coordinated by one nitrogen atom from an L ligand, three oxygen atoms of three BTC anions and one μ3-OH oxygen atom. The Co(2)–N(3#5) bond distance is 2.121(2) Å, the Co–O bond distances are from 1.9616(19) to 2.286(2) Å, which are comparable to those of 1.
Fig. 2 (a) The coordination environment for the CoII ion in complex 2 (at 30% probability level, #1 −x + 1, −y + 1, −z; #2 x − 1, y + 1, z; #3 −x + 1, −y + 2, −z; #4 −x + 2, −y + 1, −z; #5 x, y, z − 1; #6 x, y, z + 1). (b) The 2D network formed by the CoII4 clusters and BTC anions in 2. (c) Schematic representation of the 3D (3,8)-connected topology of complex 2.

It is noteworthy that four CoII ions are interlinked through two bridging μ3-OH groups to form a basic tetranuclear [Co4(μ3-OH)2] subunit (Fig S5, ESI†). The ligands L, displaying a μ2-bridging coordination mode, as illustrated in Chart 1, connect adjacent tetranuclear subunits to form a 1D chain (Fig. S2b, ESI†). The BTC anion, acting as a μ6-bridging ligand, links three Co1 and three Co2 ions, in which three carboxyl groups adopt two types of coordination modes: μ2-η1:η1-bidentate bridging mode and μ2-η2:η0-bidentate bridging mode (Chart 1b). Each tetranuclear subunit is connected by six BTC anions to give rise to a slightly corrugated 2D layer (Fig. 2b). There are three types of 16-membered rings of Co2(BTC)2 in the 2D layer, the dimensions of which are ca. 5.21 × 8.68 Å2, ca. 6.84 × 7.35 Å2 and ca. 8.06 × 8.16 Å2, respectively (Fig. S3b, ESI†). Each CoII ion in the 2D layer is further linked by μ2-bridging L ligands to generate a 3D framework (Fig. S4b, ESI†), which contains a macro ring composed by L ligands and BTC anions with the dimensions of ca. 13.81 × 14.70 Å2 (Fig. S3c, ESI†). A better insight into the complicated framework can be achieved by topological approach (Fig. 2c). The BTC acts as a 3-connected node and two L ligands are considered as a doubled linker. Whereas the [Co4(μ3-OH)2] subunit is connected by six BTC anions and four L ligands, which can be considered as an eight-connected node, thus resulting in the (3,8)-connected tfz-d net with Schläfli symbol of (43)2(46·618·84).19,20

It should be pointed out that although complexes 1 and 2 have been synthesized from one pot, their final structures are different. In complexes 1 and 2, the BTC anions show different coordination modes (Chart 1) and link the CoII centers to construct distinct 2D layers. In 1, each BTC anion adopts three types of coordination modes and bridges four metal ions to form two kinds of loops, whereas in 2, each BTC anion shows two types of coordination modes and links six CoII ions to generate three types of loops (Fig. S3, ESI†). In addition, there exist dinuclear subunits in 1 and tetranuclear clusters in 2. The L ligand has three potential coordination sites of nitrogen atoms in pyridyl rings. All the nitrogen atoms of the three pyridine moieties are coordinated to metal ions in 1, forming a Co4(L)4 50-membered ring with the dimensions of ca. 22.68 × 11.21 Å2. Whereas in 2, only two nitrogen atoms from the L ligand are linked by metal ions and no ring was obtained by L ligands and metal ions. Three pyridyl rings within each L ligand are almost parallel in the complexes 1 and 2, so the dihedral angles are 0°. The distances between N-coordination sites are 7.901 Å [N(5)⋯N(1)], 7.861 Å [N(3)⋯N(1)] and 13.104 Å [N(5)⋯N(3)] for complex 1, and 8.328 Å [N(5)⋯N(1)], 8.379 Å [N(3)⋯N(1)] and 8.404 Å [N(5)⋯N(3)] for complex 2. The difference of N⋯N distances may cause diverse coordination modes of the N-donor ligand. Du et al. reported two related complexes, {[Co(bpt)(HBTC)(H2O)3]·2.25H2O}n (3) and [Co(bpt)(HBTC)(H2O)]n (4), constructed from bipyridyl-based ligands and H3BTC (bpt = 4-amino-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole).21 In 3 and 4, only two carboxyl groups of BTC coordinated to the metal CoII ions, the third one is protonated. The carboxylates link CoII ions to produce wavelike 1-D polymeric chains, rather than to form polynuclear clusters, which is different from the case in complexes 1 and 2. In 3, the bpt ligands just act as monodentate ligands and did not extend the 1D chains into a high dimensional network. In 4, the bpt ligands take bidentate bridging modes to produce a greatly corrugated 2-D (4,4) net. From the structure descriptions and comparison above, it can be clearly seen that the coordination modes of N-donor ligands and polycarboxylates play important roles in the formation of the final polynuclear cluster-based frameworks.

Thermal stabilities and powder X-ray diffraction of complexes 1 and 2

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were carried out to study the thermal stability of complexes 1 and 2. The TG curves (Fig. S6, ESI†) of complexes 1 and 2 both exhibit two obvious weight loss steps. The first weight loss of 12.29% (calcd: 10.49%) for 1 at 65–165 °C and 9.24% (calcd: 6.36%) for 2 at 50–160 °C corresponds to the complete dehydration. The chemical decomposition starts at 420 °C for 1 and 350 °C for 2 and finishes at 700 °C. The weight loss is ascribed to the loss of organic ligands and the final residue is probably CoO (found: 15.71%, calcd: 16.36% for 1 and found 21.25%, calcd: 21.19% for 2). The TGA analysis shows that complexes 1 and 2 have good thermal stability.22

The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns for complexes 1 and 2 are presented in Fig. 3. The as-synthesized patterns are in good agreement with the corresponding simulated ones, indicating the phase purities of the samples. Besides that, we further carried out powder X-ray diffraction experiments to investigate the stability of complexes 1 and 2 upon the removal of water molecules according to TG analysis. It can be seen from the TG curves of Fig. S7 (ESI†) that the samples lose water molecules after being heated at 170 °C. The PXRD patterns of the samples after heating at 170 °C match with the simulated ones except for some intensity differences. So, the frameworks of 1 and 2 are stable and have not collapsed after being heated at 170 °C.

Electrochemical properties of complexes 1 and 2

Because complexes 1 and 2 are insoluble in water and common organic solvents, the bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (1-CPE and 2-CPE) become the optimal choice to investigate the redox properties of 1 and 2. The electrochemical behavior of 1-CPE was measured in 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution (pH = 3 adjusted by H2SO4). Fig. S8 (ESI†) shows the cyclic voltammograms of the 1-CPE at different scan rates in the potential range of 950 to −100 mV. A reversible redox peak is observed at the 1-CPE, which can be attributed to the redox of Co(III)/Co(II).23 The mean peak potential E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 is approximately 400 mV for 1-CPE with the scan rate of 100 mV s−1. There is no redox peak at the bare CPE in the potential range of 950 to −100 mV. The effect of scan rates on the electrochemical behavior of the 1-CPE is also reported. When the scan rates are varied from 20 to 450 mV s−1, the peak potentials change gradually: the cathodic peak potentials shift toward the negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shift toward the positive direction. As shown in Fig. S9 (ESI†), the peak currents are proportional to the scan rates, which indicates the redox process of 1-CPE is surface-controlled. The electrochemical behaviour of 2-CPE is similar to that of 1-CPE, as shown in Fig. S10 (ESI†). In the potential range of 950 to 0 mV, a reversible redox couple was obtained, which can be attributed to the redox of Co(III)/Co(II) from complex 2 with mean peak potential E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 of 394 mV (80 mVs−1).23

Fluorescence properties of complexes 1–2

Luminescent complexes are attracting more and more attention due to their potential applications in photoactive materials.24 The solid state photoluminescent properties of the free organic ligand and complexes 1–2 at room temperature are shown in Fig. 4. An intense broad photoluminescence emission at 432 nm (λex = 315 nm) for the L ligand is observed, which is probably attributable to the π*–π transition of L. The free H3BTC ligand shows an emission band at 380 nm (λex = 334 nm).7b,25a The emission band for the BTC ligand can be attributed to the π*–n transition, which is very weak compared with the π*–π transition of the L ligand.25b,c Therefore, the BTC ligands almost have no contribution to the fluorescent emission of the title complexes. Intense fluorescent emissions are observed at 416 nm (λex = 260 nm) for 1 and 417 nm (λex = 270 nm) for 2, which can be probably assigned to the intraligand charge transition of L due to their similar emission peaks.14a,26a,b In addition, the fluorescent intensity of complex 2 is weaker than that of complex 1, which indicates that the structures of the complexes have a subtle but significant influence on the photoluminescent intensity.26c

Photocatalytic properties of complexes 1–2

Photocatalysts have attracted much attention because of their potential applications in purifying water and air by decomposing organic compounds.27 The photocatalytic performances of the title complexes were investigated with photodegradation of methylene blue (MB) under UV irradiation through a typical process: 50 mg of powder of the title complexes was mixed with 200 mL of MB solution (10.0 mg L−1), magnetically stirred in the dark for 30 min to ensure the equilibrium of the working solution. Then the solution was exposed to UV irradiation from an Hg lamp and kept continuously stirring. 5 mL of the suspension was taken for centrifugal separation every 30 min. Then, the transparent solution of the up layer was taken out for analysis. Under the same conditions, a simple photolysis comparative experiment was also performed without any catalyst. As shown in Fig. 5, it can be clearly seen that the absorption peak of MB decreased obviously with increasing of reaction time for the title complexes. Besides that, changes in the concentration of MB solution were plotted versus irradiation time. The calculation results show that the MB degrades approximately from 100% to 28% for complex 1 and 55% for complex 2 after 180 min. As illustrated in Fig. 5 (down), the decomposing rate is almost the same between the two complexes at the first 30 min, but the rate of 1 is much faster than 2 after 30 min. The results indicate that the photocatalytic performance of 1 was better than that of 2. As is known, the differences of photocatalytic activities for MOFs may be related to the final framework structures of the complexes.28

Magnetic properties of complexes 1–2

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of 1 and 2 were measured in the temperature range of 2–300 K at an applied field of 1000 Oe. The χm−1vs. T and χmT vs. T plots for 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 6. For 1, the temperature dependence of the reciprocal susceptibility (χm−1) in the high-temperature range obeys the Curie–Weiss law with C = 13.21 emu mol−1 K and θ = −58.5 K. The χmT value is equal to 11.17 emu mol−1 K at 300 K, which is higher than the theoretical value (5.62 emu mol−1 K) for three spin-only CoII ions (S = 3/2, g = 2.0). The high C and χmT values indicate the significant orbital contribution of the CoII ions.29 With a decrease of temperature, the χmT value gradually decreases down to 3.49 emu mol−1 K at 2 K. The negative θ value and χmT vs. T plot display an antiferromagnetic behavior between the CoII ions, which should result from the antiferromagnetic interactions between the dinuclear CoII ions through the carboxyl bridges (Co–OCO–Co) as the effective coupling pathways.30 For 2, the susceptibility data in the high-temperature range can be fitted by the Curie–Weiss law with C = 15.54 emu mol−1 K and θ = −124.8 K. At 300 K, the χmT value is 11.02 emu mol−1 K, which is higher than the spin-only value (7.50 emu mol−1 K) expected for four CoII ions (S = 3/2, g = 2.0). The high C and χmT values could be attributed to the orbital contribution of the CoII ions.31 Upon cooling, the χmT value gradually decreases down to 0.27 emu mol−1 K at 2 K. Both the negative θ value and the trend of χmT vs. T exhibit a dominant antiferromagnetic behavior between the CoII ions. The antiferromagnetic behavior may originate from the multiple superexchange interactions between the CoII ions within the CoII4 cluster by the mixed syn,syn-μ2-η1:η1-COO−, μ2-η2:η0-COO− and part of μ3-OH pathways. In contrast, owing to the Co1–O9–Co2 (Co1#4–O9#3–Co2#3) angle (Fig. 2) of 97.380(77)° (between 96° and 99°), the two μ3-OH pathways may result in a weak ferromagnetic coupling between the Co1 and Co2 ions.32

Conclusions

In summary, two new cobalt cluster-based MOFs have been synthesized by employing a semirigid “V”-shaped tri-pyridyl-bis-amide ligand and polycarboxylate under one-pot hydrothermal reaction. Complex 1 exhibits a 3D (3,4,4,5)-connected framework, while complex 2 features a 3D (3,8)-connected tfz-d net. The results herein reveal that the pH value plays a crucial role in the formation of 1 and 2, while various coordination modes of the L ligand and polycarboxylate steer the final structures of the title complexes. This work may provide an informative example in preparing various MOFs using semirigid tri-pyridyl-bis-amide ligands. Moreover, the fluorescent, magnetic and photocatalytic properties for the complexes indicate that they may be good candidates for the potential applications in the functional materials field. Currently, further studies involving other metals, other auxiliary polycarboxylate ligands and tri-pyridyl-bis-amide ligands are under investigation in our laboratory.
