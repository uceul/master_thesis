A pyridyl-decorated MOF-505 analogue exhibiting hierarchical porosity, selective CO2 capture and catalytic capacity†

Qin-Qin Dang , Yu-Fen Zhan , Li-Na Duan and Xian-Ming Zhang *
School of chemistry & Material Science, Shanxi Normal University, Linfen, 041004, P. R. China. E-mail: zhangxm@dns.sxnu.edu.cn; Fax: +86 357 2051402; Tel: +86 357 2051402

Received 23rd May 2015 , Accepted 1st October 2015

First published on 16th October 2015

An expanded pyridyl-decorated MOF-505 analogue[Cu2(L)(H2O)2]·Gx (H4L = 5,5′-(pyridine-2,5-diyl)diisophthalic acid, G = solvent molecule) has been solvothermally synthesized and reported. It exhibited rare hierarchical meso- and microporosity. With exposed unsaturated CuII sites and Lewis basic pyridyl sites, the material shows both large CO2-uptake capacity (123.4 cm3 g−1 at 273 K, 1 bar) and high selectivity for CO2 over N2 (55.7) at 273 K. Furthermore, for the first time the compound has been exploited for its heterogeneous catalytic performance toward the cyanosilylation reaction under solvent-free conditions. The compound can be recycled up to five times with only a minor loss of activity.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks constructed from tetracarboxylates containing basic m-benzenedicarboxylate moieties have gained tremendous attention in the past decade because of their high surface area, tunable pore size, and gas adsorption.1 In 2005, Chen et al. firstly reported a copper–tetracarboxylate framework named MOF-505 with a three-dimensional (3D) structure based on a NbO net.2 After the pioneering work by Chen et al., the NbO-related MOF-505 analogues derived from linear diisophthalate linkers have been significantly explored because catenation is difficult in such cage-based MOF networks.

Schröder et al.3 reported a series of MOF-505 analogues based on polyphenyltetracarboxylates of varying lengths and functional groups. Subsequently, the same group synthesized two more isostructural framework compounds by using curved tetracarboxylates which show high H2 adsorption because of the pocket effect.4 Hu et al.5 synthesized a MOF-505 analogue by incorporating a CC triple bond into the organic backbone, which shows extraordinarily high C2H2 uptake. Further by a ligand extension strategy, Bai et al.6 constructed a MOF-505 analogue from a nanosized tetracarboxylate with alkyne units. The framework shows a high BET surface area of 3038 m2 g−1 and exhibits excellent H2 and CO2 uptake. The incorporation of nitrogen donor sites into the pore walls can drastically impact their gas uptake capacity and selectivity. Along this strategy, Suh et al.7 synthesized three MOF-505 analogues by using azo-functionalized tetracarboxylate. Subsequently, they synthesized a SNU-50 with a 3D NbO-based network by using a long tetracarboxylate functionalized with an imide group, which shows high adsorption capacities for H2, CO2 and CH4.8 Chen et al. synthesized a microporous metal–organic framework by using a tetracarboxylate with Lewis basic pyridyl sites, which exhibits high CH4 and C2H2 storage capacity.9 In spite of a large number of porous MOF-505 analogues derived from linear diisophthalate linkers have been reported, all of them adopt the microporous regime (pore size < 2 nm). Even using a nanosized tetracarboxylate ligand, the pore width is restricted to the range of 1.18–1.27 nm.3b This greatly limits its application in gas storage, and as a heterogeneous catalyst because of the negative effect on the diffusion of gaseous and reactive species. As a result, the development of hierarchical meso- and microporous MOF-505 analogues is of great importance.

Recently, hierarchically porous MOFs with controlled porosity have also received considerable attention, because they provide many novel properties and have important prospects in practical industrial processes, such as catalysis, adsorption, and chemical sensing.10 Hierarchical structures are of great advantages, as they feature high pore volumes, large surface areas, together with potentially larger pore sizes. Herein, we synthesized a hierarchically porous MOF-505 analogue, complex 1 [Cu2(L4−)(H2O)2]·Gx (G = solvent molecule), from dicopper paddlewheel Cu2(COO)4 SBUs and a tetracarboxylate with a Lewis basic pyridyl site (H4L = 5,5′-(pyridine-2,5-diyl)diisophthalic acid). With exposed unsaturated CuII sites and Lewis basic pyridyl sites, the material shows large CO2 uptake (123.4 cm3 g−1) and improved adsorption selectivity for CO2/N2 (55.7) at 273 K and 1 bar. Furthermore, the material shows efficient heterogeneous catalytic activity for cyanosilylation of carbonyl compounds under solvent-free conditions using only 1 mol% loading of the catalyst.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All chemicals purchased from commercial sources were analytically pure and used without further purification. The elemental analysis was carried out using an Elementar vario MACRO cube Elemental Analyzer. X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) data were recorded on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray powder diffractometer (CuKα, λ = 1.5418 Å). The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum BX FT-IR spectrometer. The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in air using SETARAMLABSYS equipment at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Low-pressure gases (N2, H2, O2 and CO2) sorption isotherms (up to 1 bar) were performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area and pore size analyzer. In order to remove guest solvent molecules in the framework, the collected crystals of compound 1 were washed with DMF and then the samples were exchanged with dry acetone 10 times in three days. The samples were activated by drying under a dynamic vacuum at room temperature and at 100 °C overnight for gas sorption studies.

Syntheses

A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·2H2O (0.022 g, 0.12 mmol), H4L (0.016 g, 0.04 mmol) in DMF/EtOH/H2O (2:1:1 v/v/v, 15 mL) was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel container. The resulting blue-green slurry turned clear upon addition of 1 drop of aqueous HCl (37%). The solution was gradually heated at 85 °C for 5 d and then cooled down to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. The blue crystalline product was collected and washed with DMF and dried in air to give the product; yield, 68% based on Cu. Elemental analysis: calcd. for [Cu2(L)(H2O)2]·(H2O)4(C2H5OH)2(DMF)·(C28H40N2O17Cu2, %): C, 41.48; H, 5.23; N, 3.59; found: C, 41.79; H, 4.98; N, 3.48.

The sample was activated by drying under a dynamic vacuum at room temperature and at 100 °C overnight to produce activated sample 1′. After grinding, 1′ was placed into a dryer for catalytic reaction. Recycling experiments

A reuse experiment was carried out for the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde. The reaction was carried out under the standard conditions. After the reaction was completed, the catalyst was recovered by filtration, washed with acetone, and dried under vacuum for 12 h prior to reuse. The catalytic recyclability was checked five times with the same batch of catalyst, and no obvious decrease in activity was observed.

Results and discussion

Description of structures

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction showed that 1 is a pyridine-decorated analogue of MOF-505. It was formulated as [Cu2(L)(H2O)2]·(H2O)4(C2H5OH)2(DMF) based on the elemental analysis, TGA and single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies. 1 crystallizes in a trigonal space group R3m. The C and N atoms of the pyridine ring of a L4− ligand are indispensible due to imposed crystallographic symmetry. Each CuII ion is coordinated to five O atoms in a square pyramidal geometry (Fig. 1a). Pairs of CuII centers are bridged by four carboxylate groups, forming paddlewheel units. One H2O molecule binds to each metal centre along the paddlewheel axis. Paddlewheel dinuclear Cu2(COO)4 secondary building units (SBUs) are bridged by four L4− ligands to form a 3D periodic network. As expected, this framework has the same NbO-based topology. The network has two types of cages. The small cage is defined by six inorganic SBUs with a pore diameter of 10.3 Å (Fig. 1b), while the larger elongated cage is defined by six organic SBUs with 9.3 × 22.2 Å (Fig. 1c). Two types of cages are alternately stacked in a 1:1 ratio with the formation of the 3D framework (Fig. 1d). The solvent accessible volume calculated using PLATON is as high as 66.9% (7765.0 Å3 out of the 11599.5 Å3 per unit cell volume). Temperature-dependent powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) confirms that 1 remains crystalline up to 290 °C in agreement with the TGA (Fig. S1 and S2).†

Gas sorption

To study the sorption isotherms of the frameworks, the as-synthesized samples of 1 were exchanged with dry acetone 10 times. Then, the sample was activated by drying under a dynamic vacuum at room temperature and 100 °C overnight to produce activated samples 1′. The isotherms for N2, H2 and O2 sorption were measured at 77 K and CO2 sorption was performed at 273 K and 298 K.

As shown in Fig. 2, the N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K shows an intermediate mode between type I and type IV, which are related to hierarchical meso and microporous materials, respectively. The sharp uptake in the low pressure region (P/P0 < 0.01) implied a significant microporosity for the networks, whereas the hysteresis loops of isotherms demonstrate the presence of mesopores. The guest-free 1′ takes up 512.2 cm3 g−1 of N2, characterized by a moderate BET surface area and Langmuir surface area of 1470 m2 g−1 and 2823 m2 g−1 with a total pore volume up to 0.79 cm3 g−1. According to t-plot analysis, the micropore volume is around 0.58 cm3 g−1. Thus, the mesopore volume (Vmeso) accounts for about 26.6% of the total pore volume in 1′. The mesopore size distribution curve, calculated from Barrett–Joyner–Halenda analysis, is also shown in Fig. 2. The mesopore diameter of 1′ is centered at 6.9–8.7 nm. Although the single-crystal structure of compound 1′ is the same as ZJU-5,9 their N2 isotherms are greatly distinct under the same activated conditions. ZJU-5 exhibited a typical type I behaviour, which shows no mesopores at all, while compound 1 exhibited rare hierarchical meso- and microporosity. The mesopore formation for 1 may be related to ethanol in the syntheses conditions while ZJU-5 was prepared in the absence of ethanol. A possible interpretation would be that ethanol can react with the tetracarboxylic acid H4L in the synthesis conditions and then the resulting tetraethyl tetracarboxylate may act as a surfactant, template-directing mesopores within the structure in compound 1. The similar phenomenon has also been reported by Serge K. et al.11 The mesopore walls in the material are formed from crystalline microporous frameworks, resulting in hierarchically micro- and mesoporous MOFs.


Fig. 2 N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K and pore size distribution inset.

The selective capture and sequestration of CO2 has been considered to be an effective way of controlling greenhouse gas emissions. The high surface areas, exposed unsaturated CuII sites and Lewis basic pyridyl sites of 1′ prompt us to examine its CO2 adsorption behaviour. Low-pressure CO2 sorption of 1′ was initially measured at 273 and 298 K (Fig. 3). High CO2 uptake of 122 and 71.8 cm3 g−1 is observed under 1 bar at 273 K and 298 K, respectively, which is significantly higher than that of Mg-based pyrene-1,3,6,8-tetracarboxylic acid MOFs (44.2 and 23.6 cm3 g−1, 1 bar at 273 and 298 K, respectively),12 tetragonal magnesium-carboxylate framework CPF-1 (83.5 and 31.4 cm3 g−1, 1 bar at 273 and 298 K, respectively)13 and NTU-101-Cu (101 cm3 g−1 at 273 K)14 but lower than the CO2 uptake of HNUST-1 (156.4 and 93.0 cm3 g−1, 1 bar at 273 and 298 K, respectively),15 NJU-Bai 14 (187 and 100 cm3 g−1, 1 bar at 273 and 298 K, respectively).16 In contrast to CO2 adsorption, only limited amounts of N2 can be adsorbed in 1′ (2.25 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and 1 bar Fig. S3†). The ratios of the initial slopes of gas adsorption are used to estimate the adsorption selectivity, and the CO2/N2 selectivity is calculated to be 55.7 (Fig. S4†), which is higher than the value of HNUST-1 (39.8),15 nitro-decorated NJU-Bai 14 (28)16 and MOF-177 (17.5).17 This indicates that 1′ may have potential application in separation of CO2 over N2.


Fig. 3 CO2, H2, and O2 uptake of 1′.

To better understand these observations, the coverage dependent CO2 adsorption enthalpies of 1′ were calculated from the CO2 isotherms collected at 273 and 298 K upon the Clausius–Clapeyron equation (Fig. S5, S6 and S7†). The adsorption enthalpy for 1′ at zero loading is approximately 27.5 kJ mol−1. The value is higher than that of nitro-decorated NJU-Bai 14 (24.5 kJ mol−1).16 As CO2 loading increases, Qst slightly decreases and approaches a value of 21.4 kJ mol−1 at 50 ml g−1 which indicates the strong interaction between the pore surface of 1′ and CO2 even at high loading. This might be due to the fact that the higher quadrupole moment and polarizability of CO2 molecules may induce a better interaction with pyridyl-decorated pore walls and the open copper(II) sites of the framework.

We also explored the hydrogen and oxygen storage capacities of 1′ (Fig. 3). At 77 K and 1 bar, the total H2 uptake of the 1′ reach 223 cm3 g−1 (2.01 wt%), which is higher than CPF-1 (1.29 wt%, 77 K, 1 bar),13 Mg-based pyrene-1,3,6,8-tetracarboxylic acid MOFs (92.2 cm3 g−1, 77 K, 1 bar),12 NJU-Bai12 (1.91 wt%)6 and NOTT-116 (1.90 wt%),18 even competing with the best known metal–organic frameworks for hydrogen storage under similar conditions.19 The O2 uptake of 1′ reaches 645.1 cm3 g−1 at 77 K, 1 bar. This value is higher than that of N2 under the same conditions. The preference of O2 over N2 may be attributed to the fact that vacant coordination sites of Cu interact with O2 more strongly than with N2. In addition, O2 is more accessible to the channels because of its smaller kinetic diameter (3.47 Å) than that of N2 (3.64 Å).

Catalytic study

Considering that complex 1′ has large channels and vacant metal coordination sites, it can be used as a Lewis acid heterogeneous catalyst. One of the most important Lewis acid catalyzed reactions is the cyanosilylation reaction. First of all, benzaldehyde was taken as a reference to test the catalytic activity of 1′ (Table S3†).The catalyst 1′ shows high activity in the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde and 76% conversion was reached in 8 h at 293 K, while the reaction shows only a little conversion (4%) under the same conditions without the catalyst. At higher temperature, 313 K, a high conversion (99%) is obtained. The catalytic activity of 1′ is comparable to other Cu- or Zr-based MOFs20 in the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde but is much higher than other reported results such as Cu3(BTC)2 (50–60%, 72 h),21 Cd-MOF (77%, 24 h),22 and Ln-based MOFs (5 mol% loading of catalysts, 77.8–94.8%, 323 K),23 presumably due to the high density of the exposed metal sites and the improved molecular accessibility of the hierarchical meso- and microporosity. Importantly, removal of 1′ by filtration after only 2 h completely shuts down the reaction, affording only 42% total conversion and no significant increase of the conversion was observed after 24 h. This demonstrates that no homogeneous catalyst species exists in the reaction solution and 1′ is truly heterogeneous in nature. The catalyst could be recycled and reused with only a minor loss of activity for at least five cycles (Fig. 4). The catalyst was removed from the reaction media, washed, and analyzed to confirm the conservation of the structure by powder X-ray diffraction (Fig. S8†). From Fig. S8,† we can see that some of the diffraction peaks disappeared after catalytic reaction, which is due to the preferred orientation after recycling five times.
Fig. 4 Reusability of 1′ catalyst experiments.

In order to check the selectivity of 1′, we also studied the catalytic efficiency towards different aldehyde substrates and conversion results are summarized in Table 1. As evident from Table 1, a significant size-selectivity effect is observed with 1′. Aryl- (Table 1, entries 1–4) and heteroaryl- (Table 1, entry 7) aldehyde with smaller molecular dimensions were suitable substrates and gave the corresponding cyanohydrin trimethylsilyl ethers B in high to excellent conversions. When a larger 1-naphthaldehyde with a molecular dimension of 9.7 × 8.4 Å2 was used,22 the conversion reduced to 76%. In contrast, the more sterically demanding 9-anthraldehyde, with a molecular dimension of 12.6 × 8.8 Å2, hardly reacted. This suggests that the 1′ is selective for small molecules and the pore size in 1′ is too small to accommodate the transition state geometry of 9-anthraldehyde. Besides, as depicted in Table 1, the electronic effect or the position of substituent on the aromatic ring did not show any significant change in the overall conversion.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have successfully prepared a hierarchical porous expanded MOF-505 analogue decorated with Lewis basic pyridyl sites. With exposed unsaturated CuII sites and Lewis basic pyridyl sites, the material shows both large CO2-uptake capacity and high selectivity for CO2 over N2. Moreover, the material showed excellent heterogeneous catalytic performance toward the cyanosilylation reaction under solvent-free conditions.
