Synthesis, structures and luminescence properties of 3d–4f heterometallic–organic frameworks (HMOFs) constructed from different copper halide clusters†

Guang Zeng , Shanghua Xing , Xiuru Wang , Yulin Yang , Yang Xiao , Zhenhua Li , Guanghua Li *, Zhan Shi and Shouhua Feng
State Key Laboratory of Inorganic Synthesis and Preparative Chemistry, College of Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun 130012, PR China. E-mail: leegh@jlu.edu.cn; Fax: +86 431 85168624; Tel: +86 431 85168511

Received 24th December 2015 , Accepted 23rd February 2016

First published on 23rd February 2016

Four novel 3d–4f heterometallic–organic frameworks (HMOFs), namely [Gd2(Cu2I2)(C6H4NO2)6(C3H7NO)(H2O)]·(C3H7NO) (1), [Gd2(Cu4I4)(C6H4NO2)6(C3H7NO)2] (2), [Gd2(Cu6I6)(C12H8NO2)6(C2H6O)2(H2O)2] (3) and [Gd2(Cu8I8)(C12H8NO2)6(H2O)4]·5(C4H8O2) (4), have been solvothermally synthesized and characterized. The four compounds incorporate Cu2I2, Cu4I4, Cu6I6 and Cu8I8 clusters in three-dimensional frameworks, respectively, and the influence of Ln3+ ions, copper halide clusters and ligands on the construction of the structures has been analyzed. The four compounds display four different emission colours. Interestingly, compound 3 responds to external mechanical force. After grinding, the fluorescence has been obviously quenched. Compound 4 displays a reversible luminescence behavior, accompanying the removal and recovery of the guest molecules in the channel.

Introduction

As important and burgeoning metal–organic framework (MOF) materials, heterometallic–organic frameworks (HMOFs) usually are constructed by connecting different metal cations together through organic ligands. Because of their aesthetically beautiful architectures and potential applications in gas storage/separation, molecular magnetism, optics, catalysis and electrochemistry, HMOFs have received increasing attention in recent years.1 Though a great amount of 3d–4f, 4d–4f and 3p–3d–4f HMOFs have been successfully synthesized,1,2 there are still many scientific issues about crystal engineering to be addressed in this research field. In their synthesis, the influential factors are too many to efficiently design and synthesize these compounds, such as organic ligands, inorganic metal ions, solvents, temperature, pH values, reaction time, etc.3 Thus, exploration of effective synthetic strategies to construct HMOFs has become one of the most important subjects in this research field.

Polynuclear copper(I) halide clusters with abundant structural motifs are important inorganic units or complexes. Various kinds of copper(I) halide clusters with different coordinated models have been reported, such as Cu2I2, Cu3I4, Cu4I4, Cu6I6, Cu6I8, Cu8I8, etc.4 Due to their abundant and diverse structural characteristics, copper(I) halide clusters are ideal inorganic building units for constructing MOFs and a large number of MOF materials have been constructed by making use of copper(I) halide clusters.5 Apart from having diverse structures, copper(I) halide cluster compounds are a class of promising luminescent materials with potential applications as organic light-emitting diodes.6 Interestingly, they also respond to external stimuli such as thermal treatment (thermochromism) and mechanical grinding (mechanochromism) which have been extensively investigated.7 Consequently, our group has conducted some studies on the synthesis and properties of copper(I) halide cluster MOFs. On the basis of previous studies, we try to use copper(I) halide clusters as inorganic building units to construct HMOFs and focus on the efficient synthesis, structures and properties of these materials.

In this work, we aim to design and synthesise a series of HMOFs with different copper(I) halide clusters. Lanthanide (Ln) metal ions are selected as the second inorganic building block, and nicotinic acid (Hna) and 3-(pyridin-4-yl)benzoic acid (Hpba) are selected as the organic linkers. Through studying the hard and soft (Lewis) acids and bases (HSAB) theory, it was found that Ln3+ ions have high affinity to bond to O-donor atoms and can provide more optical properties. On the other hand, Cu+ ions prefer to coordinate to N-donor atoms. According to these characteristics of metal ions, organic ligands containing O- and N-donor atoms can efficiently avoid the competition between Ln3+ and Cu+ ions. Thus, organic ligands with two different functional groups (pyridyl and carboxyl) are ideal linkers for connecting the two inorganic building blocks. Four 3d–4f HMOFs, namely [Gd2(Cu2I2)(C6H4NO2)6(C3H7NO)(H2O)]·(C3H7NO) (1), [Gd2(Cu4I4)(C6H4NO2)6(C3H7NO)2] (2), [Gd2(Cu6I6)(C12H8NO2)6(C2H6O)2(H2O)2] (3) and [Gd2(Cu8I8)(C12H8NO2)6(H2O)4]·5(C4H8O2) (4), have been synthesized by using nicotinic acid (Hna) and 3-(pyridin-4-yl)benzoic acid (Hpba).

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All reagents and solvents used in the syntheses were obtained from commercial sources without any purification. X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) experiments were performed on a Rigaku D/max 2550 X-ray powder diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation. Elemental analyses were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. The infrared (IR) spectra were recorded by using a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer with KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric experiments were carried out by using a TGA Q500 V20.10 Build 36 from room temperature to 800 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The solid-state luminescence spectra were measured on a FluoroMax-4 fluorescence spectrometer at room temperature.
Synthesis of compound 1. Gd(NO3)3 (0.045 g, 0.1 mmol), CuI (0.038 g, 0.2 mmol), NaI (0.030 g, 0.2 mmol) and nicotinic acid (0.049 g, 0.4 mmol) were placed in 5 mL of DMF/ethanol (v:v = 2:3) solution and stirred for a few hours, then transferred and sealed in a 20 mL Teflon-lined steel autoclave. The autoclave was kept in an oven at 100 °C for 3 days. After cooling to room temperature, yellow block crystals were collected by filtration, washed with ethanol and dried in air (yield 42%). X-ray crystallography

X-ray single crystal diffraction data for compounds 1 and 3 were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer equipped with a graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation source. The SMART software was used for data collection, indexing of the reflections and determination of the unit cell parameters; the collected data were integrated by using the SAINT software. Single crystal diffraction data for compounds 2 and 4 were collected on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID equipped with a narrow-focus, 5.4 kW sealed tube X-ray source (graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å). Data processing was accomplished with the PROCESS-AUTO processing program. Direct methods were used to solve the structure using the SHELXL crystallographic software package. All non-hydrogen atoms were easily found from the difference Fourier map. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Results and discussion

Structural descriptions

The structures of the four compounds have been determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. The 3D frameworks of the four compounds are generated by connecting a copper(I) halide cluster and a Gd3+ ion together through organic ligands. For compound 1, it crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/m space group. Two adjacent Cu+ ions and two I− ions are connected together to form a rhombic unit [Cu2I2]. Each Cu center of the [Cu2I2] cluster binds two neutral nitrogens from two Hna (Fig. 1a). The coordination sphere of the Gd3+ ion is made up of eight oxygen atoms from six carboxylate groups of six Hna ligands and one terminal N,N-dimethylformamide. The adjacent eight-coordinated Gd3+ ions are further bridged by carboxylate groups to form an infinite 1D chain running along the a axis (Fig. 2a). The [Cu2I2] clusters and Gd–carboxylate chains are connected together through nicotinic acid ligands to form a 3D framework (Fig. 3a). Importantly, the space group imposed symmetry in the structure of compound 1, such that the two I atoms of the [Cu2I2] cluster lie on a mirror plane and that the non-bonded DMF molecule is also disordered about a mirror plane. Compound 2 crystallizes in the tetragonal I2d space group. Four Cu+ ions and four I− ions are connected together to form a typical cubane unit [Cu4I4]. The cubane-like [Cu4I4] cluster binds four neutral nitrogens from four Hna (Fig. 1b). Different from that in compound 1, the Gd3+ ion lies on a twofold axis in the structure and the coordination sphere of the Gd3+ ion is made up of seven oxygen atoms from six carboxylate groups of six Hna ligands and one terminal N,N-dimethylformamide. The adjacent seven-coordinated Gd3+ ions are further bridged by carboxylate groups to form an infinite 1D chain running along the c axis (Fig. 2b). The 3D frameworks of 2 are also generated by connecting the copper(I) halide clusters and Gd–carboxylate chains through Hna (Fig. 3b). Importantly, two of the six ligands which coordinated to the Gd3+ ion have not connected with the copper halide clusters in compounds 1 and 2. On the basis of the successful construction of compounds 1 and 2, we replace nicotinic acid with 3-(pyridin-4-yl)benzoic acid (Hpba) and synthesize compounds 3 and 4. Compound 3 crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c space group. Six Cu+ ions and six I− ions are connected together to form a ladder-like fragment [Cu6I6]. The [Cu6I6] cluster binds six nitrogen atoms from six Hpba (Fig. 1c). The coordination sphere of the Gd3+ ion is made up of eight oxygen atoms from five carboxylate groups of five Hpba ligands and two terminal solvent molecules (one is ethanol and the other is water). The adjacent coordinated Gd3+ ions are further bridged by carboxylate groups to form an infinite chain running along the b axis (Fig. 2c). The [Cu6I6] cluster and Gd–carboxylate chains are linked together through Hpba ligands to form a 3D framework (Fig. 3c). Compound 4 crystallizes in the triclinic P space group. The [Cu8I8] cluster is formed by connecting two cubane-like [Cu4I4] clusters together through two Cu–I bonds (Fig. 1d). The Gd3+ ion is coordinated by eight oxygen atoms from six Hpba ligands and two terminal water molecules. The carboxylate groups bridge adjacent Gd3+ ions, forming a 1D Gd–carboxylate chain along the a axis (Fig. 2d). The 3D frameworks of compound 4 are generated by connecting the [Cu8I8] clusters and Gd–carboxylate chains through Hpba (Fig. 3d). 1D channels are generated in compound 4 and the channels are filled with solvent molecules (1,4-dioxane). The Cu–I, Cu–N and Gd–O distances in the four compounds are in the ranges of 2.522(11)–2.7585(15) Å, 1.979(6)–2.048(8) Å and 2.262(18)–2.663(6) Å, comparable to those found in the literature.2,4

Structural analysis

By comparing and analyzing the structures of the four compounds, we find that the coordination numbers and modes of the Gd3+ ions are different in the four compounds. However, the infinite 1D chain structure units in the four compounds constructed from the Gd3+ ions bridged by carboxylate groups show similar structural features. Notably, the copper halide clusters which are connected to similar Gd–carboxylate chains by organic linkers exhibit different sizes and shapes. The [Cu2I2] and [Cu4I4] clusters in compounds 1 and 2 are relatively small while the [Cu6I6] and [Cu8I8] clusters in compound 3 and 4 are relatively large. Based on the above structural comparison and analysis, we think that space steric hindrance may play a crucial role in the construction and structural tuning of copper halide clusters. It is well known that Gd3+ ions whose radii are larger than those of transition-metal ions have high coordination numbers. This character determines that the Gd3+ ion needs to be coordinated by more organic molecules in the structure construction. When the organic linker is relatively short, such as Hna in compounds 1 and 2, the organic ligands which coordinate to the Gd3+ ion cannot connect to the large copper halide clusters which have high coordination numbers and some of the ligands do not even connect to the copper halide cluster. Conversely, due to space steric hindrance, the copper halide clusters with high coordination numbers cannot connect to more neighbouring Gd–carboxylate chains through the short organic linkers. On the other hand, when the organic linker is relatively long, such as Hpba in compounds 3 and 4, the long linker would weaken space steric hindrance. The Gd–carboxylate chains can connect to a large copper halide cluster. By further analyzing the structures of compounds 1 and 2, we find that the copper halide clusters in compounds 1 and 2 have different sizes and shapes while the framework of compound 1 is very similar to the framework of compound 2 (Fig. 3a and b). We think that this result originates from the organic linker. In compound 1, the [Cu2I2] cluster with four coordination sites provides a planar quadrangular coordination geometry while the cubane-like [Cu4I4] cluster with four coordination sites provides a distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry in compound 2 (Fig. 4). These two kinds of clusters have the same coordination number but provide different geometries. Because the Hna ligand is a bent organic linker, the ligand can change the coordinating direction of the copper halide cluster to a certain degree when they connect to the copper halide cluster. The four ligands (Hna) which coordinate to the [Cu2I2] cluster display a “transoid” fashion while the four ligands (Hna) which coordinate to the [Cu4I4] cluster display a “cisoid” fashion (Fig. 4). The flexibility of the ligand reduces the difference between the coordination geometries of the [Cu2I2] and [Cu4I4] clusters to a certain degree, which results in similar structural features appearing in the two different compounds. Though the flexibility of the bent ligand can adjust the coordination geometry of the copper halide cluster, this effect would be weakened when there are great differences in the coordination geometry of the clusters. This result can be observed in the structures of compounds 3 and 4. The framework of compound 3 with [Cu6I6] clusters is completely different from the framework of compound 4 with [Cu8I8] clusters. The [Cu6I6] and [Cu8I8] clusters have the same coordination number but provide different coordination geometries. In compound 3, the [Cu6I6] cluster with six coordination sites provides a ladder-like coordination geometry. The Hpba ligands can only coordinate to the sites along the two sides of the ladder-like cluster. In compound 4, the [Cu8I8] cluster with the six coordination sites provides a trigonal antiprismatic coordination geometry. The Hpba ligands coordinate to the sites of the [Cu8I8] cluster along six different directions in space. Because of the great difference in the coordination geometry of the copper halide clusters, the effect of the flexible ligand (Hpba) is weak and the structural differences are very obvious in the two compounds. In comparison to the porous structure of compound 4, the structure of compound 3 is relatively voidless.

Thermogravimetric analysis, IR spectra and XRPD patterns

Measurements of the thermal behavior of the four compounds are performed in air from room temperature to 800 °C at 10 °C min−1. For compound 1, the first sharp mass loss was in the temperature range of 200–230 °C corresponding to the mass loss of one DMF molecule. The mass loss is about 4.8% (calc. 4.5%). For compound 2, it was stable up to ca. 250 °C and the only one sharp mass loss was attributed to coordinated DMF and organic ligands. For compound 3, the mass loss is about 5.9% (calc. 4.47%) at 240 °C, which corresponded to the mass loss of coordinated solvent molecules. For compound 4, the first sharp weight loss in the temperature range of 25–100 °C corresponded to the mass loss of 1,4-dioxane guest molecules. The mass loss is about 10.4% (calc. 12.44%). The solvent molecules in the structures of the four compounds were clearly revealed by the IR spectra (Fig. S7, ESI†). The IR spectra of compounds 1 and 2 clearly display the infrared absorption peak of DMF molecules around 1630 cm−1. In the IR spectrum of compound 3, the peaks around 2977 cm−1 are assigned to the stretching vibration of ethanol molecules. In the IR spectrum of compound 4, the peaks around 2957 and 2855 cm−1 correspond to the stretching vibration of 1,4-dioxane. The IR spectrum of 4a shows that the 1,4-dioxane molecule has been removed by heat (Fig. S7, ESI†). The XRPD patterns of the as-synthesized compounds were identified with the simulated patterns, which indicated that the phases of the synthetic samples were pure (Fig. S4, ESI†). Furthermore, the XRPD patterns of 4 and 4a confirmed that the framework of compound 4 has obviously changed, accompanying the removal and recovery of the 1,4-dioxane molecules.

Luminescence properties

Copper(I) halide cluster complexes are well known for their photoluminescence properties. Thus, we are very interested in studying the photoluminescence properties of HMOFs which contain copper(I) halide clusters. Apart from having novel structures, the four compounds also possess good photoluminescence. The solid-state photoluminescence properties were measured at room temperature. The results show that the emissions of the four compounds are completely different from each other. Because the characteristic emissions of the Gd3+ ion have not been detected, the emissions originate from the copper halide clusters. The emission light of compound 1 is at 569 nm under excitation around 407 nm while that of compound 2 has an emission maximum at 496 nm after excitation around 390 nm (Fig. 5). Under optimal excitation (λ3Ex = 417 nm, λ4Ex = 384 nm), compounds 3 and 4 also display broad emission bands at 520 nm and 615 nm, respectively (Fig. 5). According to the analysis of the structures of the four compounds and the literature about the luminescence of copper(I) halide cluster complexes, their emission lights should be attributed to the triplet cluster-centered (3CC) excited state, a combination of iodide-to-copper charge transfer (XMCT) and d–s transitions.4,8
Fig. 5 The solid-state luminescence spectra of compounds 1–4 at room temperature.

According to reports on copper(I) halide cluster complexes, they have been potential stimuli-responsive materials.7 Through grinding the four compounds, we find that compound 3 responds to external mechanical force. Grinding the solid sample of compound 3 using a pestle in an agate mortar leads to a change in the emission color. According to the reported literature on mechanochromic luminescence, copper(I) halide cluster compounds usually show a bathochromic shift of emission after grinding.9 Different from the mechanochromic luminescence, the green emission light of compound 3 has been obviously quenched after grinding and the emission cannot recover after soaking in a solvent (Fig. 6). Elemental analyses of the synthetic and ground samples of compound 3 show that no chemical reaction has taken place after grinding (Table S5, ESI†). Furthermore, Cu+ 2p and Gd3+ 4d X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) of compound 3 before and after grinding provides the same spectra, indicating that the microenvironments of Cu+ and Gd3+ have not changed (Fig. S8 and S9, ESI†).10 In other words, no atmospheric molecules or coordinated organic molecules have been introduced or removed by grinding. By analyzing the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of the synthetic sample of compound 3, its sharp and intense diffraction peaks indicate that it has good crystallinity. After grinding, the PXRD pattern shows only a few weak and ambiguous diffraction peaks. These results indicate that the crystal lattice has been significantly damaged leading to a crystal-to-amorphous phase conversion (Fig. S3, ESI†).9,11 These structure changes may directly weaken the Cu⋯Cu interactions, which result in fluorescence quenching.7e,9 Due to the porous structure of compound 4, it displays a reversible luminescence behavior, accompanying the removal and recovery of the guest molecules. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis shows that the guests (1,4-dioxane) in compound 4 can be removed by heat (Fig. S5, ESI†). The desolvated sample of compound 4 (4a) is obtained by calcination of the synthetic samples under vacuum at 100 °C for 4 hours and the IR spectra and TG analysis curves show that the 1,4-dioxane molecules have been removed (Fig. S5 and S7, ESI†). The photoluminescence behavior of 4a was measured and the luminescence color completely changed. The emission light exhibited a large red shift (610 → 685 nm) (Fig. 7). By analyzing the XRPD pattern of 4a, we find that the first diffraction peak shifted to a lower angle, which indicates that the framework structure has changed (Fig. S4, ESI†). Interestingly, the emission would rapidly recover after soaking 4a in 1,4-dioxane solvent for a few minutes (Fig. 7). At the same time, the XRPD pattern of 4a has become the same as the XRPD pattern of compound 4 (Fig. S4, ESI†). This reversible luminescence behavior should originate from the structure change which may directly affect the Cu⋯Cu interactions. The energy of the 3CC excited state is intensively dependent on the extent of the Cu⋯Cu interactions, which indicates that the energy level of the 3CC excited state would be different and the optical signals would correspondingly change.7,12 Furthermore, we have studied and discussed this reversible luminescence behaviour in our previous paper. The paper about four analogues of compound 4 which contain La3+, Ce3+, Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions has just been accepted by Inorg. Chem. of ACS.13 The characteristic emissions of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions have been successfully introduced in the analogues, which enrich the luminescence properties of HMOFs.

Conclusions

In summary, four novel heterometallic–organic frameworks which respectively contain [Cu2I2], [Cu4I4], [Cu6I6] and [Cu8I8] clusters in different frameworks have been synthesised under solvothermal conditions. The structural differences have been analyzed in detail. They exhibit a broad emission band of the copper(I) halide cluster in different luminescence colors. Interestingly, compound 3 displays an optical signal “turn-off” by fluorescence quenching after grinding. This unusual phenomenon is different from the conventional mechanochromic luminescence. Because of the structure change, compound 4 shows a unique reversible photoluminescence behavior. The framework structure of compound 4 correspondingly changes, accompanying the removal and recovery of the guest molecules in the channel.
