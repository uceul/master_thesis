    Abstract

    To explore the catalytic activity of heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks, three 3d-3d heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks based on the cooperative assembly of tetrahedral Cu4I4 and metal-carboxylate clusters with potential open metal sites were synthesized and described in detail. The structure of 1 consists of tetrahedral [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligands (INA = isonicotinate) linking unusual Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 clusters into a (4,12)-connected network. Compound 2 features a 2-fold interpenetrating 4-connected bbf-type framework based on new [Cu4I4(INA)2(DBO)2]2– metalloligands (DBO = 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane) and paddle-wheel Zn2(COO)4 units. The 2-fold interpenetrating diamond-type framework 3 is constructed from tetrahedral [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligand and Mn2(COO)4(H2O)2(DMF) unit with potential open Mn centers. All three framework materials have notable catalytic activity on the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes, and the catalytic properties of compound 1 have been studied in detail. The results reveal that the heterogeneous catalysis of the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes by using cluster-organic framework as catalyst is determined by several experimental factors, such as temperature, reaction time, and the incorporation of electron-withdrawing groups in aromatic aldehydes.

    Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs) are receiving increasing attention because of their potential applications in the areas of separation, heterogeneous catalysis, ion exchange, and gas storage.(1-4) With large internal surface areas and uniform pore and cavity sizes, MOFs share some of the catalytically relevant features of inorganic zeolites. Although MOF-based catalysis has become a hot topic in recent years, it is still in an immature phase and many detailed catalytic mechanisms are worthy of further exploration.(5)
    One promising point for MOF-based catalysis is the presence of active metal centers in the structure. For example, the catalytic activity of the well-known HKUST-1 and MIL-101 in the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde was due to the well-defined accessible Lewis acidic sites exposed to the inner surface.(6) Therefore, how to give birth to accessible active metal centers may be extremely challenging. An effective approach is to construct inorganic building blocks with possible unsaturated coordination sites, typically similar to the paddle-wheel Cu2(COO)4 unit combined with terminal molecules, the removal of which leads to active metal sites.(6) Another effective approach is to incorporate catalytic active metal sites into organic ligands, such as metalloporphyrins and other metalloligands with Schiff-base unit or binaphthyl unit.(7)
    Compared to single-metal-based metalloligands, the cluster-based metalloligands remain rarely known in the formation of MOFs with promising catalytic properties. As a tetrahedral cluster with accessible active metal centers, Cu4I4 bound with pyridine carboxylate ligand (L), is apt to form series new metalloligands, featured [Cu4I4(L)4], whose carboxylic groups can unite other metal centers to produce different inorganic building blocks. There is a useful strategy toward heterometallic MOFs to use organic ligands with different functional groups and different donor atoms, such as O and N atoms.(8, 9) Although a number of 3d-4f compounds containing Cu+ ions, lanthanide ions (Ln3+), and pyridine carboxylate ligands have been reported previously,(8, 9) the combination of this [Cu4I4(L)4] metalloligands with other transition metal ions to form new heterometallic 3d-3d compounds is rarely explored. Moreover, little experimental effort has focused on determining the heterometallic MOFs based on tetrahedral Cu4I4 cluster for effectively heterogeneous catalysis.
    In this work, we report three three-dimensional (3D) heterometallic cluster-organic frameworks, namely [(Cu4I4)3Zn8(μ4-O)(INA)12(H2O)4]·2(CH3COO)·x(guest) (1; INA = isonicotinate); [(Cu4I4)2Zn2(INA)4(DBO)6]·x(guest) (2; DBO = 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane); and [(Cu4I4)Mn2(INA)4(H2O)2DMF]·x(guest) (3). All three compounds consist of metal carboxylate building units linked by [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– or [Cu4I4(INA)2(DBO)2]2– metalloligands, but they exhibit distinct structural topologies because of the different conformation of inorganic building blocks. The catalytic activity of these compounds was evaluated by using the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes as a test reaction. Factors that have been considered in this context include (1) the creation of open metal sites on the framework, (2) reaction time, (3) reaction temperature, and (4) the incorporation of electron-withdrawing groups in aromatic aldehydes.

    General Procedures

    All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. All powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded on a Rigaku Dmax2500 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) with a step size of 0.05°. Thermogravimetric studies were carried out on a NETSCHZ STA-449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C/min under an air atmosphere. Gas adsorption measurement was performed in the ASAP (accelerated surface area and porosimetry) 2020 system. Fluorescence spectra were measured at room temperature with a HORIBA Jobin-Yvon FluoroMax-4 spectrometer. Conversion and yield values were determined by GC on a Supelco b-Dex 120 column.

    Synthesis of [(Cu4I4)3Zn8(μ4-O)(INA)12(H2O)4]·2(CH3COO)·x(guest) (1)

    A 20 mL vial containing CuI (0.095 g, 0.5 mmol), isonicotinic acid (0.062 g, 0.5 mmol), Zn(CH3COO)2·4H2O (0.080 g, 2 mmol), 5 drops triethylamine (TEA), 3 mL N,N-dimethyl formamide (DMF), and 1.5 mL ethanol was sealed and heated in an oven at 100 °C for 3 days. The vial was taken out and naturally cooled to room temperature. After washed with fresh ethanol, yellow polyhedral crystals were obtained in pure phase (Yield: 150 mg, 75% based on CuI).

    Synthesis of [(Cu4I4)2Zn2(INA)4(DBO)6]·x(guest) (2)

    A 20 mL vial containing CuI (0.095 g, 0.5 mmol), isonicotinic acid (0.062, 0.5 mmol), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.080 g, 2 mmol), 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane (0.121 g, 1 mmol), 3 mL DMF, and 1.5 mL ethanol was sealed and heated in an oven at 100 °C for 3 days. The vial was taken out and naturally cooled to room temperature. After being washed with fresh ethanol, yellow block crystals were obtained in pure phase (Yield: 60 mg, 47% based on CuI).

    Synthesis of [(Cu4I4)Mn2(INA)4(H2O)2DMF]·x(guest) (3)

    A 20 mL vial containing CuI (0.095 g, 0.5 mmol), isonicotinic acid (0.062, 0.5 mmol), Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O (0.080 g, 2 mmol), 3 mL DMF, and 1.5 mL ethanol was sealed and heated in an oven at 100 °C for 3 days. The vial was taken out and naturally cooled to room temperature. After being washed with fresh DMF, yellow club-shaped crystals were obtained in pure phase (Yield: 35 mg, 15% based on CuI).

    Diethylzinc Additions to Aromatic Aldehydes with Solid Catalyst

    Actived catalysts (20 mg) dried under vacuum at 100 °C for 8 h was placed in a 25 mL Schlenk flask and then vacuumized for 10 min. n-hexane (3 mL) was added via syringe under nitrogen. After the mixture was stirred for 30 min, aromatic aldehydes (0.5 mmol), and diethylzinc (1 M in n-hexane, 1 mL, 1 mmol) were added. The mixture was stirred under nitrogen at corresponding temperature and time and then quenched with saturated NH4Cl solution. The organic phase was separated and passed through a short silica gel column. An aliquot was analyzed by GC to give the conversion and yield values.

    X-ray Crystallography

    The diffraction data of 1–3 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. Absorption corrections were applied by SADABS. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined with full-matrix least-squares technique using SHELXTL. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined on F2 full-matrix least-squares using the SHELXTL-97 program package. The total interstitial solvent molecule contents were determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). Crystal data for 1: cubic, a = b = c = 18.9120(3) Å, V = 6764.14(19) Å3, T = 293(2) K, space group P4̅3n, Z = 2, 4145 reflections measured, 1416 independent reflections (Rint = 0.0355). The final R1 value was 0.0821 (I > 2σ(I)). The final wR(F2) value was 0.2199 (I > 2σ(I)). Crystal data for 2: orthorhombic, a = 28.7510(8) Å, b = 18.0562(4) Å, c = 16.7272(5) Å, V = 8683.7(4) Å3, T = 293(2) K, space group Cmca, Z = 8, 7588 reflections measured, 3541 independent reflections (Rint = 0.0247). The final R1 value was 0.0468 (I > 2σ(I)). The final wR(F2) value was 0.1439 (I > 2σ(I)). Crystal data for 3: monoclinic, a = 18.0043(8) Å, b = 21.1164(9) Å, c = 18.1594(14) Å, β = 93.344(5)°, V = 6892.2(7) Å3, T = 293(2) K, space group P2(1)/c, Z = 4, 16937 reflections measured, 8722 independent reflections (Rint = 0.0582). The final R1 value was 0.0577 (I > 2σ(I)). The final wR(F2) value was 0.1308 (I > 2σ(I)). The contribution of the disordered solvent molecules in 1–3 was subtracted from the reflection data by the SQUEEZE method as implanted in PLATON program. CCDC-893051–893053 (1–3) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/conts/retrieving.html.

    It is notable that the employment of mixed CuI and Zn2+ (or Mn2+) with INA ligand into such a solvothermal assembly system is based on our careful consideration. The hard and soft coordination mode of the INA ligand is widely known in many heterometallic 3d-4f coordination polymers.(8, 9) Here, our interest is to apply this special coordination mode of the INA ligand to create a new cluster-based 3d-3d system. From these three structures, we achieved two aspects: (1) the both anticipated tetrahedral [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– and [Cu4I4(INA)2(DBO)2]2– metalloligands can be in situ generated; (2) the second 3d metal center (Zn2+or Mn2+) is participated into the assembly with carboxylate groups to form additional metal carboxylate cluster including possible open metal sites.
    The structure of 1, where the tetrahedral [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligands link unusual Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 clusters into a (4,12)-connected network, is of particular interest. Following the hard and soft (Lewis) acids and bases (HSAB) theory, the INA N atom binds to a tetrahedral Cu center and its carboxylate O atoms coordinate to two Zn centers. The geometry of [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligand generated in situ is just tetrahedral (Figure 1a).
    One prominent feature in the structure of 1 is the presence of another 12-connected tetrahedral Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 cluster. Such a cluster is truly unusual and can be considered as a tetrahedral μ4-O2– center bringing four Zn2(COO)3 units together (Figure 1b). Compared to the famous Zn4(μ4-O) unit in MOF-5, this Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 cluster is a more super one with tetrahedral symmetry and higher connectivity. It is interesting to note that each Zn center in this cluster also exhibits tetrahedral coordination geometry with four oxygen atoms. Four vertical Zn centers of this cluster are all coordinated by water molecules that are possibly removed to generate open metal sites for catalysis.
    The resulting Cu4I4 cluster and Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 cluster are alternately linked each other through the INA ligand into a 3D framework (Figure 1d). From the viewpoint of structural topology, the whole framework of 1 can be topologically represented as a (4,12)-connected ith net with vertex symbol of (430.630.86)(46)3 by reducing each Cu4I4 cluster as a 4-connected node and each Zn8(μ4-O)(COO)12(H2O)4 cluster as a 12-connected node (Figure 1e).(10) Interestingly, the structural topology of 1 also can be considered from another point of view. If the μ4-O2– groups can be neglected in advance, the nodes in 1 have changed to Cu4I4 and Zn2(COO)3(H2O) clusters (Figures 1a and 1c). Such assembly between 3-connected Zn2(COO)3(H2O) node and 4-connected Cu4I4 node leads to a 3D open-framework with (3,4)-connected ctn topology (Figures S1 and S2 in the Supporting Information). For such an empty framework, structural interpenetration is difficult to avoid. Actually, a 4-fold interpenetration of these ctn-type frameworks is found in the whole structure by using the TOPOS program (Figure 1f). However, the assembly does not stop here, and the next net-to-net assembly occurs. Through the μ4-O2– centers, four interpenetrating ctn-type frameworks are coordinatively linked into one single framework with 4-connected self-penetrating topology (Figures 1d and f). Now each Zn2(COO)3 unit acts as a 4-connected node, and the Cu4I4(INA)4 metalloligand keeps its 4-connectivity. The resulting 4-connected net (denoted: tyx) derived from ctn subnets has a vertex symbol (86) and exhibits interesting self-penetrating feature (Figure 1f). To our knowledge, this new tyx topology has not been predicted before. The observation of such unusual ctn net to tyx net assembly is mainly ascribed to presence of the μ4-O2– group, which creates a chance of interpenetration. Furthermore, the self-penetrating feature of the tyx net is much easy to identify, because it is derived from the interpenetrating ctn nets.
    Despite the much high level of connectivity, the whole framework of 1 still has solvent-accessible volume approximately 1755.9 Å3 per unit cell (6764.1 Å3), being equal to about 26.0% of the total crystal volume estimated by PLATON program. The solvent guests in the free spaces were evidenced by TGA. The TGA curve of 1 showed the release of guest molecules between 25 and 250 °C, leading to a weight loss of 8.5% (Figure S3 in the Supporting Information). The phase purity of the bulk product of 1 was independently confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information).
    When additional DBO was used in the reaction system of 1, another new compound 2 with distinct structural feature was obtained. Our origin consideration on adding DBO for synthesis is to test the template effect of DBO, but the coordination of DBO with metal centers results in the formation of a new compound 2. The structure of 2 also consists of similar Cu4I4 clusters, but each Cu4I4 unit is surrounded by two INA and two DBO ligands, forming a new [Cu4I4(INA)2(DBO)2]2– metalloligand (Figure 2a). Moreover, the metal carboxylate cluster in 2 is just a paddle-wheel Zn2(COO)4 unit with two monodentately coordinate DBO ligands in two apical sites (Figure 2a). Each Cu4I4 cluster exhibits as a tetrahedral 4-connected node to link two other Cu4I4 clusters through two DBO ligands and two Zn2(COO)4 units through two INA ligands. The cooperative assembly between tetrahedral Cu4I4 cluster and 4-connected planar Zn2(COO)4 unit leads to an open framework with large empty spaces (Figure 2b). Topological analysis indicates that one simple framework of 2 features an unusual binodal (4,4)-connected bbf topology (Figure 2c).(10) Furthermore, 2-fold interpenetration is occurred in the whole framework, but the final framework still has a solvent-accessible volume approximately 2494.7 Å3 per unit cell (8683.7 Å3), being equal to about 28.7% of the total crystal volume estimated by PLATON program (Figure 2d). The TGA curve of 2 shows only slight release of guest molecules between 25 and 250 °C, because the big terminal DBO ligands occlude the entrance to each cavity (Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). The phase purity of the bulk product of 1 was independently confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information).
    Compound 3 was solvothermally synthesized by mixing HINA, Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O and CuI in DMF/ethanol solution at 100 °C for 3 days. The structure of 3 also contains two kinds of clusters—the familiar Cu4I4 and Mn2(COO)4(H2O)2(DMF) clusters. The in situ generated [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligand in 3 is almost the same with that in 1. Two independent Mn centers in the Mn2(COO)4(H2O)2(DMF) unit show octahderal coordination geometry. One Mn ion is fully coordinated by the carboxylate oxygen atoms, while another one is only bonded by three carboxylate oxygen atoms and has three additional coordination sites with two water molecules and one DMF molecule (Figure 3a). If each carboxylate group is treated as one connecting point, each Mn2(COO)4(H2O)2(DMF) unit has a pseudo-tetrahedral geometry and is thus connected to four Cu4I4 clusters in a tetrahedral fashion. Further tetrahedral assembly results in a diamondoid framework with large cages (Figures 3b and 3c). The edged Cu4I4···Mn2 distances in each cage are in the range from 9.759 to 12.200 Å, and the maximum pore dimension in each cage is about 15.0 Å. The resulting large cavities further induce 2-fold interpenetration of the framework, whose interpenetration vector runs along the c axis (Figure 3d). Despite 2-fold interpenetration, the structure generates 1D channel with a window size of 6 × 4.5 Å2 along the c-axis. The free space in the structure of 3 without guest molecules is 50.4%, as calculated by the PLATON program. These channels are occupied by the solvent molecules.
    Both TGA and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were carried out to examine the thermal behavior of 3 (Figures S7 and S8 in the Supporting Information). The TGA curve of 3 shows that the trapped and coordinated solvent molecules can be drastically removed before 310 °C (weight loss: 26.6%). After an obvious plateau between 310 and 350 °C, the framework decomposition started. However, such high porous material after dichloromethane exchange can hardly show any gas adsorption (9.1 cm3/g for CO2 at 273 K and 1 bar, 6.1 cm3/g for H2 and 4.8 cm3/g for N2 at 77 K and 1 bar), because of the relaxation of the 2-fold interpenetrating framework attested by PXRD measurements (Figure S8c and S9 in the Supporting Information).
    From the above structural description of three compounds, it is clear the formation of these cluster-based framework structures is influenced by multiple factors, such as the incorporation of auxiliary ligands or the usage of different metal centers. The distinct structural topologies between 1 and 2 are obviously associated with the incorporation of DBO ligand in the structure of 2. For 1 and 3, they have the same [Cu4I4(INA)4]4– metalloligands but different secondary metal centers (Zn2+ in 1 and Mn2+ in 3), so different frameworks are generated.
    The photoluminescent properties of the as-synthesized 1 and 2 were studied in the solid state with excitation wavelength at 350 nm at room temperature (Figures S10 in the Supporting Information). 1 and 2 display emissions with maximum at 564 and 547 nm, respectively. Unlike other previously reported Cu4I4L4 (L = ligand) cluster complexes with emission peaks occurring at ∼590 nm,(13) the emission peaks observed in 1 and 2 all exhibit obvious blue shift, which attributed to the cooperative effect between Cu4I4 unions and zinc-based clucters. As we know, no photoluminescent property of MOFs constructed by Cu4I4 and zinc-based clucters has been reported before.
    To evaluate the catalytic activity of compounds 1–3 activated at 100 °C under vacuum for 8 h, we used the diethylzinc addition to benzylaldehyde as a test reaction (Table 1 and Figure 4) and affirmed the corresponding alcohol as the principal product. In a typical reaction, 20 mg (0.48 mmol %) sample of 1 was used for the conversion of 0.5 mmol benzaldehyde in n-hexane at 0 °C. An excess of 100% ZnEt2 was employed with respect to the amount of benzaldehyde. The composition of the reaction mixture was determined by gas chromatography (GC) (Figure S11 in the Supporting Information). After 8 h, the reaction leaded to a 99.8% conversion of benzaldehyde, a 91.8% selectivity of product and a TOF of 6.5 h–1. The same reaction conditions were used for testing the catalytic activity of compound 2 (20 mg, 0.73 mmol %) and 3 (20 mg, 1.53 mmol %). The results show that catalytic activity of 2 (Conv. of 99.8%, selectivity of 94%, TOF of 8.5 h–1) is similar to that of 1 but much higher than that of 3 (Conv. of 98.2%, selectivity of 79.0%, TOF of 2.7 h–1). The lower selectivity in 3 may be attributed to its unstable porous framework. Moreover, the catalysts 1 and 2 show a higher activity and selectivity in the addition reaction of benzaldehyde than that obtained with other MOFs.(8b, 11) For example, the same reaction has also been catalyzed by a chiral MOF [Cd3Cl6L3]·Ti (L = (R)-6,6′-dichloro-2,2′-dihydroxy-1,1′-binaphthyl-4,4′-bipyridine) reported by Lin groups, giving high ee% of 83% and high yield of 99% but low TOF of 1.3 h–1 at room temperature; the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde catalyzed by the MOF {[Cd(L)2(DMF)2]·(ClO4)2·(2·DMF)}n (L = bis-(4-imidazol-1-yl-phenyl)-diazene) gave TOF of 4.3 h–1 in 14 h with 5 mmol % catalyst loading at 0 °C; the yields and TOFs of the cyanosilylation of benzaldehyde catalyzed the two activated Ln(btc) MOFs (btc =1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylates; Ln = Er, Yb) reported by Zou groups were always less than 61% and 3 h–1, respectively. Thus, compound 1 was used for a more comprehensive study because of its high yield and high catalytic activity. In addition to this remarkable high catalytic activity, 1 has the advantage that Zn(II) and Cu(I) cannot be easily reduced by benzaldehyde. Thus, the framework of 1 is intact after using as a catalyst, judging from the PXRD pattern of the catalyst after diethylzinc addition (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information). Compared to the PXRD pattern of the as-synthesized sample, a slim redistribution of the peak intensities, but no change of the peak positions was observed.

    To prove the heterogeneous mechanism of the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes, leaching test of 1 by removing the solid catalyst after 1 h and continuing reaction for another 8 h were performed. No further conversion was noted, proving heterogeneity of the catalyst (Figure S12 in the Supporting Information).
    A recycling test with three consecutive runs was performed to check if catalyst 1 could be reused. For all three reactions, the conversion after 8 h was always great (99%). A selectivity of 92.2% was achieved in the first run after 8 h. However, the selectivity in the following two runs decreased to 88.0% and 80.1%, respectively. The results show that catalyst 1 can be reused several times with a somewhat reduced activity.
    The catalytic activity of 1 at different reaction temperatures (0, 20, and 40 °C) were also checked (Figures S13 and S14 in the Supporting Information). For the reaction at 20 °C, 99.8% conversion of benzaldehyde was achieved within 4 h, but the selectivity decreased to 77.5%. Once the reaction temperature increased to 40 °C, 1 h was enough to get a complete conversion, but the selectivity further decreased to 54.7%. It is clear that reaction temperature strongly affects the reaction rate and selectivity. At lower temperature, the catalyst benefits from a longer lifetime to avoid deactivation processes while the reaction rate also decreases, maybe attributing to slower removal of products from active metallic sides.
    Aiming to determine the potential applicability of the protocol, the substrate scope of the diethylzinc addition reaction was then investigated with the use of three different aromatic aldehydes, 4-trifluoro-p-tolualdehyde, 4-chlorobenzaldehyde, and 4-methoxybenzaldehyde. The results are summarized in Table 1 and Figure 5. As shown in Table 1, entries 8–10, 99.8% conversion of 4-trifluoro-p-tolualdehyde was obtained within 3 h to give the corresponding alcohol in 92.0% selectivity. Compared with 4-trifluoro-p-tolualdehyde, 4-chlorobenzaldehyde gave a similar conversion within 4 h but a lower selectivity of 75.8%. However, the addition reaction of 4-methoxybenzaldehyde with diethylzinc afforded the incomplete conversion of 85.1% and low selectivity of 74.2% within 8 h. The results indicate a strong correlation between the electronic properties of substituents and the reaction rates.(12) Thus, electron withdrawing substituted groups on the armoatic aldehyde, such as −CF3 and −Cl, favor the addition reaction, while electron donating group, such as −OMe, disfavor the reaction.

    In summary, through the cooperative assembly of tetrahedral Cu4I4 and metal-carboxylate clusters, three 3d-3d heterometallic MOFs with potential catalytic meter centers and interesting structural topologies have successfully synthesized and structurally characterized. All three cluster-organic frameworks have notable catalytic activity on the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes, and the catalytic properties of compound 1 with unusual cluster-based (4,12)-connected structure have been studied in detail. The results reveal that the heterogeneous catalysis of the diethylzinc addition to aromatic aldehydes by using MOF as catalyst is determined by several experimental factors, such as temperature, reaction time, and the incorporation of electron-withdrawing groups in aromatic aldehydes. It also demonstrates that cluster-based MOFs are potential catalysts for future applications.
