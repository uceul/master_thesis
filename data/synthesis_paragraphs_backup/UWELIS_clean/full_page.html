Abstract

A new three‐dimensional (3D) porous framework [Zn(INAIP)]·DMA·H2O (1) [INAIP = 5‐(isonicotinamido)isophthalate, DMA = N,N′‐dimethylacetamide] was synthesized by solvothermal methods and characterized by single‐crystal and powder X‐ray diffraction, as well as thermogravimetric analysis. The results of X‐ray diffraction analyses revealed that complex 1 has an unusual 3D architecture with the (3,6)‐connected rutile (rtl) topology. The adsorption behavior shows that compound 1 exhibits selective adsorptions of CO2 over N2 after the removal of the solvent molecules within the pores.

Introduction

Nowadays, the design and synthesis of new porous coordination polymers (PCPs) have attracted considerable attention due to their potential applications in gas sorption and storage as functional porous solid materials.1–5 Furthermore, the design and control of metal atoms bridged by multidentate ligands often reveal extended polymeric networks with void spaces that are ideally suited for such applications. More recently, extensive studies were carried out to construct PCPs with novel topologies and flexible and dynamic characterization for gas adsorption, and polycarboxylate and polypyridine ligands are the most extensively exploited ligands.6–10

Based on the above discussion, as an extension of our continuous research on the crystal engineering of d10 metal‐organic coordination polymers, in this paper, we report a novel 3D porous framework [Zn(INAIP)]·DMA·H2O (1) based on 5‐(isonicotinamido)isophthalate (INAIP) (DMA = N,N′‐dimethylacetamide) and ZnII salts synthesized by solvothermal reactions.

Experimental Section

Materials and Instrumentation: All commercially available chemicals are of reagent grade and used as received without further purification. Solvents were purified according to the standard methods. C, H, and N analyses were made with a Perkin–Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded with a Bruker model Vector22 FT‐IR spectrophotometer using KBr disks. Thermogravimetric analyses were performed on a simultaneous SDT 2960 thermal analyzer in a nitrogen atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1. Power X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were measured with a Shimadzu XRD‐6000 X‐ray diffractometer with Cu‐Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation at room temperature. Gas sorption isotherms were measured with a volumetric adsorption apparatus (Bel‐max).

Preparation of [Zn(INAIP)]·DMA·H2O (1): A mixture containing Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (30.1 mg, 0.1 mmol) and H2INAIP (28.6 mg, 0.1 mmol) dissolved in DMA (6 mL) was sealed in a 16 mL Teflon lined stainless steel container and heated at 120 °C for 4 days. After the reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature, pale yellow crystals were collected by filtration, washed by DMA, and air‐dried. Yield 48 %, based on ZnII. IR (KBr): = 3444 (s), 1671 (m), 1618 (s), 1572 (s), 1426 (m), 1366 m), 1287 (w), 1063 (w), 1029 (w), 853 (w), 782 (w), 730 (m), 682 (w), 609 (w) cm–1. C18H19ZnN3O7: calcd. C 47.50, H 4.18, N 9.24 %; found C 47.46, H 4.11, N 9.27 %.

X‐ray Structure Analysis: The data collection for 1 was performed with a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.071073 nm) at 18(2) °C. The absorption corrections were carried out by program SADABS.11 The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares technique using SHELXS‐97 and SHELXL‐97 programs.12 All the hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and refined isotropically using the riding model. Result and Discussion

Crystal Structure Description

The single‐crystal X‐ray structural analysis revealed that the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one ZnII atom, one INAIP2– ligand, one free water molecule and one DMA molecule. The structure of the complex 1 is shown in Figure  1.

It can be clearly seen that each ZnII ion is coordinated in a square‐pyramidal fashion by four carboxylate oxygen atoms at the basal positions (O2, O3A, O4B, and O5C) and one pyridyl nitrogen atom at the apical position (N1D). It is interesting that each pair of ZnII ions is bridged by four carboxylate groups to generate a dinuclear paddle‐wheel SBU (secondary building unit), and four adjacent SBUs are interlinked into a square‐shaped structural unit [Zn2(COO)2]4 (Figure  2a) through bridging INAIP2– ligands neglecting the coordination of 5‐isonicotinamido groups, which results in a metal‐carboxylate layer. Additionally, the 2D 44 layers are further joined together by 5‐isonicotinamido groups to result in the formation of the 3D framework. The resultant 3D framework contains one‐dimensional (1D) channels along the a axis (Figure  2b). The cavities of the framework are occupied by DMA and water molecules. After omitting the solvent molecules, PLATON13 analysis revealed that the 3D porous structure was composed of large voids of 1371.0 Å that represent 56.2 % per unit cell volume (Figure  2b).

A better insight into the nature of 1 can be achieved by the application of topological approach, reducing multidimensional structures to simple node‐and‐linker nets. As discussed above, the Zn2 subunits can be regarded as one node. As each Zn2 is surrounded by six adjacent INAIP2– ligands through sharing Zn2 subunits, thus each Zn2 is a six‐connected node. On the other hand, each INAIP2– ligand can be considered as a three‐connecting node since it links three Zn2 subunits. Consequently, according to the calculation of TOPOS,14 the framework of 1 belongs to a uninodal (3,6)‐connected rtl type of topology with Schläfli symbol of (4·62)2(42·610·83) (Figure  2c).

Thermal Analyses, X‐ray Powder Diffraction, Gas Sorption, and Photoluminescent Properties

The results of thermogravimetric (TG) analysis indicate that complex 1 lost its noncoordinated DMA and water molecules in the 30–200 °C temperature range (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The weight loss found of 23.11 % is in agreement with that calculated (23.09 %). After the loss of the DMA and H2O molecules, the 3D framework was stable up to 340 °C, followed by another weight loss after that temperature. PXRD was used to check the purity of complex 1. The results show that all signals displayed in the measured patterns at room temperature closely match those in the simulated patterns generated from single‐crystal diffraction data, indicating single phases of 1 were formed, as shown in Figure S2.

As discussed above, the frameworks of 1 is stable even at temperatures of more than 340 °C. Before gas adsorption tests, the activation for removing the guest molecules from the pores by heating under vacuum is a required pre‐treatment and the as‐synthesized crystal samples were heated at an optimized temperature of 140 °C for 20 hours. To verify whether the framework of 1 can be sustained after the removal of the solvent molecules, PXRD patterns were measured and the results are shown in Figure S2. The framework of 1 still has good crystallinity without solvent molecules, and thus the gas sorption property of 1 was investigated. The N2 and CO2 adsorption isotherms for 1 are shown in Figure  3. The results indicate that no N2 uptake was observed at 273 K, and hysteresis behavior, which is characteristic for type‐H3. However, it was found that significant amounts of CO2 (273 K) were adsorbed and the isotherm presents typical type‐I curves,15 which is characteristic for a microporous material. The amounts of CO2 uptake increase abruptly at the beginning and reach to 46.78 cm3(STP)·g–1 at 1.0 atom for 1, meaning that approximately 0.47 CO2 molecules per formula unit were adsorbed, which indicates a uniform microporous structure. The gas sorption isotherms show no obvious hysteresis between the adsorption‐desorption curves. It is noteworthy that no N2 was adsorbed in spite of the channel size larger than the kinetic diameter of N2, therefore, the selective sorption of CO2 rather than N2 gas can also be attributed to the significant quadrupole moment of CO2 (–1.4 × 10–39 C·m2), which induces specific interactions with the host framework to open up the channels.16

The photoluminescent properties for 1 and Na2INAIP were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. As shown in Figure  4, intense emission bands were observed at 465 nm (λex = 390 nm) for Na2INAIP ligand and 480 nm (λex = 390 nm) for 1, respectively. These emissions can be attributed to neither metal‐to‐ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand‐to‐metal charge transfer (LMCT) because the ZnII ions are in d10 configuration and difficult to oxidize or to reduce. Therefore, the emission observed in 1 is attributed to the π–π* intraligand photoluminescence due to its resemblance to that of Na2INAIP ligand. The results suggest that complex 1 may be a good candidate of potential blue‐fluorescent materials due to its highly thermally stable and insoluble in common solvents.
