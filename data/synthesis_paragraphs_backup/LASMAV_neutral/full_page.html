Unprecedented binodal (7,9)-connected network based on distinct tricobalt(II) clusters: structure, topology and cooperative magnetism†

Qing Chen a, Wei Xue a, Bao-Ying Wang a, Ming-Hua Zeng *b and Xiao-Ming Chen *a
aMOE Key Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry, State Key Laboratory of Optoelectronic Materials and Technologies, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, 510275, China. E-mail: cxm@mail.sysu.edu.cn
bKey Laboratory for the Chemistry and Molecular Engineering of Medicinal Resources (Ministry of Education of China), School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Guangxi Normal University, Guilin, 541004, P. R. China. E-mail: zmh@mailbox.gxnu.edu.cn

Received 8th August 2011 , Accepted 23rd November 2011

First published on 11th January 2012

A new Co(II)-based coordination polymer, [{Co3(ina)7.5(μ3–O)}{Co3(ina)2.5(μ3–OH)(μ–OH)(C2H5OH)(H2O)2}3]NO3·3C2H5OH·24H2O (1, also denoted as MCF-39), has been synthesized from the solvothermal reaction of cobalt(II) nitrate and the ligand isonicotinic acid (Hina) with the presence of hmq (2-(hydroxymethyl)-quinolin-8-ol) as a necessary additive. The crystal structure of 1 consists of the 7- and 9-connected trinuclear clusters, and exhibits the first highly-connected binodal network with (34·49·56·62)3(36·415·515) topology. Magnetic studies show that the spin canting and spin-glass-like behaviours were found in 1, and the cooperative effect among intra- and inter-trimer could greatly influence the bulk magnetic behaviour of the three-dimensional (3D) framework.

Introduction

Cluster-based coordination polymers have currently attracted extensive attention because of their intriguing architectures and potential applications as functional materials.1,2 The utilization of metal clusters as secondary building blocks (SBUs) has proved to be a feasible method to construct unusual coordination polymers with unique network topologies and desirable properties.2,3 In this field, the subtle changes in the self-assembly process can often cause unexpected and interesting results, which are important to make the source of topological diversity and supramolecular isomersim.4 To date, the known nets with high frequency are topologically symmetrical and, in particular, have the nodes with lower connected numbers (no more than six).5 The construction of cluster-based frameworks with more complicated connectivity still remains challenging due to the difficulties to acquire highly-connected nodes and/or the obstruction of the variability and steric hindrance of organic linkers, especially for a highly-connected network with different kinds of nodes.6–8 On the other hand, the assembly of magnetic clusters with organic linkers may offer an attractive route to open up interesting possibilities for magnetism and related properties. This approach provides a chance to investigate bulky magnetic behaviour, which is modulated by the cooperative interaction distributed in the three-dimensional (3D) system.9,10

Pyridyl-carboxylates have been confirmed as suitable ligands to assemble multifunctional coordination frameworks due to their flexible and multi-directional coordinated character. Therefore, the combination of pyridyl-carboxylates and oxo-bridged metal clusters has the potential capabilities for the construction of highly-connected nets with interesting topologies.8a,10c Recently, we have successfully utilized isonicotinic acid (Hina) and cobalt(II) ion to create an 8-connected network, [Co3(ina)4(μ3-OH)(C2H5OH)3]NO3·C2H5OH·3H2O (MCF-38), composed of a new oxo-centered tricobalt(II) cluster as SBU, which features interesting active metal sites.8a As a continuous work, we describe herein an unprecedented (7,9)-connected network, [{Co3(ina)7.5(μ3-O)}{Co3(ina)2.5(μ3-OH)(μ-OH)(C2H5OH)(H2O)2}3]NO3·3C2H5OH·24H2O (1, also denoted as MCF-39), obtained by similar solvothermal reaction of MCF-38, in which the 3D coordination framework is based on the distinct [8 ± 1]-connected trimers; the trimers in 1 can be viewed as evolved forms of the 8-connected oxo-centered trimer in MCF-38. Furthermore, the interesting cooperative magnetic behaviours in such a 3D system is demonstrated.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

Solvents and reagents were of reagent grade and used as received. Infrared spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker TENSOR 27 Fourier transform infrared spectrometry (FT-IR) spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer (C, H, N). Thermogravimetry analyses (TGA) were performed on a Netzsch TG 209 instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were performed on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation. The magnetic susceptibility data of random orientation powder samples were collected in the temperature range of 2.0–300 K with Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 SQUID magnetometer. The diamagnetic corrections were estimated from the Pascal's constants.11

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (1 mmol, 0.291 g), Hina (1 mmol, 0.121 g), hmq (2-(hydroxymethyl)-quinolin-8-ol, 0.25 mmol, 0.044 g), triethylamine (0.20 mL) and EtOH (10 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel parr bomb at 140 °C for 96 h which was then slowly cooled to room temperature. Purple prismatic crystals of 1 were obtained in ca. 5% yield based on Hina. X-Ray crystallography

Diffraction intensities of all compounds were collected on a Rigaku R-AXIS SPIDER Image Plate diffractometer (Mo-Kα, λ 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were applied by using the SADABS program. The structures were solved with direct methods and refined with a full-matrix least-squares technique with the SHELXTL program package.12 All nonhydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The solvent molecules in the compounds were disordered and located from difference maps. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically (C–H 0.96 Å). An O atom of the carboxylate group was treated with disorder models of two equivalent parts. The coordinated EtOH and water molecules jointly occupy the coordination site of Co3, this disorder was modeled with the occupational ratio refined as 50:50. The disordered guest molecules and nitrate anions cannot be modeled and were treated by the SQUEEZE routine,13 and the amount of them were determined by TGA results and elemental analyses. Results and discussion

Synthesis

On the basis of the solvothermal synthesis of MCF-38, equal molar amounts of Co(NO3)2, Hina and triethylamine were mixed in EtOH, with hmq added as an auxiliary starting material. The results afforded purple prismatic crystals of 1, with distinct morphology compared to the symbiotic product of MCF-38. Two phases could be facilely separated (Fig. 1). Interestingly, the synthesis of 1 relies on subtle control over various solvothermal parameters, especially the introduction of hmq, which may act as a chelator to slow down the release of free coordination metal sites. Therefore, it can be reasonably speculated that hmq is a necessary coordination buffer in the reaction system of 1.

Crystal structure

X-Ray crystal structure analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in the hexagonal space groupP63/m. The asymmetric unit contains two half Co(II) ions on mirror planes and one Co(II) ion in a general position, two and a half ina ligands, one hydroxyl group, one-sixth oxygen group, a half coordinated EtOH molecule and one coordinated water molecule. There are two independent ina moieties in general positions and a half ina ligand lying across a mirror plane. The counter nitrate anions and some solvent molecules are located in the crystal lattice. The remarkable feature of the structure is the presence of two configuratively different tricobalt(II) clusters [Co3(μ3–OH)(μ–OH)(C2H5OH)(H2O)2(O2CR′)3(pyR′′)4] (trimer-1) and [Co3(μ3–O)(O2CR′)6(pyR′′)3] (trimer-2) with O2CR′ and pyR′′ representing the O- and N-donor groups, respectively (Fig. 2). All the Co(II) ions are six-coordinated in distorted octahedral geometry. In trimer-1, three Co(II) ions are linked by one μ3–OH bridge (Co2–O2 2.120(5) Å, Co3–O2 2.038(7) Å), one μ–OH bridge (Co2–O3 2.196(5) Å), three carboxylate groups (Co–O 1.966(1)–2.098(5) Å) and four pyridyl groups (Co2–N 2.161(8)–2.164(6) Å) to build an unprecedented tricobalt cluster with approximate Cs symmetry. Meanwhile, O1 atom is at the site with a crystallographic −6 symmetry, the central μ3–O group is shared by three Co atoms to form a planar Co3(μ3–O) cluster core (Co1–O1 2.0281(1) Å, Co–O1–Co 120°) with C3 symmetry, which is encapsulated by six carboxylate groups and three ina pyridyl groups to furnish trimer-2, and this kind of highly symmetric trinuclear SBU is rare.14 Each trimer-1 displays a similar capped trigonal-prismatic coordination motif, which is enclosed by seven adjacent tricobalt clusters, containing four trimer-1 and three trimer-2. On the other hand, the trimer-2 shows a tricapped trigonal-prismatic geometry, which is connected to nine trimer-1via ina ligands (Fig. 3a). Considering trimer-1 and trimer-2 with a ratio of 1:3 for the 7- and 9-connected nodes, the overall framework of 1 can be simplified as a unique binodal (7,9)-connected net with a new (34·49·56·62)3(36·415·515) topology analyzed by TOPOS (Fig. 3b).15 To the best of our knowledge, this new topology is not only unobserved so far, but also not theoretically predicted in the databases RCSR, EPINET and TOPOS. Therefore, it fills a lacuna for binodal coordination nets.16

Fig. 3 (a) Views of the two trimers in 1, where the connected modes of the SBUs are shown by their relevant polyhedra in different colours. (b) The 3D structure and binodal (7,9)-connected topological net of 1.

The types of M3(μ3–O)(O2CR)nLmclusters (M = Cr, Fe, Ni, Coetc.) have been widely used as 3- and 6-connected SBUs in the assembly of coordination polymers.17 However, the higher connected forms have been rarely reported besides the 9-connected mode.13 It can be seen from the detailed structural comparison between MCF-38 and 1 that, ina adopt the same μ3:η1:η1:η1 coordinated mode, the largest dihedral angles between the carboxylate and pyridyl groups are 22.1° and 18.7° in MCF-38 and 1, respectively, showing the flexibility of ina ligand. In addition, it has been confirmed that the 8-connected SBU in MCF-38 involves multiple active metal sites for adsorption due to the labile apical solvent molecules. Thus, in combination with the flexible linkers and active metal centres, even subtle changes occurring in the coordination sites may give rise to structural rearrangement of the SBUs, resulting in distinct topological types of the ultimate networks.

As shown by previous studies, the replacement of an ina linker by terminal ligand on the trimer may lead to the evolvement from the 8-connected SBU observed in MCF-38 to [8 − 1] viz. 7-connected motif of the trimer-1 in 1. Moreover, completing all the available coordination sites by ina linkers, the highest [8 + 1] viz. 9-connected mode of trimer-2 in 1 is created (Fig. 2). Although the yield of 1 is quite low, this unexpected finding not only provides valuable insights into the highly connected network topology, but also is useful to explore the flexibility of cluster-based SBUs, and the above serendipitous assembly of the unprecedented product 1 shows the significance for the crystal engineering.

Thermal stability

The 3D framework of 1 has irregular void (Vvoid = 39%), affording 3D intersecting open channels with a largest dimension of ca. 5.0 Å (excluding the van der Waals radii), which are occupied by EtOH and water molecules (Fig. 4). The TGA curve of 1 displays that the weight losses of the guests and coordinated solvent molecules are not well resolved. The step from 30–110 °C with ca. 16.0% weight loss may be attributed to the release of guest EtOH and water molecules (weight ca. 16.2%), and then that of 8.5% corresponds to the loss of coordinated EtOH and water molecules up to ∼250 °C (ca. 7.0%). After 320 °C, the framework of 1 eventually decomposes (Fig. S2†). However, up to now, the very low yield of 1 still obstructs further study of sorption properties.

Magnetic studies

The magnetic data were measured on the polycrystalline sample of 9.9 mg from crushed single crystals of 1. The χmT value for Co3 unit at 300 K is 8.64 cm3 K mol−1 (Fig. 5a), similar to that of MCF-38 (9.14 cm3 K mol−1), which is much larger than that (5.63 cm3 K mol−1) expected for three magnetically isolated high-spin Co(II) ions (S = 3/2). This is a common phenomenon for Co(II) ion due to its strong spin–orbit coupling interaction.18 As the temperature decreases, the χmT gradually decreases to 11 K, then, after a slight increase, it drops rapidly to 2 K with a minimum value of 1.48 cm3 K mol−1. Between 70 and 300 K, The χm curve obeys the Curie–Weiss law with a C value of 10.8(1) cm3 mol−1 K and θ value of −73.9(1) K (Fig. 5a), and the large negative θ value confirms the strong antiferromagnetic exchanges and the competitive effects between Co(II) ions. The slight increase at the low temperatures of 1 suggests a spin phase transition. However, there is no obvious anomaly in the dc susceptibility of MCF-38. The χmT value of 1 is dependent on the applied field at low temperatures, being larger at smaller applied fields (Fig. 5b inset), suggesting a spin canting behaviour induced by the competitive effect of intracluster magnetic exchanges, which is consistent with the triangular geometry structural characteristic formed by three Co atoms in each SBU through the μ3-oxo-bridges (Fig. 6).19

To further investigate the phase transformation at low temperature, the field-cooled (FC) and zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization measurements were performed for 1 at 50 Oe in the 2–20 K range (Fig. 5b). Significant divergence between the FC and ZFC data in 20 Oe was observed below ca. 8.0 K for 1, which indicates a possible phase transition from a paramagnetic state to either a long-range ordered, a spin-glass or a superparamagnetic state.20 The ac magnetic measurements were performed for 1 in an ac driving field of 6 Oe at different frequencies (Fig. 5d), and both the real part and imaginary part of ac susceptibility reveal a peak at ca. 6.0 K, which is slightly frequency-dependent. This frequency dependence can be quantitatively studied by the equation ϕ = ΔTp/[TpΔ(logf)], where Tp is the peak temperature and f is the frequency. The parameter ϕ is 0.048, corresponding to a canonical spin-glass.21 However, for MCF-38, even down to temperatures of 2 K, no sharp transition indicative of magnetic order has been observed in the ac susceptibilities.

The spin canting behaviour of 1 was also further verified by field-dependent isothermal magnetization (Fig. 5c). The magnetization at 2 K first increases steeply at the initial low field, and then increases slowly at the higher fields, which is a feature of spin-canting antiferromagnets. Finally, the magnetization value 2.35 Nβ achieved at the highest field (7 T), is significantly lower than the expected 9 Nβ of Co3 species.8a Upon the demagnetization process, a small hysteresis loop is observed at 2 K with the remnant magnetization Mr = 0.007 Nβ and the coercive field Hc = 45 Oe (Fig. 5c inset), which is normal for the canted antiferromagnets.10 Being different from 1, no magnetic hysteresis loop was observed in MCF-38.

All the dc and ac measurements reveal that 1 exhibits unusual magnetic behaviours and can be ascribed to the canted antiferromagnet with spin-glass-like relaxation, while a traditional antiferromagnetic behaviour was shown in MCF-38. Why did the two compounds exhibit different magnetic behaviours, despite having similar μ3-oxo-centeral bridged tricobalt(II) clusters with frustrated triangular geometries? Two major factors must be considered. Firstly, different from the quasi-equilateral triangular Co3 core in MCF-38, the presence of the μ–OH bridges makes the three spins sitting at the corners of isosceles triangle in Co3 core of trimer-1 in 1, and this significant distortion weakens the cooperative spin frustration effect. Secondly, the subsequent change in the inter-cluster arrangement is triggered by the geometrical changes in the SBUs, yielding more effective non-compensated moments and resultant moments through the 3D networks of 1 with more complicated connectivity (Fig. 6).10a,22 So, the changes in symmetries of Co3 SBUs and their interconnected ways via ina linkers in the 3D networks greatly influence the effective magnetic interactions and bring about different cooperative magnetic behaviours in these two systems, which is consistent with those found in the similar systems containing oxo-centered trinuclear clusters.17,23

In this work, correlation between two kinds of Co3 clusters in 1 results in a spin-glass-like form, which is interesting, with similar behaviour previously observed in a few cases including Cu2(OH)3(CmH2m+1COO) (m = 7, 9 or 11), [Co8(μ3–OH)4(SO4)2(dcpbpy)4(H2O)4]·12DMF·4EtOH·24H2O (H2dcp-bpy = 2,6-di-p-carboxyphenyl-4,4′-bipyridine), Co4(mna)4(H2O) (mna = 2-mercaptonicotinate) and [Ni2(fum)2(bpt)2(H2O)]·3H2O (fum = fumaric ion, bpt = 1H-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole).10c,24 The frustrated geometric arrangement of metal spins with antiferromagnetic coupling is the main source of the spin-glass-like behaviour. Some other Co(II) cluster-based coordination polymers exhibiting interesting magnetic properties have been reported in the literature (Table 2). For example, the special slow relaxation and quasi-2D XY magnetic properties have been shown in an 8-connected 3D network with the Co4 cluster as the node.22a The assembly of M2 subunits created a chiral six-connected magnetic network, in which the magnetism was tuned by mixed-metal (M = Co(II) or Ni(II)) compositions of the solid solutions.10a Apparently, the magnetic properties can be changed by the strength of interaction, the dimensionality of correlation, as well as the short or long range of correlation. Quantitative evaluation of the observed properties remains the next important task in understanding the produced materials.9b

Conclusion

This work demonstrates an unprecedented finding of the first (7,9)-connected metal–organic network, structurally experienced the evolution from previously reported 8-connected trimer to current “8 ± 1”-connected trimers. The modification of the SBUs by fine-tuning the reaction conditions reported herein provides new perspectives on the preparation of highly-connected architectures based on new types of cluster-based SBUs, as well as the significance of accidental discovery in crystal engineering. On the other hand, the structural variability of the cluster-based SBUs can not only lead to topological diversity in the final frameworks, but also have significant impact on cooperative magnetism in the frustrated systems. The spin canting and spin-glass-like behaviours of 1 show unambiguously that the short- or long-range correlation of similar cluster-based SBUs in 3D networks can make significant impact on different bulk cooperative magnetism in the frustrated systems.
