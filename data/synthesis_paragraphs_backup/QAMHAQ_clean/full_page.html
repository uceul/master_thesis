A pillared-layer framework with high uptake and selective sorption of light hydrocarbons†

Qing-Rong Ding and Fei Wang *
State Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, P. R. China. E-mail: wangfei04@fjirsm.ac.cn

Received 19th January 2016 , Accepted 9th March 2016

First published on 9th March 2016

A new pillared-layer metal–organic framework [Zn2(NH2-BTB)(2-nim)] (1) was successfully synthesized based on mixed ligands, where binuclear [Zn2(CO2)3]+ cluster-based cationic layers [Zn2(NH2-BTB)]+ (NH2-H3BTB = 1,3,5-(three-benzoic acid)aniline) are connected by 2-nitroimidazoles. Compound 1 shows high uptake and good adsorption selectivities for C3/C1 and C2/C1. Particularly, the C2H2 uptake capacity of 1 is up to 152 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and the selectivity for C3H8/CH4 is over 85 at room temperature.

Introduction

In recent years, porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted much attention not only due to their fascinating architectures and intriguing topologies,1 but also due to their potential applications in selective gas storage and separation.2 Light hydrocarbons are very important raw materials for the chemical industry and global climate.3 Methane, as the principal component of natural gas, is the important alternative fuel. C2 hydrocarbons (C2s) play a crucial role in industrial processes. High purity hydrocarbon gas is a prerequisite for these chemical procedures.4 So it is necessary to explore materials for highly efficient storage and separation of hydrocarbons.

Compared to distillation-based technologies, adsorptive separation is widely considered as a more environment-friendly and cost-efficient alternative.5 To date, a variety of porous adsorbents, such as zeolites, carbon materials, and diatomite, have been investigated for hydrocarbon separation.6 A number of MOFs have been studied for applications in the purification and storage of the C1, C2 and C3 light hydrocarbons.7 However, the applications of these promising materials are limited by the separation ratio of C3/C1 or C2/C1 compared to the commercial materials.8 More efforts are needed to improve the selectivity of light hydrocarbons and enhance the gas uptake capacity.

Here, one pillared-layer MOF [Zn2(NH2-BTB)(2-nim)] (1, NH2-H3BTB = 1,3,5-(three-benzoic acid)aniline, 2-nim = 2-nitroimidazole) was designed and synthesized via the method of charge balance and mixed ligands. The rigid tritopic aromatic polycarboxylate ligands coordinate binuclear zinc ions to form a cationic layer [Zn2(NH2-BTB)]n with special tritopic paddle-wheel zinc clusters, then the layers are further connected to a 3D neutral pillared-layer framework by 2-nitroimidazole pillars. The porous material of 1 exhibits high CO2 and light hydrocarbon storage capacity as well as good C3H8/CH4, C2H6/CH4, C2H4/CH4 and C2H2/CH4 selective separations. Particularly, the selectivity of C3H8/CH4 is over 85 at room temperature and the C2H2 uptake capacity of 1 is up to 152 cm3 g−1 at 273 K, which is the highest one ever reported.

Experimental section

Material and instrumentation

All reagents and solvents used in this work were commercially available. 1,3,5-(Tri-benzoic acid)aniline was synthesized via the Suzuki reaction.9 Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected on a MiniFlex-II diffractometer using Cu (λ = 1.54178 Å) radiation with a speed of 1° min−1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed under nitrogen by using a Netzsch STA449C equipped with a platinum pan and heating at a rate of 10 °C per minute. Gas sorption experiments were carried out with Accelerated Surface Area and Porosimetry (ASAP) 2020 system, and the materials maintain the crystallinity after the removal of solvent molecules. The N2 sorption isotherms were acquired in the pressure range of P/P0 from 0.01 to 0.99 at 77 K in a liquid nitrogen bath. The gas sorption experiments of CO2, CH4, C2H2, C2H4, C2H6, C3H8 and N2 were conducted at 273 K in an ice-water bath or at 297 K. Elemental analyses were performed on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer.
Synthesis of [Zn2(NH2-BTB)(2-nim)] (1). A mixture of 2-nitroimidazole (2-nim, 0.0156 g, 0.14 mmol), NH2-H3BTB (0.0156 g, 0.036 mmol), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.0416 g, 0.14 mmol), tetraethylammonium bromide (0.075 mmol, 0.0156 g), which was used to facilitate the crystallization of the product, and DMF/H2O (v:v = 3:0.5, 3.5 mL) was heated in a 20 mL scintillation vial at 100 °C for 24 hours, followed by cooling to room temperature. The resulting mixture was washed with DMF and water, and colorless block crystals were collected and dried in air. Calcd for [Zn2(NH2-BTB)][2-nim]·DMF·6H2O: C, 45.32; H, 4.26; N, 8.04; found: C, 44.97; H, 4.54; N, 8.37.
X-ray diffraction analysis. Single-crystal X-ray structural measurement analysis was performed on an Agilent SuperNova X-ray diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λCu-Kα = 1.54178 Å) at 100 K. The structure was solved using direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 by using the SHELX-97 program package.10 Non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of the ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. Part of solvent molecules in the structure was randomly dispersed, and thus their positions were impossible to refine using conventional discrete-atom models. The SQUEEZE option of PLATON was used to eliminate the contribution of disordered guest molecules to the reflection intensities.11 Crystallographic data for compound 1 are listed in Table S1.†
Gas selectivity. The selectivities of C3 and C2 over CH4 were calculated by using the ideal adsorption solution theory (IAST).12 IAST is a method for predicting the adsorption equilibria for components in a mixture using only single-component adsorption data at the same temperature and on the same adsorbent.

Results and discussion

Compound 1 crystallizes in the space group P21/m and features a 3D neutral framework with dinuclear Zn2(CO2)3 units. As shown in Fig. 1, the asymmetric unit of 1 contains one zinc atom, a half NH2-BTB and a half 2-nitroimidazole molecule. Two zinc cations are eight-coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from three NH2-BTB3− ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two 2-nitroimidazoles ligands, forming a similar paddle-wheel building unit Zn2(CO2)3. Compound 1 is a typical layered-pillar structure, in which the axial coordination sites are ligated by μ2-im groups which act as a pillar inter-connecting the Zn2(NH2-BTB)-layers in the a axis to form a three dimensional neutral framework [Zn2(NH2-BTB)(2-nim)] (Fig. 1b). Due to the large size of the NH2-BTB3− ligand, the pores are large enough for a second net, and two such frameworks are mutually interpenetrated (Fig. 1c). From a topological point of view, the [Zn2(CO2)3]+ paddle-wheel-unit can be considered as a 5-connected node, the NH2-BTB3− anion ligand can be reduced as the 3-connected node and the 2-nitroimidazole ligand can be reduced as a line. Therefore, the framework of 1 can be simplified as a 2-fold interpenetrated hexagonal-planar (3,5)-connected hms net with the point (Schläfli) symbol of (63) (69·8) (Fig. 1d).
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environment in compound 1; (b) 3D pillared-layer framework of 1 along the a axis (blue pillar, 2-nitroimidazole; green hexahedron, [Zn2(CO2)3]+ cluster); (c) 3D framework of 1 along the b axis; (d) 2-fold interpenetrated (3,5)-connected hms net of 1.

In order to confirm that the crystal structures are truly the representatives of the crystal materials, the as-synthesized samples of 1 have been characterized by powder X-ray diffraction. As shown in Fig. S2,† the experimental PXRD pattern is in good agreement with the corresponding simulated pattern, implying that the synthesized materials and the measured single crystals are the same. The after-activated PXRD patterns also correspond well to the result simulated from the single crystal data (Fig. S2†), indicating the structural stability of 1.

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 was performed on polycrystalline samples to determine their thermal stability from 25 to 800 °C. The TGA curve of 1 indicated that a weight loss of 20% occurred in the temperature range of 100–200 °C, corresponding to the loss of H2O and DMF guest molecules (expected 20.7%) and it began to decompose from 430 °C (Fig. S3†). The TGA curve of the activated 1 indicates that the host framework has no weight loss occurred before 410 °C, and then the framework decomposed. The results show clearly that there is nothing remaining in the framework after the activation.

The rigidity of these porous frameworks as well as the accessibility of guest molecules to the porous framework of 1 were assessed by solid–gas sorption experiments with N2 and CO2 as probe molecules. Prior to measurements, the as-synthesized crystalline samples were activated as follows: immersing in methanol for three days (the solvent was exchanged nine times), followed by the thermal/vacuum activation at 303 K, 8 h to generate the activated compound 1. It has a larger pore void comprising ∼37% of the cell volume as estimated by PLATON.11 The N2 sorption isotherms show type-I behaviors, indicating these crystals are the typical microporous materials (Fig. S4†). The BET surface area is 893.83 m2 g−1 and the total pore volume is 0.450 cm3 g−1 (Fig. S4†). The CO2 uptake for 1 is 82.40 cm3 g−1 at 273 K, and 37.55 cm3 g−1 at 297 K (Fig. 2a and 3a).

Compound 1 exhibits different adsorption capacities in response to C3H8, C2H6, C2H4, C2H2 and CH4 at 273 and 297 K (Fig. 2a and 3a). In general, MOFs have an adsorption capacity with the following trend: C3 > C2 > C1. Nevertheless, the impressive C2H2 uptake of 152 cm3 g−1 at 273 K in compound 1 (Fig. 2a), surpassing those of the previously reported porous adsorbents, such as UMCM-150 (150)13 and NOTT-102 (142),14 is the highest one ever reported.1c More interestingly, a so-called gate-opening effect is observed in the C3H8 adsorption isotherm of 1, which displays a tiny hysteresis phenomenon at 273 K (Fig. 2a), indicating that the framework has some degree of flexibility. As shown in Fig. 1, the 2-nitroimidazole ligand as the pillar plays an important role in this framework. The pore properties can be tuned via rotation of the nitro group, thereby inducing gate-opening type adsorption.7,15 However, when other kinds of hydrocarbons were used as adsorptive probes, the phenomenon did not occur. Taking many factors into consideration, the molecular polarizability and the molecular size have the dominant effects.16 First, compared with CH4, C2H2, C2H4, and C2H6, the steep isotherm of C3H8 indicates that C3H8 has the highest affinity to the framework, consistent with the polarizability of this molecule. Second, the C3H8 molecule with the largest molecular size has more opportunities to interact with the 2-nim pillar, which can cause the rotation of 2-nim to generate the open effect. A typical example of the family of pillared-layer frameworks of the type [Zn2(fu-bdc)2(dabco)]n, where the fu-bdc pillars with dangling different chemical side groups coordinate Zn2+ ions to form two-dimensional square grids, further interconnected to 3D architecture by dabco, shows the excellent effect and hysteresis adsorption on the affinity of CO2via a combination of functionalized linkers.17 The isosteric enthalpy of adsorption (Qst) of light hydrocarbons was calculated based on the virial method at zero coverage. The Qst values of CH4, C2H2, C2H4, C2H6, C3H8 and CO2 are 16.22, 19.72, 19.81, 21.52, 28.16 and 22.38 kJ mol−1, respectively, for 1 (note: the hysteresis of C3H8 adsorption isotherms is tiny, and the adsorption isotherm of C3H8 was fitted as the type I curve).

To estimate the adsorption selectivity of light hydrocarbons, the adsorption selectivities of C3/C1, C2/C1 and CO2/CH4 (equimolar binary mixtures) were calculated by the ideal adsorption solution theory (IAST) at 273 and 297 K (Fig. 2b and 3b). The IAST calculations indicated that the hierarchy of adsorption capacity is commonly CH4 < C2H4 < C2H6 < C2H2 < C3H8, indicating the feasibility of this material for the separation of light hydrocarbons. This framework displays a great separation ratio of C2H6/CH4, exceeding 16 at 297 K, the value of 1 is higher than those of ZIF-8(10)18 and Mg-MOF-74(7),19 while it is close to the C2H6/CH4 selectivity of Fe-MOF-74(18).19 Noteworthily, the selectivity of C3H8/CH4 for 1 is over 85 at room temperature, much higher than the very high values of UTSA-35a (80).20 Such high selectivities further confirm that this material has great potential in the separation application of light hydrocarbons.

Conclusion

In summary, we report a 2-fold interpenetrated pillared-layer compound with paddle-wheel building [Zn2(CO2)3] units and linear 2-nitroimidazole ligands. The adsorption and separation of carbon dioxide and small hydrocarbons were characterized, the C2H2 uptake capacity of 1 is up to 152 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and it also shows good adsorption selectivities for C3/C1 and C2/C1. Particularly, the selectivity of C3H8/CH4 is over 85 at room temperature. These results demonstrate that this compound may be of great value as a promising material for the fuel gas purification and the separation of light hydrocarbons.
