Introduction

Microporous metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as one particular class of multifunctional materials owing to their fascinating topology and potential applications in gas storage, separation, catalysis and detection of high explosives.1â€“4 There is an increasing interest in the synthesis of MOFs using lanthanides because of their unique photoluminescent and magnetic properties, as well as their characteristic coordination preferences.5 Taking the advantage of photophysical properties of lanthanide ions, lanthanide-based porous MOFs could be promising sensory materials with respect to small molecules or ions.6 Compared to first-row transition metals, lanthanide ions have a larger coordination sphere and more flexible coordination geometry.7 So far, work on the construction of new porous lanthanide MOFs and their potential applications in molecular sensing is very scarce.6a

2,5-Pyrazinedicarboxylic acid is a multidentate ligand that contains two distinct coordination modes involving the carboxylic groups and the pyrazine ring, which may lead to a variety of coordinating modes and interesting structures upon coordination with metal ions. In recent years, a number of 2,5-pyrazinedicarboxylate (pzdc) complexes with transition metals have been reported.8 Several lanthanide complexes with this ligand system have also been synthesized, whereas all of which are non-porous.9

Herein, we report a new type of microporous lanthanideâ€“pzdc MOF, [Eu2(Î¼2-pzdc)(Î¼4-pzdc)(Î¼2-ox)(H2O)4]Â·8H2O (ox = oxalate) (1), that features three-dimensional (3D) coordination network with 1D open channels running across the coordination layers. Complex 1 contains the pzdc and oxalate ligands, which were formed in situ by ligand reaction of 5-methylpyrazine-2-carboxylic acid. As is well known, the methyl groups can be easily oxidized to carboxyl under the solvothermal conditions.10 The oxalate ligand could be formed via a decarboxylation reaction.11 Such a metalâ€“organic network also acts as a luminescent receptor for reversible and selective molecular sensing.

Fluorescence measurements

The PL properties of 1 were investigated in solid-state and in various solvent emulsions at room temperature. Results and discussions

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies revealed that compound 1 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and is a 3D coordination framework constructed by infinite rod-shaped europium-carboxylate SBUs and pyrazinyl links. As illustrated in Fig. 1, its asymmetric unit has one Eu(III) ion, one-half of a Î¼2-pzdc and Î¼4-pzdc ligand, respectively, one-half of an oxalate ligand, two coordinated water molecules, and four free water molecules. The europium is nine-coordinated by three oxygen and two nitrogen atoms from three pzdc ligands, two oxygen atoms from one chelating oxalate ligand and two coordinated water molecules and can be described as having a distorted monocapped square-antiprism geometry with Euâ€“O, Euâ€“N distances and Oâ€“Euâ€“O, Oâ€“Euâ€“N, Nâ€“Euâ€“N bond angles ranging from 2.360(8) Ã… to 2.736(9) Ã… and 62.0(3)Â° to 144.6(3)Â°, respectively, all of which are within the range of those found in other nine-coordinate Eu(III) complexes with oxygen and nitrogen donating ligands.18
Fig. 1 Perspective view of the asymmetric unit of 1. All H atoms were omitted for clarity. Symmetry code: (i): âˆ’1 + x, y, x; (ii): 1 âˆ’ x, 1 âˆ’ y, âˆ’x.

In the polymeric structure of 1, the pzdc ligands adopt two different bridging modes: one acts as a Î¼2 linear bridging mode to link two Eu(III) ions, the other in a Î¼4 bridging mode to connect four Eu(III) ions. The oxalate ligands exhibit a Î¼2 linear bridging and chelating mode to link two Eu(III) ions (Scheme 1). On the basis of these connections of Î¼2 linear bridging pzdc and oxalate ligands, Eu(III) centers were connected together to form a europiumâ€“carboxylate chain in the a-axis direction (Fig. 2a), and the adjacent separations of Eu(III) ions are 6.321(2) and 8.253(3) Ã…, respectively. The infinite rod-shaped europium-carboxylate chain can be regarded as a second building unit (SBU). Theses SBUs are sustained by rigid pyrazinyl rings of Î¼4 bridging pzdc ligands to constructed a three-dimensional coordination framework. The framework can be regarded as interlinked infinite rod-shaped SBUs with channels of about 6 Ã— 10 Ã… along the a axis (Fig. 2b).17 The solvent water molecules are located in cavities of the metalâ€“organic framework, allowing them to participate in various Oâ€“Hâ‹¯O hydrogen bonds with the coordinated water molecules and carboxylate O atoms, which help to stabilize the packing structure. The size of the total void created by the formal removal of the coordinated and uncoordinated water molecules is 300.9 Ã…3, which is about 40% of the unit cell volume.19

As for the ligand, the 4-connected (pzdc) serves as the planar 4-connected node with vertex symbols 42Â·62Â·82, and as a result, the extended framework can be topologically recognized as a familiar 4-connected binodal Moganite network with the SchlÃ¤fli symbol of (4Â·64Â·8)2(42Â·62Â·82) (Fig. 3).

Examination of the sorption properties of 1 for N2 indicated that very little amount of N2 was adsorbed at 77 K after thermal activation at 120 Â°C under a dynamic vacuum overnight. This is attributed to a partial decomposition of its framework with the removal of the terminal water molecules, as confirmed by PXRD results (see ESI, Fig. S1).â€  TGA data indicate that 1 releases its free (calcd, 15.3%) and terminal (calcd 7.7%) water molecules in the temperature range of 40 to 290 Â°C (see ESI, Fig. S2).â€  The absence of a plateau in the TGA curve suggests that the structure collapses gradually after being heated over 120 Â°C. However, the free water in the voids could be mostly removed upon heating at 80 Â°C overnight14a keeping the crystal lattice intact (Fig. 4). With coordinated water molecules retained in 1, the potential void is approximately 33.5% of the unit cell volume.19

To verify this observation, we used a computational method to estimate the potential porosity of the partially dehydrated coordination polymer 1a. We used a probe sphere presenting a radius of 1.8 Ã…, which is the usual kinetic radius of N2 (Fig. 5).17 With this value the potential specific accessible surface area of 1a was calculated to be 1250 m2 gâˆ’1. Furthermore, as can be seen on the porosity profile (reported in Fig. 5), this compound is able to host molecules with kinetic radii as big as 2.1 Ã…, which is in a perfect agreement with what could be anticipated from the crystallographic data. Experimentally, we modified the outgassing temperature to 80 Â°C to insure the integrity of the structure, and a subsequent N2 adsorption experiment measured at 77 K shows a type I isotherm characteristic of microporous nature (see ESI, Fig. S3).â€  The BET surface area was calculated to be 1225 m2 gâˆ’1, in good agreement with the value generated by theoretical calculations.

The luminescent property of 1a was determined at the excitation wavelength of 308 nm under ambient temperature. The partially dehydrated complex 1a exhibits characteristic transitions of the Eu(III) ion at 591, 614, 649, and 695 nm, which correspond to 5D0 â†’ 7Fn (n = 1â€“4) transitions, respectively (Fig. 6).

To examine the potential of 1a for sensing of small molecules, the complex was immersed in different organic solvent emulsions for luminescence studies. As shown in Fig. S4 (see ESI),â€  the photoluminescence (PL) is heavily dependent on the identity of the solvent molecule. Methanol has a negligible effect on the luminescence intensity, whereas others exhibit varying degrees of quenching effects. Acetone has the most significant influence on the luminescence intensity (Fig. 7), which disappeared when complex 1a was immersed in pure acetone. Such solvent-dependent luminescence properties are very interesting and important for the selective sensing of acetone solvent molecules.


Fig. 7 The 5D0 â†’ 7F2 transition intensities of 1a introduced into various pure solvents when excited at 308 nm.

The PL properties of 1a in methanol solutions of increasing concentration of acetone were examined with 1a as the standard emulsion. As shown in Fig. 8, the fluorescence intensity of Eu3+ ion in 1a emulsion gradually decreased with the addition of acetone solvent. The luminescence intensity almost disappeared at an acetone content of 5.75 vol%. 1 thus can be seen as a candidate for selective sensing of acetone.


Fig. 8 The PL spectra of 1a methanol emulsion in the presence of various content of acetone solvent.

It is well-known that the luminescence intensity of the lanthanide ion (Ln3+) depends on the efficiency of the energy transfer from the ligand to Ln3+ center.20 According to the above results, the energy transfer process is less effective with the introduction of small solvent molecules. In a recent publication, Chen et al. has attributed the quenching effects on the luminescence intensity to binding of the solvates with the luminescent open metal sites.6a However, in this work the coordination of europium ions in 1 was saturated since the emulsion experiments were carried out on samples with removal of only the uncoordinated water molecules. Thus, the guest solvent molecules can only interact with some other sites of the coordinated molecules in 1, in order to affect the energy transfer process. The incorporation of acetone in the framework was confirmed from the IR analyses (see ESI, Fig. S5).â€  The luminescence quenching for acetone could be ascribed to the energy transfer though Oâ€“Hâ‹¯O hydrogen bonding interactions between coordinated water molecules (by Oâ€“H bonds) and acetone solvent molecules (by oxygen atoms of the carbonyl group), as in the case of ionic sensing through hydrogen bonding interactions.6d,6e,21 Other solvents (such as DMF) can also be incorporated in the framework by hydrogen bonding interactions, but the quenching effects are not significant for these solvents as compared to acetone, as shown in Fig. 7. The origin for the remarkably different quenching effects of various solvents are not yet clear, and a more in depth study is required to understand the mechanisms for such quenching effects.

It is worth mentioning that the powder XRD patterns of 1a obtained after the introduction of various solvents are almost identical to that of as-synthesized 1 (Fig. 4), indicating that the original framework remains unchanged upon solvent treatment. The solvent molecules inside the lattice diffused out when immersed in pure methanol for 1 day after sensing tests. Regenerated 1a exhibits the same luminescence response with respect to solvent molecules (see ESI, Fig. S6).â€  Hence, the channels show excellent reversibility for acetone inclusion.

Conclusion

In summary, a new 3D microporous lanthanide metalâ€“organic framework structure was synthesized via an unprecedented in situ 2,5-pyrazinedicarboxylate and oxalate ligands synthesis. The luminescence study shows that the compound (after removal of guest molecules) exhibits a very high quenching effect with acetone, making it promising for use as a luminescent probe of acetone. This work represents the first example of a MOF for selective molecular sensing without the removal of coordinated solvent molecules prior to the sensing applications. Our new findings contribute to molecular sensing with a microporous lanthanide based MOF structure that shows high selectivity and full reversibility.
