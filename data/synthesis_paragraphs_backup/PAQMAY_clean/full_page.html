Abstract

Luminescent metal–organic frameworks (LMOFs) containing fluorescent probes for the detection of pollutants such as organic solvents and heavy metals are becoming increasingly important, with lanthanide‐MOF (Ln‐MOF) materials receiving greater attention owing to the possibility of achieving fine‐tuned luminescent properties. Herein, two unusual isostructural nanocage‐based three‐dimensional Ln‐MOFs, 1‐Ln (Ln=Tb, Eu), are constructed, using a new diisophthalate ligand with active Lewis basic triazole sites. Selective gas adsorption, especially the removal of CO2 from CH4, a primary component of natural gas and biogas, is desirable in terms of both economic and environmental considerations. 1‐Eu is found to exhibit highly efficient luminescent sensing for Fe3+ cations and Cr2O72− anions, as well as selective CO2 capture over CH4.

Introduction

Chemical sensors for the fast and highly selective detection of high explosives and heavy‐metal‐ion substances have attracted increasing attention owing to their homeland security, environmental, and humanitarian implications.1-4, 5 Furthermore, Fe3+ is the most important transition metal involved in living systems, playing crucial biological roles in the transport and storage of oxygen and also in electron transport in diverse metalloenzymes,5 so its sensitive and selective detection is highly desirable.

Recently, the advantages and challenges of luminescent metal–organic frameworks (LMOFs) containing fluorescent probes for the detection of pollutants such as organic solvents and heavy metals have been reviewed extensively.6-9 Such progress within this field allows the rational design and synthesis of porous LMOFs with functional sites for specific host–guest recognition, and thus, tuning of their functional properties.10 Immobilization of Lewis basic sites within porous MOFs, however, has been a challenge, because such sites tend to bind other metal ions to form condensed structures.11, 12 Few examples of porous MOFs with Lewis basic sites have shown highly multiresponsive luminescent sensing of analytes; however, these examples do highlight the importance of Lewis basic sites in porous MOFs for chemical sensing applications.11, 12

In addition, continuous efforts have been dedicated to the design and preparation of LMOF materials. Among the reported materials, lanthanide‐MOF (Ln‐MOF) materials have received greater attention owing to the advantages of narrow and fine‐tuned luminescent properties.13-15 For example, a wide range of luminescent Ln‐MOFs for sensing cations, anions, small molecules, and vapors have been reported by the groups of Chen, Lee, Harbuzaru, and Liu et al.16-21

However, there has been little investigation into the synthesis of Ln‐MOFs based on tetratopic carboxylate ligands with tetrazole groups. We report herein two Ln‐MOFs containing a tetracarboxylate ligand,22 3,5‐bis(isophthalic acid)‐1 H‐1,2,4‐triazole (H4L), which contains two isophthalic acid fragments linked by a triazolyl group. The triazolyl group contains potential nitrogen atom “docking” sites to boost the sensing properties through various host–guest interactions, and also improves the polarization of the framework, and thus, enhances its gas adsorption volume and selectivity.21b–21d Selective gas adsorption, particularly the removal of CO2 from CH4, a primary component of natural gas and biogas, is of significant interest owing to economic and environmental considerations.22b

Therefore, in this work we report two unusual isostructural microporous Ln‐MOFs, that is, [Ln3L2(OH)(DMF)0.22(H2O)5.78]⋅guest (1) (La=Tb, Eu); 1‐Eu displays sensing of Fe3+ cations and Cr2O72− anions, as well as selective CO2 uptake over CH4.

Only 1‐Tb gave crystals of suitable quality for single‐crystal structure determination, although powder diffraction revealed 1‐Eu to be isostructural (Figure S8 a in the Supporting Information). 1‐Tb crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I4/mcm and contains three different Tb3+ ions, one hydroxyl group, and one fully deprotonated L4− ligand in the asymmetric unit (Figure 1). Ligand L4− exhibits monodentate, bidentate chelating, and bidentate bridging modes for its four carboxylate groups (Figures S1 and S2 in the Supporting Information), and the metal ions form two different Tb2 clusters (SBU1 and SBU2) (Figure 1 a). In SBU1, two symmetry‐related Tb1 atoms are bridged by four carboxylate bridges from four L4−, and each metal coordination sphere is completed by two additional monodentate carboxylates from two L4− and one aqua ligand. In SBU2, two Tb atoms, Tb2 and Tb3, are bridged by two carboxylates from two L4− and one μ2‐OH group. The coordination environment of Tb2 is completed by two chelating carboxylate groups of two L4− and one aqua ligand, whereas that of Tb3 is completed by four aqua ligands. One SBU1 and one SBU2 are extended by eight and four L4− linkers, respectively, and each L4− is connected to two SBU1 and two SBU2 clusters.

The most striking feature is that four SBU1 clusters, eight SBU2 clusters, and eight L4− ligands interlink to form an M12L8 nanocage with a diameter of 1.7 nm (excluding van der Waals radii of the atoms; Figure 1 b), which represents one of the larger sizes reported for analogous discrete metal–organic cages. Although such metal–organic cages or polyhedra have been investigated well, lanthanide‐based cages are rare.23 Moreover, the equivalent M12L8 cage has not been reported. Excluding DMF and aqua ligands, each cage in 1‐Tb contains two equivalent square windows of approximately 6.4×6.4 Å aligned along the c axis, with each window defined by four SBU2 clusters. Neighboring cages are joined together by the sharing of these windows, generating 1 D channels along the c axis (Figures S3–S5 in the Supporting Information). In addition, each cage connects with four other cages by sharing four SBU1 clusters along the 110 and 1 0 directions, giving rise to a 3 D framework (Figure 1 c). Notably, this arrangement of cages generates further 1 D nanochannels with approximate dimensions of 13.6×13.6 Å (Figures 1 d and S5 b). Because of the coexistence of nanocages and nanochannels, the overall 3D framework of 1‐Tb forms a highly porous system with a solvent‐free void of 65 % (Figure 2 a). Topologically, SBU1, SBU2, and L4− can be simplified as 8‐, 4‐, and 4‐connected nodes, respectively, and thus, 1‐Tb displays an unusual tridonal (4,4,8)‐connected net with (44.62)4(44.62)2(48.64.812.104) topology, as shown in Figure 2 b.24 Note that this topological type is different from the (4,4,8)‐connected topology documented previously.24b

The solid‐state photoluminescence spectra show strong emission peaks at 592, 615, and 699 nm for 1‐Eu, and at 489, 542, 586, and 621 nm for 1‐Tb, corresponding to 5D0→7F1, 5D0→7F2, and 5D0→7F4 f–f transitions of Eu3+ ions, and 5D4→7F6, 5D4→7F5, 5D4→7F4, and 5D4→7F3 f–f transitions of Tb3+ ions, respectively (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information). 1‐Eu displays bright red luminescence under UV irradiation (Figure S7). 1‐Eu was dispersed in DMF solutions containing different M(NO3)x salts (M=Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Pb2+, Al3+, Hg2+, Ca2+, Fe3+, Mn2+, Co2+, Cu2+, Zn2+) as microcrystalline solids for potential luminescent sensing studies. Interestingly, the luminescence intensity of the samples was greatly dependent on the identity of the metal ions. The luminescent properties of Mn+@1‐Eu are recorded and compared in Figure 3 a. The intensity of 1‐Eu is enhanced slightly by the addition of Hg2+, Al3+, Ca2+, and Mg2+, but other metal ions lead to a dramatic weakening of the luminescence intensity. In particular, Fe3+ almost quenches the luminescence of 1‐Eu, indicating highly selective sensing of Fe3+ ions by 1‐Eu through this quenching effect.25, 26 The powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (Figure S8 in the Supporting Information) indicate possible structural and/or symmetry changes within the crystal structure of 1‐Eu upon dispersion in the metal ion solutions, although notably, these changes were consistent across all metal solutions, as well as upon soaking in Cr2O72− solutions (vide infra) and upon activation for gas sorption studies. Furthermore, the fluorescence lifetime of 876.06 μs in 1‐Eu is reduced to 615.93 μs in the presence of 1.0 mm Fe3+ (Figure S9), indicating that the energy transfer from the ligand to the Eu atom makes some contribution to the quenching effect.26 Thereby, we infer that the energy transfer may be responsible for the quenching phenomenon. For elucidation of the possible mechanism of such luminescence quenching by metal cations, N 1s X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) studies were performed on 1‐Eu and 1‐Eu@Fe3+. The N 1s peak from the free triazolyl N atoms at 400.2 eV in 1‐Eu is shifted to 400.8 eV upon the addition of Fe3+ (Figure S10 in the Supporting Information), indicating weak binding of triazolyl N atoms to Fe3+ in Eu@Fe3+. The peak at 406.8 eV in the N 1s XPS spectrum for Eu@Fe3+ is attributed to NO3− counterions. Such binding of the triazolyl nitrogen atoms to Fe3+ reduces the antenna efficiency of the L4− organic linkers to magnify the f–f transitions of Eu3+, resulting in luminescence quenching.11

For quantitative investigation of the luminescence response behavior of 1‐Eu toward Fe3+ ions, the emission spectra of 1‐Eu in the presence of different concentrations of Fe3+ ions in DMF were measured at room temperature. As illustrated in Figure 3 b, the luminescence intensity of 1‐Eu is quenched almost completely at an Fe(NO3)3 concentration of 3.0 mm. Meanwhile, a plot of I0/I versus Fe3+ ion concentration matches well with the equation: I0/I=3.94×exp(c/−1.39)−3.56 (I0 and I are the luminescence intensities of 1‐Eu in the absence and presence of Fe3+, respectively, and c is the molar concentration of Fe3+; Figure S11 in the Supporting Information), which indicates that the quenching process controlled by the concentration of Fe3+ ions can be quantitative. Furthermore, a good linear correlation for (I0−I)/I0 and Fe3+ ion concentration was obtained in the concentration range 0.1–0.7 mm, and following the 3δ/slope, a detection limit of 0.018 mm was calculated (Figure S12 in the Supporting Information), which is relatively high compared to reported examples (Table S1 in the Supporting Information).27 Thus, 1‐Eu can be used as a highly sensitive luminescent probe for the quantitative detection of Fe3+ ions.

The potential sensing function for anions was also examined by immersing 1‐Eu in aqueous solutions of different anions.27 The luminescence measurements illustrate that the different anions have a great influence on the luminescence intensity of 1‐Eu, as shown in Figure 3 c. Remarkably, Cr2O72− has the largest quenching effect on the emission of 1‐Eu (Figure 3 a). It is known that the Cr2O72− ion often creates serious and unanticipated problems in the environment, and therefore, its detection is extremely important. To understand the luminescent sensing of Cr2O72−, we recorded the emission spectra of 1‐Eu in the presence of different concentrations of Cr2O72− ions in DMF (Figure 3 b). The luminescence intensity of 1‐Eu decreased to 50 % at 150 μm, with complete quenching at 1000 μm. The Cr2O72− ions may compete with the organic ligand for the absorption of light, thereby reducing the amount of light absorbed by the ligand, and thus, reducing the efficiency of energy transfer from ligand to metal ions in the framework.28, 29 Quantitatively, this quenching effect can be also rationalized by I0/I=6.08×exp(c/−989.03)−5.012, indicating a diffusion‐controlled process with the coexistence of dynamic and static quenching processes (Figure S13 in the Supporting Information).27d The Ksv value for Cr2O72− is 6630 m−1, obtained by the good line of I0/I versus the concentration of Cr2O72− below 348.6 μm according to the Stern–Volmer equation I0/I=1+Ksv[M] (Figure S14 in the Supporting Information),11 which indicates the very significant quenching effect of Cr2O72− on the luminescence intensity of 1‐Eu. PXRD patterns (Figure S8) confirmed that the crystallinity of 1‐Eu was intact after the Cr2O72− anion exchange process, though with likely structural and/or symmetry changes (vide supra). In the time‐dependent intensity measurements, as shown in Figure S15 in the Supporting Information, the whole fluorescence quenching process only needs a time of 20 s. This indicates the potential of 1‐Eu as a highly efficient sensor for both Fe3+ and Cr2O72− targets.

The gas sorption isotherms for CO2 at 195 K and N2 at 77 K were measured on a sample activated by heating at 180 °C under vacuum, and show almost nonporosity for N2, but a type‐I microporous behavior for CO2 with an uptake of 44.9 cm3(STP) g−1 at 100 kPa (Figure 4 a). Fitting of the CO2 adsorption isotherm at 195 K yielded a BET surface area of 63 m2 g−1 (Langmuir surface area 84 m2 g−1). Considering the larger free voids of 1‐Eu, the low surface area possibly arises from the incomplete activation of the sample and/or framework distortion. To assess the porosity of 1‐Eu further, we measured the sorption isotherms of CO2 and CH4 at 298 K, with maximum loadings of 16.8 and 3.4 cm3(STP) g−1 at 100 kPa (Figure 4 b), respectively, suggesting significant adsorption selectivity for CO2 over CH4. By employing the ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) on the adsorption curves of CO2 and CH4 at 298 K, for CO2/CH4 mixtures with volume ratios of 50:50 and 5:95 (Figures 4 c,d and S16 in the Supporting Information), the typical feed compositions of landfill gas and natural gas, respectively, the corresponding CO2/CH4 selectivities at 1 atm were calculated as 16.7 and 11.4, respectively. These values are comparable with those of Cu‐TDPDA and MAF‐X7,30 and are superior to the values reported for most MOFs.31 We also calculated the isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) in 1‐Eu from the virial equation from the adsorption isotherms of CO2 at 273.15 and 298 K (Figure S17 in the Supporting Information). At zero coverage, Qst is 49.6 kJ mol−1, which is very high, and comparable with Qst values in HKUST‐132 but lower than those in UiO‐66‐EA33 and M2(dobpdc) (M=Zn/Mg; dobpdc4−=4,4′‐dioxido‐3,3′‐biphenyldicarboxylate).34 The high Qst value of CO2 may be attributed to the strong interactions between CO2 and the triazolyl group and the exposed active metal sites in the framework.

In summary, we have synthesized two isostructural nanocage‐based 3 D Ln‐MOFs, with 1‐Eu showing promise as an efficient multifunctional fluorescence sensing material with high sensitivity for Fe3+ and Cr2O72−, as well as exhibiting remarkable selective capture of CO2 over CH4. The present results could provide a facile route to the design and synthesis of further cage‐based Ln‐MOFs as promising functional materials.

Experimental Section

Materials and methods

All chemicals were purchased from Jinan Henghua Sci.&Tec. Co. Ltd., and used without further purification. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max‐2550 diffractometer with CuKα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å). Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed on a Vario MICRO (Elementar, Germany) instrument. Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on TGA Q500 thermogravimetric analyzer used in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1.

Synthesis of 1‐Tb/1‐Eu

Single crystals of compound 1‐Tb were obtained through the solvothermal reaction of Tb(NO3)2⋅6 H2O (250 mg) and H4L (55 mg) in N,N‐dimethylformamide (DMF) (3 mL) with HNO3 (0.45 mL; 63 %, aq.) at 105 °C for 72 h. The mixture was then cooled to room temperature. Crystals were obtained and air‐dried (yield 25 %, based on Tb). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for 1‐Tb {[Ln3L2(OH) (DMF)0.22(H2O)5.78]⋅H2O⋅DMF}: C 33.08, H 2.50, N 6.59; found: C 33.29, H 2.60, N 5.50. The same synthetic method was used for 1‐Eu except that Eu(NO3)2⋅6 H2O was used instead of Tb(NO3)2⋅6 H2O (yield 55 %, based on Eu). The experimental PXRD pattern of 1‐Eu was in good agreement with the simulated one based on the single‐crystal X‐ray data for 1‐Tb, indicating isomorphism and phase purity (Figure S8 a). The PXRD pattern for activated 1‐Eu was a less perfect match (Figure S8 a), suggesting some decomposition and/or structural changes upon desolvation; similar changes were observed upon sorption of metal ions and Cr2O72− anions (Figure S8).

Single‐crystal X‐ray structure determination

Crystallographic data for 1‐Tb was collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer using graphite‐monochromated MoKα (λ=0.71073 Å) radiation at 123 K. All non‐hydrogen atoms were found easily from the difference Fourier map. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares on F2. All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The highly disordered guest molecules trapped in the channels of 1‐Tb could not be modeled properly, and thus, the final formula of 1‐Tb was derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. Crystal data for 1‐Tb: C36.81H15.88N6.25O23Tb3, Mr=1390.44, tetragonal, space group I4/mcm, a=35.963(4) Å, c=31.900(8) Å, V=41257(12) Å3, Z=16, ρcalc=0.895 g cm−3. A total of 96 081 reflections were collected, of which 9919 were unique (Rint=0.0338). R1 (I>2σ(I))=0.0591, wR2=0.1898, GOOF=1.208. CCDC 1438517 (1‐Tb) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data are provided free of charge by The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre.

Stability analysis

The stability analysis of 1‐Tb (Figure S18 in the Supporting Information) indicated that 1‐Tb exhibits good thermal stability. In addition, the powder XRD patterns of different solvent‐exchanged samples were similar to that simulated from the single‐crystal structure, underlining the stability of the structure. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) for 1‐Tb showed a weight loss of 8.5 % between 30 and 108 °C, which corresponds to the loss of coordinated H2O molecules and one free H2O molecule (calcd: 8.2 %). The second weight loss of 5.6 %, between 120 and 350 °C, can be attributed to the loss of guest DMA molecules and coordinated DMA molecules (calcd: 5.9 %). Upon further heating, the weight loss of 25.6 % corresponds to the release of the organic L ligand and the coordinated OH−, and then the collapse of the framework (calcd: 27.1 %, Figure S18 in the Supporting Information).
