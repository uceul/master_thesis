A microporous MOF [Zn4(OH)2(1,2,4-BTC)2] (1,2,4-BTC = Benzene-1,2,4-tricarboxylate) with two immobilized open metal Zn2+ sites was obtained by solvothermal reaction, which exhibits highly selective guest sorption and sensing of nitrobenzene.

Microporous metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) have been rapidly emerging as a new type of porous materials for gas storage, separation, sensing and heterogeneous catalysis.1â€“41 The tunable pores and the immobilized functional sites within such microporous MOFs have enabled them to direct specific recognition of certain molecules, and thus for their highly selective guest sorption and separation. In fact, one of the most powerful CO2/CH4 separation materials has been realized in a rod-packing microporous MOF Mg-MOF-74,1 exhibiting extremely high selectivity. Although thousands of MOFs have been synthesized and structurally characterized over the past two decades, the ones with open metal sites are still relatively few,5,34 this is mainly because such open metal sites are very reactive and tend to bind the atoms from the neighboring ligands to form the condensed structures. Herein we report a rod-packing microporous MOF with open metal sites [Zn4(OH)2(1,2,4-BTC)2] (MOF 1a, 1,2,4-BTC = Benzene-1,2,4-tricarboxylate) for highly selective guest sorption and sensing of nitrobenzene.

The permanent micropore feature of the activated MOF 1a was further established by N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K, which displays typical type-I sorption behavior (Fig. S4â€ ), with the BET surface area and pore volume of 408 m2 gâˆ’1 and 0.205 cm3 gâˆ’1, respectively. We thus examined its potential application on the gas and vapor selective separation. As shown in Fig. 2a, MOF 1a takes up differential amount of C2H2 (53 cm3/g), CO2 (42 cm3/g) and CH4 (10 cm3/g) at 295 K and 1 atm, highlighting its potential application for the separation of C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4. The adsorption enthalpies of 1a to C2H2, CO2 and CH4 gases at low coverage are of 28.2, 20.2 and 14.8 kJ/mol, respectively, as calculated based on virial method, a well established and reliable methodology from fits of their adsorption isotherms at 273 and 295 K.38 From these results, the Henry's constant (KH) and the Henry's Law selectivity Sij = KHi/KHj for gas component i over j at 295 K are also given in Table S1. The separation selectivity for C2H2/CH4 and CO2/CH4 mixtures is 14.7 and 4.5, respectively. The open Zn2+ sites contribute significantly to the stronger interactions between the pore surface with acetylene molecules. The acetylene adsortption enthalpy at the coverage of 0.16 mmol/g of 28.1 kJ/mol of MOF 1a is even higher than that of 24.0 kJ/mol of Zn-MOF-74 with well orientated open Zn2+ sites,22 leading to its high separation selectivity of 14.7 for the C2H2/CH4 separation.

The most interesting feature might be unique sorption isotherms and the highly selective sorption behaviors of MOF 1a with respect to different alcohols at 295 K. The existence of the open metal sites leads to hysteresis sorption. The small methanol molecules are able to bind the two open metal sites step by step as clearly shown in the methanol sorption isotherm at P/P0 = 0.54 and 0.84, respectively. It is speculated that the orientation of the two Zn2+ open metal sites has limited their binding of larger alcohols simultaneously, so the sorption isotherms of 1a for ethanol, n-propanol and iso-propanol do not exhibit two-step adsorption, and the saturated sorption capacity for ethanol (62.2 cm3 gâˆ’1), n-propanol (32.6 cm3 gâˆ’1) and iso-propanol (24.7 cm3 gâˆ’1) are much smaller than the saturated methanol adsorption amount of 111.7 cm3 gâˆ’1 (Fig. 2b). This features MOF 1a as a very promising separation material for these small alcohols.

Motivated by the pioneering works by Kitagawa and Li to make use of the charge transfer electron transitions between the microporous MOFs and the guest substrate molecules and thus for the sensing of aromatic compounds,39â€“41 the potential of MOF 1a for the sensing of aromatic compounds was explored. As shown in Fig. 3, the addition of small amount of different aromatic compounds to methanol differentially affect the luminescence intensities. Of particular importance is the significant quenching effect of nitrobenzene on the luminescence intensities of MOF 1a. The inclusion of nitrobenzene within MOF 1a was confirmed by IR spectra in which the nitrobenzene included MOF 1a exhibits new peaks at 1522 and 1347 cmâˆ’1 attributed from NO stretch of nitrobenzene (Fig. S11â€ ).43 Such quenching effect should be attributed to the charge transfer electron transitions from the benzene ring of BTC ligands to nitrobenzene due to the electron-deficient property of nitrobenzene and the Ï€â€“Ï€ interactions between MOF 1a framework and nitrobenzene molecules.
