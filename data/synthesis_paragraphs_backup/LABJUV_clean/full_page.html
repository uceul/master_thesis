Anion-dependent construction of copper(I/II)-1,2,4,5-tetra(4-pyridyl)benzene frameworks†

Sheng Hu ab, Ze-Ming Zhang a, Zhao-Sha Meng a, Zhuo-Jia Lin *a and Ming-Liang Tong *a
aKey Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry of Ministry of Education, State Key Laboratory of Optoelectronic Materials and Technologies, School of Chemistry & Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, 510275, China. E-mail: tongml@mail.sysu.edu.cn
bFaculty of Chemical Engineering and Light Industry, Guangdong University of Technology, Guangzhou, 510006, China

Received 27th April 2010 , Accepted 11th June 2010

First published on 16th August 2010

The unique tetrapyridyl ligand 1,2,4,5-tetra(4-pyridyl)benzene (bztpy) isolated from previous hydrothermal in situ metal ligand reaction is found to exhibit remarkable anion-dependent assembly of a series of novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), [Cu2(CN)2(bztpy)] (1), [Cu(SO4)(bztpy)]·1.5H2O (2), [Cu2Br2(bztpy)]·MeCN (3) and [Cu10I10(bztpy)2]·2H2O (4), which were synthesized under hydro/solvothermal conditions. These MOFs were characterized by elemental analysis, IR and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 consists of [Cu(CN)]n chains that link the neighbouring ones via tetradentate bztpy bridges to form a corrugated 2D layer. When bztpy is treated with CuSO4, a 3D (3,5)-connected network 2 is obtained, in which novel [Cu(bztpy)]n ladders are interconnected by sulfate anions μ-bridges. However, Br− and I− anions assist the formation of {Cu2Br2} and {Cu10I10} SBU in the cluster-based metal–organic frameworks 3 and 4, respectively, and result in a completely different topology. Compound 3 shows a 3D PtS net, while compound 4 has a new 3D (4,8)-connected topology. A discussion of the crystal structures, as well as the coordination behaviour of the special tetrapyridyl ligand upon different geometries of the central connecter is provided. In addition, the photoluminescent properties of 1, 3 and 4 in the solid state at ambient temperature are also investigated.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of novel polymeric or cluster-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have attracted intense interest owing to their structural and topological diversity as well as the realization of their potential for use in magnetism, catalysis, gas storage, ion exchange, molecular recognition and optical properties, etc.1 In this case, crystal engineering principles provide a facile approach to the assembly of one-, two- and three-dimensional (1–3D) extended networks and help to predict theoretically the topology and geometries of the networks to produce useful functional materials.2 However, to establish precise principles of constructing desirable framework topologies remains a long-term challenge. Therefore, enormous elaboration is being focused on constructing novel framework of inorganic materials by choosing versatile organic ligands or functional building blocks with specific structural features.3 One of the most fruitful choices consists in taking advantage of neutral organic groups containing pyridyl groups, such as bidentate pyridyl ligands 4,4′-bipyridine4 and tridentate ligands 2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)triazine,5 which have been frequently employed in the formation of MOFs. In this regard, developing new multitopic building blocks is one of attractive central tasks.

Novel hydro/solvothermal in situ metal ligand reactions as a non-conventional method has provided a few novel functional MOFs, as well as unusual organic ligands that are inaccessible or not easily obtainable by conventional methods.6 We have found that the 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane ligand would simultaneously undergo dehydrogenative coupling and hydroxylation in the presence of copper(II) ions under hydrothermal conditions, resulting in the formation of a dihydroxylcyclohexane ligand 1a,4a-dihydroxy-1e,2e,4e,5e-tetra(4-pyridyl)cyclohexane (chtpy) in its CuI MOFs, which could be isolated by demetallization using Na2(H2edta).7 Fortunately, 1,2,4,5-tetra(4-pyridyl)benzene (bztpy) has also been successfully isolated from the powder product of the above hydrothermal systems, which can also be applied to construct various MOFs with superior performance. Tetrapyridyl ligands with a symmetric disposition have not been explored extensively.

Employing a planar tetratopic bridging ligand promotes the formation of 2–3D MOF structures with specific topologies. We previously reported a twofold interpenetrating (4,4) net, a rare moganite net, a PtS net and a (3,4)-connected net via coordinative interactions constructed using tetrahedral CuI node and the planar tetratopic chtpy ligand.7 Generally, the topologically tunable properties of MOFs are influenced by the coordination nature of the central connecter of metal ion or cluster subunit.8 We also showed that treatment of planar tetratopic ligands chtpy or bztpy with octahedral Cd ions or cuboidal Cd cluster lead to novel supramolecular isomers α-[CdCl2(bztpy)]·H2O and β-[CdCl2(bztpy)]·H2O, displaying (4,4) and NbO net topologies, respectively, and a novel cluster-based MOF [Cd4(O2CPh)2Cl2(OH)(chtpy)2(H2O)2](NO3)3·4.5H2O with a (4,8)-connnected scu topology.9

During these investigations, we felt that it is really important where the ligand system is neutral, the essential anions can dictate assembly by either coordinating strongly to the vacant sites on the metal center in the monodentate or bridging fashion to reduce or increase the dimensionality of the resulting polymeric network, or remaining as non-coordinating counterions and acting as templates. As part of our ongoing efforts in the extension of the functional four-connected ligand to novel frameworks of inorganic materials, and to reconcile the dependence of anions on self-assembly, we have further investigated the self-assembly of bztpy with the copper ion in the presence of some moderately or strongly coordinating anions CN−, SO42−, Br−, I− and obtained a 2D lacunary layer [Cu2(CN)2(bztpy)] (1), a rare 3D (3,5)-connected ladder-based network [Cu(SO4)(bztpy)]·1.5H2O (2), a 3D cluster-based PtS network [Cu2Br2(bztpy)]·MeCN (3) and a 3D cluster-based (4,8)-connected network [Cu10I10(bztpy)2]·2H2O (4), respectively.

Experimental

Materials and methods

The reagents and solvents employed were commercially available and used as received without further purification. The C, H, and N microanalyses were carried out with an Elementar Vario-EL CHNS elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Bio-Rad FTS-7 spectrometer. X-Ray powder diffraction (XRD) intensities for 1 and 4 were measured at 293 K on a Rigaku D/max-IIIA diffractometer (CuKα, λ = 1.54056 Å). The emission/excitation spectra were measured on an Edinburgh FLS-920 spectrophotometer.

Preparation of [Cu2(CN)2(bztpy)] (1)

A mixture of CuI (20 mg), bztpy (10 mg), trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid (10 mg), NaOH (5 mg) and MeCN (15 ml) in a 23 mL Teflon reactor was heated at 160 °C for three days and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 K h−1. Red block crystals were obtained in 20–25% yield (based on bztpy) after washing with MeCN and drying in air. Anal. calcd for C28H18Cu2N6: C 59.46, H 3.21, N 14.86. Found: C 59.49, H 3.22, N 14.81. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3450 (w), 3074 (w), 3039 (w), 2097 (s), 1933 (w), 1850 (w), 1599 (s), 1544 (m), 1474 (w), 1416 (m), 1374 (w), 1321 (w), 1285 (w), 1214 (w), 1096 (w), 1067 (w), 1004 (w), 932 (w), 870 (w), 811 (s), 739 (w), 700 (w), 559 (w), 535 (w), 500 (w).

Preparation of [Cu(SO4)(bztpy)]·1.5H2O (2)

A mixture of CuSO4·5H2O (0.25 mmol), 4-cyanopyridine (0.25 mmol), bztpy (10 mg) and H2O (15 ml) in a 23 mL Teflon reactor was heated at 175 °C for three days and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 K h−1. Blue block crystals were obtained in 30–35% yield (based on bztpy) after washing with MeOH and drying in air. Anal. calcd for C26H21CuN4O5.5S: C 54.49, H 3.69, N 9.78. Found: C 54.53, H 3.72, N 9.75. Preparation of [Cu2Br2(bztpy)]·MeCN (3)

A mixture of CuBr (40 mg), thiocyanuric acid (5 mg), bztpy (5 mg), trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid (10 mg) and MeCN (15 ml) in a 23 mL Teflon reactor was heated at 160 °C for three days and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 K h−1. Red block crystals were obtained in 35–40% yield (based on bztpy) after washing with MeCN and drying in air. Anal. calcd for C28H21Br2Cu2N5: C 47.07, H 2.96, N 9.80. Found: C 47.03, H 2.94, N 9.82. X-Ray crystallography

The diffraction data of 1–4 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD diffractometer with graphite monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The data integration and reduction were processed with SAINT10a software. An empirical absorption correction was applied to the collected reflections with SADABS.10b The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2 with SHELXTL program package.11 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The water hydrogen atoms in 2 and 4 and the H atoms on acetonitrile in 3 were not located due to the highly disorder of the lattice aqua and acetonitrile molecules. Rrestraint ISOR was applied to the highly disordered and half occupied solvent water molecules O2w and O3w in 2 as well as O1w and O1W′ in 4. In 3, the same restraint ISOR was applied to two terminal atoms N3 and C14 on the solvent acetonitrile also because of the disorder of the lattice acetonitrile. Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

Hydro/solvothermal syntheses favour crystallization of 1–4 (Scheme 1). In our first attempt to synthesize 1, the CN− anion was yielded in situ from the cleavage of C(sp3)–C(sp) in acetonitrile, which led to the formation of the essential [Cu(CN)]n chains in 1. The in situ generation of CN− has also been found in our or the other's previous works.12 As for 2, the sulfate anions form μ2-bridges between copper ions, linking the adjacent second building blocks into a 3D network. However, appropriate auxiliary small ligand is frequently employed in the construction of MOFs based on various cluster units.13 When CuBr or CuI was allowed to react with bztpy in acetonitrile, two cluster-type building units, Cu2Br2 and unprecedented Cu10I10, were found in 3 and 4, respectively. The structures of the resulting cluster-based MOFs were totally different from the single metal species in 1 and 2. Furthermore, the incorporation of bigger polynuclear metal clusters as building blocks creates more opportunities to construct highly connected frameworks due to their larger coordination spheres, meaning that they can more readily accommodate the steric demands of organic linkers. Inspired by the interesting structures formed from CuBr/CuI with bztpy, we have tried the reaction of CuCl and bztpy. Different phase from the CuBr and CuI products was yielded; unfortunately no single-crystals suitable for X-ray analysis were obtained so far.

We also found that bztpy has many desirable characteristics, including two stereoisomers (staggered and eclipsed conformations). The two kinds of conformation isomers differ in the orientation of four pyridyl rings as left- and right-handed propellers around the central phenyl rings.9 It is somewhat surprising that all the bztpy ligands in 1–4 adopt the same eclipsed conformation. The dihedral angles between the pyridyl rings and centered benzene ring are distorted with angles of 35.57–56.29°, and the two opposite pyridyl rings are approximately eclipsed. In our previously reported work, we have indeed found some ancillary reagents such as trans-1,4-cyclohexanedicarboxylic acid, 4-cyanopyridine, thiocyanuric acid, and Cu(BF4)2 can significantly help to expand the diversity of metal–organic frameworks and enhance their crystallization. In this paper these organic or inorganic templates were also selected to assist the self-assembly of tetradentate bztpy into extended frameworks in a way similar to the template effect of charged organic molecules on the synthesis of aluminosilicate zeolites, microporous phosphates, and many other inorganic open-framework materials.

Crystal structures

The molecular structure of 1 displays a two-dimensional layer where bztpy acts as a tetradentate ligand (Fig. 1). The Cu(I) centers coordinating to two CN− anions and two pyridyl groups (Cu–N: 1.991–2.187 Å, Cu–C: 1.911 Å) are linked by the linear-bridged μ1,2-CN− bridges via the carbon or nitrogen atoms, forming 1D Cu(CN) inorganic chains. Notably, the bridging fragments Cu(I)–CN–Cu(I) are not linear with Cu–N–C and N–C–Cu bond angles of 155.2(3) and 168.2(3) Å, respectively, while four pyridine pendants on the bztpy ligand connect the [Cu(CN)]n chains. As shown in Fig. 1c, every two Cu(I) atoms and two bztpy ligands build up a 22-membered metallomacrocycle, which propagates into two-dimensional extended structures by μ1,2-CN− bridges. The unusual layered network can also be considered as two corrugated 2D 44 layers that continually intersect at the shared 4-connecting bztpy nodes with the short Schläfli symbol of (4·64·8)2(42·64). Similar example of coordination polymer with this type of topology is recently reported framework of [Co2(mip)2(bpp)2(H2O)] (mip = 5-methylisophthalate and bpp = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane).14 The metallomacrocycles are stacked in parallel to form one-dimensional parallelogrammic channels as viewed along the b axis. Inside the metallomacrocycles, the effective window size of the channels is ca. 3.19 × 2.53 Å, taking account of the van der Waals radii for constituent atoms. The close AB molecular packing of the 2D layers minimizes the void volume in the lattice (3.7% as culculated by PLATON15). The {Cu(CN)} inorganic chains insert into the spaces created by pyridyl rings of adjacent layers and hence maximize the intermolecular interaction for complementary shape, resulting in the cooperative self-assembly of the final structure. Weak interlayer C–H⋯Cu interaction might play a vital role in the crystallization of the solid structure (C⋯Cu distance 3.12 Å).
Fig. 1 The coordination environment of Cu atom in 1 (some equivalent atoms have been generated to complete the Cu coordination) (a), and a 2D topological net in 1 (b), the AB molecular packing of 2D layers showing pore windows (c).

The structure of 2 is built from one-dimensional [Cu(bztpy)]n ladders, which are further interlinked into an interesting porous 3D MOF by the bridging sulfate groups. The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one crystallographically unique square pyramidal Cu(II) atom, one unique bztpy ligand, one SO42− counteranion, and three guest water molecules with one-twice occupancy. The basal plane of the Cu(II) site is defined by three pyridyl nitrogen donors with Cu–N distances of 2.012(4)–2.051(4) Å and one sulfate oxygen donors in the Cu–O length of 1.965(3) Å (Fig. 2a). The axial position is occupied by a second sulfate with Cu–O distance of 2.217(4) Å. In contrast to 1, each bztpy ligand in 2 acts as a T-shape three-connected ligand and links three metal atoms via three pyridyl groups to generate a one-dimensional ladder, which extends along the crystallographic c-axis with two different metallomacrocycles ‘A’ and ‘B’ as illustrated in Fig. 2b. The fourth pyridyl group is uncoordinated. These [Cu(bztpy)]n ladders are further linked in turn through bidentate bridging sulfate groups, which also generate a [Cu(SO4)]n undulating chain with the O–S–O bond angle of 107.7°. While sulfates extend the structure into the b-axis, the meshes of type-A in the [Cu(bztpy)]n ladders are sheltered from the uncoordinated pyridyl groups of bztpy ligands of adjacent layers. However, as shown in Fig. 2c, one-dimensional channels are still formed via the regular arrangement and packing of the metallomacrocycles type-B with the approximate dimensions of 3.24 × 2.40 Å (taking account of the van der Waals radii for constituent atoms). An analysis using PLATON suggested that the channels occupy 9.2% of the crystal volume. The guest aqua molecules are located within these channels. The topological analysis of this net was performed by considering just the copper atoms and three-coordinating bztpy ligands as trigonal bipyramid and T-shape nodes, respectively. The bidentate μ-SO42− bridges can be represented simply as linear linkers between the copper nodes. As shown in Fig. 2d, 2 is a rare (3,5)-connected net which can be characterized by the long topological vertex symbol 4.4.62 for bztpy node and 4.4.6.6.6.6.84.84.88 for the Cu node (O'Keeffe), giving the short vertex symbol (42.6)(42.65.83). To the best of our knowledge, this type of 3D network based upon mixed three- and five-connected nodes is rare,16 and is totally different from the hms and gra nets from stacked hex layers17 or the other reported (3,5)-connected net with ftw-a, mcd, mcf, py-a, tht-a, scu-g, soc-a and spn-a topology.18

For clarity, the guest water molecules are not shown (c) the 3,5-connected topology of the 3D non-interpenetrating net in 2. The red and blue spheres represent Cu and bztpy nodes, respectively (d).

The structure of 3 contains one crystallographically unique tetrahedral Cu(I) atom, which lies on a general position, one unique bztpy ligand lying across an inversion center, one unique Br− anion and one acetonitrile guest molecule with one-twice occupancy. The approximately tetrahedral Cu(I) center is bound by two bromide anions and two pyridyl N atoms with the Cu–Br and Cu–N distances of 2.498(1), 2.564(1) and 2.021(4), 2.041(4) Å, respectively, which results in a 3D structure with bztpy acting as a tetradentate ligand connected to Cu2Br2 dimer cores (Fig. 3). The Cu⋯Cu distance (2.743(1) Å) is slightly shorter than the sum of the van der Waals radii (2.80 Å). Notably, the Cu2Br2 system is not planar. The two Cu2Br planes meet with a dihedral angle of 47.95° at the Cu⋯Cu line, which rarely happens in most of known planar 4-connected Cu2X2 dimer.12a,19 The planar tetratopic bztpy ligands bridge the Cu2Br2 cores into a non-interpenetrating 3D network. By considering just the Cu2Br2 cores and the tetradentate ligands as tetrahedral and square-planar nodes, respectively, 3 is a 4-connected net with tetrahedral and square-planar nodes in the ratio 1:1. The long topological (O'Keeffe) vertex symbol is 4·4·87·87·87·87 for the Cu2Br2 node and 4·4·82·82·88·88 for the bztpy node, giving the short vertex symbol 42·84, which belongs to the PtS topology,7,20 as shown in Fig. 3c. There are 1D channels with small effective windows size (less than 1.5 Å) running along the a- and c-axes in the 3D net structure of 3. An analysis using PLATON suggested that the vacancy occupy 18.9% of the crystal volume. The guest MeCN molecules are located between adjacent pyridine rings.

Single-crystal X-ray analysis of 4 revealed that there are five crystallographically unique CuI atoms, five I− anions and two bztpy ligands, which lie across inversion centers, and two water guest molecules with one-twice occupancy in the asymmetric unit. Cu2, Cu3, Cu4 and Cu5 are included in a larger I4 tetrahedron, whose vertexes cap the faces of the Cu4 polyhedron, while Cu1 and I1 are edged-connected with the tetramers (Fig. 4a). The Cu–I and Cu⋯Cu distances (2.591(1)∼2.860(2) and 2.597(1)∼3.000(2) Å) are slight different with those of known Cu4I4 cluster21 and other multinuclear copper(I) complexes.22 In the current multinuclear cluster, μ3- and μ4-iodide anions bridged ten copper centers giving an unprecedented neutral Cu10I10 inorganic unit which adopts a novel structural type. The complicated great cluster can be described as two symmetrical cuboidal Cu4I4 tetramers that are bound together by the central rhomboid Cu2I2 dimer (Fig. 4b). As for the central Cu2I2 dimer, a distorted chair-like Cu4I4 ladder is formed by associating with the Cu–I of cuboidal Cu4I4 tetramers. It is notable that the decanuclear unit Cu10I10 is eight-connected to other units via tetradentate bztpy ligands, resulting in a 3D framework. The CuI cations (except Cu2 and Cu2a) pointing to the outwards of the cluster coordinate the pyridyl N atoms of the bztpy ligands with the Cu–N distances of 2.013(6)∼2.044(7) Å. Each bztpy ligand acts as a tetradentate bridge via all four pyridyl groups. Two crystallographically unique bztpy ligands display two different bridge modes: the first one connects only with the Cu4I4 part (bztpy1, as shown in Fig. 4c) and the other one connects with both Cu4I4 and Cu2I2 part in Cu10I10 units (bztpy2, as shown in Fig. 4d). Each Cu10I10 cluster serves as a hexagonal bipyramidal node and is coordinated by eight bztpy ligands, which link 24 equivalent cluster cores and form a 3D non-interpenetrated net (Fig. 4e). It is worthy to note that coordination architectures constructed by discrete high-nuclear CunIn (n > 4) clusters have rarely been documented.21 The novel trinodal high-connected topology can be simplified by considering just the two types of bztpy ligands (represented by two square-planar nodes: blue bztpy1 and green bztpy2) and Cu10I10 cluster (represent by a cyan hexagonal bipyramidal node). The resulting net is shown in Fig. 4f, which is a rare (4,8)-connected net with square-planar nodes and hexagonal bipyramidal nodes in the ratio 2:1. A topological analysis of this net was performed with OLEX.23 Thus, the overall network manifests an unreported high-connected trinodal (44.62)(44.62)(48.68.812) topology for (bztpy1)(bztpy2)(Cu10I10), which greatly differs from the other reported (4,8)-connected scu9,24 and flu25 network.

Photoluminescent properties

The photoluminescent properties of compounds 1, 3 and 4 have also been studied. The excitation and emission maxima (λexmax and λemmax) at room temperature are shown in Fig. 5. Upon excitation at 378 and 360 nm, 1 and 3 in the solid state exhibit strong photoluminescence at room temperature with emission maxima at ca. 434 and 475 nm, respectively. According to the literature, the emission peak at 434 nm for 1 may probably be assigned to metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) where the electron is transferred from the copper(I) center to the unoccupied π* orbital of cyanide ligand.26 The emission of 3 can be assigned to a combination of metal-centered (ds/dp) states modified by Cu⋯Cu interaction and n–π* transition of bztpy.27 For 4, excitation at 346 nm produced an intense yellow emission with a peak maximum at 538 nm, which might be assigned as a combination of a CC* excited state containing mixed halide-to-metal charge-transfer (XMCT) and d–s transitions by Cu(I)⋯Cu(I) interactions.28

Conclusions

In this study, we successfully synthesized and characterized four MOFs constructed from bztpy ligand with the assistance of various anions. The employment of different bridging CN−, SO42−, Br−, I− anions leads to diverse structures from 2D layer, 3D single metal core (3,5)-connected framework, 3D PtS-type network and 3D cluster-based (4,8)-connected MOF. These structures reveal that the anions as well as the orientation of tetrapyridyl ligand dictate assembly of the resulting networks. For instance, CN− and SO42− coordinate to the metal centers as μ-bridges and lead to [Cu(CN)]n and [Cu(SO4)]n chains in the structures of 1 and 2, respectively. Br− and I− anions, on the other hand, facilitate the formation of polynuclear metal clusters as multi-connected SBUs in the fantastic topological frameworks of 3 and 4. One of the promising features of 2D and 3D MOFs of this series is the presence of distinct void and channels within the networks although the pores are still baby-sized. The potential of this ligand to produce porous MOFs using suitable non-coordinating or coordinating anions with bridging properties is currently under way in our laboratory.
