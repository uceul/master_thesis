Large pores generated by the combination of different inorganic units in a zinc hydroxide ethynylene diisophthalate MOF†

Steffen Hausdorf a, Wilhelm Seichter b, Edwin Weber b and Florian O. R. L. Mertens *a
aInstitut für Physikalische Chemie, TU Bergakademie Freiberg, Leipziger Str. 29, D-09596 Freiberg/Sachsen, Germany. E-mail: florian.mertens@chemie.tu-freiberg.de; Fax: +49-3731-39-3588; Tel: +49-3731-39-3737
bInstitut für Organische Chemie, TU Bergakademie Freiberg, Leipziger Str. 29, D-09596 Freiberg/Sachsen, Germany

Received 4th August 2008 , Accepted 30th September 2008

First published on 28th November 2008

An ethynylene diisophthalic acid linker molecule was synthesized and used to form a zinc carboxylate-based metal organic framework (MOF) with very large pores and unit cell volume resulting from the unusual combination of structurally different inorganic units forming the secondary building blocks (SBUs). The structure is the first zinc hydroxide carboxylate structure where the inorganic units do not form layers or ribbons but isolated islands. The structure forms true pores with a significantly narrowed pore entry similar to zeolites. The pores are, thus, not created simply by intersecting channels as in most other MOF structures. Although the pore shape is highly asymmetric the spherical free volume is with 10.8 Å still large. The stability of the SBUs in respect to exchange and removal of coordinated solvent molecules is investigated.

Introduction

A fundamental goal in the chemistry of metal organic frameworks (MOFs) has always been the generation of stable structures with large open pores and with very high intrinsic surface areas.1 Among the crystal systems with the highest surface areas found to date are those based on organic linkers whose functional groups that form secondary building blocks (SBUs) with the metal centres do not bond in an unidirectional way, but point in different directions. Well known examples are MOF-177 and MIL-101.2,3 These linkers should also be beneficial in respect to large pore size formation since they do not promote crystallization in high crystal symmetries and thus are less likely to form interpenetrated structures.

In this article we present the formation of a network that combines the use of a slim but rigid linear linker based on a tolane unit having non-unidirectionally orientated docking groups like the 5,5′-(ethynylene)diisophthalate moiety. Although both the ethynylene4,5 and isophthalate groups6 have been used separately as structural units, there is no example showing the two units in one and the same linker molecule. However, the combination of a central ethynylene unit with attached isophthalate groups seems to be an ideal building block because it promotes large pore volumes due to its small rod shape geometry. Moreover, the ethynylene moiety does not interfere with the network formation in the case of carboxylate-based MOFs4 but may allow subsequent modification in order to create specific sites for molecular recognition or catalysis.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

The preparation of the organic linker molecule 5,5′-(ethynylene)diisophthalic acid (1) involved the Pd-catalysed coupling reaction7 of dimethyl 5-iodoisophthalate8 with dimethyl 5-ethynylisophthalate9 and hydrolysis of the tetraester. The complex BAF-2a was obtained as truncated octahedral crystals which were grown under solvothermal conditions similar to the standard MOF-5 synthesis10 from a solution of 1 and Zn(NO3)2·4H2O and additional water in N,N-diethylformamide (DEF).

Structural description

The structure of BAF-2a (Fig. 1) represents a hydroxide-based MOF of the composition Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·12C5H11ON. The most striking feature of the structure is the presence of three configuratively different SBU geometries, which are used to form a very large pore. Although the number of porous MOF structures is vast, we have not come across any other structure that uses the combination of several structurally different SBUs. Additionally, all known zinc hydroxide carboxylate MOFs to date display chain- or layer-like SBUs of continuously connected zinc centred oxygen polyhedra,11,12 while the current structure forms isolated hydroxide SBUs separated from each other in all directions by organic linkers. Each SBU consists of three zinc centred oxygen polyhedra, leading to an overall trigonal connectivity of the whole SBU. Although the E-value statistics indicate the presence of crystallographic inversion symmetry, a conclusive structure model was found in the non-centrosymmetric orthorhombic space groupPca21. The asymmetric unit of the cell is represented by a fragment of the composition Zn12(OH)4(edip)5(DEF)12 [edip = 5,5′-(ethynylene)diisophthalate], in which four independent secondary building blocks (designated as A–D in Fig. 1) are the basic elements of a non-interpenetrating three-dimensional network.
Fig. 1 Cage of the structure Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·12C5H11ON (BAF-2a). The 5,5′-(ethynylene)diisophthalic acid units 1,2,3 are part of a ring system containing six such elements. For clarity reasons only the three foremost ones are shown.

Each of these SBUs, the structures of which are displayed in Fig. 2, consists of three Zn(II) centres, two of them having a distorted tetrahedral and one an octahedral oxygen coordination environment. Within the tetrahedral entities, the Zn–O distances [1.907(8)–1.995(7) Å] are slightly shorter than those of the octahedral coordination polyhedra [2.011(7)–2.161(11) Å]. The non-bonding Zn⋯Zn distances cover a range between 3.156(6) and 3.412(6) Å. The Zn(II) centres within each SBU have the OH oxygen as a common coordination site. The hydrogen of the hydroxy anion is involved in the formation of a strong O–H⋯O hydrogen bond to one of the non-coordinated carboxylate oxygens [d(O⋯O) 2.601(6)–2.777(7) Å]. Three solvent molecules complete the coordination structure of each trinuclear building unit. The formamide hydrogens of the solvent molecules act as donors for relatively short intra-molecular C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds13 to carboxylate oxygens [d(H⋯O) 2.05–2.52 Å].14 The Zn3(μ3-OH) core of each SBU is coordinated by five carboxylates, three of them bridging the metal cations in a bidentate coordination and two providing only one linkage to a Zn(II) centre. A remarkable feature of the crystal structure can be seen in the presence of two structurally different Zn3-SBUs (type I and II in Fig. 2) according to different modes of carboxylate and solvent coordination. In the SBUs of type I, the oxygens of three solvent molecules are incorporated in the sphere of the octahedrally coordinated Zn(II) centre, one being in an axial and two in equatorial positions. In the metal clusters of type II, two solvent molecules and two carboxylates are involved in the environment of the octahedrally coordinated Zn(II) centre while the third solvent is included in the sphere of a tetrahedrally coordinated Zn(II). The SBUs are interconnected by five 1,2-diphenylethyne linkers each, thus generating a microporous metal organic framework with large ellipsoidal cavities.

The pore structure can be regarded as being defined with ten ligand molecules, six of them forming a ring system which is capped on both sides by another four ligands. Extensive metal–ligand interaction induces more or less distortion of the ligand molecules along their ethynyl spacer. A stereo plot showing the cavity structure is depicted in Fig. 3. Taking into account van der Waals radii, the pores have a maximum extension of 19.5 Å, measured between the midpoints of the ethynyl groups of the ligands 4 and 5 (see Fig. 1). The calculated “free” sphere (i.e. the largest sphere which can be placed into the cavity15) has a diameter of 10.8 Å, which corresponds to the closest distance between opposite 1,2-diphenylethyne moieties. Fig. 4 presents a packing diagram of the MOF structure viewed along the crystallographic a-axis. It clearly shows that the pore shape is of low symmetry and only a small portion of the crystal volume is occupied by the metal oxygen clusters.

Besides the solvent molecules that are part of the SBUs, the structure will also contain free solvent molecules after the synthesis. From the crystallographic data, the nitrogen content of BAF-2a is 4.4%, which relates to 31.79% solvent in the otherwise solvent-free structure. Since nitrogen is the only species exclusively belonging to the solvent, one can determine the content of non-coordinated, free solventviaelemental analysis. Experimentally we found a nitrogen content of 6.26%, which relates to 44.9% DEF and 21 DEF molecules per formula unit and pore (theoretical value N = 6.22%) instead of the 12 SBU coordinated ones, leaving nine free but enclosed DEF molecules.

TGA decomposition experiments coupled with IR detection of the released DEF (υ(CO) 1710 cm−1) of the original sample (heating rate 10 K min−1) displayed the maximal DEF release rate at 170 K (Fig. 5). Up to 300 °C, the mass loss was 40%. Above this temperature, the decarboxylation of the linkers sets in and causes in addition the release of CO2 (maximal release at 460 °C). The XRPD diffractogram of material that was heated to 300 °C showed the loss of crystallinity, although IR measurements still indicated the presence of some DEF. During the drying of a small sample in air at 100 °C a mass loss of 6% was detected. From the nitrogen content of 5.33% of the dried sample, a DEF content of 38.8% is calculated, which leads to the following composition for Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·17C5H11ON (Calcd.: 47.5; H, 5.03; N, 5.38. Found: C, 47.6; H, 5.33; N, 5.33%). Hence, it was found that four of the original nine uncoordinated DEF molecules per pore can be removed without the loss of crystallinity.

Solvent exchange

Although the DEF coordination to the SBUs appears to be comparably strong, immersing BAF-2a crystals as synthesized in N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) leads to a complete solvent exchange within half an hour leaving the crystals in tact. The completeness of the exchange was detected by the absence of the C–H methylene vibration at 2980 cm−1 in solid state IR and the absence of DEF release during a thermal decomposition experiment performed with TGA and IR spectroscopy monitoring.

To distinguish the compound prepared by the exchange of DEF in BAF-2a for DMF from BAF-2a itself, it will be labeled as BAF-2b in the following. Its elemental composition was determined to be C: 43.8, H: 5.28, N: 7.73%. Using the nitrogen content as the indicator species for the DMF content, the total composition of BAF-2b was determined to be Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·24C3H7ON (N, 7.66; DMF, 40.0%). Assuming that still 12 solvent molecules per formula unit are coordinated, there must also be 12 molecules per formula unit that are not coordinated, but simply enclosed in the pores (20%). Combined TG/DSC/IR records, taken while heating the material at 10 K min−1 in an argon stream, did not show a clear distinction of the coordinated and the uncoordinated DMF. Its release occurred in the whole temperature range from 30 to 300 °C with a maximal desorption rate at 190 °C, recognizable by the minimum of the derivative of the TG curve with respect to temperature (dTG). Despite the lower boiling point of DMF compared to DEF, the minimum is located at a slightly higher temperature than in the DEF case (Fig. 6).


Fig. 6 TGA (10 K min−1, Ar-stream) measurement of the DMF saturated structure Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·24C3H7ON (BAF-2b), and of a sample of Zn12(OH)4(C18H6O8)5·21C5H11ON (BAF-2a) washed with N,N-diisopropylformamide. In the latter case, the solvent loss of BAF-2a occurs in two clearly visible steps. The solvent loss is displayed in numbers of solvent molecules per pore.

Up to 300 °C the mass loss was 41%. Since no additional decomposition product was observed, a loss of 24 DMF molecules per formula unit can be assumed. It is noteworthy that the mass loss for BAF-2b samples heated to 100 °C in air and kept there for 5–10 min is 20%, which corresponds to 12 DMF molecules per pore, and occurs without any sign of loss of crystallinity. Measurements of the BET surface area in the p/p0 range 0.05–0.2 delivered a value of 26.8 m2 g−1 (Vads = 7.8 cm3 g−1 STP). This value is still rather high for a coarse-grained material and thus demonstrates that a certain accessibility for the N2 molecule to the emptied pores exists. The material heated to 300 °C did not show any traces of the solvent in the solid state IR spectrum.

An analogous solvent exchange experiment was also carried out with N,N-diisopropylformamide (DIPF). The crystals of BAF-2a were immersed in DIPF for 12 h. Their IR spectra recorded during the thermal decomposition showed signals at 2853.3, 1105.9, and 822.5 cm−1, which are characteristic for DEF. In the case of a complete exchange, these signals should be missing. In the range from 1350 to 1250 cm−1 the DEF signal at 1310 cm−1 and the DIPF signal at 1286 cm−1 were replaced by a signal that shifted during the thermal decomposition. It changed from initially 1298 to 1294 cm−1 at the end of the experiment at 300 °C. The presence and the shift of this signal may be explained as a superposition of the DEF and the DIPF signal with a stronger contribution of the DIPF signal at high temperatures. In general, the amount of DIPF can be seen as marginally small, and in contrast to the experiment where DEF was exchanged for DMF there was no observation of a significant exchange of DEF for DIPF even after 12 h of immersion.

However, the thermal decomposition experiment from 30 to 300 °C differs from the one using the original BAF-2a material by the presence of two clearly distinguishable desorption steps (dTG minima in Fig. 6). The mass loss connected with the first step is 9.3 wt%, which formally corresponds to 4.6 of the original 21 DEF molecules per formula unit assuming that only DEF was given off. The value for the second step with the desorption rate maximum at 180 °C is 21.1 wt% (9.9 DEF molecules). The DEF release was so slow that at 300 °C, where the decarboxylation of the MOF material sets in, the crystal still contained 3–4 DEF molecules per formula unit. As expected, all observed release signals were endothermic.

Because of the uncertainty of the DSC baseline (see insert in Fig. 7), it was difficult to derive a precise value for the desorption enthalpies. However, a rough estimate for the partial molar desolvatisation enthalpy for both steps can be given by choosing a straight baseline and determining the area between the baseline and the heatflow signal, which yields q(t) = Δh(t) and allows determination of d(Δh)/dt. The TG curve provides n(t) and its derivative dn/dt. The estimate for the partial molar desolvatisation enthalpy is then given by HDEF = (d(Δh)/dt)/(dn/dt) = d(Δh)/dn.

The straight line is the assumed baseline used for the evaluation of the partial molar desolvatisation enthalpy.

The procedure resulted for the first step in 40 ± 10 kJ mol−1 (DEF) and for the second step in 75 ± 10 kJ mol−1 (DEF). Since the BAF-2a structure contains 12 coordinated DEF molecules, total desolvatisation will lead to a drastic change of the zinc coordination sphere and cause the structure to collapse. In fact, without the loss of crystallinity, only 5 DEF molecules of the total 21 enclosed molecules per pore can be removed, most likely because of the dynamic exchange between coordinated and uncoordinated solvent molecules. The exchange of DEF with the chemically similar DMF occurs almost instantaneously without the loss of the primary structure. Using a mild decomposition procedure at 100 °C, all 12 non-coordinated DMF molecules can be removed while preserving the original lattice. However, heating in an inert gas stream at a rate of 10 K min−1 results in a DMF release maximum at 190 °C and is accompanied by the loss of all solvent molecules and the collapse of the primary structure.

The high selectivity of the solvent exchange is recognizable by the rapidly occurring exchange of DEF with DMF and the hardly occurring one with DIPF, although the DIPF molecule is only slightly larger than the DEF one. There are several possible explanations for the differences in the solvent exchange processes we have observed, such as kinetic limitations, size exclusion, or the differences in the thermodynamic equilibria of the two exchange reactions. If the thermodynamic interpretation is valid, the equilibrium would need to be on the side of the DMF structure, BAF-2b, in the DEF–DMF case and in the DEF–DIPF one on the side of the DEF structure, BAF-2a. The release of DEF in two distinct steps from the BAF-2a samples treated with DIPF is explained best by the removal of externally attached DEF whose desorption peak masks the signal of the coordinated DEF molecules. Combined DSC/TG measurements demonstrate that the value of the desolvatisation enthalpy corresponding to the release of the first 4 or 5 DEF molecules is approximately the same as the one of the evaporation enthalpy of pure DEF, whereas the value related to the second step is significantly higher and therefore should be interpreted as the averaged partial molar coordination enthalpy of all the remaining molecules. In both cases, DEF and DMF, the total loss of the coordinated solvent leads to the loss of crystallinity as was checked by XRPD.

Conclusion

With BAF-2a, we have presented a structure that in contrast to most known metal organic frameworks does not form its cavities via intersecting channels, resulting in pore cross-sections that are approximately of the same size as the pore entry, but forms truly zeolite-like pores where the cavities widen significantly after the pore entry. Besides the special fact that the BAF-2a structure is the first known zinc hydroxide carboxylate in which the inorganic polyhedra do not cluster in layers or ribbons but in isolated islands, thus demonstrating a closer relationship for this class of materials to the structural richness of silicates, it also displays the exceptional feature of forming very large pores by combining several geometrically different SBUs.

Experimental

General

Commercial chemicals and solvents were of reagent grade quality and were used as received. The precursor compounds dimethyl 5-iodoisophthalate8 and dimethyl 5-ethynylisophthalate9 were prepared according to literature procedures. Melting points were determined using a microscope heating stage PHMK Rapido (VEB Wägetechnik). IR spectra of linkers and their precursors were measured on an FT-IR 510 Nicolet instrument as KBr pellets. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were measured in the given solvents at room temperature on a Bruker Avance DPX 400 at 400 and 100 MHz, respectively. Mass spectra were obtained using Hewlett-Packard 5890 (GC-MS) and Finnigan Mat 8200 (EI) spectrometers. Elemental analyses were performed on a Heraeus CHN rapid analyzer. TGA/DSC data were measured with a Setaram/Sensys instrument. The gaseous components emitted during the temperature programmed experiments were fed to an FT-IR spectrometer (Varian Excalibur 3100, 650–400 cm−1, MCT detector). IR spectra of MOFs on the same instrument were taken by diluting the material in KBr pellets. The BET surface area was determined using a Coulter, SA 310 apparatus.

Preparations

Tetramethyl 5,5′-(ethynylene)diisophthalate . Crystallography

Relevant crystallographic data are summarized in Table 3. Against expectation, data collection at low temperatures gave crystal data of poorer quality, so the diffraction experiments were carried out at room temperature. In order to prevent crystal decomposition during data recording, the crystal was coated with a thin layer of epoxy resin. The intensity data were recorded on a X8APEX II four circle diffractometer (Bruker-AXS) using graphite-monochromated MoKα-radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data were collected with a redundancy of 7, an acquisition time of 20 s per frame and a scan width of 0.4° for all scans (ω and φ). An empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS (SAINT-NT) program.16 The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least squares on the basis of F2 using the SHELXTL package.17 The non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically and hydrogen atoms were placed using geometrical constraints. Under the conditions of data collection, ethyl groups of coordinated solvent molecules show high atomic displacement parameters and in some cases their positions could not be gained from the Fourier map, which explains the difference between calculated and reported formula. Thus, only the formyl group of the solvent molecules has been refined anisotropically. Relevant hydrogen bond interactions and conformational parameters are given in Tables 1 and 2, respectively.
