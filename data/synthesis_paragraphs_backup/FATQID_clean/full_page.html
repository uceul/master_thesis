An anionic metal–organic framework with ternary building units for rapid and selective adsorption of dyes†

Shuo Yao , Tong Xu , Nian Zhao , Lirong Zhang *, Qisheng Huo and Yunling Liu *
State Key Laboratory of Inorganic Synthesis and Preparative Chemistry, College of Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun 130012, P. R. China. E-mail: zlr@jlu.edu.cn; yunling@jlu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-431-85168624; Tel: +86-431-85168614

Received 17th January 2017 , Accepted 9th February 2017

First published on 10th February 2017

A novel metal–organic framework, [(CH3)2NH2]2[Cu3O(SO4)3Cu2L2(DMF)(H2O)]·9DMF (JLU-Liu39) H2L = pyridine-3,5-bis(phenyl-4-carboxylic acid), has been successfully synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Structure analysis indicates that the framework of JLU-Liu39 is constructed by ternary building units which include a rare hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6] cluster, a classical [Cu2(CO2)4] paddlewheel, and a 3-connected hetero-N, O donor ligand. The whole 3D framework possesses a (3,4,4)-connected fjh topology and a large window size of 25.3 Å with high solvent-accessible volume accounting for approximately 75.8% of the cell volume. On the basis of the anionic framework and large pore volume, JLU-Liu39 can efficiently adsorb cationic dyes such as MLB, MV, RhB and neutral dye NR but exclude anionic dyes such as MO and Orange II. Moreover, JLU-Liu39 also exhibits size-exclusion performance for dyes of different sizes, and the dye adsorption amounts decrease with the increase of dye size. With respect to the proportional mixture dyes with different charges for MO & MLB and Orange II & MLB, JLU-Liu39 can be able to selectively adsorb the cationic dye MLB efficiently. Based on the aforementioned considerations, JLU-Liu39 is a good anionic-skeleton MOF material for the adsorption and separation of organic dyes.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), as a type of emerging porous material, have been intensively investigated owing to their diverse architectures,1 high surface areas,2 large pore volumes and tunable pore sizes.3 The porous nature of MOFs provides a platform for favorable host–guest interactions, which include gas storage and separation,4 organic molecule adsorption,5 ion-exchange,6 and so on.

Dyes are organic molecules which are widely applied in many industrial applications such as paper, printing, textiles, plastics, cosmetics and pharmaceuticals.7 However, they are also plagued by the hazards of toxic, carcinogenic, mutagenic and environmental pollution, which may seriously affect humans and other terrestrial animals.8 Therefore, from an environmental and safety perspective, it is a critical task to make efforts to develop techniques to remove dyes from liquid waste before being discharged into nature. To carry out this task, several approaches have been explored, including chemical oxidation, photolytic degradation, biological treatment, adsorption etc.9 Among these methods, adsorption is perceived as one of the most feasible, effective, economic competitive and simple operation approach. A number of materials such as activated carbon, zeolites, ion-exchange resins, porous organic polymers and MOFs have been developed to adsorb dyes.10 In terms of the various charges and sizes of dyes, ionic MOFs with tunable pore size and charged framework are convinced to be competent adsorption and separation medium to replace conventional materials.11 This can be ascribed to the following aspects: (1) with regard to the dyes with similar charge to MOFs, ion-exchange and coulombic interaction can promote the high efficiency of the adsorption process;12 (2) in terms of dyes with the opposite charge to MOFs, they would reject each other to resist the adsorption and achieve the separation of dyes with different charges; (3) as for dyes with different sizes or molecular weights, they can also be selectively adsorbed by the MOFs with tunable pore size, which is another efficient method called size-exclusion.13 All the above aspects evidence that ionic MOFs provide more unique advantages for the selective adsorption of dyes by host–guest electronic interactions. Consequently, the reasonable design and synthesis of porous ionic MOF materials for the adsorption and separation of organic dyes is essential to address environmental issues.

MOFs are composed of an inorganic metal and an organic ligand. From the metal point of view, sulphate is preferable compared to nitrate or halide to construct multinuclear ionic MOFs because the sulphate radical can provide more negative charge and various coordination modes to participate in the architecture, for example, chelate two,14 three15 and even four16 metal cores. In addition, copper is preferred for the fabrication of a classical paddlewheel.17 Therefore, we adopt CuSO4·5H2O as the metal source to configure ionic MOFs with multiple secondary building units (SBUs). Since copper sulfate is usually connected by N-donor ligands and the paddlewheel is chelated by carboxylic groups, we choose a hetero-N, O donor ligand pyridine-3,5-bis(phenyl-4-carboxylic acid) (H2L) to construct MOFs. Fortunately, we successfully synthesized a novel anionic multinuclear MOF, JLU-Liu39, with ternary building units: two inorganic SBUs based on the hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6] cluster and the classical bi-nuclear [Cu2(CO2)4] paddlewheel, and one organic SBU based on a hetero-N, O donor ligand. It is worth mentioning that the anionic framework of JLU-Liu39 also exhibits high pore volume which provides us the impetus to investigate the properties of dye adsorption and separation. The results illustrate that JLU-Liu39 assuredly adsorbs anionic and neutral dyes but exclude anionic dyes. Furthermore, it can selectively adsorb cationic dyes in an equal molar mixture of cationic and anionic dyes. Additionally, with respect to cationic dyes with different sizes, JLU-Liu39 shows different adsorption rates. Based on the aforementioned considerations, JLU-Liu39 is a good anionic skeleton MOF material for the adsorption and separation of organic dyes.

Materials and methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max-2550 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were achieved by using a Vario MICRO (Elementar, Germany). Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed using a TGA Q500 thermogravimetric analyzer in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The liquid state UV–vis spectra were recorded on a SHIMADZU UV-2450 UV–visible spectrophotometer within 400–700 nm by using the same solvent as the blank.

Synthesis of JLU-Liu39

A mixture of CuSO4·5H2O (8 mg, 0.03 mmol), H2L (5 mg, 0.015 mmol), N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (2 mL) and HNO3 (0.05 mL) (2.2 mL HNO3 in 10 mL DMF) was sealed in a 20 mL vial and heated at 85 °C for 24 h. Green block crystals were collected and air-dried (yield 62%, based on H2L). Elemental analysis (%) calcd for JLU-Liu39 [(CH3)2NH2]2[Cu3O(SO4)3Cu2L2(DMF)(H2O)]·9DMF: C, 41.23; H, 5.286; N, 9.35; Found: C, 39.27; H, 5.23; N, 9.29. The experimental PXRD pattern is in good agreement with the simulated one based on the single-crystal X-ray data, indicating the purity of the as-synthesized product (Fig. S1†).

X-ray crystallography

Crystallographic data were collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at room temperature. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using version 5.1.18 All the metal atoms were located first, and then the oxygen and carbon atoms of the compound were subsequently found using difference Fourier maps. The hydrogen atoms of the ligand were placed geometrically. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The final formula was derived from the crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. The detailed crystallographic data are listed in Table S1,† respectively. Crystallographic data for JLU-Liu39 (1525694) has been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre. Topology information for the two compounds were calculated by TOPOS 4.0.19

Dye adsorption and separation

Six dyes namely positively charged Methylene Blue (MLB), Methyl Violet (MV), Rhodamine B (RhB), electrically neutral Neutral Red (NR), and negatively charged Methyl Orange (MO) and Orange II were selected to evaluate the abilities of JLU-Liu39 for the adsorption and separation of dyes. All the tests were performed in ethanol at room temperature under constant shaking and by avoiding light. The adsorption rate experiments were performed by adding 10 mg freshly prepared JLU-Liu39 in 8 mL of 20 ppm different organic dye ethanol solutions. The UV-vis absorbance measurements were carried out periodically for the upper clear solution which was poured back to the original system after the measurement to maintain the total amount of sample. In addition, a similar method was adopted to investigate the properties of dye separation, 10 mg of freshly prepared JLU-Liu39 was immersed in 8 mL of 10 ppm dye mixture of MO & MLB, and Orange II & MLB. The maximum adsorption amounts for various kinds of dyes were determined in 20 mL different initial concentration dye solutions, which have been shaken for 12 h in the presence of 10 mg freshly prepared JLU-Liu39. The upper clear solution was extracted to detect the results.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure descriptions

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that JLU-Liu39 crystallizes in the tetragonal crystal system with the space group of P42/mbc. The overall framework possesses ternary SBUs which are an inorganic SBU with a rare hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6] cluster, an inorganic SBU with the familiar paddlewheel and an organic SBU with hetero-N, O donor ligand, respectively. The rare reported hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6] cluster is represented in Fig. 1. Cu atoms possess two different coordination modes: Cu1 is five connected to one μ4-O, one μ2-sulfato, two μ3-sulfato and one nitrogen atom from the pyridyl group of the ligand; Cu2 is four coordinated to two μ4-O and two μ3-sulfatos. As seen in Fig. S2,† four Cu1 atoms are located at the vertex of a distorted rectangle, while two Cu2 atoms are situated above and below the distorted rectangle. In addition, four μ3-sulfatos are also located at the vertex of a distorted square and two μ2-sulfatos are situated above and below the distorted square. The two distorted rectangle planes based on Cu atoms and μ3-sulfato are perpendicular to each other. The hexa-nuclear metal cluster is similar to the reported discrete [Cu6O2(SO4)6(DMF)4]4− anion cluster.20 The difference between them is that the hexa-nuclear metal cluster in JLU-Liu39 is coordinated to four nitrogen atoms from the pyridyl groups of the ligand, further constructing a 3D framework. By contrast, the reported anion cluster is connected to four oxygen atoms from terminal coordinated DMF molecules and fabricates a 0D discrete unit.
Fig. 1 View of the hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6L4] cluster.

As represented, Fig. 2 distinctly illustrates the formation of the 3D framework along the [001], [110] and [1−10] directions. Along the [001] direction, the ligands connected to the Cu paddlewheel by two carboxyl groups to construct a 1D chain architecture and a 1D channel with the dimensions of 7.8 Å × 7.8 Å was configured considering the van der Waals radius. The 1D chains link to hexa-nuclear metal clusters by the pyridyl groups to form a 3D framework. The framework possesses the same channels from the directions of [110] and [1−10]. For one chain of the framework, a window size of 25.3 Å × 7.8 Å is observed. Nevertheless, the AB stacking mode instead of the AA stacking mode leads to the channel size being halved to 13.8 Å × 13.8 Å considering the van der Waals radius.


Fig. 2 The formation of a 3D framework from a 1D channel along the [001] (a), [110] and [1−10] (b) directions.

From a topological point of view, the ligand is regarded as a 3-connected node with a triangular geometry, the 4-connected paddlewheel can be seen as a square geometry and the 4-connected hexa-nuclear metal cluster can be simplified as a rectangular geometry (Fig. 3a and S3†). Therefore, the structure of JLU-Liu39 can be described as a (3,4,4)-connected network which belongs to the fjh topology with a Schläfli symbol of (83)4(86)3.21Fig. 2c shows the space-filling model which can illustrate the window size of the channel along the [001] direction distinctly. In addition, JLU-Liu39 features three types of tiles for (85), (84) and (84), respectively (Fig. 3d and S5†). PLATON calculation reveals that a total solvent-accessible volume of JLU-Liu39 is 27509 Å3 per unit cell, which accounts for approximately 75.8% of the cell volume, exhibiting high porosity and offering possibilities for molecule adsorption.


Fig. 3 (a) The organic N, O-hetero linker, paddlewheel and hexa-nuclear clusters which can be viewed as 3-, 4- and 4-connected node with a triangular, square and rectangular geometry, respectively. The single crystal structure of JLU-Liu39 is shown as polyhedron (b), topology (c), space-filling model (d) and tiles (e) along the [001] direction.

Thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA)

To evaluate the thermal stability of JLU-Liu39, TGA were performed in an atmospheric environment. As seen from Fig. S6,†JLU-Liu39 is stable up to 300 °C with about 40% weight loss owing to the removal of guest molecules. Approximately 40% weight loss from 300 to 800 °C leads to the collapse of the host framework. The thermal stability is comparable to most reported MOFs.22

Adsorption and separation of dyes

The high pore volume and anionic framework of JLU-Liu39 encourage us to explore its organic dye adsorption and separation properties in ethanol. As shown in Scheme 1, we choose six kinds of organic dyes with different charges and the dyes can be classified as cationic dyes (MLB, MV and RhB), neutral dye NR and anionic dyes (MO and Orange II). The three kinds of cationic dyes have the same charge (Z = +1) with different sizes (MLB < MV < RhB), the size of neutral dyes NR is similar to MLB, and the sizes of anionic dyes MO and Orange II with the same charge (Z = −1) are between that of MLB and RhB.
Scheme 1 Schematic structural illustration of the six dyes with different charges.

All the mentioned cationic and neutral dyes could be absorbed as demonstrated by the fact that the colour of the green crystals changed when they were immersed in the dye solutions (Fig. S7†) and the crystalline integrity of JLU-Liu39 was retained after the successful adsorption of the dyes as shown by PXRD (Fig. S1†). As shown in Fig. 4, the UV-Vis spectroscopy analyses and the photographs before and after the adsorption also indicate the efficient adsorption equilibrium of the dyes within 30 min. The first-order derivative of the adsorption amount (Qe) with time leads to the initial adsorption rates of MLB, MV, RhB and NR which are 2.1, 0.7, 0.4 and 1.9 mg g−1 min−1, respectively (Fig. 4f and S8†). The adsorption rate is relatively higher than other reported MOF materials.23 The above results illustrate that the adsorption rate for JLU-Liu39 is influenced by the size of the dyes. With the increase in the molecular weight and dimensions of the dyes, the adsorption rate decreases.


Fig. 4 UV-vis spectra of MLB (a), MV (b), RhB (c) and NR (d) in ethanol solutions at different time during the adsorption for JLU-Liu39 as the host. The insets are the corresponding photographs before (left) and after (right) the adsorption. The variable adsorption amounts under the same initial concentration at different times (e) and the corresponding adsorption rates (f) of the dyes for MLB, MV, RhB and NR.

It is commonly known that dye adsorption capacity is also influenced by the concentration of the dye solutions. Therefore, the maximum adsorption amounts were carried out by testing the adsorption amount at different initial dye concentrations for 12 h at room temperature. As represented in Fig. 5, the maximum adsorption amounts for MLB, MV, RhB and NR are 308, 84, 16 and 132 mg g−1, respectively. The adsorption capacity of MLB is really higher than many reported materials such as MIL-101(Al) and LPTNs.24 In addition, in spite of the adsorption capacity of RhB being lower than MLB, it is higher than other MOFs such as UPC-5 (4.38 mg g−1).25 In addition, Langmuir and Freundlich models are used to fit the maximum adsorption isotherms and it is found that the adsorptions can be described well by the Langmuir isotherm model (Fig. S9†). The different adsorption amounts indicate that the maximum adsorption capacity is also associated with the sizes and charges of dyes. Similar to the adsorption rate, the maximum adsorption capacity decreases with the molecular weight, and the dimensions of the dyes increase. It is worth noting that although the sizes of NR and MLB are similar, the maximum adsorption amount of NR is much less than MLB. This can be ascribed to the charge of MLB being positive which is opposite to the framework of JLU-Liu39. The opposite charge enhances the columbic interaction between the framework and dyes, and the ion-exchange between [(CH3)2NH2]+ and cationic dyes facilitate the adsorption simultaneously. With regard to the neutral dye NR without charge, the interactions are weaker and the maximum adsorption amount is less. As has been stated above, JLU-Liu39 has potential for the selective removal of cationic and neutral dyes from effluents.


Fig. 5 (a) Langmuir fitting plots for the adsorptions of MLB, MV, RhB and NR. (b) The maximum adsorption amount for the respective dyes.

In virtue of the anionic dyes MO and Orange II displaying similar negative charge to the framework, they would reject the framework of JLU-Liu39. Consequently, the anionic dyes could not be absorbed even though their sizes were achievable. Based on the aforementioned consideration, we detected the separation abilities of JLU-Liu39 for the dyes with the opposite charge at room temperature. The selective adsorption behaviours toward these dyes were monitored by UV-Vis spectroscopy and photographs. It is obviously seen from Fig. 6 that JLU-Liu39 can selectively adsorb cationic dyes from a proportional mixture of MO & MLB and Orange II & MLB quickly and efficiently, despite the size of the anionic dye molecules being permissible. Preliminary selective adsorption explorations denote that JLU-Liu39 could be a promising material in the selective removal of cationic and neutral dye molecules from effluents.


Fig. 6 UV-vis spectra of the proportional mixture of dyes for MO & MLB (a) and Orange II & MLB (b). The inset shows the corresponding photographs before (left) and after (right) the adsorption.

Conclusions

In summary, benefitting from the introduction of copper sulphate and a hetero-N, O donor ligand, we have successfully synthesized an anionic skeleton MOF material JLU-Liu39 with a rare reported hexa-nuclear [Cu6O2(SO4)6] cluster and a classical bi-nuclear [Cu2(CO2)4] paddlewheel simultaneously. JLU-Liu39 can efficiently adsorb cationic dyes MLB, MV, RhB and neutral dye NR, but exclude anionic dyes MO and Orange II. As for cationic dyes MLB, MV and RhB which feature different molecule sizes, JLU-Liu39 can rapidly adsorb MLB compared to MV and RhB, and the maximum adsorption amount is decreased with the increase of dye size. In addition, JLU-Liu39 also exhibits good performance for the selective adsorption of MLB from MO and Orange II on the basis of the different charges. In brief, JLU-Liu39 is a suitable material for organic dye adsorption and separation.
