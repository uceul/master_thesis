    Abstract

    Six zinc phosphonocarboxylate hybrid materials, namely, Zn2(pbc)2·2Hdma·DMF·H2O (1), Zn2(pbc)2·H2teta·H2O (2), Zn(pbc)·H3O (3), Zn4(pbc)3·HTEA (4), Zn3(pbc)2(en)2 (5), and Zn3(pbc)2(H2O) (6) (H3pbc = 4-phosphono-benzoic acid, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide, dma = dimethylamine, teta = triethylene tetramine, TEA = triethylamine, en = ethylenediamine) have been synthesized using organic amines as structure-directing agents under mixed-solvothermal condition. These new compounds have been characterized by X-ray single-crystal diffraction together with infrared (IR) spectroscopy, thermogravimetric (TG) analysis, and powder X-ray diffraction (XRD). In structures 1 and 2, the double crankshaft chains constructed by the corner-sharing of ZnO4 and CPO3 tetrahedra are cross-linked by the organic moieties of the ligands into three-dimensional (3-D) structures with GIS (gismondine) and GIS-related zeolite topologies. In structure 3, the connectivity between ZnO4 and CPO3 results in inorganic (4.6.12) sheets, which are pillared by the benzoic acid group −C6H5−COO− into a 3-D open framework structure with zeolitic SOD (sodalite) topology. Structures 4−6 are pillar-layered structures where the Zn−O−P layers show different configuration due to the directing effect from different organic amines and solvents employed during the synthesis. The results show that the amines and organic solvents acting as structure-directing agents along with control of reaction condition are effective in achieving varieties of zinc phosphonocarboxylate structures.

    Synopsis

    Six zinc phosphonocarboxylate hybrid compounds 1−6 have been synthesized by using organic amines as structure-directing agents under mixed-solvothermal condition. Structures 1, 2, and 3 show zeolitic GIS (gismondine), GIS-related, and SOD (sodalite) topologies. Structures 4−6 are pillar-layered structures. The results show that the structure-directing agents (amine and organic solvents) along with control of reaction condition are effective in achieving varieties of open-framework zinc phosphonocarboxylates.

    Recently, a variety of metal phosphonates have been synthesized not only due to their structural diversity but also for their potential or practical application in the area of catalysis, sorption, ion exchange, magnetic studies, and intercalation chemistry, etc.(1-4) Among them, organic−inorganic hybrid metal monophosphonates (M/R-PO3) show unique layered structures with potential applications in the area of intercalation chemistry.(1, 5) Linkers with two or more different functional groups providing varieties of coordination modes of phosphonates have been used as bridging ligands to synthesize even more interesting open frameworks, such as [Zn3(O3PCH2CO2)2(O3PCH2CO2H)(NH3CH2CH2NH3)·BTC] (BTC = 1,3,5-benzene-tricarboxylic acid) with 24-membered-ring (24-MR) channels,(6) the first enantiomerically pure layered (R)-Zn[O3PCH2P(O)(CH3)(C6H5)]·H2O,(7) homochiral Zn2[(S)-O3PCH2NHC4H7CO2]2 with helical channels,(8) Mx[Fe(O2CCH2)2NCH2PO3]6·nH2O (M = Na, K, Rb) with a maple leaf lattice,(9) and a doubly interpenetrated diamondoid copper phosphonate [Cu3(H3L)(OH)(H2O)3]·H2O·MeOH [L = 1,3,5,7- tetrakis(4-phosphonatophenyl)adamantine].(10) Compared with their phosphate analogs, the open framework structures of metal phosphonates are tunable via modifying length and shape of the organic units.(11-13) Whereas the chemistry of metal phosphonates has been investigated in detail, it is still challenging to design and synthesize metal phosphonates with unique microstructures, such as zeolite analogues. Although the templating along with the charge-matching principle has been shown to be an effective method in the rational design of microporous zeolites and transition metal phosphates,(14, 15) there are few examples of metal phosphonates synthesized in the presence of organic cationic template.(16, 17) Zheng et al. demonstrated that the template influences structure of metal phosphonates based on 1-hydroxyethylidenediphosphonate [CH3C(OH)(PO3H2)2, hedpH4].(18-22) They found that organic amine templates such as NH2(CH2)2NH2 with a small size can direct the formation of a linear single chain. The large size templates such as NH2(CH2)nNH2 (n = 4, 5, 6) result in the formation of anionic double chains.(23) However, the synthesis of metal phosphonocarboxylates templated by the organic amines remains unexplored despite the current intense interest on metal phosphonocarboxylates with the topology of zeolites.(24, 25) Recently, we have reported the synthesis of a zinc phosphonocarboxylate with zeolite topology by using a ligand 4-phosphono-benzoic acid (H3pbc),(26) and shown that dimethylamine, a decomposition product of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), acts as structure-directing agent (SDA) and charge-compensating cation. It is obvious that the organic amines and solvents can greatly influence the architecture of zinc phosphonocarboxylates. Herein, a series of experiments have been performed, and six novel zinc phosphonocarboxylates, 1−6, Zn2(pbc)2·2Hdma·DMF·H2O (dma = dimethylamine) (1), Zn2(pbc)2·H2teta·H2O (2), Zn(pbc)·H3O (3), Zn4(pbc)3·HTEA (4), Zn3(pbc)2(en)2 (5), and Zn3(pbc)2(H2O) (6), were successfully obtained by using triethylene tetramine (teta), triethylamine (TEA), and ethylenediamine (en) as templates in mixed organic solvents at 170 °C.

    Chemicals

    The ligand 4-phosphonobenzoic acid (H3pbc) was synthesized using procedures reported previously.(26) Other chemicals with reagent-grade quality were purchased from commercial sources and used without purification.

    Measurements

    IR spectra were recorded from a KBr pellet on a Nicolet FT-IR 360 spectrometer in the range of 4000−400 cm−1. Absorptions were described as follows: strong (s), medium (m), weak (w), and broad (br). Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA851 analyzer in air with a heating rate of 5 K·min−1, from 30 to 800 °C. X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns were measured using a Bruker D4 powder diffractometer at 40 kV, 40 mA for Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å), with a scan speed of 0.2 s/step and a step size of 0.02° (2θ).

    Synthesis of Zn2(pbc)2·2Hdma·DMF·H2O (1)

    A mixture of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.112 g, 0.511 mmol), H3pbc (0.095 g, 0.480 mmol), and N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (5.0 mL) was stirred for dissolution and then heated at 170 °C for 48 h in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). After slow cooling to ambient temperature, transparent stick crystals of 1, accompanied by the formation of unidentified white powder impurities, were obtained. A suitable single crystal of 1 with the size of 0.10 × 0.06 × 0.05 mm3 was selected for X-ray single-crystal characterization.

    Synthesis of Zn2(pbc)2·H2teta·H2O (2)

    A solution of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.113 g, 0.516 mmol) in 2.5 mL water was carefully layered onto a solution of H3pbc (0.096 g, 0.485 mmol) and triethylene tetramine (0.139 g, 0.950 mmol) in 1,3-butanediol (5.0 mL) in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). The resulting mixture was then heated at 170 °C for 48 h. The final product was obtained as transparent column crystals with the size of 0.18 × 0.15 × 0.10 mm3. The crystals were filtered, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature. The yield was about 44.6% (0.075 g) based on H3pbc. IR (cm−1): 3437 (m), 2919 (br), 1608 (s), 1552 (m), 1399 (s), 1137 (s), 1086 (m), 994 (s), 775 (w), 728 (m).

    Synthesis of Zn(pbc)·H3O (3)

    A solution of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.112 g, 0.511 mmol) in 2.5 mL of water was carefully layered onto a solution of H3pbc (0.142 g, 0.717 mmol) and triethylene tetramine (0.160 g, 1.09 mmol) in ethylene glycol (5.0 mL) in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). The resulting mixture was then heated at 170 °C for 48 h. The final product was obtained as transparent block crystals with the size of 0.12 × 0.10 × 0.05 mm3. The crystals were filtered, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature. The yield was about 36.1% (0.053 g) based on Zn(Ac)2·2H2O. IR (cm−1): 3421 (s), 3058 (w), 1603 (s), 1552 (m), 1384 (s), 1148 (m), 1147 (m), 1086 (s), 1004 (m), 776 (m), 738 (m), 615 (m), 482 (m).

    Synthesis of Zn3(pbc)2(en)2 (4)

    A solution of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.113 g, 0.516 mmol) in 2.5 mL of water was carefully layered onto a solution of H3pbc (0.097 g, 0.490 mmol) and ethylenediamine (0.087 g, 1.45 mmol) in 5.0 mL of 1,2-propylene glycol in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). The resulting mixture was then heated at 170 °C for 48 h. The colorless plate crystals with the size of 0.20 × 0.10 × 0.05 mm3 were filtered, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature. The yield was about 82.8% (0.101 g) based on Zn(Ac)2·2H2O. IR (cm−1): 3300 (s), 3259 (s), 3150 (m), 2924 (w), 2868 (w), 1618 (s), 1546 (m), 1398 (m), 1384 (m), 1120 (s), 1062 (s), 1039 (s), 997 (m), 855 (w), 768 (m), 732 (m), 604 (w).

    Synthesis of Zn4(pbc)3·HTEA (5)

    A mixture of H3pbc (0.085 g, 0.429 mmol) and triethylamine (0.101 g, 1.00 mmol) was dissolved in 2.0 mL of cyclohexanol at room temperature for 30 min. This solution was layered onto a solution of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.094 g, 0.429 mmol) in 5.0 mL of water in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). The resulting mixture was then heated at 170 °C for 48 h. The final product was obtained as colorless needle crystals with the size of 0.15 × 0.10 × 0.05 mm3. The crystals were filtered, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature. The yield was about 44.6% (0.046 g) based on Zn(Ac)2·2H2O. IR (cm−1): 3431 (s), 1598 (s), 1542 (s), 1423 (m), 1400 (s), 1160 (s), 1130 (s), 1052 (s), 954 (m), 865 (w), 773 (m), 734 (m), 485 (m).

    Synthesis of Zn3(pbc)2(H2O) (6)

    A solution of Zn(Ac)2·2H2O (0.139 g, 0.635 mmol) in 5.0 mL of water was carefully layered onto a solution of H3pbc (0.097 g, 0.490 mmol) and triethylamine (0.109 g, 1.08 mmol) in 5.0 mL of ethyleneglycol in a Telflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (33 mL). The resulting mixture was then heated at 170 °C for 48 h. After the mixture was allowed to cool slowly to ambient temperature, colorless needle crystals with the size of 0.10 × 0.08 × 0.05 mm3 were obtained. The crystal products were filtered, washed with distilled water, and dried at ambient temperature. The yield was about 88.7% (0.115 g) based on Zn(Ac)2·2H2O. IR (cm−1): 3437 (br), 1593 (s), 1552 (s), 1399 (s), 1163 (s), 1127 (s), 1064 (m), 1045 (s), 953 (m), 779 (w), 732 (m), 697 (w).

    Crystallography

    Suitable single crystals of 1−6 were subjected to X-ray diffraction studies on a Bruker APEX CCD diffractometer equipped with a normal focus, 2.4 kW sealed tube X-ray source (Mo Kα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å) operating at 50 kV and 30 mA. A hemisphere of intensity data was collected at room temperature with a scan width of 0.60° in ω. Empirical absorption corrections were based on SADABS program.(27) The structures were solved by a direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares refinement using the SHELXTL-97 program.(28) The positions of all non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement factors. The hydrogen atoms on C atoms were positioned geometrically and refined using a riding model. CCDC-643980 (1), -643981 (2), -660008 (3), -643982 (4), -643983 (5), and -660009 (6) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. Copies of the data can be obtained free of charge on application to CCDC, 12 Union Road, Cambridge, CB21EZ, UK (fax +44-1223-336-033; e-mail: deposit@ccdc.cam.ac.uk).     Synthesis and Characterization

    The solvents and structure-directing agents used in the syntheses of compounds 1−6 are summarized in Table 3. Compound 1 was obtained by the solvothermal reaction of phosphonic acid (H3pbc) and zinc ion in equal molar ratio at 170 °C for 48 h. Our results show that DMF plays a dual role in the formation of structure 1. Under the solvothermal conditions at 170 °C, DMF not only serves as a solvent but also can be decomposed to produce dimethylammonium cation, which acts as a structure-directing agent (SDA). The in situ slow release of dimethylamine is advantageous to the slow growth of compound 1, which results in sufficiently large single crystals to allow X-ray single-crystal structural determination. Compounds 2−6 were synthesized in the presence of organic amines at 170 °C for 48 h. The mixed solvents were employed in the preparation of compounds 2−6, which could provide a relatively stable environment for the growth of high-quality single crystals. It is found that the molar ratio of Zn2+/ligand, the amines, and solvents can greatly influence the formation of the hybrid Zn−monophosphonate structures. Both compounds 2 and 3 are synthesized by using triethylene tetramine as a SDA. Pure phase of 2 could only be obtained in a mixed solvent of 1,3-butanediol/water, and pure phase of 3 is obtained by using an ethylene glycol (EG)/water mixture as a solvent with a little lower molar ratio of Zn2+/ligand = 0.7. However, a mixed solvent of 1,2-propylene glycol/water results in mixed phases of compounds 2 and 3. Compounds 4−6 can be synthesized with the molar ratio of Zn2+/ligand = 1; the structural differences are attributed to the distinguishing organic amines and solvents. In structure 4, the organic amine en acts as a second ligand due to its strong affinity with zinc cation. Compounds 5 and 6 are synthesized by using the same organic amine, triethylamine. The solvent is essential in the formation of strucutures 5 and 6. Cyclohexanol can direct structure 5, whereas the other solvents such as ethylene glycol or butanol, result in structure 6. It is noted that the other organic amines, such as dipropylamine or butylamine, can be used to synthesize compound 6 mixed with an unidentified phase. X-ray single-crystal structural analysis shows that triethylamine molecules are not combined into the structure of 6, suggesting that the organic amines only act as additives.

    Powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) of compounds 2−6 all match those simulated from single-crystal X-ray data, clearly indicating that pure phases are obtained (Figure S1−S4, Supporting Information). IR spectra (Figure S5, Supporting Information) of those compounds are consistent with their formulations. The bands at two regions of 1656−1522 and 1427−1346 cm−1 are ascribed to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibration of carboxylate groups. The lack of peak at around 1710 cm−1 is indicative of complete deprotonation of all carboxylate groups in structures 2−6. The stretching vibrations of the tetrahedral phosphonate CPO3 groups are indicated by the set of strong bands in the region of 900−1100 cm−1. Compounds 2, 3, and 6 show a broad band at around 3420 cm−1, confirming the presence of hydrogen-bonded lattice water molecules. The lack of such broad band in structures 4 and 5 is consistent with the absence of lattice water molecules. Weak bands at around 3400 and 2800 cm−1 are observed in 2 and 5, suggesting the presence of amine molecules. However, the obvious bands at 3300 and 3229 cm−1 assigned to NH2 groups and 2924 and 2860 cm−1 for CH2 groups can be observed in compound 4, suggesting that the framework contains en molecules.

    Structure of Zn2(pbc)2·2Hdma·DMF·H2O (1)

    Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurement reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in triclinic space group P1̅. The asymmetric unit contains two independent Zn2+ atoms, two pbc3− groups, two Hdma+, one DMF, and one water molecule. The two Zn atoms show similar tetrahedral geometries, coordinated by three phosphonate oxygen atoms from different pbc ligands and one carboxylic oxygen atom from an additional pbc ligand. Each of the three oxygen atoms of the PO3 group coordinates to one zinc atom. The coordination mode of the PO3 group in 1 is referred as a (111) connection,(29) which in combination with one additional carboxylate oxygen furnishes a μ4:η1:η1:η1:η1 coordination mode shown in Scheme 1A. Such a mode makes the pbc ligand behave as a four-connected unit and favors the construction of the structure with zeolite topology. Examples can be found in other phosphonate−carboxylate O3P−R-CO2 ligands, including Zn(O3PC2H4COOH)·1.5H2O,(24) Na[Zn(O3PC2H4COO)]·H2O,(25) and Zn(O3PCH2COO)(H3O).(30) In structure 1, the coordination of zinc atoms with PO3 groups results in double-crankshaft (dc) chains, which are further cross-linked by the organic moieties of the ligands. The resulting 3-D framework contains large channels along the a axis (Figure 1a). The channels are filled with guest Hdma+ cations, water, and DMF molecules that interact with the framework through hydrogen bonds with N···O contacts ranging from 2.704(8) to 3.112(8) Å (Table 2). When the guest molecules are excluded, the channels account for 49.8% of the unit cell volumes as calculated using PLATON,(31) which is the highest in metal−phosphonate frameworks and comparable to the high-porosity MOFs.
    Only Zn and P atoms are considered as framework atoms, 1 exhibits a structure like the (4;2)-connected topology of zeolites. The topological analyses were performed using the OLEX program.(32) As shown in Figure 1b, this 3-D net is formed by the dc chains and the organic linkers. This structure can also be described as stacking of parallel 2-D (4.82) sheets formed by 4-MRs and 8-MRs (the ring number represents the number of tetrahedra-centered atoms). Those sheets in structure 1 are linked to each other in an arrangement that half of vertices of the 8-MRs are directed up while the other half are directed down; indicating that structure 1 has the topology of GIS-type zeolites.(33-35) It is noted that the zeolitic topology GIS has not been previously reported in any other metal phosphonates. The 8-MR channels along the a axis have the size of 17.5 × 11.5 Å2 (the size is estimated by measuring the distances between the centers of opposite atoms; hereafter, all dimensions are reported in this way), which is significantly larger than those in GIS-type aluminosilcate (7.2 × 5.8 Å2 for CaAl2Si2O8(H2O)4(34)) and comparable to those in GIS-type metal−organic frameworks (17.5 × 12.9 Å2 for zinc imidazolates(36, 37)).

    Structure of Zn2(pbc)2·H2teta·H2O (2)

    Compound 2 crystallizes in a monoclinic space group P21/c. The zinc centers are tetrahedrally coordinated and each ligand links four zinc atoms, showing a coordination mode as in Scheme 1A. So structure 2 also exhibits a (4;2)-connected zeolitic framework. It contains distorted dc “zinc phosphate” chains, which propagate in the [100] direction and connect with four neighboring chains by coordination of zinc atoms and carboxylate groups along the b and c axes to form a 3-D network (Figure 2a). Structure 2 shows a 1-D straight channel with a large size of 12.8 × 10.0 Å2 along the a axis. The protonated teta molecules are located on the near two sides of the channels, interacting with the ligand by hydrogen bonds with N···O contacts ranging from 2.760(8) to 2.865(7) Å (Table 2). Also, structure 2 has a high pore volume ratio of 46.1% calculated by PLATON.
    The topology of structure 2 belongs to a family of related zeolites, including MER,(38, 39) GIS,(33-35) and PHI(40, 41) structure types. This family can be described as consisting of the dc chains. In structure 1 and other zeolites, all four-rings of the dc chains are coplanar (Figure 1b). However the dc chain of structure 2 contains a nonplanar 4-MR (Zn1−P2−Zn2−P1), which adopts a puckered structure with dihedral angles of 61.5° between the two trigonal planes (Zn1−Zn2−P2 and Zn1−Zn2−P1, Figure 2b). These distorted dc chains form a layer with each chain being a mirror image of the next (Figure 3a). The layers are then joined together in an ABAB sequence to give a 3-D structure with large straight channels (Figure 3b). There are four types of unique nodes present in this network topology and their vertex symbols (VS) are 4.62.4.85.8.82, 4.62.4.86.82.82, 4.6.4.84.6.85, and 4.6.4.85.6.62, which are different from any known zeolites.

    Structure of Zn(pbc)·H3O (3)

    Compound 3 crystallizes in a trigonal form with a space group R3̅. The asymmetric unit contains one zinc atom, one pbc, and two lattice water molecules. To keep charge balance, one water molecule is assigned as [H3O]+. The zinc atom is tetrahedrally coordinated and the pbc group is in a μ4 coordinated environment similar to those in structures 1 and 2. The connections between ZnO4 and CPO3 tetrahedra result in an inorganic layer consisting of 4-, 6-, and 12-MRs (Figure 4a), which are linked by the organic moieties into a 3-D organic−inorganic hybrid framework in ABC sequence along the [001] direction (Figure 4b). If only Zn and P atoms are considered as framework atoms, structure 3 also exhibits a (4;2) zeolitic topology. The structure can be described as 4- and 6-MR secondary building units (SBUs), from which the subunits of 4668 cages are constructed. There are two kinds of irregular 4668 cages with different shapes due to the different lengths of the spacers (Figure 5a). The lengths along the [001] direction of these two cages are about 19.0 and 12.1 Å, while they have the same effective 6-MR window size of 5.6 Å. These cages are arranged alternately along the c axis to give 1-D channels. Those channels are then in a hexagonal array by sharing all the 4- and 6-MRs with neighboring channels, resulting in a zeolitic sodalite (SOD) topology. The protonated water molecules, as well as cyclic (H2O)12 water clusters, with half-occupation ratio are observed in the channels. Interestingly, all of the water molecules are localized within the zinc phosphonate layers rather than in the organic part of the cages (Figure 5b), indicating the coexisting hydrophilic and hydrophobic domains defined by the inorganic layers and organic linkers, respectively. The unique pillar-layered structure 3 with SOD topology may be attributed to the cyclic water cluster acting as a “template”.

    Structure of Zn3(pbc)2(en)2 (4)

    Compound 4 shows a symmetry with monoclinic space group P21/n. Its framework is neutral with the asymmetric unit consisting of three Zn2+ cations, two pbc3− anions, and two ethylenediamines. Zn(1) exhibits a rare coordination mode of square-pyramidal geometry. Four basal positions are filled with N(1) and N(2) from the en molecule and O(4A) and O(5A) atoms from the same carboxylate group. The average Zn(1)−O and Zn(1)−N bond lengths are 2.189(9) and 2.061(9) Å. The axial site is filled with phosphonate oxygen O(1) with a shorter Zn(1)−O(1) distance of 1.938(7) Å. The Zn(2) atom is tetrahedrally coordinated with two nitrogen atoms from the en molecule and two phosphonate oxygen atoms. Zn(3) also shows tetrahedral geometry, being coordinated by one carboxylate oxygen and three phosphonate oxygens. The two pbc3+ serve as tetradentate and pentadentate ligand as shown in Scheme 1B,C (μ4 and μ5, respectively). Two [Zn(3)O4] and two [CP(2)O3] tetrahedra form a 4-MR SBU. Those 4-MRs are connected together by the −O−Zn(2)−O−P(1)−O− units, thus leading to 2-D inorganic layers containing 4-MRs and large rectangular 16-MRs. Those 16-MRs are filled with the en molecules coordinated with Zn(2) atoms and Zn(1)O3(en)2 units, which share the vertices with [CP(1)O3] tetrahedra (Figure 6a). The inorganic layers are further linked by the organic groups of the ligands into a 3-D pillar-layered structure (Figure 6b).

    Structure of Zn4(pbc)3·HTEA (5)

    Crystal structure of compound 5 features in a chiral space group P21. The asymmetric unit consists of four crystallographically independent zinc atoms, three pbc3− anions, and a protonated TEA. The tetrahedral coordination environments of the four zinc atoms can be classified into two categories: Zn(1) and Zn(2) are coordinated by three phosphonate oxygen atoms and one carboxylate oxygen atom; Zn(3) and Zn(4) are connected with two phosphonate oxygen atoms and two carboxylate oxygen atoms. The three ligand cations have two types of coordination modes, two serve as pentadentate ligands (Scheme 1C) and one as a hexadentate ligand (Scheme 1D). O(6) acts as μ3(O) and links Zn(2)O4, Zn(3)O4 tetrahedra and a CP(2)O3 tetrahedron into a unique vertex-sharing 3-MR. The 3-MR shares its vertices with the remaining tetrahedra, forming a pagoda-shaped SBU. These SBUs are interconnected together, forming an inorganic layer with semicircle-shaped apertures containing 12 TO4 tetrahedra (Figure 7a). The organic moieties of pbc ligands are oriented above and below the layers, linking the layers into a 3-D structure by the coordination of carboxylate groups and zinc atoms from adjacent layers. All three carboxylate groups adopt bidentate bridging mode, connecting two zinc atoms. Two of them connect Zn(3) and Zn(4) atoms on both sides of the layer; the other one connects Zn(1) and Zn(2). The layers in the network arrange in an opposite ABAB sequence so that two types of linkages are observed with different interlayer distances of 11.2 and 8.3 Å, respectively (Figure 7b). The protonated HTEA+ cations locate between two layers, interacting with the adjacent layer by hydrogen bonds, such as N1−H1···O14a [dN1···O14a = 2.92(2) Å] and N1−H1···O1b [dN1···O1b =3.39(4) Å] (symmetries codes, (a) −x, y + 1/2, −z + 1; (b) x + 1, y, z).

    Structure of Zn3(pbc)2(H2O) (6)

    Compound 6 shows a neutral framework with the asymmetric unit consisting of three Zn2+ cations, two pbc3 anions, and water molecular. Both Zn(1) and Zn(2) atoms show tetrahedral coordination with three phosphonate oxygens and one carboxylate oxygen. Zn(3) has trigonal bipyramidal geometry, being coordinated by two oxygen atoms from carboxylate groups, two phosphonate oxygen atoms, and one coordinated water. Both coordination modes of −P(1)O3 and −P(2)O3 are referred to as (112) connections,(30) which are in combination with the bidentate bridge mode carboxylate group as shown in Scheme 1D, μ6 coordination mode. The corner-sharing of Zn(1)O4, Zn(2)O4, and CPO3 generates an infinite chain totally consisting of (−Zn−Zn−P−) 3-MRs along the b axis. The adjacent chains are linked into Zn−O−P layer through corner-sharing between [Zn(3)O3(H2O)2+] and [CPO3] tetrahedra (Figure 8a). The neighboring layers are linked by the organic pillars in ABAB sequence along the a axis with an interlayer distance of 5.5 Å (Figure 8b).

    Structural Comparison and Organic Amine Effect

    Scheme 2 summarizes the different structures induced by the organic amines and solvents. From overview of the structures of 1−6, the differences are mainly attributed to the organic amines including dma, TEA, teta, and en, and solvents including DMF, ethylene glycol, 1,3-butanediol, cyclohexanol, etc., employed during the synthesis. Both structures 1 and 2 contain Zn−O−P dc chains, which are cross-linked by the −C6H5−COO− groups, while structures 3−6 are Zn−O−P layers pillared by the organic groups. It seems that the organic amines with high charge density, such as dma and teta, can direct the formation of zeolitic open-frameworks 1−3. Protonated dma and teta function as templates to adjust the network via hydrogen bonds and counterions to balance charge. The strong hydrogen bonds between all end nitrogen atoms and the corresponding framework oxygen atoms are observed (the shortest N−O distance is 2.76 Å). However, our results show that using amines with higher charge density such as en leads the formation of dense pillar-layered structure 4; en acts as a second ligand not a SDA due to the strong affinity with zinc atoms. The pillar-layered structures 5 and 6 are obtained by using TEA or other amines with low charge density such as dipropaneamine or butylamine as SDAs. Their structures are determined by the polarity of the solvent. Cyclohexanol with weak polarity results in structure 6, which does not contain the organic amine used during the synthesis. These results demonstrate that the organic amine and solvents show significant structure-directing effects on the open-framework zinc phosphonocarboxylates.

    Thermogravimetric Analyses

    The decomposition behaviors of structures 2−6 are shown via TGA curves (Figure S6, Supporting Information). A small weight loss of 2.57% is observed before 150 °C for compound 2, corresponding to the loss of crystalline water molecules (calcd 2.60%). After that, 2 shows two obvious weight losses of 47.4% between 320 and 580 °C, corresponding to the removal of teta and framework collapse. Compounds 3−5 show no weight loss below 380 °C, suggesting that the thermal stability is higher than that temperature. At high temperature (>380 °C) continuous weight losses of 29.9% (for 3), 45.3% (for 4), and 42.0% (for 5) in two steps are observed, corresponding to the departure of organic amines and framework collapse. The TGA curve of compound 6 shows a small weight loss at temperature <300 °C along with a sharp weight loss of 36.7% at 530 °C, suggsting the loss of coordinated water molecules and the collapse of the framework (calcd 36.9%). It is concluded that the pillar-layered structures of 3−6 have a little higher thermal stabilities than the open structure of 2.

    Six new zinc phosphonocarboxylates have been successfully synthesized under mixed-solvothermal conditions in the presence of organic amines at 170 °C. Their structures are directed by the organic amines together with the solvents employed in the syntheses, exhibiting 3-D structures with zeolite topology and pillar-layered structures. Structures 1 and 2 are zeolite analogues with GIS and GIS-related topologies, which have not been observed in the metal phosphonates systems. The structure of 3 featured as the (4668) cages frequently found in zeolites. The cages assemble in hexagonal array into SOD zeolite topology. In structure 4, en acts as a second ligand and coordinates with zinc atoms together with H3pbc ligand, resulting in a pillar-layered structure. Compounds 5 and 6 are pillar-layered structures directed by organic amines with low charge density. From the above results, we conclude that the structure-directing agents along with control of reaction conditions, such as solvents, are effective in achieving a wide variety of zinc phosphonocarboxylate structures.
