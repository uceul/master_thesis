Abstract

A new open‐framework iron phosphite, [C4N2H12][Fe4(H2O)3(HPO3)7]·(H2O)x x = 0.6 (1), has been prepared by the hydrothermal reaction between FeCl3·6H2O, H3PO3, and piperazine. The three‐dimensional structure is comprised of a network of FeO6, FeO5(H2O), and HPO3 polyhedral units connected through their vertices. The structure has layers formed by the well‐known 4.6.12‐net connectivity and is stacked in an AAAA... sequence, penetrated by the one‐dimensional pillar units, forming a three‐dimensional pillared‐layer structure. This connectivity gives rise to two independent 12‐ring channel systems. Magnetic studies indicate antiferromagnetic behavior at high temperatures, with a small ferromagnetic polarization at low temperatures.(© Wiley‐VCH Verlag GmbH & Co. KGaA, 69451 Weinheim, Germany, 2008)

Abstract

A two‐dimensional layer, formed by the 4.6.12‐net connectivity, is penetrated by one‐dimensional chain units forming a three‐dimensional pillared‐layer structure with two independent 12‐ring channel systems. A schematic diagram of the pillared‐layer structure is shown. Magnetic studies indicate antiferromagnetic behavior at high temperatures, with a small ferromagnetic polarization at low temperatures.

Introduction

A large number of open‐framework metal phosphates have been synthesized and characterized during the last two decades.1 The interest in these materials is largely based on their potential applications in the areas of catalysis, sorption and separation processes. Recently, it has been shown that the phosphite group, [HPO3]2–, can be employedsuccessfully to replace the traditional phosphate group, [PO4]3–, in the assembly of open‐framework structures. It is expected that the phosphite group with less available coordination centers, [3; (HPO3)2–], compared to the phosphate group, [4; (PO4)3–] will give rise to novel structures. In addition, the fact that the average charge per oxygen on the phosphite group (0.66) is less than that of the phosphate (0.75), could lead to interesting frameworks with unusual metal:phosphorus ratios. Intense research during the last few years has shown that open‐framework phosphites of Zn,2 V,3 Cr,4 Mn,5 Fe,6 Co7 etc. can be prepared. It is becoming clear that the phosphite structures also show considerable variety and diversity in their structures.

Of the many open‐framework compounds that have been synthesized and characterized those of the transition elements are some of the most interesting. Thus, transition metal phosphates and related compounds have been investigated with great interest resulting in a large number of compounds.8 The impetus for research, of course, stems from the possibility of studying the interplay between the structure and magnetism. Iron phosphites have also been investigated for a similar purpose, but only a few iron phosphite compounds exist.6 Rojo and coworkers have studied the iron phosphites with interest.6a,6e,6g,6h Of the compounds reported in the literature the three‐dimensional ironphosphite, [C5N3H18][Fe3(HPO3)6]·3H2O, appears to be unique.6g The structure consists of FeO6 and HPO3 units connected to form the SBU‐9 units, which are further linked to form the three‐dimensional structure. Magnetic studies indicate an antiferromagnetic behavior (TN = 32 K), which is also confirmed by specific heat measurements. Our studies on iron phosphites resulted in two one‐dimensional structures.6c,6d In continuation of this study, we have now isolated a new iron phosphite, [C4N2H12][Fe4(H2O)3(HPO3)7]·(H2O)x x = 0.6 (1), with a three‐dimensional structure. The compound consists of a pillared‐layered structure formed from one‐ and two‐dimensional iron phosphite units. In this paper, we present the synthesis, structure, and magnetic behavior of this compound.

Results and Discussion

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains 17 non‐hydrogen atoms, of which three iron and phosphorus atoms are crystallographically independent. Of these, two iron atoms, Fe(1) and Fe(2), and one phosphorus atom, P(1), occupy special positions with site multiplicities of 0.16 for Fe(1) and Fe(2) and 0.33 for P(1). All the iron atoms are octahedrally coordinated with six oxygen atom neighbors with an average bond length of 1.999 Å for Fe(1), 2.014 Å for Fe(2), and 2.016 Å for Fe(3). The Fe(1) and Fe(2) atoms form six Fe–O–P bonds [av. 133.69° for Fe(1) and 142.60° for Fe(2)] and Fe(3) makes five Fe–O–P bonds (av. 139.20°) and one terminal Fe–OH2 bond. All the phosphorus atoms create three Fe–O–P linkages with the P–O distances in the range 1.508(3)–1.531(3)° and the O–P–O bond angles in the range 108.63(18)–114.57(17)°. The framework structure of 1 comprises a network of FeO6, FeO5(H2O), and HPO3 units connected through their vertices forming the three‐dimensional structure. The structure may be considered as a series of layers, stacked in an AAAA... sequence, connected perpendicularly by one‐dimensional pillar units. The Fe(3)O5(H2O) octahedra and HP(1)O3 and HP(3)O3 units are connected through their vertices giving rise to a layer of the formula, [Fe3(H2O)3(HPO3)4]+, encompassing circular 12‐membered rings as shown in Figure 1 (a). The layer is topologically identical to that seen in many layered aluminophosphates based upon the [Al3(PO4)4]3– species,9 with which the present compound shares the space group P‐3c1. The 12‐membered rings are surrounded by a series of four‐membered rings, of which there are two distinct types. In the first type, the phosphorus atoms are part of the wall of the 12‐membered rings, while in the second, a HP(1)O3 group caps a six‐membered ring and alternates above and below the plane of the 12‐membered aperture. If we remove the capped phosphite units it will then give a 4.6.12‐net.10 Identical layers have been observed in the iron phosphate‐oxalate, [H3N(CH2)2NH3]1.5[Fe3(PO4)(HPO4)3(C2O4)1.5]·xH2O (x = 1.5–2.0),11 and in the iron arsenate‐oxalate, [H3N(CH2)CH(NH3)CH3]3[Fe6(AsO4)2(HAsO4)6(C2O4)3].12 However, in the present structure we find that the Fe(1)O6 octahedra sits in the middle of the 12‐membered aperture and bonds with the HP(2)O3 units. The HP(2)O3 units also connect with Fe(2)O6 octahedra to give rise to a one‐dimensional chain structure (Figure 1, b). Thus, the 12‐membered aperture is filled with Fe(1)O6 and HP(2)O3 units and linked via Fe(2)O6 octahedral units to the nearest neighbor layers (Figure 2, a). This arrangement gives rise to a pillared‐layered structure with two independent channel systems (Figure 2, b).13

The present structure has a close relationship withthe iron phosphate‐oxalate, [H3N(CH2)2NH3]1.5[Fe3(PO4)(HPO4)3(C2O4)1.5]·xH2O (x = 1.5–2.0),11 as both the structures possess the same layers (Figure 1, a). In the iron phosphate‐oxalate, the layers are arranged in a AAAA... fashion and are pillared by the oxalate group forming the three‐dimensional structure. The 12‐membered apertures with layers are not disturbed resulting in a uniform 12‐membered one‐dimensional channel, which is occupied by water and amine molecules. In the present structure, the layers are arranged similarly (AAAA.... fashion) and the 12‐membered aperture is filled by one‐dimensional chains, resulting in a new pillared‐layered three‐dimensional structurewith two independent channel systems. A very closely related structure has also been observed recently for the indium phosphate, [C10N2H9]3[In9(HPO4)14(H2O)6F3]·(H3O)·(H2O)2.14

The ESR spectra of 1 was recorded on the powder sample at the X band between 3.8 and 280 K (Figure 3, a). The spectra remain essentially unchanged upon cooling the samples from 280 K to approximately 50 K. However, below this temperature the line widths of the spectra broaden and the intensity of the spectra decrease. The spectra appear to be isotropic with a g value of 2.027, which remains unchanged with a variation in the temperature (Figure S1). This g value is characteristic of octahedrally‐coordinated high spin FeIII ions.15 The peak‐to‐peak intensity of the signal increases with decreasing temperature and reaches a maximum at around 50 K. After this temperature the intensity shows an abrupt decrease, indicating the occurrence of the antiferromagnetic transition at low temperatures (Figure 3, b). The line width of the ESR signal slightly increases from 280 K to approximately 100 K as a consequence of the dipolar homogeneous broadening.16 When the temperature is further decreased the line width increases sharply due to strong spin correlations. Similar behavior has been encountered in other related systems.15,16

The present compound with its pillared‐layered structure appears to be a good candidate to investigate the magnetic interactions. We have carried out detailed magnetic studies using a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum Design Inc., USA) over the temperature range 2–300 K. The temperature variation of the molar magnetic susceptibility (χM) at 100 Oe is shown in part a of Figure 4 (Figure S2) with the 1/χM vs. T behavior as the inset. At room temperature the effective magnetic moment value is slightly lower than the spin only value of the Fe3+ ion. Above 100 K the magnetic behavior can be fitted by the Curie–Weiss law, with C = 3.17 cm3 K mol–1 and θp = –14.7 K. The negative θp value indicates an overall antiferromagnetic coupling. Interestingly, the ZFC curve shows an antiferromagnetic transition at TN = 6 K. To understand the correlation between the spins, we employed a dimer model comprising of two Fe3+ spins interacting via the Heisenberg superexchange pathway.17 For a dimer of spin‐5/2 species, the total spin (S) can have values ranging from 0 to 5, and the susceptibility is given by

χdimer = g2μB2/KBT{∑S[S(S + 1)exp(ES/KBT)]/∑Sexp(ES/KBT)},S = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5

where ES is the energy of the spin‐S state. The above equation was fitted with experimental data that gave a value of –10 K for J, which is comparable to the θp value obtained using the Curie–Weiss fit. This indicates that the magnetic behavior may not be limited to the dimer only, but has a long‐range character.

Though the present compound has a three‐dimensional structure formed by the pillared‐layered arrangement, the layers are separated by about 9.1 Å. Under this circumstance we can assume that the interaction mechanism may be governed only by the FeIII ions within the two‐dimensional layers. In order to have a better understanding of this aspect, we fitted the χ(T) curve with the high‐temperature expansion series for 1/χ(T) up to a very high order with the equation:

1/χ(T) = T/C(1 + J/KBT + 1/2 J2/KBT2 + ...)

We find that this function fits well down to the same temperature where the field‐cooled (FC) and zero‐field cooled (ZFC) χ(T) deviate. This observation indicates that the models (dimer, high temperature expansion models) are not sufficient to explain the observed behavior at very low temperatures. This may be because the magnetic interactions are not limited within the layers, but are extended three‐dimensionally via the one‐dimensional chain.

Since the models do not fit the low‐temperature behavior, the exchange mechanism must have components involving correlations between the layers through the FeIII present in between part of the chains. To probe whether there are any additional interactions at low T we have carried out magnetization measurements at 2.5 K. The M vs. H behavior at 2.5 K (Figure 4, b) indicates a very small hysteresis loop atvery low applied field strengths (Hc = 330 Oe and Mr = 325 emu/mol). However, at higher field strengths the behavior appears to be linear. This shows that at low temperature and at low field strengths the exchange mechanism between the FeIII ions is also governed by some polarization components extending into the three‐dimension structure.

For a magnetic system with a variety of interactions the temperature plays a crucial role in probing their magnitudes if they are energetically far apart. In our system, the intra‐layer couplings are antiferromagnetic as found by Curie–Weiss and high‐temperature fittings. The small polarization component observed in the magnetization at low field strengths and low temperatures must be because of the distance dependent coupling between the layers through the FeIII center.18 Such a variation of the magnetic data with the temperature signifies the structure dominated exchange pathways, which if properly tuned can be of immense value in appropriately designing multi‐featured magnetic systems for advanced applications.

Experimental Section

Synthesis and Initial Characterization

The iron phosphite, [C4N2H12][Fe4(H2O)3(HPO3)7]·(H2O)x x = 0.6 (1), was synthesized under hydrothermal conditions using ferric chloride hexahydrate as the source of iron. The FeCl3·6H2O (0.270 g, 1 mmol) was dispersed in milli‐Q water (10 mL). H3PO3 (0.492 g, 6 mmol) and piperazine (0.516 g, 6 mmol) were then added and the mixture was homogenized for 30 min at room temperature. The final mixture with the composition 1.0 FeCl3·6H2O/6.0 H3PO3/6.0 Piperazine/555 H2O was heated in a 23‐mL autoclave at 110 °C for 96 h followed by heating at 150 °C for 24 h. The resulting product, containing large quantities of colorless, plate‐like single crystals, was filtered, washed with deionzed water, and dried under ambient conditions. The yield of the product was about 80 % based on Fe. The initial and final pH of the reaction mixture was about 4.

The initial characterization was carried out using powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD), thermo‐gravimetric analysis (TGA), and infrared spectroscopic (IR) measurements. EDAX analysis indicated an Fe/P ratio of 4:7, which is in agreement with the molecular formula obtained from the single crystal structure. Elemental analysis (ThermoFinnigan FLASH EA 1112 CHNS analyzer) (%) calcd. C 5.13, H 2.82, N 2.99; found C 4.83, H 3.13, N 3.21. The powder XRD pattern was recorded on powder samples over the 2θ range 5–50° using Cu‐Kα radiation (Philips, X′pert Pro). The experimental XRD pattern was entirely consistent with the simulated XRD pattern generated from the structure determined using the single‐crystal XRD (Figure 5).

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out (Mettler–Toledo, TG850) over the range 30–900 °C (heating rate: 5 °C min–1) in an oxygen atmosphere (flow rate = 50 mL min–1). The studies indicate a continuous weight loss up to 700 °C (observed 18.2 %), which may correspond to the loss of lattice water, coordinated water, amine molecules along with the oxidation of PIII to PV. The final decomposed product was found to be amorphous as determined by XRD studies. The TGA study also indicates that the crystallinity of the compound is affected when the lattice and coordinated water are removed from the structure at about 300 °C (Figure S3). Above 300 °C the structure completely collapses because of the removal of the occluded organic molecules during calcination.

Infrared (IR) spectroscopic studies carried out over the range = 400–4000 cm–1 using the KBr pellet method (Perkin–Elmer, SPECTRUM 1000) exhibited typical peaks corresponding to the amine, HPO3, and water molecules. The observed IR bands [cm–1]: νs(H2O) = 3690, ν(NH) = 3377, ν(CH)asym = 3105, ν(CH)sym = 3040, ν(HP) = 2396, ν(NH) = 1616, δ(CH) = 1460, νs(CN) = 1182, νas(PO3) = 1093, δ(HP) = 1026, νs(PO3) = 1006, δs(PO3) = 591, δas(PO3) = 469.

The variable‐temperature magnetic susceptibility studies were carried out on powdered samples over the range 2–300 K using a SQUID magnetometer (Quantum Design Inc., USA).

Single‐Crystal Structure Determination

A suitable single crystal was carefully selected and glued to a thin glass fiber. The single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction data were collected with a Bruker AXS Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at 293(2) K. The X‐ray generator was operated at 50 kV and 35 mA using Mo‐Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. Data were collected with ω scans with a 0.3° width. A total of 606 frames were collected in three different settings of (0°, 90°, 180°) keeping the sample‐to‐detector distance fixed at 6.03 cm and the detector position (2θ) fixed at –25°. Pertinent experimental details of the structure determination of compound 1 are presented in Table 2.

The data were reduced using the SAINTPLUS program,19 and an empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.20 The crystal structure was solved and refined using SHELX‐9721 present in the WinGx suit of programs (Version 1.63.04a), which readily revealed all the atoms of the framework (Fe, P, O). The extra framework atoms were found to be scattered, which indicated that the amine molecules were completely decomposed and only fragments were observed in the difference Fourier map. The complete amine molecule, piperazine, could not be located. The extra framework water molecules were found to be partially occupied with an occupancy of 0.6. Most of the hydrogen positions could not be located from the Fourier map. This may be because of the decomposition of the amine molecule. For the final refinement, hydrogen atoms for the P–H and water molecules were placed in geometrically ideal positions and refined using the riding mode. The final framework formula was obtained by combining the results of the elemental analysis and TGA experiment. The last cycles of refinements included atomic positions, anisotropic thermal parameters for all the non‐hydrogen atoms, and isotropic thermal parameters for all the hydrogen atoms. Full‐matrix least‐squares structure refinement against |F|2 was carried out using the WINGX package of programs.22
