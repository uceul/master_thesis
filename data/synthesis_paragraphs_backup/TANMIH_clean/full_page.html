Embedding 1D or 2D cobalt–carboxylate substrates in 3D coordination polymers exhibiting slow magnetic relaxation behaviors: crystal structures, high-field EPR, and magnetic studies†

By utilizing well-designed bifunctional ligands derived from 1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, magnetic coordination polymers (CPs) that exhibit slow magnetic relaxation at the low temperature regions were constructed and further structurally characterized. In 1, 1D cobalt–carboxyl chains were stabilized in the final structure. In contrast, by adjusting the length of the substituted arms on imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, a novel 3D CP, 2 containing 2D 63 cobalt–carboxyl layer was obtained. A combination of Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) simulations and the first-principles Density functional theory (DFT) calculations showed that compound 2 features weak ferro- and antiferro-magnetic coupling mechanisms with two different super-exchange paths of −/+/− for syn–anti carboxylate bridges and −/−/− for syn–syn carboxylate bridges. Through HF-EPR measurements performed on polycrystalline samples over the frequency range of 60–260 GHz and field range of 0–12 T, the effective g-values of 1 and 2 were all larger than 2.00, and the signs of their D values were probably positive.

Introduction

Investigations of molecular magnetic materials have attracted great attention not only because they supply explicit models for comprehending the quantum phenomenon, but also because they have potential applications in quantum computing, spintronics, and information storage.1,2 Along with the evolution of the concept of molecular-based magnets, single-molecule magnets (SMMs), single-chain magnets (SCMs), single-ion magnets (SIMs) and even magnetic molecules that showed only slow magnetic relaxation have evolved in the related fields.3,4

Recently, much effort has been made toward the synthesis of SCM, since the establishment of Glauber's model for Ising spin chains in a polymeric compound, [Co(hfac)2{NIT(C6H4OMe)}], (hfac = hexafluoroacetylacetonate; NIT(R) = 2-(4′-R)-4,4,5,5-tetramethylimidazoline-1-oxyl-3-oxide), which displays hysteresis behavior above the temperature at which Helium exists as a liquid.5 Numerous SCM complexes with interesting properties and theoretical models have been reported, which provide useful indications in terms of the oriented synthesis of SCM with anticipated magnetic behaviors.6,7 However, the concurrence of high Ising anisotropy of the coupling centers, strong intra-chain ferromagnetic interactions between the high-spin magnetic units and weak inter-chain interactions for the desired slow magnetic relaxation behaviors has still impeded the progress of the rational design of SCMs.6,8 In contrast to the common synthesis strategy (linking SMM building blocks or paramagnetic centers appropriately into a rational structure and magnitude that might present the desired properties), a new one that embeds 1D Ising spin chains into 2D or 3D networks with appropriate bifunctional ligands acting as a structural linker and magnetic separator has been developed and several examples have been successfully constructed and reported.9 Carboxylate ligands, which possess diverse coordination ability to transmit efficient magnetic exchange between adjacent magnetic centers, are utilized widely for building variable structures behaving as the high-dimensionally ordered magnets.10 Promoted by the advantages of this facile synthesis strategy, versatile metal–carboxylate chains with or without other short magnetic bridges embedded in the high dimensional coordination polymers (CPs) have been explored.11 However, the ones that contain a pure 2D metal–carboxylate layer and exhibit the phenomenon of slow magnetic relaxation are still rarely observed.12

In addition, profiting from the visual molecular models given by the versatile low- and high-dimensional magnetic coordination systems, the magnetostructural correlations, or materials with useful magnetic properties, can be investigated deeply and theoretically.13 A magnetostructural correlation can be interpreted as a relationship between the magnetic and structural parameters of a molecular substance, which has gained great progress over recent decades. Initially, the linkage between the specific structural parameters(such as bond lengths, angles, and dihedral angles) and magnetic behaviors in one complex are established to illustrate the comprehension of ferromagnetic or antiferromagnetic interactions.14 Along with the development of quantum calculations for chemical issues, the new term of superexchange parameter, usually labeled as the J value, has been drawn into the correlation, which is also co-related closely with the structural parameters.15 More recently, magnetostructural D correlation has been proposed to distinguish the J one. The large ground state spin S and zero-field splitting parameter D, which give the high relaxation energy barriers, are included in the third generation of the magnetostructural concept; these are the prerequisites for a molecule demonstrating slow relaxation of magnetic moments after removing an external magnetic field.16 Normally, the approximate D value could be obtained by fitting the magnetic behavior according to the specific spin Hamiltonian.

Apart from this, profiting from the assistance of a relatively new technique explored over the past few decades – high frequency/field electron paramagnetic resonance (HF-EPR) at the frequencies of 95–700 GHz and at correspondingly high magnetic fields (up to 25 T), the approximate information on D could also be simulated and in-depth insights about the slow magnetic relaxation could be given.17 However, due to the infant period of applying HF-EPR to molecular magnetism, the rational models and calculation methods are relatively limited. Therefore, most of the attention has been focused on the SIM and low-nuclearity SMM systems, with the aspect of SCM receiving much less interest.17,18 To establish a proper magnetostructural correlation for SCM by the utilization of HFESR and quantum calculations, the comparable easy models should be constructed firstly and investigated cautiously.

With all the above considerations, we selected the 1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid as the template to obtain two different ligands (Scheme 1), 1-(4-carboxybenzyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (L1) and 1-(carboxymethyl)-1H-imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (L2), which were used to construct the desired SCM-embedded CPs, based on several motivations. (1) To investigate the effects of ligands on the magnetic behaviors, wherein two tricarboxylate ligands were synthesized from the scaffold of imidazole-dicarboxylate by decorating different lengths of carboxylate substitutes. (2) It is found that the double chelating N–O sites of imidazole-dicarboxylate are favorable for assembling a 1D chain or 2D layer based on the repeated di-nuclear nodes.18 To gain the desired embedding 1D chains and increase the connectivity of adjacent 1D chains to form 3D coordination materials, we postulated that if one N-site was blocked with extended carboxyl arms, the remaining N–O chelating site of the resulting derivatives, which acts as a magnetic separator, would tend to symmetrically occupy the equatorial sites of the metals centers and produce 1D anticipated chains. (3) The decorated carboxylate substitutes, which act as structural linkers, would result in high dimensional magnetic CPs. Guided by this synthetic strategy, two CPs, formulated as {[Co(H2O)6]·[Co2(L1)2]}n (1) and {Co(H2O)(L3)}n (2), were synthesized and structurally characterized. In accordance with our assumptions, 1D cobalt–carboxyl chains were successfully embedded in 3D coordination topologies, and even the rare 2D 63 cobalt–carboxyl layer was presented in 2. Magnetic measurements revealed slow magnetic relaxation behaviors in 1 and 2, validating our synthesis strategy. The quantum calculations of magnetic behaviors and HF-EPR analysis of D values were also carried out to give in-depth investigations on magnetostructural correlations. Crystal structures, topological analyses, magnetic properties and HF-EPR analysis of these compounds are presented in detail.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All starting materials were obtained commercially and used without further purification. The IR spectra were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Nicolet 5DX spectrometer (KBr pellets). The ligands were synthesized using a literature method.18 Magnetic susceptibility measurements were carried out in the temperature range of 1.8–300 K with a magnetic field of 1000 Oe on Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 magnetometer. HF-EPR measurements of the polycrystalline samples were performed on a locally developed instrument with pulsed-magnetic fields up to 30 T in WHMFC, China.18d–f

Synthesis of {[Co(H2O)6]·[Co2(L1)2]}n (1)

A mixture of Co(OAc)2·4H2O (25 mg, 0.1 mmol), L1 (32 mg, 0.1 mmol), DEF (4 mL) and deionized water (4 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL borosilicate screw vial and then heated to 80 °C for 24 h; red crystals were obtained in 60% yield. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3364(m), 1683(m), 1577(s), 1508(m), 1375(s), 1236(m), 1134(w), 862(w), 764(m).

Synthesis of {Co(H2O)(L3)}n (2)

A mixture of Co(OAc)2·4H2O (25 mg, 0.1 mmol), L2 (27 mg, 0.1 mmol), acetone (2 mL), and deionized water (0.5 mL) was placed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined reactor and sealed and heated at 155 °C for 3 days. After the mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1, red plate-like crystals were obtained in 40% yield. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3580(m), 3122(m), 1666(s), 1570(s), 1413(s), 1392(s), 1282(s), 1138(w), 1004(m), 781(m).

X-ray structural determination

X-ray diffraction data of 1 (0.2 × 0.1 × 0.1 mm) and 2 (0.1 × 0.1 × 0.05 mm) were collected on a Bruker diffractometer using Mo-Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation at room temperature. The structures of the complexes were solved by direct methods, and the non-hydrogen atoms were located from the trial structure and then refined anisotropically with SHELXTL using a full-matrix least squares procedure based on F2 values. The hydrogen atom were fixed geometrically at the calculated distances and allowed to ride on the parent atoms. CCDC 1508980 (1) and 1508981 (2) contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this study.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of 1 to 2

The two ligands were synthesized according to the route in Scheme 1 summarized from the related references.18 The syntheses of 1 and 2 were carried out by a solvothermal method with different metal salts and solvent systems. In the IR spectrum, the strong bands around 1600 and 1385 cm−1 are assignable to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of the carboxylate groups. No band in the region of 1690–1730 cm−1 indicates complete deprotonation of the carboxylate groups, and this is consistent with the result of X-ray diffraction analysis.

Crystal structure of 1

Complex 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/m, and the asymmetric unit consists of three half-occupancy cobalt atoms, one completely deprotonated L1 ligand and four water molecules (Fig. 1a). Each metal ion is located on the centrosymmetric positions and is hexa-coordinated with the slightly-distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The equatorial plane of Co1 is occupied by four monodentate dicarboxyl groups on two imidazole platforms, while the axial sites are occupied by two coordinated water molecules. In contrast, two N–O chelating rings of two centrosymmetrical imidazole-dicarboxylate units occupy the equatorial sites of the Co2 atom, while the axial sites are occupied by two monodentate carboxylates from two decorated benzoate moieties. Each imidazole dicarboxylic part utilizes its two types of chelating modes (N–O and O–O modes) to join the alternative Co1 and Co2 ions to form one dimensional chains running along the crystallographic a axis, as shown in Fig. 1b. The adjacent cobalt ions are inter-connected via anti–anti carboxylate bridges with Co⋯Co distance of 5.753(4) Å. In addition, a third cobalt ion (symmetric Co2 ion) in the adjacent 1D chain is further connected by the decorated benzoate moieties. In this way, the neighboring 1D chains are further inter-connected by the fold ligands with dihedral angle of 72.69(2)° to form the 3D structure, leaving 1D channel in the packing mode along the b-axial direction. The Co(H2O)62+ ions were stabilized in these cavities via strong hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. 1c). Co–O and Co–N bond lengths fall in the range of 2.020(1) to 2.217(2) Å, in accordance with the typical Co(II) state. To gain a better insight into the complicated 3D architecture, topological analysis was carried out using the TOPOS program. Considering the different cobalt ions and ligands as two-, four- and three-connecting nodes, the 3D structure of 1 topologically possesses a 2-, 3-, and 4-connected trinodal net with the point (Schläfli) symbol {83}2{84·122}{8} calculated with TOPOS software, as shown in Fig. 2. This net was simplified to analyze supernet-subnet relations by TOPOS as subnet of the 2,3,4-connected topology, and has never been observed in CPs.

Crystal structure of 2

Comparably, substituting by the acetate group decorated onto imidazole-dicarboxylate scaffold, a new 3D coordination complex 2 with in situ generated ligand L3 was finally constructed. Complex 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/n, and the asymmetric unit consists of one cobalt atom, one completely deprotonated L3 ligand and one water molecule (Fig. 3a). Each metal ion is located on the centrosymmetric positions and is hexa-coordinated with the highly distorted octahedral coordination geometry. The equatorial plane of Co1 is occupied by one N–O chelating ring of imidazole-carboxylate units and two monodentate carboxylate groups from three different L3 ligands, while the axial sites are occupied by one monodentate carboxylate from the fourth L3 ligand and one water molecule. Observed from the final crystal structure, the designed and synthesized imidazole tricarboxylic ligand is found to have already lost one carboxylate group and in situ generated a new dicarboxylate one. The neighboring cobalt ions are linked together by the syn–anti carboxylate to form one dimensional chains running along the crystallographic c axis with Co⋯Co distance of 5.411(2) Å, which then intercross via double syn–syn carboxylate bridges to assemble the 2D cobalt–carboxylate 63 layer in bc plane with Co⋯Co distance of 4.518(2) Å, as shown in Fig. 3b. The final 3D structure is constructed by the connection of in situ generated L3 ligands (Fig. 3c). Co–O and Co–N bond lengths fall in the range of 2.038(1) to 2.250(2) Å, in accordance with the typical Co(II) state. To gain a better insight into the complicated 3D architecture, topological analysis was carried out using the TOPOS program. Considering the cobalt ions and ligands as four- and four-connecting nodes, the 3D structure of 2 topologically possesses a 4-connected uninodal sra net with the point (Schläfli) symbol {42·63·8} calculated with TOPOS software, as shown in Fig. 4. The less steric hindrance of the substituted groups in 2 must be responsible for the difference in the final structures compared to that of 1.

Magnetic properties of 1 and 2

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of 1 and 2 were measured on crystalline samples in the range of 1.8–300 K and are shown as χMT and 1/χMversus T plots.

The χMT value of 1 at 300 K is about 2.84 emu K mol−1 per CoII ion, which is much larger than the theoretical spin-only value (1.875 emu K mol−1 from g = 2.00) expected for one magnetically isolated CoII ion. As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value decreases continuously down to 1.36 emu K mol−1 at 5.5 K, followed by an increase to 1.96 emu K mol−1 at 1.8 K, as shown in Fig. 5. The data above 30 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 3.04 emu K mol−1 and θ = −19.8 K. The high temperature behaviors of χMT might indicate the ZFS effect or first spin–orbit coupling between CoII ions in cobalt–carboxylate chain of compound 1 above 30 K. The small J value obtained from the quantum Monte-Carlo simulations indicates that the presence of first spin–orbit coupling between CoII ions causes a decrease in the χMT values. The increase in χMT below 5.5 K indicates the existence of weak ferromagnetic behavior. The upturn of χMT value below 6 K suggests an uncompensated magnetic moment of the system possibly arising from ferromagnetic coupling of the Co(II) ions.19


Fig. 5 Temperature dependence of χMT per Co ion of 1 in the temperature range of 1.8–300 K under 1000 Oe. Inset: 1/χMvs. T plot with linear fitting to Curie–Weiss law.

M vs. H/T plots of 1 at different temperatures are not superimposed (Fig. S2†), confirming the presence of significant magnetic anisotropy, and this is attributed to the strong spin-orbital coupling of the CoII ion. The nearly linear increase of field-dependent magnetization at 2 K also indicates the antiferromagnetic interactions. The magnetization at 7 T is 1.76NμB, which is much lower than the expected saturation value (3NμB for g = 2). No hysteresis loop could be observed clearly at 2.0 K.

To investigate the magnetic dynamics of 1, ac magnetic susceptibility data were measured under a 0 or 2 kOe dc field and 3 Oe ac field (Fig. S3† and Fig. 6). No out-of-phase signal was observed under a zero dc field. However, upon application of a 2 kOe dc field, the slight trend of temperature- and frequency-dependent ac signals and the peak shift could be observed (Fig. 7), which could, to some extent, indicate the presence of field-induced slow magnetic relaxation behavior of 1 below 2 K.

Similar magnetic behavior of 2 could be observed compared to 1. The χMT value of 2 at 300 K is about 2.98 emu K mol−1 per CoII ion, which is much larger than the theoretical spin-only value (1.875 emu K mol−1 from g = 2.00) expected for one magnetically isolated CoII ion. As the temperature is lowered, the χMT value decreases continuously down to 1.42 emu K mol−1 at 5 K, as shown in Fig. 7. The data above 30 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 3.11 emu K mol−1 and θ = −11.1 K. The abovementioned characteristics indicate the ZFS effect or first spin–orbit coupling between CoII ions in cobalt–carboxylate chain of compound 2 above 30 K. Then, the χMT value increases rapidly up to a maximum value of 10.1 emu K mol−1 at about 3.2 K, suggesting the existence of weak ferromagnetic behavior. The upturn of χMT value below 5 K suggests an uncompensated magnetic moment of the system possibly arising from spin canting of the antiferromagnetically coupled CoII ions or the consequence of a ferromagnetic coupling through a syn–anti conformation for the coordination of carboxylate group.19 Upon further decrease in the temperature lower than 3.5 K, the χMT value drops rapidly to 5.82 emu K mol−1 at 2 K because of the saturation effect.

M vs. H/T plots of 2 at different temperatures are not superimposed (Fig. S2†), confirming the presence of significant magnetic anisotropy, which is attributed to the strong spin-orbital coupling of the CoII ion. The magnetization at 7 T is 2.30NμB, which is much lower than the expected saturation value (3NμB for g = 2); this is consistent with the antiferromagnetic interactions indicated above. No hysteresis loop could be observed clearly at 2.0 K.

To investigate the magnetic dynamics of 2, the temperature dependencies of the ac magnetic susceptibility under Hdc = 0 Oe and Hac = 3 Oe was also measured with frequencies of 1 and 1000 Hz from 2 to 7 K to further investigate the underlying magnetic nature. As depicted in Fig. 8 (Fig. S4†), a slight frequency-dependent behavior was observed in the ac susceptibility. The frequency shift parameter can be estimated using equation Φ = ΔTp/[TpΔ(logω)], where ΔTp is the shift of peak in χ′, Tp is the peak temperature and ω is the applied frequency. The value of Φ is determined to be 0.01, which falls within the usual range (10−2–10−3) of spin-glass behavior.20 Therefore, according to the Arrhenius law (τ = τ0exp(Δτ/kBT)), in which the relaxation time (τ) is determined using τ = 1/(2πν) from the χ′′ signals, kB is the Boltzmann constant and τ0 is a pre-exponential factor; the relaxation barrier (Δτ/kB) was difficult to determine due to the weak ac signals caused by the spin-glass behavior.21 Comparing 2 with 1, the different connection mode of carboxylate must be responsible for the presence of slow magnetic relaxation, which is rarely observed in 3D CPs containing 2D cobalt–carboxylate layer. Quantitative analysis of the AC susceptibility of 2 was carried out using the method of F. Canepa.20c However, no satisfactory results could be obtained, which might be due to the strong spin–orbit coupling of Co(II) complex.

Clearly, both the dc and ac magnetization data suggest the similar magnetic behaviors for 1 and 2, which might be described as spin glass behavior. The increase in the values of χMT below 6 K must be attributed to the presence of intramolecular ferromagnetic interactions between the Co(II) ions mediated by the different bridging modes of carboxylate groups. From a structural point of view for 1, the adjacent cobalt(II) ions are bridged only by the anti–anti carboxylate groups, which mediate dominant antiferromagnetic interactions observed from the magnetization values (1.76NμB). However, the in-phase and out-of-phase components of the ac susceptibility both show maxima at a frequency-independent temperature, confirming the occurrence of a weak ferromagnetic interaction.

In contrast, there are two types of coupling pathways in 2: syn–anti and double syn–syn carboxylate groups. It is well known that syn–syn carboxylate group transmits antiferromagnetic interactions. Therefore, in terms of magnetism, a weak ferromagnetic interaction in 2 should arise via the syn–anti carboxylate group.3e Generally, weak ferro- or antiferromagnetic interaction could be involved through this syn–anti carboxylate group in the light of the magneto-structural results obtained with analogous carboxylate-bridged magnetic complexes. Therefore, for 2, the spin glass may result from the competition of weak ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions due to the exchange fluctuations in the magnitude of the 2D 63 cobalt–carboxylate layer. Co(II) species containing 2D square-grid M–O–C–O–M layer is relatively common, but a few exhibit slow magnetic relaxation.12 Herein, the example that presented 2D Co–carboxylate 63 layers with the phenomenon of slow magnetic relaxation is interesting and deserves to be investigated deeply and theoretically.

QMC fitting and DFT calculations of magnetic properties of 2

Owing to the complexity of calculus caused by counter cation [Co(H2O)6]2+ in 1, only the related theoretical calculations of 2 were carried out to tentatively elucidate the origin of ferromagnetic interaction in 2. However, the theoretical magnetic analysis is complicated by the intrinsic single-ion effects of octahedral Co(II). Some compounds were reported without quantitative magnetic analyses and some were treated with oversimplified models without considering the single-ion effects. This situation makes it difficult to extract magnetostructural correlations. Due to the development of the calculation level and computer devices, difficult calculation could be carried out, which was utilized on 2.

First, to identify the magnetic coupling mechanism, the fitting of M–T curve was carried out. Scrutinizing geometrical structure of 2, we found that the magnetic property was dominated by two-dimensional 63 net {Co(COO)2}n layer, as shown in Fig. 3b. It is well known that the traditional Irreducible Tensor Operators (ITO) method had no ability to simulate the M–T curve because of a large magnetic exchange matrix derived from infinite Co(II) ions. Therefore, Quantum Monte Carlo simulation (see ESI†), over the temperature range of 5.0–300 K, was performed based on the LOOP algorithms22 of the ALPS project.23 The magnetic coupling path of 2 is simplified as 2-J mode by a careful inspection of the 63 {Co(COO)2}n layer. J1 represents magnetic exchange of two syn–syn carboxylate groups (d(Co⋯Co) = 4.518 Å), and J2 is used to describe the magnetic propagation of one syn–anti carboxylate group (d(Co⋯Co) = 5.411 Å). Temperature-independent paramagnetism (TIP) parameter in susceptibility equation was taken into account because of the large number of paramagnetic centers. The single ion anisotropy parameter (D) and weak-interaction between two-dimension {Co(COO)2}n layer (zJ) were also considered. According to the principle of the least reliability factor (R), the obtained best parameters are J1 = −0.76 cm−1, J2 = −0.49 cm−1, D = 0.78 cm−1, g = 2.39, zJ = 0.36 cm−1, TIP = 152 × 10−6 cm3 mol−1 and R = 6.91 × 10−4 (R = ∑[(χMT)obs − (χMT)calcd]2/∑[(χMT)obs]2). Two sets of negative J values show that 2 features weak anti-ferromagnetic property, which is consistent with our postulations and previous results of Co-based coordination polymers.24 The positive zJ value indicates the existence of weak ferromagnetic interactions in 2, which, to some extent, explains the origin of the spin glass phenomenon caused by the competition of different interactions. The observed mirror deviation between the experiment and the QMC simulation may be a result of the existence of spin–orbit coupling effects in six-coordinate Co(II) complex.25

Furthermore, spin-polarized DFT calculations using the VASP program26 were performed to examine the magnetic exchange mechanism. For the description of 3d-block electrons in Co(II) ions; the Hubbard Ueff value of Co(II) was first calculated using the linear response approach introduced by Cococcioni et al.27 (see ESI†). The initial configuration derived from single-crystal measurement result was further relaxed with and without the effective Hubbard Ueff (2.14 eV). A comparison of the geometrical parameter (see Table S1†) can reveal that the resulting structure under Hubbard Ueff of 2.14 eV shows better agreement with the experimental structure than that relaxed in the absence of Hubbard Ueff. Thus, the 2.14 eV of Ueff can reasonably describe the stronger correlation interaction of 3d electrons in Co(II) ions. Subsequently, the different initial magnetic states of four Co(II) ions in the cell were optimized. The most stable magnetic states of (↑↓↑↓) for the four Co(II) ions can confirm the anti-ferromagnetic property in 2. Moreover, the J1 and J2 values (see Fig. S5†) were computed by the broken symmetry (BS) approach.28 The obtained J1 (−1.98 cm−1) and J2 (−0.95 cm−1) approximated to those obtained by QMC.

Since the magnetic property is generally dominated by the distribution of DOS in the vicinity of Fermi level, partial DOS of Co(II) ions and relevant linking carboxylate bridges were identified. As shown in Fig. 9, 3d PDOS of Co(II) ions either spin up or spin down well distributed in the district of the Fermi level. The 2p PDOS of the carboxylate bridges were clearly split. The order magnetic spin arrangement was generated by linking carboxylates, leading to the effective magnetic exchange between adjacent Co(II) ions. According to the PDOS distribution, the magnetic property is derived mainly from the 3d orbital of Co(II) ions and the 2p orbitals of C and O in carboxylate bridges also contribute to the entire spin magnetic moment. Furthermore, we can see that 3d state of Co(II) ions and 2p states of linking carboxylates possess similar characters at the Fermi level, suggesting that such valence-shell orbitals orderly hybridize each other. This regular hybridization can provide a super-exchange path in the indirect interaction between two neighboring magnetic ions. Moreover, the spin magnetic moments (Ms) of Co(II) ions and linking carboxylates were also obtained, as displayed in Table S1.† Four Co(II) ions feature −2.646, 2.646, −2.646, and 2.646μB, while the linking carboxylate group exhibits little spin magnetic moment either positive or negative. Owing to the spin-polarized effect of Co(II) ions, the Ms values of the coordination oxygen atoms are much larger than those of carbon atoms. Interestingly, carboxylate bridges show two different spin nets for super-exchange of Co(II) ions: −/+/− for syn–anti mode and −/−/− for syn–syn mode. This result is compatible with the other ones of magnetic CPs.29 The calculated spin net for syn–anti carboxylate clearly manifests the co-existence of weak ferro- or antiferromagnetic interaction in the 2D layer of 2, which is consistent with the magnetostructural analysis.

HF-EPR measurements of 1 and 2

To reveal details of the magnetic anisotropy of 1 and 2, HF-EPR measurements were performed on polycrystalline samples over the frequency range of 60–260 GHz and field range of 0–12 T (Fig. 10). Considering the similarity of the structures for 1 and 2 and the small amplitude of the exchange interactions between Co ions (J1 = −0.76 cm−1 and J2 = −0.49 cm−1 for 2 as shown above), the Co ions in 1 and 2 can be treated as paramagnetic species with weak interactions in the high magnetic field limit. The following spin Hamiltonian was used to describe the magnetic properties of 1 and 2.
 
Ĥ = gμBB·Ŝ + D(Ŝ2z − Ŝ2/3) + E(Ŝx2 − Ŝy2)(1)
where D and E are the axial and rhombic ZFS parameters. For 1, we found three major series of signals that shifted to a higher field with increasing microwave frequency. The g-values of the signals were 5.70(3), 3.67(3), and 2.10(3) from the slope of the frequency-field plot. These g-values can only be associated with a high spin S = 3/2 CoII ions, and the EPR signals can only be interpreted resulting from intra-Kramers transitions within the mS = ±1/2 multiplet. Inter-Kramer transitions could not be observed owing to the large magnitude of zero field splitting. In contrast, the effective g-values of 2 are 5.05(3), 3.65(3), and 2.23(3), and its EPR spectra contain inter-Kramer transitions pointing to every gap (|2D|) of 270 GHz (9 cm−1); hence the magnitude of D is about 4.5 cm−1. Moreover, the effective g-values of 1 and 2 are all larger than 2.00, which are consistent with the results of the theoretical calculation, and therefore the signs of their D values are probably positive (as illustrated in Fig. S6†).

Conclusions

In summary, by carefully selecting and designing the bifunctional ligands, magnetic CPs that exhibit slow magnetic relaxation at low temperature regions were successfully constructed and further characterized structurally. In 1, 1D cobalt–carboxyl chains are stabilized in the final structure. In contrast, by adjusting the length of the substituted arms on imidazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid, a novel 3D CP, 2, containing 2D cobalt–carboxyl layer was obtained. The investigation of their magnetic behaviors manifests the presence of slow magnetic relaxation at low temperature regions. A combination of Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) simulation and the first-principles Density functional theory (DFT) calculations showed that compound 2 features weak anti-ferromagnetic coupling mechanisms with two different super-exchange paths of −/+/− for syn–anti carboxylate bridges and −/−/− for syn–syn carboxylate bridges. Magnetostructural analysis and quantum calculation results manifest the co-existence of ferro- and antiferromagnetic interactions via syn–anti carboxylate bridges in 2. Through HF-EPR measurements performed on polycrystalline samples over the frequency range of 60–260 GHz and field range of 0–12 T, the effective g-values of 1 and 2 are all larger than 2.00, and the signs of their D values are probably positive. This is also an illustration on the synthesis strategy wherein embedding 1D or 2D metal–carboxyl chain or layer into 3D coordination networks is an effective method to build high dimensional molecular magnetic materials.
