Abstract

Three isostructural metal–organic polyhedral cage based frameworks (denoted NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115) with (3,24)‐connected topology have been synthesised by combining hexacarboxylate isophthalate linkers with {Cu2(RCOO)4} paddlewheels. All three frameworks have the same cuboctahedral cage structure constructed from 24 isophthalates from the ligands and 12 {Cu2(RCOO)4} paddlewheel moieties. The frameworks differ only in the functionality of the central core of the hexacarboxylate ligands with trimethylphenyl, phenylamine and triphenylamine moieties in NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, respectively. Exchange of pore solvent with acetone followed by heating affords the corresponding desolvated framework materials, which show high BET surface areas of 2970, 3424 and 3394 m2 g−1 for NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, respectively. Desolvated NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114 show high total H2 adsorption capacities of 6.7 and 6.8 wt %, respectively, at 77 K and 60 bar. Desolvated NOTT‐115 has a significantly higher total H2 uptake of 7.5 wt % under the same conditions. Analysis of the heats of adsorption (Qst) for H2 reveals that with a triphenylamine moiety in the cage wall, desolvated NOTT‐115 shows the highest value of Qst for these three materials, indicating that functionalisation of the cage walls with more aromatic rings can enhance the H2/framework interactions. In contrast, measurement of Qst reveals that the amine‐substituted trisalkynylbenzene core used in NOTT‐114 gives a notably lower H2/framework binding energy.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are of enormous interest as porous crystalline materials with highly promising gas adsorption properties.1 For hydrogen (H2) storage, MOFs with high surface areas exhibit significant capacities for H2 sorption with excellent reversibility.2 The structures of these porous materials can be effectively tuned by design, whereby functionalisation of the organic linkers and choice of metal nodes with specific geometries can optimise adsorption properties.3 A promising strategy to improve H2 uptake in these materials, which function by physisorption, is to design new MOFs that not only possess high surface areas and large pore volumes, but also incorporate adsorption sites, such as exposed metal sites, that have a high affinity for H2.4

We are particularly interested in the design and synthesis of polyaromatic carboxylate linkers containing isophthalate moieties combined with copper paddlewheel {Cu2(COO)4} building blocks to construct highly porous MOFs.5–10 These materials show high BET surface areas and excellent H2 storage capacities. In addition, it is possible, by heating the as‐prepared samples, to generate coordinatively unsaturated copper sites, which give rise to strong interactions with the adsorbed H2 molecules within the MOF.7 Indeed, we have reported the use of a highly connected C3‐symmetric hexacarboxylate linker to assemble a highly porous copper paddlewheel MOF (denoted NOTT‐112),6 which has ubt topology,11a,11b a strategy which has been followed by others.8, 9 There are three types of cages in the framework: a cuboctahedron (cage A, Figure 1) which contains 24 CuII ions and 24 isophthalate moieties from the linkers, a truncated tetrahedron (cage B) and truncated octahedron (cage C). This (3,24)‐connected network can be alternatively interpreted as a rht11c,11d net if the [Cu24(isophthalate)24] cuboctahedral cage is described as a rhombicuboctahedron by connecting the 24 centres of the isophthalate units, which then define the vertices of the polyhedron (see Supporting Information). Desolvated NOTT‐112, which contains unsaturated CuII sites, is efficient in adsorbing H2 at both low and high pressures at cryogenic temperatures, up to 10.0 wt % (total uptake) of H2 at 77 K and 35 bar. Neutron powder diffraction studies on fully desolvated NOTT‐112 revealed the preferential D2 binding sites are in the smallest cuboctahedral cage incorporating 12 unsaturated copper ions. Therefore, it was concluded that the cuboctahedral cages show strong affinity for H2 molecules due to their specific geometric arrangement of exposed copper sites and the pre‐organisation of ligand sites within the framework.7

We report herein the formation of a series of topologically analogous, polyhedral cage‐based MOFs NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, which are constructed from the hexacarboxylate linkers H6LMe, H6L and H6LNPh (Figure 2)12 and CuII paddlewheel nodes. Variation of the organic linker functionality allows investigation of the effects of pore wall decoration on the internal BET surface areas, pore volumes and H2 adsorption in these framework materials.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and structural description of frameworks NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115: H6LMe and H6L were synthesised using Sonogashira coupling of diethyl 5‐iodoisophthalate with the appropriate trisethynylbenzene precursor (Scheme 1). H6LNPh was prepared by Suzuki coupling of tris(4‐bromophenyl)amine with 3,5‐bis(ethoxycarbonyl)phenylboronic acid (Scheme 1). All of these hexacarboxylate ligands have lower solubility in DMF than related tricarboxylate or tetracarboxylate polyaromatic ligands,5 and therefore can be readily purified by recrystallisation from DMF. The solvothermal reaction of H6LMe with Cu(NO3)2⋅3 H2O in an acidified mixture of DMF/H2O/DMSO afforded the solvated material [Cu3(LMe)(H2O)3]⋅4 DMSO⋅9 DMF⋅9 H2O (NOTT‐113), while [Cu3(L)(H2O)3]⋅9 DMF⋅18 H2O (NOTT‐114) and [Cu3(LNPh)(H2O)3]⋅5 DMF⋅10 H2O (NOTT‐115) were synthesised under solvothermal conditions using acidified DMF/H2O solvent mixtures. The phase purity of the bulk crystalline solids was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD). The synthesised complexes were characterised by single‐crystal X‐ray crystallography, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), and elemental analysis (see Supporting Information).

Single‐crystal X‐ray structural studies confirmed that the frameworks of NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115 are isostructural, all crystallising in the cubic space group Fmm with large unit cells (a>42 Å). In all three cases each carboxylate of the ligand L6− bridges two CuII ions to form a dicopper paddlewheel {Cu2(COO)4} node. Each L6− moiety connects six {Cu2(COO)4} paddlewheels to form a hexagonal face of the cuboctahedron (Figure 3), which can be considered as a 3‐connected node, containing three isophthalate groups that are coplanar and covalently interconnected via rigid backbone linkages (trisalkynylbenzene moieties for NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114, and triphenylamine for NOTT‐115). All the frameworks adopt the same cuboctahedral cage, constructed from 24 isophthalate units and 12 {Cu2(COO)4} paddlewheels, which serves as a 24‐connected node via the edges of the cuboctahedron. The cuboctahedron exhibits two types of small window, one triangular and one square, which share edges 9.3 Å in length, and encompasses an inner spherical cavity of approximately 13 Å in diameter. Thus, the isostructural frameworks in the three compounds form (3,24)‐connected networks. The frameworks can also be viewed in terms of nanosized cages interconnected through cuboctahedral cages as nodes (Figure 4). The face‐centred packing of the smallest cuboctahedral cages thus generates rigid polyhedral networks.

Two further polyhedral cages are generated within these structures: firstly, a truncated tetrahedron with four triangular windows and four hexagonal faces, encompassing an inner sphere of diameter of 13 Å (NOTT‐113), 13 Å (NOTT‐114) or 11 Å (NOTT‐115) (Figure 2); secondly, a truncated octahedron that encompasses an inner sphere of diameter of 20 Å (NOTT‐113), 20 Å (NOTT‐114) or 18 Å (NOTT‐115) (Figure 5). (The size of the polyhedral cage is calculated by fitting a sphere from the centroid of the cage to the van der Waals surface of its walls.) The ratio of the three types of cage in the framework [cage A (cuboctahedral): cage B (truncated tetrahedron): cage C (truncated octahedron)] is 1:2:1. Despite the slightly larger linker used in NOTT‐115 a smaller inner sphere diameter is observed for cages B and C as a result of the increased number of phenyl rings in the hexagonal face of (LNPh)6− in comparison to (LMe)6− or (L)6−.

The total solvent‐accessible volume for the desolvated framework after removal of guest solvents and coordinated water molecules was estimated to be 75 % (NOTT‐113), 76 % (NOTT‐114), and 72 % (NOTT‐115), calculated using the PLATON/VOID13 routine. The calculated densities of the desolvated frameworks are 0.592, 0.574 and 0.611 g cm−3 for NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114, and NOTT‐115, respectively. This also confirms that with more atoms in the hexagonal faces, desolvated NOTT‐115 has a higher crystal density than either NOTT‐113 or NOTT‐114.

Thermal stability: Compounds NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115 show very similar thermal behaviours as determined by TGA, with rapid loss of solvent under a N2 atmosphere below 100 °C and high stabilities up to 300 °C. By suspending the as‐synthesised crystalline samples of the frameworks in acetone it is possible to prepare samples that are acetone‐solvated, replacing other guest species. These acetone‐exchanged samples can be activated by heating at 110 °C under high vacuum, resulting in removal of guest species and the formation of deep purple‐blue crystalline materials. It should be noted that with nanosized linkers and cavities, the highly porous non‐interpenetrated frameworks of the NOTT materials are very stable, presumably as a result of the specific packing of the stable cuboctahedral cages (Figure 4).6, 7

BET surface area and pore size distribution: The permanent porosities of desolvated NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115 were confirmed by Ar sorption isotherms at 87 K (Figure 6), which show type I adsorption behaviour for all three materials. All three isotherms show small changes in gradient between 0.01 and 0.2 bar, attributed to filling of the largest nanosized cages in the frameworks. The apparent surface area of the desolvated material is estimated to be 2970, 3424 and 3394 m2g−1 for NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, respectively, by using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method from N2 isotherms, representing high surface areas even for MOF materials (Table 1). The Ar sorption data for these materials were analysed using non‐local density functional theory (NLDFT) implementing a hybrid kernel based on a zeolite/silica model containing cylindrical pores.14 The pore size distributions are calculated to be around 8, 15, and 20 Å for both NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114. As for NOTT‐115, there is a narrow distribution of micropores at 7–9, 10–15 and 17–18 Å (Figure 6). These pore sizes are consistent with the three types of cages observed in the single‐crystal structures of NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115. Pore volumes of 1.25, 1.36 and 1.38 cm3g−1 for NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, respectively, were calculated from the Ar isotherms. It is interesting to note that introducing methyl groups to the interior of the framework of NOTT‐113 blocks the cavities connecting cages A, B and C, resulting in reduced total surface area and pore volume compared with NOTT‐114.

Hydrogen adsorption: Gravimetric H2 adsorption data for the three desolvated NOTT materials were recorded using the IGA system over the range 0–20 bar at 78 K (Figure 7, top). All H2 isotherms show good reversibility and an absence of adsorption–desorption hysteresis.15 At 78 K and 20 bar, the highest total uptake was observed for NOTT‐115 with a value of 64.7 mg g−1 H2, and both NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114 show similarly high total H2 uptake with values of 63.1 and 62.1 mg g−1, respectively, under the same conditions. Comparison with related copper–tetracarboxylate MOFs reveals that NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114 show higher H2 adsorption capacities than either NOTT‐101 (60.6 mg g−1) or NOTT‐102 (60.7 mg g−1) at 78 K and 20 bar,5a,5b while NOTT‐115 shows a H2 capacity comparable to NOTT‐103 (65.1 mg g−1),5b NOTT‐110 (65.9 mg g−1) and NOTT‐111 (64.8 mg g−1).5c Interestingly, due to their high BET surface areas of more than 2900 m2 g−1, NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115 can adsorb 23.9, 22.8 and 24.2 mg g−1 of H2, respectively, at 1 bar and 78 K. These H2 uptake capacities at 1 bar and 78 K are high for frameworks containing [Cu2(O2CR)4] paddlewheels (Table 1).5

At low pressures the H2 adsorption capacities of these polyhedral frameworks are not influenced by their high surface areas and large pore volumes, which normally give rise to high uptake values at higher pressures. Thus, the efficient low‐pressure performance can be attributed to the high affinity of the H2 molecules for the exposed CuII centres inside the cuboctahedral cages16 of the three MOFs, which has been demonstrated by neutron powder diffraction studies of D2 adsorption in desolvated NOTT‐112.7 It is noticeable that, although they have very similar cage dimensions, NOTT‐113 shows a slightly higher H2 uptake below 20 bar than NOTT‐114. We conclude that the trismethylbenzene‐decorated central core of the ligand used in NOTT‐113 increases the H2‐framework interaction, because of overlapping potential‐energy fields from the cage walls (see below).

High‐pressure volumetric H2 adsorption measurements at 77 K confirm that the MOFs achieve maximum excess H2 adsorption of 53.7 mg g−1 (NOTT‐113) or 52.9 mg g−1 (NOTT‐114) at 30 bar, and 59.3 mg g−1 at 33 bar (NOTT‐115) (Figure 7, middle). Up to pressures of 20 bar, H2 adsorption results obtained using the volumetric method are in very good agreement with those using gravimetric approaches. Up to 60 bar, the total H2 uptake is 71.7 mg g−1, equivalent to 6.7 wt %17 for NOTT‐113, 72.4 mg g−1 (6.8 wt %) for NOTT‐114 and 80.7 mg g−1 (7.5 wt %) for NOTT‐115, indicating high H2 adsorption capacities. NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114 exhibit very similar H2 storage capacities at high pressures, despite NOTT‐114 displaying both a higher surface area and a larger pore volume than NOTT‐113. This indicates that the conjugation of the amine‐substituted central core adopts less favourable interactions with H2 than the other ligand systems studied. As expected, NOTT‐115, with the highest pore volume (1.38 cm3g−1) of the compounds studied, shows higher H2 uptake than either NOTT‐113 or NOTT‐114, confirming the importance of large pore volumes for H2 adsorption at high pressures, in agreement with previous theoretical modelling analyses.18

The H2 adsorption capacities for the NOTT series of materials are also impressive in volumetric terms and are amongst the highest reported for coordination framework materials (Figure 7, bottom). At 60 bar and 77 K the total volumetric capacities are 42.5 g L−1 for NOTT‐113 and 41.6 g L−1 for NOTT‐114, calculated using crystal densities derived from the single crystal structural studies. With a higher crystal density, NOTT‐115 shows remarkably high volumetric H2 uptake (49.3 g L−1), comparable to NOTT‐103 (50.0 g L−1 at 60 bar and 77 K), which shows the highest volumetric H2 uptake among the Cu–tetracarboxylate MOFs.

Analysis of isosteric heats of hydrogen adsorption: To gain insight into the H2 sorption behaviour in NOTT‐113, NOTT‐114 and NOTT‐115, the H2 adsorption isotherms were also measured at 88 K and isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) were determined by fitting a virial‐type19 equation [Eq. (1) in which P is pressure, N is amount of H2 adsorbed, T is temperature, and ai and bj are temperature‐independent empirical parameters] to the adsorption isotherm data for H2 at both 78 and 88 K (Supporting Information).

The values of Qst at zero coverage are estimated to be 5.9 kJ mol−1 (NOTT‐113), 5.3 kJ mol−1 (NOTT‐114) and 5.8 kJ mol−1 (NOTT‐115), with the values all gradually decreasing with increasing H2 loading (Figure 8). Although introducing bulky methyl groups into the trisalkynylbenzene ligand core does not significantly improve the overall H2 adsorption capacity at higher pressures due to reduced surface area and pore volume, the heats of adsorption for NOTT‐113 are generally ≈1.0 kJ mol−1 higher than those for NOTT‐114 at all measured H2 loadings. This suggests that amine functionalisation of the ligand core in NOTT‐114 may generally weaken H2 affinity. In contrast, NOTT‐115, incorporating triphenylamine moieties in the cage walls, shows higher values for Qst than NOTT‐113 with increasing H2 loading (Figure 9), suggesting that introduction of additional aromatic rings into the cage walls enhances the H2/framework binding energy.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we present here the synthesis of metal–organic polyhedral framework materials based upon trigonal hexacarboxylate isophthalate linkers, a strategy that generates highly porous materials with relatively high stability and surface areas. The polyhedral MOFs NOTT‐113 and NOTT‐114 show high H2 adsorption capacities of 6.7 and 6.8 wt % H2, respectively, at 77 K and 60 bar. Significantly, NOTT‐115 achieves 80.7 mg g−1 (7.5 wt %) total H2 adsorption at 77 K and 60 bar. Due to exposed CuII sites in the frameworks, NOTT‐113 to NOTT‐115 can also adsorb higher amounts of H2 at 1 bar and 78 K, compared to MOFs which lack such exposed metal sites.2e,2f The observed higher heat of adsorption for H2 in NOTT‐115 at low loading indicates that functionalisation of the central core of the hexagonal face with more aromatic rings can be used as the basis of a strategy to increase H2‐framework wall interactions. However, amine functionalisation of the conjugated ligand core in NOTT‐114 seems to reduce the interaction energy between H2 molecules and the framework. We are currently pursuing the strategy of combining tuneable pore volumes with the ubt network topology to synthesise a range of polyhedral MOFs and to assess their ability to adsorb H2 and other gases.

Experimental Section

All chemical reagents and gases were obtained from commercial sources and, unless otherwise noted, were used without further purification.

Synthesis of ligands: H6LMe and H6L were synthesised using a Sonogashira coupling approach using analogous methodologies and experimental procedures (Scheme 1). The details of synthesis of H6LMe are presented here. A mixture of diethyl 5‐iodoisophthalate (2.3 g, 6.6 mmol) and freshly distilled THF/iPr2NH (30 mL/10 mL) was degassed by bubbling dry Ar through it for 20 min. Bis(triphenylphosphine)palladium dichloride (90 mg, 2 mol %) and copper iodide (50 mg, 4 mol %) were added, and the mixture stirred at 25 °C for 15 min. A solution of 1,3,5‐triethynyl‐2,4,6‐trimethylbenzene (0.5 g, 2.6 mmol) in anhydrous THF (10 mL) was added dropwise, and the mixture stirred overnight at 50 °C. Upon completion, volatile species were removed under reduced pressure, and water was added to the residue (100 mL), which was extracted with CHCl3. The organic phase was washed with water and then brine and finally dried over MgSO4. The CHCl3 was removed under reduced pressure and the resulting solid was purified by silica gel column chromatography using CHCl3/hexane (1:1) to afford the hexaester (1.6 g, 83 %) as a pale yellow solid. The resulting ester was mixed with NaOH (30 mL; 2 M aqueous solution) and EtOH/THF (60 mL; 1:1 v/v) and the mixture refluxed for 5 h. After the hydrolysis was complete, THF and EtOH were removed under reduced pressure. The resulting aqueous solution was filtered and acidified to pH 2–3 with concentrated aqueous HCl. The resulting precipitate was separated by filtration and washed successively with water and then recrystallised from DMF to yield pure H6LMe (1.2 g, yield 93 %).

Preparation of copper‐based MOFs: Single crystals suitable for single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction were obtained by small‐scale reactions under solvothermal conditions. Argon isotherms: Argon sorption isotherms for pressures in the range 0–1.2 bar were measured on a Quantachrome Instruments Autosorb‐1. Samples were transferred under a dinitrogen atmosphere to pre‐weighed analysis cells, which were then sealed to prevent intrusion of oxygen and atmospheric moisture during transfers and weighing. The samples were evacuated by heating at 110 °C under dynamic vacuum until the outgas rate was less than 2 mTorr min−1 (0.27 Pa min−1). The evacuated analysis tubes containing degassed samples were then carefully transferred to an electronic balance and weighed to determine the mass of sample (typically around 50 mg). Ultrahigh purity He, N2 and Ar were used for all adsorption measurements: N2 isotherms were used for determination of the BET surface areas.

Gravimetric H2 isotherms: Hydrogen isotherms from 0–20 bar were determined using a Hiden Isochema intelligent gravimetric analyser (IGA). Ultrapure plus grade (99.9995 %) H2 was purified further using calcium alumina‐silicate and activated carbon adsorbents to remove trace amounts of water and other impurities before introduction into the IGA system. All data were corrected for the buoyancy of the system, samples and absorbates. The density of H2 at its boiling point (0.0708 g cm−3) was used for the adsorbate buoyancy correction.

High‐pressure H2 adsorption measurements: Volumetric H2 sorption measurements were performed at General Motors over a pressure range of 0–60 bar using an automatically controlled Sieverts apparatus (PCT‐Pro 2000 from Hy‐Energy LLC). Sample tubes of a known weight were loaded with approximately 300 mg of sample under an argon atmosphere. All measurements were made with ultrahigh purity grade (99.999 %) H2 and He, the latter being used for dead volume measurements.
