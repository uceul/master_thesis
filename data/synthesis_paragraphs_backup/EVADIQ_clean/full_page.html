Lanthanide MOFs constructed based on a difunctional ligand with bimodal emission and Eu3+ doped Dy3+ materials: white emission and color tuning†

Peng Xiao, Ping Wang, Rui Qing Fan*, Xi Du, Wei Chen, Hui Jie Zhang, Yang Song and Yu Lin Yang*
MIIT Key Laboratory of Critical Materials Technology for New Energy Conversion and Storage, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Harbin Institute of Technology, Harbin 150001, P. R. China. E-mail: fanruiqing@hit.edu.cn; ylyang@hit.edu.cn; Fax: +86-0451-86413710

Received 8th June 2016 , Accepted 23rd August 2016

First published on 25th August 2016

Based on azabicyclo ligands, four 3D supramolecular lanthanide complexes have been synthesized through hydro(solvo)thermal reactions, generally formulated as [Dy(bipy) (NO3)3]2 (1·Dy), [Dy(bipy)2(NO3)3] (2·Dy), [Dy2(bpdc)3(DMF)(H2O)]2·(DMF)10(H2O) (3·Dy) and [Eu4(bpdc)6(CH3NH2)3(DMSO)(H2O)] (4·Eu) (Bipy = 2,2′-bipyridine; H2bpdc = 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide), and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy, elemental analysis and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD). There are three kinds of hydrogen bonding, C2–H2A⋯O6, C4–H4A⋯O4 and C8–H8A⋯O8 along the a/c, b, c axis, respectively in complex 1·Dy. They connect the 1·Dy complex into a 3D 6-connected pcu supramolecular network. 2·Dy is connected into a 3D network with 8–connected bcu architecture by two kinds of hydrogen bonding: C2–H2A⋯O1 and C9–H9A⋯O3. In order to connect the polymer to form a 3D complex, H2bpdc has been chosen in the synthesis and design of the crystal. In the network of 3·Dy, two kinds of Dy(III) cations in different coordination models construct the different binuclear [Dy2O14(DMF)2] and [Dy2O12(H2O)2], and further form the (4,4,6)-connected 3D architecture. The isostructural complex 4·Eu has been synthesised simultaneously. The property of luminescence in solid state or different solvents of the four complexes has been studied in detail. The four complexes show the unique emission peaks of lanthanide cations. Futhermore, in 3·Dy and 4·Eu, the ligand shows a rare bimodal emission, which can sensitize the emission of europium cations as an “antenna effect”, and the shorter one keeps its blue emission though coordinated with Dy(III) cations. Based on the synthesis of green or red color materials, multicolored photoluminescence tuning has been studied in detail through growth of a series of bimetallic complexes, the crystal formula as [Dy2nEu′2−2n(bpdc)3(DMF)(H2O)] (M-1–M-9; 0 < n < 1). We achieved dichromatic fine-tuning among the triangle region of red, green and white. The linear dependence of the emissions has been analyzed, and the mathematical matrix model is useful to calculate the ratio and excitation wavelength for a given color in need.

Introduction

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are hybrid inorganic–organic materials made from an assembly of metal cations with organic linkers.1–3 Their well-defined porosity and tunable chemical functionality make them extremely attractive for applications in gas storage, catalysis, separation and luminescence.4–8 Because of the strong characteristic coordination ability of lanthanide ions and diverse coordination modes, lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (LOFs) not only can construct different structures, but also possess unique optical properties, such as large Stokes shifts, high color purity, and long luminescence lifetimes.9,10

Recent research has not only limited to build a single rare earth coordination polymer, and multi-color adjustment becomes the current hot spots.11,12 Therefore, a large amount of mixed lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (Ln-MOFs) have been fabricated to tune the photoluminescence through trichromatic strategies, which feature in selective incorporation of different Ln centers and chromophoric sensitizers in regular and rigid crystal space to allow dense packing and enhanced emission, meanwhile minimizing self-quenching. In fact, many white emission and color tuning of lanthanide functionalized MOFs have been reported, which are summarized in Table S1.† Most of recent researches have been working on emitting tunable three primary colors (RGB: red-green-blue) towards white light.13–16 But the works usually turn to white colors by adjusting the concentration of three kinds of metal cations (Eu3+, Tb3+, Gd3+).17,18 Therein, in order to obtain the color blue, previous works usually used Gd complexes, without any energy sensitization process. Chen and co-workers have focused on color tuning with two lanthanide cations Tb and Eu, which shows a more simple and convenient strategy to obtain white emission.19–24 And as we have known, it's the first report to obtain white emission and color tuning by Dy cation in our work.

To date, most LOFs consist of a lanthanide cation and an organic linker/sensitizer, and therefore the lanthanide cation is an important structural component of the LOFs.25 Among the various organic ligand, 2,2′-bipyridine is chosen to construct two complexes, and in order to improve the dimension of the complex, 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid is also selected. The ligand plays critical roles in the synthesis and characters, and also provides a rigid scaffold that can serve to protect lanthanide cations from solvent quenching. Based on the conjugacy of the ligands 2,2′-bipyridine and 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid, they can be the “antenna” to sensitize the lanthanide ions luminescence by the energy transport from ligand to metal ions.26–28 Thus, we choose Dy(III) and Eu(III) cations as metal source via hydro-thermal or solvothermal method to obtain four complexes, [Dy(bipy)(NO3)3]2(1·Dy), [Dy(bipy)2(NO3)3] (2·Dy), [Dy2(bpdc)3(DMF)(H2O)]2·(DMF)10(H2O) (3·Dy) and [Eu4(bpdc)6(CH3NH2)3(DMSO)(H2O)] (4·Eu). The ligand bpdc has an infrequent property of bimodal emission and the shorter-wavelength emission keeps the shape when coordinated to Dy(III) cations, which gives the possibility of color tuning. Based on the ratio of Eu/Dy = 4:6 (M-6) and the excitation wavelength at 365 nm, we have obtained the white emission. More significantly, to the best of our knowledge, M-6 is the first MOF of white emission material based on Dy and Eu cations. According to IR spectra and powder X-ray diffraction confirmed the structure of the bimetallic complexes, the photoluminescence property was studied in detail, and the luminescence decay was used to prove the transportation between the ligand and metal cations.29

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All of the compounds were synthesized by solvothermal method performing in Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclaves under autogenous pressure. All chemical were commercially available sources of analytical grade and used without further purification. IR spectra were obtained from KBr pellets using a Nicolet Avatar-360 infrared spectrometer in the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses were performed on a PerkinElmer 240c element analyzer. The thermal analysis was performed on a ZRY-2P thermogravimetric analysis from 30 to 750 °C with heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a flow of air. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded using a PAN alytical X-ray diffractometer. The solid-state and solution photoluminescence analyses were carried out on an Edinburgh FLS920 fluorescence spectrometer in the range of 200–800 nm. Nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms were measured using an ASAP2020 analyzer.

Synthesis of [Dy(bipy)(HNO3)2(NO3−)]2 (1·Dy)

A mixture of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O (43.8 mg, 0.1 mmol), 4-(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (18.8 mg, 0.1 mmol) and 2,2′-bipyridine (15.2 mg, 0.1 mmol) was dissolved in acetonitrile (CH3CN, 5 mL) and then stirred at room temperature for 20 min. After that it was transferred into a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 120 °C for 5 days. After slow cooling to room temperature, light yellow block crystals of 1·Dy were obtained by filtration (yield 68% based on Dy). Elemental analysis for C20H20N10O18Dy2 (Mr: 1009.43): C, 23.80; N, 13.88; H, 1.60%. Found: C, 23.77; N, 13.90; H, 1.62%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1) for 1·Dy: 3444(br, s), 1608(s), 1517(s), 1415(vs), 1384(vs), 1305(m), 1245(w), 1186(w), 1110(w), 1060(m), 1014(w), 960(w), 927(w), 862(w), 782(s), 740(w), 694(w), 651(w), 509(s).

Synthesis of [Dy(bipy)2(NO3−)3] (2·Dy)

A mixture of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O (43.8 mg, 0.1 mmol), 2,3-quinoline dicarboxylic acid (21.8 mg, 0.1 mmol) and 2,2′-bipyridine (15.2 mg, 0.1 mmol) was dissolved in acetonitrile (CH3CN, 5 mL) and then stirred at room temperature for 20 min. After that it was transferred into a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 120 °C for 5 days. After slow cooling to room temperature, colorless block crystals of 2·Dy were obtained by filtration (yield 40% based on Dy). Elemental analysis for C20H16N7O9Dy (Mr: 660.90): C, 36.31; N, 14.82; H, 2.42%. Found: C, 36.32; N, 14.80; H, 2.44%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1) for 2·Dy: 3454(br, s), 3118(m), 2526(m), 1598(s), 1569(s), 1515(vs), 1469(vs), 1384(m), 1307(vs), 1172(m), 1016(m), 879(m), 771(vs), 738(s), 647(m).

Synthesis of [Dy2(bpdc)3(DMF)(H2O)]2·(DMF)10(H2O) (3·Dy)

A mixture of Dy(NO3)3·6H2O (21.9 mg, 0.05 mmol) and 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid (24.4 mg, 0.1 mmol) was dissolved in a mixture solution of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF, 7 mL), dimethyl sulphoxide (DMSO, 1 mL) and water (1 mL), then it was stirred at room temperature for 30 min. After that it was transferred into a 20 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 120 °C for 4 days. After slow cooling to room temperature, colorless rectangular crystals of 3·Dy were obtained by filtration (field 79% based on Dy). Elemental analysis for C108H128Dy4N24O41 (Mr: 3149.9): C, 41.14; N, 10.67; H, 4.06%. Found: C, 41.11; N, 10.65; H, 4.08%. IR (KBr pellet, cm−1) for 3·Dy: 3419(br, s), 2557(w), 2360(w), 1689(vs), 1594(vs), 1419(vs), 1309(s), 1124(m), 1022(s), 860(s), 779(vs), 667(w), 553(vs).

Synthesis of [Eu4(bpdc)6(CH3NH2)3(DMSO)(H2O)] (4·Eu)

The synthesis procedure for 4·Eu was the same as that for 3·Dy, expect that Dy(NO3)3·6H2O was replaced by Eu(NO3)3·6H2O. Colorless block crystals of 4·Eu suitable for X-ray analyses were collected by filtration (field 66% based on Eu). Though the synthesis factor is different from a previous paper, the cell parameters are same as the complex.30

Synthesis of Eu3+/Dy3+ bimetallic complexes (M-1–M-9)

A similar process was employed to prepare Eu3+/Dy3+ bimetallic complexes, by adding a certain amount of Eu(NO3)3·6H2O and Dy(NO3)3·6H2O. In a typical doping experiment, the total molar amounts of Eu3+ and Dy3+ were kept the same as that for 3·Dy, and the ratios of two different lanthanide cations equal to 1/9, 2/8, 3/7, 4/6, 5/5, 6/4, 7/3, 8/2, 9/1.

Result and discussion

Synthesis and characterization

Structural description for 1·Dy. According to the single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, it shows that 1·Dy belongs to triclinic space group P. The asymmetric unit of 1·Dy contains one crystallographically independent Dy(III) cation, three nitrate ions and one 2,2′-bipyridyl molecule. And two Dy(III) cations in the same coordination environment construct the binuclear structure. The center Dy(III) cation is coordinated by seven oxygen atoms from five nitrate ions and two nitrogen atoms from 2,2′-bipyridyl molecule, forming a nine coordination environment (See Fig. S1†). Both Dy–O (2.301 Å to 2.533 Å) and Dy–N (2.546 Å to 2.579 Å) are well-matched to other similar complexes reported.31 The nitrate ions display three kinds of modes to connect the Dy(III) cations. The first one (O1, O2, O3) bridges two metal ions by anti-anti-μ2-η1-η1 mode. The second one (O4, O5, O6) displays the bidentate–chelate mode to connect the metal ions. And another one (O7, O8, O9) connects two center metal ions with a μ2-bridging + chelating mode. The 2,2′-bipyridyl connect metal ions by a bidentate–chelate mode. The detailed data of the bond length and bond angel are listed in Table S2.†

In the structure of 1·Dy, the hydrogen bonding plays an important role to connect the binuclear into three-dimensional structure.32–34 There are three kinds of hydrogen bonds, forming three one-dimensional chains along the b, c, a/c axis, respectively (Fig. 1). As shown in Fig. 1b, in the plane A of b/c, the C8–H8A⋯O8 hydrogen bonds generates a chain along c axis, and further forms a two-dimensional nets by the C4–H4A⋯O4 hydrogen bonds along the vertical line, which the distances of H8A⋯O8 and H4A⋯O4 are 2.511 and 2.499 Å, respectively. There is another hydrogen bond C2–H2A⋯O6 along c axis, and linked through C4–H4A⋯O4 to from the other plane B with angle 173.301° between plane A. The distance of H2A⋯O6 is 2.479 Å (Fig. 1c). And according to the π–π stacking interaction, the complex shows 1D chain alone c axis (Fig. 1e). The final supramolecular architecture can be reduced to three dimensional 6-connected pcu topology structure under the circumstance of the binuclear unit viewed as the node and the hydrogen bondings C–H⋯O as linkers.

Structural description for 2·Dy. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 2·Dy crystallizes in orthorhombic space group Pbcn. In the asymmetric unit of 2·Dy, there are one crystallographically independent Dy(III) cation, three nitrate ions and two 2,2′-bipyridyl molecules. The center Dy(III) cation is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from three nitrate ions and four nitrogen atoms from two 2,2′-bipyridyl molecules, forming a ten coordination environment (Fig. 1a and b). And all of the five molecules are in the bidentate–chelate mode to connect the center metal. Both Dy–O (2.461 Å to 2.557 Å) and Dy–N (2.516 Å to 2.525 Å) are well-matched to other similar complexes reported.35

Similar to 1·Dy, hydrogen bonding connects the polymers in two ways. One of the hydrogen bonding C2–H2A⋯O1 connects the binuclear into two-dimensional net structure in the a/b plane, and the distance between H2A and O1 is 2.448 Å. And the other one is C9–H9A⋯O3 whose length is 2.492 Å, it also constructs a two-dimensional net structure vertical with the plane before. Therefore, the two vertical planes form the three-dimensional structure. The lengths and bond angles of hydrogen bongding in 1·Dy and 2·Dy are shown in Table 2. With the influence of the π–π stacking interaction, the complex can be constructed to 2D net shown in Fig. 2f.36 The supramolecular architecture can be reduced to 8-connected bcu topology structure under the circumstance of the monomeric unit viewed as the node and the hydrogen bonding C–H⋯O as linkers (Fig. 2g).

Structural description for 3·Dy. The conjugate system of azabicyclo ligands can sensitize the luminescence property of lanthanide cations as “antenna”. And it is rare condition for dysprosium to coordinate with nitrogen ligand in 1·Dy and 2·Dy. Lanthanide atoms have the characters that they are more tending to coordinate with oxygen atoms. In this consideration, we decide to “assemble” carboxyl group on the dipyridyl ligand.

According to the single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, it shows that 3·Dy belongs to triclinic space group P. The asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically independent Dy(III) cation, three 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid molecules, four coordinated H2O, two coordinated DMF molecules, eight free H2O, and ten free DMF molecules (Fig. 4a and S2†). Two center Dy(III) are in different coordinated environments. Type one of the Dy(III) is seven-coordinated formed by six oxygen atoms from six carboxylates (Dy–O distances, 2.243–2.362 Å), one oxygen atom from DMF molecule. Each bpdc ligand acts as a tetradentate bridging ligand, and its two carboxylic groups display a μ2-bridging mode coordinated to four Dy(III) atoms forming a chain along b axis. The neighbouring DyI cations are connected by bpdc ligand to form a [Dy2O14(DMF)2] binuclear unit. The other Dy(III) center is eight-coordinated formed by six oxygen atoms from six carboxylates (Dy–O distances, 2.304–2395 Å), two oxygen atoms from H2O molecules. And similar to DyI, type II cations form a [Dy2O12(H2O)2] binuclear unit. Same centers are connected by two bpdc ligands forming the zigzag chains. And two Dy(III) cations in different coordination environment construct the nuclear structure, respectively (Fig. 3). Both of the two type Dy(III) cations are connected by bpdc forming binuclear 1D chain. Furthermore, alternate permutation of the different binuclear to form a 3D framework consisting of elongated rhombus-shaped channels with diagonal distances of ca. 12.79 × 27.23 Å2 (Fig. 4d). As mentioned above, the water and DMF molecules don't have any effect for improving dimensionality. The Dy⋯Dy distances via bpdc spacers amount to 4.378–5.440 Å. The large channels are filled with guest DMF and H2O molecules. Upon removal of these guest molecules, 3·Dy has 46.1% (1451.3 Å3) solvent-accessible voids as calculated by PLATON, out of the total unit cell volume of 3149.9 Å3. The supramolecular architecture can be reduced to (4,4,6)-connected topology structure with a point symbol of {42·84}{44·62}2{49·66}2 under the circumstance (Fig. S3†).

The IR spectra of free ligands and complex 1·Dy, 2·Dy and 3·Dy are shown in Fig. S4.† There is a broad band at 3300–3600 cm−1, which should be ascribed to the stretching vibrations of O–H, suggesting the presence of coordinated water and solvent water molecules. In the IR spectra of 3·Dy, the peaks of 1696 and 1598 cm−1 which belong to the ligand anti-symmetric νas (CO) and symmetric νas (CO) vibration modes, have split into two peaks (1628, 1555 and 1394, 1351 cm−1, respectively), because of the coordination to the Ln(III) cations.37


Thermal analysis. In order to study the stability of the supramolecular architectures, the thermal gravimetric experiments of 3·Dy have been investigated. As shown in Fig. S5,† 3·Dy show tree continuous weight loss steps. The TGA curve of 3·Dy keep stable up to 195 °C, following the first loss of 29.25% in the range of 195–280 °C, shows the loss of ten DMF solvent molecules (calcd: 28.49%). During the temperature of 355–400 °C, the loss of 7.01% means the loss of two coordinated DMF and four H2O solvent molecules (calcd: 7.11%). After about 510 °C, ligands of 3·Dy continue to decompose. These complexes finally transformed to the final residual of the Dy2O3 phase (calcd: 24.32%).

Energy transfer process

Mostly, the direct excitation is very inefficient because the optical transitions within the 4f subshells of lanthanide ions are parity forbidden. The indirect excitation by energy transfer from an organic antenna chromophore not only circumvents this excitation problem, but also allows excitation at wavelengths where the lanthanide ion does not display a significant absorption. In order to make energy transfer effective, the energy level match between the triplet states of the ligands and the ground state of Ln(III) becomes one of the most important factors dominating the luminescence properties of lanthanide complexes. To elucidate the energy transfer processes in lanthanide complexes, the energy levels of the relevant electronic states should be estimated. The data of the triplet excited state T1 of Bpdc (20618.6 cm−1) was calculated by the low-temperature (77 K) phosphorescence spectrum of the Gd(III) complex. The singlet energy level (S1) of Bpdc ligand was 27855.2 cm−1 (443 nm), which was calculated from the UV-vis absorbance edges (Fig. S9†). As it is known that the intersystem crossing (ISC) process becomes effective when ΔE(S1 − T1) of the ligand is over at least 5000 cm−1, the energy gap between S1 and T1 levels being 7236.6 cm−1 for Bpdc. Therefore, the ISC process will run smoothly.

The energy differences between the lowest triplet state of ligands and resonant energy levels of Eu3+ (5D0, 17500 cm−1), and Dy3+ (4F9/2, 21000 cm−1) are shown in Scheme S1.† As it is known that an optical ligand-to-metal energy transfer process for Ln(III) needs 2500–4000 cm−1 for Eu(III) and 2500–4500 cm−1 for Dy(III). Therefore, the transitions from triplet level of Bpdc ligand to Eu(III) is efficient but inefficient to Dy(III). According to the analysis above Bpdc ligands could participate in the energy transfer process in complexes 4·Eu, which makes the ligand-to-metal energy transfer process efficiently.

Photoluminescence property

The luminescence property of bipy ligand and the complex 1·Dy and 2·Dy was studied detailed by solid state and the performance in different solvents (Fig. S6 and S7†). As shown in Fig. 5, we explored the luminescence property of the complexes in solid state. The excitation spectra of 1·Dy and 2·Dy were obtained by monitoring the emission of the 4F9/2 → 6H13/2 transition at 573 nm and displayed a prominent broad band with peaks at 321 nm and 330 nm for the electronic transitions of the coordinated bipy ligand (Fig. S9 and S10†). The typical peaks of 1·Dy and 2·Dy can be found at 482, 573, 616, 683 and 751 nm which can be contributed to 4F9/2 → 6H15/2, 4F9/2 → 6H13/2, 4F15/2 → 6H11/2, 4F15/2 → 6H9/2, 4F9/2 → 6H9/2, respectively. But the emission intensity of the 2·Dy is much stronger than 1·Dy. The stronger luminescent intensity and higher luminescent lifetimes of 2·Dy than that of 1·Dy may due to the crystal structures and coordinated environment of the ligands. The bond lengths of Dy–N are 2.579 and 2.546 Å for 1·Dy, and 2.516, 2.525 Å for 2·Dy a little bit shorter than 1·Dy. The angle between two ligands of 2,2′-dipyridine in 1·Dy is 0.000° (184). Though there is a higher coplanarity, two ligands of 2,2′-dipyridine are coordinated on the metal ions in 2·Dy, which has more influence on the emission spectrum means more energy transfers from ligand to the Dy ions. Furthermore, the π–π stacking interaction influences the property, simultaneously. It constructs a higher dimension structure in 2·Dy, which improve the stability of the architecture. The luminescence lifetimes of complex 2·Dy (36.64 μs at 298 K, 69.15 μs at 77 K) is also longer than 1·Dy (16.26 μs at 298 K, 21.07 μs at 77 K), shown in Fig. S7.†

The solid-state photoluminescence spectra of Bpdc ligand, 3·Dy and 4·Eu were investigated at room temperature (Fig. 6). The emission spectra of ligand bpdc displays the wide bands centered around 473 nm and 553 nm, due to the intra-ligand π → π* transition. The band of 473 nm also displays in the emission spectra of 3·Dy upon excitation at 370 nm, which shows sharp peaks at 575 nm and 617 nm simultaneously. The unique peaks of 3·Dy at 575 nm and 617 nm can be corresponding to the 4F9/2 → 6H13/2 and 4F15/2 → 6H11/2, respectively. As for 4·Eu, several sharp peaks appear at 578, 591, 617 and 650 nm in the emission spectra upon excitation at 369 nm, corresponding to the 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–3) transitions, respectively. The appearance of the unique peaks proves the “antenna effect” of the ligand. According to the 1931 CIE chromaticity diagram, 3·Dy and 4·Eu fall within the green and red region, respectively. It is worth mentioning that the band of the ligand around 473 nm keeps the shape though it has been coordinated to the Ln(III) cations, which gives the possibility of designing white emission by controlling the ratio of the three kinds of colors. Notably, the 4F9/2 → 6H13/2 transition is much more intense than the 4F9/2 → 6H15/2 transition, which reflects a low symmetry of the Dy(III) site and the so-called hypersensitive transition responsible for the red-green emission of 3·Dy and 4·Eu. Compound 3·Dy and 4·Eu can emit white light because of the colors green and red from Dy and Eu cations, and color blue from bpdy ligand. So it may act as white light luminescent material.38–40 In this consideration, a series of functional luminescent MOF materials have been successfully obtained by doping isostructural Eu3+ to Dy3+ complexes to tune the emission colors by adjusting the Eu3+ concentration. Energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) analysis of the bimetallic complexes confirmed the presence of europium and terbium. The EDX spectra also revealed that the ratios of Eu3+ and Dy3+ are 0.11, 0.24, 0.42, 0.65, 0.99, 1.49, 2.32, 3.98 and 8.94, which agrees well with the original ratio (1/9, 2/8, 3/7, 4/6, 5/5, 6/4, 7/3, 8/2, 9/1) in the precursor solution.

A key step to generate linearly dependent multicolor photoluminescence by the mixed-Ln-MOFs is to incorporate different Ln atoms into the same coordination framework in the desired proportions with the uniform structure integrity and statistic space distribution. Since Eu3+, Dy3+ have resembling ionic radii (1.066, 1.031 Å with CN = 7–8), and ligand Bpdc is semiflexible to be able to tolerate slight coordination bond variation, formation of both monometallic and heterometallic Ln-MOFs in isomorphous structures is expectable.14

The series of sharp peaks characteristic of red-Eu and green-Dy metal-centered (MC) luminescence appear, corresponding to 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–3) transitions for Eu3+ and 4F9/2 → 6H13/2, 4F15/2 → 6H11/2 for Dy3+, respectively. In order to explain the process of energy transport, the luminescence lifetime of the two coordinated complexes are investigated. The lifetime of Eu3+ is 297.26 μs at 615 nm. As for 3·Dy, the lifetime at 575 nm is 8.76 μs. In both cases, effective ET from ligand adsorption to f-levels of Eu3+ and Dy3+, confirming competent antenna effect of bpdc. At the same time, the lifetime of broad band at 473 nm has been investigated, verified by the short lifetime (τ = 8.48 ns), similar to that of free ligand. The three primary RGB colors emitted by Dy3+, Eu3+, and ligand, make it possible to produce multicolored photoluminescence by integrating different ratios to build bimetallic complexes.

It is the method to obtain bimetallic complexes that by integrating Eu3+ ions into the single-crystal of Ln-MOF. As shown in Fig. 7, the Eu/Dy series display sharp characteristic emission peaks from both Dy3+ and Eu3+ centers, this appearance indicates that the ligand bpdc embedded in Eu/Dy-mixed Ln-MOFs is able to sensitize these two Ln3+ centers simultaneously. By increasing the amount of Eu3+ while the by increasing the amount of Eu3+ while proportionally decreasing the amount of Dy3+ in mixed-Eu/Dy-MOF crystals, the red-emission intensity was increased, while that of green-emission decreased accordingly. And the lifetime of different ratios materials has been investigated simultaneously. As shown in Table 3, in these mixed-Dy/Eu crystals, the lifetimes increase of Eu3+ and decrease of Dy3+ are observed comparing to the pure Dy/Eu complexes. This strongly indicates that the crossing energy transport from Dy3+ to Eu3+ occur here. At the same time, the emission spectra of the doped materials with different rates were investigated at room temperature under different excitation wavelength between 315 and 385 nm per 10 nm. By increase of Dy and decrease of Eu, the color turns from red to orange to green, shown in Fig. 7a, and because of the appearance of the CT between different metal ions, the emission peaks are influenced by the excitation wavelength. Conclusion

In summary, by designing a multifunctional ligand suitable for photosensitization of multicomponent phosphor as well as blue self-emission, we succeeded in synthesis of a novel tunable luminescent material base on 2,2′-bipyridine-5,5′-dicarboxylic acid. The bimetallic complexes can emit multicolored photoluminescence and achieve linear color turnability. On the basis of the isostructural monometallic complexes of Eu3+ and Dy3+, which allows for dichromatic fine-tuning of RG colors. In addition, the luminescent emission colors can be easily tuned within the excitation wavelength and the ratios of the lanthanide emitters. In this way, we can fine-tune the emission colors in a linear fashion. Such bimetallic complexes can be easily tuned to white-emission by adjusting the ratios between Dy3+ and Eu3+, and the wavelength of UV-excitation. The generality and synthesis of both MOFs and bimetallic complexes provide a novel strategy to tune the emission color by Dy3+, and the materials are potential candidates for applications in the field of color displays, luminescence sensors, and structural probes.
