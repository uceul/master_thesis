Abstract

Two cadmium(II) coordination polymers, namely [Cd3(bpt)2(DMA)2]n (1) and [Cd2(bpt)(btz)(DMF)]n (2) (H3bpt = biphenyl‐3,4′,5‐tricarboxylic acid, Hbtz = 1H‐benzotriazole, DMA = N,N‐dimethylacetamide; DMF = N,N‐dimethylformamide), were solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction. Compound 1 displays a 3D framework based on trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} subunits and can be simplified into a (4,8)‐connected topological network by viewing bpt3– ligands and trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} units as 4‐, 8‐connected nodes, respectively. Compound 2 also displays a 3D framework but based on 1D chain subunits controlled by carboxylate groups and btz– ligands. In addition, the thermal stabilities and luminescent properties of compounds 1 and 2 were also investigated.

1 Introduction

During the past few decades, metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) have experienced an explosive growth not only because of their charming topological architectures but also owing to their potential applications in luminescence, gas storage and separation, magnetism and catalysis.1–5 However, controllable assembly of MOFs with unique structural features and certain functions remains a fundamental scientific challenge to chemists and material scientists.6,7 Therefore, for the self‐assembly of MOFs, selection of an appropriate organic ligand and synthetic strategies are key factors for achieving expected compounds.8–10 Among various organic ligands, polycarboxylate ligands, such as terephthalic acid, isophthalic acid, 1,3,5‐benzenetricarboxylic acid, and 1,2,4,5‐benzenetetracarboxylic acid have been widely used to construct functional coordination polymers owing to their variable coordination modes and strong coordination abilities to transition metal ions.11–14 On the other hand, the building‐block method has been demonstrated as an effective approach towards the synthesis of MOFs with predictable structures.15–17

Stimulated by the above considerations, in this work the unsymmetrical tricarboxylate ligand biphenyl‐3,4′,5‐tricarboxylic acid, which may exploit its carboxylate groups to bridging or chelating metal ions into various secondary building subunits (such as dinuclear, trinuclear, or tetranuclear clusters, and rod‐shaped chains), was selected to obtain two CdII compounds, namely [Cd3(bpt)2(DMA)2]n (1) and [Cd2(bpt)(btz)(DMF)]n (2) (H3bpt = biphenyl‐3,4′,5‐tricarboxylic acid, Hbtz = 1H‐benzotriazole, DMA = N,N‐dimethylacetamide, DMF = N,N‐dimethylformamide). Compound 1 features a 3D (4, 8) connected framework based on trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} subunits, whereas compound 2 also features a 3D framework based on 1D chain subunits controlled by carboxylate groups and btz– ligands. Their synthesis, crystal structures, thermal stabilities, and luminescent properties are reported.

2. Experimental Section

2.1. Materials and Methods

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. Elemental analysis was carried out with a EA1110 CHNS–0 CE elemental analyzer. Infrared (FT‐IR) spectra were recorded with a Bruker Equinox 55 FT‐IR spectrometer as dry KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm–1 range. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses were recorded with a Rigaku Dmax2500 diffractometer with Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) with a scan speed of 5°·min–1. TG data were collected at a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 with a NETSCHZ STA‐449C thermal analyzer in the temperature range 30–800 °C in a nitrogen atmosphere. Solid state fluorescence spectra were recorded with a Hitachi F‐4600 spectrometer equipped with a xenon lamp and a quartz carrier at room temperature.

2.2 Syntheses of Compounds

2.2.1 [Cd3(bpt)2(DMA)2]n (1)

A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.030 g, 0.10 mmol), H3bpt (0.014 g, 0.05 mmol), and DMA (3 mL) was sealed into a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 120 °C for 4 d. After being slowly cooled to room temperature, colorless block crystals suitable for single crystal X‐ray diffraction were obtained by filtration. Yield: 43 % (based on H3bpt). C38H32Cd3N2O14 (1077.89): calcd. C 42.30; H 2.97; N 2.60 %; found: C 42.34; H 3.02; N 2.55 %. IR (KBr): = 3435 (m), 2421 (w), 1598 (s), 1487 (s), 1342 (s), 1297 (s), 1203 (m), 1135 (w), 996 (w), 920 (s), 878 (s), 814 (m), 782 (w), 716 (w), 675 (w) cm–1.

2.2.2 [Cd2(bpt)(btz)(DMF)]n (2)

A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.030 g, 0.10 mmol), H3bpt (0.014 g, 0.05 mmol), Hbtz (0.05 mmol), and DMF (5 mL) was sealed into a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel vessel and heated at 120 °C for 4 d. After being slowly cooled to room temperature, colorless block crystals suitable for single crystal X‐ray diffraction were obtained by filtration. Yield: 33 % (based on H3bpt). C24H18Cd2N4O7 (699.24): calcd. C 41.19; H 2.57; N 8.01 %; found: C 41.21; H 2.56; N 8.05 %. IR (KBr): = 3421 (m), 2412 (w), 1548 (s), 1487 (s), 1341 (s), 1296 (s), 1213 (m), 1134 (w), 987 (w), 912 (s), 878 (s), 824 (m), 765 (w), 726 (w) cm–1.

2.3 X‐ray crystallography

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analyses for compounds 1 and 2 were carried out with a computer‐controlled Oxford Xcalibur E diffractometer with graphite‐monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at T = 293(2) K. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares methods on F2 using the SHELXL‐97 program package.18 All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms bound to carbon atoms were generated by geometrical calculations and their positions and thermal parameters were fixed during the structure refinement. The crystallographic data and experimental details of structural analyses for compounds 1 and 2 are listed in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and bond angle parameters are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).

3 Result and Discussion

3.1 Description of Crystal Structures

3.1.1 [Cd3(bpt)2(DMA)2]n (1)

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in monoclinic space group C2/c with the asymmetric unit containing one and a half CdII ions, one bpt3– ligand, and one coordinated DMA molecule. As shown in Figure 1a, Cd1 is five‐coordinate with a slightly distorted square pyramidal arrangement, which is defined by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different bpt3– ligands. Cd2 has a octahedrally coordination environment with the equatorial plane occupied by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different bpt3– ligands and apical positions occupied by two oxygen atoms from two coordinated DMA molecules. The Cd–O distances range from 2.176(2) to 2.325(3) Å, which are comparable with previously reported CdII compounds.19 The Cd2 ion resides on a inversion center, so two symmetry‐related Cd1 ions and one Cd2 ion are bridged together by four carboxylate groups to form a trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} subunit with Cd···Cd separation of 3.814(3) Å (Figure 1b). These trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} subunits are further extended into a 3D framework via the connection of bpt3– ligands (Figure 1c). In 1, the bpt3– ligand links six different CdII ions in μ6‐(η2‐O,O′),O′,O′′,O′′′,O′′′′,O′′′′′ mode (Scheme 1a). To better understand this complicated framework of 1, topological analysis method was employed to simplify this framework. If the bpt3– ligands and the trinuclear {Cd3(COO)4} subunits are reduced into 4‐, 8‐connected nodes, respectively, compound 1 can be simplified into a binodal (4, 8)‐connected topological network with the point symbol of {44.62}2{48.617.83} (Figure 1d).

3.1.2 [Cd2(bpt)(btz)(DMF)]n (2)

The single crystal X‐ray analysis reveals that compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c featuring a complicated 3D framework. The asymmetric unit of 2 has two crystallographically independent CdII ions, one bpt3– ligand, one btz– ligand, and one coordinated DMF molecule. As shown in Figure 2a, both Cd1 and Cd2 adopt slightly distorted octahedral coordination arrangement. For Cd1, it is coordinated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different bpt3– ligands and one nitrogen atom from btz– ligand. For Cd2, it is coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from two different bpt3– ligands, one oxygen atom from DMF molecule, and two nitrogen atoms from two different btz– ligands. The Cd–O and Cd–N distances are in the range of 2.195(2)–2.549(2) Å, 2.218(3)–2.330(3) Å, respectively, which fall in the accepted range according to previously reported CdII compounds.19 In 2, the bpt3– ligand bridges six different CdII ions in μ6‐(η2‐O,O′),O,(η2‐O′′,O′′′),O′′′,O′′′′,O′′′′′ mode (Scheme 1b), and the btz– ligand bridges three different CdII ions using its three nitrogen donors. Under the synergetic effect of btz– ligands and carboxylate groups of bpt3– ligands, CdII ions are linked into 1D chain subunits (Figure 1b). Finally, these 1D chains are connected together via the bpt3– ligands, giving rise to the 3D framework of 2 (Figure 2c).

3.2 PXRD and Thermal Analysis

To prove the purity of compounds 1 and 2, the synthesized bulk powder samples of the two compounds were characterized by powder X‐ray diffraction (RXPD). As shown in Figure S1 (Supporting Information), all the diffraction peaks of compounds 1 and 2 in the measured patterns matched well with the simulated patterns based on the single crystal diffraction data, indicating the phase purity of the as‐synthesized samples.

To investigate the thermal behaviors of 1 and 2, thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) were performed on polycrystalline samples of 1 and 2 in the temperature range of 30–800 °C in a nitrogen atmosphere (Figure 3). For 1, it displays two main steps of weight loss: the first step with the weight loss of 15.96 % occurs in the temperature range of 120–200 °C, which can be assigned to the departure of two coordinated DMA molecules per unit cell (calcd.16.14 %). The second step occurs from 310 to 615 °C corresponding to the decomposition of the coordination framework with the CdO as the final products (obsd. 35.53 %, calcd. 35.74 %). For 2, it also shows two main step weight losses in the TGA curve. The first weight loss of 10.00 % in the temperature range 110–160 °C can be assigned to the release of one coordinated DMF molecule per unit cell (calcd. 10.30 %). The framework collapsed in the temperature range of 305–543 °C. The final weight is attributed to the final CdO powder (obsd. 36.32 %, calcd. 36.73 %).

3.3 Luminescent Properties

Considering that metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) based on d10 transition metal ions and organic ligands are potential photoactive materials in the fields of chemical sensors, photochemistry, and electroluminescent display.20,21 Hence, the solid‐state luminescent properties of compounds 1 and 2 and free H3bpt ligand were investigated at room temperature. As shown in Figure 4, compound 1 shows a luminescent emission band maximum at 420 nm (λex = 322 nm), compound 2 displays the emission maxima at 437 nm when excited at 327 nm, and the H3bpt ligand shows an emission band at 410 nm (λex = 325 nm). According to the literature, the Hbtz ligand shows an emission band at 350 nm (λex = 300 nm).22,23 22–23. The emission spectra of compounds 1 and 2 are similar with that of the free H3bpt ligand. Therefore, the luminescence of compounds 1 and 2 can be tentatively assigned to intraligand charge transfer due to their similarity to the free H3bpt ligand. The red shifts (10 nm for 1 and 27 nm for 2) relative to the free H3bpt ligand may be attributed to the coordination of bpt3– ligand to the central metal atoms, which can effectively increase the rigidity of the organic ligand and reduce the non‐radiative decay of energy loss.24 Notably, the bpt3– ligands in these two compounds feature different coordination modes and dihedral angles between the two phenyl rings, which may cause the differences of fluorescence intensities and maximum emission peak positions between compounds 1 and 2.

4 Conclusions

Two cadmium(II) compounds were obtained by solvothermal reactions of CdII ions and H3bpt ligand or H3bpt and Hbtz ligands. Compound 1 displays a 3D framework with (4,8)‐connected topology, whereas compound 2 displays a 3D framework based on mixed bpt3– and btz– ligands. The bpt3– ligands in these two compounds display distinct coordination modes. Moreover, compounds 1 and 2 show strong ligand‐centered luminescence.
