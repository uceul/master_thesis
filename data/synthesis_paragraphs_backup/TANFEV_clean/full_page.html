Controllable self-assembly of four new metal–organic frameworks based on different phosphomolybdate clusters by altering the molar ratio of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4†

Jing-Xin Meng , Ying Lu *, Yang-Guang Li , Hai Fu and En-Bo Wang *
Key Laboratory of Polyoxometalate Science of Ministry of Education, Department of Chemistry, Northeast Normal University, Ren Min Street No. 5268, Changchun, Jilin 130024, P. R. China. E-mail: wangeb889@nenu.edu.cn; wangenbo@public.cc.jl.cn

Received 13th September 2010 , Accepted 21st December 2010

First published on 3rd February 2011

By adjusting the molar ratio of the reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4, four new metal–organic frameworks, namely, CoH(bix)4[PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2] (1), (H2bix)2(NaHP2Mo5O23)·2H2O (2), H2(bix)4[Cd(H2O)4][Cd(HPO4)4(H2PO4)4(MoO2)12 (OH)6]·10H2O (3) and (H2en)3(Co3P4Mo4O28) (4) (bix = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, en = 1,2-ethylenediamine), have been hydrothermally prepared and characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectroscopy, TG analyses, and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 consists of straight chains based on pseudo-Keggin [PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2]3−polyanions and [CoH(bix)4]3+ complex fragments, which are further connected into a three-dimensional (3D) open framework by hydrogen bonding interactions between polyanions and organic bix ligands . Compound 2 shows 3D supramolecular networks constructed from weak interactions between free biprotonated bix, water and oxygen atoms of polyanions [P2Mo5O23]6−. In compound 3, [Cd(HPO4)4(H2PO4)4(MoO2)12(OH)6]4−polyanions are linked by [Cd(H2O)4]2+ cations to build one-dimensional (1D) chains. Then weak interactions between free bix ligands, water and oxygen atoms of the polyanions extend these 1D chains to 3D supramolecular frameworks. Compound 4 exhibits open-framework structures. Interestingly, it also shows 1D left and right-handed helical chains and 2D (6,3)-layers. The molar ratio of the reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 influence the transformation of the phosphomolybdate clusters in compounds 1–4. The electrochemistry and photocatalysis properties of these compounds have also been investigated in this paper.

Introduction

Recently, much attention has been paid to the formation of novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), not only due to their intriguing topological structures1 but also their potential applications in medicine assays, biological analysis, as well as gas storage, ion exchange and catalysis.2 In view of their multifarious topologies and properties, it is often a promising strategy to introduce functional components into MOFs. On the other hand, polyoxometalates (POMs),3 as a unique class of metal–oxide clusters with controllable shape and size, highly negative charges, and oxo-enriched surfaces, represent an outstanding secondary building unit (SBU) for the design and synthesis of new functional MOFs. The efficacious combination of the “structures” of MOFs and the “functions” of POMs is propitious to the design and assembly of new POM-based MOFs with fantastic chemical and physical properties.

During the preparation of novel POM-based MOFs, four main considerations should be taken into account. The first one is to obtain the desired structures and properties of the final products by adjusting the reaction conditions such as reaction time, temperature, pH value and structure directing agents, which have been thoroughly discussed by Long4 and Peng.5 The second one is to choose suitable metal linkers, which should be easily ligated, and in various coordination modes. These properties are beneficial for them to link the POM SBUs and organic ligands into multifunctional POM-based MOFs, and meanwhile construct specific topological structures.6 The third one is to select a suitable organic ligand. Generally, ligands possessing both flexible and rigid parts are the first choice to synthesize supramolecular frameworks for the following two reasons: (1) the flexible parts could tolerate a large structural twist and acquire a desirable coordination environment for metal ions; (2) the rigid parts could increase the weak interaction between MOFs and POM clusters, making the generation of the supramolecular frameworks more feasible. Therefore, the remarkable bix (bix = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene), bearing both flexible {–(CH2)–} parts and rigid {phenyl ring} parts, greatly attracts chemists' attention. For example, we have obtained six octamolybdate-based metal–organic frameworks decorated by the bix ligand such as [CuI(bix)][(CuIbix)(δ-Mo8O26)0.5], H(CuIbix)[(CuIbix)2(β-Mo8O26)]·2H2O, and so on.7 The last consideration is to choose controllable and multivariate POM cluster units, which allow the construction of diverse supramolecular isomers and polymorphs.8 Phosphomolybdates are widely used inorganic building blocks. Under controllable hydrothermal conditions, they could easily be prepared by mixing H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 to assemble different structural units such as {P2Mo5O23}, {P4Mo6O31}, and {PMo12O40}.9 Therefore, plenty of materials based on these structural units have been obtained and investigated by Zubieta and cowokers.10 However, little attention has been paid to the influence of the POM units on the final products by altering the molar ratio of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4.

On the basis of the aforementioned four points, herein we adopted metal linkers, the bix ligand and phosphomolybdate clusters by altering molar ratios of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 to assemble successfully four new MOFs, namely, CoH(bix)4[PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2] (1), (H2bix)2(NaHP2Mo5O23)·2H2O (2), H2(bix)4[Cd(H2O)4][Cd(HPO4)4(H2PO4)4(MoO2)12(OH)6]·10H2O (3) and (H2en)3(Co3P4Mo4O28) (4) (en = 1,2-ethylenediamine) (as shown in Scheme 1). To the best of our knowledge, compound 1 consists of straight chains based on pseudo-Keggin [PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2]3−polyanions and [CoH(bix)4]3+ complex fragments, which are further connected into a three-dimensional (3D) open framework by hydrogen bonding interactions between polyanions and organic bix ligands . Compound 2 shows 3D supramolecular networks constructed from weak interactions between free biprotonated bix, water and the oxygen atoms of polyanions [P2Mo5O23]6−. In compound 3, [Cd(HPO4)4(H2PO4)4(MoO2)12(OH)6]4−polyanions are linked by [Cd(H2O)4]2+ cations to build one-dimensional (1D) chains. Then weak interactions between free bix ligands, water and oxygen atoms of the polyanions extend these 1D chains to 3D supramolecular frameworks. Compound 4 exhibits open-framework structures. Interestingly, it also shows 1D left and right-handed helical chains and 2D (6,3)-layers. Interestingly, the molar ratio of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 influences the transformation of the phosphomolybdate clusters in compounds 1–4. The electrochemistry and photocatalysis properties of these compounds have also been investigated in this paper.

Experiment

Materials and methods

All chemicals were commercially purchased and used without further purification. The ligand 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene (bix) was prepared by the method reported in the literature.11 The C, H and N elemental analyses were conducted on a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer, and Mo, V, P, Co, Na and Cd were determined with a Leaman inductively coupled plasma (ICP) spectrometer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on an Alpha Centaurt FT/IR spectrometer. UV-vis absorption spectra were recorded on a 756 CRT UV-vis spectrophotometer. The XRPD patterns were recorded on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer with Cu KR (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation. TG analyses were performed on a Perkin-Elmer TGA7 instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. A CHI 660 Electrochemical Workstation connected to a Digital-586 personal computer was used to control the electrochemical measurements and for data collection. A conventional three-electrode system was used. The working electrodes were 1 and 2 bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (1–2CPE). A SCE was used as the reference electrode and Pt gauze as a counter electrode. The working electrode was prepared by following our earlier method.12
Synthesis of CoH(bix)4[PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2] (1). A mixture of Na2MoO4·2H2O (0.121 g, 0.5 mmol), H3PO4 (0.07 mL, 1 mmol), NaVO3·2H2O (0.079 g, 0.5 mmol), CoCl2·6H2O (0.119 g, 0.5 mmol), bix (0.119 g, 0.5 mmol), and H2O (6 mL) was stirred for 0.5 h in air. The pH was then adjusted to 4.0 using dilute en solution and 4 M HCl. The mixture was transferred to a Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and kept at 160 °C for 5 d. After the sample had slowly cooled to room temperature, black crystals were filtered off, washed with distilled water and dried in a desiccator at room temperature to give a yield of 40.5% based on Mo. X-Ray crystallography

Suitable single crystals were mounted on a glass fiber and the crystallographic data for 1–4 were collected at 150(2) K on a Rigaku R-axis Rapid IP diffractometer using graphite monochromatic Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) and IP technique. The structures of them were solved by the direct method and refined by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2 using the SHELXTL-97 crystallographic software package.13 Anisotropic thermal parameters were used to refine all non-hydrogen atoms. The H atoms on the C atoms were included in calculated positions. The H atoms on water molecules cannot be found from the weak residual electron peaks but were directly included in the final molecular formula. Results and discussion

In compounds 1–4, the oxidation states of V and Mo were confirmed by charge neutrality, coordination environments, and valence sum calculations14 which have been shown in Table S6.† The detailed conformations of bix ligands in these compounds have been exhibited in Fig. S1.† The table of hydrogen bonds for compounds 1–3 has been shown in Table S5.†15

Structure description of compound 1

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that compound 1 is made up of a polyanion [PMoVI8VV4O40(VIVO)2]3− (abbreviated as PMo8V6), one crystallographically independent Co (Co1) atom and one kind of bix (bix1) ligand (Fig. S1a†) in the structural unit of compound 1 (Fig. 1a).
Fig. 1 (a) ORTEP drawing of the coordination environment of the CoII centers in compound 1 with thermal ellipsoids at 50% probability (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity). #1 −0.25 + y, 1.25 − x, 1.25 − z #2 1 − x, 1.5 − y, z #3 1.25 − y, 0.25 + x, 1.25 − z(b) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 1D chain structure in 1. (c) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 3D supramolecular framework.

In the pseudo-Keggin cluster PMo8V6, four V atoms replace the Mo atoms in the famous α-Keggin structure PMo12O403− with two capped five-coordinating terminal {VO} units. Here, all the vanadium atoms show distorted {VO5} square pyramidal geometries, and the molybdenum atoms exhibit distorted {MoO6} octahedral environments. The central P1 atom lies at a site with crystallographically imposed 4-symmetry, which exhibits a tetrahedral environment surrounded by four symmetry-related unit-occupancy O10 atoms. Each Co1 atom also lies at a site with crystallographically imposed 4-symmetry, which shows a distorted octahedral environment {CoN4O2} coordinated by four imidazolyl N1 atoms from four symmetry-related unit-occupancy bix molecules (Co1–N1 = 2.125(4) Å), and two crystallographically imposed 2-symmetry O2 atoms from two PMo8V6 clusters with the distances of Co1–O2 = 2.122(4) Å. Furthermore, the crystallographically imposed 2-symmetry V2 atom from the PMo8V6 clusters is linked to the Co1 atom by the O2 atom with a distance of V2–O2 = 1.628(4) Å. Interestingly, each bix1 ligand (Fig. S1a†) adopts a “Z-type” trans-conformation to link the Co1 atom with one terminal N atom leading to the propeller-shaped structures of the [CoH(bix)4]3+ complex fragments (see Fig. S2†). Then these fragments are further connected by the terminal oxygen of the PMo8V6 clusters with Co–O bonds to construct a 1D straight chain (Fig. 1b). With the aid of hydrogen bonds (C–O) from 3.276(5) to 3.295(5) Å (Table S5†), these 1D chains extend to 3D supramolecular frameworks (Fig. 1c).

Structure description of compound 2

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that there is one [P2Mo5O23]2− anion (denoted P2Mo5), one crystallographically unique Na atom and three kinds of bix (bix1, bix2 and bix3) ligands (Fig. S1b†) in the fundamental unit of compound 2 (Fig. 2a).
Fig. 2 (a) ORTEP drawing of the fundamental unit in compound 2 with thermal ellipsoids at 50% probability (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity). (b) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 1D supramolecular chain in 2. (c) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 3D supramolecular framework in 2.

Interestingly, bix2 and bix3 lie about inversion centers. As is well known, the P2Mo5 cluster can be viewed as two {PO4} tetrahedra capping either side of an irregular ring of five distorted {MoO6} octahedra linked by one corner-shared and four edge-shared contacts. Each phosphate subunit shares three oxo-groups with the molybdate ring. One of these oxo-groups adopts a μ2-bridging mode, linking one molybdenum atom and a phosphorus atom; while the other two adopt a μ3-bridging mode, linking two molybdenum atoms and a phosphorus atom. The Na atom exhibits a distorted {NaO6} octahedral geometry surrounded by five O atoms from two adjacent P2Mo5 clusters, and one O atom from a water molecule with the distances of Na–O bonds in the range of 2.315(1)–2.518(5) Å. Two Na atoms combine with two P2Mo5 clusters into a centrosymmetric dimer Na2(P2Mo5)2 (Fig. S3†). These dimeric fragments are linked together by bix ligands into 1D supramolecular chains with the distances of hydrogen bonds (N–O) from 2.815(1) to 3.470(9) Å (Fig. 2b). Then, 3D supramolecular framework (Fig. 2c) was generated by hydrogen bonds between different 1D chains with bond lengths of C–O from 2.571(1) to 3.451(1) Å (as shown in Table S5†).

Structure description of compound 3

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that there are two [(HPO4)2(H2PO4)2(MoO2)6(OH)3]3−clusters (abbreviated as P4Mo6), two crystallographically independent Cd (Cd1 and Cd2) atoms and three kinds of bix (bix1, bix2 and bix3) ligands (Fig. S1c†) in the structural unit of compound 3 (Fig. 3a). Interestingly, these two Cd atoms lie on inversion centres. Bix1 ligand lies in a general position, bix2 and bix3 lie about inversion centres.
Fig. 3 (a) ORTEP drawing of the fundamental unit in compound 3 with thermal ellipsoids at 50% probability (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity). #1 −x, 1 − y, 1 − z(b) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 1D chain structure in 3. (c) Ball-and-stick and polyhedral representation of the 3D supramolecular framework in 3.

The polyanion P4Mo6 consists of six {MoO6} octahedra and four {PO4} tetrahedra. These six molybdenum atoms are coplanar and constitute a hexametric molybdenum cluster with alternating Mo–Mo bonds and non-bonding Mo–Mo contacts. The central {PO4} group provides three oxygen atoms that bridge the bonding Mo–Mo contacts, while each of the remaining three {PO4} groups has two oxygen atoms to span the non-bonding Mo–Mo contacts. The P–O bond length is in the range of 1.492(1)–1.582(1) Å. Thus, the molybdenum hexamer can be formulated as (HPO4)2(H2PO4)2(MoO2)6(OH)3]3−. The Cd1 atom has regular octahedral coordination and bridges two [P4Mo6] cluster units to form a centrosymmetrical dimeric cluster Cd[P4Mo6]2 with three μ3-O atoms. The Cd2 atom shows a distorted {CdO6} octahedral geometry coordinated by two O atoms from two adjacent Cd[P4Mo6]2 cluster units and four O atoms from water molecules with Cd2–O distances in the range of 2.061(1)–2.224(1) Å, and links adjacent Cd[P4Mo6]2 cluster units into 1D chains (Fig. 3b). These 1D chains extend to 2D layers (Fig. S4†) through hydrogen bonding interactions with distances N4–O3 = 2.576(2), N5–O24 = 2.752(2), N6–O1 = 2.854(1) Å, and further 3D supramolecular frameworks are also assembled by hydrogen bonds with bond lengths of C–O from 3.08(2) to 3.43(3) Å (as shown in Table S5† and Fig. 3c).

Structure description of compound 4

Single-crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that there are two independent Co (Co1 and Co2) atoms, one P atom, one Mo atom (abbreviated as PMo) and two different en molecules in the asymmetric unit of compound 4 (Fig. 4).
Fig. 4 ORTEP drawing of the fundamental unit in compound 4 with thermal ellipsoids at 50% probability (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity). #1 −0.75 + y, 1.25 − x, 0.25 + z #2 0.5 − x, 1.5 − y, 0.5 − z #3 1.25 − y, 0.25 + x, 0.25 − z #4 1 − x, 1.5 − y, z #5 1.25 − y, 0.25 + x, −0.75 − z #6 −0.25 + y, 1.25 − x,−0.75 − z #7 −0.25 + y, 1.25 − x,−1.75 − z.

Interestingly, these two Co atoms show different coordinated environments where Co1 lies at a site with inversion symmetry and Co2 is at a site with crystallographically imposed 4-symmetry: the Co1 atom exhibits a distorted octahedral geometry, coordinated by six oxygen atoms and the Co2 atom is tetrahedrally coordinated by four oxygen atoms, with average Co–O bond distances of 2.080(4) Å for Co1, 1.926(5) Å for Co2. The O–Co–O bond angles are in the range of 87.02(2)–92.98(2)° for Co1, 104.82(1)–119.2(3)° for Co2. The Mo1 atom also exhibits a distorted octahedral geometry coordinated by six oxygen atoms with an average Mo–O bond distance of 1.954(4) Å. The O–Mo–O bond angles are in the range of 78.66(2)–165.19(2)°. The P1 atom exhibits a distorted tetrahedral geometry coordinated by four oxygen atoms with an average P–O bond distance of 1.535(5) Å. The O–P–O bond angles are in the range of 106.0(2)–112.3(2)°. One of the en molecules (N2–C2–C3–N3) is in a general position, which has been assigned half-occupancy. The other one (N1–C1–C1#7–N1#7) lies disordered about a site with crystallographically imposed 4-symmetry at the centre of the C–C bond with C1 on a two-fold axis.

Here, an equal number of 1D left and right-handed helical chains (Fig. 5b) are assembled from the covalent linkages of the heavy atoms (P, Mo and Co) with μ2-O atoms in the 3D framework without en molecules. Interestingly, reverse directional en chains entangle with these helical chains as templates. Then, by the linkages of Co atoms with octahedral and tetrahedral environments, alternate 1D left and right-handed helical chains are linked together to build a 2D (6,3)-layer (Fig. 5c) and three-dimensional (3D) frameworks (Fig. 5a).

Influence of molar ratios of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 on metal–oxygen clusters

In recent paper, Peng and coworkers reported four hybrid compounds, [CuI4(bte)4(SiW12O40)], [CuII2(bte)4(SiW12O40)]·4H2O, [CuI4(btb)2(SiW12O40)]·2H2O, and [CuII2(btb)4(SiW12O40)]·2H2O [bte = 1,2-bis(1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)ethane, and btb = 1,4-bis(1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)butane], hydrothermally synthesized through use of the same Keggin polyoxometalate as the template and tuning the molar ratio of the bis(triazole) ligand to the CuII ion.16 It reveals that the ratio of the ligand to metal ions has a crucial influence on the structures of this series. Does the alteration of the POM clusters influence the structures and properties of the products? We have done lots of parallel experiments to investigate this aspect by altering the molar ratio of reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 (as illustrated in Scheme 1). Under similar reaction conditions (T = 160 °C, pH = 4.0, and 5 days), different POM clusters (PMo8V6, P2Mo5, P4Mo6, and PMo) have been continuously isolated from the different ratio of reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 in compounds 1–4. With the increasing ratio of reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4, the molar ratio of P and Mo in 1–4 augments. If the ratio of reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4 was changed, the quality and productivity of these crystals could be reduced. A suitable ratio of P and Mo seems to be the necessary conditions for the isolation of compounds 1–4. We also tried to investigate the influence of POM SBUs on the products by altering the ratio of P, Mo and V. Further work to clarify this point is underway.

FT-IR spectra and TG analyses

The IR spectra of compounds 1–4 have been depicted in Fig. S5†. In the infrared spectrum of compound 1, the characteristic peaks at 1082, 972, 945, 733, 660 cm−1 are attributed to the ν(P–Oa), ν(MOd), or ν(M–O–M) (M = V or Mo). Bands in the 1640–1080 cm−1 regions in the infrared spectrum of compound 1 are attributed to characteristic peaks of the bix groups. In compound 2, the characteristic peaks at 1090, 926, 692 cm−1 are attributed to the ν(P–Oa), ν(MoOd) or ν(Mo–O–Mo). A series of medium intensity bands in the 1630–1130 cm−1 range is associated with bix, and a broad and strong band at about 3500 cm−1 is associated with the water of crystallization. For compound 3, the characteristic peaks at 1100, 949, 733 cm−1 are attributed to the ν(P–Oa), ν(MoOd) or ν(Mo–O–Mo). Bands in the 1610–1100 cm−1 regions are attributed to characteristic peaks of the bix groups and a strong band at about 3450 cm−1 is associated with the water of crystallization. In compound 4, the characteristic peaks at 1096, 943, 726 cm−1 are attributed to the ν(P–Oa), ν(MoOd) or ν(Mo–O–Mo). A series of medium intensity bands in the 1654–1099 cm−1 range is associated with en.

The thermal stabilities of compounds 1–4 have been determined on single crystalline samples in a nitrogen atmosphere by thermo gravimetric analysis (TGA) (see Fig. S6†). For compound 1, the TGA curve shows that chemical decomposition starts at 300 °C and ends at 520 °C with the weight loss of 34.55%, equivalent to the loss of the bix molecule (calcd 34.17%). The TGA curve of 2 exhibits two weight losses: one (2.62%) from 50 °C to 100 °C is attributed to the loss of water (calcd 2.48%). Another weight loss (33.28%) from 380 °C to 470 °C, corresponds to the removal of the bix ligand (calcd 33.14%). For 3, the weight loss of 10.05% from 60 °C to 110 °C is equivalent to the loss of water (calcd 9.38%). The second weight loss of 49.20% from 305 to 495 °C corresponds to the loss of the bix ligand (calcd 49.63%). The TGA curve of 4 displays one weight loss (14.64%) from 220 °C to 400 °C which is attributed to the loss of en (calcd 14.13%).

Electrochemistry study and photocatalysis properties

The electrochemical behaviors of compounds 1 and 2 were studied in a 0.1 M H2SO4 solution with 100 mV s−1 scan rate. The cyclic voltammetric behaviors of 1 and 2 exhibit three well-defined redox couples (I–I′, II–II′, and III–III′) in the potential range of +500 to −500 mV, attributable to the PMo8V6 clusters and P2Mo5 clusters (Fig. S7†).17

In a typical process, 5 mg of compounds 1–4 was dissolved in 100 mL rhodamine B (RhB) solutions (2 × 10−5 mol L−1), then magnetically stirred in the dark for about 30 min. The solution was then exposed to UV irradiation from a 125 W Hg lamp at a distance of 4–5 cm between the liquid surface and the lamp. The solution was stirred during irradiation. At 30 min intervals, 3 mL of samples were taken out from the beaker, and subsequently analyzed by UV-visible spectroscopy (as shown in Fig. 6).

The photocatalytic performances of compounds 1–4 have been investigated with photodegradation of RhB.18 Changes in Ct/C0 plot of RhB solutions versus reaction time were shown in Fig. 7. It reveals that compounds 1–4 are outstanding photocatalysts for photocatalytic degradation of RhB.

XRPD patterns of 1–4

The XRPD patterns for compounds 1–4 are shown in the Supporting Information (Fig. S9†). The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match well in key positions, thus indicating the phase purities of compounds 1–4.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we present herein four new MOFs by adjusting the molar ratio of the reactants H3PO4 and Na2MoO4. With the increasing ratio of H3PO4 and Na2MoO4, the molar ratio of P and Mo in 1–4 augments. A suitable ratio of P and Mo seems to be necessary for the isolation of compounds 1–4. We also tried to investigate the influence of POM SBUs on the products by altering the ratio of P, Mo and V. Further work to clarify this point is underway.
