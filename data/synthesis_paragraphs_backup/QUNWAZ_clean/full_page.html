Metal organic frameworks from extended, conjugated pentiptycene-based ligands†

Angela K. Crane , Nicholas G. White and Mark J. MacLachlan *
Department of Chemistry, University of British Columbia, 2036 Main Mall, Vancouver, British Columbia V6T 1Z1, Canada. E-mail: mmaclach@chem.ubc.ca; Tel: (+1) 604 822 3070

Received 6th May 2015 , Accepted 1st June 2015

First published on 11th June 2015

Two new conjugated, iptycene-containing, organic proligands were synthesized for incorporation into zinc-containing metal–organic frameworks (MOFs). A 2D pentiptycene-containing, square-paddlewheel MOF was first synthesized and characterized. By varying the reaction conditions slightly, the capping ligand could be changed, but the long-range order and packing of the 2D sheets was not altered. Addition of 4,4′-bipyridine allowed the deliberate synthesis of a 3D pillared MOF. The crystal structure of this material shows very large void spaces, but solvent-exchange and evacuation led to the collapse of the structure and a loss of crystallinity. This work demonstrates the potential to achieve high porosity by preparing MOFs from iptycene-containing ligands.

Introduction

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are a class of coordination polymer containing potential voids.1 Perhaps the first examples of what would now be considered MOFs were reported by Robson,2 and shortly after various groups investigated the adsorption of guest molecules by porous coordination networks.3 MOF chemistry has subsequently grown dramatically, inspired in part by potential applications in gas storage,4 gas separation,5 chemical sensing6 and catalysis.7

Triptycene, pentiptycene and higher order iptycenes are rigid molecules with phenyl rings attached to one or more [2.2.2]bicyclooctatriene bridgehead systems. Iptycenes have recently gained interest in supramolecular and materials chemistry.8 The rigid geometry of the aromatic groups prevents iptycenes from packing efficiently, such that void space is inevitably present in their solid-state structures. This space is known as the internal molecular free volume (IMFV) and has been exploited to prepare polymers that can sense TNT,9 or are intrinsically microporous.10 Combining MOF architectures with the intrinsic porosity of iptycene derivatives is a potential route to highly porous architectures including extended exposed aromatic surfaces, which are suggested to be one route to obtaining high H2 capacity.11

The first such iptycene MOF used a phenazine-functionalised triptycene derivative and copper(I) iodide to give coordination polymers with void space that could absorb benzene from water.12 Subsequently, iptycenes functionalized with carboxylic acids have been used as ligands for zinc cations in research influenced by Yaghi's pioneering work in this area.13 Rieger reported a series of MOFs based on triptycene functionalised with carboxylate groups in the bridgehead positions, some of which showed appreciable surface areas as determined by gas adsorption measurements.14 We have reported MOFs based on triptycene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid,15 as well as flexible triptycene and pentiptycene ligands, including the first example of a pentiptycene-containing MOF.16 We now report the preparation of two new carboxylic acid-containing proligands containing triptycene or pentiptycene extended with rigid ethynylphenyl groups, and their reaction with zinc cations to give MOF architectures.

Results and discussion

Organic synthesis

The new rigid dicarboxylic acid proligands 12H and 22H were each prepared in two steps from known 1,4-diethynyltriptycene (3)17 and 6,13-diethynylpentiptycene (4),9 respectively (Scheme 1). Sonogashira coupling with excess methyl-p-iodobenzoate gave the rigid diesters 5 and 6, and subsequent deprotection using NaOH in THF/H2O gave 12H and 22H in 32 and 39% overall yields, respectively.

Syntheses and single crystal structures of zinc MOFs

MOFs prepared from ligand 1. Proligand 12H was reacted with three molar equivalents of zinc nitrate hexahydrate in DMF under solvothermal conditions at 110 °C. Slow cooling of the solution gave a precipitate containing crystallites that were too small for analysis by single crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD). Despite several attempts, we were unable to obtain crystals suitable for SCXRD and so have not been able to definitively identify this reaction product. The structure of this material is discussed in more detail in the ESI.†
MOFs prepared from ligand 2. Proligand 22H was reacted with three molar equivalents of zinc nitrate hexahydrate in DMF under solvothermal conditions at 110 °C. Slow cooling of the solution afforded crystals suitable for analysis by SCXRD. This showed the formation of an extended 2D pentiptycene MOF structure PMOF-2a (i.e., [Zn2(22−)2(DMF)2]n·solvents, Fig. 1, Scheme 2) comprised of a square-paddlewheel secondary building unit (SBU) bridged by four 22− ligands, with each of the zinc ions in the paddlewheel capped by a DMF molecule.
Fig. 1 Solid-state structure of PMOF-2a: (a) DMF-capped square paddlewheel dinuclear Zn2+ SBU; (b) single 2D MOF sheet; (c) packing of 2D MOF sheets in A–B–C pattern.

Scheme 2 Synthesis of pentiptycene zinc MOFs from 2.

This arrangement gives a 2D grid-like sheet of ~25 × 25 Å square pores18 containing extended π-surfaces. An open π-rich framework like this would be desirable for hydrogen gas storage applications, but unfortunately, each 2D sheet is close-packed in the structure, in an A–B–C fashion. Each layer is positioned such that the central pentiptycene moiety has short phenyl⋯phenyl contacts (3.4–3.6 Å) with orthogonal pentiptycene groups from adjacent sheets above and below it. Overall, this gives a densely packed structure of 2-D sheets (Fig. 1b) where the open pore channels are blocked by the SBU of an adjacent 2D sheet.

We attempted to lower the concentration of coordinating DMF in solution and hence eliminate the capping DMF group, and therefore allow formation of a 3D MOF. To this end, the solvothermal reaction was repeated with a 1:1 solution of chlorobenzene and DMF. Keeping all other reaction conditions the same, the resulting reaction mixture once again yielded a white crystalline precipitate, containing single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction. Structural elucidation showed the formation of a new 2D MOF structure, PMOF-2b (i.e., [Zn2(22−)2(H2O)2]n·solvents, Fig. 2), which is very similar to PMOF-2a but with water molecules capping the square-paddlewheel SBU instead of DMF solvates. This material also shows a tightly packed structure of 2D sheets and contains the same A–B–C packing motif of the sheets, organised through close phenyl⋯phenyl contacts (3.4–3.6 Å) between the pentiptycene moieties of orthogonal ligands in adjacent 2D sheets.


Fig. 2 Solid-state structure of PMOF-2b: (a) H2O-capped square paddlewheel dinuclear Zn2+ SBU; (b) single 2D MOF sheet; (c) packing of 2D MOF sheets in A–B–C pattern.

In a further attempt to synthesize a MOF with a 3D structure, 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) was used as a ditopic bridging ligand. In this case, it was expected that bpy would displace the monotopic capping ligands observed in PMOF-2a and PMOF-2b and the resulting MOF would have a 3D pillared structure. The reaction was carried out solvothermally in 1:1 DMF:chlorobenzene, again at 110 °C. Slow cooling gave a yellow crystalline precipitate containing single crystals suitable for SCXRD analysis. Structural elucidation revealed that PMOF-3 (i.e., [Zn2.5(22−)2(NO3)(DMF)]n·solvents, Fig. 3) does indeed show pillared 2-D sheets, giving an overall 3D MOF.


Fig. 3 Solid-state structure of PMOF-3: (a) bpy-capped square paddlewheel dinuclear Zn2+ SBU; (b) trinuclear Zn2+ nitrate cluster SBU; (c, d) single 2-D sheet bridged by bpy pillars; (e) full structure showing two sheets (red and blue) bridged by bpy (green).

Interestingly, the individual sheets are quite different than those observed in PMOF-2a and PMOF-2b, with two different SBUs observed. The first, as expected, is a square-paddlewheel capped by the 4,4′-bipyridine pillars. The second, however, consists of a trinuclear zinc cluster. The central zinc has an octahedral geometry and is surrounded by four bridging carboxylate oxygen atoms and two μ2-NO3− oxygen atoms. The outer zinc ions are five-coordinate with distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry. Each of these zinc ions is bound to two bridging carboxylate oxygen atoms, two μ2-NO3−, an additional DMF molecule and the bridging 4,4′-bipyridine pillars. Overall, the framework consists of alternating SBUs, bridged by 22− ligands to give a 2-D grid-like sheet with square pores, pillared by bpy. The resulting 25 × 25 × 11 Å rectangular pore encompasses a volume of approximately 6800 Å3.

Powder X-ray diffraction studies. The bulk samples of PMOF-2a, PMOF-2b and PMOF-3 were investigated by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD). For all three MOFs, the PXRD traces of the as-synthesized MOFs show that they are at least partially crystalline. There are substantial differences between the PXRD data and patterns simulated from the SCXRD structures owing to partial desolvation and collapse of the structures. The PXRD spectra of PMOF-2a and PMOF-2b are very similar to one another, as is expected given their very similar SCXRD structures. Unfortunately, when the MOFs were solvent-exchanged (by soaking the crystals in dichloromethane) and air-dried, the structures appeared to collapse and completely lose crystallinity (Fig. S9–S11†).
Thermogravimetric analysis. The thermal stability of the MOFs was investigated by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA, Fig. S12 and S13†). Generally, the as-synthesized forms of all three MOFs show significant weight loss between 30 and 200 °C, consistent with removal of DMF solvents from the crystal lattice. The percentage weight loss shows reasonable agreement with the estimated number of non-hydrogen atoms removed using PLATON-SQUEEZE.23 Between 200 and 300 °C, the MOFs show small weight losses consistent with removal of coordinated DMF and water molecules. Thermal decomposition of the organic parts of all three MOFs begins at around 400 °C.

TGA of the solvent-exchanged, then evacuated forms of PMOF-2a and PMOF-2b show only small weight losses beginning at approximately 200 °C, consistent with loss of coordinated solvent molecules, followed by decomposition at around 400 °C. Even after desolvation, PMOF-3 still shows a residual weight loss of 10% from 30 to 100 °C, likely corresponding to DCM solvents that have not been fully removed from the material. Subsequently, loss of the coordinated DMF molecules is observed at approximately 300 °C, followed by decomposition of organic materials above 400 °C.

Conclusions

Two new rigid extended dicarboxylic acid proligands have been prepared. One of these was used to synthesize a series of pentiptycene-containing zinc MOFs. Using rational design we were able to move from 2D sheet architectures to a pillared 3D structure. This 3D MOF (PMOF-3) contains large void spaces in the SCXRD structure, but unfortunately appears to collapse on solvent exchange and evacuation. Our results demonstrate the potential porosity that can be achieved by integrating the internal molecular free volume of pentiptycene derivatives into MOF architectures. Future work should investigate methods to produce more robust, porous pentiptycene-based MOFs that can withstand solvent exchange, so that the ability of their large aromatic surfaces to absorb H2 gas can be thoroughly studied.

Experimental

General remarks

1,4-Diethynyltriptycene17 and 6,13-diethynylpentiptycene9 were synthesized as previously described. Other reagents were obtained from commercial suppliers and used as received.

NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AV400 spectrometer with 13C NMR spectra recorded using a proton decoupled pulse sequence; spectra were referenced to the residual solvent signal. IR spectra were obtained neat with a Thermo Nicolet 6700 FT-IR with a Smart Orbit attenuated total reflectance accessory. Electrospray ionisation (ESI) mass spectra were obtained on a Bruker Esquire LC instrument. Elemental analyses were performed at the UBC Microanalytical Services Laboratory. Melting points were obtained on a Fisher–John's melting point apparatus. Powder X-ray diffraction experiments were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance with CuKα sealed tube X-ray source and a NaI scintillation detector. Thermogravimetric analysis data was obtained using a Perkin Elmer TGA6 instrument.

Single crystal X-ray crystallography

Single crystal X-ray data were collected on a Bruker APEX DUO diffractometer using graphite monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). All data were collected at 90 K to a resolution of 0.77 Å. Raw frame data (including data reduction, interframe scaling, unit cell refinement and absorption corrections) for all structures were processed using APEX2.19 Structures were solved using SUPERFLIP20 and refined using full-matrix least-squares on F2 within the CRYSTALS suite.21 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. Hydrogen atoms were generally visible in the Fourier difference map and were initially refined with restraints on bond lengths and angles, after which the positions were used as the basis for a riding model.22

The crystals of all three MOFs were small and diffracted reasonably weakly, with few reflections observed past 1.0 Å. Despite the weak data, the structures of the complexes could be determined unambiguously. In all MOFs areas of diffuse electron density were present, which appeared to arise from disordered solvent molecules. In all cases the data quality was not sufficient to model this electron density, even with the use of restraints, and so PLATON-SQUEEZE23 was used to include the electron density in the refinement. Further details are provided in the CIF files.
