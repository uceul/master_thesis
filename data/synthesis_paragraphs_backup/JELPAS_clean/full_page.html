Volume 8, Issue 6, June 2006, Pages 692-697

Inorganic–organic hybrid structures: Synthesis, structure and magnetic properties of a new iron oxalatoarsenate, [NH3(CH2)CH(NH3)CH3]3[Fe6(AsO4)2(HAsO4)6(C2O4)3]

Author links open overlay panelVandavasi KoteswaraRaoaKinson C.KambAnthony K.CheethambSrinivasanNatarajana
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.solidstatesciences.2006.02.031Get rights and content

Abstract

A hydrothermal reaction of a mixture of iron oxalate, arsenic acid, 1,2-diaminopropane and water at 150 °C gave rise to a new three-dimensional iron oxalatoarsenate, [NH3(CH2)CH(NH3)CH3]3[Fe6(AsO4)2(HAsO4)6(C2O4)3], I. Crystal data: hexagonal, space group = P-3c1, a=13.9899(12), c=14.936(3)Å, V=2531.6(5)Å3. The structure consists of layers of vertex-sharing FeO6 octahedral and AsO4 tetrahedral units that form 12-membered apertures. The layers are connected together by the oxalate units, forming a uniform one-dimensional channel that is occupied by protonated amine molecules. Variable temperature powder XRD studies indicates that I loses crystallinity at ∼300 °C forming an amorphous phase that gives way to crystalline FeAsO4 at ∼650 °C. Magnetic studies reveal that I is antiferromagnetic with TN=31K.

1. Introduction

It is becoming clear that inorganic open-framework compounds templated by organic amines exhibit a large diversity and novelty in their structures [1]. The main interest in investigating this class of compounds stems from their potential applications in the areas of catalysis, sorption and separation processes. Among the many open-framework compounds, those based on the phosphate frameworks appears to be dominant [1], [2], [3]. In addition to the use of tetrahedral phosphate groups as building units, other anionic moieties such as borates [4], arsenates [5], sulphates [6] and selenates [7] have been used successfully in the preparation of novel open-framework structures. Of these, the arsenates are promising because, although As belongs to the same group as phosphorous, the larger size of the AsO4− anion can give rise to different structures and/or properties. Thus, many arsenate frameworks of different structure and composition has been prepared and characterized [5], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13], [14].

Recently, it has been shown that rigid linkers such as oxalates can partially replace the traditional phosphates giving rise to a new series of inorganic–organic hybrid structures [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], [20]. The resulting hybrid oxalatophosphate frameworks are unique and typically adopt three-dimensional structures. A study of oxalatoarsenates should also yield novel structures, as the association of rigid oxalate linkers along with the larger arsenates could be an interesting combination. During the course of a study aimed at preparing new inorganic–organic hybrid compounds, we have isolated a new oxalatoarsenate, [NH3(CH2)CH(NH3)CH3]3[Fe6(AsO4)2(HAsO4)6(C2O4)3], I, possessing a three-dimensional structure. In this paper, we present the synthesis, structure and properties of I.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis and initial characterization

The compound [H3NCH2CH(NH3)CH3]3[Fe6III(HAsO4)6(AsO4)2(C2O4)3], I, was synthesized under hydrothermal conditions using iron (II) oxalate dihydrate as the source of iron. In a typical synthesis, 0.297 g of iron (II) oxalate dihydrate was dissolved in 4 mL of millipore water. Then, 1.249 g of H3AsO4 was added to the mixture. Finally 0.28 mL of 1,2-diaminopropane (1,2-DAP) (99%) was added and the mixture was homogenized for 30 min at room temperature. The final mixture with the composition, 1.5FeC2O4⋅2H2O : 8H3AsO4 : 3(1,2-DAP) : 200H2O, was transferred to a 23 mL acid-digestion bomb and heated at 150 °C for 3 days. The initial and final pH of the reaction mixture was ∼2 and there was no appreciable change in the pH during the reaction. The resulting colorless needle-like crystals were filtered and washed with distilled water and dried at ambient conditions. The yield of the product was ∼80% based on iron (II) oxalate. An EDAX analysis on many single crystals indicated an As : Fe ratio of 4 : 3, agreeing with the single crystal structure. C, H, N analysis, obsd. (calcd.): C = 10.10 (9.26), H = 2.78 (2.2), N = 4.35 (4.32).

The compound was characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (XRD), infra-red (IR) spectroscopy and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The powder XRD studies indicated that the pattern was new; it was entirely consistent with the simulated pattern based on the structure determined using single crystal X-ray diffraction (Fig. 1).

IR spectroscopic studies (Perkin–Elmer, Spectrum-1000) were carried out with a KBr pellet in the range 400–4000 cm−1. The results indicate characteristic peaks. The various observed bands and their assignments are: νNH = 3351 cm−1 (m); νCH = 3094 cm−1 (m) and 2927 cm−1 (w); νCO = 1643 cm−1 (s); δs (NH) 1532 cm−1 (w); δ+NCH2 (asymmetric drift) = 1467 cm−1 (w), δCH = 1356 cm−1 (m) and 1313 cm−1 (m); νs AsOH = 1216 cm−1 (w) and 1178 cm−1 (w); νCN = 1058 cm−1 (w); νas AsO = 924 cm−1 (w), 850 cm−1 (s), and 824 cm−1 (s); νs AsO = 732 cm−1 (w); ωNH = 610 cm−1 (w); δas AsO = 487 cm−1 (m).

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of I was carried out in an atmosphere of flowing air (flow rate = 50 mL min−1) in the temperature range 25–850 °C (heating rate = 5 °C min−1). The results indicate two weight losses. The sample loses weight continuously up to 300 °C and the loss in this region may be due to the decomposition of the organic amine and the oxalate moiety. The next broad weight loss from 400–600 °C corresponds to the condensation of terminal OH group and partial removal of As2O5. The total observed weight loss of 52% may be correlated with the loss of the amine, the oxalate, the condensation of terminal OH group and some arsenic oxide. The final decomposition product was poorly crystalline with the majority of the XRD lines identified as FeAsO4 (JCPDS-00-021-0910) [21]. It is likely that the calcined product may also contain some X-ray amorphous material.

In order to understand the decomposition behavior of I, we carried out X-ray thermodiffractometry studies on a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer equipped with an Anton Parr HTK 16 High Temperature Stage and a Braun Position Sensitive Detector. The sample was carefully mounted onto a platinum heating strip and heated in air at 20 °C intervals up to 800 °C. Data were collected over an angular range of 5–45 (2θ) with a resolution of 0.0147° at 1.00 s/step. The feature at 39.7 (2θ) corresponds to the Pt strip. The sample was spread uniformly on the Pt-strip and the XRD lines from the Pt were used as an internal calibration standard. The results are presented in Fig. 2. As can be noted, the sample decomposes to an amorphous phase at ca. 300 °C. It is likely that the organic amine and the oxalate units were removed at this temperature, agreeing with the observation of the TGA studies. On heating further, the sample continues to be X-ray amorphous until ca. 560 °C. In the temperature range 300–560 °C, in accordance with the TGA studies, the terminal OH group condenses along with the removal of some arsenic. The sample starts to be become crystalline after 560 °C. As the temperature is raised further, there is no appreciable change in the powder XRD patterns up to 800 °C. The majority of the XRD lines correspond to FeAsO4, the same phase obtained after the TGA experiments. During the entire experiment, the XRD lines of the Pt-strip were observed at a 2θ value of ca. 40°. This study clearly indicates that the thermal stability of I is quite limited and it forms a crystalline phase via an amorphous intermediate. Similar observations have been made earlier on other open-framework materials [22], [23], [24].

2.2. Single crystal structure determination

A suitable colorless single crystal was carefully selected under a polarizing microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber. The single crystal diffraction data were collected on a Bruker AXS Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at room temperature (293 K). The X-ray generator was operated at 50 kV and 35 mA using Mo Kα (λ=0.71073Å) radiation. Data were collected with ω scans of width 0.3°. A total of 606 frames were collected in three different settings of φ (0, 90 and 180°), keeping the sample-to-detector distance fixed at 6 cm and the detector position fixed at −25°. 3. Results and discussion

The structure of I consists of a network of FeO6 octahedral, AsO4 tetrahedral and C2O4 units linked together giving rise to a three-dimensional structure. The Fe atoms are coordinated to six oxygen atom neighbors with four short and two longer FeO distances. The FeO distances are in the range 1.929(3)–2.145(3) Å (av. 2.018 Å), the shorter distances corresponding to the FeOAs and the longer distances corresponding to the FeOC linkages; these distances are consistent with the iron being in the 3+ oxidation state. The OFeO bond angles are in the range 87.05(11)–174.35(12)°. While all the FeO6 vertices are shared with either the As or C atoms through the oxygen linkages, only three of the vertices of the AsO4 units are shared, the remaining ones being either AsO or AsOH bonds. The As atoms have tetrahedral coordination with AsO distances in the range 1.664(3)–1.717(3) Å [av. 1.685 Å for As(1) and 1.682 Å for As(2), respectively]. The OAsO bond angles are in the range 105.09(9)–114.80(14)° (av. 109.3°). The CO bond distances and OCO bond angles are in the range expected for this type of bonding. Selected bond distances and angles are given in Table 2.

The framework consists of FeO6, AsO4, HAsO4 and C2O4 moieties. Connectivity between the FeO6, AsO4 and HAsO4 polyhedral units leads to an inorganic layer of formula [Fe3(HAsO4)3(AsO4)], with the FeO6 and AsO4 tetrahedra strictly alternating within the layer. The layers are porous, encompassing a circular 12-membered ring as shown in Fig. 3(a); this layer is topologically identical to that seen in the porous aluminophosphate based upon the Al3P4O163− ion, with which it shares the same space group, P-3c1 [27] (note that other layered aluminum phosphates with the Al3P4O163− stoichiometry are based entirely on tetrahedral nets [28]) The architecture of I has also been seen in an iron oxalatophosphate [15]. The 12-membered pores within the layers are surrounded by 4-membered rings, of which there are two distinct types. In one set of these rings, the arsenic atoms are part of the wall of the 12-membered ring, while in the other an arsenate group caps a six-membered ring and alternates above and below the plane of the inorganic layers (Fig. 3(b)). The layers are arranged in AAAA… fashion and are held in place by oxalate pillars. The oxalate units are coordinated to the iron centers and connect the adjacent layers (Fig. 4).

The negative charge of the framework of the iron oxalatoarsenate, I, is compensated by the 1,2-diammoniumpropane cations. Within the structure of I, the porous layers are arranged exactly one over the other, giving rise to a supermesh of apertures. The organic amine molecules, which are disordered in I, occupy the one-dimensional channels that are oriented perpendicular to the inorganic sheets. This results in completely filled channels and no adsorption behavior was observed. While the structure of I is closely related to that of the iron oxalatophosphate, [NH3(CH2)2NH3]3[Fe6(PO4)2(HPO4)6(C2O4)3]⋅xH2O (x=3–4), reported earlier [15], the 1,2-diaminopropane in I is replaced by ethylenediamine and labile water. The replacement of larger amines by smaller ones plus water has been observed in other systems, such as open framework gallium phosphates [29].

Variable temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements on I have been carried out in the range 5–325 K on a powder sample using a SQUID magnetometer. Plots of χm and 1/χm verses T are shown in Fig. 5. The thermal evolution of χm in the region 150–325 K follows a Curie–Weiss behavior, with Cm=4.51emu/mol and θ=−59K. The magnetic moment calculated from the slope of the Curie–Weiss fit is 6.01 μB, in good agreement with the value of 5.91 μB that is expected for FeIII in the high-spin state. The molar magnetic susceptibility increases with decreasing temperature and reaches a maximum at ca. 31 K, indicating that long-rang antiferromagnetic ordering is established at this temperature. Similar magnetic behavior has also been observed in the oxalatophosphate structure of iron, [NH3(CH2)2NH3]3[Fe6(PO4)2(HPO4)6(C2O4)3]⋅xH2O (x=3–4), where TN is again 31 K and θ=−60K [15]. The similarity between the values of TN and θ for the two compounds is rather surprising. In other comparisons between phosphates and arsenates, the values for these parameters tend to be smaller for the arsenates due to their longer superexchange pathways [30].
