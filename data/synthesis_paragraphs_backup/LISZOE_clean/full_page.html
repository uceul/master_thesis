A metal–organic framework assembled from Y(III), Li(I), and terephthalate: hydrothermal synthesis, crystal structure, thermal decomposition and topological studies†

Mohammed S. M. Abdelbaky a, Zakariae Amghouz *ab, Santiago García-Granda a and José R. García a
aDepartamentos de Química Física y Analítica y Química Orgánica e Inorgánica, Universidad de Oviedo-CINN, 33006 Oviedo, Spain
bServicios Científico-Técnicos, Universidad de Oviedo-CINN, 33006 Oviedo, Spain. E-mail: amghouz.uo@uniovi.es; Fax: +34 985 103 686; Tel: +34 985 103 000 (Ext. 5456)

Received 4th December 2013 , Accepted 26th January 2014

First published on 27th January 2014

A novel metal–organic framework assembled from Y(III), Li(I), and terephthalate ligand, formulated as [LiY(BDC)2(H2O)·2(H2O)] (1) (H2BDC = terephthalic acid), has been obtained as single phase under hydrothermal conditions. The crystal structure was solved by single-crystal X-ray diffraction and the bulk was characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), thermal analyses (TG-MS and DSC), vibrational spectroscopy (FTIR), scanning/transmission electron microscopy (SEM-EDX, TEM, SAED, and BF-STEM-EDX), and powder X-ray thermodiffractometry (HT-XRD). 1 crystallizes in monoclinic space group (P21/c, with a = 11.6415(7) Å, b = 16.0920(4) Å, c = 13.2243(8) Å and β = 132.23(1)°) and possesses a 3D framework with 1D trigonal channels running along the [101] direction containing water molecules. The structure of 1 is made up of unusual four-membered rings formed by edge- and vertex-shared {YO8} and {LiO4} polyhedra. The four-membered rings are isolated and connected to each other via carboxylate groups. HT-XRD reveals that 1 undergoes phase transformation upon the dehydration process which is a reversible process involving a spontaneous rehydration characterized by fast kinetics. Topological studies were also performed revealing that 1 has a new 2-nodal net.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), also known as coordination polymers, a class of crystalline organic–inorganic hybrid materials in which metal-containing nodes (metal cations or clusters) are connected by organic bridges, still attract considerable attention for applications such as gas storage,1–4 and separation,3,5 catalysis,6–9 magnetism,10–15 and luminescence.1,3,16–20 A large variety of inorganic and organic components can be used to construct MOFs, and this versatility has enabled the rational design and assembly of materials having novel topologies and exceptional properties. Designing such hybrid materials can take advantage of both the specific physical properties of inorganic units and the huge variety of suitable organic linkers. Dicarboxylate linkers exhibit a versatile coordination behavior displaying distinct bonding modes toward metal cations. A particularly well studied organic linker is the dianion of 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (BDC), due to its structural rigidity, a diversity of coordination geometries, and the possibility to facilitate the formation of structures with large voids.21–23 First row transition metals are the most popular choice in constructing the framework because of their well-known coordination preference with BDC. LnMOFs (MOFs based on trivalent lanthanides) containing dicarboxylate linkers show an intriguing variety of crystal structures due to their usually high coordination number, leading to dimeric units and polymeric chains most frequently observed for these compounds. In particular, LnMOFs, in which both Ln3+ and linkers can be used to give rise to luminescence materials (through antenna effect) with increased brightness and emission quantum yield,17–20,24–26 are a very promising class of materials in a wide range of optical applications including lasers,26 imaging agents,27–29 lighting systems,30 and display applications.31 Those based on the lightest metal in the periodic table, lithium,32–38 are not extensively explored even if lithium is of particular interest due to recent experimental and theoretical studies showing enhanced H2 uptake in lithium doped MOFs,39–46 and are promising candidates for replacing the conventional electrode in Li-ion batteries.47

A developing area of MOF chemistry is the synthesis of mixed-metal frameworks not only to obtain materials with novel architectures, but also to control their properties by borrowing a well known strategy in classical solid-state chemistry based on replacement of one metal by another. Numerous studies have been carried out using the hydrothermal route to prepare Ln–BDC18,20,48–52 or alkali–BDC53–57 based frameworks. However, to the best of our knowledge, studies on the alkali–Ln–BDC system have not been reported to date. Amghouz et al.18,50 have reported the synthesis and structural characterization of two series of isoreticular chiral metal–organic frameworks, formulated as [NaLn(Tart)(BDC)(H2O)2] (S1) and [NaLn(Tart)(biBDC)(H2O)2] (S2) (H2Tart = tartaric acid; H2BDC = terephthalic acid; H2biBDC = biphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid), and exhibiting room temperature tunable UV–vis–IR light emission. As part of our ongoing research motivated by these previous reports, the study of the effect of the Li+ cation on S1-type materials leads to a novel compound, formulated as [LiY(BDC)2(H2O)·2(H2O)] (1), exhibiting a binodal net with a new topology.

Experimental section

Synthesis

[LiY(BDC)2(H2O)·2(H2O)] (1) was synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. 0.31 g (1 mmol) of YCl3·6H2O, 0.21 g (1 mmol) of L-tartaric acid (H2Tart), 0.08 g (0.5 mmol) of terephthalic acid (H2BDC), and 0.14 g (3.5 mmol) of LiOH·H2O were dissolved in a mixture of distilled water (5 mL) and ethanol (5 mL). The reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature to homogeneity and then placed in a Teflon-lined stainless vessel (40 mL) and heated to 180 °C (1) for 3 days under autogenous pressure and afterwards cooled to room temperature. The resulting product contains large single crystals and microcrystalline powder, which were separated by ultrasound, filtered off individually, washed thoroughly with distilled water, and finally air-dried at room temperature. Yield: 27 mg, ca. 12% for both compounds based on Y. Elemental analysis: C16H14O11YLi (Mr = 478.12). Calcd: C, 40.19; H, 2.93%. Found: C, 39.6; H, 2.3%. Many attempts have been made for optimizing the synthesis conditions with and without adding H2Tart (ESI, Table S1†). Even though H2Tart has not been incorporated into the crystal structure, an amount of 1 mmol was necessary to obtain the desired compound 1 as single-phase microcrystalline powder.

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies

Data collection was performed at 293 K on an Agilent Gemini CCD diffractometer using CuKα radiation. Images were collected at a 55 mm fixed crystal–detector distance, using the oscillation method, with 1° oscillation and variable exposure time per image. The crystal structure was solved by direct methods. The refinement was performed using full-matrix least squares on F2. All non-H atoms were anisotropically refined. All H atoms were either geometrically placed riding on their parent atoms or located from the difference Fourier map, with isotropic displacement parameters set at 1.2 times the Ueq of the atoms to which they are attached. Crystallographic calculations were carried out using the following programs: CrysAlis CCD58 for data collection; CrysAlis RED59 for cell refinement, data reduction and empirical absorption correction; SHELXS-9760 for structure solution; XABS261 for refined absorption correction; SHELXL-97 for structure refinement and preparing materials for publication; PLATON62 for the geometrical calculations; Diamoned63 for molecular graphics.

Powder X-ray diffraction studies

Powder X-ray diffraction patterns were recorded on an X'pert Philips diffractometer with CuKα radiation. The samples were gently ground in an agate mortar in order to minimize the preferred orientation. All data were collected at room temperature over the angular 2θ range 4–60° with a step of 0.01° and a counting time of 1.5 s per step. The PXRD patterns of the compound were compared with the theoretical ones (Fig. 1), indicating that the product has been successfully obtained as pure crystalline phase.
Fig. 1 Powder XRD patterns of 1 compared with the theoretical one.

Powder X-ray thermodiffraction studies

These measurements were performed in air; the sample was placed in an Anton Paar HTK 1200N oven-chamber on a PANalyticalXPERT-PRO diffractometer, using CuKα radiation, equipped with a PIXcel linear detector with 255 channels. Each powder pattern was recorded in the 5–60° range (2θ) at intervals of 25 °C up to 200 °C and cooling down to 25 °C with a step of 0.013° and a counting time of 0.314 s per channel. The temperature ramp between two consecutive temperatures was 10 °C min−1.

Morphological characterization

SEM micrographs and X-ray microanalysis (SEM/EDX) were recorded using a JEOL-6610LV scanning electron microscope, operating at 30 kV, coupled with an Oxford X-Max microanalysis system (EDX). TEM and BF-STEM images, SAED patterns, and BF-STEM/EDX were recorded on a JEOL JEM-2100F field emission transmission electron microscope, at 200 kV, equipped with a STEM unit and an Oxford X-Max microanalysis system (EDX). SEM and TEM images show microcrystalline powder and EDX confirmed the presence of yttrium, carbon, and oxygen (ESI, Fig. S1 and S2†).

Infrared spectra

The infrared data were collected at room temperature using a FT-IR Bruker Tensor-27 spectrometer from KBr pellets. The spectra were collected over the range 4000–400 cm−1 by averaging 15 scans at a maximum resolution of 4 cm−1.

Elemental analysis

The carbon and hydrogen contents were measured on a C–H–N Perkin-Elmer model 2400B elemental analyzer.

Thermal characterization

A Mettler–Toledo TGA/SDTA851e and a DSC822e were used for the thermal analysis under an oxygen dynamic atmosphere (50 mL min−1) at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. In this case, ca. 10 mg of the powder sample was thermally treated, and blank runs were performed. In TG tests, a Pfeiffer vacuum ThermoStar™ GSD301 T mass spectrometer was used to determine the evacuated vapours. The m/z 18 (H2O), 44 (CO2) and 78 (C6H6) were tested using a detector C-SEM, operating at 1200 V with a time constant of 1 s.

Results and discussion

The detailed crystallographic data and the structure refinement parameters are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond distances and angles are given in Table 2. The asymmetric unit of compound 1 comprises one Y3+, one Li+, one BDC, two half-BDC, one coordinated water molecule, and two non-coordinated water molecules (ESI, Fig. S3†). The Y3+ cation is bonded to eight oxygen atoms, all of them coming from six carboxylate groups which belong to four non-equivalent groups O1–C1–O2, O3–C9–O4, O5–C13–O6, and O7–C8–O8. Its coordination geometry may be described as a distorted bicapped trigonal prism (Fig. 2). Out of the four carboxylate groups surrounding the Y3+ which belong to two different carboxylate groups O1–C1–O2 and O7–C8–O8, each of two contribute with one oxygen atom O1 or O2, and O7 or O8. The remaining two, O3–C9–O4 and O5–C13–O6, act in chelating mode via O3 and O4, and O5 and O6, respectively. The Y–O bond distances range from 2.243(2) Å to 2.456(3) Å with the average value of 2.363 Å. The coordination geometry around the tetracoordinated Li+ cation resembles a distorted tetrahedron formed by the coordination to four crystallographically independent oxygen atoms (Fig. 2). The oxygen atoms come from three independent carboxylate groups (O1–C1–O2, O3–C9–O4, O5–C13–O6) and one water molecule (O1W) which contribute with O1, O3, O6 and O1W, respectively. The Li–O bond distances range from 1.896(8) Å to 2.052(1) Å, with the average value of 1.984 Å. The coordination modes of BDC anions are shown in Fig. 3. The four independent carboxylate groups in 1, belonging to three different terephthalate anions, may be divided into three groups: (i) O1–C1–O2 coordinates O2 to one Y3+ cation where O1 is bonded in bridging mode between Y3+ and Li+ cations, (ii) O7–C8–O8 bridges two adjacent Y3+ cations, (iii) O3–C9–O4 and O5–C13–O6 are bonded to one Y3+ cation via O4 and O5, and in bridging mode between Y3+ and Li+ cations through O3 and O6, respectively. The structure of compound 1 is made up of unusual four-membered rings formed by edge- and vertex-shared {YO8} and {LiO4} polyhedra (Fig. 4a–c) through oxygen atoms O1–O6 (edge) and O3 (vertex). The isolated four-membered rings stacked along the [101] direction are bridged via two equivalent carboxylate groups O7–C8–O8 forming inorganic–organic hybrid chains (Fig. 4d). The distance between adjacent Y–Li cations in the chain is 3.393(3) Å. Each chain is in turn cross-linked to six neighboring chains through six terephthalate bridges, forming a 3D framework with channels running along the [101] direction containing the crystallization water molecules (Fig. 4e). In this structure strong hydrogen bonds are found between the uncoordinated water molecules O2W and O3W, which act as: (i) donors with the carboxylate oxygen atoms O2, O4, O5 and O8 of O1–C1–O2, O3–C9–O4,O5–C13–O6 and O7–C8–O8, and as (ii) acceptors with coordinated water molecule O1W (Table 3, Fig. S4 in ESI†).

The topological analysis64 reveals that 1 exhibits a novel binodal net topology (Fig. 5, Fig. S5 in ESI†). 1 is simplified as a 2-nodal 3,10-c net with the point symbol of {4.5^2}2{4^14.5^10.6^18.7.8^2} considering the three different BDC linkers as two rod-connector (Fig. 5a,c) and one 3-connected node (Fig. 5b), and the isolated four-membered ring [O18Li2Y2] as a 10-connected node (Fig. 5d).


Fig. 5 Topological representation of 1.

In the previously reported compounds [NaLn(Tart)(BDC)(H2O)] (S1),18,50 the Na+ cation was bonded to six oxygen atoms describing a distorted trigonal prismatic geometry (ESI, Fig. S6†) and tartaric acid (H2Trat) was incorporated in the structure. In order to study the effect of alkali cations in this system, through replacing Na+ by a Li+ cation and following similar hydrothermal conditions as for S1, the new compound 1 was obtained in which Li+ is bonded to four oxygen atoms exhibiting a distorted tetrahedral geometry; however, in this case Tart was not incorporated into the structure. Since the coordination numbers and geometries of alkali cations are unpredictable, but their coordination nature may depend on the size and the nature of the ligands.65 So, the main cause of the structural differences between compound type S1 and the new compound 1 is attributed to the small size of the Li+ cation unable to satisfy the more chelating centers of Tart compared with BDC.

The IR spectrum of compound 1 is shown in Fig. S7 (see ESI†). The broad band observed in the 3710–2820 cm−1 region is assigned to O–H stretching vibrations of coordinated and uncoordinated water molecules. The characteristic bands for the antisymmetric νasym(CO) and symmetric νsym(CO) vibrations appear at ca. 1589 cm−1 and 1409 cm−1, respectively. The difference between the bands (Δνasym(COO)–sym(COO) = 180 cm−1) confirms the bridging mode of the carboxylate groups found in this compound.66,67

The thermal stability of 1 was investigated. TG/DTG, SDTA curves, and the mass spectrometry analysis are depicted in Fig. 6, while DSC traces are depicted in Fig. S8 (see ESI†). TG/DTG curves of 1 reveal a total mass loss of ca. 72% (calc. 70%) from room temperature up to 1000 °C leading to Y2O3 and LiYO2 as final products of thermal decomposition (confirmed by PXRD, see Fig. S9 in ESI†). The mass loss of 1, in air, proceeds in six steps. The first one between 25 and 105 °C (which reaches its maximum velocity at 96 °C) with the mass loss of ca. 4.1% (cal. 4.2%), associated with endothermic peaks at 100 °C and 75 °C on SDTA and DSC curves, respectively, corresponds to the loss of one uncoordinated water molecule. The second step between 105 and 170 °C (which reaches its maximum velocity at 146 °C) with the mass loss of ca. 4.6% (cal. 4.2%), associated with endothermic peaks at 151 °C and 170 °C on SDTA and DSC curves, respectively, is attributed to the loss of the second uncoordinated water molecule. The third step between 215 and 265 °C (which reaches its maximum velocity at 240 °C) with the mass loss of 4.5% (cal. 4.2%), associated with a weak endothermic peak on SDTA at 225 °C, corresponds to the loss of the coordinated water molecule. The fourth step between 330 and 470 °C (which reaches its maximum velocity at 409 °C) with the mass loss of about 22.6%, associated with exothermic peaks at 414 °C and 422 °C on the SDTA and DSC, respectively, may be attributed to the beginning of decomposition of the BDC ligand. The fifth step between 480 and 550 °C (which reaches its maximum velocity at 513 °C) with the mass loss of 21.3% associated with sharp exothermic peaks at 517 °C and 511 °C on SDTA and DSC curves, respectively, may be attributed to the complete decomposition of the BDC ligand.


Fig. 6 TG-DTG-SDTA curves and m/z 18 (H2O), m/z 44 (CO2) and m/z 76 (C6H4) MS signals for 1.

The last step between 720 and 1000 °C with the mass loss of about 14.8% may be due to the further liberation of trapped organic moieties. The associated mass spectroscopy m/z 18 (H2O), 44 (CO2), and 76 (C6H4) curves are in agreement with TG/DTG/DSC data. The m/z 18 curve has five maxima; the first and second maxima at 106 °C and 155 °C correspond to the loss of the two uncoordinated water molecules. The third maximum at 230 °C corresponds to the loss of the coordinated water molecule. The fourth maximum at 420 °C coincides with the first one of the m/z 44 curve, which is attributed to the first decomposition step of the BDC ligand. The last maximum at 515 °C coincides with the second maximum of m/z 44 and with the maximum of m/z 76 which corresponds to complete oxidation of the whole organic moiety leading to a mixture of Y2O3 and LiYO2 as the final product. Taking into account the fact that the mass spectrometry analysis is a semiquantitative method, the integration of the fourth and fifth bands (2442 nA) is almost 1.9 of the integration of the first three ones (4640 nA) in the m/z 18 curve, proving the loss of approximately four water molecules in the fourth and fifth stages, corresponding to the amount of hydrogen atoms resulting from the decomposition of two BDC ligands in the formula of 1. The dehydration and oxidative decomposition enthalpies estimated by DSC were −160 kJ mol−1 and 3259 kJ mol−1, respectively.

The powder X-ray thermodiffraction studies performed under air atmosphere in successive heating and cooling ramps (Fig. 7) revealed that compound 1 shows a phase transformation corresponding to the dehydration process by losing the two non-coordinated water molecules and one coordinated water molecule, which is a reversible process involving a spontaneous rehydration after cooling down to room temperature. No significant change in the unit cell parameters has been observed below 100 °C. The beginning of the phase transformation to anhydrous form without losing the crystallinity is observed at 100 °C, and the total transformation is achieved at 110 °C. From 120 °C up to 200 °C and cooling down to 90 °C the anhydrous phase is preserved. The rehydration starts at 80 °C and continues until 70 °C, and the total rehydration is achieved at 60 °C with no noticeable diffraction intensity drop and peak broadening. The powder XRD pattern of dehydrated compound 1, treated at 200 °C in air, has been indexed in a monoclinic system with the following unit cell parameters: a = 16.490(7) Å, b = 8.225(4) Å, c = 14.711(4) Å and β = 107.04(3)°, V = 1886.31 Å3 by using both DICVOL68 (M(16) = 11, F(16) = 17) and TREOR69 (M(16) = 11, F(16) = 15) indexing programs implemented in the FullProf suite.70 The results reveal that the dehydrated form of 1 preserves the monoclinic symmetry and displays some similarities in cell parameters.


Fig. 7 X-ray thermodiffractometry of 1 recorded in air heating up from 25 °C to 200 °C and cooling down to 25 °C. Color code corresponding to the structural change, blue: starting compound, magenta: dehydration, red: dehydrated compound, green: rehydration, blue: rehydrated compound.

Conclusions

By the hydrothermal method a novel lithium–yttrium–terephthalate based metal–organic framework, formulated as [LiY(BDC)2(H2O)·2(H2O)] (1), has been obtained and characterized. The differences in ion size and coordination requirements between Li+ and Na+ cations and the synthesis conditions (presence of tartaric acid) proved to play an important role in obtaining such a compound. Currently, we are directing our focus to obtain new members of [M(I)–Ln(III)–BDC)] and [M(I)–Ln(III)–biBDC] (M(I) = alkali metals, Ln(III) = lanthanide element, H2BDC = terephthalic acid, and H2biBDC = biphenyl-4,4′-dicarboxylic acid) families, as these may be promising materials in the fields of photoluminescence and gas storage.
