    Abstract

    Hydrothermal reactions of Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O with two new triazine-based phosphonic acids, 2,4,6-triglyphosate-1,3,5-triazine (H9L1) and 4,6-biglyphosate-2-hydroxyl-1,3,5-triazine (H6L2), afforded two new zinc phosphonates, namely, [(C5H14N2)Zn3(HL1)(H2O)]·3.5H2O (1) and [(C5H14N2)Zn2L2]·3H2O (2), respectively. The two zinc phosphonates were structurally characterized through single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 is a three-dimensional (3D) framework in that Zn–O–P chains are connected by HL18– anions with hendecadentate modes. In compound 2, each L26– anion links four Zn2 binuclear units to form a 3D framework. In both compounds 1 and 2, the protonated 2-methylpiperazines are encapsulated into the channels of 3D frameworks. Thermogravimetric analysis and powder X-ray diffraction reveal that compounds 1 and 2 are thermally stable up to 200 and 160 °C under an air atmosphere, respectively. Compounds 1 and 2 display near-UV and purple luminescence with maximal bands at 377 and 402 nm, respectively. It worth noting that the luminescence of solids 1 and 2 can be preserved after compounds 1 and 2 are heated at 200 and 160 °C for 2 h under an air atmosphere, respectively. In addition, the ion-exchange property of compound 1 was also studied.

    Synopsis

    A new three-dimensional zinc phosphonate exhibits thermal stability and an ion-exchange property and displays near-UV luminescence, which can be preserved after the phosphonate has been heat-treated at 200 °C for 2 h under an air atmosphere.

    Metal phosphonates are of great research interest because of their structural and compositional diversity, as well as their thermal and chemical stability for potential applications as porous materials, ion exchangers, proton conductors, Langmuir–Blodgett films, nonlinear optics, molecular sensors, catalysts, magnets, etc.(1-13) Because of the low solubility and crystallinity of metal phosphonates, it is still difficult to grow single crystals of a suitable size and high quality for X-ray structural analysis. In recent years, much effort has been devoted to modifying phosphonic acids with additional groups, including amino, carboxylate, pyridyl, triazole, thienyl, and imidazole groups.(14-37) This is due to the fact that additional groups can enrich coordination modes to improve the solubility and crystallinity of metal phosphonates. As a result, a variety of intriguing metal phosphonates have been obtained and structurally characterized, such as calcium phosphonates with tuned proton conductivity.(19) On the other hand, triazine derivatives have been widely exploited to construct interesting coordination polymers, especially flexible polycarboxylate groups connecting with triazine rings.(38-49) However, triazine rings with phosphonate groups have been rarely reported in previous work.(50, 51) Recently, an effect method has been used to synthesize functionalized phosphonic acids by a combination of the N(CH2COOH) (CH2PO3H2) moiety and other organic groups. On the basis of these functionalized phosphonate ligands, many luminescent metal phosphonates with three-dimensional (3D) frameworks, hybrid layers or polynuclear clusters, have been obtained under hydrothermal conditions.(52-57) In this regard, we suggest that the combination of the N(CH2COOH) (CH2PO3H2) moiety and triazine rings would result in new functionalized phosphonate ligands. Thus, two new triazine-based phosphonic acids, 2,4,6-triglyphosate-1,3,5-triazine [H9L1 (Scheme 1)] and 4,6-biglyphosate-2-hydroxyl-1,3,5-triazine [H6L2 (Scheme 1)], have been successfully synthesized. Remarkably, ligands H9L1 and H6L2 possess 21 and 16 coordination sites, respectively. Such new multifunctional phosphonate ligands would adopt diversified coordination modes to form many interesting metal phosphonates. Herein, we report the synthesis, crystal structures, thermal stabilities, luminescence, and ion-exchange property of two new 3D zinc phosphonates: [(C5H14N2)Zn3(HL1)(H2O)]·3.5H2O (1) and [(C5H14N2)Zn2L2]·3H2O (2).

    General

    Phosphonate ligands H9L1 and H6L2 were prepared according to the methods reported previously.(58, 59) Other chemicals were obtained from commercial sources without further purification. Elemental (C, H, N) analyses were conducted with a Vario EL III element analyzer, while metal contents were tested using an Ultrimas ICP spectrometer. Infrared spectra were obtained on Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR and VERTEX 70 FT-IR spectrometers. The emission and excitation spectra were recorded in the solid state at room temperature with a model F-7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer, while the lifetime and quantum yield were investigated in the solid state with an Edinburgh model FLS920 fluorescence spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Netzsch STA449C instrument at a heating rate of 10 °C min–1 from room temperature to 1000 °C under an air gas flow. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were acquired on DMAX-2500 and Ultima IV diffractometers using Cu Kα radiation in an ambient environment.

    A mixture of Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O (0.1021 g, 0.4651 mmol), H9L1 (0.0591 g, 0.102 mmol), and 2-methylpiperazine (1.00 g, 9.98 mmol) in 8.0 mL of distilled water with the pH value adjusted to around 3.9 was sealed in a Parr Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and heated at 110 °C for 120 h. After the sample had been slowly cooled to room temperature, colorless crystals were obtained as a homogeneous phase based on powder XRD patterns. Yield: 0.0254 g (26%). Anal.     Heating Treatment

    Solids 1-200, 1-250, and 2-160 were obtained after polycrystalline 1, 1, and 2 had been heated at 200, 250, and 160 °C, respectively, for 2 h under an air atmosphere and then naturally cooled to room temperature.

    Ion-Exchange Experiment

    Synthesis of 1-Ag

    Newly synthesized powder of 1 (0.0281 g, 0.0295 mmol) was added to a solution containing AgNO3 (0.1690 g, 1.00 mmol) and 5.0 mL of ethanol. Then, the mixture was sealed into a Parr Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and heated at 80 °C for 20 h. After the sample had been cooled to room temperature, the final product was filtered, washed with 3 × 50 mL of distilled water and then 3 × 50 mL of ethanol, and dried at ambient temperature. ICP analysis shows that the content of Ag+ in solid 1-Ag is 0.151 mmol g–1. Elemental analyses: C, 20.34; H, 3.72; N, 11.26.

    Synthesis of 1-Li

    Newly synthesized powder of 1 (0.0304 g, 0.0319 mmol) was added to a solution containing LiCl·H2O (0.0610 g, 1.01 mmol) and 6.0 mL of ethanol. Then, the mixture was sealed in a Parr Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and heated at 80 °C for 20 h. After the sample had been cooled to room temperature, the final product was filtered, washed with 2 × 50 mL of distilled water and then 2 × 50 mL of ethanol, and dried under ambient temperature. ICP analysis shows that the content of Li+ in solid 1-Li is 0.30 mmol g–1. Elemental analyses: C, 21.08; H, 3.48; N, 11.57.

    Synthesis of 1-Eu

    Newly synthesized powder of 1 (0.0314 g, 0.0329 mmol) was added to a solution containing Eu(NO3)3·6H2O (0.4560 g, 1.022 mmol) and 6.0 mL of ethanol. Then, the mixture was sealed in a Parr Teflon-lined autoclave (23 mL) and heated at 80 °C for 20 h. After the sample had been been cooled to room temperature, the final product was filtered, washed with 2 × 50 mL of distilled water and then 2 × 50 mL of ethanol, and dried at ambient temperature. ICP analysis shows that the content of Eu3+ in solid 1-Eu is 0.037 mmol g–1. Elemental analyses: C, 20.94; H, 3.88; N, 11.56.

    Synthesis of 1-Cu

    Newly synthesized powder of 1 (0.0414 g, 0.0434 mmol) was soaked in a solution containing CuCl2·2H2O (1.7004 g, 9.9771 mmol) and 5.0 mL of distilled water for 18 days. The color of the powder turned from white into green. After filtration, the green powder was washed with a vast amount of distilled water until the filter liquor was colorless and then dried at ambient temperature. ICP analysis shows that the content of Cu2+ in solid 1-Cu is 1.803 mmol g–1. Elemental analyses: C, 17.72; H, 3.76; N, 9.94.

    Synthesis and Characterization

    Compounds 1 and 2 were synthesized through the reaction of Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O, 2-methylpiperazine, H9L1, and H6L2 under hydrothermal conditions. Powder XRD patterns of compounds 1 and 2 are in agreement with those of simulated single-crystal X-ray data. Furthermore, elemental analyses of compounds 1 and 2 are also in accord with respective calculated values. These results suggest that final products of compounds 1 and 2 are in the homogeneous phase.

    Structural Descriptions

    Single-crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that the asymmetric unit of 1 contains three crystallographically independent Zn(II) ions, one HL18– anion, one protonated 2-methylpiperazine, one coordinated water molecule, and 3.5 free water molecules (Figure 1a). Zn1 is in a distorted [ZnO4] tetrahedral coordination geometry, which is defined by four phosphonate oxygen atoms from four different HL18– anions. Zn2 is surrounded by three HL18– anions in a distorted [ZnO4] tetrahedral coordination geometry. One HL18– anion chelates to Zn2 through two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O2 and O11), resulting in a 12-membered ring (Zn–O–P–C–N–C–N–C–N–C–P–O). The other two HL18– anions interacted with Zn2 through two other phosphonate oxygen atoms (O3b and O8a). Zn3 is surrounded by two HL18– anions and one coordinated water molecule (O16) in a distorted [ZnO4] tetrahedral coordination geometry. One HL18– anion chelates to Zn3 through one phosphonate oxygen atom (O7) and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O9), resulting in an eight-membered ring (Zn–O–P–C–N–C–C–O), while the other HL18– anion interacted with Zn3 through one other carboxylate oxygen atom (O4d). The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.862(8)–1.992(8) Å, which are comparable with those of other reported zinc phosphonates.(52-56, 61, 62)
    On the other hand, the HL18– anion exhibits a hendecadentate mode to combine nine Zn(II) ions through nine phosphonates and two carboxylate oxygen atoms (Figure 1b). To the best of our knowledge, the coordination number of the HL18– anion is larger than those of triazine-based carboxylate ligands.(38-49) The protonated carboxylate atom (O14) provides one hydrogen atom to form a strong hydrogen bond with another carboxylate atom [O14···O10e, 2.547(11) Å], while protonated 2-methylpiperazine provides three hydrogen atoms to form three hydrogen bonds with phosphonate oxygen atoms [N7···O7f, 2.809(16) Å; N8···O3, 2.989(17) Å; N8···O12, 2.930(19) Å]. Meanwhile, the protonated 2-methylpiperazine also hydrogen bonds with one free water molecule [N7···O20, 2.87(6) Å].
    As shown in Figure 1c, [Zn1O4] and [Zn2O4] tetrahedra are bridged by [P1CO3] and [P2CO3] tetrahedra through corner sharing, and [Zn3O4] tetrahedra interact with [Zn1O4] and [Zn2O4] tetrahedra via [P3CO3] tetrahedra through corner sharing. As a result, a wavelike Zn–O–P chain along the a axis is formed. The Zn–O–P chains are further cross-linked by HL18– anions into a 3D framework (Figure 1d,e). The 3D framework contains two types of channels along the a and c axes. The protonated 2-methylpiperazines are encapsulated into the channels through the hydrogen bonds mentioned above between the protonated nitrogen atoms and phosphonate oxygen atoms.
    The asymmetric unit of 2 consists of one Zn(II) ion, one-half of an L26– anion, a half-protonated 2-methylpiperazine, and 1.5 free water molecules (Figure 2a). Zn1 is surrounded by three L26– anions in a distorted [O3ZnN] tetrahedral coordination geometry. One L26– anion chelates to Zn1 through one phosphonate oxygen atom (O1) and a nitrogen donor (N2) of the triazine ring, resulting in a seven-membered ring (Zn–O–P–C–N–C–N), while the other two L26– anions contact Zn1 via one phosphonate oxygen atom (O2a) and one carboxylate oxygen atom (O4b). The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.942(4)–1.971(4) Å, which are comparable with those of other reported zinc phosphonates.(52-56, 61, 62) The Zn1–N2 bond length is 2.022(4) Å. On the other hand, each L26– anion exhibits an octadentate mode to combine six Zn(II) ions through four phosphonates and two carboxylate oxygen atoms, as well as two nitrogen donors of the triazine ring. This is obviously different from those in compound 1, in that the nitrogen donors of the triazine ring do not take part in coordination. As shown in Figure 2b, two neighboring Zn(II) ions are bridged by two O–P–O groups to form a binuclear unit. Each L26– anion connects four neighboring binuclear units. Thus, such binuclear units are cross-linked by L26– anions into a 3D framework (Figure 2c).

    Thermal Stabilities

    TGA and powder XRD measurements were taken to examine thermal stabilities (Figure 3). For solid 1, an obvious stage occurs from room temperature to 150 °C with a 10.5% weight loss, which is attributed to the loss of 4.5 water molecules per formula (calculated value of 9.1%). When the sample is heated further, small weight losses appear until an abrupt stage starts from 290 °C due to the decomposition of the HL18– anion and the protonated 2-methylpiperazine. As a result, the 3D framework collapses. A TGA curve of solid 2 indicates that there is an obvious stage in the temperature range of 40–160 °C with a 7.87% observed weight loss, which corresponds with the release of three free water molecules per formula (calculated value of 7.58%). Then, the subsequent weight loss over 300 °C is assigned to the decomposition of the L26– anion and the protonated 2-methylpiperazine, resulting in the collapse of the 3D framework. Furthermore, powder XRD patterns of solids 1-200 and 2-160 are essentially in agreement with those of as-prepared 1 and 2, respectively. These results indicate that the 3D frameworks of compounds 1 and 2 are stable up to 200 and 160 °C, respectively, under an air atmosphere.

    Luminescenct Properties

    Solid-state luminescent properties were investigated at ambient temperature. Solid 1 displays near-ultraviolet (UV) luminescence with a maximal band at 377 nm (Figure 4). The external quantum yield reaches 2.9% upon excitation at 293 nm. Because both emission and excitation profiles of solid 1 are similar to those of H9L1, the UV emission of solid 1 may be assigned to a ligand-centered π–π* transition.(39) The emission peak of solid 1 shows a 17 nm bathochromic shift in comparison with that of H9L1. Solid 1-200 can also display similar UV emission with a maximal band at 363 nm. This result is in accord with the observation that the 3D framework of solid 1 is stable up to 200 °C under an air atmosphere. Although the structure of solid 1-250 disagrees with that of as-prepared 1, solid 1-250 can also emit similar near-UV luminescence. This indicates that the near-UV luminescence can be preserved after compound 1 is heated at 250 °C for 2 h under an air atmosphere. In addition, solid 1 can display more strong near-UV luminescence with a maximal band at 367 nm at 10 K.
    Solid 2 displays purple luminescence with a maximal band at 402 nm via excitation at 273 nm (Figure 5). The external quantum yield reaches 9.05% upon excitation at 273 nm. The lifetime for λem = 402 nm is 1.82(4) ns. Because both emission and excitation profiles of solid 2 are in agreement with those of H6L2, the purple emission of solid 2 may be assigned to the ligand-centered π–π* transition.(39) The emission peak of solid 2 shows a 41 nm bathochromic shift in comparison with that of H6L2. Furthermore, the purple emission of solid 2 can be preserved after compound 2 is heated at 160 °C for 2 h under an air atmosphere. This result is in accord with the observation that the 3D framework of solid 2 is stable up to 160 °C under an air atmosphere.

    Ion Exchange

    The theoretical ion-exchange capacity of solid 1 is 2.097 mequiv g–1. ICP measurements reveal that the contents of Ag+, Li+, and Eu3+ in solids 1-Ag, 1-Li, and 1-Eu are 0.151, 0.30, and 0.037 mmol g–1, respectively. Correspondingly, the C content of solids 1-Ag, 1-Li, and 1-Eu is less than that of as-prepared 1.     On the basis of new functionalized phosphonate ligands, two 3D zinc phosphonates, namely, [(C5H14N2)Zn3(HL1)(H2O)]·3.5H2O (1) and [(C5H14N2)Zn2L2]·3H2O (2), have been successfully synthesized and structurally characterized.
