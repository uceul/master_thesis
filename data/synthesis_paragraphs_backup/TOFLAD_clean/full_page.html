Abstract

Four cadmium coordination frameworks, namely, {[Cd(L1)(1,4‐ndc)]·1.5H2O}n (1), {[Cd(L1)(2,5‐tpdc)]·H2O}n (2), {[Cd2(L1)2(btc)(Ac)]·8H2O}n (3), and [Cd2(L1)(sdba)2]n (4) [L1 = 4′‐(4‐pyridyl)‐4,2′:6′,4′′‐terpyridine, 1,4‐H2ndc = 1,4‐naphthalene‐dicarboxylic acid, 2,5‐H2tpdc = 2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylic acid, H3btc = benzene‐1,3,5‐tricarboxylic acid, Ac– = acetate, and H2sdba = 4,4′‐sulfonyldicarboxylic acid], were synthesized under hydrothermal/solvothermal conditions. Structural analyses revealed that the four complexes have different three‐dimensional (3D) frameworks due to the different CdII‐multicarboxylate subnets and the coordination modes of the ligand L1. Moreover, the fluorescence properties of 1, 2, and 4 are reported herein.

Introduction

In recent years, the design and synthesis of coordination frameworks (especially for metal‐organic frameworks or porous coordination polymers) are attracting increasing attention in chemistry and material science.1–4 The resulting structures of coordination frameworks are dependent on multiple factors.5 During the past several years, chemists and material scientists have devoted considerable efforts to direct the assemblies of coordination frameworks,6–8 however, how to rationally design and realize the target crystalline materials still remains a fundamental scientific challenge.9,10

Up to now, several effective assembly strategies have been developed.11–14 Among the strategies to construct coordination frameworks, the mixed‐ligand approach is one of the most effective ways to synthesize new complexes with diverse structures and topologies.15–20 N‐donor organic ligands combined with multicarboxylate ligands are usually selected in this approach. The triangular rigid ligand 4′‐(4‐pyridyl)‐4,2′:6′,4′′‐terpyridine (L1) has been rarely used for the construction of metal‐organic supramolecular architectures in coordination polymers.21 L1 is an exotridentate ligand, which is able to form a one dimensional (1D) or two dimensional (2D) 63‐hcb subnet with metal ions according to the different coordination modes. On the other hand, aromatic dicarboxylates or tricarboxylates can serve as good candidates to construct coordination polymers with a wide range of structural diversities and potential applications as functional materials.22

On the basis of the above‐mentioned points, the preparation and crystal structures of four cadmium coordination frameworks, {[Cd(L1)(1,4‐ndc)]·1.5H2O}n (1), {[Cd(L1)(2,5‐tpdc)]·H2O}n (2), {[Cd2(L1)2(btc)(Ac)]·8H2O}n (3), and [Cd2(L1)(sdba)2]n (4) (1,4‐H2ndc = 1,4‐naphthalene‐dicarboxylic acid, 2,5‐H2tpdc = 2,5‐thiophenedicarboxylic acid, H3btc = benzene‐1,3,5‐tricarboxylic acid, Ac– = acetate, and H2sdba = 4,4′‐sulfonyldibenzoato) are described herein. Complexes 1 and 2 are isomorphous and exhibit 3D cds frameworks. Complex 3 has a 3D (3,5)‐connected fsc structure, and the Ac– anions are generated from the decomposition of the solvent N,N‐dimethyl acetamide (DMA). Complex 4 has a 3D twofold interpenetrating network with unique (3,5)‐connected hms topology.

Results and Discussion

Description of Crystal Structures

{[Cd(L1)(1,4‐ndc)]·1.5H2O}n (1) and {[Cd(L1)(2,5‐tpdc)]·H2O}n (2)

Single‐crystal X‐ray analysis indicates that complexes 1 and 2 are isomorphous and crystallize in space group C2/c (see Table 1). Thus, only the structure of complex 1 is discussed herein. As shown in Figure 1a, each CdII ion is hexacoordinated by two nitrogen atoms belonging to L1 ligands [Cd–N 2.343(6)–2.356(7) Å], and four oxygen atoms from three 1,4‐ndc2– anions with the Cd–O bond lengths in the range of 2.190(5)–2.498(6) Å, showing a trigonal prismatic arrangement. In 1, all 1,4‐ndc2– anions adopt a similar coordination mode, however, two carboxylate groups of each 1,4‐ndc2– anion exhibit different coordination fashions (monodentate bridged and bidentate chelated‐monodentate bridged). The adjacent CdII ions are bridged by 1,4‐ndc2– ligands to form a dinuclear Cd2 unit with Cd···Cd distance of 3.842 Å. Furthermore, the neighboring Cd2 units are connected by 1,4‐ndc2– anions to form a 1D double chain structure (Scheme 1a), and a similar 1D chain structure was also observed in 2 (Scheme 1b). Notably, 1D double chains are arranged in the different fashion for whole 3D framework (Figure 1b). In complex 1, L1 ligand exhibits bidentate bridged coordination mode (Scheme 2a), which connected the Cd‐1,4‐ndc2– chains along the different direction leading to the formation of a 3D pillar‐layer framework (Figure 1c). To provide further insight into the structures of 1 or 2, it is necessary to simplify the node‐and‐linker reference nets from the topological point of view. Each Cd2 unit is linked to other four Cd2 units by L1 ligands and 1,4‐ndc2– anions or 2,5‐tpdc2– anions in 1 or 2, respectively (Scheme 3a and Scheme 3b). Thus, the Cd2 units can be considered as 4‐connected nodes. As a result, the overall 3D networks of 1 or 2 can be rationalized as a 4‐connected net with Schläfli symbol (65·8) (topological type is cds or CdSO4, TD10 = 1489) (Figure 1d).

{[Cd2(L1)2(btc)(Ac)]·8H2O}n (3)

The H3btc ligand was employed with the intention to observe the effect of tridentate ligand on the assembly of the Cd‐L1 complex. In 3, Ac– anions act as a charge balance ligand, generated from the decomposition of solvent DMA. Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis revealed that 3 crystallizes in space group P21/c, and the coordination environment around CdII is shown in Figure 2a. The selected bond lengths and angles are summarized in Table S1 (Supporting Information). There are two crystallographically independent CdII ions. Cd(1) ion is seven‐coordinate supplied by three oxygen atoms from the carboxylate groups of two unique btc3– anions, two oxygen atoms from Ac– anion, and two nitrogen atoms belonging to L1 ligands. The Cd(2) ion is coordinated by three oxygen atoms of btc3– anions, one oxygen atom from Ac– anion, and two nitrogen atoms of L1 ligands. The Cd–O bond lengths were in the range of 2.194(5)–2.481(6) Å and the Cd–N bond lengths range from 2.319(7) to 2.378(7) Å. Three carboxylate groups of btc3– anion adopts different coordination mode: chelating bidentate and bridging bidentate. Two neighboring CdII ions in 3 are connected by carboxylate groups of btc3– anion and in situ generated Ac– anion to form a Cd2 unit. Further the dinuclear Cd2 units are linked by three btc3– anions, leading to the construction of a 2D subnet tectons with a 63‐hcb net (Scheme 1c). Interestingly, there are two kinds of Cd–btc–Ac ring structures. Similar to complexes 1 and 2, all L1 molecules act as bridging bidentate ligands linking CdII ions to form the 1D chain pillar. Furthermore, the layers are pillared by theCd‐L1 chains to give a 3D framework (Figure 2b). If the Cd2 units are considered as 5‐connected nodes, and the btc3–anions are considered as 3‐connected nodes (Scheme 3c), the whole 3D structure exhibits a (3,5)‐connected fsc topology with the Schäfli symbol (4·62)(4·66·83) (TD10 = 1143) (Figure 2c).23,24

[Cd2(L1)(sdba)2]n (4)

To evaluate the effect of “V” type dicarboxylate ligand on the framework formation of complex, we selected H2sdba to react with cadmium salt in the presence of the same L1 ligand and obtained complex 4. Complex 4 crystallizes in space group P, and the coordination environment around CdII is shown in Figure 3a. In the asymmetric unit, there are two crystallographically independent CdII ions. The Cd(1) ion exhibits a slightly distorted octahedral coordination environment, which supplied by two pyridyl nitrogen atoms of two L1 ligands and four oxygen atoms from the carboxylate groups of four unique sdba2– anions. However, the Cd(2) ion is five‐coordinate, defined by four oxygen atoms from sdba2– anions and one nitrogen atom ofthe L1 ligand. One important structural feature of complex 4 is that it possesses a “paddle wheel” Cd2 unit, which is constituted by sdba2– anions. Moreover, the Cd2 units are connected by sdba2– anions to form a double chain (Scheme 1d). Different from complexes 1–3, all nitrogen atoms of L1 ligand were coordinated to CdII ions in 4, and the neighboring CdII ions are connected by L1 to form a 1D chain (Scheme 2b). Meanwhile, the third CdII ion linking by the same L1 ligand is attached to one side of the 1D chain. View of the whole network, the Cd2 units in turn are 3‐connected by L1 ligands to form a “honeycomb‐like” net with short topological terms of 63‐hcb (Figure 3b). Finally, complex 4 exhibits a 3D framework constructed from 2D Cd2‐L1 network and 1D Cd2‐sdba2– chain (Figure 3c), if the L1 ligands are considered as 3‐connected nodes and the Cd2 units are considered as 5‐connected nodes, respectively (Scheme 3d). As a result, the overall 3D network of 4 can be rationalized as a binodal (3,5)‐connected hms net with Schläfli symbol (63)(69·8) (TD10 = 1174). Careful inspection of the structure of complex 4 suggests that it is a twofold interpenetrating 3D framework, and the resulting twofold interpenetrating network belongs to class IIa (Figure 3d).

Furthermore, π–π stacking interactions between the aromatic rings of L1 ligand within the 3D network of four complexes are found [Cg–Cg, distance between ring centroids, is in the range of 3.716(6) to 3.795(5) Å for 1, 3.601(5) to 3.761(5) Å for 2, 3.653(1) to 3.697(8) Å for 3, and 3.766(5) to 3.883(5) Å for 4]. The weak noncovalent interactions also play an important role in the formation and stabilization of the final structure.

Fluorescence Properties

Luminescence is of great importance in photochemistry and photophysics.25 CdII complexes are a potential class of functional materials with interesting luminescence properties.26 The fluorescence spectra of complexes 1, 2, and 4 were examined in the solid state at room temperature (Figure 4). The free L1 ligand displays emission at ca. 380 nm (λex = 310 nm). This emission bands of L1 can probably be assigned to the π→π* transitions. For complexes, it is clear that there are emission bands at ca. 523 nm for 1, ca. 526 nm for 2, and ca. 373 nm for 4 under excitation at 310 nm, respectively. Comparing with the emission peak of L1 ligand, an obvious red shift was observed in complexes 1 and 2. Therefore, the fluorescence behavior of complexes 1 and 2 are best ascribed to ligand‐to‐metal charge‐transfer (LMCT) according to the literature.27–29 Complex 4 exhibits main emission bands at 373 nm, and this emissions may probably be assigned to the intraligand (n→π* or π→π*) transfer since similar emission was observed for the free L1 ligand.30 Complex 4 was found to show the highest energy emission relative to complexes 1, 2, and L1 ligand, which may be attributed to the different rigidity of the crystal packing in the solid state.16,17,31

Conclusions

Four CdII coordination frameworks based on terpyridine and multicarboxylate ligands were synthesized under hydrothermal/solvothermal conditions. Structural analyses revealed that the four complexes possess different 3D frameworks due to the different CdII‐multicarboxylate subnets and coordination modes of L1 ligand. The strong fluorescence properties of complexes 1, 2, and 4 reveal that they may be excellent candidates for potential functional solid materials.

Experimental Section

Materials and Measurements: All the chemicals used for synthesis are of analytical grade and commercially available·L1 was synthesized according to the literature.32 IR spectra were measured with a Tensor 27 OPUS (Bruker) FT‐IR spectrometer with KBr pellets. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) spectra were recorded with a Rigaku D/Max‐2500 diffractometer at 40 Kv, 100 mA for a Cu‐target tube and a graphite monochromator. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis was carried out with a Rigaku standard TG‐DTA analyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 from ambient temperature to 800 °C, an empty Al2O3 crucible was used as reference. Fluorescent spectra were recorded at room temperature with a Hitachi F‐4500 fluorescence/phosphorescence spectrophotometer.

After the autoclave was cooled to room temperature at 10 °C·h–1, pale yellow block crystals were collected by filtration, washed with 3 × 10 mL of deionized water and air‐dried. X‐ray Crystallography: X‐ray single‐crystal diffraction data of all complexes were collected with a Rigaku SCX‐mini diffractometer with graphite monochromatic Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The program CrystalClear was used for integration of the diffraction profiles. All structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares methods with SHELXL.33 Metal atoms were located from the E‐maps and other non‐hydrogen atoms excluded in conterions were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. The hydrogen atoms were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. The diffuse electron densities resulting from these residual solvent molecules of complex 3 were removed from the data set using the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON and refined further using the data generated.34 All the crystal data and structure refinement details for the four complexes are given in Table 1. The data of relevant bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).
