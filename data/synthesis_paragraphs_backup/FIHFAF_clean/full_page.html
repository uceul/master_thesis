New amine-functionalized cobalt cluster-based frameworks with open metal sites and suitable pore sizes: multipoint interactions enhanced CO2 sorption†

Bo Liu‡, Ruili Zhao‡, Kefen Yue*, Jingtao Shi, Yang Yu and Yaoyu Wang
Key Laboratory of Synthetic and Natural Functional Molecule Chemistry of the Ministry of Education, Shaanxi Key Laboratory of Physico-Inorganic Chemistry, College of Chemistry & Materials Science, Northwest University, Xi'an 710069, China. E-mail: ykflyy@nwu.edu.cn

Received 14th May 2013 , Accepted 16th July 2013

First published on 16th July 2013

By employing the mixed-linker synthetic strategy, two new cobalt(II) cluster-based frameworks, [Co2.5(btc)(atz)2(Hatz)(DMF)]·2DMF (1) and [Co2.5(btc)(Hbtc)0.5(atz)(CH3CN)(H2O)]·H2O (2), constructed from the same initial materials Co(NO3)2, 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3btc) and 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (Hatz), have been synthesized by solvothermal reactions. Crystal-structure analyses demonstrated that 1 and 2 are three-dimensional (3D) porous frameworks based on Co-triazolate chain/layer and tricarboxylate pillars. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and power X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements showed that both of them have high thermal stability and good water stability. Remarkably, both the structures of desolvated 1 and 2 contain suitable pore sizes and highly polar channel systems functionalized by amino groups, open metal sites, carbonyl or free carboxylic acid sites and uncoordinated triazolate nitrogen atoms on the pore surfaces, exhibiting multipoint interactions between CO2 molecules and frameworks, resulting in high CO2 uptake and selectivity for CO2 over N2.

Introduction

Selective capture and efficient removal of CO2 from industrial flue gas is becoming a severe environmental issue due to the escalated global warming.1 Porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are rapidly emerging as a new class of porous material which show promise for their application as CO2 capture and separation materials and have attracted considerable attention owing to their well-defined chemical structures and facilely porous modification.2 To be a CO2 capture material, the MOF should have high selectivity and adsorption capacity of CO2 at ambient temperature and lower pressures, the lower cost of synthesis and the stability toward water vapor,3 not only based on big pore size and large pore volume alone with high CO2 uptake at low temperature or high pressure. In efforts to develop better porous MOFs for such application, one of the ongoing endeavour is enhancing the CO2 binding affinity in MOFs;4 some strategies, for example, generation of open metal sites, forming charged frameworks, and the incorporation of specific polar functional groups (–NH2, –OH, –CF3, etc.) within the porous frameworks have been investigated to augment the CO2 adsorptive affinity and selectivity.5 Thus, how to create such porous environment with rich interacting sites for CO2 molecules in a MOF structure has therefore become a hot topic.6 Although great progress in gas storage capacity and adsorption selectivity of MOFs has been made, seeking new strategies to further improve the gas storage capacity of MOF materials is still a great challenge.

Recent studies have demonstrated that multipoint interactions could effectively enhance the CO2 adsorption capacity of MOFs when they exist among adsorbed CO2 molecules and the wall surface of channels in microporous MOFs.7 On the other hand, to obtain novel targeted structures and excited properties of MOFs, several effective synthetic strategies such as ‘node-and-spacer’ and ‘secondary building units’ (SBUs) have been successfully established and widely developed.8 The mixed-linker synthetic strategy, which can combine the advantages of two such types of ligands,9 has been applied to design and prepare a variety of typical crystalline materials with attractive network architectures and potential applications in gas storage, heterogeneous catalysis, and light harvesting. By employing the mixed-linker synthetic strategy, in this work, we present the cooperative assembly of mixed 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid and 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole with Co(II) centers into two 3D cobalt(II) clusters microporous frameworks, [Co2.5(btc)(atz)2(Hatz)(DMF)]·2DMF (1) and [Co2.5(btc)(Hbtc)0.5(atz)(CH3CN)(H2O)]·H2O (2), constructed on the basis of the same reactants through changing the reaction solvents. Interestingly, as we expect that, both of them represent rare examples containing multipoint interactions sites in the wall surface of channels. The adsorption properties of 1 and 2 were investigated by N2, H2 and CO2 gases and both complexes exhibited high CO2 uptake and remarkable selectivity of CO2 over N2.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All reagents and solvents were commercially available and were used without further purification. Infrared spectra were obtained in KBr discs on a Nicolet Avatar 360 FTIR spectrometer in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses of C, H, and N were performed with a Perkin Elmer 2400C Elemental Analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in nitrogen stream using a Netzsch TG209F3 equipment at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray powder diffractometer (Cu Kα, 1.5418 Å). The sorption isotherms were measured with an automatic volumetric adsorption apparatus (Micrometrics ASAP 2020M).

Synthesis of [Co2.5(btc)(atz)2(Hatz)(DMF)]·2DMF (1)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (1.0 mmol, 0.293 g), H3btc (1 mmol, 0.210 g) and Hatz (1 mmol, 0.084 g) in DMF (8 mL) and water (2 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (15 mL) and heated at 150 °C for 60 h, and then cooled to 100 °C at a rate of 5 °C min−1. It was kept constant at 100 °C for 8 hours, it was then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C min−1. The resulting deep purple block crystals of 1 were isolated by washing with DMF–H2O, and dried in air (yield: 83%, based on H3btc). Anal. Calcd for C24H33N15O9Co2.5: C, 35.03; H, 4.04; N, 25.53. Found: C, 34.85; H, 4.21; N, 25.62.

Synthesis of [Co2.5(btc)(Hbtc)0.5(atz)(CH3CN)(H2O)]·H2O (2)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (1.0 mmol, 0.293 g), H3btc (1.5 mmol, 0.315 g) and Hatz (1 mmol, 0.084 g) in CH3OH (2 mL), CH2CN (4 mL) and H2O (4 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (15 mL) and heated at 150 °C for 60 h, and then cooled to 100 °C at a rate of 5 °C min−1. It was kept constant at 100 °C for 8 hours, then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C min−1. The resulting reddish block crystals of 2 were isolated by washing with ethanol, and dried in air (yield: 79%, based on H3btc). Anal. Calcd for C17.5H13N5O11Co2.5: C, 34.09; H, 2.13; N, 11.36. Found: C, 33.98; H, 2.19; N, 11.26.

Crystallography

Diffraction data were collected with a Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) on a Bruker-AXS SMART CCD area detector diffractometer. Absorption corrections were carried out utilizing SADABS routine.10 The structures were solved by the direct methods and refined by full matrix least squares refinements based on F2. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically with the hydrogen atoms added to their geometrically ideal positions and refined isotropically. In 2, the H atom of one carboxylate group is not added due to the disordered oxygen atoms. The contribution of the disordered solvent molecules was subtracted from the reflection data by the SQUEEZE method as implemented in PLATON11 program. The final formulas for 1 and 2 were determined by combining single-crystal structures, elemental microanalyses and TGA data. Results and discussion

Synthesis and crystal structure

Solvothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2·6H2O, H3btc and Hatz in a mixed solvent of DMF–H2O afforded deep purple block crystals of 1 with a high synthetic yield, which crystallizes in the triclinic space group P, showing a 3D noninterpenetrated framework built on 1D Co-triazolate chains and btc3− linkers. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains two and a half cobalt atoms, one deprotonated btc3−, two atz−, one Hatz and one coordinated DMF molecule (Fig. 1a). Three kinds of central Co atoms are found. Co1 is coordinated by four 1,2-position N atoms from four atz− and two Hatz, forming a distorted octahedral geometry. Co2 shows a distorted tetrahedral geometry surrounded by two O atoms from two btc3− and two 4-position N atoms from two atz−. Co3 is also six-coordinated by three 1,2-position N atoms from two atz− and one Hatz, two O atoms from one btc3− and one O atom of DMF. All the H3btc in 1 are deprotonated and adopted one bridging coordination fashion (Fig. S1, ESI†), in which the carboxylate groups exhibit η1:η0:μ1 and η1:η1:μ1. Interestingly, the Hatz displays two coordination fashions and the amino groups remain free, one of them (atz−) shows a three linker connecting with Co1, Co2 and Co3 through 1,2,4-position N atoms, and the other one (Hatz) is connecting to Co1 and Co3 through 1,2-position N atoms as a bridged linker. Noteworthy, such interlinkage between the Hatz and Co atoms generates a 1D Co-atz chain along the b-axis (Fig. 1b). Each chain is connected with six neighbouring ones by btc3− linkers to form a 3D framework. This arrangement leads to two different sizes rectangular channels generated along the b-axis (Fig. 1c), in which the pore openings are about 4.1 × 4.7 and 6.8 × 8.1 Å2 (excluding van der Waals radii of the atoms) respectively. Upon removing the DMF molecules, 1 forms a microporous framework containing 45.6% solvent void accessible.
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environment of Co2+ ions in 1, (b) the 1D Co-atz chain, and (c) the 3D framework of 1.

Upon changing the reaction solvents, reddish block crystals of 2 belonging to the C2/c space group were successfully obtained, showing a 3D framework built on 2D Co-triazolate layers and btc3− linkers. Similar to 1, there are also three types of central Co atoms in 2, and all of them display six-coordinated with distorted octahedral geometry (Fig. 2a). Co1 is bonded with one 1-position N atom from one atz−, four O atoms from two btc3−, one Hbtc2− and one O atom of coordinated water molecule. Co2 is connected to one 4-position N atom from one atz− and one N atom from one MeCN, four O atoms from two btc3−, one Hbtc2− and one solvent water molecule. Co3 is coordinated by two 2-position N atoms from two atz− and four O atoms from two btc3− and two Hbtc2−. The H3btc and Hatz adopt different coordination modes with Co2+ ions in 2 compared to 1, the H3btc in 2 adopt two coordination fashions (Fig. S2, ESI†), one of them (btc3−) connects with five Co2+ ions, in which the carboxylate groups exhibit η1:η0:μ1 and η1:η1:μ2, the other one (Hbtc2−) is connecting to six Co2+ ions, in which two carboxylate groups exhibit the same link type η2:η2:μ3 and the remaining carboxylate groups free. All the atz− adopt the same coordination fashion which as a three linker bridges three Co2+ ions through 1,2,4-position N atoms, while the amino groups remain free. As shown in Fig. 2a, the neighboring Co2+ ions are connected by the carboxylate groups to form a linear pentanuclear cluster (Co5). Each Co5 cluster is ligated to four adjacent Co5 by four atz− via Co–N bonds to induce a 2D layer (Fig. 2b), and adjacent layers are connected by btc3− and Hbtc2− to form a 3D framework (Fig. 2c) with two different sizes (3.5 × 5.3 and 5.0 × 5.8 Å2) open channels generated along the c-axis. A total of 32.2% of solvent accessible volume for 2 was estimated by using the PLATON program upon removing the MeCN and H2O molecules.


Fig. 2 (a) The coordination environment of Co2+ ions in 2, (b) the 2D Co-atz layer, and (c) the 3D structure of 2.

The formation of MOFs is significantly influenced by the reaction temperature, solvent, pH value, and so on. Comparing the structural difference between 1 and 2, we could conclude that the solvents play an important role in influencing the crystallization of porous MOFs. Noticeably, the key feature of the frameworks for 1 and 2 is that the free –NH2, –CO/–COOH, and uncoordinated triazolate N atom are exposed on the pore wall which should be very useful in the enhancement of the adsorption ability for CO2.

PXRD and thermal analysis

The PXRD patterns of the bulk samples of both 1 and 2 indicate a good agreement with the simulated patterns based on X-ray crystallography, showing the phase purity of the as-synthesized products (Fig. S3, ESI†). TGA reveals that 1 loses all the solvents below 270 °C under N2 atmosphere, with a total weight loss of a 25.2%, corresponding to 3DMF per formula unit (calc. 24.5%) (Fig. S4, ESI†), then followed by a plateau of stability from 270–360 °C, whereupon immediately by the framework collapse with an abrupt weight loss. For 2, the preliminary weight loss of 11.5% beginning at 60–250 °C corresponds to the removal of 1CH3CN and 2H2O per formula unit (calc. 11.4%). Above 340 °C, a further heating induces an abrupt weight loss owing to the decomposition of 2. All the guest and coordinated molecules in 1 and 2 can be completely removed via heating at 170 °C for 6 h and subsequent 250 °C for 2 h under high vacuum, which are verified by the TGA of the desolvated samples 1 and 2, no weight loss occurs below 360 and 340 °C, respectively, thus giving two porous frameworks leaving exposed Co2+ centers in the porous surface. Both the desolvated 1 and 2 remain crystalline with PXRD patterns resembling the diffraction patterns of the single crystal, only showing a slightly weakening and shifting of some peaks, implying slight framework distortion due to the solvent release. Moreover, accompanied with pronounced colour change from original deep purple to red for 1 and reddish to maroon for 2 after the dehydration, is indicative of a structure change upon removal of solvent molecules (Fig. 3). Furthermore, we also measured the water stability of 1 and 2, which demonstrated that the framework integrity could be retained in water at room temperature even after one week. While the desolvated samples of 1 and 2 are collapsed after immersing in water for two days.

Gas sorption properties

Based on the above discussion, 1 and 2 constitute the suitable effective free volumes, the most thermally stable frameworks and the solvent molecules can be completely evacuated to leave a robust porosity. Hence, here, we selected 1 and 2 to study the gas adsorption property. To assess the permanent porosities of 1 and 2, the N2 sorption isotherms were performed at 77 K, which display type-I isotherms with apparent Langmuir surface areas of 609 and 471 m2 g−1, the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas of 465 and 356 m2 g−1, and the pore volumes estimated by the Dubinin–Astakhov (DA) method are 0.21 and 0.17 cm3 g−1, respectively (Fig. 4). The desorption hysteresis is related to the narrow channel systems of both 1 and 2, which hints the escape of adsorbed gas molecules,12 as well as probably involving the structural breathing of the framework during the adsorption–desorption progress.13 Using the Horvath–Kawazoe model, a median pore width of 5.4 and 4.8 Å are available, consistent with the X-ray analysis for 1 and 2, respectively. Meanwhile, H2 sorption isotherms were also collected at 77 K and 1 atm. 1 adsorbs 158.7 cm3 g−1 (1.41 wt%) H2 which is relatively high for pores MOFs.14 Compared with MOF-5 (1.35 wt%) and MAF-7 (1.38 wt%) under the same conditions.15 The H2 uptake of 2 (65.5 cm3 g−1, 0.58 wt%) is relatively smaller than the best zeolite ZSM-5 (0.7 wt%).16 It notes that the hydrogen sorption isotherms display remarkable hysteresis between adsorption and desorption curves, indicating strong interaction between H2 and host frameworks.17 We consider that both the small pore sizes and the more polar pore surfaces of 1 and 2 may contribute to the large hydrogen sorption hysteresis and enhance H2 uptakes.18
Fig. 4 N2 and H2 sorption isotherms for desolvated 1 and 2 at 77 K.

To further explore the reliable pore characteristic of 1 and 2, CO2 adsorption were measured at 195 K (Fig. 5). The CO2 adsorption isotherms of both 1 and 2 display steep up takes in the low-pressure region and show significant high adsorption amounts of CO2 at 760 Torr (174.4 cm3 g−1, 34.3 wt% for 1; 134.0 cm3 g−1, 26.3 wt% for 2). The values of CO2 uptakes are much higher than those of recently reported zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIF-68–ZIF-82)19 which have larger pore sizes (7.1 to 15.9 Å) and comparable with some high pore volume MOFs.2b,c The exceptional adsorption capacity of both 1 and 2 for CO2 encourages us to further explore its potential properties on CO2/N2 gas separation under ambient conditions. The CO2 sorption isotherms at 273 and 295 K and N2 sorption isotherms at 295 K were also measured for 1 and 2. The CO2 uptakes of 1 at 1 atm are 103.2 cm3 g−1 (20.3 wt%) at 273 K and 77.3 cm3 g−1 (15.2 wt%) at 295 K (Fig. 5a), for 2, the CO2 uptakes are 66.2 cm3 g−1 (13.0 wt%) at 273 K and 38.6 cm3 g−1 (7.58 wt%) at 295 K (Fig. 5b). Both frameworks display preferential adsorption of CO2 over N2 (1.2 cm3 g−1 for 1 and 0.6 cm3 g−1 for 2 at 295 K), and show remarkably high CO2 uptakes comparable with those well examined values in recently reported frameworks with multifunctional groups and volumes, such as open metal sites (OMSs) and Lewis basic sites (LBSs).20 The enhanced CO2 uptakes of 1 and 2 should be attributed to their free –NH2 groups, the exposed Co2+ sites, stemming from the release of coordinated DMF–MeCN, –CO/–COOH sites, and high density of uncoordinated triazolate nitrogen atoms within the pore surfaces. Calculating the ratios of adsorption amounts by employing weight percentage, the CO2/N2 adsorption selectivities of 1 and 2 at 295 K are 93 and 101, respectively. To our knowledge, MOFs with such high CO2 selectivities over N2 are rarely reported.21 It should be noted that, at 0.16 atm, which is a typical partial pressure of CO2 in industrial flue gas, the ratios of CO2/N2 adsorption amounts of 1 and 2 are up to 139 and 199, respectively, and the CO2 loading are 26.5 cm3 g−1 (52.05 mg g−1) for 1 and 15.3 cm3 g−1 (30.05 mg g−1) for 2. The high ratios of CO2/N2 are comparable or even superior to that of many MOFs with highly polar functional sites in the pores.22 Moreover, using the adsorption isotherms measured at 273 and 295 K (Fig. S6, ESI†), the enthalpies of CO2 adsorption at zero coverage were calculated to be 29.9 and 30.6 kJ mol−1 for 1 and 2, respectively (Fig. 5c), reflecting a strong CO2–framework interaction.


Fig. 5 Gas sorption isotherms of desolvated 1 (a) and 2 (b) for N2 at 295 K, and CO2 at 195, 273 and 295 K, respectively, (c) the isosteric heat of CO2 adsorption for desolvated 1 and 2 estimated by the virial equation from the adsorption isotherms at 273 and 295 K.

The significant CO2 sorption selectivity over N2 in both 1 and 2 can be mainly associated with the differences in the electrostatic interactions between porous surface and adsorbate. In 1 and 2, besides that the –NH2 and –CO/–COOH functional groups in the pores are more attractive to CO2 than other gases due to forming strong quadrupole–quadrupole interactions,23 the exposed Co2+ sites and uncoordinated triazolate N atom lined in the porous surfaces can give rise to an electric field interacting with the quadrupole molecules, which induces strong interactions between framework and CO2 because of its much larger quadrupole moment (−1.4 × 10−39 C m2) than N2 (−4.7 × 10−40 C m2). In addition, the smaller kinetic diameter of CO2 (3.30 Å) than N2 (3.64 Å) enables it to enter easily into the pore. On the other hand, the framework–CO2 interactions can be augmented in small pores, owing to the overlap of potential fields from multiple sides of the pore walls which results in a higher affinity for CO2.24 Moreover, CO2 molecules are forced to be closer together in smaller pores, which causes stronger CO2–CO2 interactions and give additional contribution for enhancing the CO2 uptake.

Conclusions

In summary, two cluster-based microporous frameworks have been rationally constructed by employing the mixed-linker synthetic strategy, which exhibit high CO2 uptake and remarkable selectivity of CO2 over N2 at 295 K. This work makes a good case that introducing –NH2, –CO/–COOH, open metal sites and uncoordinated triazolate N atom within one porous structure with suitable pore spaces to construct an efficient CO2 selective sorption material, which makes it a promising candidate for the capture of CO2 from industrial flue gas or the removal of CO2 from natural gas.
