Rigid versus semi-rigid bis(imidazole) ligands in the assembly of two Co(II) coordination polymers: structural variability, electrochemical properties and photocatalytic behavior†

Jing-Wang Cui a, Suo-Xia Hou a, Kristof Van Hecke b and Guang-Hua Cui *a
aCollege of Chemical Engineering, Hebei Key Laboratory for Environment Photocatalytic and Electrocatalytic Material, North China University of Science and Technology, Tangshan, Hebei 063009, P. R. China. E-mail: tscghua@126.com; Fax: +86-315-2592170; Tel: +86-315-2592169
bXStruct, Department of Inorganic and Physical Chemistry, Ghent University, Krijgslaan 281-S3, 9000 Ghent, Belgium

Received 7th December 2016 , Accepted 31st January 2017

First published on 31st January 2017

The hydrothermal reactions of 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic acid (H4L) with CoCl2·2H2O and rigid or semi-rigid bis(imidazole) ligands were able to generate two Co(II) coordination polymers (CPs), {[Co5(L)2(1,4-bimb)(μ3-OH)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (1), {[Co(L)0.5(1,4-bib)]·H2O}n (2) (1,4-bimb = 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, 1,4-bib = 1,4-bis(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzene). CPs 1 and 2 were structurally characterized by elemental analysis, IR spectroscopy, X-ray powder diffraction and single crystal X-ray diffraction. CP 1 features a 3D 3,3,4,4,5-connected framework with an unprecedented {42·8·102·12}{43·6·86}2{43}2{46}2{62·8}2 topology, which represents the first example of CPs with such a topology. CP 2 possesses a three-fold interpenetration 3D framework with mog topology. The distinct structures of the two CPs may result from diverse coordination modes of the (L)4− ligands and different structural characteristics of rigid or semi-rigid N-donor ligands. The thermal stabilities, photoluminescence properties and electrochemical behavior in the solid state for CPs 1 and 2 have been investigated. The photophysical studies indicated that CPs 1 and 2 are potential semiconductive materials. Moreover, both CPs 1 and 2 show high photocatalytic efficiency for the degradation of methylene blue (MB) under UV light irradiation and exhibit good stability and recyclability. A possible photocatalytic mechanism is speculated by introducing t-butyl alcohol (TBA) as a widely used ˙OH scavenger.

Introduction

Currently, organic dyes have become a global concern because of their adverse effects on the human health and environment.1–4 Most of them are highly toxic and difficult to biodegrade effectively.5–7 So far, there are lots of powerful methods for the removal of organic dyes, for instance, adsorption, precipitation and coagulation.8–10 Nevertheless, these traditional techniques have potential limitations because they simply transfer pollutants from one phase to another or concentrate pollutants in one phase rather than degrading them.8,11 Therefore, effective and economical degradation techniques need to be developed to eliminate organic pollutants completely. In recent years, the development of photocatalysis has attracted much attention to degrade organic pollutants from wastewater under UV light irradiation. This method can decompose a wide range of organic pollutants into easily biodegradable compounds or less toxic molecules.12 In this regard, some coordination polymers (CPs) have already been demonstrated to be efficient photocatalysts for the degradation of organic dyes.13–16

The rational design and synthesis of CPs have been of great interest in coordination chemistry and crystal engineering, not only for the intriguing topological structures encountered, but also for their potential applications as functional materials in catalysis, fluorescence, gas adsorption/separation and magnetism.17–22 Among numerous strategies for constructing CPs, the self-assembly of transition metal salts with N-donor and polycarboxylate mixed ligands under solvothermal conditions is an effective method widely adopted in this field. However, it is well known that many intrinsic and external factors, including the coordination nature of metal centers, structural characteristics of organic ligands, temperature, solvent, pH value, molar ratio, and so on, may greatly influence the formation of the resulting CPs.23–25 Of all the influencing factors, the design and selection of appropriate organic ligands are the most important, and in some cases, a subtle alteration of organic motifs may lead to a novel architecture.26–28

Organic polycarboxylate ligands, as multidentate O-donor ligands, have been extensively used in the preparation of new CPs.29–31 In the family of polycarboxylates, rigid polycarboxylates, such as 1,3-benzenedicarboxylic acid (H2bdc), 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylic acid (H2btec), 4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylic acid (H2bpdc) and 2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylic acid (H2ndc), are of special interest. In contrast, only a number of CPs modified by flexible polycarboxylates have been reported to date. Recently, more and more research has been focused on the use of flexible polycarboxylate ligands, which are characterized by their versatile coordination modes and good coordination ability to metal centers.32,33 In particular, they can show more versatility through their flexible conformations compared to rigid polycarboxylates.34,35 Among them, flexible 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic acid (H4L) is a good candidate for the construction of CPs with attractive structures and topologies. For example, Tong's group obtained two metal 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylate frameworks showing spin-canted antiferromagnetic behavior.36 Additionally, neutral N-donor ligands have attracted increasing attention in assembly processes, which may modify the final architectures and physical properties of the resulting CPs.37–39 Until now, a great number of CPs based on bis(imidazole) derivatives have been reported by several groups.40–42 However, the investigation of the effect of rigid and semi-rigid bis(imidazole) ligands on the formation of the resulting CPs is still scarce so far. Thus, in this paper we opted for 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene (1,4-bimb) and 1,4-bis(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzene (1,4-bib) as main ligands to investigate how the different structural characteristics of N-donor ligands influence the structures and properties of the ternary mixed Co(II) CPs. Furthermore, no crystal data with the rigid or semi-rigid bis(imidazole) ligands and 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic acid (H4L) have been reported yet, according to the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD, version 5.37, May 2016 plus 2 update).43

Herein, two new Co(II) coordination polymers, namely, {[Co5(L)2(1,4-bimb)(μ3-OH)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (1) and {[Co(L)0.5(1,4-bib)]·H2O}n (2) were obtained under hydrothermal conditions. CP 1 features a 3D 3,3,4,4,5-connected framework with an unprecedented {42·8·102·12}{43·6·86}2{43}2{46}2{62·8}2 topology, while CP 2 possesses a three-fold interpenetration 3D framework with mog topology. The effects of diverse coordination modes of the H4L ligands and different structural characteristics of the N-donor ligands on the final structures and properties of the target CPs were investigated. The thermal stability, photoluminescence properties and electrochemical behavior of CPs 1 and 2 have been investigated in detail. In addition, both CPs 1 and 2 show a high photocatalytic degradation of methylene blue (MB) under UV light irradiation and exhibit good stability and recyclability.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

1,4-bimb and 1,4-bib were synthesized according to literature procedures.1 All other chemicals were obtained commercially and used without further purification. Elemental analysis (C, H, and N) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C Elemental Analyzer. X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) analysis was carried out with a Rigaku D/Max-2500PC X-ray diffractometer using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 0.1542 nm) and ω–2θ scan mode at 293 K. The FT-IR spectra were recorded on KBr discs using an Avatar 360 (Nicolet) spectrophotometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed using a NETZSCH TG 209 thermal analyzer from room temperature to 800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under an N2 atmosphere. The fluorescence spectra were measured with a Hitachi F-7000 spectrophotometer at room temperature. The electrochemical analysis was carried out using a CHI 660E electrochemical workstation (Chenhua Instrument, Shanghai, China). The solid-state UV/vis spectra were measured at room temperature using a UV-vis spectrophotometer (Puxi, TU-1901), and a BaSO4 plate was used as the standard. The absorptivity value of methylene blue (MB) was recorded with a UV-vis spectrophotometer (Puxi, TU-1901) at the maximum wavelengths of 664 nm and 497 nm.

Synthesis of {[Co5(L)2(1,4-bimb)(μ3-OH)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (1)

A mixture containing CoCl2·2H2O (0.2 mmol, 47 mg), H4L (0.2 mmol, 45 mg), 1,4-bimb (0.2 mmol, 47 mg), NaOH (0.4 mmol, 16 mg) and H2O (10 mL) was placed in a 25 mL Teflon autoclave reactor and heated at 140 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure. Then the mixture was cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. Purple block-like single crystals of CP 1 were obtained in about 55% yield based on Co. Anal. Synthesis of {[Co(L)0.5(1,4-bib)]·H2O}n (2)

CP 2 was obtained as purple block-like single crystals by a similar route to that used for the isolation of CP 1, starting from CoCl2·2H2O (0.2 mmol, 47 mg), H4L (0.2 mmol, 45 mg), 1,4-bib (0.2 mmol, 42 mg), NaOH (0.4 mmol, 16 mg) and H2O (10 mL). X-ray crystallography

Crystallographic data of CPs 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker Smart CCD diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) by using a ω-scan technique. The hydrogen atoms of all water molecules were located from difference electron density Fourier maps. The other hydrogen atoms were included in calculated positions and refined with isotropic thermal parameters riding on the parent atoms. Results and discussion

Synthetic and spectral aspects

The solvothermal reactions of CoCl2·2H2O with H4L and 1,4-bimb in a 1:1:1 molar ratio in H2O at 140 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure gave rise to purple block-like single crystals of CP 1 in about 55% yield. The analogous reactions of CoCl2·2H2O with H4L and 1,4-bib (molar ratio = 1:1:1) produced also purple block-like single crystals of CP 2 (53% yield). As will be described later in this paper, CPs 1 and 2 feature distinct coordination networks. Such structural diversities may be attributed to various coordination modes of the H4L ligands and different structural characteristics of rigid or semi-rigid N-donor ligands. In addition, multiple factors, such as temperature, solvent, pH value and molar ratio, may also play important roles in the formation of CPs 1 and 2. When the reactions were performed at lower temperatures (e.g. 130 °C) or higher temperatures (e.g. 150 °C), CPs 1 and 2 were in relatively lower yields. Moreover, we could not isolate any products 1 and 2 from similar reactions in other solvents, for example, MeOH–H2O, MeCN–H2O and DMF–H2O. If the reactions of the same components were carried out at different pH values, no good-quality crystals of the same products were observed. Furthermore, we also attempted to change the molar ratio of the three components to 1:2:1 or 1:1:2, however, this afforded the same products in low yields.

CPs 1 and 2 are air-stable and insoluble in water and other common organic solvents. The elemental analysis of CPs 1 and 2 is in good agreement with the theoretical composition. In order to check the phase purity of CPs 1 and 2, X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns were measured at room temperature (Fig. S1†). The experimental XRPD patterns are in good agreement with the corresponding simulated ones, indicating the purity of the synthesized material and the as-simulated crystals. The difference in reflection intensities between the simulated and the experimental patterns may be attributed to the different orientations of the crystals in the powder samples.46 By comparison, the minor peak shifts may be ascribed to the baseline drift of the XRPD diffractometer and the different measuring temperatures for single-crystal diffraction and powder X-ray diffraction.47

The IR spectra for CPs 1 and 2 are presented in Fig. S2.† The characteristic bands at 1520 cm−1 for CP 1 and 1465 cm−1 for CP 2 are assigned to the ν(CN) stretching vibration of the imidazole rings. The bands around 3400 cm−1 can be associated with the O–H stretching vibration modes of water molecules or hydrogen bonds in CPs 1 and 2. There is no characteristic band at about 1700 cm−1, indicating that all carboxylate groups are fully deprotonated. The bands in the regions of 1340–1665 cm−1 for CPs 1 and 2 are mainly attributed to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of the carboxylate groups. Some weak bands at 2860 and 2940 cm−1 for CP 1 and 2360 cm−1 for CP 2 are consistent with the ν(C–H) stretching vibration of the imidazole rings.48

Crystal structure of {[Co5(L)2(1,4-bimb)(μ3-OH)2(H2O)8]·2H2O}n (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that CP 1 crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system and P space group with an asymmetric unit consisting of half a formula unit. The asymmetric unit of CP 1 contains two and a half independent Co(II) centers in a slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry, one (L)4− ligand, half a 1,4-bimb ligand, one μ3-OH anion, and four coordinated and one lattice water molecules. There are three types of coordination environments around the Co(II) centers (Fig. 1a). The Co1 center is coordinated by three oxygen atoms from two different (L)4− ligands, two coordinated water molecules and one nitrogen atom from one 1,4-bimb ligand (Co1–N = 2.114(2) Å, N–Co1–O = 86.93(8)–175.08(8)°, Co1–O = 2.004(2)–2.216(2) Å, O–Co1–O = 61.00(6)–163.85(7)°). Co2 is six-coordinated by two oxygen atoms from two individual (L)4− ligands, two oxygen atoms from different μ3-OH anions and two oxygen atoms from two coordinated water molecules. The Co–O bond lengths vary from 2.052(2) to 2.210(2) Å and the O–Co–O angles range from 83.22(7) to 174.89(6)°. The coordination environment of Co3 is different from that of Co2, which is six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from two (L)4− ligands and two oxygen atoms from two μ3-OH anions (Co3–O = 2.041(2)–2.169(2) Å, O–Co3–O = 85.24(6)–180.00(6)°). The bond distances and angles around the Co(II) centers are all within the normal ranges.49,50

Symmetry codes: A: x, y − 1, z; B: −x + 2, −y + 2, −z; C: −x + 3, −y + 2, −z; E: −x + 1, −y + 2, −z + 1; F: x − 1, y, z; (b) coordination modes of the (L)4− ligand; (c) view of the 2D framework of CP 1 containing small channels on the a,b-plane; (d) view of the 3D framework of CP 1; (e) the 3,3,4,4,5-connected topological network of CP 1.

In CP 1, the adjacent Co2 and Co3 centers are linked by the μ3-OH anion to form an interesting Co3(μ3-OH)2 diamond chain along the c axis (Fig. S3†). The repeating trimeric unit in the Co3(μ3-OH)2 chain consists of two edge-sharing symmetry-related cobalt octahedra (Co2–Co2 = 3.111(5) Å and Co2–O9–Co2 = 95.47(7)°) linked via the μ3-OH anion to a vertex of a Co3 octahedron (Co2–Co3 = 3.562(4) Å and Co2–O9–Co3 = 119.52(7)°), which are held together by bridging carboxylate groups around the periphery. The fully-deprotonated (L)4− ligand adopts a μ5 coordination mode (Fig. 1b), connecting three different Co(II) centers in the Co3(μ3-OH)2 chain through two a-carboxylate groups in the μ2–η1:η1 fashion and two Co1 centers through two e-carboxylate groups in monodentate and chelate modes. Thus, the chains are connected by the (L)4− ligands and Co1 centers to construct a 2D network containing small channels with dimensions of approximately 10.529(9) Å × 8.597(7) Å on the a,b-plane (Fig. 1c). The 1,4-bimb ligand adopts an anti-conformation and the imidazole rings are parallel to each other. Moreover, the 1,4-bimb ligands behave as bis-monodentate linkers with two terminal nitrogen atom distances of 10.395(3) Å (N1–N1) (Fig. S4†) further connecting the Co1 centers in the adjacent 2D layers to form a complicated 3D framework (Fig. 1d).

From a topological perspective, each (L)4− ligand, the μ3-OH anion and Co(II) centers (Co1, Co2 and Co3) can be simplified as a 5-connected node, 3-connected node, 3-connected node, 4-connected node and 4-connected node, respectively, thus, a 3,3,4,4,5-connected network is generated with the topological point symbol of {42·8·102·12}{43·6·86}2{43}2{46}2{62·8}2. The resulting 3,3,4,4,5-connected topology is an unprecedented 5-nodal network based on the 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic acid ligand, and it has not been deposited in the TOPOS database (http://www.topos.samsu.ru). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example of a CP with such a topology. In addition, the 3D framework is further stabilised by two kinds of O–H⋯O hydrogen bond interactions. One type of hydrogen bond interaction is observed between the water ligands and carboxylate groups, and the second type is between the water ligands. The geometrical parameters for hydrogen bonds are summarized in Table S1.†

Crystal structure of {[Co(L)0.5(1,4-bib)]·H2O}n (2)

CP 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic crystal system and C2/m space group. The asymmetric unit consists of one Co(II) center, half of a (L)4− ligand, one 1,4-bib ligand and one lattice water molecule. As shown in Fig. 2a, the Co1 center is tetracoordinated, completed by two oxygen atoms from two different (L)4− ligands (Co1–O2 = 1.951(4) Å, Co1–O2A = 1.951(4) Å) and two nitrogen atoms from two individual 1,4-bib ligands (Co1–N1 = 2.012(3) Å, Co1–N1A = 2.012(3) Å), resulting in a distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry. The bond angles around the Co(II) centers range from 104.90(2)° to 125.10(2)°.
Fig. 2 (a) Coordination environment of the Co(II) center in CP 2. Hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity. Symmetry codes: A: x, −y + 1, z; E: −x + 1.5, −y + 1.5, −z + 1; G: −x, y, −z + 1; (b) coordination modes of the (L)4− ligand; (c) view of the 3D framework of CP 2 containing larger open channels along the [110] direction; (d) the three-fold interpenetration mog topology network of CP 2.

The fully-deprotonated (L)4− ligands in CP 2 exhibit an ordinary μ4–η1:η1:η1:η1 bridging mode (Fig. 2b) linking four neighboring Co(II) centers to construct a 1D [Co(L)]n chain running along the b axis with the Co⋯Co distance of 5.563(1) Å (Fig. S5†). In addition, the 1,4-bib ligands display a bis-monodentate coordination mode with two terminal nitrogen atoms at a distance of 9.888(5) Å (N1–N1) connecting adjacent Co(II) centers to form a 1D zigzag chain along the c axis (Fig. S6†), where the intrachain adjacent Co⋯Co distance is 13.585(1) Å. Furthermore, these two kinds of 1D chains are cross-linked with each other to generate a complicated 3D framework containing larger open channels with dimensions of approximately 13.585(1) Å × 9.587(1) Å in cross section along the [110] direction (Fig. 2c). If the Co(II) center is simplified as a 4-connected node, the (L)4− ligand as a 4-connected node, and the 1,4-bib ligand as a linker, the 3D structure can be considered as a 4-connected mog topology with the topological point symbol of {4·64·8}2{42·62·82}. In order to avoid an extremely large void space, CP 2 exhibits a 3-fold interpenetrating structure (Fig. 2d). The three interpenetrated nets are related by a single translational vector (Class Ia),51 with Zt = 3 and Zn = 1, where Zt represents the number of interpenetrated nets related by translation and Zn denotes the number of interpenetrated nets related by a crystallographic symmetry.52 To the best of our knowledge, CP 2 represents the first three-fold interpenetration 3D network with mog topology based on rigid bis(imidazole) ligands.

Effects of diverse coordination modes of H4L and structural characteristics of N-donor ligands on the structures of CPs 1 and 2

It is known that the structural diversities of CPs are undoubtedly related to various coordination modes of polycarboxylate ligands.53 Hence, comparison and comprehension of the coordination modes of polycarboxylate ligands are crucial for the predesign of the target architectures. As shown in Fig. 1b and 2b, H4L are all completely deprotonated and their coordination sites are fully occupied by metal centers. There are two different coordination modes of the (L)4− ligand in CPs 1 and 2. In CP 1, the (L)4− ligand adopts a μ5–η2:η2:η1:η1 coordination mode, and can be simplified as a 5-connected node connecting five different Co(II) centers (Co1, Co2 and Co3) to produce a 2D network, which is linked by 1,4-bimb to form a complicated 3D 3,3,4,4,5-connected framework. For CP 2, the (L)4− ligand has a tetradentate bridging mode and bridges four neighboring Co(II) centers to construct a 1D [Co(L)]n chain, which is further connected by 1,4-bib into a 3D 4-connected mog topology with the topological point symbol of {4·64·8}2{42·62·82}. Obviously, the diverse structures of CPs 1 and 2 are seriously affected by the different coordination modes of H4L.

Due to the rigid or semi-rigid character, 1,4-bimb and 1,4-bib can show stable cis or trans-conformations and symmetric (with the same Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of two imidazole arms) or asymmetric (with different Ndonor⋯N–Csp3⋯Csp3 torsion angles of two imidazole arms) conformations.54 In CP 1, the semi-rigid 1,4-bimb ligands show a symmetric trans-conformation and act as bis-monodentate linkers further connecting the adjacent 2D layers to form a complicated 3D framework, while in CP 2, the rigid 1,4-bib ligands exhibit a symmetric trans-conformation. Along the c axis, 1,4-bib also displays a bis-monodentate coordination mode, which connects the adjacent Co(II) centers to construct a 1D zigzag chain. Then, the zigzag chains bridge the (L)4− ligands to form a 3D mog topology framework with the topological point symbol of {4·64·8}2{42·62·82}. These results undoubtedly indicate that the structural characteristics of N-donor ligands have a great influence on the structures of the resulting CPs.

Thermal properties and photoluminescence properties

The thermal properties of CPs 1 and 2 were examined by thermogravimetric (TG) analysis under an N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the temperature range from room temperature to 800 °C. Both of the TG curves show two distinct steps of weight losses (Fig. S7†). In CP 1, the first weight loss of 16.81% below 258 °C corresponds to the decomposition of coordinated hydroxyl groups, and coordinated and lattice water molecules (calcd 16.99%). The second weight loss occurs from 306 °C to 683 °C, which can be attributed to the loss of organic ligands (L)4− and 1,4-bimb. The remaining weight (29.63%) is presumed to be CoO (29.78%). For CP 2, the TG curve displays an initial weight loss of 4.16% between 110 °C and 180 °C, representing the loss of lattice water molecules (calcd 4.33%). Then, the removal of organic ligands (L)4− and 1,4-bib is observed in the range of 256 °C to 548 °C. The remaining weight (17.98%) corresponds to the percentage (18.04%) of the Co and O components in CoO.

The photoluminescence properties of CPs 1 and 2 and the corresponding free ligands have been investigated at room temperature in the solid state (Fig. 3). The main emission peak for the free H4L, 1,4-bimb and 1,4-bib ligands is observed at 431 nm (λex = 300 nm), 368 nm (λex = 300 nm) and 368 nm (λex = 260 nm), respectively, which may be attributed to the n → π* or π → π* transitions.55 CPs 1 and 2 exhibit strong emission bands with maxima at 401 nm (λex = 275 nm) and 444 nm (λex = 320 nm), respectively. In comparison with the H4L ligand, the emission peak of CP 1 is blue-shifted, which may be due to the π → π* intraligand fluorescence because of close resemblance to the emission band of the H4L ligand.56 However, a red shift for CP 2 with respect to the free H4L ligand may be due to the metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) with electrons being transferred from the Co(II) centers to the unoccupied p* orbitals of the H4L ligand, according to the literature.57

Electrochemical properties

The electrochemical properties of CPs 1 and 2 were investigated by cyclic voltammetry in 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution (see Electrochemical experiment part of the ESI†). The two CPs have been used as modifiers to fabricate chemically bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (1-CPE or 2-CPE) because of their high thermal stability and low solubility in water and common organic solvents. Fig. 4 and S8† show the cyclic voltammetric behaviors of 1-CPE and 2-CPE at 500 mV s−1. It can be noticed that a pair of reversible redox peaks (I–I′) are observed in the potential range from −400 to +900 mV for 1-CPE and from −500 to +900 mV for 2-CPE, respectively, which should be ascribed to the redox of CoIII/CoII. The differences in the mean peak potentials may be assigned to the final composition and structures of CPs 1 and 2.
Fig. 4 Cyclic voltammograms of CP 1 in 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution at 500 mV s−1.

We have further studied the cyclic voltammetric behavior of 1-CPE and 2-CPE in 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates. As shown in Fig. 5 and S9,† the mean peak potentials E1/2 = (Epc + Epa)/2 are −102 mV for 1-CPE and 212 mV for 2-CPE, respectively. With the scan rates increasing from 40 to 500 mV s−1, the peak potentials change gradually: the cathodic peak potentials shift toward the negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials toward the positive direction; the peak-to-peak separation between the corresponding cathodic and anodic peaks increase, but the mean peak potentials don't change on the whole. The anodic and cathodic peak currents are proportional to the scan rates up to 500 mV s−1 for 1-CPE and 2-CPE, suggesting that the redox processes of 1-CPE and 2-CPE are surface-controlled.58

Photophysical properties and optical band gaps

The diffuse-reflectance UV-vis spectra of CPs 1 and 2, H4L, 1,4-bimb and 1,4-bib were investigated at room temperature in the solid state (Fig. 6). The H4L ligand exhibits one intense absorption band at 217 nm, which is attributed to the π → π* transition.59 The UV-vis spectra of the N-donor ligands occur at 235 and 267 nm for 1,4-bimb and 254 and 330 nm for 1,4-bib, which are assigned to the typical π → π* transition of the aromatic rings.60 Compared with the above results, the lowest energy absorption bands in the N-donor ligands are lower than that of the H4L ligand in the energy level, which indicates that N-donor ligands might have more influence than the lowest energy absorption band of the H4L ligand by coordination with the metal center.61 The diffuse-reflectance UV-vis spectra also reveal the absorption features of CPs 1 and 2, which consist of absorption components in the UV and Vis regions. The lower-energy bands in the visible region at 527 (600) nm for CP 1 and 541 nm for CP 2 can be ascribed to the d–d spin-allowed transition of the Co2+ (d7) ions, whereas the main UV absorption bands at 214 nm and 249 nm for CPs 1 and 2 can be attributed to metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT).62 In addition, the band gap energy of the material can be calculated according to the formula: Eg = 1240/ëg (eV), where Eg is the band gap energy (eV) and ëg is the absorption edge wavelength (nm). Following the above method, the band gap energies of CPs 1 and 2 were estimated to be 2.31 eV and 2.54 eV, respectively (Fig. 7). Thus, CPs 1 and 2 may be regarded as semiconducting materials and employed as potential photocatalysts under UV light irradiation.

Photocatalytic properties

As a chemically stable and poorly biodegradable dye, methylene blue (MB) is selected as a model dye contaminant to examine the photocatalytic activities of CPs 1 and 2. The detailed photocatalytic experimental procedures are depicted in the ESI.†Fig. 8 and 9 illustrate the time dependent absorption spectra of the MB solution degraded by CPs 1 and 2, and the characteristic absorption of the MB at approximately 664 nm was used to monitor the process of photodegradation. It can be clearly seen that the absorption peaks of MB decreased obviously with increasing of reaction time for CPs 1 and 2. The control experiment was also accomplished in the following reaction conditions: (1) in the dark; and (2) without any photocatalyst. When CPs 1 and 2 were placed into an aqueous solution of the MB in a dark environment for 120 min, there is no obvious decrease in the absorbance value, which may avoid the possibility of adsorbing such a dye molecule into the frameworks and the catalytic degradation efficiency of MB is only 1.50% for CP 1 and 2.66% for CP 2. In addition, the MB in the control experiment without any photocatalyst showed no noteworthy decrease in the absorbance value (Fig. S10†). The changes in the Ct/C0 plot of the MB solution against irradiation time are depicted in Fig. 10 (wherein C0 is the initial concentration of the MB solution and Ct is the concentration of the MB at any given time t). The calculations show that the photocatalytic efficiency increases from 11.9% (without any catalyst) to 91.4% for CP 1 and 89.1% for CP 2 after 120 min of UV irradiation. The increased photocatalytic degradation efficiency indicates that CPs 1 and 2 are both excellent candidates as photocatalysts in decomposing MB under UV light irradiation. It is obvious that the photocatalytic efficiency of CP 1 is better, which proves that the differences of the components and structural characteristics of the resulting CPs may influence their photocatalytic activities.63 Their efficiencies are somewhat higher than those obtained by coordination polymers, recently reported, under the UV light irradiation. In order to elucidate the possible photocatalytic reaction mechanism of the two CPs, CP 1 was chosen as the representative example. In this study, we conducted another photocatalytic experiment in the presence of t-butyl alcohol (TBA), a widely used ˙OH scavenger.70,71 The effect of the TBA on the photocatalytic degradation of the MB with CP 1 was evaluated. As depicted in Fig. 12 and 13, the photocatalytic efficiency decreased obviously from 91.4% to 68.4%, proving that the TBA can greatly depress the photodegradation of MB solution with CP 1. The possible photocatalytic reaction mechanism for the above degradation reactions is proposed as follows (Scheme 1). Under a high-pressure mercury lamp irradiation, one electron (e−) could be excited from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO), resulting in the equal amount of positive vacancies left (holes, h+). The HOMO is mainly contributed by oxygen and (or) nitrogen 2p bonding orbitals and the LUMO by empty Co orbitals, so charge transfer occurs from oxygen and (or) nitrogen to Co under the photoexcitation.72 The HOMO strongly demands one electron to return to the stable state. Then one electron combines with O2 absorbed on the surface of the photocatalysts to produce oxygen radicals (˙O2), which will transform into hydroxyl radicals (˙OH).73 At the same time, the water molecules react with the holes (h+) to yield hydroxyl radicals (˙OH). Finally, hydroxyl radicals (˙OH) can effectively degrade the MB.

Conclusion

In conclusion, two Co(II) coordination polymers from the reactions of 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic acid (H4L) with CoCl2·2H2O and rigid or semi-rigid bis(imidazole) ligands under hydrothermal reactions have been synthesized and characterized. CP 1 is a 3D 3,3,4,4,5-connected framework with a {42·8·102·12}{43·6·86}2{43}2{46}2{62·8}2 topology. This resulting 3,3,4,4,5-connected topology is an unprecedented 5-nodal net, and it has not been deposited in the TOPOS database (http://www.topos.samsu.ru). CP 2 presents a three-fold interpenetration 3D framework with mog topology. The distinct structures of the two CPs may be due to the diverse coordination modes of H4L ligands and different structural characteristics of rigid or semi-rigid N-donor ligands. The photocatalytic experiments show that CPs 1 and 2 are both excellent candidates as photocatalysts in decomposing MB and Congo red under UV light irradiation.
