Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 14, Issue 12, December 2011, Pages 1906-1910

An unusual 3D 3d–4f heterometallic coordination polymer based on the linkages of Sm2(IN)6 pillars and 2D [Cu7Br6]nn+ layers: Crystal structure and luminescent property

Author links open overlay panelLe-QingFanJi-HuaiWuYun-FangHuang
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2011.09.008Get rights and content

Abstract

An unusual three-dimensional (3D) pillared-layer 3d–4f (Cu+–Sm3+) heterometallic coordination polymer, {Sm2Cu7Br6(IN)7(H2O)5·3H2O}n (1) (HIN = isonicotinic acid), has been successfully synthesized by hydrothermal reaction of Sm2O3, CuBr2, HIN, HClO4 and H2O, and characterized by elemental analyses, IR, PXRD, and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. The structure determination reveals that 1 possesses 3D heterometallic framework constructed upon unprecedented [Cu7Br6]nn+ inorganic layers linked by dimeric Sm2(IN)6 pillars. Additionally, the thermogravimetric analysis and luminescent property of 1 were investigated and discussed.

Graphical abstract

The {Sm2Cu7Br6(IN)7(H2O)5·3H2O}n is the first example of 3D 3d–4f heterometallic coordination polymer based on 2D Cu–Br inorganic layers.

Research Highlights

► A novel 3D Sm–Cu coordination polymer based on 2D Cu-Br layers has been synthesized. ► CuBr2N triangles and CuBr3N tetrahedra construct the unprecedented 2D Cu-Br layer. ► This compound exhibits good luminescent property.

The current growing interest in designing and synthesizing 3d–4f heterometallic coordination polymers (HCPs) has been significantly provoked not only by their impressive structural diversity but also by their potential applications as functional materials in magnetism, luminescence materials, molecular adsorption, and bimetallic catalysis [1]. As is known to all, according to the hard–soft acid base theory, 3d and 4f metal ions have different affinities for N and O donors. Generally, 3d metal ions easily bond to the ligand containing N donor [2]. However, compared with 3d metal ions, 4f metal ions more easily bond to the ligand including O donor [1a,b]. Therefore, if an organic ligand simultaneously contains O and N donors, it should be employed to bond to both 3d and 4f metal ions to form HCPs. This is a simple and valid method at the present time used to synthesize 3d–4f HCPs. And, to date, various organic ligands, like pyridinecarboxylate, pyridinephosphonate, imidazolecarboxylate, amino acid, etc., containing O and N donors are selected to construct 3d–4f HCPs with interesting structures and physico-chemical properties [3]. But the preparation of high dimensional, especially three-dimensional (3D) 3d–4f HCPs by choosing suitable organic ligands is still a challenge, owing to the following factors: firstly, high coordination number and low stereochemical preference for 4f metal ions; secondly, the variable and versatile coordination behavior of 3d and 4f metal ions; finally, the competitive reactions between 3d and 4f metal ions coordinated to the same ligand [4].

The coordination chemistry of the copper(I) halides (CuX, X = Cl, Br, I) has been of great interest for their large structural variation and rich electronic and optical properties [5]. Therefore, the introduction of a Cu–X inorganic motif into lanthanide (Ln)-organic frameworks to form high-dimensional 3d–4f HCPs can provide new materials with not only novel architectures but also useful physico-chemical properties. A pillared-layer approach to 3D 3d–4f HCPs constructed upon the connections of Ln-organic pillars and Cu–X inorganic layers via coordination bonding has been reported [4], [6]. At present, all existent such 3D 3d–4f HCPs base on Cu–I inorganic layers where I− ions have high coordination numbers and versatile coordination modes [4], [6]. On the other hand, it is found that, in many complexes, Br− ions can act as μ4-, μ3- and μ2-bridging ligands [7]. So, in view of the similarity between Br− and I− ions, the formation of such 3D 3d–4f HCP containing Cu–Br inorganic layers linked by Ln-organic pillars may become true.

Because isonicotinic acid (HIN) is a linear and potentially bridging ligand with O and N donors on opposite sides of the molecule as well as the diverse coordination modes [8], we chose HIN to prepare such 3D pillared-layer 3d–4f HPC. And a novel such 3D pillared-layer Cu–Sm HCP, {Sm2Cu7Br6(IN)7(H2O)5·3H2O}n (1), has been successfully prepared and characterized by elemental analyses, IR, PXRD, single-crystal X-ray diffraction, thermogravimetric analysis and luminescence. Complex 1 is the first example of 3d–4f framework based on Cu–Br inorganic layers, namely, unprecedented two-dimensional (2D) [Cu7Br6]nn+, which are further connected by dimeric Sm2(IN)6 pillars to give rise to 3D pillared-layer network.

Complex 1 was obtained from the reaction of Sm2O3, CuBr2, HIN, HClO4, and H2O with pH = 2 under hydrothermal condition in a Teflon-lined autoclave at 180 °C for 5 days [9]. The phase purity of 1 was confirmed by elemental analysis and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) (Fig. S1). The IR spectrum of 1 (Fig. S2) shows the strong and broad absorption band about 3426 cm− 1, which is assigned as characteristic peak of OH vibration. The strong vibrations appearing at 1614 and 1418 cm− 1 correspond to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of carboxylate group, respectively. The absence of strong bands in the range of 1690–1730 cm− 1 indicates that all carboxyl groups of HIN are deprotonated [10].

Single crystal X-ray diffraction study revealed that 1 is a 3D HCP based on [Cu7Br6]nn+ inorganic layers linked by dimeric Sm2(IN)6 pillars [11]. An ORTEP view of 1 is shown in Fig. 1. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one two Sm3+ ions, seven Cu+ ions, six Br− ions, five coordinated water molecules, three uncoordinated water molecules, and seven IN− ligands. Sm(1) center is eight-coordinated and displays distorted bicapped trigonal-prism coordination environment: six oxygen atoms from four IN− ligands, two oxygen atoms from two coordinated water molecules. However, Sm(2) center is nine-coordinated and behaves distorted tricapped trigonal-prism coordination geometry: six oxygen atoms from four IN– ligands, three oxygen atoms from three coordinated water molecules. The SmO bond lengths vary from 2.292(9) to 2.596(9) Å, and the OSmO bond angles are in the range of 50.9(3)–157.5(4)° (Table S1), thus being in the normal range observed in other compounds [8], [10], [12]. All Cu(1), Cu(2), Cu(6) and Cu(7) centers exhibit tetrahedral conformation, being coordinated by one nitrogen atom from IN− ligand and three Br− ions. But, all Cu(3), Cu(4) and Cu(5) centers present trigonal geometry, being bonded by one nitrogen atom from IN− ligand and two Br− ions. The CuN and CuBr bond lengths range from 1.988(12) to 2.022(12) Å and from 2.397(3) to 2.570(3) Å (Table S1), respectively, all within the range of those observed for other Ln–Cu compounds [2], [4]. In this structure, the IN− ligands have three evidently different coordination modes: one behaves as a bridging ligand to coordinate one Sm3+ ion and one Cu+ ion (Scheme 1a), another also behaves as a bridging ligand, but it coordinates two Sm3+ ions and one Cu+ ion (Scheme 1b), and the third behaving as a terminal ligand only coordinates to one Cu+ ion (Scheme 1c). Although Cu2+ ions were used as starting materials in 1, the Cu centers have an oxidation state of + 1, attributed to a reduction reaction involving the IN− ligands [2], [13], and are consistent with the geometry of the Cu+ ions and evidenced by the yellow color of crystals.

Sm(1) and Sm(2) centers are connected by two carboxylate groups from two different IN− ligands to form a Sm2(IN)6 dimer with a Sm⋯Sm distance of 5.5667(14) Å (Fig. 2). As shown in Fig. 3, two adjacent Cu(1)Br3N tetrahedra are connected by common edge (Br(2)–Br(2)) to form a Cu2Br4N2 dimer, which further link two Cu(2)Br3N tetrahedra via sharing edges (Br(1)–Br(2)) to produce Cu(1, 2)Br6N4 tetramer with step-like Cu4Br4 structure. Two Cu(6)Br3N and two Cu(7)Br3N tetrahedra are also joined by common edges (Br(6)–Br(6), Br(1)–Br(6)) to generate Cu(6, 7)Br6N4 tetramer structurally similar to Cu(1, 2)Br6N4 tetramer. In Cu(1, 2)Br6N4 and Cu(6, 7)Br6N4 tetramers, both Cu(1)–Cu(1) and Cu(7)–Cu(7) distances are 2.659(4) Å, which is much shorter than the double van der Waals radius of the Cu+ ion (1.4 Å) [2f], implying strong Cu–Cu interactions. Cu(3)Br2N, Cu(4)Br2N and Cu(5)Br2N triangles connect each other through common vertices (Br(3), Br(4), Br(5)) to give rise to Cu3Br3N3 trimer with the Cu(3)–Cu(4) and Cu(4)–Cu(5) distances of 2.681(3) and 2.688(3) Å, respectively, which also indicate strong Cu–Cu interactions. The phenomena of Cu–Cu interactions have been observed for other Ln–Cu compounds [3], [14]. The Cu3Br3N3 trimers link Cu(1, 2)Br6N4 and Cu(6, 7)Br6N4 tetramers by sharing vertices (Br(3), Br(5)) to engender one-dimensional (1D) chains along b axis, which are further connected by common vertex (Br(1)) to form unprecedented 2D [Cu7Br6]nn+ inorganic layers extending along ab plane (Fig. 3). As a consequence of the connectivity of CuBr3N tetrahedra and CuBr2N triangles in 1, the Br− ions act as μ2 (Br(4)), μ3 (Br(2), Br(3), Br(5), Br(6)) and μ4 (Br(4)) ligands. Although a variety of zero-dimensional and one-dimensional Cu–Br substructures have been introduced into Ln-organic frameworks [2], [4], [15], up to now, 2D-layered Cu–Br inorganic motif in 3d–4f system is reported for the first time. The linkages between dimeric Sm2(IN)6 pillars and [Cu7Br6]nn+ inorganic layers give birth to such a novel 3D pillared-layer network (Fig. 4).

To study the thermal stability of 1, the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on polycrystalline sample of this complex in N2 atmosphere from 30 to 600 °C (Fig. S3). The lattice-water and coordinated water molecules were gradually lost in the temperature ranging 30–190 °C (calcd/found: 6.48/6.34%). Thereafter 1 is stable to ca. 240 °C. Above this temperature, the weight loss is due to the decomposition of the organic ligand and the collapse of the whole framework.

Because of the excellent luminescence properties of d10 Cu+ ions, the photoluminescence of 1 was examined in the solid state at room temperature. It shows broad fluorescent emission spectrum from 500 to 750 nm, with an intense emission occurring around 643 nm and quantum efficiency of 0.85% when excited at 360 nm (Fig. 5). To understand the nature of the luminescent property of 1, the emission spectrum of pure HIN was also measured under the same condition, which exhibits one emission band at 452 nm upon excitation at 330 nm (Fig. S4). The obvious discrepancy of the emission spectra of 1 and pure HIN suggests that the emission spectrum of 1 should not be attributed to an intraligand π–π* transition. According to the results of the similar copper(I) halide compounds [6], [8], [16], the emission band might be assigned to a combination of bromide-to-copper charge transfer (LMCT) and d–s transitions by Cu–Cu interaction.

In conclusion, an unusual 3D pillared-layer 3d–4f (Cu+–Sm3+) HCP based on 2D Cu–Br inorganic layers linked by dimeric Sm2(IN)6 pillars has been obtained for the first time. Furthermore, the thermal stability and luminescence of this complex have been investigated. Our results provide an intriguing example of 3d–4f HCP and further demonstrate that the 2D Cu–Br inorganic motif can be used for constructing novel function materials.
