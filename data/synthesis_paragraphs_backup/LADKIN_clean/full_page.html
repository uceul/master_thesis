Journal of Solid State Chemistry

Volume 237, May 2016, Pages 219-224

A novel 3D framework indium phosphite-oxalate based on a pcu-type topology

Author links open overlay panelMengmengZuoMingdongZhouDianwenHuFanGaoSijieDongLiangliangHuang
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jssc.2016.02.019Get rights and content

Abstract

A new inorganic–organic hybrid indium phosphite-oxalate, formulated as H[In5(HPO3)6(H2PO3)2(C2O4)2]·(C4N2H11)2·H2O 1 has been hydrothermally synthesized in the presence of piperazine acting as a structure directing agent (SDA). The single crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that compound 1 shows three-dimensional open-framework with intersecting 12-ring channels along the [010] and [001] directions, which is constructed from strictly alternating double 6-ring units (D6Rs), [C2O4]2− groups and [H2PO3]− pseudo-pyramids. It is noted that the classical D6R SBU is firstly reported in main metal phosphite/phosphite-oxalate. By regarding D6R as the 6-connected nodes, the inorganic–organic hybrid framework is based on a pcu-type topology. The as-synthesized product was characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), ICP-AES and elemental analyses.

1. Introduction

Microporous materials, such as zeolites and metal phosphates, have been extensively studied for their potential applications in catalysis, ion-exchange, gas storage, separation, and sensor technology [1], [2], [3]. Since the pioneering work reporting the crystalline aluminophosphate molecular sieves in 1982 [4], a huge number of metal phosphates with different second building units (SBUs), such as 4-ring (4R), 6-ring (6R), double 4-ring (D4R), double 6-ring (D6R), have been prepared and structurally characterized [5]. Among metal phosphates, indium phosphate (InPO) constitute an important family that exhibits a rich structural and compositional diversity due to the versatility of indium in terms of its variable coordination geometry combined with the structure directing effect of the organic component. To date, a large number of indium phosphates with different In/P ratios have been reported which exhibit numerous frameworks with 1D chain, 2D layered and 3D open-frameworks [6], [7], [8], [9], [10]. For example, the 3D open-framework indium phosphate [H3DETA]2[HDETA][In6.8F8(H2O)2(PO4)4(HPO4)4]·2H2O (DETA=diethylenetriamine) possesses 16-members ring channels [7] and NH4In(OH)PO4 contains chiral chains [10].

More recently, metal phosphites, regarded as the development of metal phosphates, are of special interest because the presence of three-connected [HPO3]2− centers favors the reduction of M–O–P connectivity and generates interrupted open-framework with large pore sizes and low framework density (FD) [11], [12], [13]. Typical examples include [HR]2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (R=cyclohexylamine, cycloheptylamine) [11] and [(C4H12N)2][Zn3(HPO3)4] [12] possessing extra-large 24-ring channels and NIS-4 [13] with a very low FD of 9.0. To rich and complicate the structures of metal phosphites, many attempts have made it possible by using various ligands. As one of the simplest imaginable organic linkers, oxalate has attracted considerable attention in the construction of hybrid frameworks. In recent years, inorganic–organic hybrid compounds containing both oxalate and phosphite units have also been prepared [14], [15], [16], [17], [18], [19], which display attractive structures. Notable example is the first organically templated iron oxalate-phosphite, [C4N2H12][Fe4II(HPO3)2(C2O4)3], possessing infinite Fe–O–Fe chains [19]. However, compared to transition-metal phosphite-oxalates, the main group metal phosphite-oxalate is rarely reported [20], [21], [22], [23]. Typical examples include NTHU-7 containing nanosized channel structure, which is the first organically templated gallium oxalatophosphite on the environmentally friendly deep eutectic solvent (DES) [23].

During the last few years, our group has worked on the design and structural investigation of main group metal phosphites with novel open-framework [24], [25]. In order to expand the field of research pertaining main group metal phosphites materials, we have chosen to explore the design and synthesis of novel open architectures by introducing the oxalate ligand as part of our design strategy, such as [Ga2(HPO3)2(H2PO3)(C2O4)](C6N2H16)0.5 possessing 3D open-framework [24]. Here we report the hydrothermal synthesis and characterizations of a new 3D open-framework indium phosphite-oxalate, H[In5(HPO3)6(H2PO3)2(C2O4)2]·(C4N2H11)2·H2O 1, containing 12-ring channels, which is constructed with a classical D6R SBU. It is noted that the SBU is firstly reported in the family of main metal phosphite/phosphite-oxalate compounds.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and instrumentation

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. The elemental analysis was conducted on a Perkin Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. ICP-AES (inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectroscope) analysis was performed on a Perkin Elmer Optima 3300DV ICP instrument. The powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data collection was carried out using SHIMADAZU XRD-6000 diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ=1.5418 Å), with the step size and the count time of 0.02° and 4 s, respectively. FT-IR spectra was recorded on a Nicolet Impact 410 spectrometer between 400 and 4000 cm−1 using the KBr pellet method. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was conducted on a SHIMADZU DTG 60 thermogravimetric analyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from room temperature to 800 °C in the air.

2.2. Synthesis

Compound 1 was prepared from a starting mixture containing indium chloride tetrahydrate (InCl3·4H2O) (Aladdin, 98%), phosphorous acid (H3PO3) (Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Beijing Co., Ltd, 99%), oxalic acid (H2C2O4) (Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Beijing Co., Ltd, 99%), piperazine (Aladdin, 99%) and water (H2O) under mild hydrothermal conditions and autogenous pressure. Typically, 0.10 g InCl3 and 0.45 g H3PO3 were mixtured in 6 ml water, which was followed by addition of 0.45 g piperazine and 0.4 g H2C2O4 at stirring. The mixture was further stirred for 30 min at room temperature and heated at 130 °C for 3 days. After slow cooling to the room temperature, the resulting colorless crystals of 1 was recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water and dried in the air. The yield of product was 56% in weight based on indium.

2.3. Determination of crystal structure

Suitable colorless single crystals of 1 (block, dimensions 0.20×0.18×0.17 mm3) were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber with epoxy resin. The intensity data were collected on a Bruker APEX CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ=0.71073 Å) radiation at a temperature of 293 K. Data processing was accomplished with the saint-processing program. The structure was solved by direct method using the SHELXTL 97 crystallographic software package [26], [27]. The indium and phosphorus atoms were first located, whereas the carbon, nitrogen, oxygen atoms were found in the difference Fourier maps. P(2) and P(4) are disordered over two positions. Therefore, the occupancy for P(2) was assigned to be 0.761100 and 0.2389, while P(4) was assigned to be 0.599390 and 0.40061 in the refinement. All the hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically and refined in a riding model. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterizations

The powder X-ray diffraction pattern of as-synthesized compound 1 and the simulated pattern based on their single crystal structure were compared (Fig. 1). The diffraction peaks on simulated and experimental patterns corresponded well in position, indicating the phase purity of the as-synthesized compound 1. The reflection intensity difference between the simulated and experimental patterns was due to the variation in crystal particle orientation in the powder sample.

The IR spectrum of compound 1 shows the stretching vibrations of O–H group at 3451 cm−1. The strong and broad bands centered at 3227 and 3134 cm−1 and bands around 1462, 1402 and 1323 cm−1 can pertain to the stretching and bending vibrations of the N–H and C–H groups of the organic molecules. The characteristic bands at 2428 and 2338 cm−1 for 1 are attributed to the stretching vibration of the P–H bonds. The broad bands at ca. 993–1185 cm−1 are associated with the stretching vibrations of P–O bonds. The strong stretching vibration of C=O bond is observed at 1641 cm−1. In conclusion, the IR results show clearly the vibrations from phosphite group, organic molecule and oxalate ligand.

The results of ICP and elemental analyses of the samples are in agreement with the values calculated on the base of the empirical formula given by single crystal structure analysis. For compound 1, analysis found (wt%): In, 28.62; P, 13.25; C, 12.40; H, 3.17; N, 6.02; Calcd. (wt%): In, 28.97, P, 13.02; C, 12.61; H, 3.39; N, 6.02.

To investigate the thermal stabilities of compounds 1, thermogravimetric analysis was performed under an oxygen flow at the heating rate of 10 °C/min. As shown in Fig. 2, three-step weight-loss processes are present from 150 °C to 800 °C. As shown in Fig. 2, the weight loss before 200 °C is caused by release of water molecules (observed: 1.97%; expected: 1.20%). However, the observed weight loss (1.97%) was higher than the excepted one (1.20%). The weight variation in the temperature range of 385–630 °C is attributed to the release of two piperazine molecules from the structure (observed: 7.72%; expected: 10.9%). The lower reduction was due to the retention of carbon in the solid residue (black in color). The remaining residue for the sample is amorphous, and its phase is unidentified.

3.2. Structural description

As shown in Fig. 3, the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of 32 crystallographically independent non-hydrogen framework atoms, 25 of which belong to the “host framework” (three indium atoms, four phosphorus atoms, two carbon atoms and 16 oxygen atoms) and the remaining atoms to the guest amine molecule (four carbon atoms and two nitrogen atoms) and a water. In(2) and In(3) atom locates in a general position and In(1) atom occupies a special position with site multiplicity of 0.5. All indium atoms adopt six-coordination geometry, but the coordination environment is different. For In(1), it is coordinated by six oxygen atoms from [HPO3]2− units. The In(1)–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.145(9)–2.163(9) Å and O–In(1)–O angles range from 84.4(4)° to 95.6(4)°. In(2) and In(3) atoms have the similar coordination environment. Each shares four bridging oxygen atoms with the adjacent P atoms and two bridging oxygen atoms (O(10) and (12) for In(2), O(6) and (13) for In(3)) with C atoms. The slight distortion observed for both InO6 octahedra is due to the attached oxalate groups, as indicated by the wide range of In–O bond lengths [2.079(9)–2.216(10) Å for In(2) and 2.063(10)–2.209(8) Å for In(3)] and the narrow O–In–O angles [75.3(3)°–106.6(4)° for In(2) and 76.8(3)°–108.3(4)° for In(3)], similar to distortions observed in other metal phosphate-oxalates [28]. All P atoms adopt pseudo-pyramidal coordination geometry. The P(1), P(2) and P(4) atoms link to three In atoms via bridging oxygen atoms and to one H atom. The P(3) atom bridges two In atoms and possesses one terminal –OH group (P–OH:1.531(10) Å) and one P–H bond. The P–O distances are in the range 1.470 (11)–1.542(10) Å, and the O–P–O bond angle are in the range 107.9(6)°–114.5(6)°, which are in agree with those observed in the known metal phosphites [19]. The existence of P–H bonds is also confirmed by the characteristic band of phosphite anion [ν(H–P)=2428 cm−1] in the IR spectrum. The framework stoichiometry of In5(HPO3)6(H2PO3)2(C2O4)2 creates a net charge of −3, which is balanced by two monoprotonated piperazine cation and one hydrogen proton.

Compound 1 displays a 3D open-framework, which involves InO6 octahedra, [HPO3]2− pseudo-pyramids, [H2PO3]− pseudo-pyramids and [C2O4]2− units. Two types of 12 MR channels are displayed in compound 1 along the [010] and [001] directions. The diameters of the two different 12-ring windows are about 13.156 Å×7.753 Å and 10.626 Å×7.854 Å, respectively, as calculated from the distance between the indium atoms across the windows. As viewed along the [100] direction, the structure exhibits three types of 8-MRs channels (Fig. 4). A pair of them is delimited by InO6 octahedra and [HPO3]2−/[H2PO3]− pseudo-pyramids and [C2O4]2− units, which are occupied by piperazine molecules. The other is delimited by InO6 octahedra and [HPO3]2− pseudo-pyramids, which are occupied by water molecules. The well-ordered guest groups occupying all of the 8-membered ring channels interact with the host framework by strong hydrogen bonds. On the basis of geometrical position of hydrogen atoms described above, the N–H bonds from SDAs and O–H from water are involved in the guest-to-host hydrogen bonds and formed extensive N–H···O links and O–H···O with the host framework (Table 2). The average bond length distance of the O–H···O and N–H···O are 2.38 Å and 2.00 Å, respectively. Also, the extensive hydrogen bonds existed between the terminal O–H of [H2PO3]− groups (O14···O14: 2.382(19) Å), which are from the opposite vertex of the 8-membered ring window. The hydrogen-bonding interactions are listed in Table 2. The analysis by PLATON, performed only on the inorganic framework structure, suggests a solvent accessible volume of approximately 293.4 Å3 per unit cell (1020.1 Å3) amounting to 28.8% of the crystal lattice for compound 1.

3.3. Topology analysis

The three-dimensional structure of 1 can be described based on a classical D6R second building unit (SBU) containing six InO6 octahedra and six [HPO3]2− pseudo-pyramids, as shown in Fig. 5a. Similar SBU is referred in BePO4-GME [29]. The difference between them is that the 4-MRs and 6-MRs from the D6R of BePO4-GME are normal while the 4-MRs and 6-MRs of compound 1 are distorted. Along the [100] direction, the adjacent classical SBUs are linked with each other by sharing In(1) atom to form a 1D chain (Fig. 5b). The adjacent 1D chains are linked by the oxalate ligands through a typical bisbidentate chelating coordination mode to create a 2D layer showing 8-ring and 12-ring windows in the ab plane as shown in Fig. 5c. The 2D layered structures are further connected with [H2P(3)O3]− pseudo-pyramids to form the 3D open-framework (Fig. 5d). By regarding D6R as the 6-connected nodes, P(3)-centered pseudo-pyramids and oxalate groups as the linkers, the framework structure of 1 can be understood as a uninodal pcu network (Fig. 5e). As far as we know, such topology has been reported in beryllium phosphite and zinc phosphite [30], [31], [32]. However, it is firstly observed in indium phosphite and phosphite-oxalate.

D6R is one of the most important SBUs to construct the zeolite structures such as FAU, CHA and AFX [33]. The zeolite structures with D6R as SBU are highly desirable structural features for industrial applications. For example, zeolite Y having the faujasite type structure is one of the most widely used catalysts for a variety of oil refining processes in the petroleum industry [34]. SAPO-34 having CHA type structure is application in catalytic conversion of methanol to olefins (MTO process) [35], [36]. D6R is also one of the most important SBUs to construct attractive framework of metal phosphates. Bu reported several crystal structures of phosphate-based zeolite analogs constructed with D6Rs, such as CAP-CHA5, ZAP-CHA2, BePO4-GME [29], [37], [38]. However, to our knowledge, the D6R is rarely observed in metal phosphite/phosphite-oxalate. Compound 1 is the first main metal phosphite/phosphite-oxalate constructed with D6Rs. Although the structure of 1 is not based in zeolite topology, our investigation shows that it is possible to prepare structurally complex open-framework indium phosphite-oxalate possessing classical SBU to construct the zeolite structures under appropriate reaction conditions. The framework density (FD) defined as the number of tetrahedra per 1000 Å3 is 12.74 for compound 1. Compared to the D6R-containing structures, such as EMT (13.3), faujasite (15.1) and AEI (15.1) [30], the framework density of compound 1 is lower. It also demonstrates that the 3D framework contains large 12-MRs (13.156 Å×7.822 Å and 13.745 Å×6.348 Å) channels.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a new 3D open-framework indium phosphite-oxalate with uninodal pcu network has been hydrothermally synthesized using piperazin as the SDA, which shows intersecting 12-ring channels along the [010] and [001] directions. The open-framework is constructed with classical D6Rs, [C2O4]2− units and [H2PO3]−. The existence of D6Rs in one structure is unique and, to the best of our knowledge, is firstly observed in main metal phosphite/phosphite-oxalate materials. Our investigation shows that it is possible to prepare structurally complex open-framework indium phosphite-oxalate possessing classical SBU to construct the zeolite structures under appropriate reaction conditions. Further investigation of indium phosphite-oxalate is in progress.
