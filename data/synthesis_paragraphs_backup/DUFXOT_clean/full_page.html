Journal of Solid State Chemistry

Volume 230, October 2015, Pages 243-248

A novel second-order non-linear optical coordination polymer with three-fold interpenetrated CdSO4-type network constructed by carboxylate–sulfonate ligands and strontium ions

Author links open overlay panelLeiGuanaYingWangb
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jssc.2015.07.008Get rights and content

Highlights

•

A novel coordination polymer with a CdSO4-type network structure was synthesized.

•

It shows a second harmonic generation response that is 4 times that of KH2PO4.

•

It is constructed from the helixes paralleled to each other.

Abstract

A novel strontium carboxylate–sulfonate coordination polymer, [Sr(HSIP)(H2O)3]n·nH2O (1) (NaH2SIP=5-sulfoisophthalic monosodium salt) has been synthesized by hydrothermal reaction. It was characterized by X-ray single crystal diffraction, infrared spectroscopy, elemental and thermogravimetric analysis. Each strontium atom is eight-coordinate with a distorted bicapped trigonal prismatic arrangement. The whole HSIP2− ligand acts as a η5μ4 bridge to generate three-fold interpenetrated CdSO4-type network structure, which is constructed from the left- and right-handed helixes paralleled to each other bridged by the HSIP2− ligands. The luminescence spectrum indicates an emission maximum at 459 nm. Compound 1 shows a second harmonic generation (SHG) response that is 4 times that of KH2PO4.

Graphical abstract

The whole HSIP2− ligands act as η5μ4 bridges with strontium ions, and the strontium ion is eight-coordinated, showing a distorted bicapped trigonal prism geometry.

1. Introduction

Recently, various metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with fascinating structural diversities as well as their interesting properties were synthesized by the self-assembly of suitable metal salts through choosing carefully rigid ligands [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], among which the carboxylic ligands were widely investigated, such as benzoic acid [6], 1,3-benzenedicarboxylate [7], 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate [8] and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylate [9], because of the diversity of the binding modes of carboxylate group to metal atom. Some new synthetic methods, such as surfactant-thermal method, have been used, beside the solvothermal method used in synthesizing MOFs [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. However, introduction of a sulfonate group into rigid carboxylate ligands may result in the formation of unexpected frameworks, as the coordination of the sulfonate group may form highly flexible frameworks, which result in a potential for structural transformation involving a significant rearrangement of the bonds in the solid [17]. The sulfonate group is able to perform a wide range of coordination modes, which lead to complexes with diverse structures and properties [18], [19].

A multifunctional SIP3− ligand has been chosen to build open frameworks owing to the following two considerations: (a) a multifunctional ligand can realize the combination of metals with distinct electric configurations in MOFs, and a simple MOF built of a metal and organic ligand with a coordination preference is more likely to be obtained; and (b) carboxylic ligands have been demonstrated to be a predominant structural linkage to construct MOFs with transition metals, and organic sulfonates prefer to coordinate to alkaline earth metals [20], [21], [22], [23], [24], [25].

In addition to the organic ligands, the rational and controllable synthesis of MOFs is greatly affected by the nature of the metal ions [26], [27], [28]. In the past ten years, the studies of MOFs concentrated on transition metal complexes [29]. However, the formation of MOFs with s-block metal ions, especially alkaline earth metal ions, has not been widely investigated [30]. Because of the large radius of alkaline earth metals, such as calcium, strontium, barium, these metal ions have large coordination numbers. Alkaline earth metals have several advantages for application in materials science; they are nontoxic, cheap, and generally amenable to aqueous preparation [31], [32].

Herewith, we report on the synthesis and characterization of a novel second-order non-linear optical coordination polymer with three-fold interpenetrated CdSO4-type network constructed by HSIP2− ligands and strontium ions. Accurate SHG, photoluminescent, thermogravimetric analysis, and IR measurements were performed.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials and general measurements

All reagents were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. X-ray single crystal diffraction data were collected at 298(2) K from a single crystal mounted atop a glass fiber with a Bruker Apex-II diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Ka (λ=0.71073 Å) radiation. Elemental analysis was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C instrument. The FT-IR spectrum was recorded on a Nicolette FTIR spectrometer using KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis was carried out on a NETZSCH STA 449 C unit at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under an nitrogen atmosphere. Photoluminescence analysis was performed on a Perkin-Elemer LS55 fluorescence spectrometer. The measurements of the powder frequency-doubling effects were carried out by the method reported [33]. A 1064 nm radiation generated by a Q-switched Nd: YAG solid-state laser was used as the fundamental frequency light. The crystal samples were ground and sieved into several distinct particle sizes (30–40 μm, 46–62 μm, 63–84 μm, 135–165 μm, 191–228 μm, 235–277 μm). The samples with distinct particle sizes were packed in round glass boxes and then they were exposed to laser radiation. Second harmonic radiation generated by the randomly oriented microcrystals was detected by a photomultiplier tube and displayed on an oscilloscope.

2.2. Synthesis of compound 1

A mixture of SrCl2 (0.159 g, 1 mmol), NaH2SIP (0.268 g, 1 mmol), and water (15 mL) was sealed into a bomb equipped with a Teflon liner, and heated at 160 °C for 3 days; colorless crystals of compound 1 were obtained when the sample was cooled to room temperature. The crystals were recovered by filtering, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (yield: 72% based on Sr). Elemental Anal. Calc.(%) for C8H12O11SSr: C23.77, H2.97. Found(%): C24.19, H3.49.

2.3. X-ray crystallographic measurement

Data collection for compound 1 was carried out on a Bruker Smart CCD diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromated Mo Ka radiation (λ=0.71073 Å) at 298 K. Data reduction was performed with SAINT [34], and empirical absorption corrections were applied by the SADABS program. Structures were solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program and refined with the SHELXL program [35]. Heavy atoms and other non-hydrogen atoms were directly obtained from a difference Fourier map. Final refinements were performed by full-matrix least-squares methods with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms on F2. C-bonded H atoms were placed geometrically and refined as riding model. O-bonded H atoms were placed in idealized positions and constrained to ride on their parent atoms. Crystal data as well as details of data collection and refinement for compound 1 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond distances and bond angles are given in Table 2. Hydrogen bond lengths and bond angles are listed in Table S1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Structure descriptin of compound 1

Compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Ama2. Its asymmetry unit consists of one crystallographically independent strontium ion, one HSIP2− ligand, three coordinated water molecules and one lattice water molecule (Fig. 1a). The coordination arrangement of the Sr1 atom is a eight-coordinate distorted bicapped trigonal prism (Fig. 1b). Its coordination sites are occupied by two oxygen atoms (O3, and O4) from one carboxylate group and three oxygen atoms (O1, O2i and O2ii) from three different sulfonate groups, and three terminal water molecules (O7, O7iii and O8). The Sr–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.542(7)–2.826(8) Å. The O–Sr–O bond angles are in the range of 47.4(3)–166.3(3)° (Table 2). These distances and angles are comparable to those reported in other metal–carboxylate–sulfonates [33]. The HSIP2− ligand shows only one kind of bridging mode (Fig. 1c), it links four strontium ions, where one carboxylate group chelates to one strontium ion, the sulfonate group adopts a μ1−η1:η1:η1 coordination mode to coordinate with three different strontium ions, respectively. The oxygen atoms of the other carboxylate function do not participate in coordination.

In addition, the chelating and bridging coordination modes of HSIP2− ligands we reported are different from the ones reported in references. As reported, the SIP3− ligand displays diverse coordination modes, which together with the versatile coordination ability of the bridging ligand makes it adaptable to metal ions of different sizes and leads to the various topologies of the coordination networks [36]. As depicted in reference, the two carboxylate groups of SIP3− adopt different coordination modes, μ2−η1:η1-bridging, and μ2−η2:η1-bridging. The sulfonate group coordinates to three different barium ions in a tridentate μ3 mode to generate a ladder-like one-dimensional chain [37].

The strontium centers can be simplified as the nodes of the 3D framework structure, and the HSIP2− ligands can be seen the connected bridges. The HSIP2− ligands coordinate to strontium ions, forming a rigid [Sr(HSIP)] secondary building units (SBU). This kind of SBU is widely observed in metal–carboxylate–sulfonate compounds [36]. Then the SBU are interconnected to generate the linear [Sr(HSIP)]n SBU. These SBU chains further connect to the neighboring ones through sulfonate oxygen atoms, generating a 2D bilayer structure, which is best described as a tongue-and-groove structure. These tongue-and-groove structures can be assembled into a 3D CdSO4-type (65.8) network (Fig. 2). The network is three-fold interpenetrated to each other in structure of compound 1, which is stabilized by the hydrogen bonding interactions (Table S1). Up to now, a large number of CdSO4-type frameworks have been reported, such as self-threading 3D (65.8) CdSO4-type framework [Co2(HL)2(L)(H2Mo8O28)] (L=1,1′-(1,4-butanediyl)bis(imidazole)) [38], 3D silicate CdSO4-type framework Rb3In(H2O)Si5O13 [39], five-fold interpenetrated CdSO4-type network [Ag(C5H4NCONH2)2][BF4] [40], two-fold interpenetrated CdSO4 coordination network of the formula {[Cu2(4-pmpmd)2(CH3OH)4(opd)2]·2H2O}n (4-pmpmd=N,N′-bis(4-pyridylmethyl)phenyldiimide; opd=o-phthalic acid) [41], and non-interpenetrated CdSO4 topology {[Cu(iph)(dpa)]·0.5H2O}n (iph=isophthalate) [42]. However, the CdSO4-type framework constructed from HSIP2− ligand has never been observed in coordination chemistry. From the description above, we can see that two factors play important roles in the formation of CdSO4-type network structure: (i) the existence of rigid bridging linker HSIP2− ligand, where one of the carboxylate groups is protonated and does not coordinate with metal ions; (ii) the presence of sulfonate group, which can coordinate with metal ions in the three directions, utilizing three sulfonate oxygen atoms.

Viewed along the a-axis, the structural feature of compound 1 can be easily found out. Obviously, the basic quadrilateral helical motifs of compound 1 are shown in Fig. 3. Further analysis shows that the quadrilateral helices [Sr1–O2–S1–O1]n in compound 1 are left-handed or right-handed, as shown in Fig. 3a and b. The dimensions of the helical channel are about 4.0×4.0 Å. The helices are interconnected to form a honeycomb-like structure along the b axis, as shown in Fig. 3c. The left- and right-handed helical chains are generated around the crystallographic axis with a long pitch of 8.49 Å. It is noted that the adjacent helical chains are connected by the HSIP2− ligands as shown in Fig. 3c, where each left- or right-handed helical chain is constructed by strontium ions and sulfonate groups of SIP3- ligands. The structure of compound 1 can be considered as the combination of many parallel helical chains. This result is different from the one reported. As illustrated in reference [33], the net is constructed by two types of hexagonal helical chains with opposite chirality (left and right-handed helices) along the b axis. Each hexagonal helix connects to six adjacent helices by sharing a common edge, which projects as a (6,3) net. The dimensions of the helical channel are about 7.6×9.1 Å.

3.2. FT-IR

The FT-IR spectrum of compound 1 is shown in Figure S1. It exhibits broad absorption band between 3486 and 3629 cm−1 due to the presence of water molecules in the asymmetric structure. The strong bands between 1398 and 1706 cm−1 are characteristics of the carboxylate groups. The absorption bands in the region of 1062–1278 cm−1 are typical of the sulfonate group. The absorption bands around 894 cm−1 and 798 cm−1 are attributed to the bending vibrations of C–H. Additional bands centered at 771 and 698 cm−1 can be due to Sr–O stretching vibrations and bending vibrations of the carboxylate groups [43].

3.3. Second-order non-linear optical property

In view of the fact that compound 1 crystallized in the noncentrosymmetric space group Ama2, so, the SHG measurements were investigated on sieved powdered samples of compound 1. Fig. 4 shows the relative SHG intensity verses particle size for compound 1, which indicates that the compound 1 was phase-matchable. A comparison of the second-harmonic signal produced by samples of compound 1 and KH2PO4 in the same particle range 135–165 μm reveals that compound 1 exhibits a SHG efficiency 4 times that of KH2PO4. The SHG efficiency can be mainly attributed to the unioriented arrangement of the helical chains in compound 1. The lone pair-containing strontium ion and the HSIP2− ligand could be the two potential non-linear optical phore units. The present work serves as an elegant example in which second-order non-linear optical materials can be rationally designed based on a aromatic sulfonate–carboxylate ligand. We are currently extending such a synthetic technique to explore other SHG-active metal sulfonate–carboxylates [33].

3.4. Luminescent property

Previous studies have shown that polymeric compounds exhibit photoluminescent properties [44]. Hence, we investigated the photoluminescent property of compound 1. The room temperature solid state photo luminescent spectra of compound 1 and its related ligand are presented in Fig. 5. Compound 1 exhibits an intense emission maximum at 459 nm and three shoulder peaks at 419 nm, 439 nm and 489 nm by using an excitation wavelength of 315 nm. In order to access the emission behavior, a comparison with the corresponding spectrum of the NaH2SIP ligand was made. The free ligand exhibits emission band with maxima at 356 nm upon excitation at 315 nm. The emissions of compound 1 occur at much longer wavelength (the red shift is higher than 100 nm) than the free ligand. The large bathochromic shifts in compound 1 indicate that the functional groups of ligand are effectively interacting with the metal ions [45]. Similar bathochromic shifts are observed also in other polymeric compounds [46]. In addition, the fluorescence spectrum of compound 1 in the peak profile is indicative of the slight influence of the coordination geometry of strontium ions and coordination modes of HSIP2− ligands on the electronic structures. This indicates that the emission bands in compound 1 arise neither from intraligand nor from metal-to-metal charge transfer, but attribute to the ligand-to-metal charge transition. The blue emissions observed for compound 1 in the solid state imply that it may be potentially applicable as materials for blue-light-emitting diode devices [47].

3.5. Thermal analysis

The thermogravimetric analysis diagram of compound 1 indicates three main steps of weight losses, as shown in Fig. 6. The first dehydration step is completed at 217 °C with mass reduction of 4.29% (cacl. 4.46%), indicating the loss of one lattice water molecule. Next dehydration occurs in the temperature range of 217 °C to 560 °C (exp. mass loss ca. 12.38%, calc. 13.37%) corresponding to the loss of the three coordination water molecules. The deaquation temperature range supports the presence of H-bonding among the coordinated, lattice water molecules and HSIP2− carboxylate groups. On further heating, the HSIP2− ligand is decomposed in the temperature range of 560–900 °C. The total weight loss is ca. 57.22% with a final residue of the mixture of strontium oxide and strontium sulfate [47].

4. Conclusion

In summary, The HSIP2− ligand and strontium ions were employed to synthesize a novel coordination polymer, [Sr(HSIP)(H2O)3]n·nH2O, under hydrothermal condition. It features a three-fold interpenetrated CdSO4-type network structure, which derived from the left- and right-handed helixes paralleled to each other interconnected by the HSIP2- ligands through the coordination interaction of the sulfonate and carboxylate groups to strontium ions, adopting μ3η3- and μ2η1-bridging modes. It exhibits a SHG signal and is phase-matchable. Its photoluminescent property is investigated, and exhibits a photoluminescent peak with a maximum at 459 nm. All of these attributes make it an attractive candidate for future practical applications.
