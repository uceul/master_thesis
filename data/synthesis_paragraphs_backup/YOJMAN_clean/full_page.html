Assembly of organic–inorganic hybrid materials constructed from polyoxometalate and metal–1,2,4-triazole units: synthesis, structures, magnetic, electrochemical and photocatalytic properties†

A series of organic–inorganic hybrid compounds built from Keggin and Wells–Dawson polyoxometalates and TM-trz complexes (Htrz = 1,2,4-triazole) were obtained at different pH values under hydrothermal conditions, namely, [Co2(H2O)2(Htrz)5][SiW12O40]·2.5H2O (1), [Zn5(Htrz)8(trz)2(H2O)8][SiW12O40]2·10H2O (2), [CuI2CuII4(Htrz)4(trz)4Cl2][GeW12O40]·15H2O (3), [CuI2CuII4(Htrz)4(trz)4Cl2][SiW12O40]·15H2O (4), K2Na2(H2O)2[Co11(trz)14(H2O)14][P2W18O62]2·29H2O (5) and [Cu12(trz)10(Htrz)2(OH)4(SO4)2(H2O)6][P2W18O62]·28H2O (6). All of the compounds were characterized using single-crystal X-ray diffraction, TG analysis and powder X-ray diffraction. Compound 1 contains a kind of bimetallic–organic segment [Co2(Htrz)5]4+, which could be regarded as a quasi-sinusoid-like chain. 2 is a dimer constructed from two [SiW12O40]4− units linked by a [Zn5(trz)2(Htrz)8]8+ motif. In 3 and 4, the 2D wall-like layers constructed by the Cu(II) ions and Htrz ligands are extended into 3D frameworks by polyanions. 5 shows a complicated three-dimensional (3D) framework with a {412·612·84}{46}2 topology. Compound 6 is also a complicated 3D structure based on a triangular trinuclear Cu-trz unit and [P2W18O62] polyanions. To the best of our knowledge, 6 represents the largest copper cluster linked by amines in a POM system. Structural diversities reveal that the pH value of the reaction system plays a crucial role in the assembly of POM-based hybrids. Moreover, the optical band gaps, electrochemical properties, magnetic properties and the photocatalytic properties of the compounds have been investigated.

Introduction

Polyoxometalates (POMs), famous as an intriguing and distinctive class of inorganic metal–oxygen clusters, have become one of the most varied growing areas in inorganic chemistry and have merited enduring attention not only because of their compositional diversity and structural versatility, but also owing to their promising applications in the fields of catalysis, magnetism, electrochemistry, photochemistry, and medicine.1,2 However, POMs as inorganic crystalline materials have their own disadvantages in compatibility and processability, which hinder the further engineering of POMs into multifunctional materials. As a consequence, the design and assembly of novel POM-based organic–inorganic hybrids with charming structures and ‘value-adding’ properties has been an attractive and challenging mission in POM chemistry.3

To date, a multitude of POM-based hybrids have been reported, however, examples of high dimensional POM-supported hybrids, especially with polynuclear metal clusters, are still relatively rare. Currently, the largest copper cluster linked by organic amine ligands in POM-based hybrids is the nonanuclear subunit of {Cu9(2-ptz)12(H2O)6(PMo12O40)2}·H2O.4 In this regard, the judicious choice of appropriate organic ligands is extremely important in the self-assembly process of prospective POM-based multinuclear clusters, seeing that organic ligands may control and adjust the topologies of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs).5 Generally, N-donor organic ligands with a smaller steric hindrance, more donor atoms, flexible coordination sites and good fitting of the coordination environment of metal ions and POMs are beneficial for the construction of POM-supported hybrids containing polynuclear metal complexes. In light of this, unsubstituted 1-H-1,2,4-triazole (abbreviated to ‘trz’) has captured our attention because of its strong and typical coordination capacity for bridging metal ions to result in polynuclear clusters with unusual structural diversity, combining the coordination mode of both imidazoles and pyrazoles to provide more potential coordination sites.6 Besides this, metal–triazole polynuclear clusters have been used as secondary building units to construct stable and high-dimensional MOFs.7 Notwithstanding, 1,2,4-triazole has not been widely employed in the POM-based inorganic–organic systems, due to its inherent deficiencies such as its very short bridging length and the ease of forming highly insoluble and intractable products, which are difficult to curb and control at the molecular level.8 However, Lu et al. finally made an important breakthrough in 2010, in which trz ligands played a supportive role in the polycatenane compound {Ag26(trz)20}[PW12O40]2 containing an adamantine-like {Ag24(trz)18}6+ nanocage.9 Subsequently, the novel metal–organic pseudorotaxane framework Ag14(trz)10[SiW12O40] and POM-based supramolecular aggregate Ag10(Htrz)2(trz)6[SiW12O40]2 were discovered.10

In view of the prominent work of the above mentioned, recently we have thus initiated a study on the reaction of 1,2,4-triazole and transition metals in the presence of classical Keggin/Wells–Dawson POMs. Herein, a series of novel POM-based inorganic–organic hybrids, namely, [Co2(H2O)2(Htrz)5][SiW12O40]·2.5H2O (1), [Zn5(Htrz)8(trz)2(H2O)8][SiW12O40]2·10H2O (2), [CuI2CuII4(Htrz)4(trz)4Cl2][GeW12O40]·15H2O (3), [CuI2CuII4(Htrz)4(trz)4Cl2][SiW12O40]·15H2O (4), K2Na2(H2O)2[Co11(trz)14(H2O)14][P2W18O62]2·29H2O (5) and [Cu12(trz)10(Htrz)2(OH)4(SO4)2(H2O)6][P2W18O62]·28H2O (6), have been successfully obtained under hydrothermal conditions. Compounds 3 and 4 are isostructural three-dimensional frameworks, which are constructed from CuI/II–organic sheets and polyanions, possessing two-directional intersecting channels with window dimensions of 9.4 × 5.5 Å2 along the a axis and 8.9 × 6.8 Å2 along the b axis, respectively. It is noteworthy that compound 5 contains the largest-ever number of cobalt ions found in a {P2W18}-based inorganic–organic hybrid. One structural feature of 6 is that it possesses a triangular trinuclear Cu-trz unit-based motif, in which an SO42− anion as the connector provides a great benefit for aggregating Cu3-triads into larger moieties and further generating a novel 3D framework. To the best of our knowledge, 6 features the largest copper cluster in a POM system. The optical band gaps, electrochemical properties and magnetic properties have also been investigated in detail. Besides these, compounds 5 and 6 are employed as heterogeneous catalysts for the photocatalytic degradation of methylene blue (MB), with a fairly high catalytic activity, which provides impetus for the further development of photocatalysts from other inorganic–organic hybrid materials based on multinuclear subunits and polyoxometalates.

X-ray crystallography

A suitable single crystal of each compound was glued onto a glass fiber. Data collection was performed on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer with Mo Kα (λ = 0.71069 Å) at room temperature. A multi-scan correction was applied. The structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full matrix least-squares on F2 using the SHELXL 97 program.12 During the refinement of these crystal structures, all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The H atoms on their mother carbon and nitrogen atoms were located in their calculated positions. Restraints were used to resolve the ADP and NPD errors of some O, C and N atoms by using the “ISOR” and “SIMU” instructions. Moreover, some of the five-membered rings of the organic ligands were also restrained using the “AFIX” and “DELU” commands. Furthermore, compounds 5 and 6 exhibited extra solvent accessible voids in the final refinement, but the residual peaks were too weak to be confirmed as solvent molecules. Thus, the SQUEEZE program13 was used to further estimate possible solvent accessible voids and the number of solvent water molecules in the crystal structure. Considering that an amount of disordered solvent molecules exists in 3 and 4, the SQUEEZE process has been used to remove the electronic contribution of the unidentified solvents. After the SQUEEZE process, the result implies that 216 for 3 (156 for 4) removed electrons have been found. When the electron densities, TGA, and elemental analyses are taken into account, 15 water molecules are determined. The crystal structure of 5 contains large accessible voids that are large enough to fit potential molecules. Based on charge-balance considerations, elemental analyses, and TGA, 17 water molecules were included in the molecular formula directly. Physical techniques

Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were recorded on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer using a Cu Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation source at room temperature in the 2θ range of 5° to 50°. IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm−1 on an Alpha Centaurt FT/IR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) were performed on a Perkin-Elmer TGA7 instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Diffuse reflectivity was measured from 200 to 800 nm using barium sulfate (BaSO4) as a standard with 100% reflectance on a Varian Cary 500 UV–vis spectrophotometer. Magnetic susceptibilities were measured using finely ground single crystal samples (grease restricted) with the use of a Quantum Design SQUID magnetometer MPMS-XL.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

Hydrothermal synthesis has recently proven to be a powerful technique in the preparation of organic–inorganic hybrid materials, because hydrothermal conditions can allow the reaction to shift from the thermodynamic to the kinetic so that the equilibrium phases are replaced by structurally more complicated metastable phases.14 In the process of hydrothermal synthesis, several factors can influence the formation of crystal phases, such as initial reactants, molar ratio, pH value, reaction time, temperature, etc. Subtle changes to each factor may cause great alterations to the assembly process, further impacting on the construction of the structures. In this work, many parallel experiments have proven that the pH value of the reaction is a determining factor for the isolation of compounds 1–6. Compound 1 could only be obtained in the pH range 2–4. If the pH value of the reaction was higher than 4, only large amounts of unidentified powders were obtained. At pH = 2, a high yield of 1 could be ensured. Similarly, compound 2 was obtained at pH = 3.3. Compound 5 could be synthesized in the pH region between 3.5 and 5. Increasing the pH above 5 resulted in amorphous pink-colored powder. Experimental results revealed that the formation of compounds 1, 2 and 5 was purely pH dependent. As our exploration continued, when K8[γ-GeW10O36]·6H2O was reacted with CuCl2·2H2O in the presence of 1,2,4-triazole at a pH of 3, 3 was discovered. In a similar process to that used for 3, but with K8[γ-SiW10O36]·12H2O substituted for K8[γ-GeW10O36]·6H2O, compound 4 was consecutively made at pH = 2.8, suggesting that small differences in the shape and size of the polyanions [SiW10] and [GeW10] do not affect the assembly results of the POM/Cu/trz system. However, when a dilacunary Keggin polyoxoanion ([SiW10]/[GeW10]) was displaced by a saturated polyoxoanion ([SiW12]/[GeW12]), 3 or 4 could not be formed. Besides, when the CuII ion was replaced with CoII, FeII, MnII, NiII, ZnII, CdII or AgI ions, only amorphous powders were obtained. As described above, the inherent essentiality of two dilacunary precursors and the nature of TM cations can affect the formation of crystal phases. Besides, it deserves a mention that 3 and 4 are mixed-valent compounds with Cu(II)/Cu(I) oxidation states, even though the starting copper precursor is only in the +2 oxidation state. Such a phenomenon has been observed frequently in hydrothermal reaction systems containing N-donor ligands and CuII ions.15 Compound 6 was prepared by reaction of the K6[α-P2W18O62]·15H2O, CuSO4·5H2O and 1,2,4-triazole ligands at a pH of 1.8–3. When CuSO4 was replaced by another CuII salt, such as CuCl2·2H2O, Cu(CH3COO)2·2H2O or Cu(NO3)2·3H2O, compound 6 could not be afforded, implying that CuSO4 may serve as an additive or structural directing agent for the formation of 6. Accordingly, the nature of the CuII salt is crucial for the isolation of 6. In summary, the pH of the solution, the nature of the metal salt and the structure of the polyoxoanion play important roles in the crystallization of 3, 4 and 6.

Crystal structure of 1

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2/n; the relevant crystal data and structural refinement parameters are presented in Table 1. As shown in Fig. 1a, 1 consists of two Co atoms, five Htrz ligands, one [SiW12O40]4− cluster, and 4.5 water molecules. In [SiW12O40]4− anion, the Si1 atom lies on an inversion centre and the central four μ4 oxygen atoms are observed to be disordered over eight positions with each oxygen site half-occupied. This structural feature of disorder often appears in Keggin structures, which has been explained in the previous literature.16 The Si–O bonds range from 1.566(9) to 1.682(9) Å (mean value 1.615 Å), while the W–O distances can be grouped into three sets: W–Ot (terminal) 1.620(1)–1.698(2) Å, W–Oc (central) 2.306(5)–2.476(9) Å, and W–Ob (bridge) 1.838(0)–1.939(1) Å. The crystallographically independent Co1 exhibits a slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry, defined by four N atoms from four Htrz ligands, one terminal O atom from [SiW12O40]4− and one O atom from a water molecule (Co–O 2.166(2) Å, Co–Ow 2.131(6) Å, Co–N 2.066(9)–2.135(9) Å). All of the bond lengths are within normal ranges. In 1, the Htrz ligands exhibit two different coordination modes: three of them serve as bridging ligands in a common N1, N2 mode to link with two Co1 atoms, one of which lies about a twofold axis (with N5 on the twofold axis); the others only conjugate with Co1 by means of one N atom, resulting in the metal–organic motif [Co2(Htrz)5]4+. The complexes [Co2(Htrz)5]4+ and the [SiW12O40]4− anions are alternatively arranged in a 1D zigzag chain (Fig. 1b). The adjacent 1D chains are connected by N–H⋯O hydrogen bonding interactions between the molecular sheets (Fig. 1c), which are further extended into a 3D supramolecular framework by hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. S1†). Bond valence sum calculations confirm that all of the W centers are in +6 oxidation states, the Si center is in a +4 oxidation state, and the Co center is in a +2 oxidation state.17

Crystal structure of 2

Compound 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. In the asymmetric unit, there are one [SiW12O40]4− anion, two and a half Zn atoms, four Htrz ligands, one trz ligand, four coordinated water molecules and five lattice water molecules. The Zn1 ion is six-coordinated by three nitrogen atoms from three Htrz ligands, one nitrogen atom from a trz ligand, and two oxygen atoms from two coordination water molecules, showing a distorted octahedral geometry. Zn2 is defined by two nitrogen atoms from two Htrz ligands, one nitrogen atom from a trz ligand, one terminal oxygen atom from a [SiW12O40]4− anion, and two oxygen atoms from two coordination water molecules. The Zn3 atom lies on an inversion centre, which is coordinated by two nitrogen atoms from two Htrz ligands, two nitrogen atoms from two trz ligands and two terminal oxygen atoms from two [SiW12O40]4− anions. Crystal structures of 3 and 4

Taking into account that compounds 3 and 4 are isostructural with only slight differences in their bond lengths and bond angles, 3 is described as the representative structure. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis exhibits that 3 contains a new bi-supporting cluster based on the [GeW12O40]4− (shortened as GeW12) anion (Fig. 3a). Compound 3 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. Consequently the two supporting equivalent metal–organic fragments [CuII2CuI(Htrz)2(trz)2Cl]2+ are inter-related by the inversion centre of the Ge atom. The GeW12 anion exhibits a distorted α-Keggin configuration. In the GeW12 anion, the Ge1 atom lies on an inversion centre and the central four μ4 oxygen atoms are observed to be disordered over eight positions with each oxygen site half-occupied. In compound 3, there are four crystallographically independent Cu ions exhibiting two kinds of coordination geometries. Crystal structure of 5

Compound 5 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P2/n. As shown in Fig. 4, the structure of 5 contains two kinds of motifs: P2W18 polyanions and [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ cations. The P2W18 polyoxoanion in 5 retains a classical Wells–Dawson structure, containing two [α-A-PW9O34]9− units originating from the α-Keggin polyoxoanion by removal of a suite of three corner-sharing WO6 octahedra. The P–O and W–O lengths are in normal ranges. The [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ subunit is built from two [Co5] fragments and a central [CoN6] octahedron. The [Co5] fragment is composed of one {Co2} dimer and one {Co3} triad, which are linked together by two trz ligands (Fig. S2†). Co1 is coordinated by three N atoms from three trz ligands, two O atoms from two P2W18 anions and one water molecule. Co3 exhibits a six-coordination environment with five N atoms derived from five trz ligands and one terminal O atom from a P2W18 anion. Co1 and Co3 are linked together by three trz ligands, giving rise to a {Co2} dimer. Co2 is defined by three N atoms from three trz ligands, one terminal O atom from a P2W18 anion, and two water molecules. Co5 is coordinated by two N atoms from two trz ligands, two terminal O atoms from two P2W18 anions, and two O atoms from water molecules. Co6 is in the octahedral coordination sphere, bonded to two N atoms from two trz ligands, and four O atoms which stem from two P2W18 anions and two water molecules. Co5 and Co6 are linked together through two trz ligands from [Co2N3O3] octahedra, resulting in a {Co3} triad. The Co4 atom, lying on an inversion centre, bonds to six N atoms from six trz ligands, which bridge two adjacent [Co5] units, generating a [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ unit (Fig. 5a). The P2W18 cluster acts as an octadentate inorganic ligand coordinating with four [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ motifs through eight terminal O atoms (Fig. S3†), while the [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ fragments act as bridging groups linking eight adjacent P2W18 anions (Fig. 5b), thereby forming the 3D architecture (Fig. 5c). To comprehend the structure better, the 3D structure can be rationalized as a (4, 8) connected flu (fluorite) network with the point symbol {412·612·84}{46}2, if we assign the P2W18 cluster as a 4-connected node and the [Co11(trz)14(H2O)14]8+ subunit as an 8-connected node. An interesting structural feature of compound 5 is that the P2W18 polyanion acts as a decadentate inorganic ligand, providing seven terminal oxygen atoms in the equatorial plane and three terminal oxygen atoms in the axial position. Notably, this uncommon coordination pattern of P2W18 polyanions is still relatively scarce, which facilitates the formation of high-connectivity Wells–Dawson POMs. To our knowledge, 5 contains the largest-ever number of cobalt ions in a {P2W18}-based inorganic–organic hybrid.

Crystal structure of 6

Compound 6 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/m. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that the structure of 6 contains two kinds of motifs: P2W18 polyanions and [Cu12(trz)10(Htrz)2(OH)4(SO4)2(H2O)6]6+ cations. These crystals have crystallographically imposed symmetry disorder, in which S1 and S2 are at sites with 2/m imposed symmetry while P1 lies on a twofold axis. There are four independent copper atoms. The Cu1 ion sitting on a mirror plane, is defined by two N atoms from two trz ligands, one μ3-O atom, one O atom from SO42− and two O atoms from aqua ligands. Cu2 is coordinated by three N atoms from three trz ligands, one μ3-O atom and one O atom from SO42− as well as one O atom from a P2W18 anion, resulting in a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cu3 atom lies on a mirror plane, which is in the octahedral coordination sphere, bonded to three N atoms from three trz ligands, one μ3-O atom and two O atoms which stem from SO42− anions and water molecules. The Cu4 ion has the same coordination environment as the Cu2 ion. It should be pointed out that two triangular trinuclear copper units are formed in compound 6, namely, [Cu3(trz)3(OH)(H2O)2]2+ (abbreviated as Cu3A) and [Cu3(trz)2(Htrz)(OH)(H2O)]3+ (abbreviated as Cu3B). In the triangular CuII subunits, three copper cations (Cu2, Cu1, Cu2 or Cu4, Cu3, Cu4) are linked with three trz ligands in a pyrazole-like fashion and bridged by one central μ3-O atom, as shown in Fig. 6. The identical Cu3-triad units are joined together through shared central SO42− anions, leading to the Cu6 hexamers, {[Cu3(trz)3(OH)(H2O)2]2SO4}2+ (simplified as Cu6A) for Cu3A and {[Cu3(trz)2(Htrz)(OH)(H2O)]2SO4}4+ (simplified as Cu6B) for Cu3B, respectively. Interestingly, the Cu6A and Cu6B segments are connected in an orthogonal fashion by means of possessing four trz ligands jointly, and consequently forming into a unique 1D chain (Fig. 6). The adjacent chains are interlinked by the Cu3 from the triangular Cu3B units and the third vertex (N10) of the triangular Cu3A units, resulting in a 2D ladder layer. In addition, each P2W18 polyanion is covalently bonded to four {Cu12} units from two adjacent layers, and each {Cu12} segment is connected with four P2W18 anions, constructing a beautiful 3D framework (Fig. 7). It is noteworthy that the P2W18 anion acts as an octadentate inorganic ligand via offering eight terminal O atoms in the equatorial plane, which play an important role in forming the 3D architecture. Besides, in the absence of SO42−, the expected compound could not be afforded. In this aspect, undoubtedly, the SO42− anion as a connector provides a great benefit for aggregating the Cu3-triads into larger moieties and further generating the novel 3D structure. Up to now, the largest metal cluster linked by organic amine ligands in POM-based compounds is the nonanuclear subunit from {Cu9(2-ptz)12(H2O)6(PMo12O40)2}·H2O.4 To our knowledge, compound 6 represents the largest copper cluster linked by amines in a POM system.

Thermal analysis

To characterize the compounds more fully in terms of thermal stability, their thermal behaviors were studied by TGA. Thermal gravimetric analysis was performed under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 in the temperature range 50–800 °C, and is shown in Fig. S5–S10.† The TG curve of 1 exhibits three weight loss steps. The first weight loss of 1.58% (calcd 2.14%) below 200 °C can be attributed to the loss of three lattice water molecules. The second weight loss of 1.05% (calcd 1.46%) in the temperature range of 200–340 °C is assigned to the loss of two coordinated water molecules. In the range of 340–660 °C, a weight loss of 10.84% (calcd 10.07%) corresponds to the decomposition of five Htrz ligands. The TG curve of 2 is similar to that of 1; first it gradually loses ten lattice water molecules (exptl, 3.36%; calcd, 2.54%) from 50 to 200 °C. On further heating, the materials lose weight continuously during the second step with a weight loss of 2.66% (calcd, 2.03%) from 200 to 370 °C, due to the removal of eight coordinated water molecules. Above 370 °C, the whole framework collapses with the release of ten Htrz ligands (exptl, 9.74%; calcd, 10.54%). Compounds 3 and 4 exhibit similar weight loss steps. The water molecules are released (50–380 °C for 3, and 50–400 °C for 4) with a weight loss of 6.98% (calcd: 6.45%) for 3, and 6.14% (calcd: 6.52%) for 4. The Htrz ligands decompose (at 380–540 °C for 3, and 400–560 °C for 4) with a weight loss of 13.34% (calcd: 13.19%) for 3, and 13.72% (calcd: 13.33%) for 4, followed by the decomposition of the polyoxoanion. The TGA curve of compound 5 indicates that water molecules are eliminated from the network (calcd. 7.41%; found 7.55%) when the temperature is increased from 50 to about 400 °C, after which the removal of the organic components occurs (calcd. 8.85%; found 9.55%). For 6, the TGA curve shows two weight loss processes: the first one (10.41%) from 50 to 380 °C is equivalent to the loss of 38 water molecules (calcd. 10.03%); the other (11.89%) from 380 to 550 °C corresponds to the loss of twelve Htrz ligands (calcd. 12.16%). The products of the thermal decomposition are amorphous and were not further characterized. The observed experimental values were in good agreement with the elemental analyses and the results of single-crystal X-ray structural analyses.

Electrochemistry

As is well known, POMs have the capacity to deliver electrons to other species, thus undergoing reversible multi-electron redox processes. Consequently, POMs can be utilized as chemically bulk-modified carbon paste electrodes (CPEs) to investigate electrochemical properties with advantages in many aspects: they are inexpensive, easy to handle, and easy to prepare.18a,19 Compounds 1–6 are hydrothermally obtained and have a low solubility in water and common organic solvents. The electrochemical behavior of compounds 1–4 is similar, and the electrochemical behavior of compounds 5 and 6 is also homologous. Thus, the 3-CPE and 6-CPE cases have been taken as examples to study their electrochemical properties.

For the 3-CPE, in the given range of −650 to +200 mV (Fig. S11†), there are three reversible redox peaks (scan rate: 150 mV s−1) with half-wave potentials at −498 mV (I–I′), −358 mV (II–II′), −199 mV (III–III′) (vs. Ag/AgCl), respectively, which can be attributed to the redox processes of the WVI centers of the GeW12 polyanion. In addition, an additional quasi-reversible anodic peak (IV) located at +60 mV and the corresponding cathodic peak (−35 mV) are assigned to the redox processes of the Cu centers.

The cyclic voltammograms for 6-CPE in a 1 M H2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates in the potential range of −700 to +300 mV are presented in Fig. 8. The peak potentials change gradually following the scan rates from 100 to 500 mV s−1. The cathodic peak potentials shift toward the negative direction, and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shift toward the positive direction upon increasing the scan rate. As seen, the E1/2 peak potentials were located at −596 mV, −345 mV and −122 mV (scan rate: 150 mV s−1), respectively, which correspond to the redox processes of the P2W18 polyanion. The fourth reduction wave located at +61 mV and the corresponding oxidation process (+113 mV) is attributed to the redox processes of the Cu centers.20 As expected, the WVI-based wave is located at a more negative potential than that attributable to the Cu center. The peak (II–II′) currents are proportional to the square root of the scan rate, which suggests that the redox processes of 6-CPE are diffusion-controlled when the scan rate is higher than 100 mV s−1.21 The electrochemical results indicate that the parent anions maintain their redox ability in the hybrid solids, which can extend the application of these POM-based materials to electrochemistry.

UV–vis absorption spectra and optical band gaps

The UV–vis absorption spectra of the hybrid compounds are presented in Fig. S12.† The absorption spectra of compounds 1–6 all present one band centered around 270 nm, which is the characteristic band of polyoxometalates assigned as a ligand-to-metal charge-transfer (LMCT) transition (O → W).22 For 1 and 5, the absorption band in the visible region at 500 nm arises from the d–d electron transition of Co centers. In the visible light region (at 590 nm), the spectra of the complexes 3, 4 and 6 present additional bands attributed respectively to the copper centers. The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) are composed of oxygen 2p and metal d orbitals, respectively. The diffuse reflectance UV–vis spectra of the powder samples were measured to achieve the HOMO–LUMO gap (E), which was determined as the intersection point between the energy axis and the line extrapolated from the linear portion of the adsorption edge in a plot of the Kubelka–Munk function (Fhν)0.5 against E (F = (1 − R)2/2R, where R is the reflectance of an infinitely thick layer at a given wavelength).23 As illustrated in Fig. S13,† the corresponding well-defined optical absorption associated with HOMO–LUMO (E) can be assessed at 2.72 eV for 1, 2.82 eV for 2, 2.28 eV for 3, 2.34 eV for 4, 2.41 eV for 5 and 2.84 eV for 6, which reveals that the band gaps of the title compounds fall into the range of semiconductors.

Photocatalytic properties

Recently, photocatalytic properties of POMs have attracted great attention due to their potential applications in purifying water resources. Taking their water solubility into account, typical POM salts may cause secondary contamination when they are used as photocatalysts to degrade organic dyes. In contrast, POM-based inorganic–organic hybrids display big potential because of their precious advantages of stability, ease of operation, and non-secondary pollution.20e,24 In this field, methylene blue (MB), as a model of dye contaminants, is often employed for evaluating the photocatalytic effectiveness of the purification of wastewater.25

In this paper, the photocatalytic performances of compounds 5 and 6 were investigated with photodegradation of MB under UV irradiation through a typical process: 50 mg of the compounds was suspended into 100 mL of a 1.0 × 10−5 mol L−1 MB aqueous solution in a beaker by ultrasonic dispersion for 10 min, magnetically stirred in the dark for about 30 min to ensure a surface-adsorption equilibrium. The mixture was then exposed to UV irradiation from a 125 W high pressure Hg lamp and stirred continuously. At given time intervals, 3 mL aliquots were taken out of the beaker, followed by several centrifugations to remove the particles. Then, a clear solution was obtained for UV–vis analysis.

The photodegradation of MB assisted by compounds 5 and 6 is shown in Fig. 9a and b. It is obvious that the absorption peak of MB decreases significantly as time goes by. For comparison, insoluble (NBu4)6[P2W18O62] (Fig. 9c) was also employed in the same catalytic experiments. After irradiation for 120 min, the photocatalytic decomposition rates of MB, are 97.3% for 5, 89.9% for 6, and 42.5% for (NBu4)6[P2W18O62], respectively. Furthermore, the degradation rate curves for compounds 5 and 6 are nearly linear (Fig. 9d). Additionally, the photostabilities of compounds 5 and 6 were monitored by measuring their PXRD patterns after the course of the photocatalytic processes. The PXRD patterns are nearly identical to those of the original compounds (Fig. S14†). The results indicate that compounds 5 and 6 as stable photocatalysts show outstanding photocatalytic activity for the degradation of MB, which illustrates that the formation of POM-based hybrids could improve the photocatalytic performance. This is probably due to incorporating transition metal cations into POMs can increase the valence band position by hybridization of the O 2p with the d or s orbitals of metal cations.26 As reported, metal-substitution in POMs is a viable route for tuning the electronic structure and hence the photochemical reactivity of the cluster.

POMs have similar light absorption and electrochemical band-edge positions to those of TiO2. Ultraviolet light induces POMs to undergo oxygen-to-metal charge transfers (OMCT), promoting electrons from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). After excitation by UV light, *POM is produced, which abstracts electrons from adsorbed water (H2Oads) molecules and holds the electrons (reactions (1) and (2)). The reduced POM (POM−) is rapidly reoxidized in the presence of O2 to generate superoxides (O2−) through reaction (3).27 Meanwhile, the MB dye is also excited by UV light to generate *MB molecules as shown in reaction (4). Finally, after several photo-oxidations, the MB dye is degraded into small molecules. Magnetic properties

Magnetic susceptibility data for 1, 3 and 5 were measured in the 2–300 K temperature range with an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe. Fig. 10a shows the magnetic behavior of compound 1 in the form of χMT versus T and χMversus T plots. At room temperature, the experimental χMT value is 5.81 cm3 K mol−1 higher than the expected spin-only value (χMT = 3.75 cm3 K mol−1, S = 3/2, assuming g = 2) for two high-spin Co(II) atoms in each Co2 unit per formula, which is caused by an unquenched orbital contribution arising from the 4T1g ground state of Co(II).28 As the system cools, χMT smoothly decreases over the entire temperature region. In the plot of χMversus T, a maximum at 13 K was observed, which suggests the nature of a typical antiferromagnetic interaction between the magnetic centers. In the low-temperature region below 4 K, the increase in the magnetic susceptibility suggests the presence of paramagnetic impurities in this compound.28

For 3, the observed χMT value of 0.81 cm3 K mol−1 at room temperature (Fig. 10b) is smaller than the calculated value of 1.5 cm3 K mol−1 for four isolated spin-only CuII ions for S = 1/2, g = 2.00. With decreasing temperature, the value of χMT decreases continuously to a value of 0.07 cm3 K mol−1 at about 2 K, indicating the presence of antiferromagnetic coupling interactions between the CuII ions.29

The magnetic susceptibility of compound 5 was measured in the 2–300 K temperature range under a 1000 Oe applied field. The χM slowly increases from 0.12 cm3 mol−1 at 300 K to 0.44 cm3 mol−1 at 50 K, and then rapidly reaches the maximum of 2.84 cm3 mol−1 at 2 K (Fig. 10c). The χMT product is 31.25 cm3 K mol−1, higher than the spin-only value (20.63 cm3 K mol−1) for eleven high-spin Co2+ ions (S = 3/2, g = 2.0), which illustrates that there is an appreciable spin–orbit coupling expected for octahedral coordinated Co2+ ions.30 Upon cooling, the χMT decreases and attains a minimum of 5.81 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. This behavior in all probability is indicative of antiferromagnetic interactions among the Co2+ ions.

Conclusion

In summary, a family of novel polyoxometalate-based metal organic frameworks constructed from polyoxometalates and metal–1,2,4-triazole units have been successfully prepared under hydrothermal conditions, and exhibit 0D dimer, 1D chain and 3D frameworks, respectively. Compound 1 emerges as a 1D chain built by [SiW12O40]4− anions via dinuclear [Co2(Htrz)5]4+ bridges, while 2 is a novel dimer constructed from two [SiW12O40]4− anions linked by a [Zn5(trz)2(Htrz)8]8+ motif. Compounds 3 and 4 are isostructural three-dimensional frameworks, which are made up from CuI/II–organic sheets and polyanions, possessing two-directional intersecting channels with window dimensions of 9.4 × 5.5 Å2 along the a axis and 8.9 × 6.8 Å2 along the b axis, respectively. Compound 5 contains the largest-ever number of cobalt ions in a {P2W18}-based inorganic–organic hybrid. Compound 6 has the characteristic of a triangular Cu-trz unit-based MOF, in which the SO42− anion as the connector provides a great benefit for generating a novel 3D framework. To the best of our knowledge, 6 represents the largest copper cluster linked by amines in a POM system. The preparation of compounds 1–6 may reveal a new route to explore novel metal–organic secondary building units based on TM ions and tridentate-bridging ligands by the use of POMs with different types, charges, sizes and shapes so as to construct multifunctional polyoxoanion–multinuclear transition metal complex hybrid materials with various architectures. In addition, compounds 5 and 6 exhibit remarkable photocatalytic activities for the degradation of MB, which sets a foundation for the further development of photocatalysts from other inorganic–organic hybrids based on multinuclear subunits and polyoxometalates.
