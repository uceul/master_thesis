A novel (3,4,6)-connected 3D porous cobaltâ€“organic framework formulated as {[Co2(Hbidc)2(bpt)2]Â·7H2O}n (1) (H3bidc = 1H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid, bpt = 1H-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4- triazole) has been hydrothermally synthesized and characterized by X-ray diffraction analysis. Compound 1, having a Co-Hbidc layer structure in which the layers are pillared by the bpt spacers in a 3D architecture, exhibits the coexistence of spin-canted weak ferromagnetism with TN = 10 K and long-range magnetic ordering.

Introduction

Multifunctional magnetic materials have attracted increasing attention recently due to the possibility of producing materials where magnetism can be modified by tuning other functionalities.1,2 Several research groups have reported some significant results in this field. In the past few years, Kobayashi et al. reported a number of molecule-based solids which combine electrical conductivity with magnetism.3 Optical, magnetic materials have also been obtained by several groups.4 In addition, chiral and switching (magnetism induced by light, pressure, adsorption of molecules, etc.) magnets have gained considerable attention because of their intriguing potential applications in enantioselective synthesis, asymmetric catalysis, and magnetic materials.5 Currently, an extended interest in this field is the design of molecular systems combining magnetism with porosity.6 Particularly, porous magnets displaying magnetic hysteresis are of great importance because of the search for new and more efficient magnetic recording materials.7 The most commonly used strategy to obtain these materials is to employ appropriate bridging ligands, which can not only transmit magnetic exchanges and bind to metal clusters or chains, but also stretch the metal building units into aesthetically pleasing porous frameworks.8â€“10 Careful selection of suitable organic ligands with certain features, such as versatile bonding modes and the ability to be involved in supramolecular interactions, is helpful for constructing and tailoring metalâ€“organic architectures with desirable properties.11

From the structural point of view, bi- or multidentate ligands containing a pillared linker (for example, 4,4â€²-bipyridine) as well as polycarboxylates are excellent bridging ligands, and a number of infinite frameworks have been generated.12 Herein, we want to report a novel 3D framework with an unprecedented (3,4,6)-connected net containing 4- and 6-connected Co(II) ions, 3-connected Hbidc (H3bidc = 1H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid) and bent bpt (bpt = 1H-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4- triazole) co-ligands. The mixed ligands were chosen based on the following considerations: on the other hand, the 1H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid (H3bidc) molecule possesses two pleasing characteristics: (1) as a multidentate and rigid ligand with multiproton acceptorâ€“donor sites, the bidc anion possesses two N atoms of an imidazole ring and four O atoms of carboxylate groups, and might be utilized as a versatile linker in constructing interesting porous coordination polymers; (2) the carboxylate group displays a variety of bonding geometries, such as monodentate, chelating, bidentate bridging, monodentate bridging and chelating bridging, and the collaboration of the two carboxylate groups in the bidc anion might connect metal magnetic centers serving as mediators of magnetic exchange.13 On the other hand, as we know, the rigid linear ligands, such as 4,4â€²-bipyridine, 1,4-bis(4-pyridyl) ethane etc., have been the main theme in the field of coordination polymers. However, up to now, very little attention has been paid to the porous coordination polymers generated from bent links.14 And modification by introducing spacers between the two 4-pyridyl groups results in distinct spatial effects to produce unexpected architectures upon metal complexation.15 A related triazole-containing compound 1H-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (bpt) has attracted our attentionâ€”it has a bent backbone: the angle subtended at the center of the five-membered heterocyclic spacer and two pyridyl N-donors is 143Â°. Furthermore, the N atoms of the triazole ring can provide potential coordination or hydrogen bonding sites that will influence the final coordination architectures.

In our recent research, using two rarely reported mixed ligands (H3bidc and bpt), we report herein the syntheses, crystal structures, and magnetic properties of novel 3D porous cobalt organic frameworks {[Co2(Hbidc)2(bpt)2]Â·7H2O}n (1), which possess an unprecedented (3,4,6)-connected geometric topology and show the coexistence of spin-canting and magnetic long-range ordering (LRO).

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

The polymeric structure of 1 was revealed by an X-ray single crystal diffraction investigation (Tables 1 and 2). As shown in Fig. 1, the asymmetric unit of 1 has two crystallographically independent Co centers, both displaying slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometries, and it should be noted that the two Co atoms lie on independent inversion centres and that water oxygen O6 lies on the twofold axis. The Co1 center is coordinated by two equatorial oxygen atoms and two nitrogen atoms from four Hbidc ligands, and two apical nitrogen atoms from two bpt ligands; Co2 is coordinated by four equatorial carboxylate oxygen atoms from two Hbidc ligands and two apical nitrogen atoms from two bpt ligands. The bond angles are very close to 90Â° (84.4â€“95.6Â°), resulting in a slightly distorted octahedron. This octahedral environment retains the approximate Oh point group, cannot quench efficiently the orbital moment of Co2+ and should lead to a big orbital residue, spinâ€“orbital coupling, and anisotropy, which will greatly influence its magnetic properties.19

The infinite Co-carboxylate chains are further linked by two carboxylate groups and one imidazole N donor in Hbidc2âˆ’, resulting in the generation of a 2D layer architecture and the interchain Coâ‹¯Co separation is 8.342 (2) Ã…. Better insight into this framework can be achieved by topology analysis. On the basis of the connectivity of Co1 and Co2 atoms, both of them are viewed to be 4-connected and 6-connected nodes, respectively. Interestingly, the connectivity of the two Co atoms is different: one displays the square geometry, whereas the other has octahedral geometry, and their ratio is 1:1. Moreover, according to Wells' topology definition, this 3D network can be further simplified by considering each Hbidc2âˆ’ ligand as a 3-connected node, and the bpt ligand as linker. Therefore, the overall structure of 1 which can not be simplified down to binodal or uninodal, is a trinodal (3,4,6)-connected net with a SchlÃ¤fli symbol of (63)(65.8)(612.8.102),21 as depicted in Fig. 2. The previous example of reported frameworks showing three different types of nodes is very rare. Up to date, only one trinodal (3, 5, 6)-connected net with SchlÃ¤fli symbol of (43)(34.74.82)3(33.43.76.83) had been reported.22 The trinodal (3,4,6)-connected 3D framework using covalent linkage has not been observed in MOFs.

It should be noted that there are three types of Coâ‹¯Co separation in the 3D framework, as shown above: those involving a bridging carboxylate group, those involving a bridging Hbidc2âˆ’ and those involving a bridging bpt. The long distance (the latter two Coâ‹¯Co separation types) suggests that the magnetic exchange between Co(II) atoms will be very weak, most likely through dipoleâ€“dipole interaction. Because of the lack of an inversion center between adjacent spins and the tilting of the long axes of adjacent octahedra (Fig. S3, the torsion angle of Nbptâ€“Coâ€“Coâ€“Nbpt is 26.6Â°), weak ferromagnetism due to spin-canting is allowed.23

Magnetic properties

Magnetic susceptibility measurements carried out on a polycrystalline sample of 1 with an applied field of 1000 Oe revealed a room temperature Ï‡MT value of 6.37 emu K molâˆ’1 per Co2 unit, which is much higher than the spin-only value (3.75 emu K molâˆ’1) due to the orbital contribution from the octahedral Co(II) ions. Upon cooling, it decreases to a minimum at ca. 18.0 K and then abruptly increases to reach a maximum at ca. 10.0 K, and then rapidly decreases to a minimum of 4.02 emu K molâˆ’1 at 2 K (Fig. 3). This behavior can be qualitatively explained if we bear in mind that high-spin octahedral Co(II) has an orbitally degenerate ground state 4T1. The decrease in Ï‡MT down to 18.0 K basically corresponds to a single-ion behavior, which accounts for the splitting of the 4T1 term into six Kramers doublets as a consequence of the combined effect of spinâ€“orbit coupling and distortion from ideal octahedral symmetry.24 However, the observed increase in Ï‡MT below this temperature is no longer coming from the single-ion behaviour but rather from a ferromagnetic or canted antiferromagnetic Co(II)â€“Co(II) exchange interaction, and the finally decreases in Ï‡MT below 10 K, indicating a magnetic phase transition. This is also consistent with the presence of the bifurcation point between the field-cooled (FC)/zero-field-cooled (ZFC) magnetization curves below 10 K (Fig. S4â€ ).25 At very low temperatures, the FC/ZFC curves undergo a slight increase which could be due to the presence of a paramagnetic contribution arising from defects in the crystal structure.26 Furthermore, the FC magnetizations of 1 are quite field-dependent, in which the rise of the Ï‡MT values at low temperature becomes less pronounced at higher fields (Fig. S4, inset). This is an important feature of spin canting behavior.27 Interestingly, the zero-field ac magnetic susceptibility measurement (Fig. 4) only exhibits the characteristics of an antiferromagnet at TN = 10.0 K, where the in-phase part Ï‡Mâ€² reaches a maximum, while no obvious out-of-phase Ï‡Mâ€³ reflection was observed at the same temperature (which clearly revealed the occurrence of a magnetic long-range ordering and agreed with the previous magnetic studies, though detailed information of the magnetic entropy could not be derived yet from the current specific heat data, with the lattice contribution unknown28). Thus, we think that complex 1 should be a hidden weak ferromagnet, due to spin canting. Furthermore, the absence of a frequency dependence of ac susceptibility precludes the possibility of the spin glass and the movement of domain-wall in 1.

The ferromagnetic correlations can be attributed to spin canting. i.e., perfect antiparallel alignment of the spins on the neighboring metal ions is not achieved so that residual spins are generated.5a Usually, the canting effect is caused by anti-symmetric interactions linked to the symmetry of the exchange pathways joining the magnetic centers, and also to single ion anisotropy. The canting in compound 1 may be caused by the acentrosymmetric synâ€“anti carboxylato bridge of the chains of [â€“Coâ€“Oâ€“Câ€“Oâ€“Coâ€“], thus satisfying the requirement for the Dzyaloshinskiâ€“Moriya interaction.29 In addition, magnetic anisotropy of the Co(II) ion could also give further contribution.

The magnetizations measured below 10 K show hysteresis loops with a small coercive field of 25 Oe and a remnant magnetization of 0.018 NÎ² indicating a soft-magnetic behavior of 1 (Fig. 5). The plots of M vs. H at 2 K are shown in the inset of Fig. 5. At low field, magnetization increases steeply with magnetic field; at higher field, magnetization increases at a slower rate and in an almost linear fashion. The magnetization value finally reaches ca. 3.66 NÎ² molâˆ’1 at 50 kOe, do not saturate. For octahedral Co(II), the effect of spinâ€“orbit coupling is usually to yield a Kramers S = 1/2 state at low temperature with an anisotropic g-tensor and a moment of 4.4 NÎ² molâˆ’1 per Co2 unit.24,30 The appearance of a maximum around 12.3 kOe in the dM/dH curve (Fig. 5, inset) measured at 2 K confirms that M vs. H curve increase rapidly at low fields corroborates that 1 is a weak ferromagnetic ordered solid.

Conclusions

In summary, we report here a new 3D porous cobaltâ€“organic framework formulated as {[Co2(Hbidc)2(bpt)2]Â·7H2O}n (1), synthesized by hydrothermal reactions using two rarely reported mixed ligands: 1H-benzimidazole-5,6-dicarboxylic acid (H3bidc) and 1H-3,5-bis(4-pyridyl)-1,2,4-triazole (bpt). Compound 1 has a 3D pillared-layer structure with an unprecedented (3,4,6)-connected topology. The structure induces spin canting and, thus, weak ferromagnetism and long-range magnetic ordering is observed below Tn = 10 K.
