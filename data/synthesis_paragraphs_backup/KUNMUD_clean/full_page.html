Fabrication of Cd(II)-MOF-based ternary photocatalytic composite materials for H2 production via a gel-to-crystal approach†

Chao-Wei Zhao , Yan-An Li , Xue-Ru Wang , Gong-Jun Chen , Qi-Kui Liu , Jian-Ping Ma and Yu-Bin Dong *
College of Chemistry, Chemical Engineering and Materials Science, Collaborative Innovation Center of Functionalized Probes for Chemical Imaging in Universities of Shandong, Key Laboratory of Molecular and Nano Probes, Ministry of Education, Shandong Normal University, Jinan 250014, P. R. China. E-mail: yubindong@sdnu.edu.cn

Received 27th July 2015 , Accepted 4th September 2015

First published on 4th September 2015

Cd(II)-MOF-based ternary composite materials of CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 were prepared by a unique TiO2 induced gel-to-crystal approach. CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 was demonstrated to be a highly active photocatalyst for hydrogen production under visible light based on water photolysis.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are highly ordered porous crystalline materials which are typically prepared by the self-assembly of metal ions with organic linkers.1 As a new class of porous species, they have been widely used in adsorption/separation, sensing and catalysis.2 On the other hand, MOFs can effectively combine with various other kinds of functional species to generate hybrid materials.3 Their incorporation between distinct individual doped species will endow such composites with great potential applications in optical, electrical and catalytic fields.

Among various MOF-based composite materials, MOF-based catalysts have received more and more attention during the last decade.4 As we know, MOFs themselves are inherent heterogeneous catalysts due to their inorganic–organic hybrid composition and polymeric nature. Furthermore, MOFs can also serve as a tunable platform to fabricate heterogeneous composite catalysts. For instance, porous MOFs can act as supports for the composite heterogeneous catalysts by incorporating the catalytically active species (including metal nanoparticles, metal oxide nanoparticles, quantum dots (QDs) or their precursors) into the MOF matrix by solid grinding, vapour deposition, solution infiltration and sometimes post-synthetic approaches.3,5 Although great efforts have been made in this field, general and facile methods that can easily introduce the active catalytic species into the MOF matrix are still imperative.

Recently, metal–organic gels (MOGs),6 mainly formed by means of metal–ligand interactions, have received considerable attention. Compared with crystalline MOFs, MOGs have the same components in nature, and they could be turned into their MOF counterparts by appropriate stimuli or inducing factors.7 This provides an alternative approach to access new types of MOF-based composite heterogeneous catalysts.

In this contribution, we report the synthesis of MOF-based ternary composite photocatalysts for H2 production based on water photolysis. Thereinto, the ternary composite catalyst CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2, which is a highly active photocatalyst for H2 production under visible light, is readily prepared by a unique TiO2 mediated gel-to-crystal method. In other words, the one-pot synthesized CdS quantum dots (QDs) loaded CdS@Cd(II)-MOG can be converted to a TiO2-coated MOF-based ternary composite material CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 under mild conditions (Scheme 1). The intention behind the incorporation of the photoactive CdS and TiO2 nanoparticles in the gels is to make an attempt to organize different types of active catalytic species in the MOF matrix in order to tune the catalytic properties of the composite catalysts consisting of different active nano-species.


Scheme 1 Synthesis of the Cd(II)-MOF (1), Cd(II)-MOGs (2–4), CdS QD-doped CdS@Cd(II)-MOGs (5 and 6) and ternary composites CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 (7 and 8). The single-crystal structure of 1 is inseted. CdS and TiO2 particles are shown as yellow and white balls, respectively.

As shown in Scheme 1, the porous Cd(II)-MOF (CdL2(BF4)2, 1) was synthesized as colourless crystals by the combination of L and Cd(BF4)2 (L/Cd(BF4)2 = 1:2.1) under solvothermal conditions (90 °C, MeOH–H2O, 72 h) (‡). Cd(II)-MOF (CdL2(BF4)2, 1) features a non-interpenetrating three-dimensional network which contains square-like channels with crystallographic dimensions of ca. 11 × 11 Å. The uncoordinated BF4− anions are not located in the cavities but are dispersed in the framework around the channels (Scheme 1).8 The simulated and measured XRPD patterns are well matched with each other, indicating that 1 was obtained in pure phase (ESI†).

On the other hand, we found that this combination of L and Cd(BF4)2 favours the formation of gel in a wide range of ligand/metal ratios (from 1:1.6 to 1:0.4) in the MeOH–H2O system except that a large excess amount of Cd(BF4)2 was introduced at the beginning of the reaction. For example, a MeOH solution of L was added dropwise to an aqueous solution of Cd(BF4)2 to generate colloidal suspension (L/Cd(BF4)2 = 1:0.4 (2), 1:0.8 (3), 1:1.6 (4)). The resulting white slurry was sonicated for 5 min or allowed to stand overnight to generate stable firm gels (‡). The gelation can be easily judged by a simple tube inversion test (Fig. 1), and the opaque gels Cd(II)-MOGs (2–4) were further investigated using rheology studies (Fig. 1). The dynamic frequency sweep of gels with different L to Cd(II) ratios displayed a typical gel-like rheological response.9 In each case, both the storage modulus (G′) and the loss modulus (G′′) are independent of frequency and at any given point, G′ is greater than G′′ (Fig. 1). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) were used to examine the morphology of gels 2–4. It is different from 1, a large amount of fibrillar structures with a micrometre scale in length is clearly observed under TEM (Fig. 1). SEM images (ESI†) indicate that the aggregation of the fibres exists in the xerogel (Cd(II)-xerogel can be easily obtained by dispersing the gel in MeOH and then centrifuged). Similar features, albeit with some minor differences, have been widely observed in the microscopic images of other MOGs.6 The amorphous nature of the xerogel is deduced from the silent signals on XRPD. Cd(II)-MOGs 2–4 were found to be stable for weeks at ambient temperature. In addition, there is no visible weakening even after heat treatment which was evidenced by the fact that heating of the gels up to 90 °C for 3 days neither changed its appearance nor leaked any solvent (ESI†).


Fig. 1 Top: Rheological response of 2–6 (photographs of 2–6 are inseted). Bottom: TEM images of 1–6.

With this in mind, we wonder if the photoactive CdS species could be simultaneously incorporated into Cd(II)-MOG, and furthermore, immobilized the CdS QDS in the MOG matrix by the heterocyclic moiety in the ligand.10 As expected, the CdS QD-doped CdS@Cd(II)-MOGs (5 and 6) can also be prepared in a similar way (‡). The mixture, which is obtained by the combination of a MeOH solution of Na2S/L and an aqueous solution of Cd(BF4)2, was sonicated for 5 min to result in the formation of MOGs 5 and 6 in light yellow colour (Fig. 1). Compared to Cd(II)-MOG (2–4), the strengths of 5 and 6 are greatly increased deduced from multiplying the storage modulus (G′) (Fig. 1). In addition, the morphology of xerogel is strongly dependent on the addition amount of Na2S. As shown in Fig. 1, CdS doping led the resulting fibrillar structures to be more branched. Again, 5 and 6 are very stable for weeks without changing its appearance and leaking any solvent at ambient temperature.

For fabrication of ternary composite photocatalysts, some metal oxides such as ZnO, CeO2, SiO2 and TiO2 were chosen to perform the gel-to-crystal transformation on the CdS@Cd(II)-MOGs (5 and 6). Among these metal oxides, only TiO2 was proven to be an effective species for the gel-to-crystal transformation (ESI†). As shown in Scheme 1, after addition of TiO2 fine powder to CdS QD loaded gels 5 and 6 with vigorous stirring, the TiO2 involved gels were obtained. The TiO2-containing gels were incubated at 90 °C for 20 h to generate crystalline ternary composites of CdS@Cd(II)-MOG@TiO2 (7 and 8) as yellow crystalline solids (‡).

As indicated in Fig. 2, the size of microcrystals of 7 and 8 (15–30 μm) is larger than that of 1, but the surfaces of CdS-doped crystals are more uneven. The energy-dispersive X-ray spectra (EDS) indicated that each element distributes rather averagely. The amount of coated TiO2 (43.12% in 7, 36.90% in 8) is much higher than that of embedded CdS (6.99% in 7, 12.01% in 8), which might be the reason to explain why only the peaks related to TiO2 can be found in the corresponding XRPD patterns (Fig. 2).


Fig. 2 Top left: SEM images and the corresponding element mapping of 7 and 8. Top right: XRPD patterns of 1, 7 and 8, and anatase TiO2. The photographs of 1, 7 and 8 are inseted. Bottom: HRTEM images of 7 and 8.

Besides SEM-EDS and XRPD spectra, the coexistence of CdS and TiO2 with Cd(II)-MOF in 7 and 8 is further supported by the HRTEM images. As shown in Fig. 2, TiO2 and CdS coexist in the Cd(II)-MOF (1) matrix as anatase TiO2 (d spacing of 0.357 nm, corresponding to the (101) plane of anatase TiO2 crystal)11 and cubic CdS (d spacing of 0.208 nm, assigned to the (220) lattice plane of the cubic CdS)12 forms, respectively. It is different from 1, the CdS QD-loaded crystals are yellow. The crushed crystals of 7 and 8 show that the yellow colour is dispersed throughout the crystals, indicating that the CdS QDs are homogeneously distributed in the MOF matrix. The HRTEM image shows that the size of the CdS cluster is ca. 7–10 nm, and thus these CdS QDs are not located inside the pores of the Cd(II)-MOF (pore diameter ca. 1.1 nm based on single-crystal analysis, ESI†), as they are much larger than the pore size. So these CdS QDs are presumably sandwiched between the crystallite surfaces of Cd(II)-MOF (1). Diffuse reflectance spectra (ESI†) revealed that the nanoparticle-doped catalysts 7 and 8 exhibit broader absorption bands than that of 1. In addition, the significant absorptions of the CdS-loaded materials were found within the visible region (400–500 nm), and the corresponding band edges showed clear red-shifts towards the cubic CdS adsorption edge (∼515 nm).13

To our knowledge, this is the most convenient immobilization approach of multi-type catalytic species in the MOF matrix to date. In addition to that, multicomponent doping in the MOF matrix via gel media would ensure the distribution homogeneity of the involved species in the composite materials and consequently improve the synergistic effect of different catalytic species in the matrix. So far, although a few CdS-doped MOF-based composite materials for H2 production have been reported, 7 and 8 herein are the first examples of ternary composite photocatalytic materials generated by the gel-to-crystal approach.14

Ternary composite materials of CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 (7 and 8) were used for water photolysis under visible light irradiation (λ > 400 nm). SO32−/S2− was chosen as the sacrificial reagent (ESI†).15 No significant H2 production occurred without irradiation or the catalysts, indicating that H2-production is a catalyst triggered photoinduced process. Notably, the catalysts of 7 and 8 present high photocatalytic activity in the absence of noble metals of Pt as co-catalysts. As indicated in Fig. 3, ternary composites 7 and 8 showed that the rate of H2 evolution upon illumination of visible light was found to be 1416 μmol g−1 h−1 (7) and 1604 μmol g−1 h−1 (8), respectively. The observed results were well evidenced by their corresponding quantum efficiencies (QE = 2.05% for 7, 2.31% for 8). The H2 production efficiency of 7 and 8 herein is much larger than those of reported CdS doped MOFs,14 which is believed to benefit from the CdS/Cd(II)-MOF/TiO2 tri-component synergy effect. To determine the influence of the tri-component function, Cd(II)-MOF-based binary composites CdS@Cd(II)-MOF (9) and Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 (10) were separately synthesized (ESI†). It is noteworthy that both the binary composites (729 μmol g−1 h−1, QE = 1.05% for CdS@Cd(II)-MOF (9); 217 μmol g−1 h−1, QE = 0.31% for Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2 (10)) showed much lower H2 evolution efficiency than those of 7 and 8, although the their surface areas are larger than those of ternary materials (ESI†). Therefore, the composition of hybrid materials instead of surface areas is the dominating factor for their catalytic activity.

(b) Mott–Schottky of the Cd(II)-MOF (1). (c) Energy band position diagram and the mechanism of H2 production by 7 and 8.

To determine the possible pathways for the transfer of charge carriers in these MOF-based hybrids, Mott–Schottky (MS) measurements (Fig. 3) were performed on the Cd(II)-MOF (1) using electrochemical impedance techniques. A band gap of 3.0 eV and a positive slope of the MS plot confirm Cd(II)-MOF (1) to be an n-type semiconductor.16 Mott–Schottky analysis gives a flat band potential of the MOF (found to be 0.64 V vs. NHE, Fig. 3). It is well known that the flat band potential of an n-type semiconductor equals its Fermi level.17 The conduction band edge of such n-type semiconductors is more negative by about 0.10 V than the flat band potential. Thus, the conduction band of Cd(II)-MOF (1) is estimated to be −0.54 V vs. NHE. Therefore, the low-lying conduction band edge of the MOF (−0.54 V vs. NHE) in comparison to CdS (−0.65 V vs. NHE) favours the stabilization of CdS photoelectrons on the MOF, which thereby results in efficient charge separation. As the conduction band of TiO2 (−0.29 V vs. NHE) is lower than that of Cd(II)-MOF, the excitons are believed to deliver to the TiO2 located at the outer surface and reduce protons to H2 (Fig. 3). Electrochemical impedance spectral (EIS) analysis was now used as a powerful tool in studying the charge transfer process. The ternary hybrid systems show a smaller radius of the EIS curvature than the binary hybrid system indicating higher charge transfer efficiency of the ternary hybrid system (ESI†). To test the stability of the MOF-based hybrid material, the XRPD patterns performed on the recovered catalysts from the photocatalytic reactions indicate that the structures of these MOF-based materials were maintained (ESI†). In addition, 1H NMR (DMSO-d6) (ESI†) spectra show that the ligand in the MOF matrix did not suffer any photodegradation during the catalytic process.

In conclusion, we developed a new fabrication approach to access MOF-based multiplex composite photocatalysts via TiO2-mediated gel transformation. Based on this, the one-pot synthesized CdS@Cd(II)-MOF gel can be turned into its Cd(II)-MOF counterpart (CdS@Cd(II)-MOF@TiO2) by simple addition of TiO2 under mild conditions. These CdS and TiO2 loaded ternary MOF-based materials are highly active catalysts for photoinduced H2 production. This gel-to-crystal transformation provided a practical way for the construction of more multicomponent MOF-based functional materials.
