Fluorescent heterometallic MOFs: tunable framework charges and application for explosives detection†

Kangcai Wang , Tianlin Liu , Yuji Liu , Xin Tian , Jie Sun * and Qinghua Zhang *
Research Center of Energetic Material Genome Science, Institute of Chemical Materials, China Academy of Engineering Physics (CAEP), Mianyang, 621900, PR China. E-mail: qinghuazhang@caep.cn

Received 20th August 2016 , Accepted 23rd September 2016

First published on 23rd September 2016

A series of three-dimensional fluorescent heterometallic MOFs based on rod- and cluster-like building blocks were readily prepared under solvothermal conditions. Among them, compounds 1–4 were constructed from rod-like building blocks {Cd–O–M}n (M = Na, K, Na–K, and Mg) and BDC2− ligands, featuring right-hand and left-hand channels with a cap topology. The framework charges of 1–4 can be tuned from negative to neutral via a strategy of changing the secondary metal centre (Na+, Na+–K+, K+ and Mg2+). It is worth noting that the rod-like building block of compound 2 contained three types of metal ion centres (Cd2+, Na+ and K+). Different from compounds 1–4, the topology of 5 changed from a cap net to a 610 net in the presence of Ca2+ as a secondary metal centre. Interestingly, the anionic framework of 5 features two types of linear trinuclear clusters as the building blocks, i.e. {Cd2CaO17} and {Cd2CaO16}, which are relatively rare in known heterometallic open frameworks. Moreover, fluorescent compound 5 with a microporous structure displayed sensing properties towards some nitro-containing compounds, suggesting its potential application as a fluorescent sensor for the sensitive detection of explosives.

Introduction

In the past decade, the synthesis of new crystalline open-framework materials has progressed considerably due to their intriguing topological structures and potential applications in a variety of fields including gas adsorption, catalysis, proton conductivity, photoluminescence, nonlinear optics, magnetism and so on.1 In this frontier field, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are the most well-known inorganic–organic hybrid materials, which can be readily constructed from the self-assembly of metal centres with multifunctional organic ligands.2 However, the controllable synthesis of desired MOFs with targeted structures still remains a great challenge for synthetic chemists, since there are many factors (e.g. temperature, solvents, counter-ions, pH values, templates, etc.) which may influence the micro-structures of target MOFs.3 In recent years, it has been demonstrated that the introduction of secondary metal ions into the reaction systems is an efficient strategy to tune the structural variations and properties of the desired MOFs.4,5 A typical example is that of Yao and co-workers who have successfully tuned the topological structures of heterometallic MOFs via changing the secondary metal centre among alkaline earth metal ions.6 Moreover, Du and co-workers used Li+, Na+, K+, and Rb+ ions as secondary metal centres to achieve the construction of some heterometallic MOFs with fluorescence, nonlinear optical, and ferroelectric properties.5,7 Clearly, this smart strategy of employing secondary building units (SBUs) is very useful to achieve the fine-control of the self-assembly process of MOFs, which thereby opens up new opportunities for rational design and controllable synthesis of desired MOFs with unusual structures and interesting properties.

Nowadays, the study of MOFs with different framework charges has also aroused great interest of chemists and materials scientists.8 As an important structural parameter, the framework charges of MOFs are closely related to the physical properties of the materials.9 In fact, it is a difficult endeavor to fine-tune the framework charge of a specific structure because sometimes a tiny change under synthetic conditions may influence the final structure of MOF materials.10 Against this background, despite successful fine-tuning of MOFs' topological structures, only a few studies about their charge controlling have been reported. By combining the positive charge difference of alkali or alkaline earth metal ions with the strategy of using secondary metal centres as the building blocks, the reasonable construction of MOFs with tunable framework charges becomes very meaningful via changing the secondary metal ions among alkali and alkaline earth metal ions.

Rapid and selective detection of explosives is a very important aspect concerning homeland security and environmental safety.11 It has been demonstrated that fluorescent MOFs are capable of fast, reversible and sensitive detection of high explosives, and some fluorescent MOFs have been synthesized and applied to detect 2,4-dinitrotoluene (DNT), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP) and other explosives.12 Recently, heterometallic MOFs have aroused intensive interest due to their tunable structures and properties. For example, a luminescent heterometallic MOF, [(CH3)2NH2]3 [Zn4Na(BPTC)3]·4CH3OH·2DMF (BPTC = biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic acid), with an unprecedented topological structure was designed and synthesized. Furthermore, this compound displayed extreme selectivity towards 2,4,6-trinitrophenol.13

Following on from these ideas, in this work, we tried to synthesize new heterometallic MOFs by virtue of the strategy of introducing secondary metal ions into the reaction system. In our design strategy, the attempts to tune the framework charges of MOFs will be addressed via changing the secondary metal centre amongst alkali and alkaline earth metal ions. As expected, five three-dimensional heterometallic MOFs with interesting fluorescence properties have been readily synthesized under solvothermal conditions, and the framework charges of compounds 1–4 with the same topological net (cap) varied from negative to neutral by employing various alkali and alkaline earth metal ions. Moreover, the fluorescence chromaticity of the whole family shifted from blue to green with increasing atomic number of the secondary metal ions. It is noteworthy that compound 5 with a microporous structure showed an interesting sensing ability towards some nitro-containing compounds, indicating its potential application as a chemosensor for explosives detection.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used as received without further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction data were collected using a Rigaku D/MAX-rA diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). IR spectra (KBr pellets) were acquired using a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer in the range of 400–4000 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses with heating from room temperature to 800 °C were performed using a Netzsch STA 449c analyzer in a flow of N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. In order to confirm the number of counter cations (i.e. NH2(CH3)2+) in the sample channels, all the samples after removing the trapped solvents (degassed at 140 °C for 12 h) were tested by elemental analyses using a Euro EA3000 analyzer. The gas adsorption measurements were performed using ASAP 2020 apparatuses. Prior to measurements, the sample was degassed for 12 h at 140 °C. Fluorescence spectra were obtained using a PTI QM-40 luminescence spectrometer equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp.

X-ray crystallographic structure

A suitable single crystal of each compound was carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to a glass fiber with epoxy resin. The single-crystal data were collected using an Oxford Xcalibur diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using an ω scan for 1, 2, 3, and 5 and using a New Gemini, Dual, Cu at zero, EosS2 diffractometer for 4 at room temperature. The crystal structures of 1–5 were resolved by direct methods and refined using full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 using the SHELXTL program package.14 Anisotropic refinement was applied to non-hydrogen atoms, except disordered guest molecules. The PLATON/SQUEEZE program was used to obtain non-solvent diffraction of as-synthesized compounds due to highly disordered guest molecules, which could not be modelled properly.15 The crystallographic data for 1–5 are summarized in Table S1.† The CCDC reference numbers for 1–5 are 1476775, 1476776, 1476777, 1476778, and 1476779, respectively.

Caution! All of the nitro explosives used in this work are highly explosive and should be handled carefully and in small amounts.

Sensing of explosives

3 mg of 5 (activated at 140 °C under high vacuum for 3 h) is weighed and dispersed in 20 mL of acetonitrile, treated by ultrasonication for 2 h and aged for 3 days. The fluorescence of the dispersions upon excitation at 361 nm was measured in situ after incremental addition of freshly prepared analyte solutions (1 mM) to 3.5 mL of 5 based on dispersion.

Results and discussion

Structure description

Colourless crystals of 1 were readily obtained by the solvothermal reaction of Cd(OAc)2·2H2O, NaOH, H2BDC (1,4-dicarboxybenzene), and 9-AC (9-anthracenecarboxylic acid) in a mixed solvent of methanol and DMA (N,N-dimethylacetamide) at 150 °C for 4 d. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c (no. 15) space group and is an extended 3D hybrid anionic open framework. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half independent Cd(II) ions, one and a half Na(I) ions, and three BDC2− ligands (Fig. S10, ESI†); in the structure of 1, Cd1 is eight-coordinated by carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different BDC2− ligands in an approximately monocapped pentagonal bipyramidal geometry (Fig. 1a), while Cd2 is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms in a distorted octahedral environment (Fig. 1b); Na1 is five-coordinated by carboxylate oxygen atoms from five different BDC2− ligands in a distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry, while Na2 is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms in a distorted octahedral environment (Fig. 1c). The bond lengths of Cd–O [2.228(3)–2.551(4) Å] and Na–O [2.264(4)–2.521(4) Å] are all within the normal ranges.5c,16 It is worth noting that each polyhedron shares two edges with two neighbouring polyhedra and extends into a 1D inorganic {Cd–O–Na}n chain along the [100] direction (Fig. 1d). Therefore, the 3D hybrid framework of 1 can be classified as I1O2 type. Up to now, some known hybrid open frameworks based on {Cd–O–M} inorganic chains such as {Cd3K2O16}n and {Na2Cd2O18}n have been reported.5b,17 However, rarely similar heterometallic chains with {Cd–O–Na}n have been found in the reported heterometallic MOFs.7,18
Fig. 1 (a) A structural view of compound 1: (a and b) view of the coordination conditions of Cd1 and Cd2, respectively. (c) View of the rod-like building block {Cd–O–Na} along the [100] direction contained in compound 1. (d) Polyhedral view of the heterometallic inorganic chain. (e) Polyhedral view of the 3D inorganic–organic hybrid framework along the [100] direction of 1. (f) View of helices with opposite handedness contained in 1. Polyhedral: red: NaO6, pink: NaO5, blue: CdO6, green: CdO8.

The 3D hybrid anionic framework of 1 is constructed from an infinite 1D inorganic {Cd–O–Na}n chain and 2-connected BDC2− ligands. Viewed along the [100] direction, there is an alternative arrangement of right-hand and left-hand helical chains contained in 1 (Fig. 1e and f). The pitch of each helical chain is about 18 Å. The pitches are large enough to be filled via mutual interpenetration of two equivalent frameworks, and the neighboring diamondoid nets are connected with each other via sodium ions (Fig. 1e and f). As a whole, compound 1 has no chirality as the left and right helical chains are averaged, as shown in Fig. 1e. The 1D channels contained in 1 possess a nano-pore with the size of 10 × 10 Å2. The total accessible volume of 1 after removing the counter cations and guest solvent molecules is ca. 46.2% using the PLATON program. When Na atoms are omitted, the 3D anionic framework becomes a three-fold self-interpenetrated diamondoid topological network (Fig. 4a). If the Cd atoms as 6-connected nodes and Na atoms and BDC2− ligands as 2-connected linkers are considered, the framework of compound 1 can be described as a cap network with the point (Schläfli) symbol of 48·67 (Fig. 2).


Fig. 2 View of the cap topological structure of 1, all of the balls represent 6-connected Cd atoms, the black lines represent 2-connected Na atoms and the other lines represent BDC2− ligands.

Compounds 2–5 were synthesized under conditions similar to that of compound 1 by using NaOH–KOH, KOH, Mg(OH)2 and Ca(OH)2, respectively, instead of NaOH. It is worth noting that the {Cd–O–M}n chain in the structure of 2 contains three types of metal ions (Cd2+, Na+, and K+), which is rarely reported in the known heterometallic frameworks.19 In the structures of compounds 1, 3, and 4, some of the secondary metal ions are five-coordinated by carboxylate oxygen atoms and the others are six-coordinated. However, in the structural construction of compound 2, each sodium atom is five-coordinated by carboxylate oxygen atoms and each potassium atom is coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms, as shown in Fig. S14.† Compounds 1–4 possess the same cap topological structure. Interestingly, when Ca2+ was used to replace Mg2+, the resulting compound 5 exhibited a new 610 topological structure.

Different from 1–4, compound 5 crystallizes in the orthorhombic Pna21 (no. 33) space group. In this structure, there are four Cd atoms, two Ca atoms and eight BDC2− ligands in the asymmetric unit (Fig. S15†); Cd1 is coordinated by seven oxygen atoms in a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometry, and six oxygen atoms belong to four different BDC2− ligands and one belongs to the DMA molecule. Other metal ions are all coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms in a distorted octahedral environment (Fig. 3a). The Cd–O distances range from 2.175(8) Å to 2.775(13) Å, and the Ca–O distances range from 2.194(7) Å to 2.375(8) Å. The 3D heterometallic hybrid open framework of 5 was constructed with two types of linear trinuclear building units, {Cd2CaO16} and {Cd2CaO17}, and BDC2− ligands, as shown in Fig. 3a, although a number of heterometallic hybrid open frameworks based on heteronuclear metal clusters, e.g. [Cd2NaO6(H2O)6],20 [NaCd3(μ3-OH)(COO)5],21 [Zn2Na2(COO)6(μ2-MeOH)(DMF)4],22 [Zn2K2(COO)6(DMF)2(Me2NH)2],23 and [Zn2Mg(COO)6],23 have been reported. However, no examples of open frameworks based on {Cd2CaO16} and {Cd2CaO17} clusters have been reported in the literature yet. This means that compound 5 is the first reported example of a hybrid open framework based on the linear trinuclear {Cd2CaO16} and {Cd2CaO17} clusters. Furthermore, the two types of clusters were alternately present in the framework strictly. Viewed along the [010] and [001] directions, compound 5 exhibits two kinds of rectangular channels with window sizes of 9.7 × 11.2 Å2 and 10.0 × 11.6 Å2 (Fig. 3b and S17†). The total accessible volume of 5 after removal of the guest solvent molecules was ca. 58.7% using the PLATON program. When the template ions (Ca2+) are omitted, the 3D anionic framework can be simplified as a four-connected topological net with the point (Schläfli) symbol of 65·8. If the Cd atoms as 5-connected nodes and Ca atoms and BDC2− ligands as 2-connected linkers are considered, the framework of 5 can be [6(3)·6(3)·6(3)·6(3)·6(3)·6(3)·6(3)·6(5)·6(5)·6(5)] (Fig. 3c).


Fig. 3 (a) View of the linear trinuclear building units contained in compound 5: (left) {Cd2CaO16} and (right) {Cd2CaO17}. (b) Polyhedral view of the 3D inorganic–organic hybrid framework along the [100] direction of 5. Pink: CaO6, blue: CdO7, green: CdO6. (c) View of the 610 topological net of 5.

From the viewpoint of structural analysis, the cap topological net can be regarded as constructed by the interpenetrated dia nets and 2-connected linkers, as shown in Scheme 1. If the dia nets were constructed by M(II) nodes and an L2− linker, the chemical formula of the dia net would be {M(L)22−}n. Therefore, the charge of the cap net can be controlled via the fine-tuning of the charge number of the 2-connected linkers. Theoretically, anionic, neutral, and cation cap nets can be obtained by using M(I), M(II), and M(III) ions as linkers, respectively. In this work, the dia net was constructed by Cd2+ nodes and BDC2− linkers with a chemical formula of {Cd(BDC)22−}n. Interestingly, a series of anionic heterometallic hybrid open framework with a cap net have been prepared by using Na+, Na+–K+, and K+ as linkers, respectively, while a neutral cap net has also been constructed by using Mg2+ as a linker. Unfortunately, the cationic cap net was unattained when boron group metal ions were used to act as linkers. The failure may be caused by the propensity of forming {MIIIO} clusters (B and Al),24 high coordination numbers or large atom radius (Ga and In).25 Moreover, when Ca2+ was used as a linker, a new hybrid open framework with a 610 topological net was obtained. To construct the rod-like building block {Cd–O–M} contained in 1–4 some of the linker ions need to coordinate with five oxygen atoms and others to coordinate with six oxygen atoms. However, the calcium atom preferentially coordinated with at least six oxygen atoms which promoted the assembly of the 610 topological net of compound 5.

Thermal stability

In order to confirm the phase purities of the as-synthesized compounds, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) analyses of compounds 1–5 were carried out at room temperature. As shown in Fig. S1, S3, S5, and S7,† the main peak positions of 1, 2, 3, and 5 are almost consistent with their simulated ones, demonstrating the phase purities of the products. Unfortunately, compound 4 was mixed with unknown powders and its purified phase failed to be obtained. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was also carried out in a flow of nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The results showed that compounds 1–3 possess similar thermal stabilities which were very different to that of compound 5 (Fig. S9†). The TGA curve of compound 1 has demonstrated that the weight loss of 4.15 wt% in the region of 30–110 °C may correspond to the release of guest water molecules. The weight loss of 10.09 wt% from 110 to 320 °C may be attributed to the loss of DMA molecules. Upon further heating, the counterions and frameworks started to decompose. The TGA diagram of 5 revealed that the first weight loss of 19.50 wt% from 30 to 130 °C may be due to the release of guest molecules and free guest molecules contained in the channels of 5. The weight loss of 5 from 210 to 320 °C may be attributed to the loss of the DMA molecules which coordinated with calcium atoms and the decomposition of counterions (observed: 9.00 wt%, calculated: 9.08 wt%). Upon further heating, the framework of 5 started to decompose and the observed total weight loss is 79.30 wt% at 800 °C. The TGA results of the degassed 5 revealed that no weight loss was observed before 350 °C, and upon further heating, the framework of degassed 5 started to decompose (Fig. S9†).

The robustness and the permanent microporous feature of the activated 5 (activated at 140 °C under high vacuum for 12 h) was further established by the N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K, which displayed typical type-I sorption properties (Fig. S18†). It takes up 116 cm3 g−1 of N2 at 77 K and 1 atm. The calculated Langmuir and BET surface areas are 315 and 235 m2 g−1, respectively. An average pore size of 2.45 nm was calculated. The CO2 sorption isotherms at 273 K are shown in Fig. 4. The CO2 uptake at 273 K reached 26 cm3 g−1 at 1 atm. The stability of compound 5 after degassing at 140 °C under high vacuum for 12 h was confirmed by PXRD measurements, as shown in Fig. S32.† The activated compounds 1–3 take up negligible amounts of nitrogen and CO2, indicating that channels in the frameworks may be filled by counterions and therefore will not allow the incorporation of N2 or CO2.

Fluorescence properties

The fluorescence properties of metal–organic frameworks, especially with a d10 metal center, have attracted considerable attention due to their interesting fluorescence properties and potential applications as new luminescent materials. Therefore, the solid-state fluorescence properties of 1, 2, 3, and 5 have been investigated in the solid state at room temperature. In order to understand the photoluminescence nature of these compounds, the fluorescence properties of their free ligand (i.e. H2BDC) was firstly analyzed, as shown in Fig. S19 and S20.† Interestingly, the excitation spectra of the H2BDC ligand and the as-synthesized compounds are quite different. From the excitation spectra of the H2BDC ligand, only one main peak has been observed at 365 nm. However, from the excitation spectra of 1, 2, 3, and 5, three different excitation peaks have been observed. Moreover, the H2BDC ligand emits fluorescence centered at 431 nm (λex = 365 nm) which should correspond to π* → n transitions. For compounds 1, 2, 3 and 5, two main emission bands at about 425 and 447 nm upon excitation at λex = 394 nm are observed, as shown in Fig. S20.† The emission bands are only slightly shifted as compared to that of the H2BDC ligand. This indicates that the emission bands of 1, 2, 3 and 5 may mainly be attributed to the intra-ligand fluorescence emission. In addition, the emission intensity at about 447 nm gradually increased as the secondary metal centre changed from Na+ to Ca2+ with increasing atomic numbers. A gradual chromaticity shift from blue to green was observed with increasing atomic numbers of the secondary metal ions, and the regularity is more obvious among compounds 1–3, as shown in Fig. 5. This chromaticity shift phenomena have also been observed in a series of alkaline earth metal-based fluorescent MOFs.26

Sensing of explosives

In order to explore the feasibility of these compounds as fluorescence sensors for the detection of nitro-containing explosives, the fluorescent compound 5 with a microporous structure was chosen as a chemosensor for the sensing of some nitro-containing analytes. The detection method involves measurement of the fluorescence changes of the emulsion of 5 by the low-concentration analytes. Fluorescence changes of the emulsion were obtained by successive addition of small aliquots of nitro-containing explosives including cyclotrimethylenetrinitramine (RDX), 2,4,6,8,10,12-hexanitro-2,4,6,8,10,12-hexaazatetracyclo-[5.5.0.0.0]-dodecane (CL-20), cyclotetramethylenetetranitramine (HMX), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2,4-dinitroanisole (DNAN), 1,3-dinitrobenzene (DNB) and 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP). The emission intensity changes of compound 5 upon addition of RDX are shown in Fig. 6a. As shown in Fig. 6a, along with successive addition of a solution of RDX, an obvious fluorescence enhancement of the emulsion at 465 nm was observed, which was reflected by the dramatic increase in emission intensity. When 400 μL of an acetonitrile solution of RDX (1 mM) was added to 3.5 mL of the emulsion of 5, the initial emission intensity was increased by ca. 180%. From the Stern–Volmer (S–V) plots (Fig. S21†), the enhancement constants and the enhancement efficiency of the analytes can be calculated using the SV equation (I/I0) = Ksv [A] + 1, where I0 is the initial emission intensity before the addition of analytes, I is the emission intensity in the presence of analytes, [A] is the molar concentration of analytes, and Ksv is the enhancement constant (M−1). The S–V plot for RDX was nearly linear at low concentrations, and upon further increase in concentration, the plot diverged from linearity and began to bend upwards. This non-linear nature of the S–V plot of RDX suggests self-absorption, a combination of static and dynamic enhancements or an energy transfer process between RDX and compound 5.12 The enhancement constant for RDX was found to be 2.3 × 104 M−1 (Fig. S22†). The detection limit (DL) was calculated to be 1.9 × 10−7 M via the equation of 3σ/k (where σ denotes the standard deviation for the intensity of 5 in the absence of RDX and k represents the slope of the equation), suggesting the high sensitivity of 5 towards RDX.27 Moreover, we also investigated the emission intensity changes of the emulsion in the presence of other nitro-compounds including CL-20, HMX, DNB, TNT, DNAN, and TNP. As shown in Fig. 6b, under the same conditions, six nitro-compounds with different structures also showed different degrees of fluorescence changes of the emulsion of 5, in which non-aromatic explosive like HMX and CL-20 exhibit relatively strong enhancement effects on the emission intensity compared with nitro aromatic compounds like DNB, TNT, DNAN, and TNP. Clearly, the emulsion of 5 exhibited relatively higher emission enhancement towards RDX than other nitro-containing explosive molecules, demonstrating its selectivity towards RDX. It is noteworthy that compound 5 is the first fluorescent MOF which has high fluorescence enhancement effects towards RDX. Moreover, the recyclability of compound 5 towards RDX was also investigated. As shown in Fig. S31,† the changes in the percentage of emission intensities were almost the same during the recycle sensing experiments, demonstrating the good recyclability of 5. The powder XRD results confirmed the good stability of compound 5 during the sensing experiments, as shown in Fig. S32.†
Fig. 6 (a) The emission spectra of 5 dispersed in acetonitrile upon incremental addition of an acetonitrile solution of RDX (1 mM). (b) The fluorescence quenching efficiency for different analytes.

To gain more insight into the fluorescence enhancement mechanism of compound 5, theoretical studies were also performed using the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof set in the DMol3 package of the Material Studio 8 program. The MOFs, especially with d10 metal ions, can be regarded as giant “molecules” and their valence and conduction bands can be treated in a fashion similar to molecular orbitals (MOs).28 In general, when the minimum of the conduction band (CB) of a MOF lies at lower energies than the lowest unoccupied molecular orbitals (LUMOs) of analytes, upon excitation, the analytes act as strong electron donors and therefore enhance the fluorescence emission of the MOF.29 The experimental observation of the interaction between 5 and the analytes (i.e. RDX, CL-20, and HMX) supports this prediction. As shown in Fig. S24,† the LUMO energies of RDX, CL-20 and HMX are high-lying and are far above the CBs of compound 5. Upon excitation, the explosive molecules can act as strong electron donors and therefore enhance the fluorescence emission of 5. In contrast, for the other four nitro-containing molecules (i.e. DNB, TNT, DNAN and TNP), their LUMO energies are lower than that of compound 5, and accordingly, no obvious fluorescence enhancements of compound 5 have been observed. Among the seven explosive molecules used in this work, RDX exhibited the strongest fluorescence enhancement on the emission intensity of compound 5.

Conclusions

In summary, a series of 3D heterometallic MOFs have been synthesized by using Na+, K+, Na+–K+, Mg2+, and Ca2+ ions as secondary metal ion centres. Compounds 1–4 were constructed from rod-like building blocks {Cd–O–M}n (M = Na, K, Na–K, Mg) and BDC2− ligands, featuring right-hand and left-hand helical channels with a cap topology. It is interesting that, by virtue of this strategy, the framework charges can be tuned from negative to neutral via changing the secondary metal ion center (e.g. Na+, Na+–K+, K+ and Mg2+). Compound 5 with a 610 topological net was built by two types of linear trinuclear building units, {Cd2CaO16} and {Cd2CaO17}, and BDC2− ligands. The N2 adsorption revealed that the BET surface area and the average pore size of 5 was 235 m2 g−1 and 2.45 nm, respectively. The fluorescence properties of five compounds are adjustable by changing the secondary metal ion centre. It is worth noting that compound 5 is the first fluorescent MOF which displayed fluorescence enhancement towards RDX and exhibited selective sensing ability to the RDX molecule.
