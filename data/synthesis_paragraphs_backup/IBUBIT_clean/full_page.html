Microporous rod metal–organic frameworks with diverse Zn/Cd–triazolate ribbons as secondary building units for CO2 uptake and selective adsorption of hydrocarbons†

Jian-Wei Zhang , Man-Cheng Hu *, Shu-Ni Li , Yu-Cheng Jiang and Quan-Guo Zhai *
Key Laboratory of Macromolecular Science of Shaanxi Province, School of Chemistry & Chemical Engineering, Shaanxi Normal University, Xi'an, Shaanxi 710062, P. R. China. E-mail: hmch@snnu.edu.cn; zhaiqg@snnu.edu.cn

Received 23rd November 2016 , Accepted 10th December 2016

First published on 12th December 2016

The synthetic design of new porous open-framework materials with pre-designed pore properties for desired applications such as gas adsorption and separation remains challenging. We proposed one such class of materials, rod metal–organic frameworks (rod MOFs), which can be tuned by using rod secondary building units (rod SBUs) with different geometrical and chemical features. Our approach takes advantage of the readily accessible metal–triazolate 1-D motifs as rod SBUs to combine with dicarboxylate ligands to prepare target rod MOFs. Herein we report three such metal–triazolate–dicarboxylate frameworks (SNNU-21, -22 and -23). During the formation of these three MOFs, Cd or Zn ions are firstly connected by 1,2,4-triazole through the N1,N2,N4-mode to form 1-D metal–organic ribbon-like rod SBUs, which further joint four adjacent rod SBUs via eight BDC linkers to give 3-D microporous frameworks. However, tuned by the different NH2 groups from metal–triazolate rod SBUs, different space groups, pore sizes and shapes are observed for SNNU-21–23. All of these rod MOFs show not only remarkable CO2 uptake capacity, but also high CO2 over CH4 and C2-hydrocarbons over CH4 selectivity under ambient conditions. Specially, SNNU-23 exhibits a very high isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) for C2H2 (62.2 kJ mol−1), which outperforms the values of all MOF materials reported to date including the famous MOF-74-Co.

Introduction

Methane, with the highest heat of combustion per mass unit (55.7 kJ g−1), is poised to be one of the most important energy source candidates in the future.1 In general, natural gas often contains impurities such as CO2 and C2-hydrocarbons (C2H2, C2H4 and C2H6).2 The traditional cryogenic distillation separation technology is very energy-consuming, whereas the alternative oil-absorption method is not efficient.3,4 Taking solid adsorptive materials into account, porous coordination polymers (PCPs) or metal organic frameworks (MOFs)5–9 have demonstrated significant promise for gas adsorption and separation because of their chemical tailorability, which results in specific recognition ability for components in the gas mixture.10,11 However, only a few of them have shown outstanding separation of methane from CO2 and C2-hydrocarbons up to now.12–23

The synthetic design of MOF materials with pre-designed pore properties for gas adsorption and separation remains challenging. To access this promising target, the connector (metal ions/clusters) and linker (organic spacer) approaches have been testified to be effective. Among the most popular organic linkers are polycarboxylate ligands, as their carboxyl groups can adopt various bonding modes and their phenyl ring appears rigid. On the other hand, five-membered azolate compounds such as pyrazole, imidazole, triazole, and tetrazole have also been extensively exploited in the construction of MOFs,24–43 ZIFs44,45 or MAFs.46–49

To achieve high CO2 adsorption capacity and separation of CO2 or C2-hydrocarbons from CH4, our group has focused on the combination of azolate and dicarboxylate linkers to design metal–azolate–dicarboxylate ternary frameworks.39–41 Usually, azolate derivatives tend to form diverse metal–azolate motifs (MAMs) through three or four aromatic nitrogen electron-donating atoms, which can be extended by a dicarboxylate ligand to 3-D MOFs. Moreover, the pore surface of metal–azolate–dicarboxylate frameworks can be decorated with the bare nitrogen atoms, which provide potential adsorption sites to improve gas adsorption and selective properties.

As proposed in Scheme 1, transition metals are firstly bridged by azolate derivatives to generate metal–azolate motifs (MAMs) such as 0-D polynuclear clusters, 1-D chains or 2-D layers. When combined with dicarboxylate ligands, 0-D and 2-D MAMs can be extended into high connected MOFs (connectivity > 9)41,50,51 and pillar-layered MOFs,40,52 respectively, which have been demonstrated in our previous work. For 1-D MAMs, we suppose they can act as rod secondary building units (rod SBUs) to produce another class of materials, rod metal–organic frameworks (rod MOFs).53 As a result, we report herein three such metal–triazolate–dicarboxylate frameworks, namely, {[(CH3)2NH2]2[Cd5(DATRZ)4(BDC)4]}n (SNNU-21), {Cd4(ATRZ)2(BDC)3(DMF)(H2O)}n (SNNU-22) and {[(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn5(TRZ)4(BDC)4]}n (SNNU-23) (SNNU = Shaanxi Normal University, TRZ = 1,2,4-triazole, ATRZ = 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole and DATRZ = 3,5-diamino-1,2,4-triazole, BDC = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid). In these three rod MOFs, a 1-D Cd/Zn–triazolate ribbon connects four adjacent rod SBUs via eight BDC linkers to give 3-D open-frameworks. When DATRZ, ATRZ and TRZ with different numbers of –NH2 groups are used to construct similar metal–triazolate rod SBUs, different space groups, pore sizes and shapes are observed for SNNU-21, -22 and -23. Moreover, three rod MOFs all show permanent porosity and can absorb a remarkable amount of small molecules such as H2, CO2, CH4, C2H2, and C2H4. Notably, SNNU-23 exhibits a very high isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) for C2H2 (62.2 kJ mol−1). To the best of our knowledge, this Qst outperforms the values of all MOF materials reported to date. Also, SNNU-22 and -23 both exhibit high CO2 over CH4 and C2-hydrocarbons over CH4 selectivity under ambient conditions.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Tensor 27 spectrophotometer using KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a NETZSCH STA-449C thermal analyzer at a ramp rate of 10 °C min−1 in nitrogen for SNNU-21 and -22, and under an air atmosphere for SNNU-23. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) studies were carried out with a Japan Rigaku D/Max2550VB+/PC diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å).

Syntheses

{[(CH3)2NH2]2[Cd5(DATRZ)4(BDC)4]}n (SNNU-21). A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (154 mg, 0.5 mmol), H2BDC (42 mg, 0.25 mmol), DATRZ (25 mg, 0.25 mmol) and 6 mL DMF/H2O (v/v = 2:1) solution was stirred in a 20 mL closed vial at room temperature. The mixed solution was heated at 100 °C for 5 days and then it was slowly cooled to room temperature. Colorless block-shaped crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 45% yield based on Cd(II). IR data (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3419(b), 1644(s) 1571(s), 1492(m), 1382(s), 1105(w), 995(w), 837(w), 750(m), 657(w), 547(w).
{Cd4(ATRZ)2(BDC)3(DMF)(H2O)}n (SNNU-22). A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (77 mg, 0.25 mmol), H2BDC (83 mg, 0.5 mmol), ATRZ (21 mg, 0.25 mmol) and 6 mL DMF/H2O (v/v = 2:1) solution was stirred in a 20 mL closed vial at room temperature. The mixed solution was heated at 100 °C for 5 days and then it was slowly cooled to room temperature. Colorless plate-shaped crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 39% yield based on Cd(II). IR data (KBr pellet, cm−1): 1698(m), 1565(s), 1382(s), 1289(m), 1210(w), 1052(w), 1021(w), 877(w), 823(w), 736(m), 530(w).
{[(CH3)2NH2]2[Zn5(TRZ)4(BDC)4]}n (SNNU-23). A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (74 mg, 0.25 mmol), H2BDC (83 mg, 0.5 mmol), TRZ (17 mg, 0.25 mmol) and 8 mL DMF/H2O (v/v = 5:3) solution was stirred in a 20 mL closed vial at room temperature. The mixed solution was heated at 100 °C for 6 days and then it was slowly cooled to room temperature. Colorless needle-shaped crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 53% yield based on Zn(II). IR data (KBr pellet, cm−1): 3450(b), 1667(s), 1596(s), 1509(m), 1342(s), 1390(s), 1280(m), 1153(m), 1091(m), 995(m), 894(w), 837(m), 744(s), 657(m), 578(w), 521(w).

X-ray crystallograpic determination

Single-crystal X-ray analysis was performed on a Bruker Smart APEX II CCD area diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The SADABS program was used for absorption correction. The structure was solved by direct methods, and the structure refinements were based on |F|2 with anisotropic displacement using SHELXTL.54–56 All non-hydrogen atoms in the framework were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters. The large volume fractions of solvents and [(CH3)2NH2]+ cations in the lattice pores could not be modeled in terms of atomic sites and were treated using the SQUEEZE routine in the PLATON software package.57 Crystal data as well as details of data collection and refinements are summarized in Tables S1–S4.†

Gas adsorption

Gas sorption isotherms were obtained on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 HD88 surface-area and pore-size analyzer up to 1 atm of gas pressure by the static volumetric method. All used gases were of 99.99% purity. Prior to sorption analysis, ethanol-exchanged SNNU-21 and acetone-exchanged SNNU-22/-23 were loaded into the sample tube and dried at 60–120 °C by using the “outgas” function of the surface area analyzer. The gas sorption isotherms for N2 and H2 were obtained at 77 K with liquid nitrogen. The gas sorption isotherms for CO2, CH4, C2H2, and C2H4 were obtained at 273 or 298 K, respectively.

Selectivity prediction for binary mixture adsorption

Ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST)59,60 was used to predict binary mixture adsorption from the experimental pure-gas isotherms. To perform the integrations required by IAST, the single-component isotherms should be fitted with an appropriate model. The Langmuir–Freundlich (LF) equation was found to be the best fit (R2 > 0.999, see details in the ESI†) to the experimental pure isotherms for CO2, C2H2, and C2H4 and CH4 of SNNU-22 and -23. On the basis of the equation parameters of pure gas adsorption, the IAST model was further used to investigate the separation of CO2/CH4, C2H2/CH4 and C2H4/CH4.

Results and discussion

Syntheses

At the same temperature and in the DMF/H2O binary solvent system, solvothermal reactions of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O or Zn(NO3)·6H2O, the corresponding triazole ligands and BDC afforded pure crystals of SNNU-21–23. All are expected rod MOFs containing Zn–triazolate rod SBUs. The powder X-ray diffraction patterns SNNU-21–23 are consistent with the simulated ones from single-crystal structures, indicating the phase purity of the as-synthesized samples (Fig. S1†). FT-IR spectra display the characteristic absorption band for the main functional groups (Fig. S2†). The absorption bands in the range of 1250–1750 cm−1 are assigned to the carboxylate groups. The bands in the 3000–3750 and 800–1300 cm−1 regions are related to the triazolate ligands.

Crystal structure of SNNU-21

Rod MOF SNNU-21 crystallizes in the orthorhombic Ibca space group with a 1-D [Cd5(DATRZ)4]6+ cationic ribbon as a rod SBU. As shown in Fig. S3,† the asymmetric unit contains three crystallographically independent Cd(II) cations, two BDC anions and two DATRZ, where the Cd2 locates on a twofold axis, and other atoms are located in general positions. All Cd atoms display distorted octahedral geometry, whereas there are two different detailed environments. The Cd1 and Cd3 are bound to four oxygen atoms from two BDC ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two DATRZ ligands, forming a severely distorted octahedron. This irregularity should originate from the fact that two carboxylate groups both chelate the Cd center on the same side. The Cd2 is coordinated by two oxygen atoms from two BDC ligands and four nitrogen atoms from four DATRZ ligands. The bond distances range from 2.240 to 2.497 Å and 2.199 to 2.337 Å for Cd–O and Cd–N, respectively (Table S2†).

As stated above, 1,2,4-triazolate derivatives easily form a polynuclear cluster via their diverse bridging mode. Two Cd1 and one Cd2 are firstly bridged by four DATRZ ligands using the N1,N2-fashion to form a typical linear trinuclear cluster with Cd2 as the symmetrical center. The Cd⋯Cd separation is of 3.7335(7) Å. Adjacent trinuclear clusters are linked by two Cd3 centers through the N4 sites to give a 1D metal–organic ribbon along the a-axis direction (Fig. 1a). Also, [Cd4(DATRZ)4] metal–organic quadrangles were observed in this ribbon with the Cd⋯Cd separations of 6.6891(9) and 6.5384(7) Å, respectively. In order to ease the repulsive force between amino groups, each triazole plane is basically perpendicular to the [Cd4] quadrangle plane. Furthermore, the carboxyl groups from two independent BDC ligands help to finish the octahedral sphere of three Cd atoms.


Fig. 1 (a) The 1D Cd–DATRZ ribbon structure. (b) The connections between four adjacent ribbons by BDC linkers. (c) View of the 3D open framework of SNNU-21.

Overall, each Cd–DATRZ ribbon connects the adjacent four same ribbons through eight symmetrically distributed BDC ligands as depicted in Fig. 1b. Thus, 1-D ribbon motifs are finally extended into the 3-D open framework of SNNU-21. Two types of 1-D channels exist along the a-axis direction with the dimensions of about 6.0 Å × 7.0 Å and 7.5 Å × 10.5 Å (Fig. 1c), which should be entrapped with [(CH3)2NH2]+ cations to balance the total charge of the open framework. According to the rod-packing concept, the 3-D framework of SNNU-21 exhibits a rather simple topological framework with Cd–DATRZ ribbons as rod SBUs. Each rod SBU links four adjacent rods through eight BDC linkers to generate the 3-D rod MOF architecture of SNNU-21 as shown in Scheme 1.

Crystal structure of SNNU-22

When DATRZ were replaced by ATRZ under the same solvothermal conditions, SNNU-22, the other rod MOF crystallizing in the orthorhombic Pbcn space group, was produced. As given in Fig. S3,† the asymmetric unit contains four crystallographically independent Cd atoms, three BDC anions, two ATRZ, one coordinated H2O and one coordinated DMF molecule. Cd1, Cd2 and Cd3 atoms all are of six-coordinated octahedral geometry. Cd1 is bound to two N atoms from two ATRZ ligands, two oxygen atoms from one carboxyl group, one coordinated H2O and one coordinated DMF molecule. Cd2 is surrounded by two nitrogen atoms from two ATRZ ligands and four oxygen atoms from four different BDC ligands. While the Cd3 octahedron is completed by one nitrogen atom from the ATRZ ligand and five oxygen atoms from four different BDC ligands. Different from other Cd centers, Cd4 is of 7-coordinated distorted pentagonal bipyramidal spheres completed by one nitrogen atom from the ATRZ ligand and six oxygen atoms from four BDC ligands. The bonds distances range from 2.227 to 2.487 Å and 2.181 to 2.266 Å for Cd–O and Cd–N, respectively (Table S3†).

Unlike SNNU-21, Cd atoms are bridged by triazole anions through the N1,N2,N4-fashion to form a 1-D helical chain along the c-axis direction in SNNU-22 (Fig. 2a). The screw pitch is 14.3533(11) Å. Cd⋯Cd separations range from 3.5915(9) Å to 6.5227(11) Å. Carboxyl groups of three unique BDC ligands take the μ2-, μ3- or μ4-bridging mode to bond with four Cd centers in the 1-D chain to complete their 6- or 7-coordinated geometries. Just like SNNU-21, each Cd–triazolate chain connects four near chains via eight BDC ligands and finally leads to the 3-D open framework of SNNU-22 (Fig. 2b). Also, two types of 1-D channels exist along the c-axis direction with the dimensions of about 4.0 Å × 5.0 Å and 6.0 Å × 10.0 Å. Clearly, SNNU-21 and SNNU-22 have a similar rod packing topology with 1-D Cd–triazolate chains as rod SBUs. The distances between the two nearest rods are of about 13.5 Å and 20.0 Å for SNNU-21, and 9.5 Å and 20.0 Å for SNNU-22, respectively.


Fig. 2 (a) The 1D Cd–ATRZ ribbon structure. (b) The connections between four adjacent ribbons by BDC linkers. (c) View of the 3D open framework of SNNU-22.

Crystal structure of SNNU-23

The third rod MOF SNNU-23 crystallizes in the orthorhombic Imma space group, which has a similar metal–triazolate rod SBU as SNNU-21. The asymmetric unit contains three independent Zn atoms, one TRZ and two BDC anions. Three Zn centers are of 4-, 5- and 6-coordinated environments, respectively (Fig. S3†). Zn1 bonds to three oxygen atoms from two BDC ligands and two nitrogen atoms from different TRZ ligands to give a distorted square pyramidal geometry. Zn2 is octahedral surrounded by two axial oxygen atoms from two BDC ligands and four nitrogen atoms from four TRZ ligands. The Zn3 tetrahedral structure is finished by two oxygen atoms from two BDC ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two TRZ ligands. The bonds distances range from 1.923 to 2.238 Å and 1.966 to 2.111 Å for Zn–O and Zn–N, respectively (Table S4†).

As shown in Fig. 3a, three unique Zn atoms are bridged by TRZ linkers through the N1,N2,N4-mode to form a 1D metal–organic ribbon like that of SNNU-21. Due to the absence of NH2 groups, the distribution of triazole groups around metal centers in SNNU-23 is much regular. The Zn⋯Zn separations in the trinuclear cluster and metal–organic quadrangle are of 3.5693(11) Å and 6.1506(8) Å, respectively. Like SNNU-21, each metal–triazolate rod SBU connects adjacent four rods to give the 3-D rod-packing framework of SNNU-23 (Fig. 3b). Since the coordination modes of Zn are different from Cd atoms, this leads to different orientations of the BDC linker around each ribbon-like motif, and thus the slightly different connectivity between two rod SBUs. The absence of NH2 groups from triazole ligands and different arrangements of BDC linkers in SNNU-23 also help to change the pore type in the framework. Different from the monodirectional channels along the a-axis direction in SNNU-21, intersectional channels are observed in SNNU-22. Two types of channels with dimensions of about 3.5 Å × 11 Å and 6.0 Å × 12 Å extend along the a-axis, and one type of channel (3.0 Å × 6.5 Å) exists along the b-axis direction. Such intersecting channels may help the adsorption and separation of small gas molecules.


Fig. 3 (a) The 1D Zn–TRZ ribbon structure. (b) The connections between four adjacent ribbons by BDC linkers. (c) View of the 3D open framework of SNNU-23.

Gas adsorption properties

Rod MOFs SNNU-21, -22 and -23 all possess 1-D channels. PLATON calculations show that their guest accessible volumes are 9336.4 Å3, 3753.4 Å3 and 4368.0 Å3, which occupy approximately 51.4%, 34.6% and 53.0% of the volume of the whole crystal, respectively. Furthermore, the TGA results show that these 3D rod MOF frameworks all can be stable up to about 250 °C. Such high porosity and thermal stability make them suitable candidates for gas sorption applications.

To confirm the permanent porosity of SNNU-21, -22 and -23, N2 adsorption measurements were carried out at 77 K. As depicted in Fig. 4a, the N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms all indicate reversible type-I sorption behaviour.61 The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas derived from the N2 sorption isotherms are 190.1, 222.9 and 624.7 m2 g−1 for SNNU-21, -22 and -23, respectively. Apparently, these three rod MOF materials exhibit moderately the characteristics of micropores, which prompted us to further investigate their adsorptive properties of small gas molecules such as H2, CO2, CH4, C2H2 and C2H4.


Fig. 4 Adsorption isotherms of N2 (a) and H2 (b) at 77 K, CO2 at 273 K (c), and hydrocarbons (C2H2, C2H4 and CH4) at 273 K (d) for SNNU-21–23.

The low-pressure H2 sorption isotherms are given in Fig. 4b and Table 1. At 77 K and 1 atm, the H2 uptake is 59.9 cm3 g−1 (0.53 wt%) for SNNU-21, 71.7 cm3 g−1 (0.64 wt%) for SNNU-22 and 165.6 cm3 g−1 (1.47 wt%) for SNNU-23. Specially, the H2 uptake of SNNU-23 is comparable to many famous frameworks such as MOF-5 (1.32 wt%),62 MOF-74-Zn (1.35 wt%),63 MOF-177 (1.39 wt% at 1.4 atm)63 and NJU-Bai11 (1.43 wt%)64 under the same conditions.

The sorption behaviors of SNNU-21/-22/-23 toward CO2 at 273 and 298 K (Fig. 4c and S5–S7†) were investigated carefully. At 273 K and 1 atm, the CO2 uptake is 31.2 cm3 g−1 for SNNU-21, 32.8 cm3 g−1 for SNNU-22 and 78.4 cm3 g−1 for SNNU-23. The CO2 uptake of SNNU-23 outperforms a lot of similar metal–triazolate–dicarboxylate MOFs, even though most of them are decorated with amino groups like SNNU-21 and -22. At 298 K, SNNU-23 exhibits the CO2 uptake of 48.3 cm3 g−1, which also surpasses the values of some amino-decorated MOFs, such as [Zn(ATRZ)(BDC)0.5].40 Clearly, the explanation of the enhanced CO2 uptake of metal–triazolate–dicarboxylate ternary MOFs in the presence of amino groups is debatable. Other factors such as the synergistic effect of pore size and functionality may play a greater role. This point has also been demonstrated in our previous work on the pillar-layered MOF system.

Due to the very similar N2 and CO2 uptake performances of SNNU-21 and -22, only one of them was selected to further adsorb light hydrocarbons (Table 1). SNNU-22 can take up CH4 (2.51 cm3 g−1), C2H2 (40.9 cm3 g−1) and C2H4 (29.2 cm3 g−1) at 1 atm and 273 K. For SNNU-23, under the same conditions, the uptake is 19.0 cm3 g−1 for CH4, 93.0 cm3 g−1 for C2H2, and 76.9 cm3 g−1 for C2H4 (Fig. 4d and S5–S7†). Such adsorption capacities inspired us to further investigate the interactions between the gas molecules and frameworks, and also the separation of CO2, C2H2 and C2H4 from CH4 by rod MOFs SNUU-22 and -23.

Heat of adsorption

To understand the affinity of SNNU-22/-23 to small gas molecules, the isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) is calculated by the virial model (Fig. 5 and S8–S11†). For SNNU-22, the Qst values at zero coverage are 39.1, 17.8, 20.7 and 5.8 kJ mol−1 for C2H2, C2H4, CO2 and CH4, respectively. The corresponding values are 62.2, 31.7, 21.1 and 25.2 kJ mol−1 for SNNU-23. The higher Qst values stand for the stronger interactions between the gas molecules and MOF frameworks at low pressure. Although the C2H2 storage capacity of SNNU-23 is not top high, the Qst value for C2H2 reaches 62.2 kJ mol−1 at zero coverage, which is the highest value among all the reported MOF materials to date (Fig. 5c) including famous MOFs such as MOF-74-Co (50.1 kJ mol−1), MOF-74-Fe (46 kJ mol−1), UTSA-34b (50 kJ mol−1), HKUST-1 (30.4 kJ mol−1) and SIFSIX-2-Cu-i (41.9 kJ mol−1) (Fig. 5c).65–69 In our opinion, the exceptionally high Qst of SNNU-23 is mainly attributed to the suitable pore size (3.5 Å × 11 Å and 3.0 Å × 6.5 Å) and its anionic skeleton. The uncoordinated carboxylate O atoms distributed in the narrow channels may also help for the C2H2 uptake.

Separations of CO2 or C2-hydrocarbons from CH4

The unique pore structure in SNNU-22/-23 encouraged us to examine their potential applications for selective separation of CO2 and C2-hydrocarbons over CH4. In order to establish the feasibility of these gas separations, an ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) calculation based on a Langmuir–Freundlich (LF) simulation was employed on the basis of the single-component CO2, C2H2, C2H4 and CH4 adsorption isotherms at different temperatures. At 273 K, for the mixtures composed of equimolar CO2 or C2-hydrocarbons to CH4 mixtures, the initial selectivities of SNNU-23 are 8.7, 30.6 and 24.9 for CO2/CH4, C2H2/CH4 and C2H4/CH4, respectively (Fig. 6 and S12–S15†). Under the same conditions, these values are 7.1, 35.7 and 20.3 for SNNU-22. At 298 K, these values are 15.8, 17.4 and 40.0 for SNNU-23 and 4.5, 9.9 and 12.1 for SNNU-22 (Fig. S16†). The CO2/CH4 selectivity of SNNU-23 at 298 K is much higher than those of ZJU-60 (5–5.5),2 JLU-Liu22 (9.4)70 and 4-In-EBPTC (6).71 Furthermore, the C2H2/CH4 and C2H4/CH4 selectivities of SNNU-23 are also much higher than many reported MOFs such as Zn-NH2BTB-1-PIM,72 CoTZB(INT)73 and ZJU-3169 under the same conditions. In our opinion, the high C2-hydrocarbons over CH4 separations should be due to the synergistic effect of the anionic framework, suitable pore size and the moderate surface area. Also, the different polarizability of hydrocarbons (CH4 = 25 × 10−25 cm3, C2H2 = 33–39 × 10−25 cm3 and C2H4 = 42 × 10−25 cm3)74 may also help for the C2-hydrocarbons selectivity over CH4.
Fig. 6 Comparison of experimental and simulated adsorption isotherms (left Y axis), and mixture adsorption selectivity predicted by IAST analysis (right Y axis) of SNNU-23 for equimolar binary-mixture at 273 K: CO2/CH4 (a), C2H2/CH4 (b) and C2H4/CH4 (c), and the comparison of selectivity predicted for an equimolar binary-mixture at 273 K by IAST for SNNU-22 and -23 (d).

Notably, the temperature has a different influence on the gas separation performance of two similar rod MOFs (Fig. S16†). For SNNU-22, CO2/CH4, C2H2/CH4 and C2H4/CH4 selectivities all decrease with the enhancement of temperature. But for SNNU-23, the C2H2/CH4 selectivity is 30.6 at 273 K, and only 17.4 at 298 K. In contrast, the C2H4/CH4 selectivity is 24.9 at 273 K, but 40.0 at 298 K. Overall, these differences should be mainly caused by the different interactions between gas molecules and MOF frameworks, which have been indicated by the heat of adsorption (Qst) values. These results may also help the evaluation of work conditions for these MOF materials. Overall, SNNU-23 should be a good candidate to purify CH4 from gas mixtures containing CO2, C2H2 or C2H4 species under ambient conditions.

Conclusions

In summary, on the basis of the metal–triazolate–dicarboxylate ternary system, we have designed and synthesized three novel microporous MOFs with similar rod-packing topology. In the metal–triazolate rod SBUs decorated with different NH2 groups, different space groups, pore sizes and shapes are observed for three MOFs. All rod MOFs show permanent porosity and can absorb a remarkable amount of small gas molecules. Specially, SNNU-23 shows significantly high CO2 over CH4 and C2-hydrocarbons over CH4 selectivity. Also, SNNU-23 exhibits a very high isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) for C2H2, which outperforms the values of all MOF materials reported so far. Further explorations on new MOF members from the metal–azolate–dicarboxylate family for gas adsorption and separation applications are ongoing in our lab.
