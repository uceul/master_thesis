Unprecedented interweaving of single-helical chains into a chiral metal–organic framework based on a flexible ligand†

Yan-Hong Xu , Ya-Qian Lan , Shui-Xing Wu , Kui-Zhan Shao , Zhong-Min Su * and Yi Liao *
Institute of Functional Material Chemistry, Key Lab of Polyoxometalate Science of Ministry of Education, Faculty of Chemistry, Northeast Normal University, Changchun, 130024, China

Received 19th January 2009 , Accepted 29th April 2009

First published on 21st May 2009

An unprecedented chiral metal–organic framework has been constructed based on diethyl 2,2′-(1H,1′H-2,2′-biimidazole-1,1′-diyl)diacetate ligand, in which the original chirality derives from the configurational effect of a flexible ligand and transfers to the whole network through interweaving three kinds of single-helical chains. Moreover, it possesses a novel four-connected (32.72.82)(3.4.73.8)2 topology. In addition, compound 1 also exhibits strong blue photoluminescence in the solid state at room temperature.

Introduction

Crystal engineering based on metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) has recently attracted considerable interest, owing to their versatile framework topologies as well as their potential applications as functional materials in molecular magnetism, catalysis, gas sorption, fluorescent sensing, and optoelectronic devices.1,2 Especially, the construction of chiral coordination polymers has aroused a great deal of attention due to their intriguing potential applications, for example, in medicine and asymmetric heterogeneous catalysis.3 Abundant chiral MOFs have been synthesized by using chiral organic ligands, chiral metal compounds, or spontaneous solution from achiral materials without any chiral auxiliaries.4 Meanwhile, helical structures, which are ubiquitous in nature and are the foundation of the genetic code, have been attracting increased attention in coordination chemistry and materials chemistry due to their importance in biological systems, optical devices and asymmetric catalysis.5,6 Many chemists have made large contributions to this area, and the design of helical coordination polymers through the self-assembly of organic ligands and metal cations has shown significant progress to date.7 Although the basic features of the design necessary to assemble discrete helices or low dimensional helical coordination polymers have been extensively reported, while control of the assembly of the helical units into a multi-helical array is still a great challenge.8 Up to now, only a few examples which are involved with equal or unequal helical chains, have been reported concerning harmonic single and multiple helices in one coordination compound.9

2,2′-Biimidazole (H2biim) has been widely used as a biomimetic ligand in antitumour drugs,10 bioinorganic chemistry,11 a bridging ligand in oligometallic chemistry for catalysis,12 and building blocks of supermolecular frameworks.13 The coordination polymers of N-substituted H2biim, which is governed by the larger dihedral angle (67 to 89°) between the two imidazole rings as a result of repulsion between the substituents and thus prefer bridging to chelating coordination fashions.14 In addition, polycarboxylate ligands have been one of the most useful multifunctional ligands in the construction of MOFs due to their strong coordination ability, chemical stability and structural rigidity.14c, 15

Taking these points into account, we have designed and synthesized a ligand, namely, diethyl 2,2′-(1H,1′H-2,2′-biimidazole-1,1′-diyl)diacetate (L′), in which the –COOC2H5groups can be hydrolyzed into carboxylic groups (L) (Scheme 1). Our interest in L is based on the following considerations: (I) Two rigid imidazole rings of L are connected by a rotatable C–C bond, which allows the L ligand with subtle conformational adaptation. (II) The additional strong coordinating ability of carboxylate arms is expected to exhibit more varied coordinating patterns in the construction of coordination frameworks. (III) The –CH2– spacer between the imidazole rings and –COO donor groups offers flexible orientations of the carboxylate arms, favoring the formation of unique supramolecular structures. (IV) Upon coordination as a bridge, the freedom of the imidazole rings to rotate around the C–C single bond and the flexible carboxylate arms, and the ligand may be locked in a twisted chiral conformation. Herein, we report fascinating interweaved helices based on Zn(II) compound with the versatile L ligand. In addition, the thermogravimetric analysis and luminescent properties of the compound have been investigated in detail.

Experimental

General procedures

All reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. 2,2′-biimidazole (H2biim) was synthesized readily by the procedure reported in the literature.16
Synthesis of diethyl 2,2′-(1H,1′H-2,2′-biimidazole-1,1′-diyl)diacetate(L′). A solution containing 2,2′-biimidzole (0.01 mol) and NaOH (0.02 mol) in DMF (25 mL) was heated at 60 °C for 30 min. Then, ethyl 2-bromoacetate (0.02 mol) was added directly, and the mixture was continuously stirred and heated for 10 h, the solvent was removed under vacuum. The rest was added into 50 ml water and then filtered, the deposits were recrystallized by ethyl acetate. After evaporating of the solvent ethyl acetate, the product diethyl 2,2′-(1H,1′H-2,2′-biimidazole-1,1′-diyl)diacetate (L′) would be obtained with yield of 70%.
Synthesis of {[Zn3(L)2(µ2–OH)2]·4H2O}n (1). A mixture of Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O (0.219 g, 1 mmol) and L′ (0.306 g, 1 mmol) was added to 10 mL distilled water at room temperature. When the pH value of the mixture was adjusted to about 8.5 with NaOH solution (1 M), the mixture was transferred to a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel and heated to 160 °C for 72 h and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C h−1. Colorless block crystals of 1 were obtained in ca. 88% (based on Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O). Elemental analyses calcd for C20H26N8O14Zn3 (798.60): C, 30.08; H, 3.28; N, 14.09. Found: C, 30.14; H, 3.24; N, 14.07%. Selected IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3425s, 1642s, 1475s, 1388s, 1320s, 1288s, 1146s, 963s, 922s, 781s, 738s, 697s.

Physical measurements

Elemental analyses of carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen were carried out with a Carlo Erba 1106 elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Mattson Alpha-Centauri spectrometer. Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer TG-7 analyzer heated from room temperature to 800 °C under nitrogen. The emission/excitation spectra were recorded on a Varian Cary Eclipse spectrometer. PXRD patterns were recorded on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer with Cu KR (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation.

X-Ray crystallography

Data collection of compound 1 was performed on a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. All absorption corrections were performed by using the SADABS program. The crystal structure was solved by direct methods and refined with full-matrix least-squares (SHELXTL-97) with atomic coordinates and anisotropic thermal parameters for some non-hydrogen atoms. The disordered terminal O atoms of the polyanions in the compound, the disordered O atoms of the water molecules (O2W), and the disordered C atoms were refined using C and O atoms split over two sites, with a total occupancy of 1. The hydrogen atoms of the aromatic rings were included in the structure factor calculation at idealized positions by using a riding model. Results and discussion

Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallized in the chiral space groupP3221 and the fundamental unit consists of two kinds of Zn(II) ions, one L ligand and one µ2–OH group (Fig. 1). The two crystallographic independent Zn atoms represented herein, exhibit two different coordination geometries. Zn(1) displays a distorted tetrahedral geometry, which is surrounded by two nitrogen atoms (Zn(1)–N(1) = 2.036(7) Å and Zn(1)–N(3B) = 2.027(5) Å) from two distinct L ligands, one carboxylate oxygen atom (Zn(1)–O(1A) = 1.961(4)Å) from one L ligand in monodentate fashion and one µ2–OH group (Zn(1)–O(5) = 1.894(5) Å). Whereas the Zn(2) atom lying on a twofold axis is six-coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms (Zn(2)–O(3C) = 2.435(13), Zn(2)–O(4C) = 1.949(16), Zn(2)–O(3) = 2.435(13) and Zn(2)–O(4) = 1.949(16) Å) from two different L ligands in chelate bidentate fashion and two µ2–OH groups (Zn(2)–O(5) = 1.935(7) and Zn(2)–O(5C) = 1.935(7) Å) to finish a highly distorted octahedral geometry with a O6 donor set. The Zn–O and Zn–N bond lengths are all within the normal ranges.17 Obviously, the average bond lengths around Zn(1) are somewhat shorter than those of Zn(2), which is consistent with the fact that bond lengths in a tetrahedral geometry are generally shorter than those in other geometries, which is comparable to data previously reported in the literature.18 The two imidazole rings of L are not coplanar with a dihedral angle of 80.41°.
Fig. 1 Coordination environment of Zn(II) atoms in 1, Symmetry code: (A)1 − y, x − y, −1/3 + z; (B) x − y, −y, 4/3 − z; (C) y, x, 1 − z; hydrogen atoms were omitted for clarity.

The most striking feature of compound 1 is interweaving of single helical chains and novel unequal double-helical chains formed by complicated linkages of the flexible L ligands and µ2–OH groups between different Zn(II) ions (Fig. 2). In the double-stranded helices, chains with the same chirality are interconnected through nitrogen atoms and the carboxyl arms of other L ligands and the carboxyl groups of inner helical chains. It is interesting to note that part of unequal double-helical chains and other L ligands construct another kind of single helical chains (Fig. 2b, 2d, ESI, Fig. S1).† Due to the intricacy of the structure, a separate description of several relative chains is needed, i.e., metal-H2biim-(µ2–OH) (A), metal-L-(µ2–OH) (B), and metal-L (C).


Fig. 2 (a) Two interwoven double-stranded helical chains with the same orientation. Two left-orientated chains are colored green and pink. (b) Neighboring helical chains are interconnected by nitrogen atoms and the carboxyl arms of another L. (c) and (d) View of A and B along the c-axis, respectively.

The single helix A consists of Zn(1), Zn(2), H2biim ligands and the µ2–OH groups, while the carboxyl groups of L do not participate in the helical structure. Four nitrogen atoms from two different H2biim rings are stuck to two Zn(1) centers in monodentate fashion, and then further linking Zn(2) by the µ2–OH groups, producing an infinite left-handed helical chain (Zn1–H2biim–H2biim–Zn1–(µ2–OH)–Zn2)n along the b-axis, which is generated along the crystallographic 31 screw axis with a pitch of 19.7 Å based upon the repeated unit (Fig. 2a and c, ESI, Fig. S2A). The single helix B is composed of atoms of the L ligand which are ligated to three Zn(1) centers in a monodentate mode, and then further connecting Zn(2)via µ2–OH groups, producing an infinite left-handed helical chain (Zn1–L–Zn1–Zn1–L–Zn1–(µ2–OH)–Zn2)n along the b-axis, which is also generated along the crystallographic 31 screw axis with a relatively long pitch of 24.0 Å grounded on the repeated unit (Fig. 2a and c, ESI, Fig. S2B†). The single helix C is made of Zn(1) and L ligands. The Zn(2) atoms and µ2–OH groups don not participate in the helical structure. One carboxylate atom and one nitrogen atom of the L ligand are bonded to two Zn(1) centers in a monodentate fashion, giving rise to an infinite left-handed helical chain (Zn1–L)n along the b-axis, which is generated along the crystallographic 31 screw axis with a pitch of 14.0 Å based on the repeated unit (Fig. 2b and d, ESI, Fig. S2C†).

By comparison with the three kinds of the helical chains A, B and C, we found that the L ligand adopts different coordination modes: in A, L ligand adopts µ2-coordination mode via two nitrogen atoms of H2biim ring to bond two Zn(II) ions (ESI, Fig. S3(a)†); while in B, the L ligand adopts a µ3-coordination fashion through the two nitrogen atoms and one carboxylate oxygen atom to connect three Zn(II) ions (ESI, Fig. S3(b)†); in C, the µ2-coordination fashion of L is different from that in A, the L ligand links two Zn(II) ions via one nitrogen atom and one carboxylate oxygen atom (ESI, Fig. S3(c)†) Consequently, we believe that the coordination fashion of the L ligand plays an important role in the three kinds of the helical chain. Furthermore, µ2–OH groups are also involved in constructing helices A and B, which also plays a crucial role in constructing the two kinds of helical chains.

As can be seen from Fig. 2c and 2d, the carboxyl arms and nitrogen atoms of L ligands are both bonding the metal centers, respectively, to interconnect the unequal double-stranded helices into an unprecedented three-dimensional (3D) network along the crystallographic c-axis (Fig. 3a). Such a novel 3D framework can be regarded as a unique metal–organic molecular multi-channel directly linked by helices, which can be represented by the ideal framework as shown in Fig. 3b and c (schematic view of eight single helices in the same direction). A cross section shows that the multi-channel is composed of three parts: trunk, inner shuck and outer shuck. Firstly, the open-ended channel is supported by six C in hexagonal arrangement, just like the trunk. Secondly, the inner shuck is generated by the left-handed helical chain A. Thirdly, the helical chain B along the crystallographic 31 screw axis with a long pitch forms the outer shuck. It is worth mentioning here that the inorganic helices can not be separated, channels A and B share the trunk C to build a 3D framework. Remarkably, compared with rare examples of interweaving different helical ribbons, the unusual helical motif in this compound may be one of the most complicated entanglement structures ever found in the coordination chemistry of helical arrangements.9b,c Moreover, if each metal center and each L ligand act as four-connecting nodes, the whole structure can be simplified to a novel four-connected chiral network with a Schäfli symbol of (32.72.82)(3.4.73.8)2 (Fig. 4).


Fig. 3 (a) Polyhedral view of the whole network along the c-axis. (b) An ideal view of the helix-linked 3D framework. (c) Schematic view of eight single helices along the same direction.

Fig. 4 An unusual topology of the four-connected network of compound 1 viewed along the c-axis (Zn1: turquoise atoms; Zn2: pink atoms; L: blue atoms).

To identify the stability of the main framework, thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was studied (ESI, Fig. S4†). The TGA curve for compound 1 revealed that the release of water molecules occurs in the range 25–140 °C, and the decomposition of the whole network begins above 387 °C. Moreover, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurement was also performed (ESI, Fig. S5†). The diffraction peak of compound 1 was in excellent agreement with the simulated one on the basis of the single-crystal structure, indicating the phase purity of compound 1.

In addition, the luminescent properties of the ligand and the compound have also been investigated as shown in Fig. 5. It can be observed that emission occurs at 453 nm (λex = 365 nm) for 1, and 356 nm for L ligand (λex = 293 nm). In comparison to the free ligand the emission maximum of compound 1 exists in an obvious red-shift of 97 nm. To get further insight into the nature of the excited states responsible for the emissions, time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) calculation was carried out using B3LYP19 method implemented in Gaussian 03 program suite.20 We used LANL2DZ basis set with the relativistic effective core potential (ECP) for Zn21 and the standard basis set 6-31G(d) for the other elements. Singlet point calculations were executed on the simplified calculation structure (ESI, Fig. S6†). Calculated excited state energies, oscillators strengths, and corresponding major contribution are tabulated (ESI, Table S2†), and the molecular orbitals relative to the transitions are presented (ESI, Fig. S7†). As seen from Fig. S7, the lowest four excited states can be assigned as charge transfer transitions from carboxyl groups (mainly n orbitals of oxygen atoms) in two ligands to H2biim groups (π* orbitals) of the other two. All these transitions were not involved with contributions from zinc atoms, and also could not be assigned to both π–π* transitions and intraligand-charge-transfer transitions in the ligand (ESI, Fig. S8†). Consequently, according to the above description, we think the emission of the compound was assigned to the ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT).


Fig. 5 Solid-state emission spectra of L ligand and compound 1 at room temperature.

Conclusion

In summary, a novel metal–organic framework with interweaving of three kinds of single-helical units was reported. It displays a special chiral four-connected (32.72.82)(3.4.73.8)2 network. The compound also represents an intriguing example of a metal–organic framework based on flexible ligands and µ2–OH groups, demonstrating that the flexible ligand is prone to constructing a fascinating architecture and may further contribute to understanding the assembly and recognition process in coordination chemistry.
