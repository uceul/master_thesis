Metal–organic frameworks from novel flexible triptycene- and pentiptycene-based ligands†

Angela K. Crane, Elaine Y. L. Wong and Mark J. MacLachlan*
Department of Chemistry, University of British Columbia, 2036 Main Mall, Vancouver, British Columbia V6T 1Z1, Canada. E-mail: mmaclach@chem.ubc.ca; Fax: +1 604 822 2847; Tel: +1 604 822 3070

Received 23rd July 2013, Accepted 26th September 2013

First published on 18th October 2013

Four distinct iptycene-containing metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) were synthesized and characterized using two new organic ligands. By varying the reaction conditions a series of 1-D, 2-D, and 3-D frameworks was synthesized with a range of secondary building units (SBUs) using the same triptycene-derived ligand. Furthermore, the first example of a pentiptycene-containing MOF is reported as an interpenetrated 3-D framework containing pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ clusters. The structural integrity of the MOFs upon guest solvent removal varies depending on the framework, and was tracked using powder X-ray diffraction and thermogravimetric analysis. Gas adsorption experiments were conducted on the robust frameworks and revealed no appreciable surface area. Iptycenyl groups and flexible moieties incorporated into the MOF ligand system appear to enhance structural diversity, but also crowd the pores of the MOF or facilitate framework collapse upon solvent removal, reducing accessible surface area.

Introduction

Coordination polymers and networks formed by the bonding of multidentate ligands to metal ions or metal-containing secondary building units (SBUs) can afford 1-D, 2-D, or 3-D extended structures with interesting properties. Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) based on dicarboxylate ligands bound to metal oxo clusters have emerged as a promising class of materials for a variety of applications including catalysis,1 biomedical imaging,2 drug delivery,3 chemical sensing,4 and gas storage.5 In general, metal choice and reaction conditions dictate which SBU is observed, affecting the overall topology of the network.6 Construction of isoreticular MOFs that have the same overall topology is possible with diverse dicarboxylate linkers.7 As a result, properties such as pore size, surface area, and rigidity desired for particular applications can be tuned with some predictability.

One of the simplest ligands used in the construction of MOFs is 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate (BDC). Yaghi and co-workers reacted H2-BDC with Zn(NO3)2 under various conditions to obtain 2-D and 3-D network solids. Using a mild vapor-diffusion method of triethylamine/toluene into a DMF/toluene solution of the precursors yields MOF-2, a 2-D square paddlewheel grid structure with a capped dinuclear zinc SBU.8 However, harsher solvothermal conditions applied to the same precursors in DMF gives MOF-5, a 3-D framework with a simple cubic topology where [Zn4O]6+ clusters are bridged by BDC ligands.9 The porosity and structure of MOF-5 is remarkable and it has been studied extensively for use in H2 and CH4 gas storage,7,10 catalysis,11 and molecular separation12 due to its ease of production, high surface area, and well-defined pore structure.

Moving away from zinc-based systems, Férey et al. have been using H2-BDC in conjunction with aluminum and chromium salts to create isostructural compounds known as MIL-53.13 In the original synthesis, these frameworks have a hydroxide-bridged chain of Cr3+ centers coordinated to the BDC ligand in a zig-zag fashion. These contacts expand and contract upon the introduction of guest molecules such as H2-BDC, water, and DMF in the solid state. As a result, these MOFs are often referred to as “breathable MOFs,” as they have the ability to undergo structural changes without framework degradation in the solid state. Breathable MOFs can also be constructed with a flexible moiety built into the ligand. One example is the complexation of tetrakis[4-(carboxyphenyl)oxamethyl]methane with zinc nitrate hexahydrate, using 4,4′-bipyridine as organic pillars to bridge adjacent Zn2 paddlewheel SBUs in the axial position. In this case, the 3-D nature of the MOF comes from the tetrahedral geometry of the ligand. Furthermore, the pore spaces allow for selective separation of CO2 from a mixture of gases including H2 and N2.14 The CO2 adsorption/desorption curves for this framework show a molecular gateway opening pressure, and PXRD patterns suggest expansion and contraction of the framework without collapse. Other reports of flexible MOFs and related structures have been investigated.15

We and others have been investigating the use of triptycene and related compounds to create porous solid-state structures.16 The intrinsic free volume afforded by this propeller shaped organic molecule is attractive for preventing close-packed structures with large aromatic surfaces exposed.17 Several coordination polymers and MOFs based on triptycene derivatives are now known.18,19 In our previous study, we examined the use of 1,4-triptycenedicarboxylate ligands in MOF synthesis.20 As the orientation of the carboxylate groups in this precursor is identical to that in BDC, we expected to form isoreticular structures to MOF-5, but with triptycene along each edge. Instead, MOFs were isolated from the solvothermal synthesis showing pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ SBUs, where extra solvent molecules are incorporated into the SBU. We postulated that the steric bulk of the triptycene component was forcing the structure to adopt unusual SBUs and thus inhibiting formation of the anticipated porous isoreticular MOFs. As well, the materials did not have any appreciable surface area. With these results in mind, we sought to extend the axis of the triptycene ligand using flexible ether linkages. In doing this we have also added two additional aromatic rings as adsorption sites, and hope that pore opening and closing will allow for gas molecules to pass into the channels of the materials, despite the presence of bulky triptycenyl units. Furthermore, we successfully produced a novel pentiptycene-based ligand that can be used for MOF synthesis, giving the first example of a metal–organic framework incorporating pentiptycene.

Results and discussion

The synthesis of proligand 3H2 (the acid form of dicarboxylate 3) begins with a Williamson ether synthesis of the corresponding methyl ester, 3Me2. 1,4-Triptycenedione, 2, which tautomerizes in situ to give 1,4-triptycenediol (Scheme 1), is reacted with 2 equiv. of 1 to give 3Me2 in 38% yield. Hydrolysis of the methyl ester groups afforded the desired dicarboxylic acid 3H2 in 97% yield. Both the methyl ester 3Me2 and dicarboxylic acid 3H2 were purified by recrystallization to give crystals suitable for single crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD; Fig. 1).

In the solid-state structure of 3Me2, the two aromatic rings that are tethered to the triptycene group by ether linkages are nearly coplanar with the corresponding ring of triptycene (Fig. 1a–b). In compound 3H2, on the other hand, the rings are twisted at angles of 78° and 63° relative to the aromatic ring of the triptycene moiety (Fig. 1c). Furthermore, the molecule crystallizes as an infinite chain with strong hydrogen-bonding between the carboxylic acid groups (Fig. 1d–e). This is supported by the Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectra (ESI†) that show peaks at 2672 cm−1 and 2552 cm−1 that are diagnostic overtones and combination bands of linear carboxylic acid dimers.21

We reacted proligand 3H2 with zinc salts under solvothermal conditions to afford TMOF-3, TMOF-3-bipy, and TMOF-4 (Scheme 2). In the first set of conditions, a DMF solution of 3H2 and 3 equiv. of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O was heated to 110 °C for 72 h in a Teflon-lined autoclave. Following controlled cooling of the solution, single crystals suitable for SCXRD were isolated and the structure of TMOF-3 was elucidated. This structure proved to be 1-D chains of macrocycles, which are each formed by two molecules of 3 bonded to two dinuclear zinc SBUs in a square paddlewheel formation (Fig. 2b). The SBUs are shared between two of the macrocycles in the chain. These SBUs were also capped by dimethylamine, a common decomposition product of the DMF solvent (Fig. 2a). The double-chain structure observed in TMOF-3 is analogous to that observed in tremolite,22 an inosilicate, and has been observed in other coordination polymers and MOFs.23

Preparation of the bulk solid of TMOF-3 for nitrogen adsorption experiments included a 24 h soak in DMF then DCM, followed by drying in vacuo at 120 °C. Structural changes of the bulk sample were tracked by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA; Fig. 3a) and powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD; Fig. 3b) experiments. Overall, few changes were observed in the solvent removal process; a slight shift to higher angle was observed by PXRD in the peaks at 5.5–6.5° 2θ, likely due to a slight shrinkage of the lattice. The TGA traces of TMOF-3 as-synthesized and after soaking for 24 h in DMF show a mass loss at 120 °C corresponding to encapsulated DMF solvent molecules. The mass loss at 300 °C corresponds to the capping dimethylamine ligands, and decomposition of the organic ligand occurs at 400 °C. This thermal stability of TMOF-3 is comparable to other similar MOFs that incorporate triptycene.19,20 Following desolvation, the nitrogen adsorption of the bulk sample was analyzed, but no appreciable surface area was present.


Fig. 3 (a) TGA traces of bulk TMOF-3 as synthesized (red), after soaking in DMF (blue), and after drying in vacuo at 120 °C (green) and (b) PXRD patterns of bulk TMOF-3 as simulated from SCXRD (black), as synthesized (red), after soaking in DMF (blue), and after drying in vacuo at 120 °C (green).

In the structure of TMOF-3, it is apparent that the presence of capping dimethylamine ligands prohibit extradimensional growth of the framework. In order to expand the dimensionality of the structure, we added 4,4-bipyridine to the reaction in an effort to bridge the 1-D square paddlewheel chains into 2-D sheets. Dilution of the DMF with chlorobenzene in a 1:1 ratio allowed for competitive binding of the 4,4′-bipyridine versus the DMF decomposition product dimethylamine. After 72 h of heating at 110 °C, TMOF-3-bipy was isolated as a crystalline solid. SCXRD experiments confirmed that the 1-D chains of TMOF-3 were indeed bridged by the 4,4′-bipyridine capping agent in TMOF-3-bipy (Fig. 4). This bridging, however, caused a disruption in the square paddlewheel motif observed in the structure of TMOF-3. Thus, we observe a dinuclear zinc SBU in TMOF-3-bipy, where two of the carboxylate ligands are bound in a monodentate fashion, two carboxylate ligands are bridging the zinc centers, and the 4,4′-bipyridine ligands are capping. This give an overall SBU of bridged trigonal bipyramidal zinc centers (Fig. 4a).

In the reaction with 4,4′-bipyridine, the use of chlorobenzene as a non-coordinating co-solvent appeared to suppress the coordination of dimethylamine to the metal centers. We therefore turned to this solvent combination to construct a 3-D MOF. In this reaction, a 1:1 mixture of DMF and chlorobenzene was used as before, but without another coordinating ligand such as 4,4′-bipyridine. TMOF-4 was obtained as a crystalline powder and several crystals were suitable for SCXRD analysis. The structure of TMOF-4 is shown in Fig. 5. There are many interesting aspects to this MOF. First, the SBU is not the anticipated [Zn4O]6+ octahedral unit commonly observed, nor is it a distorted pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ as often observed in sterically-hindered triptycene-containing MOFs. Instead, we observe a [Zn7(OH)3]11+ SBU in which there are 12 bridging carboxylate ligands. In order to account for the charge balance of the MOF, one of these 12 carboxylates must be protonated, although we could not determine the location of this proton from the SCXRD data.


Fig. 5 Solid-state structure of TMOF-4 as determined by SCXRD analysis. (a) [Zn7(OH)3]11+ octahedral SBU, (b) double-strutted arrangement of 3 around the SBU, (c) individual framework as viewed along the a-axis, (d) packing of 3-D frameworks as viewed along a-axis. Free solvent molecules, disorder, and hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. Carbon = black, oxygen = red, nitrogen = blue, zinc = pink.

The twelve triptycenyl ligands are arranged around the SBU to give a double-strutted MOF; that is to say that instead of one ligand bridging each SBU in all directions, there are two. These two ligands exhibit π–π stacking of the central triptycene moiety, as well as the extended benzoate rings, with Ar–Ar distances of 3.7-3.8 Å. Double- or multiply-strutted MOFs have been observed in a few cases,24 but we are not aware of any with long bridging ligands. Looking along a given axis, it can also be noted that the ligand set twists 90° each time it passes through a SBU. Lastly, this structure consists of two interpenetrating networks. Despite this, large void spaces are still evident in the solid-state structure of the MOF (Fig. 5).

Having demonstrated that the flexible triptycene-containing ligand 3 can form MOF structures, we set out to incorporate pentiptycene into a MOF by a similar strategy. Our hope was that the MOF could have substantial porosity and surface area by taking advantage of the intrinsic free volume in pentiptycene. Pentiptycene-based compound 5H2 was obtained by a similar route to compound 3H2, starting from 6,13-pentiptycenediol (4; Scheme 3). A single crystal of proligand 5H2 was obtained, and its structure was determined by SCXRD. Fig. 6 shows the molecular and extended structure of 5H2. As expected, 5H2 extends in a hydrogen-bonded chain (verified by IR spectroscopy, which shows the diagnostic carboxylic acid dimer peaks at 2665 cm−1 and 2540 cm−1; ESI†).21 Also, from the packing it can be seen that the aromatic ring of the benzoic acid moiety is interdigitated between the wings of the pentiptycene of an adjacent molecule.

We investigated the reaction of proligand 5H2 with Zn(NO3)2·6H2O under solvothermal conditions in DMF (Scheme 4). Following programmed cooling (0.1 °C min−1), a solution was recovered in which colorless crystals appeared after several days. SCXRD analysis of the product revealed the first structure of a pentiptycene-containing MOF, PMOF-1 (Fig. 7). This MOF is composed of a 3-D structure containing a pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ SBU. In this SBU, a tetrahedral μ4-bridging O2− ligand is surrounded by three tetrahedral zinc centers and an octahedral zinc center. Also, five of the coordinated ligand carboxylate units are μ2-COO− bridging ligands, leaving the sixth in a μ1-COO− monodentate binding fashion. This leaves three coordination sites on the octahedral zinc center for solvent coordination; these are occupied by two DMF molecules and a water molecule. The framework itself is open and highly porous in a 3-D near-cubic network. However, the MOF network is triply interpenetrated to give a dense framework (Fig. 7c).

Removal of the guest solvent molecules in PMOF-1 was attempted and monitored by PXRD (Fig. 8). After soaking the crystals in fresh DMF for 24 h, PXRD indicates that the structure remains intact. However, after solvent exchange in DCM, the framework loses crystallinity. This collapse is likely due to movement about the flexible components in the ligand, as pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ SBUs have proven stable to solvent removal in previous triptycene MOF examples. Attempts to resolvate the lattice and restore crystallinity by reintroducing the crystals to DMF were unsuccessful.

Conclusions

We synthesized three distinct MOFs of varying dimensionality using a triptycene-containing ligand with a flexible ether moiety. Each structure contained a different SBU that generated distinct 1-D, 2-D and 3-D structures. Although collapse of these MOFs was not observed upon solvent removal, no appreciable surface area was measured by N2 gas sorption analysis. Furthermore, the first example of a pentiptycene-containing MOF was presented. In this case, an analogue of a pseudo-[Zn4O]6+ SBU was observed similar to those seen in our previous work with triptycene containing MOFS. Efforts to exploit the porosity in this new material were unsuccessful owing to framework collapse upon solvent removal. Overall, these results demonstrate new methods to fashion metal–organic frameworks containing rigid and bulky triptycene- and pentiptycene-based components. We are continuing to develop MOFs based on these components in an effort to realize highly porous materials for hydrogen storage.

Experimental

All reactions were carried out under air unless otherwise specified. 1,4-Triptycenedione (2) and 6,13-pentiptycenediol (4) were prepared according to literature procedures.26,271H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker AV400 spectrometer. 13C NMR spectra were recorded using a proton decoupled pulse sequence. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were calibrated to the residual protonated solvent at δ 7.27 and δ 77.23, respectively, for CDCl3, at δ 2.50 and δ 39.51, respectively, for DMSO-d6, or at δ 5.32 and δ 53.84, respectively, for CD2Cl2. IR spectra were obtained neat with a Thermo Nicolet 6700 FT-IR with a Smart Orbit attenuated total reflectance (ATR) accessory. Electron Ionisation (EI) mass spectra were obtained at the UBC Microanalytical Services Laboratory using a double focussing mass spectrometer (Kratos MS-50) coupled with a MASPEC data system with EI operating conditions of source temperatures 120–180 °C and ionization energy of 70 eV. Elemental analyses (CHN) were performed at the UBC Microanalytical Services Laboratory. Melting points were obtained on a Fisher-John's melting point apparatus and corrected according to the appropriate calibration. Single crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD) experiments were performed on a Bruker X8 APEX CCD with MoKα radiation or a Bruker APEX DUO with MoKα or CuKα radiation as specified in the ESI.†25 Powder X-ray diffraction experiments were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance with CuKα sealed tube X-ray source and a NaI scintillation detector. Thermogravimetric analysis data was obtained using a Perkin Elmer TGA6 instrument. Gas (N2) adsorption data were obtained at 77 K using a Micromeritics ASAP 2010 analyzer.

Synthesis of PMOF-1

A solution of 5H2 (0.105 g, 0.144 mmol) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.129 g, 0.434 mmol) in DMF (10 mL) was prepared and transferred to a 23 mL Teflon-lined autoclave by filtering the solution through a puck of celite. The reaction vessel was sealed and heated to 110 °C for 72 h. Programmed cooling (0.1 °C min−1 from 110 °C to 25 °C) afforded a clear, orange solution which was transferred to stoppered glass vials. After 24 h, clear, colourless crystalline solids began to form in the vial (0.066 g, 59%). Single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction were isolated and used in structural elucidation of PMOF-1. Confirmation of structure uniformity throughout the bulk sample was performed using PXRD. IR (cm−1) 2929, 1652, 1597, 1480, 1253, 1184, 1099, 819, 797, 768, 749.

Gas adsorption experiments

Gas adsorption experiments were carried out on a bulk sample of desolvated TMOF-3 and PMOF-1. Desolvation was achieved over a series of steps, and structural modifications were monitored by PXRD. Prepared samples were crushed to a fine powder then soaked for 24 h in fresh DMF. This sample was recovered by suction filtration, then soaked for 24 h in dichloromethane. Once again, the sample was recovered by suction filtration and placed under vacuum at 120 °C for 24 h. This afforded desolvated frameworks, which were then tested for gas adsorption.
