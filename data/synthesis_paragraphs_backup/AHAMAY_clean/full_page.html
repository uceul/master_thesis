Abstract

By using ethylenediamine as a structure‐directing agent, two new zinc phosphonates [enH2]0.5Zn[(L)(H2O)]·H2O (1) and [enH2]0.5Zn5[(HL)(L)3(H2O)2]·3H2O (2) (H3L = H2O3PCH(OH)CO2H, en = ethylenediamine), have been synthesised by hydrothermal reaction and structurally characterised by single crystal X‐ray diffraction as well as with infrared spectroscopy, elemental analysis and thermogravimetric analysis. The structure of compound 1 comprises ZnO6 octahedra and CPO3 tetrahedra connected by [O3PCH(OH)CO2]3– to form a 2D layered structure with one‐dimensional channel system along the a axis, and the protonated ethylenediamine cations are being located between two adjacent layers. In compound 2, the connections of alternately arranged ZnO6, ZnO5 polyhedra, and CPO3 tetrahedra result in a 3D framework structure with channels containing the [NH3(CH2)2NH3]2+ cations.

Introduction

Metal phosphonate chemistry has attracted considerable attention in recent years because of their versatile framework topologies as well as their potential applications in the areas of catalysis, ion exchange, proton conductivity, intercalation chemistry, photochemistry and materials chemistry [1–4]. Many efforts have been devoted to the exploration of metal phosphonate materials with new structure types, especially the open‐framework structures. Recently, many metal phosphonates have been prepared through designing and synthesising phosphonic acids with amine, hydroxyl, and carboxylate groups. The use of bi‐ and trifunctional phosphonic acids in the synthesis of metal phosphonates may not only result in new structure types of metal phosphonates, but also bring interesting properties [5–8]. Among these studies, the phosphonic acids with hydroxyl and carboxyl groups proved to be very useful ligands for the synthesis of metal phosphonates with open‐framework structures, since they can adopt various kinds of coordination modes under different reaction conditions that may result in many interesting structures [9–11].

By using 2‐hydroxylphosphonoacetic acid (H3L) with three functional groups (–OH, –COOH, and –PO3H2), a series of metal phosphonates with 2D layered and 3D frameworks were isolated in our laboratory [12–14]. During the past decade, zinc phosphonates received a great deal of attention because of the possibility to form interesting structures. A number of zinc phosphonate compounds were prepared, most of which were structurally determined either by single crystal diffraction technique or from X‐ray powder diffraction data. The structures of these compounds range from one‐dimensional chains, two‐dimensional layers to three‐dimensional pillared layers or open frameworks [15–18]. Recently, several zinc phosphonates with different structure types have also been obtained by our group [19–21]. Among these compounds the ZnII ion is generally tetra‐ or hexacoordinated by oxygen atoms or nitrogen atoms. However, the reports about the ZnII ion pentacoordinated by oxygen atoms are still rare. In this paper, two new zinc phosphonates with a layered and a 3D framework, [enH2]0.5Zn[(L)(H2O)]·H2O (1) and [enH2]0.5Zn5[(HL)(L)3(H2O)2]·3H2O (2), were synthesised in the presence of ethylenediamine structure‐directing agent by hydrothermal technique, using H2O3PCH(OH)CO2H as ligand. The structures of the two title compounds were characterised by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction, infrared spectroscopy, elemental analysis, and thermogravimetric analysis.

Experimental Section

General Procedures

2‐Hydroxyphosphonoacetic acid solution was obtained from Taihe Chemical Factory as a water treatment agent (48.0 wt %) and used as received. All other chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. C, H, and N were determined by using a PE‐2400 elemental analyser. Zinc and phosphorus were determined by using an inductively coupled plasma (ICP) atomic absorption spectrometer. IR spectra were recorded with a Bruker AXS TENSOR‐27 FT‐IR spectrometer with KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm–1. TG analyses were performed with a Perkin–Elmer Pyris Diamond thermal analyses system in static air with a heating rate of 10 K·min–1 from 50 to 900 °C. The X‐ray powder diffraction data was collected with a Bruker AXS D8 Advance diffractometer using Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) in the 2θ range of 5–45 ° with a step size of 0.02 ° and a scanning rate of 2°·min–1.

Syntheses

[enH2]0.5Zn[(L)(H2O)]·H2O (1): A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.30 g, 1.0 mmol), NaF (0.04g, 1.0 mmol), and H3L (0.5 mL, 2.0 mmol) in deionised water (10.0 mL), adjusted to pH ≈︁ 6 with ethylenediamine whilst stirring, was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave, and then heated at 160 °C for 3 days. After the mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature, colourless block crystals were filtered off, washed with distilled water, and dried at room temperature (Yield: ca. 38 % based on zinc). Compound 1 (C3H11NO8PZn): calcd. C 12.62; H 3.88; N 4.91; P 10.85 %; Zn 22.91; found: C 12.68; H 3.82; N 4.97; P 10.81; Zn 22.85 %.

[enH2]0.5Zn5[(HL)(L)3(H2O)2]·3H2O (2): A mixture of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.30 g, 1.0 mmol), NaF (0.04g, 1.0 mmol), and H3L (1.0 mL, 4.0 mmol) in deionised water (10.0 mL), adjusted to pH ≈︁ 4 with ethylenediamine whilst stirring, was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave, and then heated at 160 °C for 3 days. After the mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature, colorless block crystals were filtered off, washed with distilled water, and dried at room temperature (Yield: ca. 46 % based on Zn). Compound 2 (C9H24NO29P4Zn5): calcd. C 10.19; H 2.28; N 1.32; P 11.68; Zn 30.82 %; found: C 10.23; H 2.21; N 1.35; P 11.73; Zn 30.77 %.

X‐ray Crystallography

The data were collected at a temperature of 295 K on a Bruker Smart APEX II X‐diffractometer equipped with graphite monochromated Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). An empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program with Tmax = 0.7584 and Tmin = 0.4555 for compound 1 and Tmax = 0.7382 and Tmin = 0.3111 for compound 2. The structures were solved in the space group P21/n (1) and P\bar{1} (2) by direct method and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares fitting on F2 using SHELXS–97 22. All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were generated geometrically, fixed isotropic thermal parameters, and included in the structure factor calculations. Hydrogen atoms for water molecules were not included in the refinement. A summary of crystal data and structure refinements for the two compounds are listed in Table 1. Selected bond lengths are given in Table S1. CCDC‐<ccdc>634961</ccdc> (1) and CCDC‐<ccdc>627194</ccdc> (2) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. Results and Discussion

Compounds 1 and 2 were synthesised by the reaction of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O with H3L under hydrothermal conditions in the presence of ethylenediamine. As is well‐known, a number of factors involved in the hydrothermal reactions can affect the final products, including the reaction temperature, molar ratio of the starting materials, the presence of the structure‐directing agent, and pH, etc. In this work, we found that the molar ratio of the starting materials and the pH of the reaction mixture played an important role in the formation of compounds 1 and 2. The ethylenediamine was employed as structure‐directing agent, charge‐compensating cation, and to adjust the pH of the reaction mixture. By using ethylenediamine as a structure‐directing agent, compound 1 with a 2D layered structure was obtained as colourless block crystals when the molar ratio of H3L/ Zn(NO3)2·6H2O is 2:1 and the pH is 6.0. When the pH is lower (≈︁ 4.0), compound 2 with a 3D framework structure was obtained as colourless block crystals when the molar ratio of H3L / Zn(NO3)2·6H2O is 4:1. Further decrease of pH (≈︁ 3.0) promotes the formation of recently reported colourless block crystals of [Zn(HL)(H2O)2] as a pure phase when the molar ratio of H3L / Zn(NO3)2·6H2O is 2:1 23. The fluoride was added in order to improve the crystallisation of the final products. The same compounds were obtained in the absence of NaF. Other organic amines were used to adjust the pH of the reaction mixture, but we failed to collect suitable single crystals for X‐ray diffraction. The powder XRD patterns and the simulated XRD patterns of the two title compounds are shown in Supporting Information (see Figure S3, Figure S4). The diffraction peaks on the patterns correspond well in position, confirming that the two compounds are a pure phase. The differences in reflection intensity are probably due to preferred orientation in the powder samples.

X‐ray single crystal diffraction reveals that compound 1 crystallises in monoclinic space group P21/n, and the asymmetric unit consists of [Zn(L)(H2O)]– building units, charge‐compensating [enH2]2+ cations and the lattice water molecule (Figure 1). The L3– (L = O3PCH(OH)CO2) ligand serves as a pentadentate ligand. All oxygen atoms except one phosphonate oxygen atom (O2) in the L3– ligand are involved in the coordination to the zinc atoms. The zinc atom is octahedrally coordinated by two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O1, O3A) from two separate L3– ligands, two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O5B, O6A) from two L3– ligands, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (O4B) from one L3– ligand and one oxygen atom (O7) from a water molecule. Such configuration is favourable because of the formation of stable 6‐atom rings (P–O–Zn–O–C–C) and 5‐atom rings (O–Zn–O–C–C). The Zn–O bond lengths range from 2.039(2) to 2.209(2) Å. These values are close to those reported for other zinc phosphonate compounds [20, 21].

Compound 1 shows a 2D layered structure, and the coordinated oxygen atoms (O7) from water molecules and the non‐coordinated phosphonate oxygen atoms (O2) are orientated toward the interlayer space. The protonated ethylenediamine cations are located between two adjacent layers (Figure 2). Within the layer, each L3– anion behaves as a bridging ligand, two carboxylate oxygen atoms and two phosphonate oxygen atoms together with one hydroxyl oxygen atom chelating one ZnO6 octahedron (Figure 3). As shown in Figure 3, the connections of alternately arranged ZnO6 octahedra and CPO3 tetrahedra result in a two‐dimensional layered structure with one‐dimensional channel systems along the a axis direction. The channel is assembled by 18‐atom rings [9.8 Å (Zn1–Zn1) × 6.7 Å (Zn1–Zn1), estimated by measuring the distances between the centres of opposite atoms], which consist of four zinc, two phosphorus, four carbon, and eight oxygen atoms with the sequences –Zn–O–C–O–Zn–O–P–C–O–Zn–O–C–O–Zn–O–P–C–O–.

Compound 2 crystallises in triclinic space group P\bar{1}. The asymmetric unit is shown in Figure 4. Within the asymmetric unit, five crystallographically independent zinc atoms are found. The Zn1 atom is pentacoordinated by two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O1, O15A) from one separate HL2– ligand and one L3– ligand, one carboxylate oxygen atom (O24B) from one L3– ligand, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (O22B) from one L3– ligand and one oxygen atom (O25) from a water molecule. The Zn2 atom is pentacoordinated by three phosphonate oxygen atoms (O3, O7, O9C) from one separate HL2– ligand, and two L3– ligands, two carboxylate oxygen atom (O5, O12) from one separate HL2– ligand and one L3– ligand. The Zn3 atom is also pentacoordinated by two phosphonate oxygen atoms (O21D, O8) from two separate L3– ligands, one carboxylate oxygen atom (O18) from one L3– ligand, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (O16) from one L3– ligand, and one oxygen atom (O26) from a water molecule. The Zn4 atom, however, exhibits hexacoordinated environment. Three of the six coordination positions are filled with phosphonate oxygen atoms (O13, O19, O19F) from three separate L3– ligands. The remaining sites are occupied by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O11E, O23F) from two L3– ligands, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (O10E) from one L3– ligand. The Zn5 atom also exhibits hexacoordinate environment. Three of the six coordination positions are filled with phosphonate oxygen atoms (O20, O14, O14H) from three separate L3– ligands. The remaining sites are occupied by two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O6G, O17H) from two L3– ligands, one hydroxyl oxygen atom (O4G) from one L3– ligand. The Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.918(2)–2.327(2) Å. These values are in agreement with those reported for other zinc phosphonate compounds [20, 21].

Compound 2 can be described as a 3D open‐framework structure type (Figure 5). Within the ac plane, the connections of alternately arranged ZnO6, ZnO5 polyhedra, and CPO3 tetrahedra result in a 3D framework structure with one‐dimensional channel system along the b axis. The channel system running along the b axis is assembled by 24‐atom rings [13.3 Å (Zn4–Zn5) × 7.9 Å (P1–P2)], which consist of six zinc, two phosphorus, twelve oxygen, and four carbon atoms with the sequences –(Zn–O–P–O–Zn–O–C–O–Zn–O–C–O)2. The protonated ethylenediamine cations and the lattice water molecules are located inside channels along the b axis (Figure 6).

The IR spectra for the two compounds are almost identical corresponding to the common groups, thus only the spectrum of compound 1 will be discussed (Figure S1). The IR spectrum of compound 1 exhibits a broad band at 3429cm–1, corresponding to the vibration of O–H stretching of hydroxyl groups (including water molecules and the 2‐hydroxyphosphonoacetic acid). The band at 3134cm–1 can be attributed to the N–H stretching vibration of [NH3(CH2)2NH3]2+ cations. The bands at 1598 and 1446 cm–1 are observed, which is shifted from the expected value of uncoordinated carboxylic acid [v(C–O) typically around 1725–1700 cm–1]. This shift is due to the carboxylate function coordinated to the metal atom, and these bands are assigned to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of C–O groups when present as COO– moieties 24. The set of bands between 1200 and 900 cm–1 are due to stretching vibrations of the tetrahedral CPO3 groups 25. Additional medium and sharp bands at low energy (between 850 and 400 cm–1) are found, these bands are probably due to bending vibrations of the tetrahedral CPO3 groups.

The thermal analyses of two compounds have been performed in the 50–900 °C temperature range in static air atmosphere (Figure S2). The TGA curve of compound 1 exhibits three main continuous weight losses. The first step started at 50 °C and was completed at 217 °C, corresponding to release of one lattice water molecule and one coordinated water molecule. The observed weight loss of 11.7 % is close to the calculated value (12.6 %). The second step, from 225 to 658 °C, corresponds to release of the [enH2]2+ cations and decomposition of phosphonate units. The third step covers a temperature range of 794–890 °C, which corresponds to the further decomposition of the compound. The total weight loss of 45.8 % is close to the calculated value (46.6 %), if the final product is assumed to be Zn2P2O7.

The TGA of compound 2 also indicates three main steps. The first step, in the temperature range 50–290 °C, is due to the removal of the lattice water molecules and the coordinated water molecules. The observed weight loss of 7.7 % is close to the calculated value (8.5 %). The second step occurred above 290 °C, during which the compound is partially decomposed. The third step between 610 and 820 °C can be attributed to the further decomposition of the organic groups. The total weight loss of 36.3 % is close to the calculated value (34.9 %) if the final product is assumed to be mixture of Zn2P2O7 and ZnO in the molar ratio of 2:1.

Conclusions

Two new zinc phosphonates with a layered and a 3D framework structure, [enH2]0.5Zn[(L)(H2O)]·H2O (1) and [enH2]0.5Zn5[(HL)(L)3(H2O)2]·3H2O (2), were prepared under hydrothermal reaction conditions by the combination of zinc cations with 2‐hydroxyphosphonoacetic acid in the presence of ethylenediamine. The structure of compound 1 comprises ZnO6 octahedra and CPO3 tetrahedra connected by [O3PCH(OH)CO2]3– to form a 2D layered structure with one‐dimensional channel system along the a axis. In compound 2, the connections of alternately arranged ZnO6, ZnO5 polyhedra, and CPO3 tetrahedra result in a 3D framework structure with channels containing the [NH3(CH2)2NH3]2+ cations. The molar ratio of the starting materials and the pH of the reaction mixture are responsible for the formation of the two title compounds with a different structure.
