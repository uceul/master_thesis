A high connectivity metal–organic framework with exceptional hydrogen and methane uptake capacities†

Demin Liu a, Haohan Wu b, Shunzhi Wang a, Zhigang Xie a, Jing Li b and Wenbin Lin *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, CB#3290, University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, NC 27599, USA. E-mail: wlin@unc.edu; Fax: +1-919-962-2388; Tel: +1-919-962-6320
bDepartment of Chemistry and Chemical Biology, Rutgers University, Piscataway, New Jersey 08854, USA

Received 12th May 2012 , Accepted 9th July 2012

First published on 9th July 2012

A 6,8-connected 3-dimensional metal–organic framework (2) of the tph topology was constructed from a new aromatic-rich, tetraphenylmethane-derived octa-carboxylate bridging ligand and trizinc cluster secondary building units (SBUs), and exhibited exceptionally high hydrogen (58 mg g−1 and 39 g L−1 at 52 bar and 77 K) and methane (276 mg g−1 and 189 g L−1 at 80 bar and 298 K) uptake capacities. 2 has the highest hydrogen uptake at 298 K among all of the MOFs that have been examined to date. The gravimetric and volumetric methane uptake capacities for 2 are the highest among thousands of MOFs that have been evaluated. We attribute the exceptionally high gas uptake capacities of 2 to the highly branched, aromatic-rich nature of the bridging ligand, optimal pore size, and the open metal sites in the trizinc SBUs in a stable high-connectivity MOF.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as an exceptionally versatile class of porous materials. MOFs are constructed from metal-ion or metal-cluster connecting points and molecular bridging ligands under relatively mild conditions. As a result, they combine the porosity with the synthetic tunability to allow the generation of a virtually unlimited number of hybrid porous materials that have been examined for a variety of applications, including gas storage,1–5 catalysis,6–10 chemical sensing,11–14 separations,15,16 and drug delivery.17,18 In particular, MOFs have been extensively studied as potential candidates for hydrogen and methane storage in the past decade owing to their extraordinarily high porosity.19

Hydrogen provides an ideal clean energy source since no environmentally detrimental molecules are emitted during energy extraction from hydrogen.20,21 As hydrogen has a very low boiling point, its storage and transportation presents a daunting challenge. Physisorption of hydrogen in porous MOFs provides an intriguing approach to hydrogen storage. Since the initial study of hydrogen storage with MOF-5,1 significant progress has been made in this area, with a great number of MOFs examined for their hydrogen uptake capacities.19 No MOF has yet met the DOE targets of reaching 0.055 kg kg−1 for system gravimetric capacity and 0.040 kg L−1 for system volumetric capacity by 2015.22 Most of the MOFs synthesized to date function best only at low temperatures due to their weak interactions (dominated by van der Waals forces) with H2.23 More efforts are needed to increase MOF hydrogen uptake capacities and to enhance MOF-hydrogen interactions before they can be considered for practical hydrogen storage applications.

As the principal component of natural gas, methane is another important small gas molecule that is a cleaner alternative to gasoline for large-scale transportation applications. A key technical hurdle for methane-based transportation also lies in the lack of safe, cheap, and convenient means for methane storage. MOFs have recently shown great potential for methane storage.24–27

Several strategies have proven successful in enhancing uptake capacities of small gaseous molecules in MOFs. First, small pore sizes, on the same order as the kinetic diameter of gas molecules, enhance gas uptake by maximizing van der Waals interactions between the adsorbate and the adsorbent. Second, framework catenation can increase gas uptake capacity by reducing the pore diameters and allowing the adsorbate to interact with multiple frameworks.28 Third, open metal sites within MOFs can lead to high gas uptake by strengthening the interactions with gas molecules.29–34 Finally, bridging ligands with aromatic backbones, such as phenylene, naphthalene and biphenylene, can increase gas uptake in MOFs by rigidifying the frameworks35,36 and enhancing the gas/framework interactions via the polarized π electrons.37

We have explored a new strategy to enhance the gas uptake capacities of MOFs by increasing their connectivity with aromatic-rich octacarboxylate ligands. The 4,8-connected MOFs of the scu topology were, for example, built from octacarboxylate bridging ligands and copper paddle-wheel secondary building units (SBUs) and showed very high hydrogen uptake capacities.38 Herein we report the design of a 6,8-connected MOF (2) based on a new aromatic-rich, octa-carboxylate bridging ligand, tetrakis{3,5-bis[(4-carboxyl)phenyl]phenyl}methane (TBCPPM, L, Scheme 1) and trizinc cluster SBUs. 2 shows an exceptionally high gas uptake capacity with an excess H2 uptake of 58 mg g−1 and 39 g L−1 at 52 bar and 77 K and of 16 mg g−1 at 80 bar and 298 K, and an excess CH4 uptake of 175 mg g−1 (167 v(STP)/v ) at 35 bar and 276 mg g−1 (264 v(STP)/v) at 80 bar and 298 K. 2 has the highest hydrogen uptake capacity at 298 K among the thousands of MOFs that have been examined to date.19 The methane uptake of 2 at 298 K and 80 bar beats the previous gravimetric record of 26.4 wt% and volumetric record of 167 g L−1 held by MOF-210 and MOF-205, respectively.26,39

Experimental section

General experimental procedures can be found in the ESI.†

Synthesis of [Cu2(L-H4)·(H2O)2]·18DMF·5H2O (1)

A mixture of L-H8 (10 mg, 0.008 mmol) and Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O (20 mg, 0.09 mmol) was dissolved in a solvent mixture of DMF/H2O (1.5 mL/0.5 mL) in a screw-capped vial. After addition of 0.5 mL of NH3 (3 M, aq.), the vial was capped and placed in an oven at 100 °C for two days. Blue crystals (9.3 mg, 40.7%) were obtained after filtration.

Synthesis of {[Zn3(μ3-OH)(H2O)]4(L)(L-H2)2}·45DMF·44H2O (2)

A mixture of L-H8 (10 mg, 0.008 mmol) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (20 mg, 0.07 mmol) was dissolved in a solvent mixture of DMF/H2O (6 mL/2 mL) in a screw-capped vial. After addition of 0.1 mL of HCl (3 M, aq.), the vial was capped and placed in an oven at 80 °C for two days. Colorless crystals (10.5 mg, 53.2%) were obtained after filtration.

Results and discussion

L-H8 was synthesized by a Pd-catalyzed Suzuki coupling between tetrakis(3,5-dibromophenyl)methane40 and 4-(methoxycarbonyl)phenylboronic acid followed by base-catalyzed hydrolysis. L-H8 was characterized by 1H NMR spectroscopy and electrospray ionization-mass spectroscopy (ESI†). A solvothermal reaction between L-H8 and Cu(NO3)2·2.5H2O in a dimethylformamide (DMF)–H2O mixture under basic conditions afforded blue crystals of [Cu2(L-H4)·(H2O)2]·18DMF·5H2O (1). A similar reaction between L-H8 and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O in a DMF–H2O mixture under acidic conditions afforded colorless crystals of {[Zn3(μ3-OH)(H2O)]4(L)(L-H2)2}·45DMF·44H2O (2) (Scheme 1). These formulae were established based on single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies, 1H NMR integration, and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) results. TGA of 1 showed a solvent weight loss of 50.4% in the 20–200 °C temperature range (expected 50.6% for 18 DMF and 5 H2O molecules). Similarly, TGA of 2 gave a solvent weight loss of 45.2% in the 20–200 °C temperature range (expected 47.5% for 45 DMF and 44 H2O molecules). Phase purity of as-synthesized 1 and 2 was confirmed by their powder X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) (Fig. 3e and f).

1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Pban41 as a 2D 4-connected uninodal network of the sql topology (ESI†).42 Each asymmetric unit contains one half of the tetra-protonated ligand L-H4, one copper atom, and one water molecule coordinating to the axial position of the copper atom. Lying on the two fold axis (0, y, 0), the central carbon atom of the L-H4 ligand has a site occupancy of 0.5. Only half of the eight carboxylate groups are deprotonated and linked through [Cu2(O2CR)4] SBUs to form 2D layers that pack along the a axis (Fig. 1b). Each unit cell includes four L-H4 ligands, four [Cu2(O2CR)4] SBUs, and eight coordination water molecules at the axial positions, leading to a framework formula of [Cu2(L-H4)(H2O)2]. The space filling model of 1 shows the largest pore size of 14.3 × 9.4 Å in the [100] plane (ESI†). In agreement with the TGA result, PLATON calculations43 of 1 indicated a void volume of 9710.3 Å3 (63.3 % of the unit cell volume of 15343.3 Å3).

Single crystal X-ray structure determination of 2 revealed a 3D framework which crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I2d.41 The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one half of the L-H2 ligand, one quarter of the L ligand, three zinc atoms, one bridging hydroxide, and one terminally coordinated water molecule. The chemically distinct L and L-H2 ligands are represented with blue and plum colors in Fig. 2b. The central carbon atom of the L ligand lies on the special position (0, 0, 0.5) with the site symmetry of 2 and an occupancy of 0.25, while the central carbon atom of the L-H2 ligand sits on the 2-fold axis (1/4, y, 7/8) with a 0.5 occupancy. The L and L-H2 ligands link the [Zn3(μ3-OH)] SBUs to form a 3D, 6,8-connected, 2-nodal net of the tph topology, with the Schläfli symbol {412·63}4{418·610}3. To our knowledge, the 6,8-connected 2-nodal nets are still very rare for MOF materials.44,45 Furthermore, only the sea topology has been reported for these MOFs46 among the four (6,8)-connected 3D network topologies listed in the RCSR database (ocu, sea, seh, and tph).472 thus represents the first example of MOFs with the tph network topology. The most distinguishing structural feature of 2 is the formation of a distorted triangular bipyramid cage by the octacarboxylate ligands in the axial positions and three [Zn3(μ3-OH] SBUs on the triangular plane (Fig. 2c). These cages are interconnected through the largest open channels of 6.8 × 2.9 Å running along the (001) direction (Fig. 2d). The approximate diameter of the inner sphere of this triangular bipyramid cage is determined to be ∼6 Å by fitting a sphere from the centroid of the cage to the van der Waals surface of its walls, which is consistent with the pore size distribution data calculated from the N2 adsorption isotherms (ESI†). PLATON calculations of 2 indicated a void volume of 30423.9 Å3 (66.1% of the unit cell volume of 45968.1 Å3), which is consistent with the TGA result (ESI†).

To characterize the permanent porosity of 1 and 2, nitrogen and hydrogen adsorption experiments were performed at 77 K (Fig. 3a and b). Both samples were pre-treated via regular vacuum drying or by the previously established freeze-drying method.48 Since only micropores are present in 1 and 2 (ESI†), a typical type I adsorption behavior was observed for both samples. The vacuum-dried sample of 1 exhibited a Langmuir surface area (SAL) of 288 m2 g−1 (SABET = 266 m2 g−1, ESI†) whereas the freeze-dried sample of 1 gave a slightly higher SAL of 362.9 m2 g−1 (SABET = 313.4 m2 g−1). At 1 atm and 77 K, a relatively low hydrogen uptake capacity was observed for 1 at 0.6 mg g−1 and 0.8 mg g−1 for a vacuum-dried and a freeze-dried sample, respectively (ESI† and Fig. 3b). Interestingly, the freeze-dried sample of 2 gave an SAL of 3120 m2 g−1 (SABET = 2718 m2 g−1), which is significantly higher than the vacuum-dried sample which showed a negligible SAL of 0.7 m2 g−1 (Fig. 3a and ESI†). Powder X-ray diffraction patterns confirmed the severe distortion of 2 after regular vacuum drying, which is responsible for the small observed surface area. On the other hand, the crystallinity of 2 was well maintained after freeze drying (Fig. 3f). The total pore volume of 1.14 cm3 g−1 calculated from the nitrogen adsorption isotherm for the freeze-dried sample of 2 is in good agreement with the value of 1.46 cm3 g−1 calculated from the crystal structure data. Hydrogen uptake studies at 1 bar and 77 K gave an exceptionally high gravimetric uptake capacity of >28 mg g−1 for the freeze-dried sample of 2 (Fig. 3b). To the best of our knowledge, the highest gravimetric hydrogen uptake in MOFs was reported to be about 31 mg g−1 under similar conditions.49,50 To gain insight into the interaction between H2 molecules and the framework of 2, the isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) were calculated by fitting the hydrogen adsorption isotherms at 77 K and 78 K to a Virial-type equation.31 The isosteric heat at zero coverage was estimated to be 6.8 kJ mol−1 (ESI†). After the expected trend of decreased Qst during continuous gas exposure, an increase in Qst was observed at higher pressure. This phenomenon has been reported before for MOF materials in taking up gas molecules (e.g. H2, CO2, and CH4),25,51,52 where it was attributed to the cooperative effect: at high-concentration of gas molecules, there exist strong adsorbate–adsorbate interactions in addition to the adsorbate and adsorbent interactions.

To further evaluate the gas uptake capacity of 2, high-pressure gravimetric (excess) hydrogen and methane adsorption experiments were carried out on the freeze-dried sample of 2 under a pressure of up to 80 bar. As shown in Fig. 3c, the excess H2 uptake of 2 reaches 58 mg g−1 and 39 mg L−1 at 77 K and 52 bar, corresponding to a total gravimetric uptake of 91 mg g−1. An uptake of 16 mg g−1 (a total uptake of 23 mg g−1) was obtained at 80 bar and 298 K, which is the highest ever observed for MOF materials at room temperature.19 Even more impressively, 2 can adsorb up to 276 mg g−1 of CH4 at room temperature and 80 bar without showing any saturation (Fig. 3d). With the data for the pore volume and the density of methane, the total gravimetric uptake was calculated to be 344 mg g−1, corresponding to a volumetric (v/v) uptake of 329 v(STP)/v. 2 has an excess CH4 uptake of 175 mg g−1 (167 v(STP)/v ) at room temperature and 35 bar. Both high pressure hydrogen and methane adsorption isotherms are reproducible on the same sample, indicating the stability of 2 under high pressures. The pore size of 2 (around 6 Å) is ideal for methane uptake (ESI†), which is consistent with a previous computational study that indicated an ideal pore size of 4–8 Å for methane storage.27 These values placed 2 among the leading MOF materials for fuel gas storage applications (Table 1). 2 has the highest hydrogen uptake at 298 K among thousands of MOFs that have been examined to date.192 has the highest gravimetric and volumetric methane uptakes at 298 K and 80 bar among all the MOFs.26,39

To investigate the methane adsorption mechanism, we performed computational studies by a combination of classical grand canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations and first-principles calculations based on density-functional theory (DFT).37,53 GCMC simulations using the standard universal force field (UFF) were performed at 298 K and at various pressures: 0.1 bar, 1 bar, and 10 bar (ESI†). The probability distribution of adsorbed CH4 was generated from the simulation, and the result obtained at 1 bar is shown in Fig. 4a as an example. As viewed down the c axis, the most heavily populated sites are around the trizinc cluster vertices (SBU sites) and the central carbon vertices (linker sites) in the distorted triangular bipyramid cage, as shown with green arrow in Fig. 4a. As further revealed by the preferential locations derived from GCMC simulations shown in Fig. 4b, the shortest distance between the carbon atom of CH4 and the carbon atom of the phenyl ring at the linker sites is 3.65 Å, which is only slightly larger than the sum of the van der Waals radii of carbon (1.70 Å) atoms, suggesting strong van der Waals interactions and overlapping potentials between the CH4 molecules and the framework. The accessible open metal sites offered by the [Zn3(μ3-OH)] SBUs provide strong electrostatic interactions between CH4 molecules and the SBU sites, which was confirmed by DFT calculations using Vanderbilt-type ultrasoft pseudopotentials and the local-density approximation (LDA) with semi-empirical dispersion correction (ESI†). The binding energies at the linker sites and the SBU sites were calculated to be 24.4 kJ mol−1 and 26.8 kJ mol−1, respectively. At higher pressures, additional adsorption sites can be found (ESI†), but they are much weaker. These calculations indicate that optimal cage dimension, open metal sites, aromatic-rich nature of the L ligand are responsible for the exceptional hydrogen and methane uptake capacities in this high-connectivity MOF.

Conclusions

We have prepared a high-connectivity MOF (Zn-TBCPPM, 2) with exceptional gas uptake capacities based on a new tetraphenylmethane-derived octa-carboxylate bridging ligand and trizinc cluster secondary building units. 2 is the first 6,8-connected 3D MOF of the tph topology. Although 2 exhibits relatively modest porosity compared to other highly porous MOFs, it has the highest hydrogen uptake at 298 K among all of the MOFs that have been examined to date. The methane uptake of 2 at 298 K and 80 bar is the highest both in gravimetric and volumetric senses. We attribute the exceptionally high gas uptake capacity of 2 to the highly branched, aromatic-rich nature of the bridging ligand, optimal pore size, and the open metal sites in the trizinc SBUs.
