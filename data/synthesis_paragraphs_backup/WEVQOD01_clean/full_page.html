Abstract

A series of heterometallic 3d–Gd3+ complexes based on a lanthanide metalloligand, [M(H2O)6][Gd(oda)3]⋅3 H2O [M=Cr3+ (1‐Cr)] (H2oda=2,2′‐oxydiacetic acid), [M(H2O)6][MGd(oda)3]2⋅3 H2O [M=Mn2+ (2‐Mn), Fe2+ (2‐Fe) and Co2+ (2‐Co)], and [M3Gd2(oda)6(H2O)6]⋅12 H2O [M=Ni2+ (3‐Ni), Cu2+ (3‐Cu), and Zn2+ (3‐Zn)], are reported. Magnetic and heat‐capacity studies revealed a significant impact on the magnetocaloric effect depending on the anisotropy of the 3d transition metal ions, as confirmed by comparison of the observed maximum values of −ΔSm between complexes 2‐Co and 1‐Cr. In these two complexes, the 3d metal ions have the same spin (S=3/2 for Co2+ and Cr3+ ions), and the theoretical calculation suggested a larger −ΔSm value for 2‐Co (47.8 J K−1 kg−1) than 1‐Cr (37.5 J K−1 kg−1); however, the significant anisotropy of Co2+ ions in 2‐Co, which can result in smaller effective spins, gives a smaller value of −ΔSm for 2‐Co (32.2 J K−1 kg−1) than for 1‐Cr (35.4 J K−1 kg−1) at ΔH=9 T.

Introduction

The magnetocaloric effect (MCE) is defined as the isothermal magnetic entropy change ΔSm and the adiabatic temperature change ΔTad of magnetic materials induced by a change in external magnetic field.1 In the last two decades, magnetic coolants made of molecule‐based paramagnetic complexes have attracted intense interest of researchers due to their potential cryogenic applications in the ultralow temperature range, such as replacing the rare and expensive 3He.2 Since the first report on the MCE in {Mn12},3a many MC molecular clusters and coordination polymers were discovered.3, 4

Among molecular refrigerants, 3d–4f mixed‐metal complexes, which combine the weak exchange of 4f ions and the high spin density of 3d ions, have proved to be an effective strategy for obtaining magnetic coolants with promising MCE. In such systems, the 4f Gd3+ ion is usually selected as a good candidate due to its high‐spin state (S=7/2) and isotropy. Moreover, due to the very efficient shielding of the unpaired electrons in 4f orbitals of Gd3+ by the fully occupied 5s and 5p orbitals, the magnetic exchange interaction can be made very weak by separating the 3d ions with Gd3+ ions to give multiple low‐lying excited states, which is necessary for improving the MCE performance. On the other hand, the reported 3d–4f magnetic coolants include various 3d transition metal ions, such as Mn2+,5 Co2+,6 Ni2+,7 and Cu2+.8 However, the effects on the MCE of 3d ions with different magnetic anisotropy and spin ground state are also non‐negligible and need systematic investigation.

Recently, we reported the magnetocaloric effect of 3D heterometallic Mn2+–Gd3+ framework [Mn(H2O)6][MnGd(oda)3]2⋅6 H2O (2‐Mn; oda=2,2′‐oxydiacetate),5a based on the combination of lanthanide metalloligand [Gd(oda)3]3− with Mn2+ ions. In the structure, the alternate arrangement of Mn2+ and Gd3+ ions successfully avoids the relatively strong MnII⋅⋅⋅MnII magnetic coupling and thus enhances the MCE. According to other reports,9 a series of heterometallic frameworks can be obtained through bridging [Ln(oda)3]3− units with different 3d transition metal ions, and this provides a good opportunity to investigate the influence on the MCE of different 3d metal ions in structures with weak magnetic coupling based on the same metalloligand [Gd(oda)3]3−.

Herein, a series of 3d–Gd3+ heterometallic complexes, [M(H2O)6][Gd(oda)3]⋅3 H2O [M=Cr3+ (1‐Cr)], [M(H2O)6][MGd(oda)3]2⋅3 H2O [M=Fe2+ (2‐Fe) and Co2+ (2‐Co)], and [M3Gd2(oda)6(H2O)6]⋅12 H2O [M=Ni2+ (3‐Ni), Cu2+ (3‐Cu), and Zn2+ (3‐Zn)], was synthesized. In 1‐Cr, which is one of the rare Cr3+–Gd3+‐based molecular magnetic coolants, the existence of very weak ferromagnetism, low ligand‐to‐metal ratio, and isotropic metal ions (Cr3+ and Gd3+) result in a considerable magnetic entropy change (−ΔSm=35.4 J K−1 kg−1, ΔH=9 T at 1.8 K), which is comparable to the highest magnetic entropy change of Cr3+–Gd3+ molecular magnetic coolants (Supporting Information, Table S2).10 Additionally, although a theoretical calculation suggested increasing maximum entropy change −ΔSm for each complex with increasing spin of the 3d metal ions, comprehensive MCE measurements confirmed that zero‐field splitting (ZFS) of 3d metal ions such as Fe2+, Co2+, and Ni2+ has a significantly impact on the MCE and leads to −ΔSm values that are clearly smaller than expected.

Results and Discussion

Structural characterization

The reaction of Cr3+ ions, Gd3+ ions, and H2oda resulted in ion‐pair complex [Cr(H2O)6][Gd (oda)3]⋅3 H2O (1‐Cr). Single‐crystal X‐ray analysis revealed that the complex crystallized in the trigonal space group R (Supporting Information, Table S1) and each asymmetric unit contains one oda2− ligand, three H2O molecules, a third of a Gd3+ ion, and two crystallographically independent Cr3+ ions with 1/6 occupation. In the structure of 2‐Cr (Figure 1), the Gd1 center is chelated by three oda2− ligands, giving a [Gd(oda)3]3− unit. Both Cr1 and Cr2 ions are coordinated by six water molecules in the form of [Cr(H2O)6]3+. As shown in Figure 1, the hexahydrated Cr1 ion is hydrogen‐bonded with the [Gd(oda)3]3− unit through a water trimer, while the hexahydrated Cr2 ion is related to the same [Gd(oda)3]3− unit through the hydrogen bonds between the coordinated water molecules (O2W) and the carboxylate oxygen atoms (O2) of oda2− ligands. The discrete [Gd(oda)3]3− and [Cr(H2O)6]3+ units are arranged in an ABCABC…︁ packing model with formation of a 1D supramolecular chain along the c axis of the unit cell (Figure 2).

Although crystals of 2‐Fe/Co were synthesized (see Experimental Section), single‐crystal X‐ray structural analyses of both complexes were unsuccessful due to their poor single‐crystallinity. However, their powder X‐ray diffraction patterns fitted well with that of the reported 2‐Mn5a and thus confirmed their isostructural nature. The elemental analysis and thermogravimetric measurements (Supporting Information, Figure S3) further indicated the same chemical formula [M(H2O)6][MGd(oda)3]2⋅3 H2O of the three complexes. Herein, we discuss the structural characteristics of 2‐Mn as a representative. In the structure of 2‐Mn, the host framework is constructed by two building blocks, namely, [Gd(oda)3]3− and Mn2+ ions. Each Gd1 center (Figure 3 a, left) is coordinated by nine O atoms of three tridentate oda2− ligands, forming a distorted tricapped trigonal prism. The octahedral coordination environment of Mn1 ions (Figure 3 a right) is formed by six oxygen atoms from the carboxyl groups of six adjacent oda2− ligands. Furthermore, the [Gd(oda)3]3− unit acts as a six‐connective node that bonds with six Mn1 atoms through the carboxyl groups of oda2− ligands in an anti–anti mode, and each Mn1 center is also connected to six Gd3+ ions, resulting in a 3D anionic cubic network (Figure 3 b) with a typical pcu topology (Figure 3 c). Because of the alternative carbon atoms of the oda2− ligands, there are two types of cavities in the structure. The larger cavities are occupied by [Mn(H2O)6]2+ counterions to neutralize the charge of the host framework, and the smaller ones are filled with solvent water molecules, which are hydrogen‐bonded to the carboxylate oxygen atoms of oda2−. A PLATON11 calculation indicated 9.7 % void volume.

When the 3d metal ions were changed to Ni2+, Cu2+, or Zn2+, the resulting crystals of 3‐Ni/Cu/Zn crystallized in higher‐symmetry space group P6/mcc. As confirmed by crystallographic studies, the structures of the three complexes were framework isomers of 2‐Mn. For the sake of simplicity, only the structure of 3‐Ni was analyzed. Compared to the structure of 3‐Ni, although the coordination environment of the Gd3+ ion in 2‐Mn (Figure 5 a left) also consists of three chelating oda2− ligands with tricapped trigonal‐prismatic geometry, the coordination environment of the 3d metal center is different. In the structure of 3‐Ni, the Ni1 center (Figure 5 b right) is coordinated by four O atoms (Ni1O2 2.027 Å) from the carboxyl groups of oda2− ligands and two O atoms (Ni1O1W 2.073 Å) from water molecules in equatorial and axial positions. Thus, each Ni2+ ion is connected to four Gd3+ ions, and each [Gd(oda)3]3− metalloligand connects six Ni2+ ions through the carboxyl groups of oda2− ligands in a syn–anti model to give a honeycomb‐like framework (Figure 5 b and c), which differs greatly from that of 2‐Mn. The 9.0×9.0 Å hexagonal channels in the structure of 3‐Ni (Supporting Information, Figure S3) are larger than those of 2‐Mn and filled with water molecules. The potential free volume is about 861.2 Å3 per unit cell, which corresponds to 30.5 % void volume, as calculated with the PLATON program.11

Magnetic properties

The introduction of various 3d transition metals with different magnetic properties such as ground state spin S and anisotropy into the 3d–Gd systems based on oda2− ligands gave us the chance to investigate their effect on the MCE. Although the MCE of 2‐Mn was reported in our previous work,5a herein we further report the −ΔSm value calculated by temperature‐dependent measurements of heat capacity C in applied magnetic fields up to 90 kOe.

Variable‐temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements of all complexes were performed in an applied dc magnetic field of 1 kOe (Figure 6). The observed and calculated χMT values at room temperature, together with the relevant parameters, are summarized in Table 1. For 2‐Fe/Co, the observed χMT values at 300 K are larger than the calculated spin‐only values, which indicates significant orbital contributions of the distorted octahedral high‐spin Fe2+ and Co2+ ions. For the other complexes, the observed χMT values at 300 K are close to the corresponding calculated spin‐only values.

Although the χMT value of each complex stays essentially constant in the high‐temperature region, on cooling, the changes of the χMT values for the complexes were observed to be different. A Curie–Weiss fitting (Supporting Information, Figure S4) indicated the existence of weak ferromagnetic interaction in 2‐Fe/Co but weak antiferromagnetic interaction in 3‐Ni/Cu, and such different behaviors may arise from the distinct bridging modes of the carboxylate groups of oda2− ligands between 3d and 4f ions, syn–anti for 3‐Ni/Cu and anti–anti for 2‐Fe/Co, which suggest an important magnetostructural relationship. For 1‐Cr and 3‐Zn, the magnetic interaction is even weaker due to the existence of diamagnetic Zn2+ ions between Gd3+ ions and hydrogen bonds between Cr3+ and Gd3+ ions, so that the main route of magnetic interaction between the metal ions (through the carboxyl groups) is effectively hindered. The resulting θ values of 0.008 and −0.05 K indicate weak ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interaction in 1‐Cr and 3‐Zn, respectively.

Magnetization measurement were also performed at 2.0–10 K in the range of 0.25‐90 kOe for each complex (Supporting Information, Figure S5). The magnetization increases steadily with increasing external field and reaches saturation values of 9.2, 19.0, 16.3, and 13.2 Nβ for 1‐Cr, 3‐Ni, 3‐Cu, and 3‐Zn, respectively, at 2.0 K and 90 kOe, which are in good agreement with the theoretical values. The M values for 2‐Fe/Co reach 21.3 and 19.9 Nβ, respectively, which are lower than their theoretical values (26 Nβ for Fe‐Gd and 23 Nβ for Co‐Gd) and suggest significant anisotropy in the systems.

The magnetic‐field‐dependent magnetic entropy change −ΔSm of each complex at various temperatures is shown in Figure 7. The −ΔSm values of all complexes gradually increase with decreasing temperature and increasing applied field. For 1‐Cr, the −ΔSm value reaches a maximum value of 35.4 J K−1 kg−1 with ΔH=90 kOe at 2.0 K, which is close to the theoretical limiting value of 37.5 J K−1 kg−1 calculated from R ln(2SCr+1)+R ln(2SGd+1) with SCr=3/2 and SGd=7/2. Since the peak of −ΔSm was not observed even at the lowest temperature (2 K), the −ΔSm value should be even larger at lower temperature. In fact, due to the hydrogen‐bonding interaction between CrIII and GdIII ions, the magnetic coupling between magnetic centers in 1‐Cr is much weaker than that of covalent bridging between d‐block ions and GdIII ions in other complexes, and thus the observed maximum of −ΔSm for 1‐Cr is closer to its paramagnetic limit.3g For 3‐Zn, the theoretical −ΔSm value (Figure 7 f) is 21.3 J K−1 kg−1, calculated as 3R ln(2SGd+1) for just three uncoupled GdIII ions due to the absence of magnetic contribution from the diamagnetic ZnII ions. The corresponding experimental −ΔSm value is 20.5 J K−1 kg−1. Figure 7 e shows the magnetic field dependent magnetic entropy change of 3‐Cu, for which the maximum value of −ΔSm is 27.8 J K−1 kg−1.

The expected maximum magnetic entropy changes of 2‐Fe, 2‐Co, and 3‐Ni are 52.0, 47.8, and 44.5 J K−1 kg−1 respectively, calculated as 3R ln(2SM+1)+2R ln(2SGd+1) for three Fe2+/Co2+/Ni2+ and two Gd3+ ions without coupling. However, as depicted in Figure 7 b–d, the observed maximal magnetic entropy changes for the three complexes for a field change of ΔH=90 kOe are much smaller than the theoretical values (−ΔSm,max=26.8 J K−1 kg−1 for 2‐Fe, 32.2 J K−1 kg−1 for 2‐Co, and 31.6 J K−1 kg−1 for 3‐Ni; Table 1). These results are attributed to the a certain ZFS effect in Fe2+, Co2+ and Ni2+ ions, giving smaller effective spins.

Comparing the changes in ΔSm in Table 1 clearly reveals that high spin and isotropy are necessary for obtaining a high MCE. Among the 3d metal ions, the isotropic Mn2+ ions with the highest spin (S=5/2) simultaneously satisfy the aforementioned conditions and lead to the highest value of −ΔSm for the reported 2‐Mn. Additionally, the complexes reported herein also exhibited a considerable MCE when compared to some reported 3d–4f magnetic coolants with the same 3d ions (Supporting Information, Table S2), which confirms that alternating separation of 3d ions by GdIII ions is an effective strategy to weaken the magnetic coupling in the system and thus give a high MCE.

Heat capacity

Low‐temperature heat capacity measurements were performed for 1‐Cr, 2‐Mn, and 2‐Co, which contain isotropic Cr3+, Mn2+ ions and anisotropic Co2+ ions, respectively, at applied fields up to 9 T. As shown in Figure 8 a, c, and e, the higher‐temperature regions for the three complexes are dominated by lattice contributions arising from the thermal vibration, which fit well to the Debye model and yield Debye temperatures ΘD of 315, 275, and 337 K for 1‐Cr, 2‐Mn, and 2‐Co respectively.

At lower temperatures, the heat capacities for the three complexes are dominated by the field‐sensitive Schottky‐type magnetic contributions. The observed increase of C on cooling is attributed to a calculated Schottky anomaly, but no obvious λ peaks are found in the measured temperature range at zero field, which indicates the absence of magnetic ordering for both complexes above 2 K.

From the experimental C values, the temperature‐dependent entropies S for the three complexes were obtained by numerical integration by using S(T)=∫C(T)/TdT. The lack of experimental C at absolute zero can lead to a large error in the extrapolation. Hence, we adopted a different approach to calculate the relative Stotal(T0,H) by integrating the total heat capacity from a high temperature instead of from 0 K.4 Thus, the isothermal magnetic entropy change −ΔSm (Figure 8 b, d, and f) for each complex can be extracted from the temperature and field dependencies of the relative Stotal(T0,H) data by vertical subtraction.

The ΔSm value of each complex shows good agreement from both independent evaluations, that is, heat capacity and magnetization respectively. The temperature‐dependent −ΔSm values of the three complexes keep rising with increasing applied field. For 2‐Mn, the resulting −ΔSm value obtained by the aforementioned method coincide well with the reported data (Supporting Information, Figure S6), which further confirms the accuracy of such an “inverse‐integration” method for calculating the magnetic entropy change.4 The −ΔSm value of 2‐Mn (Figure 4 c, d) reaches a maximum value of 49.26 J K−1 kg−1 for ΔH=9 T at about 2.42 K. Indeed, the maximum of the temperature‐dependent −ΔSm in an applied field of 9 T is a little larger and shifted to higher temperature (2.42 K) than the reported value at ΔH=3 T (ΔSm,max=42.6 J K−1 kg−1 at 1.56 K), and this is further confirmation of the energy efficiency of 2‐Mn at relatively moderate field. The magnetic entropy change −ΔSm (Figure 8 b) of 1‐Cr increases with decreasing temperature and reaches a value of 34.5 J K−1 kg−1 for ΔH=9 T at 2 K, which is a little smaller than the value of 35.5 J K−1 kg−1 obtained from magnetization data at 1.8 K. For 1‐Co, the magnetic entropy change −ΔSm (Figure 8 f) increases with decreasing temperature and reaches a value of 32.5 J K−1 kg−1 for ΔH=9 T at the 2.7 K.

Conclusion

By assembly of [Gd(oda)3]3− units with different 3d transition metal ions, we synthesized a series of heterometallic 3d–Gd complexes, which gave us a good opportunity to compare the impact of the anisotropy of 3d transition metal ions on the MCE. The impact of the anisotropy on the MCE is revealed on comparing the magnetic entropy changes of 1‐Cr and 2‐Co, in which the 3d transition metal ions have the same spin (S=3/2) but different anisotropies (isotropic Cr3+ and anisotropic Co2+) due to the different orbital contributions. For 1‐Cr, the obtained maximum value of −ΔSm (35.4 J K−1 kg−1) reaches about 94.4 % of the theoretical −ΔSm value (37.5 J K−1 kg−1), whereas only 67.3 % of the theoretical −ΔSm value (47.8 J K−1 kg−1) is achieved for 2‐Co (32.2 J K−1 kg−1). According to the magnetic entropy changes of complexes obtained independently from magnetization and heat‐capacity data, besides high spin, the use of isotropic 3d metal ions such as Cr3+, Fe3+, and Mn2+ plays an important role in obtaining heterometallic magnetic coolants with large MCE.

Experimental Section

Synthesis

All starting materials were obtained from commercial sources and used as received without further purification.

[Cr(H2O)6][Gd(oda)3]⋅3 H2O (1‐Cr): A mixture of H2oda (0.160 g, 1.2 mmol), Gd(NO3)3⋅5 H2O (0.173 g, 0.4 mmol), and Cr(NO3)⋅4 H2O(0.160 g, 0.4 mmol) was dissolved in 5 mL of deionized water and the mixture stirred for 5 min at about 50 °C. Then, a solution of NaOH (0.080 g, 2.0 mmol) in H2O (5 mL) was added to the above solution, and the mixture was stirred for a further 5 min and filtered. The resulting solution was mixed with ethanol (5–8 mL) and left to stand at room temperature. Dark blue crystals of 2‐Cr were obtained after 1–2 d. Yield: 73 % based on Gd. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C12H30O24CrGd: C 17.77, H 3.73; found: C 17.78, H 3.94. IR (KBr disk, cm−1): 3438(m), 3369(m), 2899(s), 2470(m), 2183(w), 1628(s), 1579(s), 1470(m), 1425(s), 1398(s), 1360(m), 1323(s), 1267(m), 1250(w), 1132(s), 1063(s), 964(m), 939(m), 833(m), 715(m), 688(m), 560(m), 573(m), 555(m), 478(m).

[Fe(H2O)6][FeGd(oda)3]2⋅3 H2O (2‐Fe): The synthesis of 2‐Fe was performed under a nitrogen atmosphere. A mixture of H2oda (0.080 g, 0.6 mmol) and NaOH (0.032 g, 0.6 mmol) was dissolved in 10 mL of deionized water and the mixture stirred for 20 min. The resulting solution was transferred to a Schlenk flask containing Gd(NO3)3⋅5 H2O (0.087 g, 0.2 mmol) and Fe(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O (0.11 g, 0.3 mmol). After stirring for 5 min, the solution was mixed with ethanol (5 mL) and allowed to stand at room temperature. Light green crystals were obtained after 2–3 d. Yield: 50 % based on Gd. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C24H42O39Fe3Gd2: C 20.07, H 2.95; found: C 20.11, H 2.89. IR (KBr disc, cm−1): 3487(m), 1606(s), 1464(w), 1439(m), 1360(w), 1309(m), 1124(m), 1053(w), 943(w), 598(w), 565(w).

[Co(H2O)6][CoGd(oda)3]2⋅3 H2O (2‐Co): A mixture of Co(CH3COO)2⋅6 H2O (0.18 g, 0.75 mmol), H2oda (0.012 g, 0.75 mmol), and Gd2O3 (0.09 g, 0.25 mmol) was added to 15 mL of deionized water and the mixture stirred for 10 min. The resulting solution was heated in a 25 mL stainless steel reactor with Teflon liner at 180 °C for 3 d. General characterization

Elemental analyses were performed with an Elementar Vario EL elemental analyzer. Powder X‐ray diffraction measurements were performed with a Bruker D8 X‐Ray diffractometer. The FTIR spectra were measured with a Thermo Nicolet Avatar 330 FTIR spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analysis was performed with a NETZSCH TG 209F1 Iris Analyzer.

Magnetic measurements

The magnetic measurements were performed with a Quantum Design PPMS with VSM option on polycrystalline samples with an empirical diamagnetic correction. The specific heat was studied with a Quantum Design PPMS by adopting a standard relaxation technique.
