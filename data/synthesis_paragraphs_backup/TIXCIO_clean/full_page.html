Zinc(II) and cadmium(II) metal–organic frameworks with 4-imidazole containing tripodal ligand: sorption and anion exchange properties†

The use of a 4-imidazole containing tripodal ligand 1,3,5-tri(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (H3L) enabled isolation of two three-dimensional (3D) porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) [Zn(HL)]·solvent (1) and [Cd(H3L)2](ClO4)2·10.5H2O (2) featuring 4,4- and 3,6-connected nets related to the structures of ecl and pyrite, respectively. Framework 1 has 3D channels based on Zn–Im− (Im− = imidazolate) moieties due to partial deprotonation of the 1H-imidazol-4-yl groups of H3L while porous framework 2 is constructed by the coordination of neutral H3L ligands with Cd(II). Desolvated solid 1′ showed remarkable uptake for CO2, N2, H2, CH4 gases as well as the volatile organic vapor of MeOH, EtOH, i-PrOH and C6H6, significantly, exhibiting temperature dependent selective gas sorption for CO2 over N2. Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations were performed to elucidate the mechanism of the selective gas adsorption. Cage-like motifs are found in pyrite-net 2, in which ClO4− anions are hosted, and the results of IR, elemental analysis and PXRD measurements confirm that 2 has a reversible anion exchange property among perchlorate, nitrate and nitrite anions.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with well-defined channels or inner cavities have attracted great attention from chemists owing to their specific functionalities, such as ion exchange, ionic/molecular recognition, gas storage/separation, selective guest inclusion and catalysis for specific reactions.1 The design of microporous MOFs as functional materials has made great advancements in the past few years by combining synthetic methodologies with crystal engineering in MOFs synthesis.2 The judicious choice of organic building blocks is the key point in the construction of functional MOFs. Simple imidazole and functionalized imidazolate ligands can act as effective organic building blocks to construct zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIFs), which have emerged as a novel type of porous zeolite-like crystalline material based on the similarity of the structural units between metal(II)–Im− (Im− = imidazolate) and tetrahedral Si(Al)O4 moieties.3 The specific structural characteristics of ZIFs endow them with notable gas storage and separation properties, particularly for high selective capture or separating CO2, compared with other porous materials due to the combined effects of the size of the pore apertures and the strong quadrupolar interactions of CO2 with the nitrogen atoms on the pore surface as well as the higher critical point of CO2 than of other gases.4 Because of their zeolite-like properties, such as permanent porosity, a uniform pore size, and high thermal and chemical stability, ZIFs have been employed as a new type of crystalline porous material for the fabrication of molecular sieve membranes in order to achieve application in gas separation.5

Taking the favorable constructional properties of imidazole-containing ligands into consideration, we have concentrated our efforts on the design and synthesis of 1-imidazol-1-yl-containing ligands by inducing simple imidazolyl groups into an arene core, and successfully constructed novel MOFs including individual cages, one-dimensional (1D) tubes, two-dimensional (2D) networks, and three-dimensional (3D) non-interpenetrating and interpenetrating frameworks with molecular recognition and anion exchange properties.6 Based on our previous work and other reported studies, we designed a new 1H-imidazole-4-yl-containing ligand, 1,4-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (H2L), which is an effective building unit as a neutral ligand,7 and more importantly, its outstanding characteristic is that it is likely to act as an imidazolate anion to build imidazolate frameworks by the deprotonation of the 4-imidazolyl groups.8 For example, the complex [Cu(L)], constructed from Cu(II)–Im−, possesses the same characteristics of porosity, stability and favorable gas storage/separation as ZIFs.8a In order to further investigate the coordination chemistry of the 1H-imidazole-4-yl-containing ligand with transition metal salts, we have constructed two novel porous isomeric frameworks with Co(II)–Im− moieties, which show distinct gas adsorption properties based on a new tripodal ligand 1,3,5-tri(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (H3L).8b As an extension of previous work, two MOFs, namely [Zn(HL)]·solvent (1) and [Cd(H3L)2](ClO4)2·10.5H2O (2), were obtained by assembly reactions between H3L and the corresponding metal salts under hydrothermal conditions. Herein, we report the synthesis, crystal structures, gas sorption, and anion exchange properties of the complexes. Furthermore, Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations were performed to gain insight into the different adsorption behavior of the frameworks.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All the chemicals and solvents were of reagent grade and were used as received without further purification. Ligand 1,3,5-tri(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (H3L) was prepared according to the previously reported method.9 Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C Elemental Analyzer at the analysis center of Nanjing University. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a simultaneous SDT 2960 thermal analyzer under nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. FT-IR spectra were recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Bruker Vector22 FT-IR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were measured on a Shimadzu XRD-6000 X-ray diffractometer with Cu Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation at room temperature. CO2, N2, MeOH, EtOH, i-PrOH and C6H6 sorption experiments were carried out on a Belsorp-max volumetric gas sorption instrument, and CH4 and H2 measurements on an Autosorb-1MP, Quantachrome. The samples were activated using the “outgas” function of the surface area analyzer for 24 h at 180 °C for 1.

X-ray crystallography

The crystallographic data collection for 1 was carried out on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD area-detector diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293(2) K using the ω-scan technique. The diffraction data were integrated using the SAINT program,10 which was also used for the intensity corrections for the Lorentz and polarization effects. A semi-empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.11 The structure was solved by direct methods and all the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package.12 The hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically except for the one attached to the N atoms, which was found at reasonable position in the differential Fourier map and located there. The free solvent molecules in 1 are highly disordered and cannot be modeled, thus the PLATON SQUEEZE routine13 was applied to remove the contributions to the scattering from the solvent molecules. The crystallographic data for 2 were collected on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID imaging plate diffractometer at 200 K, with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71075 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods with SIR92 and expanded using Fourier techniques.14,15 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically by the full matrix least-squares method on F2. All the hydrogen atoms of 2 in the structure were generated geometrically. All the calculations were carried out using the CrystalStructure software.16 In 2, the Cl1 and O14 atoms of the perchlorate anion are disordered into two positions with site occupancy factors of 0.250(7) and 0.084(7), respectively. Results and discussion

Structure description of [Zn(HL)]·solvent (1)

Complex 1 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group I41/a and has the same framework structure as the previously reported Co(II) analogue of [Co(HL)]·2H2O (3).8b Each Zn(II) atom is ligated by four nitrogen atoms to form a four-coordinated tetrahedral coordination geometry while the H3L ligand is partly deprotonated to give an imidazolate dianion (HL)2− to connect four Zn(II) atoms via the Zn–N coordination interaction as well as to balance the charge of the metal ion, as shown in Fig. S1 (ESI†). The dihedral angles between each imidazol group and the central benzene ring plane are 11.37, 35.08, 53.13°, respectively. Interestingly, each (HL)2− ligand, using its two of three imidazolyl groups, connects the Zn(II) atoms to form a 1D helical chain with a helical pitch of 14.92 Å (Fig. S2a, ESI†). Two helical chains with the same handedness form a 1D channel with a size of 3.5 Å in diameter on considering the van der Waals radii. The 1D chains are further linked together by the third imidazolyl group of the (HL)2−, resulting in the formation of a 3D architecture, in which the left- and right-handed helical chains are arranged alternately (Fig. S2b, ESI†). It is noteworthy that the inner surfaces of the channel are composed of electron-rich π-conjugated imidazolyl groups decorated with uncoordinated nitrogen atoms. PLATON calculations show that the porous structure of 1 consists of 3483.7 Å3 voids after the removal of the solvent molecules, which represents 42.5% per unit cell volume [8191.0(10) Å3].13 From a topological view, 1 can be rationalized as a 3D ecl net with both the Zn(II) atoms and (HL)2− ligands acting as four-connected nodes, with a Point (Schläfli) symbol of (4·63·82) (Fig. S3, ESI†).17

Structure description of [Cd(H3L)2](ClO4)2·10.5H2O (2)

The non-deprotonated neutral H3L ligand has a C3 axis of symmetry and can provide three coordination vertices with a triangular shape (Fig. 1a). Complex 2 crystallizes in the cubic space group Pa. The asymmetric unit includes one Cd(II) atom situated on a symmetry site with one sixth occupancy, one third of H3L and perchlorate (Fig. S4, ESI†). Each Cd(II) atom is octahedronally coordinated by six nitrogen atoms from different imidazolyl groups to furnish a 3D framework, where the H3L ligands act as a neutral triangular building block to link the Cd(II) ions. Interestingly, the crystallographic study provides direct evidence that each cell unit of the framework consists of a cage-like motif incorporating a nanometer-sized inner cavity (appropriately 12 Å in diameter without considering the van der Waals radii) capable of accommodating perchlorate ions and water molecules (Fig. 1b). Although the effective free volume of 2 without guest water molecules and counteranions is 59.4% of the crystal volume (3203.8 Å3 of the 5398(3) Å3 unit cell volume), calculated with PLATON, in fact it shows unavailable porosity due to its voids being occupied by the disordered counteranion ClO4−. Extension of the cage-like motifs in space gives the 3D framework (Fig. 2). The simplification of the 3D framework of 2 by regarding each H3L ligand and Cd(II) atom as 3- and 6-connecting nodes, respectively, generates a uniform (3,6) pyrite (pyr) net with a Schläfli symbol of (63)2(612·83) (Fig. S5, ESI†).18

Gas sorption property of 1′

The structure of 1 suggested that evacuation of the free water molecules may give rise to microporous solids potentially accommodating gas molecules, like the above mentioned isostructural compound 3,8b while 2 is not an effective porous material because of the invalid vacancies occupied by the counteranions. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) indicates a weight loss of 9.21%, corresponding to the release of free water molecules for the air dried sample [Zn(HL)]·2H2O (calcd 9.61%) (Fig. S6, ESI†). The decomposition of the residue was observed at about 360 °C, and the residual percentage weight (observed 21.97%) at the end of the decomposition of the complex is in accordance with the formation of ZnO (calculated: 21.72%). The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of 1 is coincident with the simulated one derived from the X-ray single crystal data of 1, implying that the bulk sample is the same as the single crystal (Fig. S7, ESI†). To characterize the porosity of 1, the sample was activated under a high vacuum at 180 °C for 24 h to give desolvated 1′ which still maintains the framework of 1, as evidenced by temperature-dependent PXRD patterns (Fig. S7, ESI†), and the sorption isotherms for CH4, CO2, N2, H2 and different volatile organic molecules (MeOH, EtOH, i-PrOH and benzene (C6H6)) were measured. As shown in Fig. 3, 1′ shows reversible type-I isotherms, which is characteristic of microporous materials. The gas sorption isotherm for CH4 measured at 195 K shows that 1′ can store up to 4.50 wt% (63 cm3 g−1), which exceeds that of inorganic zeolites and organic materials, such as tris-o-phenylenedioxycyclotriphosphazene (TPP) (Fig. 3a).19 The H2 adsorption isotherms measured at 77 and 87 K show the relatively high H2 uptake capability of 1′ (Fig. 3b). It adsorbs up to 0.79 wt% of H2 (88.45 cm3 g−1, 1.34 H2 molecules per formula unit) at 77 K and 1 atm, and up to 0.64 wt% (71.27 cm3 g−1, 1.08 H2 molecules per formula unit) at 87 K and 1 atm. The uptake amount of CO2 increases abruptly at the start of the experiment and reaches a plateau of about 85.76 cm3 g−1, corresponding to 1.3 CO2 molecules in each unit cell. Based on the CO2 isotherm, the Langmuir and Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas of 1′ are estimated to be 569 and 397 m2 g−1, respectively. The N2 sorption isotherms of 1′ at 77 K seem similar to the CO2 sorption isotherms. More interestingly, the CO2 uptakes of 8.33 wt% at 273 K and 6.04 wt% at 298 K are comparable to those for reported ZIFs under similar conditions,20 while almost no N2 uptake was observed from the sorption isotherm (Fig. 3c). In this sense, the ratios of the initial slopes of the CO2 and N2 adsorption isotherms were used to estimate the adsorption selectivity for CO2 over N2 (Fig. S8, ESI†).21 From the calculated CO2/N2 selectivity of 44 at 273 K, it can be concluded that 1′ may have potential application in the separation of CO2/N2 mixtures at ambient conditions.

(c) CO2 adsorption isotherms of 1′ at 273 and 298 K and N2 adsorption isotherm of 1′ at 273 K. In (a)–(c), filled symbols: adsorption; open symbols: desorption.

Another interesting feature is that the channels have a nitrogen-rich (or N-donor functional) wall, which may act as a basic adsorption site to interact with guest molecules, such as polar alcohols, and form hydrogen bonding sites. To test this, the adsorption isotherms of different solvent vapors (MeOH, EtOH, i-PrOH and C6H6 at 298 K) with different polarities were investigated to understand the sorption selectivity and their affinity to the pore surfaces of 1′ (Fig. 4). The sorption profiles of MeOH, EtOH and i-PrOH reveal a type I curve with hysteresis to some degree, reflecting its great affinity for the adsorbates due to the formation of hydrogen bonds.22 The sorption amount increases with increasing pressure and the calculations using the final sorption amount suggest uptakes of 1.35, 1.07. 0.83 molecules of MeOH, EtOH and i-PrOH per formula unit of 1′ respectively. The results show that the number of molecules sorbed per formula unit decreases as the size of the guest increases, indicating a size- and shape-dependent inclusion process. Similarly, the benzene sorption of 1′ measured at 298 K also exhibits a type-I isotherm with an uptake of 0.59 molecules per formula unit but just shows slight hysteresis, unlike the polar alcohols. The slight hysteresis and its onset adsorption observed for benzene starting at a relatively lower pressure, may be ascribed to the host–guest interactions arising from favorable guest–host C–H⋯π and/or π–π interactions to allow the diffusion of the guest molecules.23

From the above experimental observation, it can be seen that 1′ exhibits similar gas sorption properties to the porous Co(II) analogue 3′ (desolvated 3) since they are isomorphous and isostructural. However, it shows distinct properties with the other Co(II) isomer of [Co(HL)]·DMF·H2O (4).8b Namely, 1′ and its isostructural [Co(HL)] show favorable gas uptake capacity for CO2, N2, H2, while 4′ (desolvated 4) shows only CO2 adsorption.8b

To further understand the relevant adsorption mechanism at the molecular level, GCMC simulations were performed to provide insight into the details of the underlying mechanism. The computational details are presented in the ESI†. In order to verify the model and parameters used in this work, both the calculated and experimental data of 1′, 3′ and 4′ at low pressures are plotted for comparison (Fig. S9–S12, ESI†). These findings demonstrate that the simulation results are in reasonable agreement with the experimental data, considering the accuracy of the measurements, which demonstrates that the model and the force field parameters used in this work are able to describe the gas adsorption behaviors in porous frameworks. In line with the experimental data, the simulated adsorption isotherms of isologues 1′ and 3′ exhibit type-I isotherms while 4′ shows CO2 adsorption solely. To further confirm the adsorption mechanism, binding site analysis was employed to investigate the interactions between the gas molecules and the porous frameworks. Considering that the favorable positions are almost the same for all the gas molecules adsorbed in 1′ and 3′, we take H2 as an example to elucidate the adsorbate binding sites determined from the GCMC calculation. Fig. 5 shows a center-of-mass probability density plot from a GCMC simulation at 10 KPa and 77 K in different directions, where the darker locations display a greater probability of finding H2 in 1′. We colored the dark locations in the a axis channel red, the b axis channel green and the less probable locations blue for clarity. Obviously, the adsorbed H2 molecules mostly locate in the narrow channels along the a and b axes other than the larger channel along the c axis, as shown in Fig. 5. The results suggest that the small pores formed by the imidazole groups in 1′ are the preferential adsorption sites for the H2 molecules. This phenomena is similar to preferential adsorption in small cages, elucidated by other molecular simulation studies, due to the strong dispersion interaction between the adsorbates and surface of the channel.24 The framework in 4′ only exibits a 1D channel along the a axis, different from the 3D channel in 1′ and 3′, and adsorbed CO2 molecules with a strong quadrupole locate near the N-donor imidazole groups of the channel in the GCMC calculation but no adsorption for weak quadrupolar adsorbates, such as H2, N2 and CH4 (Fig. 6), is found. The binding site of CO2 with the highest possibility is vertical to the imidazole rings in 4′. Therefore, the effective gas binding affinity originates from the polarization interaction between CO2 with a high quadrupole and the exposed non-coordinated nitrogen atoms of the imidazole groups.25

To study the nature of the adsorbate binding, the adsorption enthalpies were calculated by the virial method and GCMC simulations, respectively and the results are listed in Table S3 in the ESI.† Generally, the virial fittings from experimental outcomes are consistent with the GCMC simulations. For 1′ and 3′, all the experimental enthalpies (Qst) were calculated by the virial equation from the measured adsorption isotherms (Fig. S13–S16, ESI†).26 The heat of adsorption of hydrogen is about 7.3 kJ mol−1 for 1′ and 3′, which is much higher than activated carbons (5.8 kJ mol−1) and those for typical van der Waals type interactions (4–6 kJ mol−1).27 However, it is lower than MOF-74 with open metal sites (8.3 kJ mol−1).28 The comparatively high adsorption enthalpy can be mainly attributed to the fact that nitrogen atoms on aromatic rings in a framework can enhance the adsorption energy of H2 and the generate frameworks with narrow pores to allow gas molecule to interact with the overlapping potential from the pore walls.29 It is also interesting to point out that the simulated value of 17.43 kJ mol−1 in 1′ surpasses 10–16 kJ mol−1 for IRMOFs and allows access to the required target adsorption enthalpy of 18.8 kJ mol−1 for methane in the U.S. Department of Energy. Similarly, the CO2 adsorption enthalpy of 32 kJ mol−1 at the onset of adsorption (Fig. S15 and S16, ESI†) is higher than the values for siliceous zeolite (27 kJ mol−1) and activated carbons (less than 26 kJ mol−1),30 but slightly lower than for amine-functionalized MOFs, further supporting the high CO2 binding affinity associated with the pore structure.31 Although 4′ also adsorbs CO2 molecules with a high quadrupole moment under all the temperatures considered, all the gas adsorption heat values are lower than those of 1′ and 3′, which indicates the adsorption enthalpies are very sensitive to the features of different channels in porous frameworks.

Further insight into the nature of the H2 binding can be gained by separating it into dispersion and electrostatic energy. ‘Pure’ dispersion energy is obtained from GCMC calculations by switching off the electrostatic interactions between the H2 molecules and the framework atoms.32 The contribution of the electrostatic energy is defined as (Ntotal − NVdw)/Ntotal (Vdw, van der Waals energy), where Ntotal and NVdw denote the value absorbed in the presence and absence of the electrostatic interactions, respectively. This analysis shows that 64% of the binding energy is due to dispersion, whereas 36% comes from electrostatics for 1′. Additionally, almost all the binding energy could be assigned to electrostatic contacts for 4′. This means that the lower polarizability of H2 molecules can ‘touch’ the surface of the narrow channels in 1′ and 3′, but cannot ‘touch’ the framework in 4′. The different features of the different sized channels therfore play an important role in the adsorption behaviors between 1′ or 3′ and 4′.

Reversible anion-exchange property of 2

As revealed by the crystal structure of 2, the H3L ligands coordinate with Cd(II) to form a 3D structure with nanometer-sized cages trapping perchlorate anions. Complex 2 is air stable and can retain its crystalline integrity at ambient conditions. However, a thermogravimetric experiment was not carried out to investigate its thermal stability due to safety reasons, since it is a perchlorate salt. 2 is not soluble in common solvents and thus, the compound with ClO4− spontaneously encapsulated within the cages is expected to display an anion exchange property. In order to see if these anions could move in and out of the host pyr framework, anion exchange was carried out and validated using IR, elemental analysis and PXRD. The results indicate that 2 has a reversible anion exchange property. Excess NaNO2 or NaNO3 was added to a suspension of powdered 2 in water at room temperature, and the exchanged products [Cd(H3L)2](NO2)2·2H2O (2A) or [Cd(H3L)2](NO3)2·2H2O (2B) were obtained. The FT-IR spectra of 2 and the exchanged products 2A and 2B are shown in Fig. S17 and S18 (ESI†), respectively. It is clear that the intense bands at 1271 cm−1, which originate from the NO2− anion, appear in the spectrum of the exchange product 2A. The bands from 1382 to 1318 cm−1 from the NO3− anion are present in the spectrum of 2B, while the intense broad band from 1150 to 1075 cm−1, which belongs to the overlapping band of imidazole ring stretching vibrations and the characteristic peak of the ClO4− anion (Fig. S19, ESI†), is split into a characteristic peak for imidazole ring stretching vibrations, accompanied by the disappearance of the characteristic peak for the ClO4− anion in the spectra of the exchanged products 2A and 2B. Namely, the ClO4− anions in the 3D framework of 2 are entirely exchanged by NO2− or NO3− anions. Furthermore, the results of the elemental analyses also confirmed that the ClO4− anions were completely exchanged by NO2− or NO3− anions. To further investigate the stability of the 3D framework of 2 during the anion exchange experiments, the PXRD patterns of complex 2 and the exchanged products 2A and 2B were measured and the results are shown in Fig. S20 (ESI†). The exchanged products are almost consistent with 2, which confirms that the framework structure of 2 has been maintained during the anion exchange. To investigate the reversibility of the anion exchange of 2, well-powdered 2A or 2B was added to an aqueous solution of NaClO4, and the resulting solids 2C and 2D were obtained. The intense broad band from the ClO4− anion appeared again in the spectra of 2C and 2D, and the NO2− bands in the spectrum of 2A and the NO3− bands in the spectrum of 2B disappeared (Fig. S17 and S18, ESI†). Combined with the elemental analysis and PXRD, it can be deduced that the exchanged products 2C and 2D belong to complex 2. The results indicate that 2 has a reversible anion exchange property for the pairs of ClO4−/NO2− and ClO4−/NO3−. When NaNO3 was added to the suspension of the exchanged solid 2A in water, the characteristic peaks of the NO2− anion for the exchanged solid 2B appeared (Fig. S21, ESI†). A similar anion exchange experiment for 2B was also carried out, and the result indicated that the NO3− anions in complex 2B can also be successfully exchanged by the NO2− anions (Fig. S22, ESI†). This implies that reversible anion exchange also occurred between the exchanged solids 2A and 2B. In this context, open “windows” in the cage can facilitate the exchange of different anions to give new complexes.

Conclusions

In conclusion, two novel transition metal imidazolate/imidazole frameworks have been synthesized and structurally characterized. It has further been shown that the ligand containing 1H-imidazol-4-yl groups can serve not only as a neutral ligand, but also as an anionic ligand by deprotonation, thus exhibiting versatile coordination modes. The imidazolate framework of 1 with 3D channels exhibited significant gas uptake or a selective gas sorption property like ZIFs, which further demonstrates the advantages to using imidazole ligands to construct porous materials. Further GCMC studies are in line with the experimental observation and show different adsorption behaviors which depend on different channel features as well as the gas characteristics. The porous 3D robust framework 2, consisting of cage-like motifs accommodating ClO4− counteranions and open motifs, can benefit the reversible exchange of different anions. In this context, the designability of imidazole ligands incorporating 1H-imidazol-4-yl groups can not only provide possibilities to construct novel frameworks but also fortify the functionalities as potential materials for adsorption, and catalysis due to the existence of pore surfaces as guest-accessible sites decorated with uncoordinated nitrogen atoms of imidazole groups.
