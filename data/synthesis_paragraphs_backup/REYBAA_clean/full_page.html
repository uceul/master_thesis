Syntheses, structures, and photoluminescent properties of ten metal–organic frameworks constructed by a flexible tetracarboxylate ligand†

Xiang He *ab, Xiao-Peng Lu a, Zhan-Feng Ju b, Chun-Ju Li a, Qi-Kai Zhang b and Ming-Xing Li *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, College of Sciences, Shanghai University, Shanghai 200444, China. E-mail: hexiang23@gmail.com; mx_li@mail.shu.edu.cn

Reactions of zinc ions with the flexible tetracarboxylate ligand, 5,5′-(1,4-phenylenebis(methylene))bis(oxy)diisophthalic acid (H4L1) gave rise to ten complexes, namely {[Zn3(L1)(OH)2(H2O)2]3H2O}n (1), {[Zn(H2L1)(H2O)2]H2O}n (2), [Zn2(L1)(H2O)4]n (3), {[Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(H2O)2(MeCN)2]}n (4), [Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(2,2′-bipy)2]n (5), [Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(1,10-phen)2]n (6), {[Zn2(L1)(4,4′-bipy)2]4H2O}n (7), {[Zn2(L1)(bpe)2]4H2O}n (bpe = 1,2-bi(4-pyridyl)ethene) (8), {[Zn2(L1)(pdp)2]4H2O}n (pdp = 4-[(E)-4-Pyridinylazo]pyridine) (9) and {[Zn2(L1)(bpmp)2]6H2O}n (bpmp = N,N′-bis-(4-pyridyl-methyl) piperazine) (10). The H4L1 ligand not only displayed different deprotonated forms, but also diverse coordination modes and conformations. Compounds 1–4 are constructed of only zinc atoms with H4L1 ligands while compounds 5–10 are constructed of zinc atoms and H4L1 ligands with various N-donor ligands (Scheme 1). Compound 1 features a 3D architecture constructed of a linear Zn3 cluster with H4L1 ligands. Compound 2 is a 2D waved layer structure with (4,4) grid as sql topology. Compound 3 displays a three-dimensional (3D) network simplified by a (3,6)-connected 2-nodal net with ant topology. Compounds 4, 5 and 6 all exhibit 1D infinite chains coordination structure, in which the H4L1 ligand is displayed as two kinds of deprotonated forms. Compounds 7, 8, 9 and 10 are similar and possess (4,4)-connected 3D frameworks with bbf topology, while also exhibiting an intriguing three-fold interpenetrated structure. These results indicate that subtle environmental factors, such as solvent, pH value and neutral auxiliary ligands play important roles in the formation of the structures of the final framework. At the same time, the photochemical properties of compounds 1–10 were tested in the solid state at room temperature, and the luminescent properties of compound 9 and 10 when dispersed in different solvents were investigated. These showed solvent-dependent luminescent spectra with emission intensities significantly quenched towards nitrobenzene. The quenching effects are observed at a low nitrobenzene concentration of 200 ppm, which indicates high sensitivities of these compounds towards nitrobenzene.

Introduction

The assembling of coordination polymers are currently of great interest in the field of coordination chemistry and crystal engineering due to the fact that they can provide versatile architectures and potential applications in gas storage, catalysis, ion exchange and luminescence.1–6 However, it is still challenging to target structures with desired properties because many factors affect the result, such as the subtle interplay of metal coordination preferences, connection modes of organic ligands, reaction temperature, the ratio of reagents, solvents, pH value, anions, and so on.7–12 The rational design and synthesis of compounds with desired architectures and promising properties seems to be a great challenge for chemists. However, it also provides an opportunity to understand the relationships between these influence factors and the diverse structures they produce. As we know, most coordination supramolecular systems are obtained from the solvent media, so the choice of solvents is an important factor in the synthesis of crystalline materials and is key to constructing various coordination compounds. The solvent, not only as the reaction medium but also as a structure directing agent, influences the crystal growth, crystalline morphology and lattice structure of the final solid.13 At the same time, the pH of the reaction, as another external stimulus, is also important in the assembly of polycarboxylate MOFs. It can affect not only the deprotonated linker but also the related coordination modes and, consequently, the resulting structures.

So far, most research efforts have been devoted to MOFs assembled by rigid polycarboxylate ligands owing to their rich coordination modes and the rational synthesis of default structures,14 while flexible carboxylate ligands in the construction of metal–organic systems is somewhat rare.15 Recently, flexible ligands have become attractive as building elements in the synthesis of multidimensional networks because their conformational freedom can be affected easily by external influencing factors, and may provide more possibility for the construction of structures with unusual topologies.16 In order to get more useful information in this field, we chose the flexible 5,5′-(1,4-phenylenebis(methylene))bis(oxy)diisophthalic acid (H4L1) as a functional ligand, based upon the following considerations: firstly, it can adopt various coordination modes owing to the tetradentate carboxylate arms; secondly, it can be completely or partially deprotonated, influenced by different pH values and so act not only as a hydrogen bond acceptor but also as a hydrogen bond donor; thirdly, the presence of a flexible –O–CH2– group in this molecule allows the phenyl and biphenyl rings to twist and rotate freely when affected by external influencing factors during the self-assembly process.

d10 Metal complexes are promising luminescent materials for their potential applications such as light-emitting materials (LEDs), because of their ability to enhance and shift the luminescent emission of organic ligands by metal coordination. Recently, many mixed inorganic–organic hybrid coordination polymers have been investigated for fluorescence properties.17 At the same time, the rich delocalized π-electron framework as well as the confinement of analytes inside the MOF pores can also allow MOFs to act as highly sensitive chemical sensors. Several MOFs have been reported to be highly sensitive to hazardous explosives, such as 2,4-DNT (2,4-dinitrotoluene) and 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), while the mechanism of this detection process is based on the electron-withdrawing properties of nitro groups.18

In this work, by using the flexible ligand H4L1, changing the solvents, reaction pH and/or using auxiliary ligands, a series of zinc coordination compounds have been assembled successfully, namely {[Zn3(L1)(OH)2(H2O)2]3H2O}n (1), {[Zn(H2L1)(H2O)2]H2O}n (2), [Zn2(L1)(H2O)4]n (3), {[Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(H2O)2(MeCN)2]}n (4) [Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(2,2′-bipy)2]n (5), [Zn2(H3L1)2(H2L1)(1,10-phen)2]n (6), {[Zn2(L1)(4,4′-bipy)2]4H2O}n (7), {[Zn2(L1)(bpe)2]4H2O}n (8), {[Zn2(L1)(pdp)2]4H2O}n (9) and {[Zn2(L1)(bpmp)2]6H2O}n (10). Their structural diversities show that the solvents, pH value of the reaction system and auxiliary ligand all play significant roles in affecting the coordination conformations and deprotonation of the H4L1 ligand during the structural self-assembly process. All the complexes were characterized by X-ray crystallography, IR, elemental, thermal stability, and powder X-ray diffraction analyses. The photochemical properties of compounds 1–10 were tested in the solid state at room temperature; also, the luminescent properties of compounds 9 and 10 dispersed in different solvents have been investigated. The results show that these compounds may be candidates for the detection of nitroaromatic explosives via a fluorescence quenching mechanism.

Experimental section

General

5,5′-[1,4-Phenylenebis(methylene)]bis(oxy)-diisophthalic acid and 4-[(E)-4-pyridinylazo]pyridine were prepared according to the literature method.19 All other chemicals were of analytical reagent grade and used as purchased without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed on a Vario EL III analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Nicolet A370 FT-IR spectrometer by KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analyses (TG–DSC) were completed on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under N2 atmosphere. The luminescent data were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. The photoluminescent (PL) spectra were recorded on a HORIBA Jobin–Yvon FluoroMax-4 fluorescence spectrophotometer.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

Although some assembly processes have been proposed in a step-by-step approach, the step-by-step deprotonated of the carboxyl groups in the H4L1 ligand is rarely investigated by changing subtle environmental factors, such as solvent, pH value and neutral auxiliary ligands. Firstly, solvent is an essential component for any reaction, and determine the activation energetics and the reaction thermodynamics. We obtained compound 1 from a basic system of DMF–H2O, and the H4L1 ligand was fully deprotonated. When we changed the kind of reaction solvent to an acidic system of CH3CN–H2O, compound 2 was obtained successfully. As we expected, the H4L1 ligand was partly deprotonated. Secondly, pH is an important factor to effect deprotonation of the carboxyl groups in the H4L1 ligand. Because of partial deprotonation of this tetracarboxylate ligand during synthesis of compound 2, the high pH value may favor the deprotonation of the H4L1 ligand, so when we changed the pH value of 4.0 to 5.5 by adding KOH, we got compound 3, in which the H4L1 ligand is fully deprotonated. The solvent and pH value of the reaction, as the external stimuli, are important factors in the assembly of MOFs. From previous studies, we already know that different pH values attributed to not only the deprotonation of polycarboxylic acids but also their related connection modes. For example, Hu21 has reported that the pH value impacts on the assembly processes of the H4btc = biphenyl-3,3′,4,4′-tetracarboxylic acid ligand and metal ions as well as the framework structures of their complexes. Yao22 also did some research on the influence of pH on the structural variations of 4,4′-oxydiphthalate coordination polymers. These results reveal the pH value of the reaction plays a key role in structural control during self-assembly processes. On the other hand, solvent effect is also a vital subject in the domain of coordination chemistry. The solvent effects can be generally explained in terms of their difference in size, shape, and coordination ability. Recently, Hong23 gave us an example of solvent induced assembly in crystal engineering of functional coordination polymers. Sun24 also reported that a choice of different solvent systems would give a profound influence on the coordination environments of metal and linking modes of the ligand in each framework. Thirdly, neutral auxiliary ligands can also decide the step-by-step deprotonation of the H4L1 ligand. Compound 4 was synthesized accidentally by using dipybi ligand as the secondary N-donor ligand at first. Although this ligand does not exist in 4, it may play an important role in the formation of 4. Without addition of this ligand in the reaction, compound 2 was formed. Although detailed studies are still needed, this ligand may play a template role in the formation of 4. Due to the H4L1 ligand present two partly deprotonated forms in compound 4, low pH will favor its formation, so we added HCl in this syntheses system, compound 4 can also be obtained. When we use 2,2′-bipy instead of dipybi ligand, compound 5 was obtained, in which the H4L1 ligand adopted two forms similar to compound 4, but the difference is that the 2,2′-bipy ligand also exists in this production. Different N-donor ligands were tried continually in this system, using the 1,10-phen ligand instead of the 2,2′-bipy ligand, we get compound 6, which has a similar structure to compound 5. If the 2,2′-bipy and the 1,10-phen ligand are substituted by 4,4′-bipy, bpe, pdp and bpmp ligands, it is interesting that the Zn(II) ions are coordinated by fully deprotonated carboxyl groups. This fact shows that neutral auxiliary ligands also play a role in regulating the solution pH. Under the similar conditions, the 2,2′-bipy and the 1,10-phen ligands only enable partial deprotonation, while the 4,4′-bipy, bpe, pdp and bpmp ligands can fully make the four carboxyl groups deprotonated.

Descriptions of X-ray crystal structures

Structure of {[Zn3(L1)(OH)2(H2O)2]3H2O}n (1). Compound 1 recrystallizes in the space group P. There are three crystallographically independent zinc ions, one L1 ligand, two hydroxyl groups and five water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, Zn1 is surrounded by two oxygen donors from two different H4L1 ligands, two oxygen atoms from two hydroxyl groups and one oxygen atom from one coordinated water molecule to display a distorted square pyramidal geometry with a τ value of 0.15.25 Except the normal Zn–O bond with the distance range of 2.047(3) Å–2.153(3) Å. There also exists weak interactions between Zn and one coordinated water molecule (Zn–O = 2.371(2) Å). Zn2 atom displayed a distorted tetrahedral geometry, coordinated by two oxygen donors from two different H4L1 ligands and two oxygen atoms from two hydroxyl groups. Zn3 atom also featured a distorted square pyramidal geometry with a τ value of 0.48, surrounded by two oxygen donors from two different H4L1 ligands, two oxygen atoms from two hydroxyl groups and one oxygen atoms from coordinated water molecules. Three Zn atoms are connected by hydroxyl groups and carboxylate groups to form a Zn3 cluster. All the H4L1 ligands are deprotonated as L4− during the reaction, and adopted two kinds of coordination mode (mode I in Scheme 1), one is bismonodentate coordination mode and another is monodentate coordination mode. Each H4L1 ligand connected six Zn atoms as an hexadentate ligand, and the two isophthalic rings are not coplanar to each other with a dihedral angle of 56.47(2)°. The central phenyl ring twists away from the two isophthalic rings with dihedral angles of 51.98(2)° and 71.55(2)°, respectively. Influence of the solvent, pH value and auxiliary ligand on the structures of complexes 1–10. Based on the above structural description, it is clear that compounds 1–4 exhibit various structures although they have similar reagents during syntheses. It can be seen that different reaction environments have an influence on the frameworks of these complexes. Compounds 1 and 2 were synthesized in different solvents. They displayed 3D and 2D structure respectively, in which H4L1 ligands present a different deprotonated fashion. Compounds 2, 3 and 4 were synthesized under the same reaction conditions, except for the pH of the reactions (pH = 4.0 for 2, 5.5 for 3 and 1.5 for 4). H4L1 ligands present various deprotonated forms in these three compounds. Comparing with 1 and 3, although possessing the similar 3D structure, compound 1 was constructed by a linear Zn3 cluster with L14− ligand, which can be simplified as 4,6 two-nodal {44.53.68}2{44.62} network topology, while 3 was constructed only by Zn atoms with L14− ligands, which can be simplified as (3,6)-connected 2-nodal net with an ant topology.

On comparing 4 and 5, although they both possess a 1D structure, H4L1 ligands present two deprotonated forms with different coordination modes, in which the H3L− form acts as a monodentate mode in 4 but as a bidentate mode in 5. Compound 6 has a similar structure to compound 5, except the 2,2′-bipy ligand is replaced by the 1,10-phen ligand. The reduction of the dimensionality of 5 and 6 may contribute to the steric hindrance of 2,2′-bipy and 1,10-phen ligands. When using bridging 4,4′-bipy ligand instead of chelating 1,10-phen ligand, compound 7 was obtained with a 3D structure as expected. Comparing compounds 7–10, although the length of the 4,4′-bipy, bpe, pdp and bpmp ligands are different, they did not lead to other new architectures but constructed similar 3D structures. The most prominent characteristic of these compounds is that they all possess a three-fold interpenetration bbf network. Photoluminescence. Luminescence of complexes containing d10 metal centers has attracted intense interest due to their potential applications such as lighting, biological probes and electroluminescent displays.32 The luminescent spectra of H4L1, the N-donor ligands and complexes 1–10 were determined in the solid state at room temperature, as shown in Fig. 9. In contrast, the PL spectra of other compounds (1, 3 and 7–10) are not only highly red-shifted, but also broader and more structureless relative to those of their free ligands, indicating a LLCT nature. Since various π–π* stacking interactions have been found in these crystal structures, it is reasonable to deduce that their broad emission bands with the maximum at 404–470 nm may originate from the ligand dimers or excimers.38 In addition, similar emission shapes for complexes 5 and 6, or compounds 7–10 are probably attributed to their closely related crystal structures, giving identical local ligand environments.

To examine the potential luminescence sensing properties of 9 and 10, the fine grinding samples 9 and 10 was immersed in different organic solvents, treated by ultrasonication for over 30 min and then aged for 2 days to form a stable emulsion before the fluorescence study. The solvents used are DMF (N,N-dimethylformamide), methanol, acetonitrile, and NB (nitrobenzene). All the PL spectra of emulsions of 9 and 10 reveal emission with a very similar broadened maximum at about 430 nm (λex = 360 nm) as that of free ligand of dpd (λem = 445 nm, λex = 350 nm) and 4-bpmp (λem = 462 nm, λex = 330 nm). As shown in Fig. 10, the PL spectra are largely dependent on the solvent molecules. In the case of DMF and NB, the spectra exhibited enhancing or quenching behavior. To examine sensing sensitivity toward NB in detail, a batch of emulsions of 9 or 10 dispersed in DMF solution with gradually increased NB contents were prepared to monitor the emissive response. As shown in Fig. 11, the results show that these materials could be used as sensors for nitrobenzene, with quenching even beginning at a concentration of 0.5 ppm, and complete quenching at 200 ppm. The electron transfer is capable of taking place upon excitation from the electron-donating framework to the high electron-withdrawing –NO2 group in nitrobenzene resulting in the detectable luminescence quenching. Such solvent-dependent luminescence phenomena are of interest for detecting trace nitrobenzene.

Conclusion

In summary, ten coordination polymers based on flexible H4L1 and N-donor ligands have been successfully synthesized. By comparing the structures of these complexes, we have found that the coordination conformation and deprotonation of the H4L1 ligand can be influenced by solvent, pH value and auxiliary ligands. Complexes 1 and 2 are obtained by varying solvents to control their structures. Complexes 2–4 represent a nice example of the pH-controlled polymeric architecture construction. N-donor auxiliary ligands also play important roles in affecting the final structures of complexes 5–10. Thus, our present research further enriches the crystal engineering strategy and offers new possibilities for controlling the formation of desired metal–organic frameworks. Furthermore, luminescent measurements reveal that complexes 1–10 are good candidates for photoactive materials, owing to their strong luminescent emissions. The solvent-dependent luminescence spectra of compounds 9 and 10 show that these compounds might also be candidates for selective sensory material for the detection of nitroaromatic explosives.
