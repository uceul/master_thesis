Ratiometric dual-emitting MOF⊃dye thermometers with a tunable operating range and sensitivity†

Dian Zhao , Dan Yue , Ke Jiang , Yuanjing Cui *, Qi Zhang , Yu Yang and Guodong Qian *
State Key Laboratory of Silicon Materials, Cyrus Tang Center for Sensor Materials and Applications, School of Materials Science and Engineering, Zhejiang University, Hangzhou 310027, China. E-mail: cuiyj@zju.edu.cn; gdqian@zju.edu.cn; Tel: +86-571-87952334

Received 1st December 2016 , Accepted 9th January 2017

First published on 10th January 2017

A tetracarboxylic acid ligand containing a highly polarized benzothiadiazole moiety was designed and used to construct the luminescent porous MOF ZJU-21, which can efficiently absorb the luminescent dye 4-[p-(dimethylamino)styryl]-1-methylpyridinium (DMASM) into the pores as well as sensitize it, yielding a dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite ZJU-21⊃DMASM. The emission color of the resulting composite can be modulated by controlling the amount of dyes, and the intensity ratio of the two emissions can be used as a ratiometric thermometric parameter for temperature measurement. ZJU-21⊃DMASM also exhibits excellent temperature-dependent photoluminescence properties in the physiological temperature range, which, as well as the temperature and sensitivity ranges, can be tuned by controlling the concentration of DMASM in the pores of the host framework. Moreover, the proposed scheme can be readily applied to other luminescent porous MOFs and organic dyes, thereby providing a new perspective for the design of MOF⊃dye ratiometric temperature sensors.

1. Introduction

Temperature is a basic physical parameter in all fields of natural science and its accurate measurement is crucial in many scientific and technological areas. Dual-emitting ratiometric measurement of temperature is one of the most promising non-invasive techniques because it is self-referenced and has high spatial resolution at the submicron scale where traditional methods are ineffective.1 At present, lanthanide-based thermometers based on two independent emissions have been realized by making use of lanthanide co-doped organic–inorganic hybrids, up-converted nanoparticles, and lanthanide metal–organic frameworks (MOFs).1–3

MOFs, constructed from the metal ions with organic ligands of diverse structure and tunable luminescence properties, have been extensively studied for various optical applications.4–10 The intrinsic luminescence of lanthanide MOFs, especially their thermosensitive characteristics, have further facilitated their use as luminescent thermometers.3 A number of promising ratiometric luminescent thermometers based on the mixed-lanthanide MOF approach have been developed.1b,11–13 However, such methodology is limited to utilizing the emission characteristics of lanthanide ions, and tuning the sensing temperature range is difficult. Recently, we have demonstrated the first dye-encapsulated MOF thermometer, namely the ZJU-88⊃perylene, which uses the europium MOF ZJU-88 to encapsulate perylene through a “one-pot” process to form a dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite for temperature measurement.14 Although such a thermometer does not have tunable sensing properties, the features of the MOF⊃dye composite indicate that the operating temperature range and sensitivity can be engineered by a judicious choice of the guest dyes, metal ions, and host matrices or ligands. In particular, if the fluorescent guest dyes can be captured into porous ligand-based luminescent MOFs and form a MOF⊃dye composite, we can make use of the temperature-dependent luminescent behaviors of both the guest dyes and linkers to develop a dual organic emitting MOF⊃dye thermometer. In addition, since the guest dyes can be directly sensitized by host linkers based on fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET),15 the energy transfer between the luminescent guest dyes and the host linkers can be readily tuned by controlling the concentration of the guest dye in the pores of the host framework. The intensity ratio based on the dual organic emissions can thus operate in different temperature ranges with different sensitivities. As a result, this MOF⊃dye composite approach should be very appealing for use in novel luminescent thermometers with a tunable sensing range and sensitivity.

To improve the ability of porous MOFs for guest species adsorption or capture, great efforts have been devoted to tailoring the pore environment of MOFs.16–21 It has been reported that modification of organic linkers with functional groups can enhance the interaction between the host framework and the guest species.16–21 The cationic pyridinium hemicyanine dye 4-[p-(dimethylamino)styryl]-1-methylpyridinium (DMASM) with red emission can be suitable as guest species10 if the luminescent MOFs can have enough space to accommodate the DMASM cations and the functional sites within the MOF can have strong interactions with DMASM. We can then develop a dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite whose temperature-dependent luminescence intensity ratio can be modulated by controlling the concentration of DMASM in the pores of the host framework.

Based on these considerations, we have designed and synthesized 5,5′-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4,7-diyl) diisophthalic acid with a highly polarized benzothiadiazole moiety (molecular dipole of 4.8 Debye calculated by Chemical 3D software) as the organic linker to construct a microporous MOF with Zn2+ ions, and encapsulate DMASM into the pores to form a dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite (Scheme 1). The large backbone of this organic linker allows the construction of large pore spaces for accommodating the DMASM cations, and the polarized benzothiadiazole unit on the pore surfaces promotes strong interaction with DMASM. In addition, the organic linker can sensitize DMASM based on FRET, but the metal ion Zn2+ does not participate in the energy transfer process. Thus the quenching effect of metal ions can be effectively reduced, facilitating the energy transfer between the host linker and the guest DMASM. As expected, we have successfully synthesized the luminescent porous MOF, ZJU-21, to encapsulate the DMASM cations to yield the ZJU-21⊃DMASM composite. With judicious control of the DMASM amount in the pores of the host framework, the color of luminescence can be tuned from green to red. More importantly, the resulting composite ZJU-21⊃DMASM exhibits a highly temperature-dependent luminescence intensity ratio in the physiological temperature range, which, as well as the temperature range and sensitivity, can be tuned by controlling the concentration of DMASM in the pores of the host framework.

2. Results and discussion

Reaction of H4L (5,5′-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4,7-diyl) diisophthalic acid) with Zn(NO3)2·6H2O via a solvothermal method in a mixed solvent of DMF (N,N′-dimethylformamide) and acetonitrile at 85 °C for 48 hours affords the pale-yellow block-like crystals of [Zn2(C22O10N2SH8)(H2O)2]·5DMF·10H2O (ZJU-21). The formula of ZJU-21 was confirmed based on single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, and the phase purity and thermostability of bulk material were demonstrated by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA), respectively (Fig. S1 and S2, ESI†). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that ZJU-21 crystallizes in the trigonal space group Rm, and isostructural to the well-known NOTT-101.20,21 Each Zn2+ ion is coordinated to five oxygen atoms, specifically, four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four different ligands and one oxygen atom from a DMF molecule (Fig. 1a). Neighboring Zn2+ atoms are linked by carbonyl units of four different ligands to form a zinc paddlewheel Zn2(COO)4 secondary building unit (SBU), which is connected by the 4-connected rectangular organic building blocks, eventually forming a three-dimensional (3D) non-interpenetrated NbO-type framework. There are two distinct types of cages in the crystal structure (Fig. 1b). The small octahedral cage about 12 Å in diameter consists of 12 ligands and 6 Zn2+ paddlewheel SBUs, while the large shuttle-shaped cage of about 9 × 18 Å is surrounded by 12 Zn2+ paddlewheel SBUs and 6 ligands. The MOF of ZJU-21 exhibits 1D channels along the c-axis of about 8.2 × 8.6 Å2 (Fig. 1c). The channels and cage spaces are large enough to accommodate guest dye molecules. It is necessary to note that the central benzothiadiazole sites of the ligands remain unbounded with the metal ions, thus providing the functional sites for the guest molecules. More importantly, the two cages are orderly arranged and decorated with polarized benzothiadiazole moieties, and are expected to facilitate the adsorption of the ionic dyes via ion-exchange.10a,19
Fig. 1 Single-crystal X-ray structure of ZJU-21. (a) Each organic building block is connected to four zinc paddlewheel Zn2(COO)4 units; (b) two types of nanocages; (c) CPK representation of a 3D open framework viewed along the c direction. The gray, red, green, blue and yellow spheres denote C, O, Zn, N and S atoms, respectively. The bigger dark-yellow and cyan spheres represent the pores within the frameworks. The H atoms are omitted for clarity.

To encapsulate the dye molecules into the cages of ZJU-21, the as-synthesized MOFs were soaked in the DMF solution of the cationic dye DMASM at 60 °C for 2 hours. The resulting samples were collected by filtration, washed with DMF several times until no characteristic emission of DMASM was observed in the filtrate upon excitation, and then they are dried in air. As confirmed by PXRD, the ZJU-21⊃DMASM is nearly identical to ZJU-21, indicating that the cation-exchange process does not change the crystal structure. Soaking the ZJU-21 in different concentration solutions (1 × 10−4, 1 × 10−5, and 1 × 10−6 mol L−1) for 2 hours at 60 °C, three dye-exchange MOFs ZJU-21⊃DMASM with different dye contents are obtained. The content of the DMASM dye in ZJU-21⊃DMASM composites was determined to be 0.046%, 0.009%, and 0.005%, respectively, showing that the encapsulated dye content can be achieved by controlling the concentration of DMASM solution. It is worth noting that ZJU-21 significantly absorbs the DMASM cations into the pores from the dilute dye solutions, which can be attributed to the energetic interactions between the dye molecules and the polarized benzothiadiazole moieties of the MOF.10a

The excitation and emission spectra of the solid powder samples of the H4L ligand, ZJU-21, and ZJU-21⊃DMASM were recorded at room temperature. Under the free ligand H4L exhibits a green emission band at around 516 nm which can be attributed to n–π* transitions (Fig. S3, ESI†). The emission wavelength of as-synthesized ZJU-21 is approximately 492 nm under the excitation at 366 nm (Fig. S4, ESI†), which shows a blue-shift compared to that of the H4L ligand, attributed to the enhanced rigidity of the linker in the framework. The DMASM in DMF solution shows a weak red emission around 628 nm in the emission spectrum under excitation at 470 nm (Fig. S5a, ESI†). Apparently, there is a large spectral overlap between the emission of ZJU-21 and the absorption of DMASM (Fig. S5b, ESI†). Therefore efficient energy transfer from MOF ZJU-21 to the dye can take place via the FRET mechanism. The MOF-to-dye energy transfer process can be confirmed by the emission profile of the ZJU-21⊃DMASM composite.

As expected, under the same conditions as that for ZJU-21, the ZJU-21⊃DMASM composite simultaneously exhibits the typical emission profiles of the rigidified linker and DMASM (Fig. 2a), implying efficient energy transfer from the MOF to the dye. Furthermore, the emission color of ZJU-21⊃DMASM can be tuned by increasing the DMASM cations in the pores of the host framework (Fig. 2b and c). We note that the color changes basically depend on the combination of the emissions from the ZJU-21 (green band) and DMASM (red band), as well as the energy transfer process between the MOF and the dye. As the DMASM content gradually increases, the red emission of DMASM is enhanced while the emission of the MOF ZJU-21 is gradually quenched, thus leading to the color changes from green to red which was depicted in the CIE chromaticity diagram for ZJU-21⊃DMASM (Fig. S6, ESI†). More importantly, the emission peak profile of DMASM in MOF ZJU-21 is obviously blue-shifted compared to that of the DMASM solution, and is significantly different from the behavior of the DMASM dye in the solid state, which can be attributed to the pore confinement of DMASM within ZJU-21 due to the strong interaction between the polarized benzothiadiazole moieties of the MOF and DMASM cations. It also indicates that DMASM is uniformly accommodated in the channels of ZJU-21 as isolated molecules, thereby avoiding the formation of excimers or aggregates in the solid phase. In addition, the emission peak of DMASM exhibits a slight red-shift as the dye concentration increases in the MOF ZJU-21, implying that the confinement effect of DMASM cations can be depressed by increasing the dye molecules. The quantum yields of ZJU-21⊃DMASM with the representative dye contents of 0.046% (1), 0.009% (2), and 0.005% (3) are determined to be 3.77%, 4.82%, and 6.46%, respectively, suggesting that these composites can serve as candidates for luminescent sensors. It should be noted that the quantum yield of these composites is much lower than that of the host ZJU-21 (29%), which can be attributed to the interaction between the host framework and the guest DMASM, such as energy transfer, nonradiative decays, etc. More importantly, these effects, especially the energy transfer process, depend strongly on temperature, so that the intensity of the two emissions from the ZJU-21⊃DMASM can be modulated and utilized as the ratiometric temperature parameter for the sensing purpose.


Fig. 2 Emission spectra of ZJU-21⊃DMASM with dye contents of (a) 0.046% and (b) 0.009% and (c) 0.005% excited at 366 nm.

We are interested in the temperature-dependent photoluminescence (PL) properties of the ZJU-21⊃DMASM composite, as well as its potential application as a luminescent thermometer. UV light at 366 nm was used to excite the MOF ZJU-21, and the temperature dependent emission spectra and normalized intensity in the temperature range from 20 to 80 °C are presented in Fig. S7 (ESI†). The emission intensity of the rigidified ligand is stable with the temperature increase, indicating that the thermal activation of nonradiative decays or relaxation is not strong enough to affect the fluorescence of the linker in the host framework ZJU-21. Under the same conditions, however, the luminescence intensities of the organic linker (cyan band) and the guest molecule (orange band) in the composite ZJU-21⊃DMASM, corresponding to the emission of the rigidified organic linker and the confined DMASM, respectively, exhibit different behavior: the intensities of the corresponding emissions are changed when the temperature increases from 20 to 80 °C. As shown in Fig. 3a, the emission intensity of the rigidified linker (492 nm, IL) from composite 1 significantly increased with temperature, while that of the DMASM (604 nm, ID) slightly decreased. Fig. S8a (ESI†) shows that the normalized intensities of the corresponding emissions show temperature dependence. As mentioned, DMASM in the pores of the host framework is sensitized by the linker, and its emission depends basically on the host–guest interaction. Thus, an enhancement of the emission intensity from the linker within composite 1 can be an energy back-transfer process, where energy is transferred from DMASM to the rigidified linker and it enhances with the temperature via the Förster transfer mechanism. The temperature dependent emissions of composites 2 and 3 are shown in Fig. 3b and c, respectively. Similar behaviors were observed in these two composites, but they exhibit different extents in the change of the corresponding emissions, which can be attributed to the matching degree of the polarized benzothiadiazole moieties of the linker with different amounts of DMASM in the pores. That is, the concentration of DMASM cations in the pores can alleviate the confinement effect of DMASM by the scaffold, and can thus affect the energy transfer and quenching processes in the ZJU-21⊃DMASM systems, leading to different PL performance of the composites 1, 2, and 3 when the temperature increases from 20 to 80 °C.


Fig. 3 Emission spectra of ZJU-21⊃DMASM with dye contents of (a) 0.046% and (b) 0.009% and (c) 0.005% between 20 and 80 °C excited at 366 nm.

The intensity ratio of the two independent emissions is generally applied as the thermometric parameter in ratiometric luminescence temperature measurement. This parameter can be defined as the intensity ratio (Δ = IL/ID) of the rigidified linker (IL) and the confined DMASM (ID) of ZJU-21⊃DMASM, and can determine the temperature from the emission spectra without additional calibration. As depicted in Fig. 4a, the intensity ratios Δ of composites 1, 2, and 3 as a function of temperature (T) exhibit excellent linear relationships in their given temperature ranges and can be fitted as three linear functions

 
T1 = 139.86Δ1 + 0.7745(1)
 
T2 = 47.824Δ2 + 0.0404(2)
 
T3 = 17.762Δ3 + 13.3(3)
with the correlation coefficients of R12 = 0.966, R22 = 0.997, and R32 = 0.986, respectively. Accordingly, they are excellent luminescent thermometers in the physiological temperature range. Obviously, the composites ZJU-21⊃DMASM with different DMASM contents of 0.046%, 0.009% and 0.005% exhibit different temperature sensing ranges of 20 to 50 °C, 25 to 65 °C, and 35 to 80 °C, respectively, implying that the operating temperature can range from 20 to 80 °C and can be modulated by changing the concentration of DMASM in the MOF ZJU-21. This property is quite different from that of the well-known Tb3+, Eu3+ systems where the range cannot be readily manipulated even by controlling the amount ratio of the Tb3+ and Eu3+ ions.


Fig. 4 Temperature-dependent (a) intensity ratio Δ of DMASM to ZJU-21 and the fitted curve; (b) the relative sensitivity (Sr) of composites 1, 2, and 3 (ZJU-21⊃DMASM with three dye contents).

The generalized relative sensitivity (Sr), as a figure of merit of ratiometric luminescence thermometry, can be defined as

 
Sr = (∂Δ∂T)/Δ(4)
where Δ and T represent the intensity ratio and temperature, respectively. According to the definition, the relative sensitivity range of composites 1, 2, and 3 is from 2.03 to 5.20% °C−1, 1.54 to 4.01% °C−1, and 1.50 to 4.61% °C−1, respectively (Fig. 4b), which are much higher than the maximum sensitivity of the previously reported MOF thermometers, such as Tb0.99Eu0.01(BDC)1.5(H2O)2 (0.37% °C−1),12a Tb0.8Eu0.2BPDA (1.19% °C−1),12c and ZJU-88⊃perylene (1.28% °C−1).14 Apparently, the improvement of the sensitivity is due to the significant increase of linker emission based on the energy back-transfer process in ZJU-21⊃DMASM, as discussed before. More importantly, the different sensitivity ranges of the three composites also demonstrate that the sensitivity can be modulated by controlling the concentration of DMASM. The temperature resolution (calculated from signal-to-noise ratios by a fluorescence spectrometer) for composites 1, 2 and 3 were determined to be 0.0098, 0.0129, and 0.0133 °C, respectively, which are precise enough for detecting the major pathological cells (e.g. brain tumour cells) in the surrounding normal tissue.14 Thus, such composites can be potentially useful in biomedical diagnosis.

The emission of ZJU-21⊃DMASM is illustrated by the CIE chromaticity diagram coordinates, as shown in Fig. 5. The luminescence colors of the three composites can be systematically modulated in their corresponding temperature ranges, which can be directly and clearly observed by the naked eye and the digital camera. Such a system is therefore very useful as a sensitive colorimetric thermometer for real-time visualization in the physiological range.


Fig. 5 CIE chromaticity coordinates of composites 1, 2, and 3 (ZJU-21⊃DMASM with three dye contents) in their corresponding temperature range (excited at 366 nm).

We note that although plenty of luminescent temperature probes based on lanthanide metal–organic frameworks, nanoparticles or quantum dots, have been developed,1–3,10–14 such systems do not have a tunable operating range and sensitivity for temperature measurements even if the amount ratio of lanthanides/emitters in the sensors is changed. In contrast, the sensing range and sensitivity of ZJU-21⊃DMASM can be readily modulated by changing the amounts of DMASM in the host framework which is controlled by the dye-exchange process. To the best of our knowledge it is the first dual-emitting MOF⊃dye using the intensity ratio of the organic linker and dye as the self-calibrated thermometric parameter for temperature sensing. In contrast to the existing luminescent thermometers, it also has a tunable range and sensitivity in the physiological regime.

3. Conclusions

In conclusion, the organic ligand 5,5′-(benzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole-4,7-diyl) diisophthalic acid containing benzothiadiazole as the polarized functional site was used for the synthesis of the novel NBO-type luminescent MOF ZJU-21, which can effectively encapsulate the luminescent DMASM dyes into the pores and sensitize it to yield the dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite ZJU-21⊃DMASM. Its luminescence color can be tuned from green to red upon adjustment of the amounts of DMASM. The unique energy transfer between the host linker and captured DMASM shows that ZJU-21⊃DMASM exhibits a highly temperature-dependent luminescence intensity ratio and much higher relative sensitivity than that of the previously reported thermometers in the physiological temperature range. More importantly, the sensing range and relative sensitivity of such a MOF⊃dye system can be readily modulated by controlling the concentration of DMASM in the pores of the host framework, which is also the first realization of such modulation in luminescence thermometry. The richness of luminescent organic dyes and porous MOFs offers wide choices for realizing a great many MOF⊃dye composites, whose emission range can be elaborately designed and expanded to tune the energy transfer between the MOF and dye, and thus to optimize the properties of the dual-emitting MOF⊃dye composite. The scheme proposed here provides a guide for designing MOF⊃dye ratiometric temperature sensors with a tunable temperature sensing range, and should promote further research on solid state MOF⊃luminescent dyes and composite materials for sensing applications.

4. Experimental

4.1 Measurements and characterization

All the reagents were commercially available and used without further purification. 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Advance DMX 500 spectrometer using tetramethylsilane (TMS) as an internal standard. Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses were performed on a Netzsch TG209F3 with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were carried out on an EA1112 microelemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded in the 2θ = 5–50° range on an X'Pert PRO diffractometer with Cu Kα (λ = 1.542 Å) radiation at room temperature. Luminescence spectra for the samples were recorded on an Edinburgh Instrument F920 spectrometer with a PolyScience Temperature Control Solution PD07R-20 under room conditions, wherein both the excitation and emission slits are 1.0 nm. The quantum yields of the samples were recorded by an absolute method using an integrating sphere (150 mm diameter, BaSO4 coating) on an Edinburgh Instrument F920 spectrometer. Single-crystal data were recorded on a Bruker Smart Apex II diffractometer equipped with a graphite monochromator and an Atlas detector at 296 K. The determination of the unit cells and data collections for the crystal of ZJU-21 were performed using CrysAlisPro.22 The data sets were corrected by empirical absorption correction using spherical harmonics, implemented in the SCALE3 ABSPACK scaling algorithm. The structure was solved by direct methods, and refined by the full-matrix least-square method with the SHELX-2013 program package.23 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters, and all H atoms on C atoms were generated geometrically and refined with isotropic thermal parameters.

4.2 Synthesis of the organic ligand H4L

To a mixture of dimethyl 5-(pinacolboryl)isophthalate (5.34 g, 16.32 mmol), 4,7-dibromobenzo[c][1,2,5]thiadiazole (2.0 g, 6.8 mmol), K2CO3 (24 g, 240 mmol) and Pd(PPh3)2Cl2 (0.30 g, 0.4 mmol) were added 1,4-dioxane (50 mL). The mixture was stirred under reflux in an argon atmosphere for 72 h, and then extracted with water and CH2Cl2, and dried under vacuum. The crude product was purified by column chromatography and then hydrolyzed with 6 M NaOH, filtered and acidified with concentrated HCl to obtain the target compound as a yellow solid. Yield: 4.55 g (9.79 mmol, 60%). 1H NMR (500 MHz, DMSO-d6): δ 13.47 (s, 4H, OH), 8.81 (d, J = 1 Hz, 4H, Ar-CH), 8.57 (s, 2H, Ar-CH), 8.15 (s, 2H, Ar-CH);

4.3 Synthesis of the MOF ZJU-21

A mixture of H4L (5.0 mg, 10.7 μmol) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (10.0 mg, 33.6 μmol) was dissolved in DMF/acetonitrile (3 mL, 9:1, v/v) in a screw-capped vial (5 mL). The vial was capped and heated at 85 °C for 48 hours. The resulting pale-yellow crystals were collected by filtration and washed with DMF several times, with the yield of 63%. Anal. Calcd for C22H12O10N2SZn2: C, 37.89; H, 5.77; N, 8.36. Found: C, 37.97; H, 5.633; N, 8.29.

4.4 Preparation of the dye-encapsulated MOF

Crystals of the MOF ZJU-21 were immersed in DMASM in DMF with different concentrations at 60 °C for 2 hours to yield a series of ZJU-21⊃DMASM composites with different DMASM contents. The products were collected by filtration, washed with DMF and ethanol several times to remove residual DMASM on the surface of ZJU-21, and dried at 60 °C for 1 hour.
