In situ resolved ligand chirality in preparing a chiral 3D zinc–cyclopentane–tetracarboxylate based coordination polymer†

Yen-Hsiang Liu *, Chia-Chien Peng , Szu-Hsuan Lee , Po-Hsiu Chien and Pei-Chen Wu
Department of Chemistry, Fu Jen Catholic University, New Taipei City 24205, Taiwan. E-mail: 056461@mail.fju.edu.tw

Received 14th June 2013 , Accepted 2nd October 2013

First published on 8th October 2013

A new chiral, three-dimensional coordination polymer, [Zn2(CPTC)(TBPE)(H2O)]·1.75H2O (FJU-5), (H4CPTC = cyclopentane-1,2,3,4-tetracarboxylic acid, TBPE = trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethylene) belonging to the chiral space group P212121 with an orthorhombic unit cell was prepared under pH controlled hydrothermal conditions. In situ resolved ligand chirality from the original meso-form of the CPTC ligand, which undergoes a configurational conversion during the synthesis of FJU-5, is responsible for the formation of the chiral pillared-layer 3D network. 1D hydrophilic channels consisting of guest water molecules are located within the chiral 3D network. The 3D framework of FJU-5 is highly robust and remains intact even after the removal of both the guest and coordinated water molecules by dehydration, as evidenced by powder X-ray diffraction and thermogravimetric analyses. The dehydration–moisture absorption behaviour of FJU-5 is reversible.

Introduction

The design and synthesis of chiral coordination polymers via the assembly of metal ions and bridging ligands has received considerable attention, owing to their potential applications as functional materials and their fascinating network topologies.1–3 Although conformation-flexible organic linkers make it more difficult to predict the resulting network topology of the synthesized coordination polymers,4 some progress has been achieved as the result of studies of coordination polymers containing organic carboxylate ligands with flexible molecular conformations, both in terms of the versatility of network topologies and stereochemistry in the area of crystal engineering design of coordination polymers.5

Very recently, rare examples of in situ conformation conversions under specific reaction conditions of tetrahydrofuran-2,3,4,5-tetracarboxylic acid (H4THFTC),6 and cyclopentane-1,2,3,4-tetracarboxylic acid (H4CPTC) have been reported.7 In such studies, the ligand chirality could be generated through breaking the mirror plane of symmetry of the meso-ligand.6 In general, pairs of enantiomers generated from the original meso-form ligands of H4THFTC and H4CPTC molecules can co-exist in one crystal structure (Scheme 1). Therefore, the resulting solid-state coordination network structure usually crystallizes in a centrosymmetric space group, as has been reported in the literature.


Scheme 1 CPTC ligand configurations reported in the literature.

In our continuing interests in the area of crystal engineering design of metal–carboxylate coordination polymers,8 we report herein on the preparation of a new chiral three-dimensional zinc coordination polymer, [Zn2(C9H6O8)(C12H10N2)(H2O)]·1.75H2O (FJU-5), consisting of the H4CPTC ligand and an auxiliary trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethylene (TBPE) ligand, which belongs to a chiral space group P212121 of an orthorhombic unit cell. To the best of our knowledge, FJU-5 represents the first example of a CPTC-containing chiral coordination network with one-type of chiral CPTC ligand presented in one crystal structure reported to date (Scheme 2).


Scheme 2 The chiral-form of the CPTC ligand generated in situ from the meso-form CPTC in the synthesis of FJU-5.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All chemicals were purchased commercially and were used as received without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed using a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses were performed on a Thermo Cahn Versa Therm TG analyzer with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were obtained using step mode with a fixed time of 5 s and a step size of 0.02° in 2θ on a Philips X'Pert powder X-ray diffractometer at 40 kV, 30 mA for Cu-Kα (λ = 1.5406 Å). Infrared spectra were obtained using a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum 100 FTIR spectrometer equipped with attenuated total reflection infrared apparatus (ATR-IR).

Synthesis of [Zn2(C9H6O8)(C12H10N2)(H2O)]·1.75H2O (FJU-5)

A mixture of cis,cis,cis-cyclopentanetetracarboxylic acid (H4CPTC, 122.2 mg, 0.5 mmol), trans-1,2-bis(4-pyridyl)-ethylene (TBPE, 91.6 mg, 0.5 mmol), NaHCO3 (40.9 mg, 0.5 mmol), and 6.0 mL distilled water were added to a 20 mL beaker. The zinc salt Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (302.3 mg, 1.0 mmol) was dissolved in 4.0 mL of distilled water. The two solutions were mixed together with an initial pH of about 3.5, and was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless vessel, which was then heated at 105 °C for 60 h under hydrothermal conditions. The process resulted in the formation of colorless columnar crystals of FJU-5 in a 36.3% yield (mg, mmol), based on the H4CPTC ligand. The solid product was washed with distilled water and isolated on a filter. The phase purity of the crystalline FJU-5 was verified by powder X-ray diffraction. The PXRD pattern of the bulk products are in good agreement with a pattern simulated from single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for FJU-5 (Fig. S1†). Elemental analysis confirmed the stoichiometry of FJU-5. Anal. calcd: C 41.71, H 3.58, N 4.63% for the formula: [Zn2(C9H6O8)(C12H10N2)(H2O)]·1.75H2O. Found: C 42.02, H 3.52, N 4.66%. IR (ATR-IR): ν = 3487 (w), 3326 (m), 2964 (w), 1601 (vs), 1535 (vs), 1510 (s), 1456 (m), 1418 (vs), 1344 (w), 1303 (s), 1257 (w), 1220 (w), 1072 (w), 1049 (w), 1034 (m), 1024 (w), 1001 (m), 862 (m), 841 (s), 805 (w), 761 (m), 730 (m) cm−1.

Dehydration–moisture-absorption measurements

A freshly ground sample of FJU-5 was heated to 120 °C at a rate of 2 °C min−1 in a thermogravimetric apparatus. The dehydrated sample of FJU-5 was then allowed to cool naturally to room temperature, and, after cooling, was stored in a moisture-rich vessel for 48 h to permit the re-absorption of moisture. The dehydration–moisture-absorption process was repeated four times.

Results and discussion

Preparation

To accomplish diverse ligand transformation/reaction processes in situ under the reaction conditions, a hydro(solvo)thermal method was utilized for producing distinctively structural or functional coordination polymers12 that are not easily obtained through conventional methods. With the help of higher reaction pressure and temperature, hydro(solvo)thermal methods not only increase the solubility of reaction solvents, but also provide a reaction environment for inducing the in situ transformation or reactivity of organic ligands, which play an essential role in constructing novel coordination polymers.

The compound [Zn2(CPTC)(TBPE)(H2O)]·1.75H2O]n (FJU-5) was successfully synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. NaHCO3 was used to adjust the pH of the reaction solution. The phase purity of the bulk products was independently confirmed by elemental microanalysis, PXRD, and TGA. On the basis of the single-crystal X-ray diffraction and elemental analysis results, the formula of FJU-5 is confirmed.

Structural description

The solid-state structure of FJU-5 was determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. The crystallographic asymmetric unit contains two crystallographically distinct zinc ions, one CPTC ligand, one TBPE ligand, one coordinated water molecule, and 1.75 guest water molecules with the formula of [Zn2(CPTC)(TBPE)(H2O)]·1.75H2O]n. All of the carboxyl groups of the CPTC ligand are fully deprotonated, thus achieving charge neutrality with the two Zn2+ ions.

The ligand coordination modes of TBPE and CPTC are shown in Fig. 1. Each TBPE ligand is μ2-bridged and is coordinated to one Zn(1) ion and one Zn(2) ion. On the other hand, the CPTC ligand is coordinated to three Zn(1) ions and two Zn(2) ions. It is interesting that the originally achiral CPTC ligand changes to a chiral configuration under the reaction conditions used, as revealed by the crystallographic analysis. Unlike other compounds reported in the literature, it is significant that the CPTC ligand in the crystal structure of FJU-5 has only one type of ligand chirality in one crystal sample. As a result, FJU-5 crystallizes in a chiral space group P212121 of orthorhombic unit cell.


Fig. 1 The ligand coordination modes: (a) TBPE; and (b) CPTC. (Zn(1): green sphere; Zn(2): yellow sphere).

In FJU-5, the Zn(1) ion is present in an tetrahedral coordination environment: it is coordinated by one pyridyl group of the TBPE ligand, and three monodentate carboxylate groups from three different CPTC ligands (Fig. S2-a†). A Zn–N bond length of 2.02 Å, and Zn–O bond lengths ranging from 1.94–1.99 Å are observed. In addition, a slightly longer Zn⋯O distance of 2.74 Å can be seen. The Zn(2) ion is coordinated by one pyridyl group of the TBPE ligand, two monodentate carboxylate groups from the same CPTC ligand, one chelating carboxylate group from another CPTC ligand, and one coordinated water molecule and is present in an octahedral coordination environment (Fig. S2-b†). The Zn–N bond length of 2.10 Å, and Zn–O bond lengths ranging from 2.04–2.30 Å are observed.

As shown in Fig. 2, the Zn(1) and Zn(2) ions are bridged by carboxylate groups to form a dimetal unit, and these dimetal units are bound by the chiral CPTC ligands, which results in the formation of a helical chain along the two-fold screw axis in the crystallographic b-direction.


Fig. 2 A view of the 1-D helical chain along the two-fold screw axis in the [010] direction of FJU-5. Inset: a view of the dimetal unit formed by carboxylate-bridged Zn(1) tetrahedra and Zn(2) octahedra.

Furthermore, the carboxylate groups of the CPTC ligands also serve as a link for the helical chains to form a two-dimensional chiral layer network (the helix-bound layer) expanding along the crystallographic ab-plane (Fig. 3). The TBPE ligands serve as pillars that connect the chiral layers and lead to a three-dimensional framework of FJU-5. As shown in Fig. 4, guest water molecules are located within the one-dimensional channels of FJU-5.


Fig. 3 A view of the 2-D chiral layer (the helix-bound layer) along the ab-plane of FJU-5.

Fig. 4 (a) A topological view of the 3-D layer-pillared net of FJU-5; (b) the layer-pillared 3-D chiral network of FJU-5 contains 1-D hydrophilic channels in the [100] direction. Large aqua sphere: the guest water molecule.

It is noteworthy that the meso-form the CPTC ligand, used as the starting material, was in situ converted into the chiral configuration FJU-5 under the reaction conditions employed (Scheme 2). Possible explanations for the conversion of the configuration of the CPTC ligand have been discussed in the literature.6 In a literature search, enantiomeric pairs of the chiral-form CPTC ligands that were generated in situ from the meso-form CPTC ligands (Scheme 1) generally co-exist in one crystal structure, and the final crystal structures are crystallized in an centrosymmetric space group as listed in Table 2. The findings herein indicate that FJU-5 crystallizes in a chiral space group (P212121), as only one type of chiral-form of the CPTC ligand was present in one crystal structure. Solid-state circular dichroism analyses of bulk samples of FJU-5 from randomly selected batches of experimental products indicated that each type of in situ generated chiral form of the CPTC ligand, as shown in Scheme 2, can form chiral FJU-5 single crystals with opposite chirality equally easily, although it was not possible to verify from the appearance of the crystal sample using an optical microscope.

Thermal stability, dehydration–moisture-absorption analyses, and PXRD observation of network integrity

Thermogravimetric measurements of FJU-5 were carried out at temperatures between 25 and 900 °C, and the results are consistent with the crystallographic observations. The observation of a weight loss of 8.1% (calcd. 8.2%) before 330 °C accounted for by the release of 1.75 guest water molecules as well as 1 coordinated water molecule per unit formula was observed, and FJU-5 began to decompose after 330 °C (Fig. S3†).

The network stability of FJU-5 upon dehydration was also investigated. A solid sample of FJU-5 was heated at 120 °C to remove the water molecules that are monitored by thermogravimetric apparatus. As evidenced by optical microscopy images, the colorless, transparent single crystal sample of FJU-5 becomes cloudy upon dehydration. It was not possible to collect single crystal X-ray diffraction data on dehydrated crystals of FJU-5. Therefore, the dehydrated sample of FJU-5 was subjected to powder X-ray diffraction analysis. It is noteworthy that the three-dimensional network of the dehydrated FJU-5 remained intact upon the loss of both guest and coordinated water molecules as indicated by the powder X-ray diffraction diagram (Fig. S4†).

The dehydration–moisture-absorption behavior of FJU-5 was also examined. As shown in Fig. 5, the results of the dehydration–moisture-absorption processes indicated that all of the dehydration traces upon heating showed similar trends in percent weight loss.


Fig. 5 Dehydration curve monitored at 120 °C: (Δ) as-synthesized solids of FJU-5; (◇) water re-absorbed solids of FJU-5 from the first dehydration process; (○) water re-adsorbed solids of FJU-5 from the second dehydration process; (□) water re-adsorbed solids of FJU-5 from the third dehydration process. All dehydrated solids were stored in a moisture rich environment for 48 h before the dehydration analysis.

This similar dehydration–rehydration trend indicates that the empty ellipsoidal-shaped one-dimensional channels were able to take up moisture again after dehydration. It is also important to note that the dehydration-induced coordinatively unsaturated metal sites within the channel surfaces of FJU-5 were able to reabsorb moisture and regain its original metal coordination environment. This reversible dehydration–moisture-absorption behaviour is another interesting feature of FJU-5. As expected, the network integrity of FJU-5 is retained, even after four cycles of dehydration–moisture-absorption processes, as shown by PXRD analysis (Fig. 6), pointing to the robustness of the three-dimensional chiral open framework of FJU-5.


Fig. 6 PXRD patterns: (a) simulated and (b) the FJU-5 sample after four cycles of dehydration–moisture-absorption.

Photoluminescence of FJU-5

The photoluminescence properties of the free ligands and FJU-5 have been investigated in the solid state at room temperature. In the solid state, the free H4CPTC ligand is very weakly emitting, while the aromatic TBPE ligand shows a broad emission band (Fig S5†). For TBPE, the maximum emission peak is observed at about 365 nm upon excitation at 207 nm (Fig S5-b†). The crystalline powder sample of FJU-5 exhibits an emission profile similar to the TPBE ligand with a maximum at 355 nm upon excitation at 299 nm (Fig. S5-a†) can probably be assigned to the intra-ligand (π–π* or n–π*) fluorescent emission.13 The slightly shift from 365 nm to 355 nm relative to the free TPBE ligand may be considered as mainly originating from the coordination interactions between the zinc ions and the ligand since photoluminescence behavior is closely associated with the metal center and the ligands coordinated around it.13a,14

Conclusions

A new chiral 3D coordination polymer FJU-5 was successfully synthesized and characterized, both structurally and chemically. The conversion of the meso-form of the CPTC ligand into a chiral configuration leads to the formation of a chiral 3D network, which is fascinating and observed for the first time. This type of adaptation paves a new route for the design of chiral coordination polymers. Further studies in this direction are currently underway.
