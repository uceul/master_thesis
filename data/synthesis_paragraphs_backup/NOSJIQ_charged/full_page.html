Abstract

Two Ln26@CO3 (Ln=Dy and Tb) cluster‐based lanthanide–transition‐metal–organic frameworks (Ln MOFs) formulated as [Dy26Cu3(Nic)24(CH3COO)8(CO3)11(OH)26(H2O)14]Cl ⋅3 H2O (1; HNic=nicotinic acid) and [Tb26NaAg3(Nic)27(CH3COO)6(CO3)11(OH)26Cl(H2O)15]⋅7.5 H2O (2) have been successfully synthesized by hydrothermal methods and characterized by IR, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), elemental analysis, and single X‐ray diffraction. Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group Cc with a=35.775(12) Å, b=33.346(11) Å, c=24.424(8) Å, β=93.993(5)°, V=29065(16) Å3, whereas 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P with a=20.4929(19) Å, b=24.671(2) Å, c=29.727(3) Å, α=81.9990(10)°, β=88.0830(10)°, γ=89.9940(10)°, V=14875(2) Å3. Structural analysis indicates the framework of 1 is a 3D perovskite‐like structure constructed out of CO3@Dy26 building units and Cu+ centers by means of nicotinic acid ligand bridging. In 2, however, nanosized CO3@Tb26 units and [Ag3Cl]2+ centers are connected by Nic− bridges to give rise to a 2D structure. It is worth mentioning that this kind of 4d–4f cluster‐based MOF is quite rare as most of the reported analogous compounds are 3d–4f ones. Additionally, the solid‐state emission spectra of pure compound 2 at room temperature suggest an efficient energy transfer from the ligand Nic− to Tb3+ ions, which we called the “antenna effect”. Compound 2 shows a good two‐photon absorption (TPA) with a TPA coefficient of 0.06947 cm GM−1 (1 GM=10−50 cm4 s photon−1), which indicates that compound 2 might be a good choice for third‐order nonlinear optical materials.

Introduction

The synthesis and characterization of lanthanide (Ln) compounds continue to be an active research area not only for their intriguing and diverse architectures,1 but also for their potential applications.2 Over the past few decades, the chemistry of lanthanide coordination polymers has become well established. Recently, more chemists have begun paying attention to high‐nuclearity lanthanide clusters, which have potential applications in magnetic,[3] fluorescent,4 and electrical materials.5 So far, a number of this kind of cluster have been reported, such as pure Ln clusters Lnx (x=6–10, 12–15, 22, 24, 26, and 36), Ho48, Er60,6 and heterometallic clusters that contain up to 108 and 136 metal ions.7 Nevertheless, most of the resulting polynuclear clusters still possess discrete structures. Once a new high‐nuclearity cluster is obtained, the accompanying question is how to make it into extended multidimensional metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) to explore their optical properties. Although most of the MOFs are constructed by using both single 3d/4d and 4f ions as nodes, recently polynuclear metal clusters with substituted single metal atoms have been used as nodes for MOFs.8

Compared with single metal ions, the use of polynuclear metal clusters to construct MOFs can make the pore size larger and make the MOFs inherit interesting magnetic, fluorescent, and electrical properties from the introduced clusters.

To design this kind of cluster‐based MOFs, the key is to choose an appropriate multifunctional ligand to connect those discrete clusters. As we know, Ln and transition‐metal ions have different affinities for O and N donors, so ligands with both O and N atoms are the best choice. Isonicotinic acid (HiNic) and nicotinic acid (HNic) have been proven to be excellent bifunctional ligands and are widely used to construct new MOFs in which the carboxylate group favors coordination to lanthanide ions and the nitrogen atom is likely to bind to transition‐metal ions such as Cu+ and Ag+.

In our previous work, by utilizing HiNic as supporting ligand, we successfully synthesized {Dy26},{Gd26} and {Ho26},{Er26} in 2010 and 2012, respectively.9 Moreover, Gu et al. also reported a tetramer constructed by {Dy26} clusters and HiNic ligands in 2007.10 In this paper, on the basis of the well‐established synthesis conditions of Ln26, we substituted HiNic for HNic with the expectation of constructing Ln26‐HNic cluster‐based multidimensional MOFs with Ag+ or Cu+ ions as linkers. Following this idea, we report herein two new Ln26 cluster‐based MOFs formulated as [Dy26Cu3(Nic)24(CH3COO)8(CO3)11(OH)26(H2O)14]Cl⋅3 H2O (1) and [Tb26NaAg3(Nic)27(CH3COO)6(CO3)11(OH)26Cl(H2O)15]⋅7.5 H2O (2) with {Dy26} and {Tb26} as nodes, respectively. Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction reveals that compound 1 exhibits a three‐dimensional (3D) structure constructed out of Dy26@CO3 building units and Cu+ centers by means of Nic− ligand bridging. All the clusters were arranged in a certain geometric configurations and exhibited a perovskite‐like structure, whereas compound 2 showed a 2D structure that is constructed out of Tb26@CO3 units and [Ag3Cl]2− centers further connected by Nic− bridges. To the best of our knowledge, most of the reports on multidimensional Lnx cluster‐based MOFs are built from Lnx clusters and 3d‐block metals such as Cu,8 thus a 4d–4f compound such as 2 is relatively rare.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis

It is well known that during a specific hydrothermal synthesis, many factors can affect the nucleation and crystal growth of the final products, such as the initial reactants, starting concentrations of reactants, pH values, solvents, reaction temperature, and time. In our case, the initial reactant HCOOH played an important role in the formation of [Dy26Cu3(Nic)24(CH3COO)8(CO3)11(OH)26(H2O)14]Cl⋅ H2O and [Tb26NaAg3(Nic)27(CH3COO)6(CO3)11(OH)26Cl(H2O)15]⋅7.5 H2O. Although HCOOH is not included in the final structures, we cannot obtain the target compounds without it.

It is worth mentioning that we selected the insoluble salts Ag(CH3COO) rather than the water‐soluble salt AgNO3 in the synthesis of 2. Thus, the slow release of Ag+ ions can significantly prevent the reduction and oxidation under hydrothermal conditions, and can provide the chances for assembly with the Nic− ligands. Since CH3COO anions are included in the final structure of 2, we changed the Ag(CH3COO) to AgNO3 to study whether nitrate would affect the structure, but no crystals were obtained. When third‐auxiliary Cl− ligands are utilized, the linkages of Tb26 clusters and three Ag+ centers on the halide template through nicotinate ligands bridging were obtained, whereas in 1, all the Cl− ions were free and filled in the pores through weak interactions. The difficulty to produce such lanthanide clusters through the Ag+ center bridge in our previous work demonstrates the superior template effect and the efficient self‐assembly of the halide ions.

Crystal structure of 1

Single‐crystal X‐ray structural analysis revealed that CO3@Dy26 nanosized clusters crystallized in monoclinic space group C2/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 contains 26 Dy3+ ions, three Cu+ cations, 24 crystallographically unique Nic− ligands, eight CH3COO− ligands, 11 CO32− anions, 26 μ3‐OH groups, 17 water molecules, and one Cl− anion (Figure 1). As shown in Figure 2a, the nanosized cluster core of 26 Dy is encapsulated by a coordination sphere formed by 19 Nic− and eight acetate ligands as well as 14 coordination water molecules and is further reinforced through ten CO32− anions by DyO coordination bonds just like the reported Ln26 clusters in our previous works.9 In addition to the ten‐coordinated CO32−, there is a free CO32− that is encapsulated in the Dy26 backbone (Figure 2a, b). The unexpected free carbonate is probably the byproduct of ligand decomposition.11 Among the 26 Dy ions, Dy3, Dy17, Dy21, and Dy22 are nine‐coordinated, and the remaining 22 Dy centers are all eight‐coordinated by terminal water molecules, μ3‐OH groups, ligands including Nic−, CO32−, and CH3COO−. Compared with the reported Ln26 clusters, Nic− ligands are used to coordinate Dy3+ and keep the Dy26 cluster stable. In all reported Ln26 clusters, the IN− ligand was chosen, whereas the Nic− ligand was first used to stabilize the Dy26 cluster in 1.

A better way of appreciating the regular core structure is to divide the 26 Dy units into two parts: five Dy4(μ3‐OH)4 units and an additional six Dy centers (Dy3, Dy16, Dy17, Dy21, Dy22, Dy26). The cubane‐like Dy4(μ3‐OH)4 unit, connected by four μ3‐OH groups, is very common in other lanthanide clusters.6a, 12 For clarity, each of the three Dy4(μ3‐OH)4 units represented as tetrahedrons are joined together by a Dy center (Figure 3). The assembly of the inner shell is further templated by nine CO32− anions in mode II (Scheme 1), whereas one free CO32− anion is situated at the center of the Dy26 clusters. Then, the remaining CO32− anion acts as a terminal ligand to connect to two Dy centers in mode I.

As we know, when using N‐containing Nic− ligands under hydrothermal conditions, the Cu2+ ions are reduced to Cu+ ions, and the d10 configurations of the Cu+ ions tend to form tetrahedral coordination geometry with four‐coordination.10, 11 In the structure of 1, all the Cu+ centers are four‐coordinated through four N atoms of four Nic− ligands to show a tetrahedral coordination geometry (Figure 4b): [Dy26‐(Nic)2‐Cu‐(Nic‐Dy26)2] for Cu1 and Cu3, and [Dy26‐(Nic)3‐Cu‐Nic‐Dy26] for Cu2. As shown in Figure 4a, for Cu1 and Cu3, two nitrogen atoms from two Nic− ligands belong to the same Dy26, and complete the coordination sphere of the Cu+ ion, whereas the other two are linked to two different Dy26 clusters. However, the coordination mode of Cu2 is different. It acts as the bridge of two Dy26 clusters. The four bridging ligands are Nic−, among which three are bonded to one Dy26 cluster, whereas the fourth Nic− is due to the contribution of the other cluster. To the best of our knowledge, the coordination modes of [Dy26‐(Nic)n‐Cu‐(Nic)n‐Dy26] are very rare in the field of crystallogeometry in exhibiting this kind of bridge. The CuN bond length is between 1.977(11) and 2.169(17) Å, whereas the N‐Cu‐N bond angle lies in the range of 96.5(6)–120.6(5)°. They also perfectly agree with the bond angles and lengths reported before.13

Each {Dy26} cluster links six other neighboring cores to give an ordered three‐dimensional channel framework of 1 owing to the Nic− ligands and Cu centers bridging. Remarkably, the construction mode of the structure is significantly different from our previous work. Compared with our previously synthesized 0D [Ho26(IN)28(CH3COO)4(CO3)10(OH)26(H2O)18]⋅20 H2O and [Er26(IN)29(CH3COO)3(CO3)10(OH)26(H2O)19]⋅26 H2O,9b compound 1 shows a 3D structure upon introducing the 3d transition‐metal Cu+ ions. If we take every {Dy26} cluster as a six‐connected node, the compound can be abstractly viewed as an perovskite‐like structure along the a axis (Figure 5). Meanwhile, four adjacent {Dy26} clusters connect to each other through the [Cu(Nic)n] groups, thereby constructing a channel. The cross‐section of the channel (Figure 6) with dimensions of about 13×14 Å is occupied by terminal ligands of Nic− and CH3COOH−, coordinated water, lattice water, and one free Cl−, which is essential for the electroneutrality of the crystal framework. Details on hydrogen bonds in 1 are given in Table S3 of the Supporting Information.

Crystal structure of 2

Compared with the 3D perovskite‐like structure of 1, we present the different topological 2D structure of [Tb26NaAg3(Nic)27(CH3COO)6(CO3)11(OH)26Cl(H2O)15]⋅7.5 H2O (2). Compound 2 crystallizes in triclinic space group P(2), as shown in Figure 7. Its asymmetric unit consists of 26 Tb3+ ions, three Ag+ cations, 27 crystallographically unique Nic− ligands, six CH3COO− ligands, 11 CO32− anions, 26 μ3‐OH groups, 22.5 water molecules, one Cl− anion, and one Na+ cation, which is different from 1. However, the metal core of compound 2 CO3@Tb26 is quite similar to that of 1 (Figures 8 and 9). The very modest difference is that all the Tb ions are connected by nine CO32− anions in mode II (Scheme 1), whereas {Dy26} uses mode I. In addition, although the Tb compounds showed excellent optical properties, the Tb26 cluster has not been reported. Compound 2 is the first example to include a nanosized Tb26 cluster.

In the structure of 2, each Ag+ center is four‐coordinated through three N atoms from three Nic− ligands (AgN 2.2312(13)–2.2755(10) Å) and one Cl− ion (AgCl 2.2662(44)–2.7448(44) Å) (Figure 10c). The [Ag3Cl]2+ entity, which is coordinated by nine Nic− ligands as depicted in Figure 10b, is located at an inversion center, and acts as a bridge of three {Tb26} clusters. Notably, the single Cl− ion is not coplanar with the three Ag+ ions, and the distance is 0.83(2) Å (Figure 10d). For the sake of clarity, we have named the three clusters cluster I, cluster II, and cluster III. As shown in Figure 10a, five Nic− ligands donated by cluster I are arranged as follows: two for Ag1, two for Ag3, and the last one for Ag2. Then, a single Nic− ligand from cluster II completes the coordination sphere of Ag1. Finally, among the other three Nic− ligands from cluster III, two are for Ag2, and the last one is for Ag1 to complete their coordination. Interestingly, along the [110] direction, each pair of Nic− ligands links the adjacent Tb26 clusters to give rise to a zigzag chain (Figure 11a), which is very rare among the lanthanide coordination complexes. However, this is not the first observation of such chains in our work; previously, interesting and analogous zigzag chains constructed by {Dy26} and bridging Zn centers have been realized.9a Then, the [‐CO3@Tb26‐(Nic)2‐]n zigzag chains are further connected to each other through [Ag3Cl]2+ bridges to form a 2D 4d–4f heterometallic layer. Compared with our previously synthesized Zn1.5Dy26(IN)25(CH3COO)8(CO3)11(OH)26(H2O)29 and Zn1.5Gd26(IN)26(CH3COO)7(CO3)11(OH)26(H2O)28, which exhibit 3D structures,9a we report here successful replacement of divalent 3d transition‐metal Zn2+ for the 4d transition‐metal Ag+ in 2, which shows a 2D structure by utilizing Cl− ions and Nic− ligands. All the Tb centers can be viewed as five‐connected nodes, and all the [Ag3Cl]2+ bridges can be viewed as three‐connected nodes, thus the 2D topology of compound 2 is obtained (Figure 11b). It should be stated that although some reports on multidimensional Lnx cluster‐based MOFs have been established,8 most of them used 3d‐block metals such as Cu. In this paper, with the help of third‐auxiliary Cl− ligands, we successfully obtained the 4d–4f cluster‐based MOF 2, which is quite rare. Details on the hydrogen bonds in 2 are given in Table S4 of the Supporting Information.

Powder X‐ray diffraction

The experimental and simulated X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of 1 and 2 are shown in Figures S1 and S2 of the Supporting Information. The experimental XRD patterns are very consistent with the calculated patterns, thus implying that both of the compounds are phase pure.

IR spectrum

The IR spectra of 1 and 2 are presented in Figures S3 and S4 of the Supporting Information. The broad band in the range of 3639–3129 cm−1 for 1 and 3670–2720 cm−1 for 2 can be ascribed to a hydroxyl stretching vibration from the ligands and water molecule. Meanwhile, the four strong peaks at 1604–1411 cm−1 for 1 and 1600–1410 for 2 attest to the existence of phenyl in Nic−. Bands at 831–698 cm−1 (1) and 830–629 cm−1 (2) are associated with ν(DyO), ν(CuN) (1), and ν(TbO), ν(AgN) (2).

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

As shown in Figure S5 of the Supporting Information, thermal analysis indicates that the total weight loss of 1 is 43.94 % (calculated 47.27 %) in the range of 30 to 1100 °C. The weight loss of 2.92 % from 30 to 280 °C corresponds to the loss of all free and coordinated H2O (the calculated value is 3.49 %). The weight loss of 41.02 % in the range of 280–1100 °C suggests the removal of CH3COO−, Nic−, and CO32− groups (calculated 43.78 %).

Thermal analysis of 2 is presented in Figure S6 of the Supporting Information. The total weight of compound 2 decreases by 46.50 % from 30 to 1100 °C (calculated 48.81 %). The first‐step loss of 3.16 % in the range of 30 to 270 °C indicates the removal of free and coordinated H2O (calculated 4.28 %). The last loss of 43.34 % (calculated 44.53 %) suggests the removal of the CH3COO−, Nic−, and CO32− ligands.

Optical properties of 2

As shown in Figure 12, the solid‐state emission spectra of pure compound 2 at room temperature was measured under excitation at 342 nm. The characteristic luminescence of Tb3+ suggested that the strong emission bands observed between 490 and 622 nm are ascribed to the 5D4→7FJ (J=6, 5, 4, 3) transitions. The two intense emission bands at 490 and 545 nm are attributed to the 5D4→7F6 and 5D4→7F5 transitions, whereas the weak emission bands at 584 and 622 nm are due to the 5D4→7F4 and 5D4→7F3 transitions. No emission that arises from the ligand could be detected, thus suggesting an efficient energy transfer from the ligand Nic− to Tb3+ ions, which we called the “antenna effect”.14, 15 However, in our previous work, all the {Ln26} clusters exhibited luminescence of IN−.9 The strong fluorescence efficiency of 2 is attributed to the coordination of Nic− to Tb3+, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by thermal vibrations.

The open‐aperture Z‐scan method with femtosecond laser pulse and a Ti:95 sapphire system (760 nm, 80 Hz, 140 fs) was used to measure the TPA values and TPA absorption coefficient (β) of [Tb26NaAg3(Nic)27(CH3COO)6(CO3)11(OH)26Cl(H2O)15]⋅7.5 H2O. Conclusion

In conclusion, we successfully synthesized two novel Ln26 cluster‐based MOFs through hydrothermal methods. In compound 1, typical CO3@Dy26 building units and Cu+ centers are linked by Nic− ligands to exhibit a 3D perovskite‐like structure, whereas compound 2 is constructed out of CO3@Tb26 units and [Ag3Cl]2− centers further connected by Nic− bridges. To the best of our knowledge, this type of 4d–4f cluster‐based MOF is very rare relative to the reported analogous compounds, which are almost all 3d–4f. The solid‐state emission spectra of compound 2 show the characteristic luminescence of Tb3+. In addition, we also studied the TPA of both compounds. It is a pity that no TPA was found in 1, whereas compound 2 shows a good two‐photon absorption with a TPA absorption coefficient (β) of 0.06947 cm GM−1, which indicates that compound 2 is an excellent candidate for third‐order nonlinear optical materials. Also, this is the first time that the nonlinear optical properties have been investigated for huge Lnx cluster compounds.

Experimental Section

Materials and methods

All chemicals purchased were of reagent grade and used without further purification. The IR spectra of compounds 1 and 2 were recorded using a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer with pressed KBr pellets in the 4000–500 cm−1 regions. The C, H, and N elemental analyses were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses were carried out under an N2 atmosphere using a Diamond thermogravimetric analyzer from 30 to 1100 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Single‐crystal structure determination was performed using a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD diffractometer at 293 K and a sealed‐tube X‐ray source (MoKα radiation, λ=0.71073 Å) operating at 50 kV and 30 mA. The solid‐state emission/excitation spectra of compound 2 were measured using a FP‐6500 spectrofluorimeter equipped with a 450 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. TPA cross‐sections were obtained by using a Chameleon II femtosecond laser pulse and a Ti:95 sapphire system (680–1080 nm, 80 MHz, 140 fs).

Synthesis of 1

A mixture of Dy2O3 (0.1895 g, 0.5 mmol), Cu(OAc)2⋅2 H2O (0.033 g, 0.3 mmol), nicotinic acid (0.2414 g, 2.0 mmol), HCOOH (27.6 mg, 0.60 mmol), and H2O (8 mL) was stirred for 12 h in air until all reagents dissolved, and then the pH of the solution was adjusted to 2 with HCl (36–38 %). Finally, this solution was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and kept at 170 °C for seven days. Light yellow block crystals were obtained (115 mg, yield of 32.22 % based on Dy), which were washed with deionized water and dried at room temperature for 24 h. IR: =3617 (w), 3430 (s), 1602 (s), 1554 (vs), 1417 (w), 1203 (s), 1144 (w), 1013 (w), 833 (w), 757 (s), 700 (s), 664 cm−1 (s); elemental analysis calcd (%) for Dy26C171H180ClCu3N24O140: C 22.11, H 1.96, N 3.62; found: C 22.18, H 2.02, N 3.70.

Synthesis of 2

A mixture of Tb4O7 (0.1837 g, 0.2457 mmol), AgOAc (0.0367 g, 0.2199 mmol), nicotinic acid (0.2472 g, 2.241 mmol), NaCl (0.0049 g, 0.0838 mmol), HCOOH (27.6 mg, 0.60 mmol), and H2O (8 mL) was stirred for 12 h in air until all reagents dissolved, and then the pH of the solution was adjusted to 2.5 with HNO3 (50 %). Finally, this solution was sealed in a 25 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and kept at 170 °C for seven days. Light yellow block crystals were obtained (114 mg, yield of 31.16 % based on Tb), which were washed with deionized water and dried at room temperature for 24 h. Single‐crystal X‐ray structural determination

Single crystals of both compounds were carefully selected under a microscope and glued at the tip of a thin glass fiber with cyanoacrylate adhesive. Both data were performed using a Bruker Apex2 CCD equipped with a normal focus at 150 K, and a sealed tube X‐ray source (MoKα radiation, λ=0.71073 Å) operating at 50 kV and 30 mA. Both structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least squares using the SHELX97 program package. Because nanosized Ln26 clusters are huge, solvent‐accessible voids are very common in this system. We have checked different maps, but no additional solvent molecules were found. All the metal atoms were refined anisotropically, while the remaining O, C, and N atoms of the organic ligands for both compounds were refined isotropically. The hydrogen atoms of the organic moieties were included in calculated positions, assigned isotropic displacement parameters, and allowed to ride on their parent atoms.
