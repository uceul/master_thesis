    Abstract

    The rational selection of the templating agents or structure-directing agents (SDAs) is important for the designed synthesis of open-framework materials with specific channel structures. By utilizing organic amines that are predicted by computer simulations as the templates or SDAs, such as cyclohexylamine (CHA), cyclopentylamine (CPA), and n-propylamine, three new open-framework zinc phosphites, including (C6H14N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ2), (C5H12N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ3), and (C3H10N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ4), have been hydrothermally prepared with desired extra-large 24-ring channels analogous to (C4NH12)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ1). Their structures are characterized by single-crystal and powder X-ray diffraction, inductively coupled plasma (ICP), IR, and thermogravimetric analyses. These organic amines predicted based on nonbonding interactions of host–guest show good matching in the space and charge density with the host inorganic framework. As with ZnHPO-CJ1 and other known extra-large micropore materials with high framework charge density, the 24-rings of these compounds are assembled around multiple small amines, exemplifying the cooperative templating mechanism of multiple small organic cations in the formation of extra-large micropore open-framework materials. This work demonstrates a useful strategy toward the template-designed synthesis of microporous materials with extra-large micropores.

    Synopsis

    Three new open-framework zinc phosphites ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) possessing desired extra-large 24-ring channels of ZnHPO-CJ1 have been successfully prepared through selecting computationally predicted organic amines as the templates. The cooperative templating mechanism of multiple small organic cations in the formation of extra-large microporous materials has been proposed upon insight into the structures of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) and other extra-large microporous materials with high framework charge density.

    Zeolites and related inorganic open-framework materials have been widely used in the catalysis, adsorption, and separation due to their unique pore architectures. With the emerging demands of new materials, the synthesis of such materials with desired pore architectures, compositions, and functions is becoming increasingly important. Even though no great success has been achieved, several synthetic strategies have been developed in recent years.(1-4) One of the feasible approaches toward the synthesis of target materials is the template-designed synthetic approach in terms of nonbonding interactions and charge-density matching of the host inorganic framework and the guest template. Significantly, computer simulation techniques have greatly assisted in the prediction of the templating ability in the synthesis of zeolitic materials.(5-8) On the basis of computation, the suitable template candidates for the predetermined structures can be designed or predicted.
    In recent years, the synthesis of zeolites and inorganic open-framework materials with extra-large micropores larger than 12-rings has attracted increasing attention because such materials are desirable for shape-selective adsorption/separations and catalysis on big molecules with a size of 10–20 Å. Some phosphate-, phosphite-, and germanate-based materials with extra-large micropores have been prepared in the past few years.(9) Except for the recently discovered NTHU-5 and SU-61 both with 26-ring channels,(10) 24-ring window is the second largest pore opening occurring in the open-framework structures with extra-large micropores, and such structures are known as zinc phosphate ND-1,(11) gallophosphate NTHU-1,(12) germanates ASU-16,(13) FDU-4,(14) and FJ-1,(15) nickel phosphates VSB-1(16) and VSB-5,(17) zinc phosphite ZnHPO-CJ1,(18) and metal phosphite Cr-NKU-24,(19) while the upper-limit of pore opening in the known zeolites is the 18-rings of ITQ-33.(20) Although considerable progress has been made in the quest for extra-large micropore materials, and numerous hypothetical structures with extra-large micropores have been predicted, the designed synthesis of extra-large micropore materials with predetermined structures and compositions is still of great challenge.
    Zinc phosphite (C4NH12)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ1) was prepared in the hydrothermal system by using n-butylamine as the template.(18) Its open framework is made up of strictly alternating ZnO4 tetrahedra and HPO3 pseudopyramids to form parallel 24-ring and 8-ring channels extending along the crystallographic c axis. More recently, we have investigated the relationship of the host inorganic frameworks and the guest templates of ZnHPO-CJ1 and some known extra-large micropore materials by using computer simulation method.(21) Our work indicates that the templating ability of organic amines in the formation of such materials can be well evaluated in terms of the space matching, charge-density matching, and the nonbonding interactions of the host–guest. In this work, we present the synthesis of three open-framework zinc phosphites, denoted as ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4), with desired extra-large 24-ring channels of ZnHPO-CJ1 by using computationally predicted organic amines as the templates or SDAs. Upon investigation of the structures of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1–4) and other extra-large micropore materials, the cooperative templating mechanism has been proposed. This work will be helpful for the designed synthesis of extra-large micropore materials.

    Materials and Methods

    The reagents and solvents employed were commercially available and used as received without further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were collected on a Siemens D5005 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analysis was performed on a Perkin-Elmer Optima 3300DV spectrometer. Elemental analysis was conducted on a Perkin-Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out on a NETZSCH STA 449C TGA/DTA analyzer in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1.

    Synthesis of (C6H14N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ2)

    Typically, zinc acetate (0.22 g, 1 mmol), was first dissolved in the water (8 mL), and then cyclohexylamine (0.38–0.41 mL, 3.2–3.4 mmol) and phosphorous acid (0.205 g, 2.5 mmol, 85%) were added with stirring. A homogeneous reaction gel was formed after stirring for 2 h, and then was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave heated at 160 °C for 5 days under static conditions. The final product containing colorless, rod-shaped single crystals was washed with distilled water, and then dried in air. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) and elemental analysis (%) calcd for C6H16NO6P2Zn1.5 (358.22): Zn, 27.40; P, 19.31; C, 20.11; H, 4.47; N, 3.91. Found: Zn, 26.98; P, 18.85; C, 19.78; H, 4.05; N, 3.67.

    Synthesis of (C5H12N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ3)

    ZnHPO-CJ3 was synthesized by using a similar experimental procedure as described above except that cyclopentylamine (0.425 mL, 4.25 mmol) was used as the template and 0.226 g (2.75 mmol) of phosphorous acid was used. ICP and elemental analysis (%) calcd for C10H28N2O12P4Zn3 (688.33): Zn, 28.51; P, 18.00; C, 17.44; H, 4.07; N, 4.07. Found: Zn, 28.04; P, 17.63; C, 16.95; H, 3.93; N, 3.77.

    Synthesis of (C3H10N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ4)

    ZnHPO-CJ4 was synthesized in the reaction mixture of zinc acetate (0.22 g, 1 mmol), water (9 mL), n-propylamine (0.67−0.72 mL, 3.2–3.4 mmol), and phosphorous acid (0.205 g, 2.5 mmol) heated at 160 °C for 5 days. The colorless, rod-shaped small single crystals were obtained after washing with distilled water and drying in air. ICP and elemental analysis (%) calcd for C6H24N2O12P4Zn3 (636.31): Zn, 30.82; P, 19.49; C, 11.32; H, 3.77; N, 4.40. Found: Zn, 29.86; P, 18.85; C, 10.79; H, 3.56; N, 4.43.

    Single-Crystal Structure Determination

    Two suitable single crystals with dimensions 0.28 × 0.07 × 0.06 mm3, and 0.23 × 0.09 × 0.05 mm3 were selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction (XRD) analyses for ZnHPO-CJ2 and ZnHPO-CJ3, respectively. The data were collected on a Siemens SMART CCD diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at a temperature of 20 ± 2 °C. Data processing was accomplished with the SAINT processing program.(22) The two structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques with the SHELXTL97 crystallographic software package.(23) All Zn, P, and O atoms were determined directly, and C and N atoms were subsequently found in the difference Fourier map. The O(1), O(2), and O(3) atoms in ZnHPO-CJ2 were disordered over two positions with equal occupancy. The H atoms of the HPO3 groups were found in the final difference Fourier map, and H atoms of the organic amines were placed geometrically. All non-hydrogen atoms of the inorganic framework were refined anisotropically. Experimental details for crystal determinations of ZnHPO-CJ2 and ZnHPO-CJ3 are listed in Table 1.

    Simulation Method

    The simulation method used to predict the templating abilities of organic amines has been described in our previous work.(21) The ratios of framework charge/framework T number and template charge/template non-H number were calculated as the charge densities of host framework and organic template, respectively. Materials Studio software(24) was used to calculate the “free volume” of the ZnHPO-CJ1 and the “occupied volume” of guest organic molecules in free status. Cerius2 package(24) was used to calculate the nonbonding interactions between the host framework and the guest molecules based on the Burchart1.01-Dreiding2.21 force field.(25) More detailed information about the calculation could be found in ref (21).

    Syntheses

    In our previous work, the templating abilities of organic templates in the formation of extra-large micropore compounds have been well interpreted in terms of the space matching, charge-density matching, and the nonbonding interactions of the host–guest. Three organic amines, such as cyclohexylamine (CHA), cyclopentylamine (CPA), and n-propylamine, have been predicted as suitable templates for the synthesis of extra-large 24-ring zincphosphite ZnHPO-CJ1.(21) Experimentally, in this work three new zincophosphites ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) have been successfully synthesized by choosing three predicted organic amines as the templates or SDAs in the hydrothermal system of 1.0 Zn(OAc)2·2H2O: (2.5–2.75) H3PO3: (443–449) H2O: (3.2–4.25) SDA at 160 °C for 5 days. The X-ray powder diffraction patterns of these compounds are in good agreement with the simulated XRD pattern of ZnHPO-CJ1 (Figure 1), confirming the phase purity and their analogous frameworks to ZnHPO-CJ1. The compositional and TG analyses suggest that these three organic amines are kept intact in the products of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) (see Experimental Section and Supporting Information).

    Crystal Structure of (C6H14N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ2)

    Large single crystals of ZnHPO-CJ2 are synthesized hydrothermally by using predicted cyclohexylamine (CHA) as the template. Single-crystal structural analysis reveals that its structure consists of a macroanionic [Zn3(HPO3)4]2− framework, and the charge neutrality is achieved by the protonated cyclohexylamine (CH2)5CHNH3+ counterions. The asymmetric unit of ZnHPO-CJ2 contains two distinct zinc sites, two distinct phosphorus sites, and six distinct oxygen sites (Figure 2a). All Zn atoms are in a tetrahedral environment, with Zn(2) located on a 2-fold axis. Each P atom shares three O atoms with nearby Zn atoms, leaving a terminal P−H bond. The P−O and Zn−O bond lengths (P−Oav. = 1.515 Å, Zn−Oav. = 1.937 Å) are in agreement with those observed in other zinc phosphites.(26) The existence of P−H bonds is confirmed by the characteristic band of the phosphite anion (νH−P = 2389 cm−1) in the IR spectrum (see Figure S2, Supporting Information).
    As with ZnHPO-CJ1, the open framework of ZnHPO-CJ2 is made up of strictly alternating ZnO4 tetrahedra and HPO3 pseudopyramids to form parallel 24-ring and 8-ring channels extending along the crystallographic c axis (Figure 3a). The approximate diameters of its 24-ring window are 10.82 × 10.82 Å, similar to those of the 24-ring channels (11.0 × 11.0 Å) in ZnHPO-CJ1. The 8-ring channel is hollow with no encapsulated guest molecules, and with the pore size of ca. 3.4 × 3.4 Å. Eight protonated cyclohexylamine (CH2)5CHNH3+ cations reside in each 24-ring window, with alkyl groups protruding into the center space of the 24-ring channel and the NH3+ groups forming H-bonds with the framework. The void center space among the organic amines in the 24-rings has free diameters of ca. 5.1 × 5.1 Å, which is measured between the end carbon atoms (C5···C5) of two CHA cations across the 24-ring channels.

    Crystal Structure of (C5H12N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ3)

    Large single crystals of zincophosphite ZnHPO-CJ3 have been prepared by choosing the predicted cyclopentylamine as the template. ZnHPO-CJ3 crystallizes in the space group of P4nc (No. 104), and one asymmetric unit contains three crystallographically distinct Zn atoms and four crystallographically distinct P atoms (Figure 2b). Zn(1), Zn(2), and Zn(3) atoms each shares four O atoms with the nearby P atoms. Each P atom shares three O atoms with the nearby Zn atoms, leaving a terminal P−H bond. The P−O and Zn−O bond lengths (P−Oav. = 1.522 Å, Zn−Oav. = 1.934 Å) are typical for the known zinc phosphites.(26) The existence of P−H bonds is confirmed by the characteristic band of the phosphite anion (νH−P = 2391 cm−1) in the IR spectrum (see Figure S2, Supporting Information).
    The alternating connection of ZnO4 tetrahedra and HPO3 pseudopyramids results in the open framework of ZnHPO-CJ3 with parallel 24-ring and 8-ring channels along the c axis (Figure 3b). Different from ZnHPO-CJ1 and ZnHPO-CJ2, the 24-ring channel of ZnHPO-CJ3 has elliptical pore opening with diameters of ca. 11.13 × 10.5 Å, and the pore size of the hollow 8-ring channel is ca. 3.2 × 3.2 Å, slightly smaller than those of the 8-ring channels (3.4 × 3.4 Å) of ZnHPO-CJ1 and ZnHPO-CJ2. Eight protonated cyclopentylamine (CH2)4CHNH3+ cations accommodate in one 24-ring pore. Interestingly, a larger void space with diameters of ca. 5.7 × 5.5 Å (C8···C8 and C4···C4 atoms) exists in the 24-rings of ZnHPO-CJ3.
    Single-crystal structure analyses indicate that ZnHPO-CJ1, ZnHPO-CJ2, and ZnHPO-CJ3 are isostructural, all of which have the same tetranodal 3- and 4-coordinated net with the highest symmetry of the net in P4cc calculated by Systre software.(27) Calculations using TOPOS software(28) give the Schlafli symbol(29) of the 3,4-coordinated net as (63)(4.62)2(42.62.82)(65.8)2, and the vertex symbols(29) of four nodes as [6.6.62], [4.6.6], [4.4.6.6.24449.24449], and [6.6.6.6.6.24769], respectively.

    Characterization of (C3H10N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (ZnHPO-CJ4)

    ZnHPO-CJ4 was prepared by using the predicted n-propylamine as the template. Unfortunately, the crystals of ZnHPO-CJ4 product are not suitable for single-crystal structural analysis. The powder XRD pattern (Figure 1) indicates that it has an analogous framework structure to those of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1, 2, 3). The ICP and elemental analyses (%) gave 29.86 for Zn, 18.85 for P, 10.79 for C, 3.56 for H and 4.43 for N, suggesting the atomic ratio of Zn/P/C/N/H of 3/4/6/2/24, which is consistent with the empirical formula of (C3H10N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4] deduced from ZnHPO-CJ1. The TG curve of ZnHPO-CJ4 in Supporting Information Figure S1c, shows that there is an obvious weight loss of 20.41% occurring at 170–400 °C. It is ascribed to the removal of two n-propylamine molecules (calcd 22.48%) per formula (C3H10N)2[Zn3(HPO3)4]. The existence of P−H bonds is confirmed by the characteristic band of the phosphite anion (νH−P = 2395 cm−1) in the IR spectrum (see Figure S2, Supporting Information).
    Three new extra-large 24-ring zinc phosphites ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) with desired framework analogous to ZnHPO-CJ1 have been prepared successfully through selecting predicted organic amines, such as CHA, CPA, and n-propylamine as the templates or SDAs. Our work reveals that the three organic amines have templating abilities similar to n-butylamine to direct the formation of the inorganic framework of ZnHPO-CJ1 with 24-ring channels. In contrast, if we choose those predicted unsuitable organic amines, such as 1,6-hexanediamine and diethylenetriamine, as the templates, no target product with structure analogous to ZnHPO-CJ1 can be obtained although various reaction conditions have been attempted. The final crystallized products are three-dimensional (3-D) zincophosphite [H3N(CH2)6NH3]·[Zn3(HPO3)4](30) and two-dimensional (2-D) layered [Zn(C4N3H13)][Zn4(HPO3)6]·(C4N3H15),(31) respectively, in our hydrothermal reaction system.

    Comparison of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1, 2, 3) Structures

    Notably, the different sizes and shapes of n-butylamine, CHA, and CPA results in some structural differences among ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1, 2, 3). First, different organic amines affect the resulting crystal structure symmetry. ZnHPO-CJ1 and ZnHPO-CJ2 templated by n-butylamine and CHA, respectively, both crystallize in the tetragonal space group P4cc (No. 103), while ZnHPO-CJ3 templated by CPA crystallizes in the tetragonal space group P4nc (No. 104). Second, the different sizes of organic amines result in different cell parameters of the final structures. With the size increase of n-butylamine, CPA, and CHA, the cell volumes per Zn3(HPO3)4 formula of their templated structures, such as ZnHPO-CJ1 (4814.4 Å3), ZnHPO-CJ3 (4969.3 Å3), and ZnHPO-CJ2 (5097.8 Å3), are increased. Correspondingly, their framework densities measured by the number of T atoms per 1000 Å decrease in the sequence of ZnHPO-CJ1 (11.6), ZnHPO-CJ3 (11.3), and ZnHPO-CJ2 (11.0). These framework densities are similar to the values of 12.1 for ND-1, 11.1 for FDU-4, and 10.9 for NTHU-1, all of which contain 24-ring channels. Third, the different organic amines affect the sizes of the pore openings. Although the pore sizes of the 24-ring and 8-ring windows in these compounds are similar, the center free void spaces among the organic cations in the 24-rings are quite different. ZnHPO-CJ2 and ZnHPO-CJ3 templated by cyclic organic amines CPA and CHA have much larger center free void spaces (ca. 5.1 × 5.1 Å and 5.7 × 5.5 Å, respectively) than that of ZnHPO-CJ1 (ca. 4.1 × 4.1 Å, the distance of C···C atoms) templated by n-butylamine. The diameters of free void space of ZnHPO-CJ2 and ZnHPO-CJ3 are even much larger than those of 8-rings observed in the known zeolites, such as ABW (3.4 × 3.8 Å), AFX (3.4 × 3.6 Å), PAU (3.6 × 3.6 Å), and RHO (3.6 × 3.6 Å), etc. Notably, the occurrence of such large free space is very unusual in the known open-framework structures. Only one example is zinc phosphate ND-1 (24-rings) templated by trans-1,2-diaminocyclohexan with free space of ca. 6.9 Å (the distance of C···C atoms).(11) This indicates that the size and shape of organic templates can not only affect the framework symmetry and cell parameters, but also control the sizes of pore openings of the inorganic frameworks.

    Templating Mechanism of the Extra-Large Micropore Materials

    Although some structure differences have been found among zinc phosphites ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1, 2, 3) templated by different organic amines, all of these organic amines display a similar arrangement in the 24-ring channels. Insight into other known extra-large micropore materials with high framework charge density, such as aluminophosphate JDF-20 (20-rings, TrEA),(32) gallophosphate ULM-5 (16-rings, 1,6-hexanediamine)(33) and ULM-16 (16-rings, cyclohexylamine),(34) MIL-31 (18-rings, NH2(CH2)nNH2, n = 9,10),(35) MIL-46 (18-rings, cyclopentylamine),(34) and ND-1 (24-rings, trans-1,2-diaminocyclohexane),(11) a common feature of the arrangement of organic amines has been found (Figure 4). Several small protonated organic amines are occluded in one extra-large pore and arrange orderly in such a fashion that the hydrocarbon groups point toward the center space of the pores, while the protonated amino groups closely interact with the charged inorganic framework predominantly through electrostatic and H-bonding interactions. It is clear that multiple small organic cations cooperatively template the extra-large micropores in contrast to extra-large-pore highly silicious zeolites in which one bulk large molecule acts as the template. On the basis of structural analysis of the extra-large micropore compounds and the above discussion, we propose the following formation mechanism (Figure 5): in the initial reaction stage, the inorganic and organic species randomly distribute in the gel. Then a self-assembly process occurs among these reaction species driven by the nonbonding interactions (electrostatic, H-bonding, etc.) between the inorganic and organic species. The arrangement of the organic and inorganic species gradually becomes orderly, where several small organic amines assemble together with their hydrophobic ends pointing inward and the hydrophilic ends interacting with the inorganic species. The formation of such ordered inorganic–organic composite species provides the fundamental structural units for nucleation and crystal growth. However, the self-assembly process of the inorganic–organic species needs to be further investigated.

    Three new open-framework zincophosphites ZnHPO-CJn (n = 2, 3, 4) possessing desired extra-large 24-ring channels of ZnHPO-CJ1 have been successfully prepared through selecting computationally predicted organic amines, such as cyclohexylamine, cyclopentylamine, and n-propylamine molecules as the templates or SDAs. Structure comparison of these compounds indicates that the organic amines influence the resulting structure symmetry, cell parameters, and the opening sizes through the size and shape control although they have similar templating abilities. The cooperative templating mechanism of multiple small organic cations in the formation of extra-large microporous materials has been further proposed upon insight into the structures of ZnHPO-CJn (n = 1, 2, 3) and other extra-large micropore materials with high framework charge density. This work further demonstrates that the template-designed synthetic approach with the assistance of computer simulation will be helpful for the rational synthesis of open-framework materials with specific structures and properties.
