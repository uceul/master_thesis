Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 38, December 2013, Pages 50-53

Synthesis, structure and magnetic properties of a 3D anionic framework based on butterfly Ni4 clusters

Author links open overlay panelYingZhaoabLu-FangMaaZhen-ZhenShiaMin-LeHanaLi-YaWangabc
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2013.10.021Get rights and content

Highlights

•

A complex containing butterfly-like tetranuclear nickel units and [Ni(H2O)6]2 + cations is constructed.

•

Such an inorganic cationic motif and anionic net combination is the first example for Ni4 cluster complexes.

•

Weak antiferromagnetic interactions are exist within Ni4 clusters.

Abstract

A 3D MOF {[Ni(H2O)6][Ni4L2(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2]}n (1) with a (4,8)-connected flu net is constructed by a butterfly-like tetranuclear [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − anionic cluster and organic linker 2,3′,5,5′-biphenyl tetracarboxylic acid (H4L). The disordered [Ni(H2O)6]2 + cations fill in the free voids of anionic framework in 1. Magnetic susceptibility measurements indicate that complex 1 shows weak antiferromagnetic interactions.

Graphical abstract

Hydrothermal reactions of NiII salt with 2,3′,5,5′-biphenyl tetracarboxylic acid afford a (4,8)-connected 3D flu net, which contains butterfly-like tetranuclear [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − anionic clusters and disordered [Ni(H2O)6]2 + cations fill in the voids of anionic framework.

Magnetic properties

The design and construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) based on polynuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) have attracted tremendous attention, due to their unique architectures, intriguing topologies and potential applications [1], [2], [3]. To date, many NiII complexes based on polynuclear clusters, such as trinuclear [4], tetranuclear [5], pentanuclear [6], hexanuclear [7], heptanuclear [8], octanuclear [9] and nonanuclear [10], [11] have been successfully achieved. In these complexes, tetranuclear nickel clusters with cubane structures have been extensively reported. Colacio et al. [12] reported three tetranuclear cluster-based complexes in which oximate-bridged Ni4 core displays cube-like structure exhibit antiferromagnetic versus ferromagnetic exchange interactions. Cortés et al. prepared four complexes in which the four tetramers exhibit a dicubane-like core and ferromagnetic interactions are found between NiII ions [13]. However, tetranuclear NiII complexes with butterfly structures have been less explored [14]. The butterfly-like tetranuclear [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − clusters, consisting of one edge-sharing metal dimer “body” and two metal “wings” connected by μ3-OH bridges, have attracted great attention as models in magnetic materials. Therefore, it is still an ongoing challenge to construct MOFs with butterfly tetranuclear nickel cluster and study their fundamental magnetic phenomena.

To date, several synthetic strategies have been developed to obtain the cluster based MOFs, in which the effective strategy for the synthesis of such MOFs is still the appropriate choice of well-designed N- and O-donor organic ligands as bridges or terminal groups (building blocks) with metal ions as SBUs [15]. Biphenyl tetracarboxylic acid possesses four carboxylic groups and its phenyl rings are able to twist around the CC single bond. Thus, it can connect metal ions in different directions and it is a good choice for construction of the cluster based MOFs. Some MOFs with polynuclear cluster SBUs based on biphenyl tetracarboxylic acid have been reported [14d]. However, MOFs based on 2,3′,5,5′-biphenyl tetracarboxylic acid (H4L) have not been reported until now. So it is necessary to construct cluster-based MOFs from H4L and investigate their physical properties.

Reaction of H4L with NiII acetate at 160 °C for 3 days under hydrothermal conditions generates green crystalline product, {[Ni(H2O)6][Ni4L2(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2]}n (1). Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic form with space group P21/n. Herein we report syntheses [16], crystal structures [17], thermogravimetric analysis and magnetic properties of 1. 1 is air stable and retain the crystalline integrity at ambient conditions.

Single-crystal X-ray analysis reveals that the structure of 1 is comprised of two distinct and crystallographically independent polymeric motifs: tetranuclear anionic butterfly-like [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − cluster and mononuclear cationic [Ni(H2O)6]2 +. To the best of our knowledge, such an interesting inorganic cationic motif and anionic net combination is the first example for Ni4 cluster complexes. As shown in Fig. 1, Ni1 is six-coordinated by three carboxylic oxygen atoms from three different H4L and two μ3-OH groups as well as one coordinated water molecule. Ni2 is also six-coordinated by five carboxylic oxygen atoms from four different H4L ligands and one μ3-OH group. The NiO bond lengths are in the range of 1.981(4)–2.187(5) Å (Table S1, Supporting Information). In the tetranuclear cluster motif, four NiII centers are connected by eight carboxylic and two μ3-OH− groups to form a butterfly-like tetranuclear [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − core, in which Ni1 and Ni2 can function as body and wing, respectively. The Ni⋯Ni nonbonding distances are 3.05, 3.29 and 3.54 Å, respectively. The L ligand with three syn–syn bidentate and one bidentate-chelating carboxylate groups is coordinated to seven NiII ions (Scheme 1). Each L ligand is linked to four butterfly-like [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − clusters, while each cluster is connected by eight L ligands with adjacent twelve clusters, forming a 3D anionic open framework (Fig. 2, left, Fig. S1, Supporting Information). 1 possesses channels along the a and b axes, which are filled by disordered cation groups [Ni(H2O)6]2 + (Fig. 2, top right). The disordered [Ni(H2O)6]2 + groups are located by hydrogen bonds OH⋯Ocarboxylate in the channels. From the topological view, each Ni4 cluster acts as a 8-connected node, and each L acts as a 4-connected node. The 3D anionic network can be described as a (4,8)-connected (46)(412.612.84)-flu topology (Fig. 3). Zhang et al. also reported a new 3D complex based on the similar butterfly-like [Ni4(μ3-OH)2(COO)8]2 − cluster, [Me2NH2][Ni2(bptc)(μ3-OH)(H2O)2] (2), (H4bptc = 3,3′,4,4′-biphenyltetracarboxylic acid) [14d]. Complex 2 is the first (4,8)-connected scu topological anionic open framework and possesses 3D channels along the a, b, and c axes, which are filled by organic cations [Me2NH2]+. Other (4,8)-connected MOFs concerned with flu, scu, alb and sqc net have also been reported in the literature [18].

The void volume of 1 without cation motifs is 889.4 Å3 per unit cell, or 38.6% of the total volume, as calculated by the PLATON program based on the crystal structures [19]. Phase purities of the bulky materials of 1 were confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (see Fig. S2, Supporting Information). The experimental and simulated PXRD patterns agree well with each other, confirming the good phase purity. The slight difference in intensity may arise from the preferred orientation of crystalline powder samples. The TGA curve of 1 shows the pyrolysis of the sample occurs with two steps of weight loss. The final residues of 33.9% (calcd. 33.3%) are closed to the percentage of NiO (Fig. S3, Supporting Information).

The magnetic susceptibilities of 1 were measured in the 2–300 K temperature range at 2000 Oe, and shown as χMT and χM versus T plots (see Fig. 4). The experimental χMT value at 300 K is 5.84 cm3 K mol− 1, which is larger than the spin-only value (4.0 cm3 K mol− 1 with g = 2.0) for four noninteracting NiII centers [20]. As temperature lowers to 2 K, the χMT value decreases first slowly and then rapidly. This behavior suggests that antiferromagnetic interactions are operative in 1. The temperature dependence of the reciprocal susceptibilities (1/χM) obeys the Curie–Weiss law above 10 K with a Weiss constant θ = − 6.82 K, Curie constant C = 5.91 cm3·K·mol− 1 and R = 7.61 × 10− 4. The negative θ value also reveals the presence of antiferromagnetic interactions in 1.

To analyze the magnetic coupling interactions within the tetranuclear NiII cluster, two coupling constants J1 and J2 are adopted. J1 stands for the outer exchange interaction of wing-body, while J2 is inner exchange interaction of butterfly's body–body. As shown in Fig. 1, the Ni2⋯Ni1 are bridged through the hydroxido and syn–syn carboxyl bridges exchange constant are both defined as J1. The Ni1⋯Ni1 are bridged through two hydroxido bridges with the Ni⋯Ni distance of 3.05 Å and NiONi angle of 97.76° exchange constant is defined as J2 (see Chart 1, Supporting Information).

Neglecting single-ion anisotropy or spin–orbit coupling of metal ions, simulation of the temperature dependence of χMT values for 1 have been attempted with a simplified Heisenberg model.H^=−2J1S^1S^3−2J2S^1S^2+S^2S^3+S^3S^4+S^4S^1.

The least-squares analysis of magnetic susceptibilities data led to J1 = − 4.01 cm− 1, J2 = 1.28 cm− 1, g = 2.36 and R = 3.21 × 10− 3. Such phenomena suggest that the competitive ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions are expected in the structure of 1. Investigations on butterfly-like clusters reveal that an intracluster often presents competing anti-ferromagnetic and ferromagnetic interactions, and the overall magnetic behavior relies on the nature of the metal as well as the ligand. In 1, the antiferromagnetic interactions (J1) are found between Ni1 and Ni2, relying on the effect of μ3-O (Ni1–O9–Ni2 = 109.96°) exchange pathway and one syn–syn carboxylate exchange pathway. The ferromagnetic interactions between the Ni ions (J2) depend on the effect of μ3-O (Ni1–O9–Ni1A = 97.76°) exchange pathway. The overall antiferromagnetic properties would be expected for the competitive effect of two kinds of μ3-O exchange pathway and one syn–syn carboxylate exchange pathway.

In summary, this work presents a NiII complex containing butterfly-like tetranuclear [Ni4L2(μ3-OH)2(H2O)2]2 −core and [Ni(H2O)6]2 + cations filling in the channels. Such an inorganic cationic motif and anionic net combination is the first example for Ni4 cluster complexes. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility results show the antiferromagnetic interactions within Ni4 clusters. Subsequent work will be focused on the construction of novel coordination polymers based on polynuclear NiII clusters by using similar organic ligands.
