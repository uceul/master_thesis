A highly stable face-extended diamondoid cluster–organic framework incorporating infinite inorganic guests†

Wei-Hui Fang a, Lei Zhang a, Jian Zhang a and Guo-Yu Yang *ab
aState Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, China. E-mail: ygy@fjirsm.ac.cn
bMOE Key Laboratory of Cluster Science, School of Chemistry, Beijing Institute of Technology, Beijing 100081, China. E-mail: ygy@bit.edu.cn

Received 18th August 2015 , Accepted 28th September 2015

First published on 28th September 2015

A highly stable face-extended cluster–organic framework incorporating infinite inorganic guests is hydrothermally constructed, in which the tetrahedral O-centred Cu4O cluster is firstly found as the face-extended structure building unit to further provide a diamondoid cluster–organic framework with high thermal and structural stability.

The metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have a history of nearly two decades from their discovery to the present day.1 As the term suggests, MOFs are porous crystalline solids assembled by inorganic structural building units (nodes) with organic ligands (linkers). Their architectures can be tailored using a rational design methodology, not only allowing the customization of materials to yield the desired physical and chemical properties,2,3 but also forming cationic, neutral and anionic MOFs. Generally, the guests are isolated inorganic anions in cationic MOFs,4–6 while the presence of infinite anionic chain guests in cationic MOFs is rare and unprecedented. Although the organic linkers can be amenably controlled in the synthesis of new MOFs, the formation of the inorganic nodes still seems “wild” to us. Until now, the most frequently used highly symmetrical inorganic nodes include the paddle-wheel,7 the triangle cornerstone shaped cluster,8 the tetrahedron,9 the octahedron,10etc.

To a tetrahedron, there are four vertices, six edges and four faces. Theoretically, the tetrahedron can be extended via three ways: vertices, edges and faces. Of these the edge-extended linkage has firstly been observed in MOF-5 in 19999 and has been well demonstrated in IRMOF-1-16.2 Six carboxylates ride on six edges of the tetrahedral Zn4O cluster to form an octahedral six-connected Zn4O(CO2)6 node (Scheme 1) in MOF-5. Apart from the six edges, if tetrahedral cluster units are extended by vertices and faces, it will result in the formation of 4-connected zeolite-like frameworks. Recently, a vertex-extended linkage of the Cu4I4 cluster led to the formation of a MTN-type zeolitic cluster–organic framework (COZ-1).11 However, it still remains a great challenge to construct cluster–organic frameworks via the face-extended linkages.


Scheme 1 Scheme representation of the edge (left), vertex (middle) and face (right) expansion strategies of the tetrahedron building units in MOF-5, COZ-1 and this work, respectively.

Our efforts focus on the design and synthesis of cluster–organic frameworks.12–14 Here, we report a highly stable cluster–organic framework based on tetrahedral O-centred Cu4O clusters, [CuII4(μ4-O)L4][CuI6Br8] (1, HL = 4-pyridin-4-yl-benzoic acid), in which the face-extended tetrahedral Cu4O clusters have been successfully realized which further connect with each other to form a 4-connected diamondoid 3-D cluster–organic framework.‡ In the tetrahedral Cu4O cluster, three Cu atoms located on each triangle face are fixed by one pyridyl nitrogen (NPY) and two carboxylate oxygen (OCOO) atoms from two L ligands with reverse orientations (Scheme 1, right). Interestingly, the 1-D rhombic channels of the host framework are filled with shape-matching inorganic chain guests.

Determination of single-crystal structures reveals that 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Fddd. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of a cationic inorganic–organic hybrid host motif and an inorganic guest (Fig. S1, ESI†).§ All Cu atoms are four-coordinated. The coordination environment of the Cu atom in the host motif is completed by one μ4-O, two OCOO and one NPY, and Cu atoms in the guest motif are tetrahedrally bridged by four Br atoms. The bond lengths of Cu–O [1.943(5)–1.968(1) Å], Cu–N [1.972(6) Å], and Cu–Br [2.478(2)–3.161(1) Å] are comparable to the related Cu complexes.7,15

In the host motif, four equivalent Cu atoms define a tetrahedral Cu4O cluster, in which a μ4-O atom is located in the center (Fig. S2, ESI†). Such an O-centred cluster is reminiscent of the familiar Zn4O tetrahedron in MOF-5,9 in which six edges of the Zn4O cluster are occupied by six carboxylates of 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, resulting in a cubic net while the Cu4O clusters herein are linked by L ligands in μ3-tridentate mode, producing a 4-connected dia cluster–organic framework. Although the isolated Cu4O clusters have been well investigated,16–18 the extended structures built by tetrahedral Cu4O clusters have never been documented. As shown in Fig. 1a, each pair of parallel L ligands has reverse orientation and connects two faces of adjacent tetrahedral Cu4O clusters. And ten such Cu4O clusters are linked to each other via twelve pairs of L ligands into a very large adamantane cage with an edge length of 14.3 Å (Fig. 1b). Such face-extended linkage is unprecedented and completely differs from that of the vertex-/edge-extended linkages of the tetrahedral clusters.2,9,11 Due to the spacious nature of a single network, the overall framework of 1 exhibits a three-fold interpenetration (Fig. 1c and Fig. S3, ESI†). The interpenetration vector runs along the a-axis with a separated distance from one net to another of 8.0 Å (Fig. S4, ESI†). Despite the existence of interpenetration, the framework is still open (porosity 31.0%19) and exists as rhombic channels (cross-section 14.3 × 14.3 Å2) along the a-axis. Also, it represents the minimum number of an interpenetrating dia net linked by L ligands to date (Table S1, ESI†).20–26 The decrease of the interpenetration degree may be attributed to the size increase of the inorganic nodes.


Fig. 1 The structure of 1. (a) The coordination environment of the tetrahedral Cu4O cluster; (b) the large adamantane unit; (c) the overall perspective view, in which the single network show different colors for clarity. (d) The ball-stick view of the infinite inorganic guest.

In the guest motif, four Cu(2–5) atoms are all on crystallographic C2 sites with half-occupy and bridge via by Br atoms to form a chain: of which Cu(2) and Cu(3) centers are bridged by μ-Br to form the well-known [Cu2Br6]4− dimer motif,27 which are further linked into chains by two μ-Br bridges and one additional copper atom (Cu(4) or Cu(5) atom) between each dicopper unit. However, the distance of Cu(4)⋯Cu(5) on their C2-symmetric lattice sites is 2.5 Å, which is much too short to be real. Hence, only Cu(4) or Cu(5) can be occupied in each asymmetric unit, probably with a random distribution down each chain (Fig. 1d), which results in a Cu3Br4 unit. The Cu:Br ratio obtained from electron probe microanalysis agrees with this assignment. Such Cu3Br4 units further link each other to form 1-D Cu–Br chain based [Cu2Br6]4− dimers and [CuBr4]3− tetrahedra.

The bond valence sum (BVS) shows that the value of the Cu(1) atom in the Cu4O motif is 1.81, and the values of Cu(2–5) atoms in the Cu3Br4 motif are 0.95, 0.96, 0.94 and 0.97, indicating that the valencies for Cu(1–5) atoms are 2, 1, 1, 1, 1, respectively (Table S2, ESI†).

The most striking structural feature of 1 is the shape-matching combination of the hybrid host framework and inorganic chain-like guests (Fig. 1c and Fig. S5, ESI†). The rhombic channel around the guests matches the shape of the chain-like guest, which is unique and different from that of known porous MOFs templated by amines.28 In the structure, the infinite inorganic guests interact with the hybrid host framework via C–H⋯Br hydrogen bonding interactions (Fig. S6 and Table S3, ESI†).

In addition to the structural novelty, 1 also shows high thermal and water stability. The thermogravimetric analysis carried out in air atmosphere from 30 to 1000 °C showed one onset weight loss. 1 does not decompose up to 400 °C, which indicates the high thermal stability of its 3D cluster–organic framework (Fig. S7, ESI†). In comparison with MOF-5 that is sensitive to moisture and water,29 the semiconductive (band gap = 2.32 eV, Fig. S8, ESI†) crystalline samples of 1 exhibit high stability. Its structure can be retained even after being exposed to air or soaked in water for months (Fig. S9, ESI†).

To reveal the flexibility between the host and guest motifs, the thermal expansion properties of 1 were studied. From 100 to 500 K, the unit-cell parameters show approximately linear changes and the unit-cell volume expands by about 2.6% (Fig. 2, Table S4, ESI†). The thermal expansion coefficients of the a and b-axes are 49 × 10−6 K−1 and 65 × 10−6 K−1, respectively, which are in agreement with the thermal expansion law. However, the length of the c-axis (−49 × 10−6 K−1) reduces with increasing temperature, resulting in a negative thermal expansion coefficient of −49 × 10−6 K−1. In order to analyze the structural origin of the thermal expansion of 1, its single-crystal structures measured at 100 K and 298 K were compared (Table S5, ESI†) and it was found that the changes were small in coordination bond lengths (<0.026 Å) but large in the angles (max = 1.3°) (Table S6, ESI†). Consequently, the 4-connected Cu4O “rhombus grid” was distorted (Fig. S10, ESI†), with two interior angles changing by approximately 1.1° and 0.5°. The positive/negative thermal expansion coefficients of 1 are not significant compared with typical thermal expansion materials,30,31 indicating that the expansion of the host framework is restricted by the inorganic guests.

In view of the robust architecture of 1, TEM images were studied to visualize its pores as those reported for IRMOF-74-VII and IRMOF-74-IX.32 However, the attempts to observe the arrangement of the channels of 1 were unsuccessful due to its sensitivity to the electron beam. Instead, spherical and uniform nanoparticles (NPs, <5 nm) are found (Fig. 3a). Fast Fourier transform (FFT) analysis was performed on the centered square areas in the HRTEM images (Fig. 3b). Six reflection spots corresponding to the 110 reflections were resolved from the FFT patterns. The interlayer distance of the selected area is calculated to be 0.201 nm, which agrees well with the (110) lattice planes of the hexagonal phase CuBr (PDF#06-0700). Considering the compositions of the Cu3Br4-based chains in 1, it is reasonable to conclude that the formation of these CuBr NPs should be attributed to the decomposition and rearrangement of these Cu3Br4-based chains.


Fig. 3 (a) TEM micrographs of the NPs of 1. Scale bar 10 nm. (b) HRTEM image of an individual particle. Inset is the FFT pattern.

In summary, a highly stable face-extended cluster organic framework incorporating infinite inorganic chains has been successfully constructed under hydrothermal conditions. The key points of the synthetic procedures have been well established. It represents a rare example of face-extended linkages, which makes it different from other cluster organic frameworks with vertex-/edge-extended linkages. This study may open up possibilities for constructing novel frameworks from the face-extended cluster building units. Further work is in progress.
