Nanoporous metal–organic framework comprising of 1D cobalt oxalate chains and flexible ligands exhibiting both dynamic gas adsorption and antiferromagnetic chain behaviours†

Hong-Qing Hao , Zhuo-Jia Lin , Sheng Hu , Wen-Ting Liu , Yan-Zhen Zheng and Ming-Liang Tong *
Key Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry of Ministry of Education / State Key Laboratory of Optoelectronic Materials and Technologies, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, 510275, China. E-mail: tongml@mail.sysu.edu.cn

Received 4th January 2010 , Accepted 8th February 2010

First published on 15th March 2010

A novel three-dimensional (3D) nanoporous metal–organic framework with moganite topology, [Co2(ox)2(dchtpy)]n·9nH2O (1·9H2O), has been obtained from the hydrothermal reaction of CoCl2, oxalic acid, NaOH and 1a,4a-dihydroxy-1e,2e,4e,5e-tetra(4-pyridyl)cyclohexane (dchtpy). X-ray analysis reveals that the bulky dchtpy ligands link four adjacent metal oxalate chains and form a 3D hybrid network with both moganite topology and 3D channels in various sizes. The largest pores (channel A) are the ones viewed along the a-axis, which are composed of two [Co(ox)]n chains and two bulky ligands in the dimensions of 10.76 × 10.67 Å excluding the van der Waals radii. Along the {101} direction there was found the second large channels (channel B) in the size of 10.51 × 4.66 Å. Notably the hydroxyl groups of dchtpy extend towards the channels on the middle of the walls, dividing the pores with two small channels in approximately 4.50 × 4.66 Å. Besides, small pores in dimensions of 7.07 × 8.24 Å and 6.98 × 6.06 Å can also be found along the a- and c-axes, respectively. The potential solvent accessible void of the framework, calculated by PLATON, is 46.1% of the total volume and is occupied with disordered aqua molecules. Variable-temperature powder X-ray diffraction experiments confirmed that the crystallinity and network integrity of 1 can be retained up to 375 °C upon the removal of guest aqua molecules, in which thermal stability is among the most stable metal organic coordination frameworks. The framework shows guest-induced dynamic flexibility and elasticity and has a gating effect on the sorption of small gas molecules. The hydroxyl groups from the ligands functionalize the walls of the channels and have strong hydrogen bonds with adsorbents, leading to high sorption capacity at ambient temperature. Magnetic studies revealed significant antiferromagnetic interaction between the Co(II) ions in 1·9H2O with J = −12.4(1) cm−1 and g = 2.450(4).

Introduction

Crystalline porous materials based on metal–organic coordination frameworks are very important adsorbents due to their well-defined and easily-tunable pore sizes1 and diverse network topologies.2 Porous metal organic frameworks (MOFs) have some characteristic advantages over traditional zeolites and AlPOs as multi-functional materials in their structural flexibility3 and in the organo-metallic hybrid frameworks which are responsible for useful physical properties such as magnetism,4 conductivity,5 photoluminescence6 and chirality.7 For example, the combination of magnetism and porosity may give rise to interesting materials in the recognition and separation of non-/diamagnetic guest molecules according to their magnetic susceptibility. Recently Kepert et al. reported the first example of guest-dependent spin crossover in a nanoporous metal organic framework.8 The coupling within one material of spin switching and reversible guest exchange has provided a new type of advanced material with applications in molecular sensing.9 In this work we report an antiferromagnetic metal organic framework with nanoporous channels.

The addition of magnetic functionality into porous materials remains, however, a great challenge because the achievement of large porosity usually requires the use of long bridging ligands whereas long-range magnetic ordering requires short exchange pathways between the nearest neighbour metal centres. For example, Wang and Kobayashi reported an interesting 3D Mn3(HCOO)6 magnet exhibiting a wide spectrum of guest inclusion behaviour and guest-modulated long-range magnetic ordering.10a The short formate ligands led to an open framework with relatively small channels in the size of 4 × 5 Å. Another fascinating example is a cyanide-bridged MnIICrIII coordination polymer,10b which shows a reversible single-crystal-to-single-crystal transformation and reversible ferromagnetic ordering between a 2D sheet and a 3D pillared-sheet framework upon dehydration/hydration. Here we present a multi-functional cobalt coordination framework [Co2(ox)2(dchtpy)]n·9nH2O (1·9H2O) in the moganite topology based on mixed 1a,4a-dihydroxy-1e,2e,4e,5e-tetra(4-pyridyl)cyclohexane (dchtpy)11 and oxalate (ox2−) bridging ligands, which have improved characteristic aspects including i) an organo/inorganic-metal-chain framework responsible for antiferromagnetic chain behaviour, ii) well-ordered and thermally stable nanoporous structures which show guest-induced flexible behaviour and iii) hydroxylation of the channel surface for chemisorption of methanol.

Experimental

Materials and physical measurements

All of the starting materials employed were commercially available and used as received without further purification. IR spectra were recorded from KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 with a Bruker-tensor 27 spectrometer. C, H, and N Elemental Analysis was carried out with an Elementar Vario-EL CHNS elemental analyzer. X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) intensities was measured on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-Ray Diffractometer (Cu-Kα, λ = 1.54056 Å). The crushed single-crystal powder sample was prepared by crushing the crystals and scanning from 5 to 60° with a step of 4°/min. The calculated pattern was generated with PowderCell. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis was performed at a rate of 10 °C min−1 under N2 using a NETZSCH TG 209 system. Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility measurements were made using a SQUID magnetometer MPMS XL-7 (Quantum Design) at 1.0 kOe. The diamagnetic correction for each sample was determined from Pascal's constants.12 The adsorption isotherms of N2 (77 K), CO2 (195 K) and H2 (77 K) were measured with BELSORP max volumetric adsorption equipment from Bel Japan, Inc, and various temperature methanol adsorption isotherms were measured with an automatic gravimetric adsorption apparatus (IGA-003 series, Hiden Isochema Ltd.). The as-synthesized sample was placed in a quartz tube and dried under high vacuum at 393 K for 10 h to remove the solvated molecules prior to measurements.

Synthesis of [Co2(ox)2(dchtpy)]n·9nH2O (1·9H2O)

Method A: A mixture of CoCl2·6H2O (0.238 g, 1.0 mmol), dchtpy·4H2O (0.248 g, 0.5 mmol), oxalic acid (0.090 g, 1.0 mmol), NaOH (0.08 g, 2.0 mmol) and H2O (16 mL) was charged in a Parr Teflon-lined, stainless-steel vessel and heated at 180 °C for 72 h. Block purple-red crystals were collected and dried in air (yields: 10%). Method B: A mixture of CoCl2·6H2O (0.060 g, 0.25 mmol), 2-cyanopyrimidine (0.026 g, 0.25 mmol), dchtpy·4H2O (0.062 g, 0.125 mmol), ethanol (4 mL) and H2O (12 mL) was charged in a Parr Teflon-lined, stainless-steel vessel and heated at 160 °C for 48 h to yield block purple-red crystals (yield: 65%). X-ray crystallography

The intensity data were recorded on a Oxford Diffraction Ltd. Germini S Ultra diffractometer with graphite monochromated Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å) at 123 K. The structures were solved with direct methods and refined with a full-matrix least-squares technique with the SHELXTL program package.13 Anisotropic thermal parameters were applied to all non-hydrogen atoms. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically; the aqua hydrogen atoms were located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. Results and discussion

Description of crystal structure of 1·9H2O

The overall framework of 1·9H2O is constructed from 1D zigzag [Co(ox)]n chains connected by flexible four-connected dchtpy ligands (Fig. 1a). The asymmetric unit contains a quarter of the formula, that is, half of a cobalt atom, half of an oxalate with an inversion centre sitting on the middle of the molecule and one quarter of dchtpy. The cobalt atom sitting on the 2-fold axis is in a distorted octahedral geometry surrounded with two ox2− ions via 1,3-chelation in the cis-arrangement (Co1–O1 = 2.136(2) Å and Co1–O2 = 2.085(3) Å) and two dchtpy ligands (Co1–N1 = 2.104(3) Å). Adjacent cobalt ions are bridged by two anti-anti carboxylates from one oxalate anion with the Co⋯Co distance of 5.4797(1) Å and form an infinite zigzag [Co(ox)]n chain along the a-axis (Fig. 1b). Interestingly the e-pyridyl groups of dchtpy are disorderly spinning between the metal and the cyclohexane ring in a 1:1 ratio in two positions with a dihedral angle of 65.7(3)° to each other. The cyclohexane ring of dchtpy also occupies two crystallographic sites in a 1:1 ratio. Considering the symmetry operation that there is one 2-fold axis running along and one mirror plane across the dchtpy, as well as the low temperature at which the single crystal diffraction data was collected (123 K), the cyclohexane ring is of statistical disorder in a chair-conformation. The thermal ellipsoid plot in Fig. S3† suggests the rationality of a 50%:50% disorder model for the ligand.

The bulky organic ligands link four adjacent metal oxalate chains and form a 3D hybrid network with 3D channels in various sizes. The largest pores (channel A) are the ones viewed along the a-axis, which are composed of two [Co(ox)]n chains and two bulky ligands in the dimension of 10.76 × 10.67 Å excluding the van der Waals radii (Fig. 1b). Along the {101} direction was found the second large channels (channel B) in the size of 10.51 × 4.66 Å (Fig. 1c). Notably the hydroxyl groups of dchtpy extend towards the channels on the middle of the walls, dividing the pores with two small channels in approximately 4.50 × 4.66 Å. Besides, small pores in dimensions of 7.07 × 8.24 Å and 6.98 × 6.06 Å can also be found along the a- and c-axes, respectively. The potential solvent accessible void of the framework, calculated by PLATON,14 is 46.1% of the total volume and is occupied with disordered aqua molecules. When the cobalt atom is conceived as a tetrahedral node, the dchtpy as a square-planar node and the ox2− ion as a linear linker, the network can be simplified in the moganite topology (Fig. 1d).

Thermogravimetric analysis. Thermogravimetric (TG) analysis was carried out in a nitrogen atmosphere to examine the thermal stability of 1·9H2O in the temperature range of 15–820 °C (Fig. 2a). The first weight loss of 18.63% from 15 °C to 110 °C corresponds to the escape of nine lattice water molecules (calcd. 18.41%), where 1·9H2O was fully dehydrated to anhydrous 1. A large plateau was observed from 110–375 °C, following which there is a sharp drop with a weight loss of 48.40% from 375 °C to 420 °C, possibly corresponding to the collapse of dchtpy (calcd. 48.21%). Upon further heating, the sample decomposed completely with a gradual weight loss of 19.71% from 450 °C to 820 °C.
Fig. 2 Stability study of 1·9H2O: (a) the TGA and DTG data; (b) the temperature-dependent power XRD patterns; (c) the enlarged view of pXRD patterns showing shifting of the (021) and (11−1) Bragg peaks between 25 °C and 375 °C and (d) pXRD of the rehydrated 1·9H2O.
Powder X-ray diffraction. The plateau between 125 °C and 375 °C in the TGA curve suggested that the framework of dehydrated 1 may have a high thermal stability. Variable-temperature powder X-ray diffraction (VT-pXRD) experiments performed in air from 25 °C to 425 °C confirmed that the crystallinity and network integrity of 1 can be retained up to 375 °C upon the removal of guest aqua molecules (Fig. 2b), in which thermal stability is among the most stable metal organic coordination framework.

Careful examination of the VT-pXRD patterns found that the two strongest Bragg diffraction peaks, (021) and (11−1), have some drastic and continuous displacements upon heating (Fig. 2b–c), indicating the guest-induced dynamic adjustment of the flexible framework during the process of water escape. The experimental powder diffraction pattern at 25 °C is in agreement with the theoretical one of 1·9H2O with (021) at 10.46° and (11−1) at 11.21°. When slightly heated, the (021) and (11−1) peaks moved stepwise to the higher 2θ of 10.80° and 12.27° at 75 °C, suggesting that channel A and channel B shrank by 0.263 Å and 0.658 Å, respectively upon the removal of most guest molecules (Fig. 1b–c). The drastic displacements of the peaks are in accordance with the sharp decrease in weight from ambient temperature to 75 °C in the TGA curve. Upon further heating, the residue aqua molecules had less and less influence on the host framework and the flexible organic ligands began to relax to the equilibrium positions. The (021) and (11−1) peaks moved back to the low 2θ of 10.24° and 11.93° and kept to almost the same Bragg angles in the temperature range of 125 °C to 375 °C (Fig. 2c). Consequently in anhydrous 1, channel A slightly expanded by 0.174 Å and channel B shrank by 0.460 Å compared with hydrated 1·9H2O. The framework collapsed completely at 425 °C.

When anhydrous 1 was exposed to wet air for 12 h, the powder XRD patterns were restored to the same as 1·9H2O (Fig. 2d). This suggests that the shrunk framework of anhydrous 1 can readsorb water molecules from wet air, resulting in the expanded network of 1·9H2O. The framework transformation of shrinking and expanding induced by aqua molecules was reversible and the XRD patterns of the samples kept the same after three dehydration-hydration cycles. This aqua-induced dynamic transformation of the framework may arise from the flexible skeleton of the bulky dchtpy ligands and the extensive hydrogen bonds between the water molecules and the hydroxyl groups on the walls of the channels.

Gas and liquid vapour sorption. The porosity of anhydrous 1 was studied by various gas sorption experiments. The N2 sorption of 1 at 77 K gives a type-I isotherm (Fig. 3a), indicating a microporous nature. The Langmuir surface area is 414.1 m2 g−1 (BET: 306.3 m2 g−1). The N2 uptake at 1 atm is 104.1 cm3 g−1 (STP) (130.1 mg g−1), suggesting adsorption of 3.3 molecules of nitrogen gas per formula unit. The pore diameter distribution calculated from the Horvath–Kawazoe (HK) model is convergent at 6.2 Å with a small shoulder peak and at 10.7 Å with a sharp peak (Fig. 3b). These two kinds of pores should correspond to the channel A along the a-axis (10.76 × 10.67 Å) and the small channels along the a- and c-axis (7.07 × 8.24 Å and 6.98 × 6.06 Å).
Fig. 3 (a) N2 sorption isotherm of 1 at 77 K (Po = 1 atm); (b) micropore size distribution with differential pore volume and pore diameter calculated from the N2 isotherm using the Horvath–Kawazoe (HK) model; (c) H2 sorption isotherm of 1 at 77 K (Po = 1 atm); and (d) CO2 adsorption–desorption isotherm at 195 K.

The H2 sorption of 1 at 77 K exhibits a similar type-I isotherm. The gas uptake is 101.1 cm3 g−1 (STP) (9.0 mg g−1), indicating the storage of 3.2 hydrogen molecules per formula unit (Fig. 3c). Considering that nine water molecules per formula are enclosed within the channels in the hydrated framework and only two kinds of pores are detected by the N2 isotherm, it is assumed that only half of the void space was occupied with N2 or H2 and channel B had not been filled. In order to probe the porosity of channel B, a sorption experiment with gas molecules of small size at a higher sorption temperature was investigated.

The CO2 adsorption at 195 K shows a type-VI isotherm with a two-step adsorption and hysteretic desorption process (Fig. 3d). The adsorption isotherm reaches the first plateau at 0.15 atm (point a) after a quick uptake. The amount of adsorbed CO2 is 3.3 mol per mole of 1 (201.9 mg g−1), approximately the same as the adsorption of N2 (3.3 mol mol−1) and H2 (3.2 mol mol−1). With an increase of CO2 pressure from 0.18 atm, 1 starts the second adsorption. The total sorption amount of CO2 at 0.6 atm (point b) is 6.7 mol mol−1 (410.0 mg g−1), twice that of the adsorption of N2 and H2. The increase of adsorption with pressure has been observed in many dynamically flexible coordination frameworks.15 In 1, the facile sorption in the first step is attributed to the large and open channels A parallel to the [Co(ox)]n chains and interconnected small pores. Channel B is perpendicular to the [Co(ox)]n chain and can be accessible only through the hydroxyl gates. The different disposition of guest molecules is in accordance with the isolated water clusters occupied in channel A and the hydrogen bonded disordered aqua chain along channel B. Since the crystal structure characterized at 123 K shows the disorder of the pyridyl and hydroxyl groups on the dchtpy ligand, they may have more dynamic displacements and rotations at higher temperature and consequently, allow small gas molecules to break the gating by pressure and enter the inside of channel B, leading to the second adsorption of CO2.

The desorption process has a three-step isotherm with hysteresis. The first step is the slow desorption from Po(1 atm) to point c (0.2 atm), which is followed by an abrupt drop to point d (0.02 atm). The hysteresis can be associated with the gating effect in that the flexible hydroxyl and pyridyl groups can help the channels to accommodate CO2 more safely.

Since channel B is accessible by CO2 at higher temperature, it is expected that the hydroxyl groups pointing towards the interior of the channels may have certain interactions with some specific guest molecules within the pores. The methanol isotherm of adsorption and desorption for anhydrous 1 at 25 °C is similar to the CO2 ones (Fig. 4a). The first step of adsorption (12.2% weight) at 0.5 kPa may arise from the physisorption in channels A and the following sorption (24.3% weight) at 3 kPa, the chemisorption in channels B. 1 has a high adsorption capacity of 305.9 mg g−1 at 15.0 kPa, which corresponds to 6.9 molecules per formula unit.


Fig. 4 (a) Methanol vapor adsorption–desorption isotherm of 1 at 298 K; (b) methanol vapor sorption isotherms at various temperatures (288 K, 298 K, 303 K and 318 K); and (c) the Arrhenius plot for MeOH sorption isotherms in the capillary condensation region (Q = 157 mg g−1) indicating the isosteric heat of 53.8 kJ mol−1.

The interactions between the host framework and lattice MeOH can be estimated by isosteric heats of adsorption Qst calculated from the Clausius–Clapeyron equation16 using isotherms at 288 K, 298 K, 303 K and 318 K (Fig. 4b). As shown in Fig. 4c, the isosteric heat of adsorption for MeOH in the capillary condensation region (the amount of MeOH adsorbed Q = 157 mg g−1) is 53.8 kJ mol−1, suggesting strong hydrogen bonding interactions between MeOH and the host framework.

Considering the kinetic diameters17 of H2O (2.6 Å), H2 (2.9 Å), CO2 (3.3 Å), N2 (3.6 Å) and methanol (3.8–4.1 Å), the selective sorption of H2O, CO2 and MeOH over H2 and N2 in channel B (Table 3) is attributed to the gating effect18 of the dynamically flexible framework rather than the result of different pore sizes. The existence of hydroxyl groups on the walls of the channels is critical for the high sorption capacity of methanol at ambient temperatures. In addition, the flexible framework is elastic and very stable upon several cycles of desorption–adsorption processes (including three aqua cycles, four MeOH cycles, three N2 cycles, one CO2 and one H2 cycles).

Magnetic properties. The magnetic properties of 1·9H2O were explored on the polycrystalline samples. The susceptibility measurement shows that the χT value at 320 K is 2.47 cm3 K mol−1 per Co(II) ion, significantly larger than the expected spin-only value (1.88 cm3 K mol−1) and accordingly indicating a large orbital contribution (Fig. 5). The χT value decreases continuously to 2 K upon cooling. The data in the range of 50–300 K obeys the Curie–Weiss law (χm = C/(T − θ)), with C = 3.02 cm3 mol−1 K and θ = −71.31 K. The C value corresponds to an effective magnetic moment of 4.92 μB, again indicating the orbital contribution of the pseudooctahedral CoII ion, and the large negative θ value indicates significant AF interactions between the CoII ions. A 1D Fisher chain model (for S = 3/2),19Ĥ = −JΣSiSi + 1, was used to fit the data above at 40 K, where the Heisenberg model is still valid for the CoII ions. The best fitting results gave J = −12.4(1) cm−1 and g = 2.450(4).

Conclusions

In summary, we report a multifunctional metal organic framework composed of zigzag [Co(ox)]n chains and bulky four-connected ligands with various types of porosity in the mognite topology. The framework shows guest-induced dynamic flexibility and elasticity and has a gating effect on the sorption of small gas molecules. The hydroxyl groups from the ligands functionalize the walls of the channels and have strong hydrogen bonds with adsorbents, leading to high sorption capacity at ambient temperature. Magnetic studies revealed significant antiferromagnetic interaction between the Co(II) ions in 1·9H2O.
