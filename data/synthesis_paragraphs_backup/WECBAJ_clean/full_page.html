[Zn5(PO4)(HPO4)4(Hbpy)2(bpy)][H2PO4]·H2O: a novel inorganic–organic metal-phosphate framework showing an unprecedented (3,4)-connected (4·62)6(4·62·103)2(64·102)2 topology and the coexistence of various phosphates†

Wen-yuan Xu , Xiao-zhao Tian , Gong-ming Sun , Yu-mei Song , Hai-xiao Huang , Yan Zhu , Zi-zun Yuan , Xue-feng Feng , Ming-biao Luo , Shu-juan Liu and Feng Luo *
College of Biology, Chemistry and Material Science, East China Institute of Technology, Fuzhou, Jiangxi, China. E-mail: ecitluofeng@163.com; Fax: 86 0794 8258320; Tel: 86 0794 8258320

Received 10th May 2012 , Accepted 25th June 2012

First published on 25th June 2012

Via solvo(hydro)thermal synthesis, a novel three-dimensional inorganic–organic Zn(II)-phosphate framework, namely [Zn5(PO4)(HPO4)4(Hbpy)2(bpy)][H2PO4]·H2O (1, bpy = 4,4′-bipyridine), was obtained. The connectivity of Zn(II) ions, PO43−, and HPO42− groups creates a 2-D anionic layer with a previously unobserved (4·62)8(64·102)2 topology matrix in the ab-plane, which is further supported by partial bpy ligands, resulting in the overall 3D charge-positive layer-pillared framework with an unprecedented (3,4)-connected (4·62)6 (4·62·103)2(64·102)2 topology. To balance the net cationic charge of the framework, an extra framework anion like H2PO4− is encapsulated in the void space, in which guest water molecules are also located.

The pursuance of framework zinc materials like zinc phosphates is mainly driven by two points. One is their intriguing, various topology matrices, and the other one is their wide applications covering separation, catalysis, absorption, and so on.1 Many such materials showing zero-, one-, two-, and three-dimensional (3D) structures have been documented over the past few decades.2 In these cases, the method of organic amine acting as both templating and directing agent, has been attested to be a powerful route to construct 2D anionic zinc-phosphate frameworks.3 Recently, extensive interest has been focused on the design and preparation of hybrid inorganic–organic zinc-phosphate frameworks that feature a previously unobserved Zn–N coordination bond.4 In this way, various N-donor ligands like, but not limited to, 2,2′-dipyridine, imidazoles, 1,4-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene,1,3-bis-(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, 1,2,4,5-tetrakis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, have been developed and a series of interesting inorganic–organic zinc-phosphate frameworks were obtained.5 4,4′-Bipyridine (bpy), a common N-donor ligand, showing a strong coordination ability and, unique size and shape, has been extensively utilized to construct hybrid inorganic–organic metal-phosphate frameworks,6 and two 3D Zn(II)-bpy-HPO42− frameworks showing a layer-pillared structure have been achieved.7 In the two cases, the Zn:P ratio is 1:1 and the connectivity of the ZnO3N tetrahedron with the HPO42− tetrahedron generates the common 4·82 net composed of 4- and 8-rings. However, their topology matrices are very different, one showing (3,4)-connected (4·6·8) (4·62·83) topology symbol, and one taking the (3,4)-connected (4·82)(4·82·103) topology symbol. From the viewpoint of synthesis, the reason for the difference between them includes several factors such as the source of Zn(II) ion, the ratio of the initial reaction materials, the reaction temperature, as well as reaction time. To further exploit structural diversity in this system, we explored here a distinct solvo(hydro)thermal route and the result was also a 3D layer-pillared metal-phosphate framework, but which was made up of a 2D (4·62)8(64·102)2 layer and displays a (4·62)6(4·62·103)2(64·102)2 topology.

Polymer 1 was synthesized by solvo(hydro)thermal reaction of Zn(NO3)2, H3PO4 and 4,4′-bipyridine in ethylene glycol and water.‡

The single structure was determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction, giving that it crystallizes in the triclinic, P space group and the asymmetric unit contains five crystallographically distinct zinc atoms and seven P atoms. (Fig. 1) Zn1, Zn2, Zn3, and Zn4 sites hold the ZnO3N tetrahedral geometry, completed by two HPO42− oxygens, one PO43− oxygen, and one bpy nitrogen [Zn–O/N = 1.88(13) –2.07(11) Å], whereas the Zn5 site is coordinated by four HPO42− oxygens, creating a ZnO4 tetrahedral geometry [Zn–O = 1.90(11) –1.95(10) Å]. Of the seven phosphorus atoms, P2, P3, P4, P6 bear three P–O–Zn linkages and one P–O terminal of OH− group [P2–O = 1.57(10), P3–O = 1.57(12)], whilst P5 possesses four P–O–Zn linkages and P1, P7 with the position occupation of 0.5 are free H2PO4− anions. The hydrogen atoms bonded to the HPO42− groups were first found in the residual e− density map and then refined with the restriction of O–H = 0.85 ± 0.01 Å, whereas the hydrogen atoms on H2PO4− groups can not be fully located in the residual e− density map and only one hydrogen atom is found for P1 and P7. To the best of our knowledge, the coexistence of three distinct types of phosphate in the same structure is still rare.8


Fig. 1 View of the five crystallographically distinct zinc atoms and seven P atoms. The hydrogen atoms except for that on Hbpy+ and H2PO4− are omitted for clarity. Color code: Zn/purple, P/yellow, C/green, N/blue, O/red, H/black.

The 2D inorganic motif of 1 is illustrated in Fig. 2. The combination of one PO43− tetrahedron, two HPO42− tetrahedra, two ZnO3N tetrahedra, and one ZnO4 tetrahedron creates a 6-ring, while the connectivity of two ZnO3N tetrahedra and two HPO42− tetrahedra generates a 4-ring. Within this 2D net, each 4-ring is surrounded by four 6-rings by sharing one edge and each 6-ring is surrounded by four 6-rings and two 4-rings by sharing one edge, and two other 6-rings by sharing one vertex. From a viewpoint of topology, this 2D net holds the previously unobserved (4·62)8 (64·102)2 topology. In literature, as observed in the two reported Zn(II)-bpy-HPO42− frameworks, the 4-ring, 8-ring, and consequentially 4·82 sheet net is common in metal-phosphates.9 A schematic version of the packing diagram is given in Fig. 3, where the 2D sheet nets are pillared by bpy ligands via Zn–N coordination bonds and stacked in the ABCDA fashion, creating an overall 3D layer-pillared charge-positive net with a 1D channel of ca. 5.3 × 5.7 Å2 (viewed down [100]) and of ca. 4.7 × 5.6 Å2 (viewed down [010]). Interestingly, all the –OH groups of HPO42− are located inside the channel, which will be very convenient to form hydrogen bonds with the H2PO4− ions and guest water molecules that occupy the channel and balance the positive charge of the framework. Notably, the bpy ligands in 1 play two kinds of role. One acts as a pillar to support the layer, and the other one that is monoprotonated acts as both ligand to fulfil the Zn–N coordination bond and structure-directing agent to form hydrogen bonds [N1–H1M⋯O10(P5) = 2.644 Å/177.77°, N6–H2M⋯O9(P5) = 2.665 Å/175.41°]. Such a dual role has rarely been reported.10 The topology analysis is carried out by the Topos40 program, giving (3,4)-connected (4·62)6(4·62·103)2(64·102)2 topology, where P2, P3, P4, P6, Zn1, and Zn2 act as a three-connecting node, P5, Zn5 are a four-connecting node with 64·102 point symbol, and Zn3, Zn4 are also four-connecting node but with 4·62·103 point symbol.11

The IR bands at 1060 cm−1 and 1099 cm−1 is contributed by the asymmetric telescopic vibration of the phosphate groups. And the IR band at 966 cm−1 is ascribed to the symmetric stretching vibration of the phosphate groups. Moreover, the IR band at 1020 cm−1 should be derived from the symmetric stretching vibration of P–OH. The thermostability of 1 is explored by TG studies at 30–600 °C (Fig. 4). The weight loss at 30–150 °C should be guest water molecules (calc. 1.3%, exp. 1.4%). The bpy ligands undergo chemical decomposition at 200–280 °C (calc. 33.8%, exp. 33.5%). The photoluminescence spectrum of 1 was measured in the solid state (Fig. 4). If excited at 334 nm, polymer 1 shows a blue emission at 400 nm, which should be derived from the π–π* transition of 4,4′-bipyridine ligands.


Fig. 4 The TG and luminescence studies of 1.

In conclusion, we present here a novel inorganic–organic zinc phosphate and its synthesis, structure, topology, and luminescence studies. Via solvo(hydro)thermal synthesis, a new Zn(II)-bpy-phosphate compound is obtained, which is very different from the two reported Zn(II)-bpy-phosphate compounds in that it includes multiple types of phosphate (PO43−, HPO42−, H2PO4−) and shows the Zn/P ratio of 5:6, as well as the bpy ligands in our case hold dual functions. Moreover, from the viewpoint of topology, our case displays an uncommon (4·62)8(64·102)2 layer composed of 4- and 6-rings, and presents a new (3,4)-connected topology prototype. To some extent, we have demonstrated here an effective approach to target structural diversity by means of different synthesis conditions, and added an interesting member into the database of metal phosphates.
