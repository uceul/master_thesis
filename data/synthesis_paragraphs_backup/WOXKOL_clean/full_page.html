Syntheses, structures and magnetic properties of four manganese(II) and cobalt(II) complexes†

Tuoping Hu *, Baohua Zheng , Xiaoqing Wang and Xuena Hao
Department of Chemistry, College of Science, North University of China, Taiyuan 030051, China. E-mail: hutuopingsx@126.com

Received 19th September 2015 , Accepted 29th October 2015

First published on 30th October 2015

Hydrothermal reactions of Mn(II)/Co(II) salts and H4qptc in the presence of corresponding auxiliary N-donor ligands produced four novel complexes, {[Mn2(qptc)(4,4′-bibp)(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (1), [Mn2(qptc)(1,4-bib)(H2O)8]n (2), [Co2(qptc)(4,4′-bimbp)(H2O)2]n (3) and {[Co2(qptc)(1,3-dpp)(H2O)3]·H2O}n (4) (H4qptc = 1,1′:4′,1′′:4′′,1′′′-quaterphenyl-2,4,2′′′,4′′′-tetracarboxylic acid, 4,4′-bibp = 4,4′-bis(imidazolyl)biphenyl, 1,4-bib = 1,4-bis(1-imidazoly)benzene, 4,4′-bimbp = 4,4′-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)biphenyl, 1,3-dpp = 1,3-di(4-pyridyl)propane). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis showed that these complexes have different structures, including a 1D chain (2), a 2D layered structure (3), and a 3D network (1 and 4), depending on reaction conditions. Complex 1 is a 4,6-connected porous 3D framework with the point symbol (44·62)(44·610·8), which is composed of infinite 2D networks pillared by a 4,4′-bibp bridging ligand. 2 is a 1D zigzag chain, and the adjacent chains extend to a 3D supramolecular structure via non-covalent interactions involving contributions from carboxyl–water clusters. 3 is a 2D layer framework with an unprecedented topology, which are packed into a 3D supramolecular architecture by π⋯π and C–H⋯π interactions. 4 is a 4,6-connected 3D network with a Schläfli symbol of (3·4·54)(32·4·55·66·7). Furthermore, the magnetic susceptibility of complexes 1–4 have been investigated. 1 and 4 display weak antiferromagnetic behavior, 2 shows weak ferromagnetic interactions, and 3 exhibits different magnetic properties depending on temperature.

Introduction

Considerable numbers of experimental and theoretical studies on the design and synthesis of complexes have been published over the past few decades because of the fascinating structural diversity of these compounds. Complexes have interesting physical and chemical properties and potential applications in fields such as gas storage, adsorption and separation,1 catalysis,2 drug delivery,3 photochemical sensors,4 and molecular magnetism.5 Various factors, such as the reaction temperature and time, solvent system, pH value, ligands, and metal centre, strongly affect the structure of target complexes.6 Therefore, designing and synthesizing desired structures with versatile properties is a significant challenge. Multifunctional ligands have been extensively investigated owing to their versatile coordination modes and potential as hydrogen bond donors and acceptors. To date, diverse complexes based on aromatic multicarboxylate ligands have been reported7 that possess complicated geometric constructions and specific functional characteristics because there are several carboxylate coordination modes and the conjugated systems allow electron transport. Furthermore, the introduction of different auxiliary ligands would help to form various mixed-ligand complexes.8 Therefore, we chose an aromatic multicarboxylate ligand (1,1′:4′,1′′:4′′,1′′′-quaterphenyl-2,4,2′′′,4′′′-tetracarboxylic acid) as the primary ligand and four different N-donor ligands (4,4′-bis(imidazolyl)biphenyl; 1,4-bis(1-imidazoly)benzene; 4,4′-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)biphenyl; and 1,3-di(4-pyridyl)propane) as the auxiliary ligands. In H4qptc, the carboxylate groups can be partially or fully deprotonated under different synthesis conditions and can coordinate with metal ions in multiple ways to form a series of complexes with interesting topologies and properties. Meanwhile, the phenyl rings can twist slightly to accommodate different coordination modes because of steric hindrance. Moreover, the N-donor auxiliary ligands act as pillars to participate in the coordination, giving a complicated, stable framework.

In this work, four novel complexes, {[Mn2(qptc)(4,4′-bibp)(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (1), [Mn2(qptc)(1,4-bib)(H2O)8]n (2), [Co2(qptc)(4,4′-bimbp)(H2O)2]n (3), and {[Co2(qptc)(1,3-dpp)(H2O)3]·H2O}n (4), were prepared containing H4qptc and four N-donor auxiliary ligands of different lengths and flexibility. The magnetic properties of the complexes were studied and analyzed.

Experimental section

1,1′:4′,1′′:4′′,1′′′-Quaterphenyl-2,4,2′′′,4′′′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4qptc) was purchased from Jinan Henhua Sci. & Tec. Co. Ltd., and other materials were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, N and H) were performed on a Vario MACRO cube elemental analyzer. The IR (KBr pellet) spectra were recorded on a FTIR-8400S spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were carried out on a ZCT-A analyzer from room temperature to 800 °C under air at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 PC diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) over the 2θ range of 5–50° at room temperature. All magnetization data were collected on a Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 SQUID instrument under an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe at 2–300 K.

X-ray crystallography

Complexes 1–4 with appropriate dimensions were determined on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer. Data were collected at 298 K by using graphite-monochromatized Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were applied with the multiscan technique. The structures were solved by direct methods with SHELXS-97 and refined by the full-matrix least-squares technique with SHELXL-97.9 All non-hydrogen atoms were located by successive difference Fourier syntheses and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 with anisotropic thermal parameters.10 The approximate positions of hydrogen atoms for H2O were obtained from a difference Fourier map, restrained to the ideal configuration of water molecules, and fixed in the final stages of refinement. The crystallographic data collection and structure refinement parameters are shown in Table S1 (ESI).† Selected bond lengths and angles are summarized in Tables S2 and S3 (ESI).† Topological analyses for complexes 1, 3, and 4 were performed with TOPOS.11

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structures

Crystal structure of {[Mn2(qptc)(4,4′-bibp)(H2O)4]·2H2O}n (1). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 possesses a 3D porous framework with fsc 4,6-connecting topology and that it crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with a space group of Pī. The asymmetric unit contains one dependent Mn1 ion, half a qptc4− ligand, half a 4,4′-bibp ligand, two coordinated water molecules and one free water molecule. As shown in Fig. 1a, Mn1 is hexa-coordinated by three carboxylate O atoms [Mn1–O4 = 2.152(2) Å, Mn1–O1D = 2.158(3) Å and Mn1–O2E = 2.119(3) Å], one imidazole ring nitrogen atom [Mn1–N2 = 2.226(3) Å] and two water molecule O atoms [Mn1–O1W = 2.246(2) Å, Mn1–O2W = 2.327(3) Å], which gives a distorted octahedron geometry. The Mn1–N/O distances are consistent with the normal values.12 The equatorial plane of the Mn1 ion consists of O1D, O2E, N2, and O2W, whereas O1W and O4 are located in the apical positions along the axis, and the bond angle (O4–Mn1–O1W) is 174.62(14)° (symmetry codes: D: +X, −1 + Y, +Z; E: 1 − X, 1 − Y, 1 − Z).
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination environment of Mn(II) ions and the linkage mode of qptc4− (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity); (b) 2D layer constructed from qptc4− and dinuclear units; and (c) space filling representation of the 3D porous network along the b-axis.

The carboxylate groups of qptc4− assume different coordinating modes, which connect two neighboring Mn(II) ions to generate the dinuclear SBUs (Mn2(COO)2) with a Mn–Mn distance equal to 4.443 Å. During the reaction, four carboxylate groups of qptc4− are all deprotonated and adopt bridging (μ2-η1:η1) and monodentate (μ1-η1:η0) coordination modes. Each qptc4− links four Mn2(COO)2 units to give a lattice-shaped 2D layer structure along the a-axis (Fig. 1b). The 2D layers are further pillared by 4,4′-bibp to form a porous 3D structure (Fig. 1c). The void volume of 1 is 4.2% of the crystal volume (43.7 of the 1038.3 Å3 unit cell volume), calculated by PLANTON.13

To understand the complicated architecture, the topological method is used to simplify the structure. The dinuclear unit is a 6-connected node and qptc4− is a 4-connected node, respectively. This framework can be reduced to a 2-nodal 3D network with Schläfli symbol of (44·62)(44·610·8) (Fig. 2).

Crystal structure of [Mn2(qptc)(1,4-bib)(H2O)8]n (2). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group Pī system. The fundamental unit of 2 is composed of two Mn(II) ions, one qptc4−, one 1,4-bib, and eight coordinated water molecules. As shown in Fig. 3a, Mn1 and Mn2 are in the same coordinating environment: each Mn2+ ion is six-coordinated in a pseudo-octahedral coordination sphere by one O atom (O6 or O8) coming from carboxylate groups, one imidazole ring nitrogen atom (N1) and four water molecule O atoms (O1W, O2W, O3W and O4W or O5W, O6W, O7W and O8W). Otherwise, Mn1–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.086(4)–2.126(5) Å, and the Mn1–N bond length is 2.124(5) Å. The bond angles around Mn1 vary from 86.48(16) to 178.46(19)°. However, Mn2–O bond lengths are in the range 2.059(4)–2.126(5) Å, and the Mn2–N bond length is longer, 2.132(5) Å. The bond angles around Mn2 vary from 85.94(19) to 177.8(2)°. These bond lengths and angles around Mn1 are different from corresponding ones around Mn2, which may be because of steric hindrance. Among these coordinating atoms, O1W and N1 (or O7W and N4) of 1,4-bib are in axial positions. qptc4− forms a μ2-bridge in a monodentate binding mode, coordinating to the manganese atoms, and of the four carboxylate groups, only two 2,2′′′-carboxylate groups are coordinated, leaving the other two free. qptc4− and 1,4-bib connect with Mn2+ ions to generate a 1D zigzag chain framework (Fig. 3b).
Fig. 3 (a) Coordination environment for Mn(II) ions in 2 (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity); and (b) 1D zigzag chain for complex 2. Red and blue bonds correspond to qpta4− and 1,4-bib, respectively.

Discrete carboxyl–water clusters, including dodecamers, hexamers and tetramers, exist on both sides of the chain, stabilized by hydrogen bonds. As shown in Fig. 4, the hybrid dodecamer is composed of six coordinated water molecules and six carboxyl-O atoms, the hexamer is made up from three coordinated water molecules and three carboxyl-O atoms, and the tetramer consists of two coordinated water molecules and two carboxyl-O atoms. The striking feature of this dodecamer is that there are two five-membered rings with the same shape, composed of five O atoms and a six-membered ring with a chair conformation, containing two O⋯H⋯O three-centered hydrogen bonds. The hydrogen bond distances and angles of the clusters are in the range of 2.660(6)–2.994(6) Å and 121–171° (Table S4†). The O⋯O distances of liquid water and solid ice are 2.85 and 2.74 Å, respectively,14 which are longer than the shortest O⋯O distances of the carboxyl–water clusters, attributed to collective intermolecular interactions. The hydrogen-bonding interactions in the clusters play a critical role in interconnecting the 1D chains to form a 3D supramolecular network (Fig. 3c), stabilizing the framework in aqueous solution.

Crystal structure of [Co2(qptc)(4,4′-bimbp)(H2O)2]n (3). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that complex 3 belongs to the triclinic crystal system with the Pī space group. The repeating unit of 3 consists of one crystallographically independent Co(II) ion, half a qptc4−, one 4,4′-bimbp and one coordinated water. The central Co(II) ion is hexa-coordinated and presents a distorted octahedral CoN2O4 coordination as shown in Fig. 5a. The equatorial sites of the polyhedron are occupied by O2, O3, and O4 from the carboxylate groups in the monodentate and chelating bidentate modes, and N4 from 4,4′-bimbp, whereas the axial vertexes are taken up by N1A from 4,4′-bimbp and O1W. The average distance of Co1–O3 and Co1–O4 is 2.188 Å, which is much longer than that of Co1–O2 (2.046(4) Å) because O2 is in another coordination mode. The bond length of Co1–N4 (2.059(6) Å) is markedly shorter than that of Co1–N1A (2.123(5) Å), although the chemical environment of the N1A and N4 from 4,4′-bimbp is uniform (symmetry codes: A: −1 + X, −1 + Y, −1 + Z). The ligand of qptc4− in 3 is completely deprotonated and acts as a μ4 node to connect with four Co(II) ions, where four carboxyl groups adopt the coordination modes μ1-η1:η0 and μ1-η1:η1. As shown in Fig. 5b, the neighboring Co(II) ions are connected by qptc4− to generate a 1D structure, whereas the N-donor 4,4′-bimbp ligands link with neighboring Co(II) ions to form a wave-like 1D chain. The two kinds of 1D chains vertically cross each other, forming a 2D structure extending along the a-axis.
Fig. 5 (a) Coordination environment for Co(II) and qptc4− ions in 3 (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity); (b) 2D layer of 3 along the a-axis; and (c) unprecedented topology with a Schläfli symbol of (4·64·8)2(42·64).

Based on this connecting mode, each Co(II) connects four adjacent Co(II) ions, and is a 4-connected node, whereas each qptc4− also connects four metal ions, and is a 4-connected node. Therefore, the structure of 3 is defined as a 4,4-connected network with an unprecedented Schläfli symbol of (4·64·8)2(42·64) (Fig. 5c).

The adjacent 2D layers of 3 interact further to form a 3D supramolecular architecture through the π⋯π and C–H⋯π interactions (Fig. 6 and Table S5†). The planes of the two benzene rings (R4 and R4F) from two 4,4′-bimbp ligands are parallel with a centroid–centroid distance of 3.64 Å. The separation between the hydrogen atom of methylene and the center of the benzene ring from 4,4′-bimbp is 2.81 Å, and between the hydrogen atom of imidazole and the center of the benzene ring from qptc4− is 2.84 Å. This kind of non-covalent interactions plays a substantial role in consolidating the 3D network.

Crystal structure of {[Co2(qptc)(1,3-dpp)(H2O)3]·H2O}n (4). According to the single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 4, it is a 3D structure crystallizing in the monoclinic P21/n space group. The asymmetric unit of 4 is made up of two Co(II) ions, one qptc4−, one 1,3-dpp, three coordinated water molecules and one dissociative water molecule. In Fig. 7a, the two Co(II) ions have different coordination environments. Each Co1 is six-surrounded by five O atoms (O8, O4D, O2W, O3W, O4W) from two carboxylate groups and three coordinated water molecules, and one nitrogen atom (N2) from 1,3-dpp, which produces a tortile octahedral geometry similar to that in 3. Each Co2 is four-coordinated by three O atoms (O6, O1E and O3F) provided by three carboxylate groups, and one nitrogen atom (N1) offered by 1,3-dpp, creating an irregular tetrahedron. The bond lengths of Co1–O vary from 2.001(5) to 2.168(5) Å, and the Co1–N bond distance is 2.143(5) Å. The bond angles around Co1 are in the range of 80.3(2)–175.7(2)°. All values of bond lengths and angles around Co1 are similar to those reported for octahedral Co(II) frameworks.15 The bond lengths of Co2–O are in the range of 1.943(4)–1.965(4) Å, and the Co2–N bond distance is 2.081(5) Å, with bond angles of 91.3(2)–126.5(2)°. The bond lengths around Co2 are longer than those around Co1 because of the different coordination environment (symmetry codes: E: 1/2 + X, −1/2 − Y, 1/2 + Z; F: 1/2 − X, 1/2 + Y, 1/2 − Z).
Fig. 7 (a) Coordination environment for Co(II) ions in 4 (hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity); (b) 3D framework of 4 along a-axis with inset showing the dinuclear units existing in the framework; and (c) schematic of the (3·4·54)(32·4·55·66·7) topology of the 3D framework for 4.

The four carboxyl groups from qptc4− combine with Co(II) ions, which adopt the coordination modes μ2-η1:η1 and μ1-η1:η0, and the μ2-bridging carboxyl group (containing O3 and O4) connects with two divalent Co ions with equal coordination numbers to give a Co2 unit with a Co–Co distance of 4.063 Å. The dinuclear units are further organized by qptc4− to generate a complicated 3D network, where the 1,3-dpp ligand stabilizes the structure (Fig. 7b).

Because one qptc4− links to four dinuclear units, it is a four-connected node. The dinuclear unit can extend in six directions, and thus it is a six-connected node. Therefore, this 3D framework can be assigned as a 2-nodal 4,6-connected network with the Schläfli symbol of (3·4·54)(32·4·55·66·7) (Fig. 7c).

Effect of qptc4− on the framework

The coordinated modes and conformation of qptc4− play a crucial role in determining the structures of ideal complexes. In complexes 1–4, the four carboxyl groups of H4qptc are completely deprotonated and exhibit versatile coordination modes (Fig. 8). In 1, the carboxylate groups adopt a μ2-η1:η1 and monodentate μ1-η1:η0 coordination modes to link Mn2(COO)2 units to give a lattice-shaped 2D layer structure, further linked by 4,4′-bibp to form a porous 3D network with the point symbol (44·62)(44·610·8). In 2, only two 2,2′′′-carboxylate groups of the qptc4− adopt a monodentate binding mode to bind with two Mn(II) ions, which are further linked by 1,4-bib to generate a 1D zigzag chain. In 3, the qptc4− ligand assumes two different coordination modes (μ1-η1:η0 and μ1-η1:η1) to combine with the neighboring Co(II) ions and generate a 1D structure, which crosses the wave-like 1D chain composed of N-donor 4,4′-bimbp ligands and Co(II) ions to form a 2D structure. In 4, the four carboxyl groups from qptc4− combine with Co(II) ions adopting μ2-η1:η1 and μ1-η1:η0 modes to give Co2 units, which are further organized by qptc4− to generate a complicated 3D network. Furthermore, to meet different coordination environments, the side phenyl rings bearing two carboxylate groups in qptc4− tilt away from the connecting biphenyl rings with dihedral angles of 31.8(1), 38.5(3)–38.6(3), 34.3(3), and 43.4(3)–45.5(3)° in 1, 2, 3, and 4, respectively. The dihedral angle between the two side phenyl rings is 30.7(3)° in 2; however, the angles are almost zero in 1, 3 and 4, justifying the side phenyl rings of qptc4− are in the same plane.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) characterization and thermal analyses

The PXRD patterns of the complexes have been used to check the phase purity of the bulk samples in the solid state at room temperature. The experimental PXRD patterns of complexes 1–4 match with their corresponding simulated patterns obtained from the single-crystal X-ray diffraction data, demonstrating the good phase purity of the complexes (Fig. S2†). The differences in intensity arise from the preferred orientation of the powder samples.

The thermal stability of complexes 1–4 was determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) at 20–800 °C under air at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S3†). Only complex 3 will be discussed in more detail because the curves for 1–4 were similar. The curve shows that the first mass loss of 3.53% below 107 °C was slightly higher than the theoretical value of 2.86%, which corresponds to the weight loss of one of the coordinated water molecules from the framework. The second weight loss occurs from 349 to 464 °C with a residual weight of 12.76% (calc. for Co2O3 13.15%), which is attributed to the decomposition of 4,4′-bimbp and qptc4−.

Magnetic properties

The molar magnetic susceptibilities (χM) of 1–4 were measured under a 1000 Oe external field in the temperature range of 2–300 K. Their magnetic behaviours are characterized by χMT and χM−1 as a function of T (Fig. 9).
Fig. 9 Temperature dependence of χMT vs. T and χM−1vs. T for (a) 1; (b) 2; (c) 3; and (d) 4 at 1000 Oe. The solid lines for 1 and 2 show the best fit curves.

For complex 1, the experimental χMT value at room temperature is 9.04 cm3 K mol−1, which is much larger than the spin-only value (8.75 cm3 K mol−1) expected for two isolated Mn(II) ions with spin value 5/2 and Landé g value of 2.0. As shown in Fig. 9a, the χMT value of 1 decreases gently from room temperature to approximately 50.0 K, and sharply drops to a minimum of 2.0 K. The shape of the χMT versus T curve indicates an antiferromagnetic interaction, which may occur between adjacent Mn(II) ions bridged by carboxylic groups. The magnetic susceptibility data were fitted with a Mn(II)–Mn(II) dimer model with exchange constant J. For a similar Mn(II)–Mn(II) dimer system,16eqn (1) is written with a spin Hamiltonian Ĥ = −2JŜ1Ŝ2. The qptc4− and 4,4′-bibp bridges may mediate the weak intermolecular magnetic interaction between Mn ions. Therefore, eqn (1) was corrected by the molecule-field approximation to give eqn (2). The best fit gives J = −0.50 cm−1, g = 2.03 and zj′ = −0.18 cm−1 with an agreement factor of R = 2.52 × 10−6, where R = ∑[(χM)obsd. − (χM)calcd.)2/∑(χM)obsd.2] (Fig. 9a).

This result also suggests that there are antiferromagnetic interactions between Mn(II) ions.18

The magnetic properties of complex 3 from the χMT vs. T plots display interesting behavior (Fig. 9c). The value of χMT at 300 K is 3.25 cm3 K mol−1, which is greater than the spin-only value of 1.87 cm3 K mol−1 for one Co(II) ion with a spin value 3/2 and a Landé g value of 2.0, indicating that the orbital contribution is involved.20 As the temperature decreases from 300 K, the value of χMT slowly decreases to a minimum of 2.38 cm3 K mol−1 at 30 K, which suggests that the decrease is mainly from the spin–orbit coupling. χMT then increases rapidly to a maximum of 3.81 cm3 K mol−1 at 11 K. Below this temperature, χMT drops sharply because of the saturation effect and antiferromagnetic interactions.21,23a This behaviour is similar to that of the complexes reported in the literature.22 The χMT vs. T plots contain sharp peaks, which is typical of ferromagnetic behaviour induced by ferromagnetic interactions.23 The temperature dependence of the reciprocal susceptibilities, χM−1, of 3 obeys the Curie–Weiss law at 60–300 K, giving the parameters C = 3.49 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −22.92 K. The negative θ and the initial decrease of χMT all are ascribed to the thermal depopulation of the Kremer levels of the Co(II) ion. The isothermal field-dependent magnetization, M(H), at fields of up to 7 T was measured for 3. As shown in Fig. S4b,† the magnetization rises abruptly at low fields, reaching about 1.67 Nβ at 1.5 T, and then increases slowly to 2.21 Nβ at 7 T, which is lower than the theoretical saturation value of 3 Nβ for one spin-only Co(II) ion (S = 3/2 and g = 2.0).24 Therefore, the strong Zeeman effect leads to the spins populating an excited state. The hysteresis loop measured at 2.0 K is sigmoidal (Fig. S5†) with a coercive field of 272 Oe and a remnant magnetization of 0.46 emu g−1. To confirm the magnetic properties, alternating current (AC) magnetic measurements of 3 were performed (Fig. S6†). Both the real χM' and the imaginary χM" parts of the AC susceptibility data are frequency-independent with a critical temperature of about 15 K, indicating ferromagnetic ordering with spin-canting behaviour.23a,25,26

The magnetic behavior of 4 was also investigated. As illustrated in Fig. 9d, the measured χMT value at 300 K is 6.64 cm3 K mol−1, which is larger than the spin-only value of 3.75 cm3 K mol−1 for two uncoupled spins with g = 2.00, S = 3/2, owing to the contributions from the orbital angular momentum at high temperatures.27 The χMT value decreases gradually as the system temperature decreases, reaches a small plateau below 20 K, and then sharply decreases, reaching a minimum of 1.39 cm3 K mol−1 at 2.0 K. The shape of the χMT versus T curve is typical of a strong orbital contribution28 with antiferromagnetic coupling between Co(II) ions. The plots of χM−1vs. T above 50 K are fitted by the Curie–Weiss Law, which can give C = 7.18 cm3 K mol−1 and θ = −24.26 K. The negative Weiss constant may indicate an antiferromagnetic interaction even if there is an orbital contribution.29

Conclusions

Four novel transition metal complexes based on an aromatic tetracarboxylate acid have been synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Complex 1 is a porous 3D framework with a point symbol of (44·62)(44·610·8). 2 is a 1D zigzag chain, and the adjacent 1D chains are extended to a 3D supramolecular structure by non-covalent interactions. To the best of our knowledge, 3 is a 2D layer framework with an unprecedented topology and a Schläfli symbol of (4·64·8)2(42·64). 4 is a 3D network with the Schläfli symbol of (3·4·54)(32·4·55·66·7). Complexes 1 and 4 exhibit antiferromagnetic behavior, whereas 2 shows weak ferromagnetic interactions. The magnetic behavior of 3 varies depending on the system temperature. Further studies of polycarboxylate-based complexes containing H4qptc and other N-donor ligands are continuing.
