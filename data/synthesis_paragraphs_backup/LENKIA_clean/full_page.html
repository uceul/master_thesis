Synthesis of a honeycomb-like Cu-based metal–organic framework and its carbon dioxide adsorption behaviour†

Raúl Sanz *a, Fernando Martínez b, Gisela Orcajo a, Lukasz Wojtas c and David Briones a
aDepartment of Chemical and Energy Technology, Rey Juan Carlos University, Móstoles 28933, Spain. E-mail: raul.sanz@urjc.es; Fax: +34-91-488-70-68
bDepartment of Chemical and Environmental Technology, Rey Juan Carlos University, Spain
cDepartment of Chemistry, University of South Florida, USA

Received 15th September 2012 , Accepted 14th November 2012

First published on 14th November 2012

Herein, we report on a new Cu-based MOF material, Cu2(dhtp), structurally homologous to the honeycomb-like M2(dhtp) series. This has been crystallized under solvothermal conditions using copper nitrate and 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid as an organic linker, being the nature of the co-solvent in the synthesis media an important variable over the final physical properties of the material. The presence of isopropanol as a co-solvent leads to the formation of a pure crystalline phase with textural properties comparable to M2(dhtp) homologues. The interesting results in CO2 adsorption properties of this new material, especially its isosteric heat of adsorption, make it a suitable MOF to be further evaluated under real conditions of industrial CO2 capture.

Introduction

The synthesis of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs)1 has become one of the fastest growing research fields in chemistry over the past two decades. The motivation comes not only from their tunable pore-size, adjustable surface, and intriguing structural diversity, but also from their tantalizing potential applications in gas storage,2 separation3,4 and catalysis.5 By a judicious selection of the metals (or clusters) and the organic linkers with fixed geometry, a vast variety of MOFs have been assembled with predesigned atom-to-atom connectivity, structural topology, and nondispersed pore size distribution.6 So, this hybrid architecture of MOFs opens the possibility to design and synthesize a great variety of new porous materials, which are in principle able to display novel functionalities for these applications. Certain MOF materials have a particular feature of having exposed and unsaturated metal sites in their framework.7–10 These metal sites have shown a more decisive role in gas adsorption with respect to others fully coordinated. It has been also demonstrated that MOFs with those metal centers can behave as Lewis acid sites, highly effective for the adsorption of basic molecules.11,12

In the last decade several groups have focused their efforts on the preparation of three-dimensional honeycomb-like metal–organic frameworks using 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid (H2dhtp) as an organic linker. This linker in the presence of different divalent metal ions such as Zn2+, Mg2+, Ni2+, Fe2+, Co2+ or even mixed (Zn2+/Co2+) has developed MOF-7410,13–15 or also known as CPO-2716–18 structures with unsaturated metal sites that may show redox properties and microporous nets with enormous potential in catalysis, gas adsorption, especially for H2 and CO2,19 and separation and purification of light hydrocarbon mixtures.20–22 These interesting materials show both a highly reactive 5-coordinate metal species23 and a potentially reactive oxo group24 that contribute to a highly successful kinetics of carbon dioxide adsorption.

Among the divalent metals, the incorporation of Cu2+ ions in MOF-74 or CPO-27 structure can be of potential interest due to the Jahn–Teller effect on the distortion of the coordination environment of the Cu2+ ions.25 Such a distortion could account for a slightly lower adsorption enthalpy as compared to other transition metal homologues because of the higher distances between the Cu2+ sites and the adsorbate molecules. Nevertheless, because of the Jahn–Teller effect, residual solvent molecules can be easily and completely desorbed to produce full-desolvated open metal sites, in which essentially all the Cu2+ sites would be available for gas molecules binding.23

Although some research groups have investigated the synthesis of copper terephthalate metal–organic frameworks (Cu-tp) as a platform for the construction of MOFs with larger porosities,26–28 one might think that the combination of 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalate ions (dhtp) with the reactive 5-coordinate copper species could give rise to a structure homologous to the M2(dhtp) family, attractive for CO2 adsorption and catalytic applications. To the best of our knowledge, no Cu2(dhtp) MOF has been published yet, although Cu-based MOFs using 2-hydroxyterephthalic acid (Cu-htp) have been already published.29 Unlike the expected M2(dhtp) structure of the analogous MOF-74 series, the use of terephthalic and 2-hydroxyterephthalic acids promoted Cu2+ paddlewheel coordination geometries.

On the other hand, one of the influential parameters in the synthesis of MOF-type materials is the nature of the solvent. These can affect the product formation as well as the growth of a certain crystalline structure, due to their polarity and the solubility of the synthesis precursors in them. One example is the use of DMF or methanol as synthesis solvents in the system Al/aminoterephthalate/solvent, leading to different structures.30

In this work, we report on the synthesis and characterization of a new member of the family of M2(dhtp) coordination polymers formed by reaction of copper nitrate and 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid, as well as on the influence of the nature of the co-solvent on its crystallinity and final CO2 adsorption properties.

Experimental

Synthesis of Cu-based MOF materials

Cu-based materials were prepared by adding 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid (H2dhtp) and trihydrated copper nitrate(II) (0.500 g) over 20 mL of N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF). Once these reagents were dissolved, 1 mL of deionized water and/or 2-propanol were added to form a clear greenish solution of different metal:linker molar ratios (1.6, 2.2 and 2.8 Cu2+:1 H2dhtp). This mixture was heated up to 100 °C for 18 hours to yield reddish needle-shaped crystals. After decanting the mother liquor and washing with DMF, the solid product was immersed in methanol, an exchanging solvent with a low boiling point, for 24 hours, renewing it once by a new fresh solvent during this period of time. Finally, the samples were dried at 70 °C under vacuum (0.2 bar). These materials were denoted as Cux-S, where x is the metal–linker molar ratio and S indicates the co-solvent used: W or P for water and 2-propanol, respectively. Samples were finally activated removing the methanol under vacuum and heating prior to N2 and CO2 adsorption–desorption isotherms.

Characterization techniques

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were collected using a Bruker-AXS SMART-APEXII CCD diffractometer using CuKα (λ = 1.542 Å). Indexing was performed using APEX231 (difference vectors method). Data integration and reduction were performed using SaintPlus 6.01.32 Absorption correction was performed by the multi-scan method implemented in SADABS.33 Space groups were determined using XPREP implemented in APEX2.31 The structure was solved using SHELXS-97 (direct methods) and refined using SHELXL-97 (full-matrix least-squares on F2) contained in APEX231 and WinGX.34–37 All non-H atoms were found in the difference Fourier map and all (except disordered atoms with low site occupancies) were refined anisotropically. A careful investigation of the difference Fourier map revealed that the electron density peaks corresponding to occupationally and positionally disordered methanol and possibly DMF molecules coordinated to Cu cations. Distance restraints have been used to refine the disordered molecule of DMF. It was not possible to locate hydrogen atoms of disordered moieties. Hydrogen atoms of the benzene ring were placed in geometrically calculated positions and included in the refinement process using the riding model with isotropic thermal parameters: Uiso(H) = 1.2Ueq(–CH). The contribution of disordered solvent molecules in the channels was treated as diffuse using the Squeeze procedure implemented in Platon program.38,39 Crystal data and refinement conditions are shown in Table S1 of the ESI.† Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were obtained in a Philips XPERT PRO using CuKα (λ = 1.542 Å) radiation. In order to avoid the effects of preferred crystal orientation, the samples were grounded.

Simultaneous thermogravimetry and derivative thermogravimetry analyses (TGA/DTG) were carried out under a nitrogen atmosphere with an N2 flow of 100 mL min−1 at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 up to 700 °C, using a TA Instruments SDT 2860 apparatus. N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms were measured at 77 K on an AutoSorb equipment (Quantachrome Instruments). Samples of about 20–40 mg were previously in situ evacuated under high vacuum (<10−7 bar) for 5 h at 150–225 °C. The micropore surface area values were calculated by using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) model. The pore volume was estimated by using the Dubinin–Raduskevich equation. Non-local DFT calculations were used to estimate the pore size distribution assuming cylindrical pore geometry. Gas adsorption isotherms for carbon dioxide at 25–65 °C were obtained in a volumetric analyzer of type VTI HPVA-100 (Scientific Instrument). Approximately 100 mg of the samples was previously evacuated under vacuum (10−3 bar) and 225 °C, and then cooled down up to the analysis temperature (45 °C) in a thermostatic polyethylene glycol bath.

Results and discussion

Preliminary synthesis of Cu-based MOF material

Initially, a Cu-based MOF sample was synthesized according to the procedure published by Yaghi et al.13 for the Zn-MOF-74 but replacing the zinc precursor by a copper one (copper nitrate trihydrated). The rest of the synthesis conditions remained similar, such as the use of 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalic acid as an organic linker, the metal–linker molar ratio of 2.2 Cu2+:1 H2dhtp and the addition of an equivolumetric mixture of water–2-propanol as co-solvents. The powder X-ray diffraction pattern of this material is shown in Fig. 1, which indicates the presence of a crystalline structure in which most of the highly intense reflections match up with the MOF-74/CPO-27 phase.13
Fig. 1 PXRD and SEM micrograph of the sample Cu2.2-W-P. The scale of the micrograph corresponds to 20 μm.

The scanning electron micrograph of the sample (Fig. 1, inset) revealed large needle-shaped crystals similar to those shown by crystalline MOF-74/CPO-27 in the literature.13,18 Additionally, there was also observed a noteworthy agglomeration of smaller needle-shaped crystals.

The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA/DTG) in an N2 atmosphere (Fig. 2) shows two different weight loss regions. The first one is centered at around 85 °C which is attributed to the methanol solvent used for the storage of the sample. The other one takes place in the range of 350–490 °C with two DTG maximum peaks centered at 370 and 475 °C. These weight losses are due to the decomposition of the organic linker and this is clear evidence of the thermal stability of the Cu-based MOF framework, because the decomposition implies the structure collapse. This thermal stability is very similar to those shown by the copper terephthalate MOF (Cu-tp) reported by Tannenbaum et al.,28 and is slightly lower than the thermal stability of pure Zn-MOF-74/CPO-27 which is up to 400 °C.13


Fig. 2 TGA/DTG of the sample Cu2.2-W-P.

The porosity of the Cu-based MOF material was measured by nitrogen adsorption at 77 K. The basically type I adsorption–desorption isotherm revealed the permanent microporosity with a BET specific surface area of 734 m2 g−1, a pore volume of 0.38 cm3 g−1 and an average pore diameter of 10.1 Å.

Influence of the metal–linker molar ratio and the solvent nature

In order to study the influence of the metal–linker molar ratio on the synthesis of Cu-based MOF materials, two new syntheses were carried out with metal–linker molar ratios of 1.6 and 2.8, keeping constant the volumetric composition of the synthesis solvent as 20 DMF:1 W:1 P. The characterization data of these new samples did not evidence significant changes in the PXRD patterns and N2 adsorption isotherms as the metal–linker molar ratio was modified in the studied range.

The composition of the solvent for the synthesis of the Cu-based MOF material was also evaluated. In this case, two additional materials (Cu2.2-W and Cu2.2-P) were prepared with a metal–linker molar ratio of 2.2 and solvent volume ratios of 20:1 for DMF:water and DMF:2-propanol, respectively. These samples also displayed characteristic PXRD patterns of MOF-74/CPO-27 structures (Fig. 3). However, the use of DMF:2-propanol (Cu2.2-P) seemed to achieve an enhanced crystallinity with a higher intensity of all the diffraction peaks, including a better resolution of peaks located at 17.1 and 29.2 degrees. In this sense, despite there being in all samples a similar crystalline phase, the SEM images of the sample synthesized with DMF:2-propanol showed a more homogeneous morphology of larger needle-shaped crystals. The other samples, synthesized with DMF:water and DMF:water:2-propanol, showed a more heterogeneous crystal size based on small needle-shaped crystals packed together with isolated larger ones (Fig. 3, inset). These images seem to be in agreement with the enhanced crystallinity of the Cu2.2-P sample from the PXRD patterns. On the other hand, also noteworthy is the disappearance of the diffraction peak at 10.6 degrees for the Cu2.2-P sample, which could be attributed to the effect of water on the copper coordination environment of crystalline Cu-based MOF material. In fact, Dietzel et al. have demonstrated how the presence of water in different CPO-27 isostructural materials affects their PXRD patterns, modifying their unit cell parameters.18 Moreover, it must be pointed out that samples analyzed by PXRD were not previously activated under exhaustive vacuum and heating conditions.


Fig. 3 PXRD patterns and SEM micrographs of the samples synthesized with different solvent compositions. The scale of each micrograph corresponds to 20 μm.

The TGA/DTG results (Fig. S1–S3 in the ESI†) revealed again a thermal stability of all the samples up to 300 °C with a decomposition of the framework between 350 °C and 490 °C. N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms at 77 K showed typical microporous type I isotherms (Fig. S4 in the ESI†). The main characterization data calculated from the N2 adsorption isotherms are summarized in Table 1. The use of 2-propanol in the solvent media for the synthesis of Cu2(dhtp) materials promotes the increasing of the BET surface area and pore volume. These results could be related to the different crystallinity and the crystal structure of the three Cu2(dhtp), as reported for MOF-5 materials by Saha et al.,40 or a less efficient activation of the materials when water is coordinated in the framework.

Finally, in order to confirm the obtainment of a Cu-based MOF belonging to M2(dhtp) honeycomb-like family, single crystal X-ray diffraction of the best crystallized sample, as-synthesized Cu2.2-P, has been carried out. Regarding its crystallographic data, an isostructural framework to those of the MOF-7413 and CPO-27 family16–18 has been obtained, whose molar composition corresponds to Cu2(dhtp)(CH3OH)2.73(DMF)0.25(H2O)1.33, where dhtp corresponds to 2,5-dihydroxyterephthalate dianion. Fig. 4 shows a bidimensional view of the honeycomb structure along the [001] direction.


Fig. 4 Bidimensional view of the Cu2(dhtp)(CH3OH)2.73(DMF)0.25(H2O)1.33 honeycomb structure along the [001] direction.

All the oxygen atoms of the organic ligands are involved in the coordination of copper atoms. This accounts for five of the oxygen atoms coordinating each copper atom, whereas the sixth coordinative bond is normally occupied by a terminal CH3OH molecule, and to a lesser extent by H2O or DMF molecules, which point toward the cavity. Because of the helical arrangement of the CuO5 units after solvent removal, the nearest Cu atoms are exposed in one-dimensional channels of 10.3 × 5.5 Å. Each Cu has three carboxyl groups and two hydroxy groups are bound as doubly bridging. The crystal data found were: C11.17H18.02Cu2N0.25O10 (Mr = 426.71, hexagonal, R-3, a = b = 25.9972(11) c = 6.2587(3), V = 3663.3(3) Å3, Z = 9, Dc = 1.741 g cm−3).

The correct activation of MOF materials is an essential step. This activation lies in the elimination of all the guest/coordinated molecules accommodated in the pores of the solid sorbent. In order to optimize the activation conditions, a thermo-X-ray diffraction analysis of the Cu2.2-P sample (Fig. 5) reveals that the structure of the sample is thermally stable until 275 °C, since at 350 °C the diffraction peaks corresponding to Cu-MOF phase disappear almost completely, which matches very well with its TGA/DTG profiles (Fig. S2 in the ESI†).


Fig. 5 Thermo-XRD patterns of the Cu2.2-P sample.

On the basis of these results, N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms for the sample Cu2.2-P were carried out, increasing the activation temperature from 150 °C until 225 °C in the evacuation treatment. This increase of temperature allows increasing the BET surface area and the pore volume up to 937 m2 g−1 and 0.44 cm3 g−1 respectively, which motivates the development of more exhaustive activation methods that enable the complete removal of solvent molecules, in particular, those accommodated over the metal open sites of the Cu2(dhtp) MOF structure.

The comparison of the BET specific surface area of this new Cu2(dhtp) with others belonging to the M2(dhtp) series shows an intermediate value between those of Zn2(dhtp)/MOF-74 and Ni2(dhtp) (Table 2).

Moreover, the BET surface area of this new Cu-MOF belonging to M2(dhtp) series is approximately two-fold higher than that reported for Cu-htp using 2-hydroxyterephthalic acid as a linker.29

Evaluation of carbon dioxide adsorption

Metal organic framework materials have shown outstanding adsorption capacities for CO2 storage due to the extraordinary development of microporous structures with pore volumes up to 3.6 cm3 g−1 for MOF-21043 that allow overcoming the CO2 adsorption capacity of other conventional porous materials.44 Likewise, these materials can also have great interest in the CO2 capture for gas separation applications by means of the ability of the framework components to enhance the affinity of the internal pore surface toward CO2 molecules. In this sense, the Cu-based materials obtained in this work, using different co-solvents, were tested in CO2 adsorption.

As in the case of the textural properties from N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms (Table 1), the CO2 adsorption capacity at 1 bar and 45 °C increases from 3.0 wt% for the sample synthesized with DMF:water (Cu2.2-W) to 5.0 wt% for the sample synthesized with DMF:2-propanol (Cu2.2-P) (Table 3). This behaviour is also related to the facts of a higher crystallinity and a more effective activation of coordinately saturated copper sites by the solvent removal when 2-propanol is employed, leading to higher affinity between the material structure and the CO2 molecules.

The influence of temperature on CO2 adsorption has been also studied for the Cu2.2-P sample synthesized with DMF and 2-propanol. A typical physisorption behavior can be observed in the isotherms shown in Fig. 6, since the amount of CO2 adsorbed decreases as the temperature increases; therefore, no chemisorption of CO2 is expected in this kind of materials. The adsorption capacity also strongly depends on the pressure, always rising in this studied pressure range, for which a plate is not reached in any of the three cases.


Fig. 6 CO2 adsorption–desorption isotherms at different temperatures of Cu2(dhtp)/Cu2.2-P.

On the other hand, the enthalpy of the CO2 adsorption is a critical parameter that has a significant influence on the performance of a given material for CO2 capture applications. The magnitude of the enthalpy of adsorption dictates the affinity of the pore surface toward CO2, which in turn plays a crucial role in determining the adsorptive selectivity and the energy required to release the CO2 molecules during regeneration. Precise control of the binding strength between CO2 molecules and metal–organic frameworks is essential to lower the energy requirements of the capture process. Specifically, the use of a material that binds CO2 too strongly would increase the regeneration cost owing to the large quantity of energy required in order to break the framework–CO2 interactions. Meanwhile, if the enthalpy of adsorption is too low, although the material would be more readily regenerated, the purity of the captured CO2 would be lowered due to the decreased adsorption selectivity, and the volume of the adsorbent beds would also be increased because of the lower density of CO2 adsorption.

Owing to the presence of a variety of binding environments for CO2 within the pores of metal–organic frameworks, the enthalpy of CO2 adsorption is frequently expressed by the isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) as a function of the quantity of CO2 adsorbed. The Qst value is a parameter that describes the average adsorption enthalpy for an adsorbing gas molecule at a specific surface coverage (n). The isosteric heat of carbon dioxide adsorption was calculated by means of the Clausius–Clapeyron (eqn (1)), employing the isotherms obtained at 25, 45 and 65 °C fitted with the SIPS model.45 The Qst is the isosteric heat of adsorption (kJ mol−1), P the pressure (Pa), R the ideal gas constant (00083143 kJ K−1 mol−1) and T the temperature (K).

This theoretical model describes well the gas adsorption behavior of microporous materials, not only in the low pressure range when monolayer is formed, but also at higher pressures considering multilayer coverage.

The isosteric heat of adsorption value of our Cu-based MOF was calculated and compared with its M2(dhtp) homologues for a relatively high CO2 coverage (0.3), at which a gas monolayer over the metal clusters is probably reached, so that the average interaction between CO2 molecules and the MOF framework can be estimated. Table 4 shows that the Qst of the Cu2(dhtp) MOF material synthesized in this work is lower than the M2(dhtp) series based on Mg, Ni, Co and Zn metals. This expected result is related to the Jahn–Teller effect, which is produced by the distortion of the coordination environment of the Cu2+ ions.25 This fact leads to a slightly lower adsorption enthalpy as compared to the other transition metal homologues because of the higher distances between the Cu2+ sites and the adsorbate molecules. So, although this new material, in general, shows a lower CO2 adsorption capacity, this low affinity between CO2 molecules and the MOF structure is an advantage for the regeneration of the material and the reutilization in several adsorption–desorption cycles, which is an important parameter for adsorbent materials in CO2 capture for industrial applications.

Conclusions

In this work, we have successfully synthesized a novel Cu2(dhtp) material based on copper structurally homologous to the honeycomb M2(dhtp) series.

The influence of the metal–linker molar ratio and the composition of the solvent in the synthesis of this new material have been studied, being the second one the most influencing variable over the crystallinity of the sample. The specific surface area, pore volume and CO2 adsorption capacity increase when 2-propanol is used together with DMF as the solvent medium instead of water, due to the increase of crystallinity and somehow a more effective activation of the exposed copper sites.

The relevant textural properties, in terms of specific surface area and pore volume, as well as the low isosteric heat of CO2 adsorption suggest that this new Cu2(dhtp) is a promising material for CO2 separation/purification processes.
