Abstract

The self‐assembly of quinolones with metal salts in the presence of aromatic polycarboxylato ligands or chloride anions produced three novel 3D metal–quinolone coordination polymers, namely [Cu3(ppa)2(oba)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1), [Cd3(ppa)2(bpdc)2(H2O)2]·5H2O (2), and [Cd2(sara)2(μ2‐Cl)2]·5H2O (3) [ppaH = pipemidic acid, saraH = sarafloxacin, oba = 4,4′‐oxybis(benzoate), bpdc = 4,4′‐biphenyldicarboxylate]. Their structures were determined by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analyses, and they were further characterized by elemental analyses, IR spectra, X‐ray powder diffraction, and TG analyses. Compound 1 represents a novel 3D (3,4)‐connected network with a (6·82)2(6·82)2(62·82·102) topology constructed from Cu atoms and ppa ligands. Compound 2 represents the first example of a 3D metal–quinolone complex with a (3,4,6)‐connected (52·6)2(52·64)(54·66·72·8·92) topology. Compound 3 is an unprecedented 3D metal–quinolone complex constructed from Cl– ions connecting the {Cd(sara)}nn+ layers. Furthermore, the luminescent properties of compounds 2 and 3 are discussed.

Abstract

Three novel 3D metal–quinolone complexes have been prepared by the self‐assembly of quinolone antibacterial agents and metal salts in the presence of polycarboxylato ligands.

Introduction

Current interest in the crystal engineering of coordination polymer frameworks not only stems from their tremendous potential applications in porous materials, optical devices, magnetism, microelectronics, and heterogeneous catalysis, but also from their intriguing variety of molecular architectures and topologies.1,2 Aided by the rapid growth of network‐based crystal engineering, a large number of attractive networks with various structural motifs have been designed and extensively discussed in comprehensive reviews by Yaghi, Kitagawa, Rao, Chen, and their co‐workers.1b,1c,3 In contrast, despite the fact that many organic drugs have the potential to act as ligands and the resulting metal–drug complexes are particularly important both in coordination chemistry and biochemistry,4–8 the study of metal–drug complexes is still in its infancy; therefore, the exploration of feasible synthetic routes to construct metal–drug complexes is still a great challenge for synthetic chemists, and further research is necessary to enrich and develop this field.

Quinolones are a large family of structurally related highly potent broad‐spectrum antibacterial agents widely used in clinical practice for the treatment of a variety of infections. Pipemidic acid (ppaH) and sarafloxacin (saraH) (Scheme S1), synthetic antibacterial agents belonging to this family, are very active against aerobic Gram‐negative microorganisms.[5–7,9,10] The mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial drugs are either their inhibition of DNA gyrase (Topoisomerase II), an essential bacterial enzyme that maintains superhelical twists in DNA, or their interaction with the DNA molecule through a metal complex intermediate.4–7 Recently, new theoretical‐experimental studies on the activity of quinolones and their metal complexes have supported the hypothesis that the mechanism of action of quinolones could be mediated by a metal ion.5,8 Despite the important role that the metal ions may play in this system, the structurally characterized high‐dimensional metal–quinolone complexes, to the best of our knowledge, are still rare, and only one 3D example has been reported by us.11 This may be attributed, at least in part, to the fact that the bulky quinolone ligands play a “passive” role by occupying coordination sites on the metal centers and providing steric constraints, which may restrain spatial extension of the skeleton and thus prevent the formation of a high‐dimensional structure. Therefore, it is still a current challenge to synthesize high‐dimensional extended metal–quinolone complexes.

Inspired by the aforementioned considerations, we used an aromatic polycarboxylate as a connector in an attempt to link the discrete metal–quinolone motif into high‐dimensional extended metal–quinolone complexes through their bridging ability. In our experiment, the aromatic polycarboxylate and quinolone are simultaneously introduced based on the following considerations: (i) The use of carboxylato‐bridged metal clusters as second building units (SBUs) to build polymeric extended structures is relatively mature;12,13 therefore, the topologies of the resulting structures can be predicted and designed.14 (ii) Coordination polymers constructed from the mixed quinolones and aromatic polycarboxylato ligands are still very rare, which may be attributed to the fact that two carboxyl‐containing ligands would yield more negative charges that would hamper charge balancing.6a,6b (iii) The combination of quinolone and polycarboxylate may lead to the formation ofunprecedented structures with novel topological features. Because poor solubility of the ligands, and the mixing of metal salt and quinolone solution usually results in a precipitation, making it difficult to grow single crystals of the complexes, we employed a hydrothermal technique and successfully synthesized three novel 3D metal–quinolone complexes, namely, [Cu3(ppa)2(oba)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1), [Cd3(ppa)2(bpdc)2(H2O)2]·5H2O (2), and [Cd2(sara)2(μ2‐Cl)2]·5H2O (3) [oba = 4,4′‐oxybis(benzoate), bpdc = 4,4′‐biphenyldicarboxylate]. The syntheses and crystal structures of these compounds are reported here. This work may provide new structural information that will aid in understanding the mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial agents.

Results and Discussion

Crystal Structures

Complex [Cu3(ppa)2(oba)2(H2O)4]·H2O (1)

Single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction studies reveal that 1 is an extended 3D network with a (6·82)2(6·82)2(62·82·102) topology. As illustrated in Figure 1, the Cu1 atom is in the center of a distorted octahedron, which is defined by two oxygen atoms [Cu1–O 2 × 2.682(4) Å] from two ppa ligands, two oxygen atoms [Cu1–O 2 × 1.9275(4) Å] from two oba ligands, and two terminal aqua ligands [Cu1–OW 2 × 1.976(4) Å], while the Cu2 center is coordinated by two oxygen atoms [Cu2–O 1.923(4)–1.930(4) Å] from one ppa ligand, one nitrogen atom [Cu2–N 2.027(3) Å] from the piperazinyl group of one ppa ligand, and one oxygen atom [Cu2–OW 2.472(13) Å] from a terminal water molecule to complete a distorted square‐pyramidal configuration. The ppa ligand in 1 acts as a tetradentate ligand connecting three Cu atoms; the carboxylate group adopts a bidentate bridging mode, while the carbonyl and piperazinyl groups exhibit a monodentate mode (Scheme S2a). To the best of our knowledge, this coordination mode of the ppa ligand has not been observed in other metal–quinolone complexes. The oba ligand adopts a bis(monodentate) coordination mode to connect two Cu atoms (Scheme S3a). On the basis of these connection modes, three Cu atoms are connected by two carboxylate groups of two ppa ligands to form a trinuclear copper unit [Cu3(CO2)2]. Each trimetallic unit is further connected by four ppa and four oba ligands (Figure S1) to generate an unusual 3D framework (Figure 2a). The overall 3D frame can be described as a (3,4)‐connected network with a (6·82)2(6·82)2(62·82·102) topology (Figure 2b) with the Cu(2) atoms and ppa ligands acting as the three‐connected nodes and the Cu(1) atoms functioning as the four‐connected nodes (Figure S2).

Complex [Cd3(ppa)2(bpdc)2(H2O)2]·5H2O (2)

Compared with compound 1, when oba was replaced by bpdc, a structurally different 3D metal–quinolone complex was formed in 2. As illustrated in Figure 3, the asymmetric unit has one and a half Cd atoms, one ppa ligand, one bpdc ligand, and three and a half H2O molecules. The Cd1 center is coordinated in a distorted octahedron geometry to three oxygen atoms [Cd–O 2.2625(13)–2.3390(14) Å] from two ppa ligands, one oxygen atom [Cd–O 2.2650(15) Å] from one bpdc ligand, one nitrogen atom [Cd–N 2.2963(17) Å] from the piperazinyl group of one ppa ligand, and one oxygen atom [Cd–OW 2.3381(16) Å] from the terminal water molecule, whereas the Cd2 center is ligated by two pairs of chelating oxygen atoms [Cd–O 2.2156(15)–2.5584(16) Å] from two bpdc ligands, two monodentate coordinated carboxylate oxygen atoms [Cd–O 2.4706(14) Å] from two ppa ligands, and one aqua ligand [Cd–OW 2.278(3) Å] to complete a distorted monocapped trigonal prism configuration. Unlike in compound 1, the ppa ligand in 2 acts as a pentadentate ligand connecting four CdII ions; the carboxylate group adopts a tridentate bridging mode, while the carbonyl and piperazinyl groups exhibit a monodentate mode (Scheme S2b). To the best of our knowledge, this coordination mode of the ppa ligand has not been observed in other metal–quinolone complexes. Two carboxylate groups of the bpdc ligand display two kinds of coordination modes, that is, the bidentate chelating and monodentate mode (Scheme S3b). On the basis of these connection modes, the Cd (Cd1 and Cd2) atoms are connected by μ3‐carboxylato ends of ppa ligands to form an infinite 1D {Cd3(COO)2}n4n+ chain running along the c axis (Figure 4a and 4b), which serves as a structurally directing rod‐shaped SBU. As shown in Figure 4c, adjacent rod‐shaped SBUs are further interconnected by bpdc and ppa ligands to generate a 3D open framework with 1D rhombic channels (14.699 × 14.699 Å) along the [001] direction, which are occupied by free water molecules (Figure S3). The dehydrated framework provides 14.2 % of void space of the total crystal volume (844.2 Å3 out of the 5945.0 Å3 unit‐cell volume, Figure 4c).15c So far, the 3D metal–quinolone complexes are extremely rare, as evidenced in a recent review.5 To the best of our knowledge, only one 3D interpenetrated metal–ppaH complex was reported by our group very recently.11 To understand the intricate 3D structure of 2, the network can be simplified as a unique trinodal (3,4,6)‐connected network with a (52·6)2(52·64)(54·66·72·8·92) topology (Figure 5). In this simplification, the ppa ligands and Cd2 atoms act as three‐connected nodes and four‐connected nodes, respectively, while dinuclear Cd1 clusters function as six‐connected nodes (Figure S4).

Complex [Cd2(sara)2(μ2‐Cl)2]·5H2O (3)

When ppaH was replaced by saraH, a structurally different 3D metal–quinolone complex was formed in 3. A single‐crystal X‐ray structural analysis shows that the asymmetric unit consists of one Cd atom, one sara ligand, one Cl– ion, and two and a half free H2O molecules (Figure 6). The crystallographically independent Cd atom adopts a distorted octahedron geometry, being coordinated by three oxygen atoms [Cd–O 2.2712(19)–2.379(2) Å] from two sara ligands, two chloride ions [Cd–Cl 2.5049(8)–2.7705(9) Å], and one nitrogen atom [Cd–N 2.347(2) Å] from the piperazinyl group of the sara ligand. Because the terminal nitrogen atom of the piperazine is also involved in the binding to the metal atom, sara acts as a tetradentate bridging ligand to link three metal centers (Scheme S2c); one μ3‐carboxylato oxygen atom (O1) bridges two CdII ions, while the carbonyl oxygen atom and nitrogen atom of the piperazine ring adopt a monodentate coordination mode. To the best of our knowledge, this coordination mode of the sara ligand has not previously been observed in metal–quinolone complexes. On the basis of this connection mode, the Cd atoms are bridged by sara ligands to generate a 2D rhombic grid (Figure S5). As shown in Figures 7a and S6, adjacent 2D rhombic grids are further extended by μ2‐Cl ions into a 3D open framework with a 1D channel along the a axis, which are occupied by free water molecules (Figure S6b). A PLATON analysis showed that the effective free volume of 3 is 16.6 % of the crystal volume (388.0 Å3 out of the 2330.6 Å3 unit‐cell volume).15c Moreover, from the presence of inorganic Cl– ions, which connect the [Cd(sara)]+ units, there are 1D Cd–O2–Cd–Cl2–Cd chains along the a axis in the 3D network (Figure 7b). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example of a metal–saraH complex and the first example of a 3D metal–quinolone complex that is constructed without other organic ligands.

By inspection of the structures of 1–3, it is believed that the polydentate bridging coordination mode of the quinolone plays a key role in the formation of the 3D structure. According to the review by Turel,5 the quinolone molecules with the piperazinyl group can exist in four possible forms: an acidic cation QH2+, a neutral nonionized species QH, an intermediate zwitterion QH± and a basic anion Q–, depending on the pH. At low pH values, both the piperazinyl group and the carboxyl group are protonated, whereas at high pH values, neither is protonated. That is to say, the coordination mode of quinolones depends on the pH of the solution. In a basic solution, the N atom of the piperazinyl group can coordinate to the metal ion, and the quinolones could equally well be used as a long bridging ligand (compounds 1–3), while in a weak acidic solution, the N atom of piperazinyl group is protonated and fails to coordinate to the metal ion.6a,6b Therefore, the pH plays a crucial role in tuning the dimension of the metal–quinolone complexes. It is of great interest to investigate the effect of the pH in metal–quinolone systems, because it helps us to understand the correlation between the reaction acidity and the resulting structure. This thus leads to the possibility of predicting the structure of the metal–quinolone complex obtained.

Thermal Stability Analyses

In order to examine the thermal stability of compounds 2 and 3, thermal gravimetric (TG) analyses were carried out. For 2, there are three stages of weight loss over the temperature ranges 35–145 °C (8.55 %), 270–425 °C (31.50 %), and 425–955 (34.22 %) (Figure S7), corresponding to the release of guest and coordinated H2O, bpdc and ppa ligands, respectively. The remaining weight (26.54 %) indicated that the final product was CdO (calcd. 24.88 %). Interestingly, when a freshly ground sample was heated at 150 °C for 24 h to release the water molecules, the crystallinity and network integrity of 2 was retained, as demonstrated by the X‐ray powder diffraction (XRPD) pattern of the dehydrated sample of 2 (Figure S10). In the DSC curve of 2, the endothermic peak at 139 °C corresponds to the release of lattice and coordinated water molecules; the endothermic peaks at 323 and 370 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the bpdc ligands; the exothermic peaks at 740 and 924 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the ppa ligands.

For 3 (Figure S8), the first weight loss is 8.09 % from 34 to 120 °C, which corresponds to the loss of the lattice water molecules (calcd. 7.79 %). The second weight loss (21.67 %) from 260 °C to 460 °C and the last weight loss (46.79 %) from 460 °C to 800 °C can be assigned to the burning of the organic groups. The total weight loss of 77.51 % is slightly more than the calculated value of 75.71 % if the final product is assumed to be CdO and CdCl2 in a molar ratio of 1:1. In the DSC curve of 3, the endothermic peak at 46 °C corresponds to the release of lattice water molecules; the exothermic peaks at 318, 563, 663, 716, and 743 °C are attributed to the decomposition of the sara and oba ligands.

X‐ray Powder Diffraction

In order to check the phase purity of the products, the X‐ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns of 2 and 3 were checked at room temperature. As shown in Figures S9 and S10, the peak positions of simulated and experimental XRPD patterns are in good agreement with each other, indicating the phase purity of the products. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples. Notably, the XRPD patterns of the dehydrated and as‐synthesized sample of 2 are nearly identical (Figure S9), which indicates that the framework is retained after the removal of water molecules by heating the sample at 150 °C for 24 h.

Photoluminescence Properties

Taking into account the excellent luminescent properties of d10 metal complexes, the luminescence of 2 and 3 was investigated. It can be observed that intense emissions occur at 411 nm (Figure 8a, λex = 359 nm) for 2, and 448 nm (Figure 8b, λex = 341 nm) for 3. To understand the nature of the emission band, the photoluminescence properties of ppaH and saraH were analyzed. It was found that two weak emissions at 506 nm and 451 nm could be observed for free ppaH and saraH ligands, respectively (Figure S11). In addition, recent studies have shown that the solid H2bpdc ligand exhibits one weak emission at 404 nm.4c Therefore, the emission of 2 and 3 may be assigned to the intraligand fluorescent emission. The enhancement of luminescence may be attributed to ligand chelation to the metal center, which effectively increases the rigidity of the ligand and reduces the loss of energy by radiationless decay.16 These observations indicate that compounds 2 and 3 may be excellent candidates for potential photoactive materials.

Gas Adsorption Study

The gas adsorption property of desolvated 2 toward N2 (kinetic diameter = 3.64 Å) at 77 K was studied in order to determine its potential use for gas storage and separation. To our surprise, almost no N2 sorption is observed at 77 K despite the stable framework (Figure S12). One of the possible reasons is that the channel aperture sizes of 2 were too small for N2 gas to enter.

Conclusions

We have successfully prepared three novel 3D metal–drug complexes by the self‐assembly of quinolone antibacterial agents with metal salts in the presence of aromatic polycarboxylato ligands under hydrothermal conditions. The successful isolation of these species not only enriches the realm of metal–quinolone complexes, but also provides new structural information that may help to understand the mechanisms of action of the quinolone antibacterial drugs. This work further demonstrates that the introduction of polycarboxylato ligands into metal–quinolone systems is a promising strategy to synthesize high‐dimensional metal–quinolone complexes, thus opening interesting perspectives in the study of metal–drug complexes with particular functions.

Experimental Section

General Materials and Methods: All chemicals were commercially purchased and used without further purification. The elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were carried out with a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN Elemental Analyzer. Cu and Cd were determined by a tps‐7000 Plasma‐Spec(I) inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometer (ICP‐AES). IR spectra were recorded over the range 400–4000 cm–1 with a Bio‐Rad FTS‐185 FTIR spectrophotometer by using KBr pellets. TG analyses were performed with a NETZSCH STA 449C instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C min–1. Excitation and emission spectra were recorded with an F‐4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer equipped with a 150 W xenon lamp as the excitation source. XRPD studies were performed with an XD‐3 diffractometer by using Cu‐Kα (λ = 1.5406 Å) radiation at room temperature.

X‐ray Crystallography: Suitable single crystals with dimensions of 0.36 × 0.31 × 0.23 mm for 1, 0.39 × 0.37 × 0.32 mm for 2, and 0.37 × 0.34 × 0.28 mm for 3 were glued on a glass fiber. Absorption corrections were applied by using the multi‐scan technique. The structures of 1–3 were solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least squares on F2 using the SHELXL‐97 software.15a,15b All of the non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The organic hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically. The aqua hydrogen atoms were located from difference Fourier maps. The disordered C atoms (C12, C13, C15, and C16) of the sara ligand in compound 3 were refined by using C atoms split over two sites, with a total occupancy of 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for 1–3 are listed in Table S1. The crystal data and structure refinement of compounds 1–3 are summarized in Table 1. CCDC‐844045 (for 1), CCDC‐821428 (for 2), and CCDC‐821429 for 3 contain the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre via www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.
