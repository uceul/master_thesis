    Abstract

    Two metal−organic frameworks (MOFs) having interesting topologies, one based on sra (SrAl2, also known as zeolite ABW) and another exhibiting an unprecedented (3,4)-connected topology, have been synthesized via the assembly of 3,4-pyridinedicarboxylic acid (3,4-H2PDC) and various metal ions. The proximal intramolecular distance of the ligand-carboxylates leads to their steric torsion out of the plane of the pyridine core, resulting in diverse three-dimensional MOFs. In each instance, the doubly deprotonated multifunctional ligands (3,4-PDC) coordinate with the divalent metal ions via MNxOy heterocoordination. The resulting MOFs possess magnetic or fluorescent properties.

    Synopsis

    Two metal−organic frameworks (MOFs) having interesting topologies, one based on sra (SrAl2, also known as zeolite ABW) and another exhibiting an unprecedented (3,4)-connected topology, have been synthesized via the assembly of 3,4-pyridinedicarboxylic acid (3,4-H2PDC) and various metal ions. The proximal intramolecular distance of the ligand-carboxylates leads to their steric torsion out of the plane of the pyridine core, resulting in diverse three-dimensional MOFs. In each instance, the doubly deprotonated multifunctional ligands (3,4-PDC) coordinate with the divalent metal ions via MNxOy heterocoordination. The resulting MOFs possess magnetic or fluorescent properties.

    Crystalline porous compounds are a significant class of solid-state materials. From catalysis to separation to gas storage, such materials are utilized in a myriad of applications.1 However, the need for logical approaches to design functional materials with enhanced or novel physical properties is ever increasing. A metal−ligand directed assembly approach offers great potential toward functional metal−organic frameworks (MOFs). This emerging new class of materials, found to possess interesting properties,2 could pave the way toward rational synthesis of solid-state materials.
    The construction of MOFs from simple building blocks typically leads to the most symmetrical assembly, and deviation from these so-called default structures2a to access more complex structures is a lasting challenge. We have recently shown that it is possible to synthesize nondefault metal−organic assemblies using the single-metal ion molecular building block (MBB) approach.3-5 We have validated the utility of MBBs, combining multifunctional ligands (possessing concurrently N- and O-coordinating moieties) with heterocoordination of single metal ions (MNxOy), as useful tools toward the synthesis of two- and three-dimensional (2D and 3D) MOFs, including supramolecular isomers, through variance of directing agents.4,6 Elaboration of new synthetic pathways or approaches to access nondefault or novel topologies and, more importantly, development of a better understanding of their assemblage, correlating building block geometrical information to the resultant structure, are of major scientific interest. Herein we elaborate on the network diversity that can be achieved through utilization of 3,4-pyridinedicarboxylic acid (3,4-H2PDC) as a nonplanar multifunctional ligand, to generate nondefault 3D networks.
    Unlike planar 3,5-H2PDC, relocation of one carboxylate to a more proximal intramolecular distance from the second carboxylate (e.g., from the 5-position to the 4-position) in 3,4-H2PDC leads to steric torsion of both carboxylate groups out of the plane,7 where the multiple functional groups offer the potential to generate nondefault 3D structures when coordinated to single metal ions.
    It should be noted that several MOFs have been obtained from 3,4-H2PDC, and multiple coordination modes for the ligand have been observed (Scheme 1).8 Numerous topologies are represented; nevertheless, to the best of our knowledge, we report the first 3,4-PDC-based MOF with sra topology, as well as the introduction of an unprecedented topology in MOFs.

This configuration results in the quasi-tetrahedral building unit (TBU), MNO3.

    Each independent divalent 3,4-PDC anion is coordinated to four Cu(II) metals:  through the aromatic nitrogen atom, in a monodentate fashion through the 4-position carboxylate to one metal, and in a bis-monodentate fashion through the 3-position carboxylate to two independent metal ions, as shown in Figure 1a. Thus, the ligand can be regarded as another quasi-tetrahedral building unit.
    The Cu−O distances (1.95−2.27 Å) are within the expected range, and the Cu−N coordination distance (2.00 Å) is consistent with the expected range in 3,4-PDC-based MOFs.8a,8j Differing from its 3,5 constitutional isomer,6 3,4-H2PDC has two carboxylates in close proximal distance, leading to steric torsion out of the plane of the molecule.7 In 1, this torsion is maintained upon deprotonation and coordination in situ. The resulting dihedral angle in 1 for the 3-position carboxylate is an average of 64.56° when viewed down an axis from the carboxylate carbon to the adjacent pyridine core carbon (Scheme 2), and an average of 43.90° at the 4-position when viewed similarly.
    The structure of 1 is built from the assembly of TBUs (Figure 1c). Analysis of the vertex symbols and coordination sequence of 1 reveals that its topology is related to SrAl2.9-11 Both the ligand nodes and the metal nodes result in the same vertex symbols (4.6,4.6,6.82), congruent with zeolite ABW9 and SrAl2.10,11 Their unique coordination sequence confirms the identity of the generated network topology as sra.12
    Thermogravimetric analysis indicates that 1 is stable up to ∼220 °C, with distinct weight losses of ∼62% from 220 to 230 °C and ∼12% from 700 to 800 °C, which correspond to the liberation of coordinated terminal DMF (estimated 24.2%) and deterioration of the extended framework.
    In Figure 2, the magnetization vs magnetic field curves for 1 are displayed in a field range from −50 k Oe to 50 k Oe. At 300 K, the M−H curve shows a paramagnetic type behavior with a straight line. However, below 20 K the M−H curve exhibits nonlinear behavior that indicates the presence of ferromagnetic ordering.8a,8d,8g,8j
    Crystals of 2 (usf-51) resulted from the reaction of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O and 3,4-H2PDC under mild conditions. There are two distinct types of zinc ions, as well as two unique 3,4-PDC ligands. One independent zinc, Zn1, assumes a trigonal bipyramidal geometry, with two oxygen atoms of two independent 3,4-PDC ligands and three terminal ligands (two oxygen atoms of two DMF molecules, and one nitrogen atom of one pyridine molecule) completing the coordination sphere.
    The terminal ligands do not contribute to the network topology, and therefore MNO4 (BU, MO2) can simply be regarded as a bridge between the two framework ligands. The other independent zinc, Zn2, is tetrahedral, coordinated to two nitrogen atoms and two oxygen atoms of four independent 3,4-PDC ligands to form a tetrahedral MBB (MN2O2), as shown in Figure 3a, which also serves as a TBU for the topological description of 2.
    Differing from 1, where there is only one type of independent 3,4-PDC that is 4-connected, 2 contains two unique 3,4-PDC molecules that can be regarded as 3-connected nodes. Both ligands are coordinated to three zinc metal ions through one oxygen atom of the two independent carboxylates in a monodentate fashion and through the aromatic nitrogen. The resulting neutral framework can simply be regarded as a network constructed from 3-connected ligand and tetrahedral metal nodes, (3,4)-mixed connectivity. (Figure 3b).
    In 2, the Zn−O distances are within the expected range (Zn1, 1.97−2.02 Å; Zn2, 1.93−1.95 Å).8b,8c,8e The Zn−N bonds (2.02−2.05 Å) are within the expected range. Analysis of the dihedral angles for the 3,4-PDC carboxylates in 2 is informative. Although there are only two crystallographically independent 3,4-PDC ligands in 2, each one exists with symmetry equivalent birotational carboxylates, resulting in two opposite, but equal dihedral angles for each carboxylate on each independent ligand. The 3-position carboxylates have dihedral angles averaging 56.48° and 45.61°, respectively, when viewed down an axis from the carboxylate carbon to the adjacent pyridine core carbon (Scheme 2). The 4-postion carboxylates have average dihedral angles of 41.63° and 58.04°, respectively.
    The structure of 2 can be analyzed as a (3,4)-connected net (Figure 3c). Topological analysis reveals two sets of vertex symbols, one set for the tetrahedral nodes (6.83.8.8.82.82) and another for the 3-connected nodes (6.83.83), as in Figure 3d. Further elaborate analysis reveals that the network of 2 is trinodal. Although the 3-connected nodes, 3,4-PDC ligands, possess similar vertex symbols, they can be regarded as two unique nodes. This discrimination is supported by the divergence of their coordination sequences and topological density, TD10 (3, 8, 17, 31, 48, 68, 95, 123, 154, 190; TD10 = 736 and 3, 8, 17, 31, 50, 68, 95, 124, 155, 195; TD10 = 746, respectively). The third independent node in 2 is the tetrahedral node (4, 8, 19, 32, 48, 72, 94, 123, 158, 193; TD10 = 751). On the basis of this analysis, to the best of our knowledge, 2 is the first MOF to possess this type of network topology (usf-51).
    It is worth mentioning that in our previous analysis, we consider Zn2 as a point of extension bridging two 3,4-PDC ligands, each ligand also connected to two independent tetrahedral Zn1 ions. This analysis results in two directly linked 3-connected nodes and, thus, an overall (3,4)-connected net with isolated pairs of 3-connected nodes and isolated tetrahedral nodes (Figure 4). An alternate analysis of the structure 2 network can be assessed by considering the two directly linked 3-connected nodes as one expanded 4-connected node, much like the relationship between ths (α-ThSi2 or (10,3)-b topology), tfa, and dia (diamond topology).13 Accordingly, if the structure is viewed in a similar manner, then, as in 1, the network is composed of two types of 4-connected nodes with identical vertex symbols (4.62.6.6.6.6), coordination sequence and TD10,14 which is consistent with cag topology, i.e., CaGa2O4.13a MOFs related in their topology to cag topology are scarce. It is worth noting that in 2, the ligand-based 4-connected node is generated, in situ, by bridging two 3-connected ligands; however, in all previous cag-like MOFs, the 4-connected nodes are based only on single metal ions or clusters and/or tetrahedral ligands.11,15,16
    The evident relationship between the (3,4)-connected net topology presented here in 2 and the cag topology illuminates a possible new approach to access networks having novel topologies. Thus, new MOFs with unprecedented topologies can be targeted and constructed to be related to existing networks, where, in this case, the 4-connected node can be substituted with a pair of 3-connected nodes. This approach can be generalized to other multiconnected nodes as a means to access new MOFs (i.e., 6-connected nodes can be divided into two directly linked 4-connected nodes and 4-connected nodes can be divided into two 3-connected nodes).
    Thermogravimetric analysis indicates that 2 is stable up to ∼200 °C, with three distinct weight losses, ∼17% from 200 to 250 °C, ∼27% from 250 to 300 °C, and ∼23% from 350 to 425 °C, which correspond succinctly to liberation of terminal ligands (estimated 48.9%) and deterioration of the extended structure.
    The as-synthesized 2 possesses photoluminescence properties with an emission maximum centered at ∼330 nm and a lifetime of ∼3.7 ns. This feature is consistent with previous findings in which 3,4-PDC-based MOFs exhibit fluorescent properties.8b,8c,8e,8f,8h Since neither the metal ion nor the 3,4-H2PDC molecule exhibits luminescence, the fluorescence most likely originates from the metal−ligand complex (See Figure S3, Supporting Information).

    Here we have presented two novel 3,4-PDC-based MOFs, one related to sra topology and the other with an unprecedented topology, each with interesting properties. We have shown that the organic ligand, 3,4-H2PDC, can lead to 3D networks, as well as distinctive frameworks from its constitutional isomer, 3,5-H2PDC, based on the steric torsion of proximal intramolecular carboxylates out of the plane of the molecule core. We have confirmed that novel frameworks and topologies can be obtained under mild conditions with noted multifunctional ligands through heterocoordination to various metal ions. Looking to the future, we have illustrated a new potential avenue toward the design and synthesis of novel MOFs.
