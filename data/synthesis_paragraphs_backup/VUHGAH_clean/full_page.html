Synthesis, structure and property of cobalt(II) complexes with 3,5-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid†

Zhi Su a, Zheng-Shuai Bai a, Jing Xu a, Taka-aki Okamura b, Guang-Xiang Liu a, Qian Chu a, Xiao-Feng Wang a, Wei-Yin Sun *a and Norikazu Ueyama b
aCoordination Chemistry Institute, State Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing National Laboratory of Microstructures, Nanjing University, Nanjing, 210093, China. E-mail: sunwy@nju.edu.cn.; Fax: 83314502
bDepartment of Macromolecular Science, Graduate School of Science, Osaka University, Toyonaka, Osaka 560-0043, Japan

Received 12th December 2008 , Accepted 19th December 2008

First published on 2nd February 2009

Two novel cobalt(II) complexes [Co(L)(NO3)] (1) and [Co(L)(H2O)2]ClO4 (2) were synthesized by reactions of 3,5-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (HL) with corresponding cobalt(II) salts, and their structures were determined by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. In the title complexes, the carboxylate group of L− ligand adopts a µ2–ŋ1:ŋ1-bridging mode to connect two cobalt(II) atoms, and each L− ligand acts as a 4-connector, and the cobalt(II) has distorted octahedral coordination geometry. In 1, one oxygen atom of the nitrate anion links two cobalt(II) atoms to complete a 3D structure which is a binodal (4,6)-connected net with Schläfli symbol of (3·42·52·6)(32·42·52·64·74·8). While in 2, the 3D structure has topology related to SrAl2, CeCu2 and KHg2 with Schläfli symbol of (42·63·8) and there are 1D channels filled with perchlorate anions. The results revealed that the nature of the counteranions, such as their shape, size and coordination ability, has a remarkable influence on the structure of the complexes. The results of the magnetic measurements showed that there are antiferromagnetic interactions between the neighboring Co(II). Furthermore, complex 2 exhibited a modest second-harmonic-generation (SHG) efficiency and anion-exchange property.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) through assembly of suitable metal salts with designed organic ligands have attracted great attention from chemists in recent years not only due to their intriguing structural and topological diversity,1 but also owing to their potential application as functional materials, such as magnetism, optics, ion exchange, catalysis, gas storage, ferroelectricity, etc.2–7 According to the previously reported studies, tripodal ligands with arene core and nitrogen donors, for example 1,3,5-tris(benzimidazole-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene, 1,3,5-tris(imidazole-1-ylmethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene, 1,3,5-tris(1-imidazolyl)benzene, 2,4,6-tris[4-(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)phenyl]-1,3,5-triazine, 1-(1-imidazolyl)-3,5-bis(imidazolyl-1-ylmethyl)benzene, have been proved to be efficient building blocks in the construction of MOFs with a specific topology of honeycomb, cage, interpenetrating networks and three-dimensional (3D) frameworks.8 On the other hand, much outstanding research has been carried out using carboxylate-containing tripodal ligands such as benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid, benzene-1,3,5-triacetic acid, etc. to construct MOFs.9 The results showed that such kinds of tripodal ligands are versatile and can adopt different conformations when they interact with metal salts. Meanwhile, our and others' reported studies also imply that the self-assembly process depends on many and/or even subtle factors and is usually difficult to predict exactly at present, and further studies are required to understand the assembly process.

As continuation of our study, a new carboxylate- and imidazol-containing ligand, 3,5-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (HL) (Scheme 1) was employed, which has remarkable features as follows: (a) it is a rigid ligand which may favor the construction of coordination architectures with high thermal stability; (b) it is a tripodal ligand with both imidazole and carboxylate coordinating groups which may be efficient to construct MOFs with high-dimensional structures; (c) the carboxylate group can have different coordination modes to satisfy the need of the center metal's coordination geometric requirement; (d) the ligand can offer additional hydrogen bonding and π–π interactions to consolidate the structure.


Scheme 1 The synthesis of ligand HL

Herein we report the synthesis, crystal structure and properties of two new cobalt(II) complexes, namely [Co(L)(NO3)] (1) and [Co(L)(H2O)2]ClO4 (2), obtained by the reactions of HL with Co(NO3)2·6H2O and Co(ClO4)2·6H2O under the same reaction temperature.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All commercially available chemicals are of reagent grade and used as received without further purification. Elemental analyses of C, H, and N were taken on a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer at the analysis center of Nanjing University. 1H NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker DRX 500 NMR spectrometer at room temperature. Infrared spectra (IR) were recorded on a Bruker Vector22 FT-IR spectrophotometer by using KBr pellets. Thermogravimetric analyses were performed on a simultaneous SDT 2960 thermal analyzer under nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The second-order nonlinear optical (NLO) intensity was estimated by measuring a powder sample of 80–150 µm diameter in the form of a pellet relative to urea. A pulsed Q-switched Nd:YA Glaser at a wavelength of 1064 nm was used to generate an SHG (second-harmonic-generation) signal from powder samples. The backscattered SHG light was collected by a spherical concave mirror and passed through a filter that transmits only 532 nm radiation. The magnetic measurements in the temperature range of 1.8 to 300 K were carried out on a Quantum Design MPMS7 SQUID magnetometer in a filed of 2000 Oe. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants for all samples.
Preparation of 3,5-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (HL). The compound was prepared by reaction of 3,5-dibromobenzoic acid with imidazole following the procedures used for synthesis of 1,3,5-tris(1-imidazolyl)benzene (Scheme 1).8b1H NMR of HL [(CD3)2SO]: δ = 8.58 (s, 2 H), δ = 8.31 (s, 1 H), δ = 8.20 (s, 2 H), δ = 8.17 (s, 2 H), δ = 8.08 (s, 2 H), δ = 7.25 ppm (s, 2 H).
Preparation of complex [Co(L)(NO3)] (1). A reaction of HL (25.4 mg, 0.1 mmol), Co(NO3)2·6H2O (29.1 mg, 0.1 mmol), NaOH (4.0 mg, 0.1 mmol) and H2O (10 ml) was carried out in a 16 ml Teflon lined stainless steel container at 160 °C for 3 d. After cooling to room temperature, red platelet crystals of 1 were collected by filtration and washed with water and ethanol several times with a yield of 59%. X-Ray crystallography

The data collection for 1 was carried out on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD area-detector diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 20(2) °C. The diffraction data were integrated by using the SAINT program.10 Semi-empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.11 The structure was solved by direct methods and all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package.12 The hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically. The crystallographic data for 2 were collected on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID imaging plate diffractometer at −73 °C, with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71075 Å). The structure was solved by direct methods with SIR9213 and expanded using Fourier techniques.14 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically by the full-matrix least-squares method on F2. Hydrogen atoms of ligand L− in the structure were generated geometrically and the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were located directly. Four H atoms were found at reasonable positions in the differential Fourier map and located there. However two O–H distances became shorter than 0.7 Å and the others were longer than 0.9 Å in the least-square cycles. On the other hand, the perchlorate anion (Cl2, O21, O22, O23 and O24) was disordered into two positions near the two-fold axis, each having a site occupancy factor of 0.5. The ellipsoids became elongated in shape and the tetrahedral geometry of the ClO4− anions was deformed in the refinements. Therefore, isotropic restraints for thermal factors (ISOR in SHELXL) were made. All calculations were carried out on SGI workstation using the teXsan crystallographic software package of Molecular Structure Corporation.15 The details of the crystal parameters, data collection, and refinements for the complexes are summarized in Table 1, selected bond lengths and angles with their estimated standard deviations are listed in Table 2.

Results and discussion

Synthesis of the complexes

1 and 2 were synthesized under hydro/solvothermal conditions, because mixing the metal salt and HL ligand together with NaOH results in precipitation of the solution, which makes it difficult to grow crystals of the complexes. In addition, NaOH was used to neutralize the acid, and the complete deprotonation of the carboxyl group of the ligand in 1 and 2 was confirmed by IR spectral data, since no IR bands in the range of 1760–1680 cm−1 were observed in the IR spectra of 1 and 2 (see Experimental section), as well as by the results of crystallographic analysis (vide infra).16 Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) was carried out for 1 and the result showed that 1 has high thermal stability since no weight loss was observed for 1 up to ca. 350 °C.

Crystal structure description of [Co(L)(NO3)] (1)

When the HL ligand was used to react with Co(NO3)2·6H2O, NaOH and H2O at 160 °C for 3 d, 1 was isolated as single crystals. The structure of 1 was studied by X-ray single-crystal structure determination. The asymmetric unit of 1 consists of half-a-molecule of [Co(L)(NO3)], namely, one Co(II) atom sitting on an inversion center, half-L− ligand and half-a-nitrate anion. The coordination environment of the Co(II) atom in 1 is shown in Fig. 1a with an atom numbering scheme. Each Co(II) atom is coordinated by two imidazole nitrogen (N12, N12C) and two carboxylate oxygen atoms (O1D, O1E) from four distinct L− ligands at the equatorial positions and two oxygen atoms (O2, O2C) from two different nitrate anions at the apical positions, respectively. The bond angles around the Co(II) atom are in the range of 84.47(9) to 180.0°, and thus the coordination geometry of the Co(II) atom is a slightly distorted octahedron with a N2O4 donor set. The Co1–N bond length is 2.100(3) Å and the Co1–O ones are 2.071(2) and 2.1965(17) Å, respectively (Table 2), which is similar to previously reported values.17 On the other hand, each L− ligand acts as a a four-connector to link four Co(II) atoms: the deprotonated carboxylate group adopts a µ2–ŋ1:ŋ1-bridging mode to connect two cobalt(II) atoms, and each of two imidazole groups coordinates with one Co(II) atom.

The most striking feature of 1 is that one of the oxygen atoms of the nitrate bridges two Co(II) atoms together with the carboxylate bridge of the L− ligand to form an infinite one-dimensional (1D) chain structure as illustrated in Fig. 1b. It is noteworthy that the nitrate usually acts as a terminal ligand or a free counter-anion, and the one oxygen atom of the nitrate bridge-linking two metal atoms is not common in the reported MOFs.18 The 1D chains are linked together by the 3-imidazole group of the L− ligand via a Co1–N12 coordination bond to form a two-dimensional (2D) network structure as depicted in Fig. 1c. Such 2D layers are further connected by a 5-imidazole unit of the L− ligand to give a 3D architecture viaCo1–N12C coordination interactions (Fig. 1d).

To get better insight into the present 3D framework structure, topological analysis was carried out for 1. Each Co(II) atom connects four distinct L− ligands (vide supra) and two other Co(II) atoms via two nitrates, hence, each Co(II) can be regarded as a 6-connected node. Each L− ligand in turn connects four Co(II) atoms using its three arms, and thus each L− can be treated as a 4-connected node. Such connectivity repeats infinitely to give the 3D framework, as schematically shown in Fig. 1e. According to the simplification principle,19 the resulting structure of 1 is a binodal (4,6)-connected net, and its Schläfli symbol is (3·42·52·6)(32·42·52·64·74·8) (the first symbol for the L− ligands, the second for the metal Co(II) atoms).

Crystal structure description of [Co(L)(H2O)2]ClO4 (2)

When Co(ClO4)2·6H2O, instead of Co(NO3)2·6H2O, was taken into the reaction with the HL ligand, a new coordination polymer 2 with a different structure was isolated. The result of X-ray crystallographic analysis revealed that 2 crystallized in a monoclinic acentric space groupC2 and a Flack parameter of −0.004(17),20 which is different from that of C2/c observed in 1 (Table 1). As shown in Fig. 2a, each Co(II) atom is six-coordinated with slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry by two O (O3, O4) atoms from two terminal coordinated water molecules, the other two carboxylate O (O1A, O2B) atoms and two imidazole N (N12, N32C) atoms from four different L− ligands. The Co1–O distances in 2 range from 2.083(2) to 2.192(3) Å, and the Co1–N distances are 2.106(3) and 2.115(3) Å, respectively, and the coordination angles around the Co(II) atoms vary from 84.26(9) to 177.81(10)°, as listed in Table 2. Thus the coordination geometry of Co(II) in 2 is also a distorted octahedron, the same as that in 1, however, it is clear that there are differences between 1 and 2. One is that the two bridging nitrate anions around the Co(II) atoms in 1 are replaced by two water molecules as terminal ligands in 2. Another difference is that the two coordinated imidazole groups around the Co(II) located in trans positions in 1 (Fig. 1a), but in cis ones in 2 (Fig. 2a).

Each L− ligand also connects four metal atoms using its deprotonated carboxylate group with a µ2−ŋ1:ŋ1-bridging mode and two imidazole groups, which is similar to that observed in 1 (vide supra). If the coordination linkage between the 5-imidazole group of the L− ligand and Co(II) is neglected, a brick-like 2D network was obtained via the Co–N and Co–O coordination of the carboxylate and 3-imidazole group, with Co(II) as illustrated in Fig. 2b. There are 22-membered microcycles in the 2D network, each formed by the carboxylate and 3-imidazole units from their L− ligands together with three Co(II) atoms. The intermetallic separations of Co1A⋯Co1B, Co1A⋯Co1C, Co1B⋯Co1C are 12.13, 9.86, 4.83 Å, respectively. Such 2D layers are further connected by 5-imidazole units of the L− ligands to give a 3D architecture via Co–N coordination interactions (Fig. 2c).

Similarly, using the topological method to get insight of the structure of 2, each L− ligand is neighbored by four Co(II) atoms, thus each L ligand could be considered as a 4-connector. Meanwhile, the central Co(II) atom could also be viewed as a 4-connector through omitting the coordination of two water molecules. Such connectivity repeats infinitely to give the 3D framework as depicted in Fig. 2d. Analysis of the vertex symbols and coordination sequence of 2 revealed that its topology is related to SrAl2. Both the ligand and metal nodes result in the same Schläfli symbols (42·63·8), i.e. a net has been referred by Smith as the ABW tetrahedral net in Li-A(BW) zeolite while O'Keeffe and Hyde refer to it as the SrAl2 net.21 In addition to the Li-A(BW) zeolite and the SrAl2, the net also appeared in the structure of CeCu2 and KHg2.22

There are 1D channels with an opening diameter of about 5.5 Å along the c axis in 2, and the perchlorate anions are filled into the 1D channels through strong hydrogen bonds (Fig. 2e, Fig. S1†) and can be exchanged by other anions (vide post). The hydrogen bonding data are summarized in Table S1.† It is noteworthy that the perchlorate anion in 2 is not coordinated to the 3D framework due to its weak coordination ability, while the nitrate anion in 1 is coordinated to the central Co(II) atom and is joined to the formation of the 3D architecture. The results imply that the counteranion has important and subtle influence on the structure of the complexes.

Nonlinear optical property of 2

As is known, only the non-centrosymmetric structure might have a second–order non-linear optical effect.23 Complex 2 crystallized in an acentric space groupC2 which belongs to the polar point group (Cs). Approximate estimations were carried out on a pulsed Q-switched Nd:YA Glaser at a wavelength of 1064 nm. The results obtained from a powdered sample (80–150 µm diameter) in the form of a pellet (Kurtz powder test)24 were compared with those obtained for urea. The preliminary experimental results revealed that 2 exhibits modest powder SHG intensity with a response 0.4 times that for urea.

Anion exchange property of 2

As revealed by the crystal structure analysis of 2, the perchlorate anions are located within the open structure of the 1D channels through O–H⋯O and C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds (Fig. S1†). Complex 2 is expected to display an anion exchange property since it is insoluble in common solvents. The FT-IR spectra of the exchanged product and the original 2 are shown in Fig. 3, respectively. The characteristic intense bands ranging from 1384 to 1356 cm−1 appeared, which originate from the NO3− ion, while the characteristic intense bands around 1094 cm−1 of the ClO4− anion disappeared in the spectrum of the exchanged solid (Fig. 3). Furthermore, the results of elemental analysis of the anion exchanged product of [Co(L)(H2O)2](NO3) also indicated a complete anion exchange. To investigate the stability of the framework of 2 during the ion exchange process, we recorded the X-ray power diffraction (XRPD) and the results were shown in Fig. S2.† The exchanged products gave a sharp XRPD pattern, which is similar to that of 2, indicating that the crystal structure has been maintained after the exchange of ClO4− by NO3−.
Fig. 3 FT-IR spectra of (a) complex 2, (b) complex 2 treated with an aqueous solution of NaNO3..

It should be noted that the anion exchange product, i.e. [Co(L)(H2O)2](NO3) is different from 1, namely [Co(L)(NO3)]. The nitrate anions in the anion exchanged product is free of coordination, just replace the perchlorate ones in the 1D channels, however, the nitrate in 1 is bridge-linking the Co(II) atoms. The different structures of the anion exchanged product and 1 are evidenced by the XRPD patterns (Fig. S2†) as well as the IR data. The coordinated nitrate in 1 showed a characteristic intense IR band around 1472 cm−1 while the free one in the anion exchanged product gave intense IR bands around 1384 cm−1.

Magnetic properties

The temperature dependence of magnetic susceptibilities of 1 and 2 was studied from 300 to 1.8 K with a 2000 Oe applied magnetic field, and diamagnetic corrections were carried out.25 The χM and χMTvsT curves for 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 4 and 5, respectively. The χMT value at 300 K is 2.934 emu K mol−1 for 1 and 3.147 emu K mol−1 for 2 which is much higher than the spin-only value of 1.875 emu K mol−1 based on a Co(II) ion (g = 2 and s = 3/2) due to spin–orbital coupling in 1 and 2. Along with the lowering temperature, the χMT values decrease slowly and then more rapidly below 75 K to reach the value of 0.183 and 1.392 emu K mol−1, respectively, for 1 and 2 at 1.8 K. There are maximums in the χMversusT curve and the shapes of the χMTversusT curve are characteristic of the occurrence of weak antiferromagnetic interactions between the Co(II) centers. In order to estimate the strength of the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction of 1, the following simple phenomenological equation was used:26
χT = Aexp(−E1/kT) + Bexp(−E2/kT)
Here, A + B is the curie constant (C), and E1, E2 represent the “activation energies” corresponding to the spin–orbit coupling and the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction. Fortunately, it is in good accordance with the experimental data. For 1, the obtained values of C = 3.2 emu K mol−1 and E1/k = 61.50 K (using the best least-squares fitting method) agree with those given in a previous report for the Curie constant of the six-coordinated high-spin Co(II) ion (C ≈ 2.8–3.4 emu K mol−1) and for the effect of spin–orbit coupling and site distortion (E1/k of the order of +100 K).26 As far as the value of the antiferromagnetic exchange interaction, it is really weak (−E2/k = −6.9 K), corresponding to J = −13.8 K and the agreement factor R = ∑[(χMT)obsd − (χMT)calcd]2/∑(χMT)2 = 0.7 × 10−5 based on the Ising chain approximation [χT ∞ exp(+J/2kT)].27,28

Similarly, the values of the Curie constant C = 3.4 emu K mol−1, E1/k = 61.21 K, −E2/k = −0.87 K, J = −1.74 K, R = 1.1 × 10−4 for 2 are obtained by the same analysis procedure as for 1. The negative value of J also indicated the antiferromagnetic interaction between two neighboring Co(II) ions. Summarizing the magnetic results of 1 and 2, the weak antiferromagnetism of the carboxylato-bridged Co (II) chains could be ascribed to the syn-syn configuration of the bridges (Co–OCO–Co), and the work described here also demonstrated this conclusion.

Conclusions

Two 3D coordination polymers with 3,5-di(1H-imidazol-1-yl)benzoic acid (HL) have been prepared and structurally characterized along with the magnetic properties present. Complexes 1 and 2 were synthesized under the same experimental conditions except for different counteranions with a distinct coordination ability and geometry and solvent. One oxygen atom of the nitrate anion links to two Co(II) centers in compound 1 with topology (3·42·52·6)(32·42·52·64·74·8), which isn't common to be found in MOFs, while compound 2 with SrAl2 (42·63·8) topology. In addition, the magnetic properties of complexes 1 and 2 are tested, showing the antiferromagnetic interaction between two neighboring Co(II) ions. Furthermore, modest powder SHG intensity and anion exchange properties of compound 2 are checked.
