Abstract

Hydrophobic metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) not only have high water stability, but also exhibit high adsorption capacity towards organic molecules, in particular hydrocarbons. Herein we report a rare metal fluoride organic framework MFOF‐1 with high hydrophobicity, which is constructed from unprecedented fluoride‐ and sulfate‐bridged cubane‐type tetranuclear cobalt clusters. MFOF‐1 consists of three types of polyhedral cages with face‐sharing configurations, and possesses a novel (3,9)‐connected 3D+3D→3D self‐interpenetrating array or the rare pyr topology. MFOF‐1 shows high thermal stability and high stability in water and even acid/base aqueous solutions, and exhibits rather high H2 and CO2 storage capacities at ambient pressure. Remarkably, MFOF‐1 shows little adsorption of water but considerably high uptakes of methanol, n‐hexane, cyclohexane, and benzene, and exhibits a certain degree of adsorption selectivity of benzene over cyclohexane.

Introduction

Metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs), which are a unique class of porous crystalline materials assembled from metal ions/clusters and organic ligands through coordination bonds,1 have witnessed a fast development over the past decade, with more than twenty thousand MOFs reported in the Cambridge Structural Database.2 In comparison with zeolites, activated carbons, and other types of sorbents, MOFs display many advantages (i.e., high surface area and pore volume, structural diversities, adjustable pore environments, and easy synthesis); however, the inherent drawbacks of MOFs (i.e., low thermal/chemical stability) seriously limits their large‐scale applications in practice and developments in industries. Most of MOFs are sensitive to water or moisture, especially in an acid/base medium/solution;2, 3 MOFs are poorly chemical resistant. Therefore, the search of water‐stable and/or hydrophobic MOFs is still challengeable for synthetic chemists and indispensable for many practical applications such as oil‐spill cleanup,4 water purification,5 heat transformation,6 component/isomer separation,7 heterogenous catalysis,8 drug delivery,9 etc.

To impart or improve the water stability and/or hydrophobicity of MOFs, different strategies have been developed,4, 10 and reviewed recently by Walton and co‐authors.2 Among these strategies, the approach using high valence metal ions with carboxylate linkers can effectively increase the strength of metal–ligand coordination bonds; while the approach using high coordination number metal oxide clusters as secondary building units (SBUs) to construct MOFs can not only create a crowding effect that prevents water cluster from forming near the metal center, but also increase tolerance for hydrolysis before framework collapse. As a result, a series of MOFs with high water stability and/or hydrophobicity have been constructed from different O‐bridged and/or OH‐bridged rigid multinuclear metal clusters (such as [M6(μ3‐O)8−x(μ3‐OH)x] (M=Zr4+ and Hf4+),11 [Ti8O8(OH)4],12 and [Ni8(OH)4(H2O)2]10j). However, the strategy by incorporating hydrophobic fluorinatied or alkyl functional groups on the ligands is the most widely used one for improving the water stability and/or hydrophobicity of MOFs.4, 10c–10h, 10o, 10p Although MOFs obtained through this strategy often have slightly reduced pore size and volume due to the displacement effect with larger groups, in comparison with their nonfluorinated or non‐alkyl‐functionalized analogues, the hydrophobic nature of fluorine and alkyl groups can significantly increase the water stability, hydrophobicity, and the hydrocarbons adsorption capacity.4, 10c, 10h, 10o, 13

Currently, many studies have been concerned with functionalizing the organic linkers by incorporating alkyl, amine, cyano, and other organic functional groups; however, the above‐mentioned multinuclear metal‐oxide clusters as nodes of MOFs are also able to be modified with fluorinated displacements and have received relatively little attention.14 Among these reported metal oxide clusters, we are particularly interested in taking the cubane‐type metal clusters [M4(μ3‐O)4] as modifiable nodes, not only for their high rigidities and defined cubane configuration, but also because they are close to the well‐known cubane core [Mn4CaO5] observed widely in native photosynthetic enzymes (Scheme 1 c),15 and are of particular interest in biological systems and magnetic properties. For example, complexes based on [Mn4(μ3‐O)4] and [Co4(μ3‐O)4] cubanes have been used as bioinspired water oxidation catalysts,16 and usually display interesting single‐molecule magnet behaviors.17 Molecular complexes based on [M4(μ3‐O)4]/[M4(μ3‐OH)4] cubanes have been extensively studied, and there are some examples of these metal oxide clusters as SBUs to construct fascinating 3D MOFs;18 however, only a few studies have focused on the fluoride‐bridged cubane‐type metal clusters;19 among which no example of MOFs has been documented. On the other hand, the high electronegative fluoride ions as the bridging ligands in the metallic core can exhibit interesting optical and magnetic properties.20 In this work, three O‐bridging corners of the [Co4(μ3‐O)4] cubane are replaced by fluoride ions, the fourth O‐bridging corner is displaced by a sulfate group through in μ3‐η1:η1:η1‐O3(S=O) tridentate coordination fashion, giving an unprecedented irregular fluoride‐bridged [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ cubane (Figure 1 d). Herein, we report a new water‐stable metal‐fluoride‐organic framework [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)(btb)(tpt)2]⋅4.5 DMA⋅5 H2O (MFOF‐1) (H3btb=4,4′,4′′‐benzene‐1,3,5‐triyl‐tribenzoate, tpt=2,4,6‐tri(4‐pyridinyl)‐1,3,5‐triazine, DMA=N,N‐dimethylacetamide) based on [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ cubane‐type clusters. MFOF‐1 possesses a rare 3D (3,9)‐connected self‐interpenetrating framework and shows excellent thermal and chemical stability. A point to note is that MFOF‐1 not only exhibits high H2 and CO2 storage capacities, but also has high hydrophobicity as well as significantly higher uptakes for benzene over cyclohexane.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and structure

The solvothermal reaction of CoSO4⋅7 H2O with tpt and H3btb in a mixed solvent of DMA/methanol/1,4‐dioxane in the presence of tetrafluoroboric acid at 120 °C for five days affords red octahedral crystals of MFOF‐1. The component of MFOF‐1 was determined by energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), elemental analysis, TGA, and singe‐crystal XRD. The EDX spectrum clearly indicates the presence of significant amounts of the fluoride element but without born boron element, confirming that the fluoride ion in MFOF‐1 originates from the decomposition of the tetrafluoroborate anion (Figure 1 a).19a, 21 The XPS measurements further confirm the Co/F molar ratio (1.51) close to the expected value (1.33) (Figure 1 b).

The single‐crystal diffraction analysis revealed that MFOF‐1 crystallizes in the chiral cubic space group P213, with an infinite 3D (3,9)‐connected self‐interpenetrating framework based on the unprecedented [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters derived from the [Co4(μ3‐O)4] cubane (Scheme 1). The asymmetric unit in the framework of MFOF‐1 consists of one crystallographically independent CoII ion (Co2), one‐third of another independent CoII ion (Co1), one‐third of deprotonated btb3− ligand, one‐third each of two independent tpt ligands, one‐third of sulfate ligand and one μ3‐F− ion, with Co1, S1, and O2 atoms located on the special C3‐symmetric axis and other atoms in general sites (Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). The Co1 ion is coordinated by three μ3‐F− ions (Co−F=2.116(2) Å) and three carboxylate oxygen atoms from three different btb3− ligands (Co−O=2.053(3) Å), resulting in a slightly distorted octahedral coordination geometry in which all the three μ3‐F− ions are in a cis‐position, and each F− ion is therefore opposite to a carboxylate oxygen atom. The Co2 ion also adopts a distorted octahedral coordination geometry defined by one sulfate oxygen atom (Co−O=2.064(3) Å), a carboxylate oxygen atom from the btb3− ligand (Co‐O=2.040(3) Å), which is opposite to the sulfate oxygen atom, two pyridyl nitrogen atoms in a cis‐position from two separate tpt ligands (Co−N=2.124(3), 2.139(3) Å), and two μ3‐F− ions (Co−F=2.081(2), 2.112(2) Å) (Figure 2 a). The Co−F bond lengths (2.081 (2)–2.116(2) Å) are well consistent with those reported in other fluoride‐bridged cobalt complexes.14c, 14d, 22 Interestingly, one Co1 and three Co2 ions are linked by three μ3‐F− ions to form an incomplete cubane‐type cluster [Co4(μ3‐F)3]5+, of which the amputated corner of the cubane is compensated by a sulfate group in μ3‐η1:η1:η1‐O3(S=O) tridentate coordination fashion, and finally gives an unprecedented tetranuclear fluoride‐ and sulfate‐bridged cluster [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+, with Co1⋅⋅⋅Co2 and Co2⋅⋅⋅Co2 distances being 3.1433(7) and 3.7303(8) Å, respectively (Figure 2 b). The resultant cluster can be regarded as a derivative of the [Co4(μ3‐O)4] cubane; it exhibits a C3V symmetry in which the C3 axis just runs along the S1=O2 double bond and through the Co1 atom (Figure 2 a), and to the best of our knowledge is observed for the first time.

As shown in Figure 2 c, each Co1⋅⋅⋅Co2 edge of the cluster unit is further bridged by a μ2‐η1:η1 carboxylate group of a btb3− ligand, while each Co2⋅⋅⋅Co2 edge is connected to two external pyridyl arms of a pair of parallel tpt ligands. In turn, each btb3− ligand links to three Co1 and three Co2 atoms from three different cluster units via three μ2‐η1:η1 carboxylate groups, and each pair of parallel tpt ligands link to six Co2 atoms from three different tetranuclear clusters via external pyridyl groups, leaving the triazine core unbound. As a result, the interconnection of [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters, btb3− and tpt ligands affords an extended 3D porous framework (Figure 2 f). An important structural feature in MFOF‐1 is the presence of three types of cages: one distorted octahedral cage (Oc) and two distorted tetrahedral cages (Tc‐A and Tc‐B) with different sizes (Figure 2 e). The effective cavity diameter of the Oc, Tc‐A, and Tc‐B are 10.6, 11.0, and 7.0 Å, respectively. Each octahedral cage Oc is connected to eight tetrahedral cages (four Tc‐A and Tc‐B) by sharing its eight triangular faces. In turn, each tetrahedral cage Tc‐A or Tc‐B is linked to four octahedral cages Oc by sharing its four triangular faces. Thus, the overall structure of MFOF‐1 can be regarded as the close packing of these distorted polyhedral cages (Figure 2 f). Notably, there are still interconnected channels along all three crystallographic axes (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information), and the pore surface is lined with uncoordinated triazine N atoms. The solvent‐accessible void for MFOF‐1 was estimated by the PLATON program to be about 62.0 % of the unit‐cell volume.

Another striking feature in MFOF‐1 is the unprecedented 3D+3D→3D self‐interpenetrating array constructed from [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ SBUs and btb3−/tpt ligands. As shown in Figure S4a–c in the Supporting Information, tpt ligands link [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters together to form a 3D (3, 6)‐connected (43)2(43⋅1012) framework with nanochannels along three crystallographic axes, whilst btb3− ligands thread through the nanochannels and connect [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters to generate a 3D 3‐connected chiral framework with the (10, 3)‐a (srs, SrSi2, short Schläfli symbol of 103 and the full vertex symbol of 105⋅105⋅105) topology (Figure S4d–f in the Supporting Information).23 The two types of frameworks are not isolated but incorporated together by sharing [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters, resulting in a 3D porous network containing a 3D+3D→3D self‐interpenetrating array (Figure S4 g in the Supporting Information). From the perspective of topology, the 3D framework of MFOF‐1 can be described as a (3, 9)‐connected self‐interpenetrating network with the Schläfli symbol of (43)2(63)(43⋅627⋅86) (Figure S4i in the Supporting Information), in which the tetranuclear cobalt cluster, connecting three btb3− and six tpt liands, should be viewed as a 9‐connecting node, and the btb3− ligand or each crystallographically independent tpt ligand, connecting three cluster units, is a 3‐connecting node (Figure S4 h in the Supporting Information). In addition, considering that tpt ligands are always parallelly arranged in pairs to link three cobalt clusters, each pair of the parallel tpt ligands can be therefore combined into a 3‐connecting node (Figure 2 d). In this way and with the help of 3‐connecting btb3− linkers, the overall structure of MFOF‐1 is then simplified topologically into a (3,6)‐connected pyr net with the point Schläli symbol of (63)2(612⋅83) (Figure 2 g). To the best of our knowledge, either the (3,9)‐connected self‐interpenetrating network24 or the (3,6)‐connected pyr network is very rare.14d, 25 The novel (3,9)‐connected self‐interpenetrating array or the rare (3,6)‐connected pyr net, the triazine N‐atom lined on the pore surface and the fluoride‐ and sulfate‐bridged cubane‐type SBUs reveal that MFOF‐1 is an unprecedented host framework among thousands of reported MOFs.

Stability of MFOF‐1

Remarkably, MFOF‐1 exhibits high thermal stability and high acid/base‐proof stability relative to most of common MOFs. The thermal stability of MFOF‐1 has been evaluated under a N2 stream from room temperature to 1000 °C. As shown in Figure S6 in the Supporting Information, MFOF‐1 undergoes successive weight losses from room temperature to 260 °C, corresponding to the release of the trapped DMA and water molecules in pores (calcd: 25.0 %; found: 25.2 %) and then follows by a relative steady plateau until 380 °C beyond which the framework starts to decompose. The crystalline phase purity of MFOF‐1 was confirmed by the good consistence of experimental powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns with the simulated one from single‐crystal data (Figure 3 a). Furthermore, the in situ variable‐temperature PXRD patterns of MFOF‐1 under air confirm the maintenance of its framework integrity up to 380 °C (Figure 3 b). As shown in Figure 3 a, although some diffraction peaks become slightly weaker, the PXRD patterns of MFOF‐1 remain almost intact upon soaking in pure water, aqueous HCl solution (pH 4), and aqueous NaOH solution (pH 11) at 25 and 100 °C for one day, suggesting that no phase transition or framework collapse occurs upon treatments in water and acid/base aqueous solutions. The high water and acid/base‐proof stabilities of MFOF‐1 are comparable with those of well‐known stable Zr4+/Hf4+‐based MOFs,10i, 11 RE‐fcu‐MOFs,10k and MOFs with rigid multinuclear metal‐oxide SBUs,10j and may be attributed to the synergistic effect of the high coordination number of Co2+, large rigid fluoride‐ and sulfate‐bridged cluster as SBUs, the absence of solvent coordination sites on Co2+ atoms, and the self‐interpenetrated array. This kind of synergistic effect makes the coordination bonds as well as the whole framework of MFOF‐1 become resistant to the attack of water and even acid/base.2

Gas adsorption and porosity of MFOF‐1

The permanent porosity of activated MFOF‐1 a has been confirmed by adsorption isotherms of several light gases (N2, H2 and CO2). The N2 adsorption by MFOF‐1 a at 77 K shows a reversible type‐I adsorption behavior characteristic of microporous materials (Figure 4 a), with a saturated uptake of 599.6 cm3 g−1 and calculated BET (Langmuir) surface area of 2287 (2402) m2 g−1. The total pore volume is estimated to be 0.93 cm3 g−1, slightly larger than the theoretical value of 0.86 cm3 g−1. The pore sizes calculated from analysis of the N2 isotherm at 77 K using DFT are distributed around 7.2 and 10.3 Å (Figure 4 a), consistent with the values estimated from the single‐crystal structure determination.

Low‐pressure H2 adsorption isotherms of MFOF‐1 a were measured at 77 and 87 K, which show rapid kinetics and good reversibility without any hysteresis (Figure 4 b). MFOF‐1 a can absorb 1.76 wt % (197.5 cm3 g−1) H2 at 77 K and 1.0 bar, which is comparable with many other MOFs under similar conditions (Table S2 in the Supporting Information).26 Furthermore, the isosteric heat (Qst) of adsorption for H2 is 5.2 kJ mol−1 at near zero coverage, comparable with many other porous MOFs (Figure S9a in the Supporting Information).26 At 1.0 bar, MFOF‐1 shows a relatively high CO2 uptake of 93.8 cm3 g−1 (18.4 wt %) at 273 K and 53.9 cm3 g−1 (10.6 wt %) at 298 K (Figure 4 c). These values are close to the Tripp‐1‐Ni and greater than those reported for other pyr‐type frameworks,14d and can be well compared to those reported for many other high porous MOFs (Table S3 in the Supporting Information).27 For CO2 adsorption, MFOF‐1 a exhibits a Qst, n=0 value (23.1 kJ mol−1) similar to those of some highly porous MOFs (Figure S9b in the Supporting Information).28 It is well documented that open metal sites in MOFs are very beneficial for enhancing the adsorption capacities of gases.26, 27 It should be noted that the activated MFOF‐1 a does not have any open metal sites. The outstanding H2 and CO2 adsorption capacities for MFOF‐1 a may be attributed to its high pore volume and high polarized pore surface decorated with fluoride atoms of tetranuclear cobalt cluster and uncoordinated N atoms from triazine ring of tpt ligand.29

Vapor adsorption of MFOF‐1

Inspired by the potential hydrophobicity arising from fluorine in metal clusters and the high water stability of the MFOF‐1, water, methanol, n‐hexane, cyclohexane, and benzene vapor sorption isotherms for activated MFOF‐1 a were measured at 298 K. As shown in Figure 4 d, the adsorbed amount of water on MFOF‐1 a is lower than 2.0 mg g−1 at P/P0=0.2, increases significantly in the region of higher relative pressures, and reaches up to 27.7 mg g−1 at P/P0=0.9. These water uptake values are slightly higher than those of ZIF‐830 and alkyl‐decorated MOFs with ultrahigh hydrophobicity,5a, 10c, 10o but lower than those reported for highly hydrophobic Ni‐pyrazolate MOFs,10j ethyl ester‐functionalized CALF‐25,10g robust MOF‐5 type [Zn4O(3,5‐dimethyl‐4‐carboxypyrazolato)3]10e and activated carbon under similar conditions,31 indicating that MFOF‐1 belongs to high hydrophobic materials. In contrast, MFOF‐1 a shows significant adsorption of MeOH, n‐hexane, cyclohexane, and benzene at 298 K, even at low relative pressures (248.5 mg g−1 for MeOH, and 180.7 mg g−1 for n‐hexane, and 170.5 mg g−1 for cyclohexane and 355.4 mg g−1 for benzene at P/P0=0.2). The benzene adsorption follow a type‐I isotherm (Figure 4 d), with a measured saturation capacity of 408.8 mg g−1 (5.23 mmol g−1) at P/P0=0.9, which is significantly superior to the capacities previously reported for other hydrophobic materials such as CuI‐pyrazolate (179.7 mg g−1),5a MAF‐2 (206 mg g−1),10c and FMOF‐1 (164.7 mg g−1) at the similar condition.4 The measured saturation capacities of methanol and n‐hexane adsorption on MFOF‐1 a are 305.3 (9.53) and 212.7 mg g−1 (2.47 mmol g−1) at P/P0=0.9, respectively. Notably, although benzene and cyclohexane have almost equivalent boiling points (80.1 and 80.7 °C, respectively) and comparable molecular dimensions (3.3×6.6×7.3 and 5.0×6.6×7.2 Å3, respectively),10c MFOF‐1 a shows a certain degree of adsorption selectivity of benzene over cyclohexane, especially at low relative pressures. For example, the benzene uptake at P/P0=0.1 and 298 K reaches up to 315.9 mg g−1 (4.04 mmol g−1), while the cyclohexane uptake under similar conditions (P/P0=0.1 and 298 K) is 128.5 mg g−1 (1.53 mmol g−1). The significant adsorption selectivity of benzene over cyclohexane at low relative pressures indicates stronger interactions of benzene molecules with the framework (possible due to the π–π stacking between the benzene molecule and the π‐electron deficient triazine ring of tpt ligands) compared to cyclohexane.32 On the other hand, all the desorption isotherms of methanol, n‐hexane, cyclohexane, and benzene on MFOF‐1 a exhibit a significant hysteresis in comparison with the absorption isotherms, suggesting the presence of strong host–guest interactions through confinement effects for absorbed guest molecules. In addition, the PXRD patterns of the samples recycled after water and organic molecules adsorption operations are well consistent with the simulated one from single‐crystal X‐ray data (Figure 3 c), confirming the high stability of activated MFOF‐1 a upon vapor adsorption of water and organic solvents. Therefore, MFOF‐1 would be a promising candidate for hydrocarbons/methanol and water separation applications.

Conclusion

In summary, we have successfully isolated a novel metal‐fluoride‐organic framework (MFOF‐1). The structural novelties of MFOF‐1 are mainly embodied in 1) the fluoride‐ and sulfate‐bridged cubane‐type tetranuclear cobalt clusters [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+, which are first discovered and used as SBUs to construct MOFs; 2) three types of polyhedral cages with different sizes formed with [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+ clusters via btb3− and tpt linkers and interconnected into a 3D chiral high porous framework; and 3) exhibiting a rare (3,9)‐connected 3D+3D→3D self‐interpenetrating array or the rare pyr topology. Furthermore, MFOF‐1 combines “three‐high” features: high thermal stability (up to 380 °C), high water stability even in acid/base aqueous solutions (pH 4–11), and high hydrophobicity (lower than 2 mg g−1 adsorption amount of water at P/P0=0.2). In addition, the activated MFOF‐1 a exhibits permanent porosity and shows high H2 and CO2 adsorption capacities, as well as a certain degree of adsorption selectivity of benzene over cyclohexane. This work demonstrates that the introduction of fluoride‐bridged metal clusters as SBU is an effective and powerful strategy to enhance water stability and pore surface hydrophobicity of MOFs, and the unique [Co4(μ3‐F)3(μ3‐SO4)]3+‐based structure platform reported herein may be used as a guide for the future design and synthesis of new hydrophobic MOFs.

Experimental Section

Materials and instrumentation

The ligand 2,4,6‐tris‐(4‐pyridyl)‐1,3,5‐triazine (tpt) was synthesized according to the literature method.33 The other solvents and reagents were commercially purchased and used without purification. FTIR spectra in the 4000–400 cm−1 region were recorded on a Vertex 70 using KBr pallets. Elemental analyses of (C, H, and N) were carried out with a Vario EL‐Cube elemental analyzer. TGA was performed with a TGA/DSC 1 STARe system at a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under nitrogen atmosphere. Powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were carried out at room temperature on a Rigaku MiniFlex600 diffractometer using CuKα radiation (λ=0.154 nm) at 40 kV and 15 mA. In situ variable temperature PXRD patterns were recorded on a Rigaku Ultima IV diffractometer. XPS data were recorded on Thermo Fisher ESCALAB 250Xi X‐ray photoelectron spectrometer. The energy dispersive X‐ray (EDX) spectroscopy was performed on samples loaded on a carbonfilm‐coated Cu grid with the aid of a JSM‐6700F field‐emission scanning electron microscope. The SEM images were taken out using a Phenom G2 microscope.

Synthesis of MFOF‐1

CoSO4⋅7 H2O (0.029 g, 0.103 mmol), tpt (0.019 g, 0.061 mmol), and H3btb (0.021 g, 0.048 mmol) were added in the solvent mixture of N,N‐dimethylacetamide (DMA, 5 mL)/methanol (CH3OH, 5 mL)/1,4‐dioxane (1 mL) solvent in a 20 mL screw‐capped vial, then 100 μL HBF4 (tetrafluoroboric acid, 40 % in water) was added into the solution and the mixture was sonicated for 30 min. Finally, the mixture was sealed and heated to 120 °C for 5 days. The red octahedral crystals of MFOF‐1 (yield based on CoSO4⋅7 H2O, 66 %) were filtered, washed with DMA and CH3OH, and dried in the air. X‐ray crystallographic study

Crystallographic data of MFOF‐1 was collected on an Oxford Diffraction/Agilent SuperNova diffractometer (graphite‐monochromated MoKα radiation, λ=0.71073 Å) at 100 K. The intensity data sets were collected with the ω‐scan mode and reduced by CrystalClear software. The structure were solved by direct methods and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares technique with SHELXL‐97. All nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically during the final cycles. The hydrogen atoms for the ligands and DMA molecules were located at geometrically calculated positions and refined by riding, while the hydrogen atoms of the water molecules were not included in the model. A summary of crystallographic refinement details for MFOF‐1 were given in Table S1 in the Supporting Information. Complete details can be found in the accompanying cif file.

CCDC 1443542 (MFOF‐1) contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data are provided free of charge by The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre.

Gas and vapor adsorption experiments

Gas adsorption measurements were performed on the Accelerated Surface Area and Porosimetry 2020 System (ASAP2020) and pore‐size analyzer. Vapor adsorption isotherms were measured with an intelligent gravimetric sorption analyser IGA100B from the Hiden Corporation. Before each sorption experiment, the fresh crystalline samples of MFOF‐1 were soaked in acetone for three days with acetone refreshing every 12 h, and then degassed under dynamic vacuum at 120 °C for 8 h to obtain the completely activated samples of MFOF‐1 a. Ultra‐high‐purity (99.999 %) gases (N2, H2, CO2) and analytical reagents (methanol, n‐hexane, cyclohexane, and benzene) were used for measurements. The temperatures were controlled by a liquid nitrogen bath (77 K), a liquid argon bath (87 K), an ice water bath (273 K) and a water bath (298 K). The isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) for H2 and CO2 were calculated through the Clausius–Clapeyron equation using the H2 adsorption isotherms (77 and 87 K) and the CO2 isotherms (273 and 298 K), respectively.
