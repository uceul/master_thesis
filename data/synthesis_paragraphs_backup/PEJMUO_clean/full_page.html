    Abstract

    Two novel metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) |(H3O)1.5(H2O)3|[Cd2K0.5(C4N3O4)2(H2O)] (1) and |(H3O)(H2O)|[Cu4(C4N3O4)3(H2O)3] (2) were solvothermally synthesized using 1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3tzdc), which exhibited novel and diverse coordination modes. In compound 1, the ligand, which shows three novel types of coordination modes, is coordinated to cadmium atoms to generate a tetranuclear cluster that leads to a 5-connected bnn network with two distinct channels along the [001] and [010] directions. In compound 2, the ligand, which serves as a T-shaped linker, is coordinated to copper atoms to form a unique metalloporphyrin-like plane, resulting in a pto network without interpenetration. Gas adsorption was investigated for compound 1. In addition, a diverse blue luminescence response toward different guest molecules by compound 1 has been observed.

    Synopsis

    Two novel metal−organic frameworks (MOFs) |(H3O)1.5(H2O)3|[Cd2K0.5(C4N3O4)2(H2O)] (1) and |(H3O)(H2O)|[Cu4(C4N3O4)3(H2O)3] (2) were solvothermally synthesized using 1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3tzdc), which exhibited novel and diverse coordination modes. Gas adsorption was investigated for compound 1. In addition, a diverse blue luminescence response toward different guest molecules by compound 1 has been observed.

    The design and construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are motivated by the prospect of potential applications in gas adsorption and separation, catalysis, ion exchange, sensors, nonlinear optics, and drug release.(1) Recently, incorporation of open metal sites into MOFs has been proposed for playing a crucial role in luminescent response processes due to their ability to impart highly selective and specific molecular transformations, transport, and storage.(2) However, the difficulty of constructing stable MOFs that hold open metal sites has limited the progress toward obtaining luminescent responses from open transition-metal sites in MOFs. To design and synthesize MOFs with a unique structural diversity and high thermal stability, it would be highly desirable to choose organic ligands with ideal coordination modes. Generally, small organic ligands with simple coordination modes often give rise to compressed frameworks and low dimensions for MOFs, whereas large organic ligands lead to large pores and surface areas, but often accompanied by interpenetration and poor stability.(3) Strategies, such as the surface-induced method or using two linear linkers with different lengths, have been produced to suppress network interpenetration and maintain the stability, and a series of successful examples have been reported.(4) However, the large ligand often has the limitations with the complicated synthetic method and low yield in organic. Therefore, utilizing simple organic ligands to assemble structurally fascinating and stable MOFs with pores is still a challenge.
    Our group, among others, has explored N-heterocyclic carboxylic acid based ligands, that is, 4,5-imidazoledicarboxylic acid (H3ImDC), as rigid and directional bridging ligands for designing metal–organic materials (MOMs), especially for zeolite-like metal–organic frameworks (ZMOFs).(1d, 1f, 5, 6) It is evident that functionalized H3ImDC ligands with diverse substituent groups, such as methyl, ethyl, and pyridyl, at the 2-positon of the imidazole ring will permit the construction of new MOMs. Accordingly, our group has effectively employed H3ImDC derivatives to construct a 3D (4,4)-connected framework with a pts net from 2-ethyl-4,5-imidazoledicarboxylic acid (2-EtImDC) as well as a (3,6)-connected net with an ant net, a (4,4)-connected bbm net, and two novel chiral frameworks with bmn and lcy-a nets from pyridine-4,5-imidazoledicarboxylic acid (H3PyImDC).(7) To explore the coordination modes of this type of ligand, we utilized 1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid (H3tzdc) in which the N atom replaces the C atom in the imidazole ring of H3ImDC to construct MOMs. Compared with H3ImDC, H3tzdc has much advantages, as follows: (1) it is more soluble in water and organic solvents; (2) the 2-site N atom of the triazole ring can directly coordinate with metal centers, which will permit diverse coordination modes, or it can induce intermolecular hydrogen bonds with adjacent O or N atoms from other groups; and (3) it can be successively deprotonated to generate H2tzdc–, Htzdc2–, and tzdc3– species, which can act as 1-, 2-, 3-, 4-, 5-, or 6-connectors and thus will permit the construction of MOMs with unique features and functionalities.(5b) It is important to mention that, in reported compounds, the 2-site N atom of triazole does not take part in coordination with metal centers, so it usually presents the similar coordination modes to the H3ImDC.(8) Here, we report the construction of two novel MOFs from H3tzdc with cadmium and copper ions: |(H3O)1.5(H2O)3|[Cd2K0.5(C4N3O4)2(H2O)] (1) and |(H3O)(H2O)|[Cu4(C4N3O4)3(H2O)3] (2). Single-crystal X-ray structures were determined for the two compounds, and the gas sorption and luminescent response to the organic solvent of compound 1 were also assessed.

    Materials and Methods

    H3tzdc was prepared from benzotriazole according to reported procedures in an 80% yield.(9) All the other reagents of analytical grade were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were collected on a Rigaku D/max-2550 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analyses were carried out on a PerkinElmer Optima 3300Dv spectrometer. The elemental analyses were performed on a PerkinElmer 2400 element analyzer. The infrared (IR) spectra were recorded within the 400–4000 cm–1 region on a Nicolet Impact 410 FTIR spectrometer using KBr pellets. The thermal gravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a TGA Q500 thermogravimetric analyzer used in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min. N2 sorption isotherm measurement of the compound was carried out on a Micromeritics ASAP2010 instrument. Fluorescence spectra were collected on a Fluoromax-4 spectrophotometer for the solid powder samples at room temperature.

    Synthesis of Compound 1

    H3tzdc (0.017 g, 0.108 mmol), Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.015 g, 0.049 mmol), KNO3 (0.009 g, 0.09 mmol), H2O (1 mL), CH3CN (2 mL), and ethanolamine (ETA, 0.025 mL) were added, respectively, to a 20 mL vial, which was sealed and heated to 85 °C at a rate of 15 °C/min, allowed to dwell for 12 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 1 °C/min. The colorless polyhedron crystals were collected and air-dried, yielding 0.012 g of product (72% based on Cd(NO3)2·4H2O). The agreement between the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns indicated the phase purity of the as-synthesized product (see the Supporting Information, Figure S1), and the as-synthesized compound 1 was insoluble in water and common organic solvents.     X-ray Structure Determination and Structure Refinement

    Crystallographic data for 1 were collected on a Bruker SMART CCD diffractometer, whereas those for 2 were collected on a Bruker SMART-APEX-II CCD diffractometer. Absorption corrections were applied by using SADABS. The structures were solved with direct methods and refined with full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2 using the SHELXTL program package.(10) All the metal atoms were located first; then the oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen atoms of the compound were subsequently found in difference Fourier maps. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic temperature parameters. The hydrogen atoms of the coordinated water molecules and the guest water molecules could not be located but are included in the formula. Approximately 39.1% and 70.3% of the unit cell volumes of compound 1 and 2, respectively, are composed of the disordered guest water molecules that could not be modeled as discrete atomic sites. 1- and 3-site nitrogen atoms, and carboxylic oxygen atoms of the H3tzdc ligand were all coordinated with metal atoms, leaving the ligand completely deprotonated, and the partial water molecules were protonated to balance the charge of the framework in the two compounds. The final formula was derived from crystallographic data combined with elemental and thermogravimetric analysis data. Crystal data and detailed data collection and refinement of compound 1 and 2 are summarized in Table 1. Crystallographic data for 1 and 2 (CCDC 890920, 890921) have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre. Data can be obtained free of charge upon request at www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif. Topology information for compounds 1 and 2 was obtained using TOPOS 4.0.(11)

    Crystal Structure of 1

    Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound 1 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group Imma. It consists of two crystallographically independent Cd2+ ions and half a K+ ion in the asymmetric unit. The K1 has a distorted bicapped dodecahedral geometry coordinated to 10 oxygen atoms, including 8 oxygen atoms from 5 different tzdc3– ligands and 2 oxygen atoms from 2 terminal water molecules. The Cd1 is 6-coordinated to three tzdc3– ligands in a bidentate fashion through N-, O- heterocoordination to generate a distorted trigonal prismatic geometry [CdO3N3], whereas the Cd2 is 7-coordinated to four oxygen atoms and two nitrogen atoms from four different tzdc2– ligands, and one oxygen atom from a terminal water molecule, to form a monocapped trigonal prismatic geometry [CdO5N2] (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The Cd–O, Cd–N, and K–O bond distances are in the ranges of 2.294–2.564, 2.239–2.366, and 2.774–3.301 Å, respectively. All of these bond distances are similar to those found in other Cd2+ and K+ complexes.(5b, 12a, 13) The deprotonated ligand tzdc3– connects two uniquely coordinated Cd atoms to construct a tetranuclear cluster [Cd4tzdc8] with Cd1–Cd2 distances of 3.805–4.742 Å (Figure 1a). The [Cd4tzdc8] tetranuclear clusters are further joined to each other via the tzdc3– ligand to create a 5-connected 3D bnn network with one unique tile [46.62] (Figure 1b,c).
    The resultant 3D frameworks also contain a molecular square [Cd4tzdc4] with the orientation and the configuration of the ligands analogous to ZSA-1.(5b) The molecular squares connect to each other to form 1D chains along the a axis (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The framework exhibits two types of infinite channels in which water guests reside. One type of channel with a quadrangle shape has small openings with an approximate diameter of 6.2 × 6.6 Å2 along the c axis (Figure 1d), and the other with a hexagonal shape has larger openings with an approximate diameter of 9.4 × 11.7 Å2 (point-to-point and not including van der Waals radii) along the b axis in which the terminal water molecules protrude (Figure 1e). The total solvent-accessible volume for the compound was estimated, using PLATON, to be 39.1%.(14)

    Crystal Structure of 2

    Compound 2 crystallizes in the cubic space group Pm-3n with two crystallographically different Cu2+ ions in the asymmetric unit. The Cu1 with the octahedral geometry [CuN2O4] coordinates to two tzdc3– ligands in a bidentate fashion through N-, O- heterocoordination at the equatorial positions, while its axial positions are occupied by two H2O molecules, and the Cu2 with the [CuO6] distorted octahedral geometry is chelated by three O, O′-bidentate sites of the tzdc3– ligand (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The Cu–O bond distances lie in the range of 1.971–2.295 Å and Cu–N bond distances are 1.939 Å. In the structure of 2, the tzdc3– ligand has a μ3-bridging mode, linking two Cu1 atoms, resulting in a [Cu4tzdc4] unit, and all the atoms of the unit are coplanar except the water molecules in the axial positions. The units connect to each other perpendicularly through a Cu2 atom to form a 3D framework (Figure 2a). Interestingly, the motif of the [Cu4tzdc4] unit resembles porphyrin with metal ions instead of connecting CH groups and triazole instead of pyrrole, as shown in Figure 2c. It differs from the reported metalloporphyrin-like compound based on the H3tzdc ligand.(12e) In the latter compound, the template Na+ resides in the center of the unit to form a metalloporphyrin-like pentanuclear NaCu4 motif, while, in compound 2, the center of the metalloporphyrin-like rings is empty.
    To understand the framework topology, it is necessary to simplify the building blocks of compound 2. The plane is composed of four Cu1 atoms, which can be represented by a 4-connected square unit, and the 3-connected Cu2 atom can be represented by a triangle unit (Figure S7, Supporting Information). The square and the triangle are further connected via the tzdc3– ligand to generate a (3,4)-connected pto topology with two different tilings (Figure 2b,d). It evidently differs from the reported compounds with a pto topology.(15) In the reported compounds, 3-connected ligands, always choosing btb, tapb, and btb-Br6 ligands, coordinated to metal atoms to form 4-connected paddle-wheel clusters, resulting in a pto topology. Because of the special geometrical configuration of the longer organic linkers, it always leads to interpenetration. However, in compound 2, the Cu1 and Cu2 are connected by the rigid and directional ligand tzdc3– to generate a pto topology without interpenetration.
    Analogous to compound 1, two types of infinite channels with disordered water molecules are observed in compound 2. One exhibits large openings with an approximate diameter of 10.9 × 11.4 Å2 in which the terminal water molecules are omitted, and the other exhibits small openings with an approximate diameter of 4.8 × 4.8 Å2, as shown in Figure 2e. The total solvent-accessible volume for the compound was estimated, using PLATON, to be 70.3%.(14) Although compound 2 has the larger pores, it does not adsorb N2 at 77 K because of the low stability after evacuation.
    Compared with the reported MOFs based on the H3tzdc ligand, it has displayed diverse coordination modes as shown in Figure 3a–r,(5b, 8, 12) including 1 0D cluster in (a) coordination mode, 9 1D chains in (b)–(h) coordination modes, 7 2D layers in (h) coordination mode, and 19 3D frameworks in (h)–(r) coordination modes, in which some of them are isostructural. It is obvious that, in the 0D, 1D, and 2D structures, the H3tzdc ligand displays 1- and 2-connected coordination modes, and in the 3D compounds, the H3tzdc ligand exhibits 3-, 4-, 5-, and 6-connected coordination modes; therefore, increasing the coordination modes number will induce 3D open frameworks. During the studies of the coordination modes of the H3tzdc ligands, it is found that all or part of the oxygen/nitrogen atoms exhibit strong chelating ability, and through carefully choosing metal centers, the diverse coordination modes of the H3tzdc ligand would be adequately explored. As expected, three unique coordination modes, (s), (t), and (u), have been displayed in compound 1, as shown in Figure 3. In (s) mode, the ligand as a 6-connector coordinated with six metal atoms in bis-N,O-chelating, bis-O,O-chelating, and N-,O-monodentate fashion; in (t) mode, the ligand as a 5-connector linked to five metal atoms in bis-N,O-chelating and bis-O,O-chelating fashion; and in (u) mode, the ligand as a 4-connector connected with four metal ions in bis-N,O-chelating and N-,O-monodentate fashion. The various coordination modes play an important role in the formation of the tetranuclear cluster for compound 1, and to the best of our knowledge, these three unique coordination modes are first reported.

    Thermogravimetric Analysis

    Thermogravimetric analysis for compound 1 shows a two-step weight loss of 14.99% between 25 and 360 °C, corresponding to the loss of guest H2O molecules and the coordinated H2O molecule (calcd 15.03%). On further heating, a loss of 65.58% between 360 and 800 °C should correspond to the release of the organic tzdc3– ligand and the collapse of the lattice structure (Figure S8, Supporting Information). PXRD study indicated that the final product, upon calcinations above 800 °C, is a main phase of CdO (JCPDS: 65-2908). The TG curve for 2 exhibits an initial weight loss at 153 °C, with the observed weight loss of 10.53% corresponding to the release of guest water molecules and the terminal H2O molecule (calcd 11.28%). The second weight loss that occurs between 210 and 425 °C (53.97%) is attributed to the decomposition of the organic ligand and the collapse of the lattice structure, as seen in Figure S9 (Supporting Information). PXRD study indicated that the final product, upon calcinations above 425 °C, is CuO (JCPDS: 65-2309).

    Gas Sorption

    To evaluate porosity of the activated compound 1, gas adsorption measurements were carried out with N2. About 150 mg of ethanol-exchanged material (washing 10–15 times) was activated at 85 °C for 8 h. N2 sorption experiments were performed at 77 K and 0–760 Torr; the fully evacuated framework exhibits permanent microporosity, as evident by the reversible type I N2 sorption isotherms (Figure 4). The maximum amount adsorbed is 82 cm3 g–1 at 77 K and 1 bar. The estimated Langmuir surface area and BET surface area of the compound are 336 and 254 m2/g, respectively. The total pore volume measured with N2 is 0.10 cm3/g.

    Luminescent Properties

    The luminescent properties of compound 1 and the H3tzdc ligand in the solid state are evaluated at the room temperature. As shown in Figure S10 (Supporting Information), upon excitation at 397 nm, compound 1 is found to be luminescent with blue emission maxima at 490 nm, corresponding to the reported Cd2+ complexes with N-donor ligands,(16) which can be assigned to an intraligand fluorescent emission since a similar emission at 505 nm is observed for the ligand. The increasing intensity for compound 1 may be attributed to the chelation of the ligand to the metal center, which reduces the nonradiative relaxation process.
    Compound 1 with the terminal H2O molecules protruding in the channels and high thermal stability, as well as insolubility in common solvents, is an attractive candidate as luminescent materials for sensing of organic solvents with open metal sites. TGA data indicate that 1 releases its guest and terminal water molecules between 25 and 360 °C, forming its activated phase. The luminescent properties of activated samples in different organic solvents were evaluated in the solid state to examine the potential of activated compound 1 for luminescent response to organic molecules. The samples were prepared by introducing 100 mg of activated compound 1 into 2.00 mL of 1,4-dioxane, acetone, DMA, DMF, formaldehyde, THF, 1-propanol, dichloromethane, ethanol, or methanol and immersing for 72 h. The PXRD pattern indicates that activated compound 1 and processed samples almost agree with as-synthesized 1 (Figure S11, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure 5, the luminescent intensity significantly changes with the different organic solvents, especially formaldehyde solvent that exhibit obvious enhancing effects. Interestingly, directly putting as-synthesized 1 into organic solvent shows a slight change of luminescent intensity. Although the mechanism of response to organic solvents is still not clear at this moment, the interaction of open Cd sites with organic solvent plays an important role as reported.(2b) The obvious enhancing effects of activated compound 1 are assigned to the release of water molecules.

    In conclusion, we have successfully constructed two novel 3D MOFs with different network topologies from the assembly of the simple organic ligand H3tzdc with cadmium and copper ions.
