A doubly interpenetrated semiconducting MOF Zn4O(2,6-NDC)3(DMF)1.5(H2O)0.5Â·4DMFÂ·7.5H2O (UTSA-38) of a cubic net has been constructed, which exhibits photocatalytic activity for the degradation of methyl orange in aqueous solution.

Establishment of permanent porosity within porous metalâ€“organic frameworks (MOFs) has led to a variety of new porous materials for gas storage, separation, sensing, catalysis and drug delivery over the past two decades.1â€“5 In order to realize specific MOFs for heterogeneous catalysis, some functional catalytic sites need to be immobilized on the pore and/or framework surfaces to induce their catalytic reactions.2 Photocatalysis is a very unique type of catalysis in which the photoreaction can be accelerated in the presence of catalysts. The pursuit of highly efficient photocatalysts is very important to effectively utilize the solar energy sources for photocatalytic water splitting, disinfection of water and oxidation of organic contaminants, and thus to address the grand energy and environment challenges. Although some inorganic compounds such as TiO2 have been extensively explored for their photocatalytic performances, very few MOFs have been realized for such important functions.5 This is mainly because the MOFs need to have the capacity to absorb solar energy to initiate the catalysis, which has been rarely fulfilled. Motivated by the recent discovery of Zn4O(BDC)3 (MOF-5) as a semiconducting material,6 we report herein, for the first time, a doubly interpenetrated porous MOF Zn4O (2,6-NDC)3(DMF)1.5(H2O)0.5Â·4DMFÂ·7.5H2O (UTSA-38; 2,6-NDC = 2,6-naphthalenedicarboxylate) with high density of Zn4O sites for the photocatalytic degradation of methyl orange.

The diffuse reflective spectra (DRS) of UTSA-38 indicate that UTSA-38 is a semiconducting MOF with a band gap of 2.85 eV (Fig. S7, ESIâ€ ), underlining its potential as a photocatalyst. We examined its photocatalytic activity for the degradation of methyl orange (MO). After UTSA-38 was dispersed into the methyl orange aqueous solution, the decomposition of MO was monitored by the characteristic absorption band of MO at 464 nm. In the dark without any light, no change in the UV-Vis spectra of MO was observed (Fig. S8, ESIâ€ ). Under the irradiation of visible light, the intensity for the absorption band of MO at 464 nm gradually decreased as a function of increasing reaction time, suggesting degradation of MO in the presence of light (Fig. S9, ESIâ€ ). When UV-visible light was utilized to initiate such a photocatalytic reaction, the photocatalytic reaction for the decomposition of MO is significantly faster (Fig. 3a). We were even able to observe such degradation by our naked eyes at different time intervals (Fig. 3b). The methyl orange completely decomposed into colorless small molecules upon UV-visible light irradiation for 120 minutes. For comparison, the different degradation behaviors of MO in the dark, visible light and UV-visible light are shown in Fig. S10 and S11 (ESIâ€ ), which indicate clearly that UV-visible light is the most efficient for this photocatalytic reaction. The MOF catalyst can be readily recovered from the reaction mixtures via simple filtration and shows no obvious catalytic efficiency decay even after recycling for seven times.

As shown in Scheme S1 (ESIâ€ ), we speculate that the photocatalytic degradation of MO occurred as follows: the initial process of photocatalysis is the generation of electronâ€“hole pairs in the UTSA-38. After absorption of energy equal to or greater than the band gap of the UTSA-38 (hÎ½ â‰¥ 2.85 eV), the electrons (eâˆ’) are excited from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB), leaving the holes (h+) in the VB. The electrons and holes migrate to the surface of the UTSA-38, then the photoinduced energy transfers to the adsorbed species: electrons reduce the oxygen (O2) to oxygen radicals (Ë™O2âˆ’), and finally they transform into hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH); in turn, holes oxidize the hydroxyl (H2O) to hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH). Hydroxyl radicals (Ë™OH) have the ability to decompose methyl orange effectively.8

Methyl orange is one of the most stable azo dyes which are extensively used in the textile industry and resistant to biodegradation. Decomposition of such dye molecules into some simple molecules can diminish the environment pollution caused by them. Our work not only highlights the emerging porous MOF materials for their catalytic degradation of organic dyes under UV-visible irradiation, but also triggers extensive research on MOF materials as photocatalysts. Given the richness of different metal sites and/or metal-containing clusters and organic bridging linkers to construct diverse MOF materials and thus to tune their capacity to absorb solar energy and initiate photocatalytic properties, it is expected that new MOF photocatalysts for their wide applications will be emerging in the near future.
