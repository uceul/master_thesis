Journal of Molecular Structure

Volume 1119, 5 September 2016, Pages 296-300

Syntheses, structures and properties of metal-carboxylate chain-based coordination polymers (CPs) with 1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylate

Author links open overlay panelXinhuiZhouLinSongLiangLiTaoYang
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molstruc.2016.04.076Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Two coordination polymers (Mg–CP and Ag–CP) have been synthesized.

•

The ligand is 1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L).

•

•

Abstract

Two coordination polymers (CPs) {[Mg2L(μ2-H2O) (μ2-DMA)]·DMA}n (1), and [Ag4L(DMF)2]n (2) (H4L = 1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylic acid, DMA = N,N-dimethylacetamine, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide) have been synthesized and structurally characterized. In 1 and 2, there exist a series of parallel aligned MOC chains, which are linked along two directions by para-terphenyl moieties of L4− ligands to lead to the metal-carboxylate chain-based three-dimensional frameworks. The photoluminescence properties of the compounds 1 and 2 have also been investigated. 1 displays blue-violet light emission with the emission maximum at 380 nm. 2 exhibits a broad emission peak from 300 to 800 nm with an emission maximum at 484 nm and some of the shoulder peaks.

Graphical abstract

Two coordination polymers based on 1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L) have been synthesized. They feature metal-carboxylate chain-based three-dimensional frameworks, and their photoluminescence properties have also been investigated.

1. Introduction

Over the past two decades, a lot of coordination polymers [1] (nonporous CPs and porous CPs also called metal-organic frameworks, MOFs) have been synthesized based on a variety of organic ligands and they exhibit the fascinating functional properties and potential applications in the fields such as luminescence [2], [3], [4], [5], magnetism [6], [7], gas storage [8], [9], [10], sensor [11], [12], [20], catalysis [13], [14], [15], [16], ion exchange [17], [18], [19], and so on. Besides the properties, the coordination polymers also show us the charming architectural aesthetics in the microscopic universe, such as the interpenetration, interlocking, self-catenation, helix, metal-carboxylate chain-based structures (also called rod-packing structures), zeolitic imidazolate frameworks featuring four-connected tetrahedral topologies, pillared layered structures and so on.

At present, we are interested in the preparation of the metal-carboxylate chain-based coordination polymers and also have reported a series of metal-carboxylate chain-based coordination polymers [12], [22], [23]. In general, the multidentate organic ligands are suitable for forming the metal-carboxylate chain-based coordination polymers, such as pyridylbenzote ligands [21], 4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate acid [24], 2,2′,4,4′-biphenyltetracarboxylic acid [25] and so many other ligands. To our knowledge, few reports studied the coordination polymers based on the [1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl]-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L) so far. Han's group [26] used H4L to obtain three transition metal coordination polymers exhibiting antiferromegnetic interaction and selective detection for Al3+ ion and Fe3+ ion in methanol solutions. Wang's group reported a Cd(II)-MOF [27] and a series of solvent-induced Mn(II)-MOFs [28] featuring high selectivity for sensing Fe3+ ions and antiferromagnetic interaction, respectively. Huang's group [29] reported a series of transition metal MOFs possessing 3D framework and good catalytic activities for the degradation of methylene blue. Based on the above interesting structures and excellent properties, it is worth researching the formation of coordination frameworks with H4L further. In recent years, many magnesium [30] and silver [31] based coordination polymers or metal-organic frameworks have been reported. In coordination polymers, magnesium ions tend to form octahedral coordination geometries and don't quench the fluorescence of ligands. Silver ions is apt to form short Ag···Ag contacts and exhibits abundant coordination geometries. Herein, we present the syntheses, structures, and properties of two 3D CPs {[Mg2L(μ2-H2O)(μ2-DMA)]·DMA}n (1), and [Ag4L(DMF)2]n (2) (DMA = N,N-dimethylacetamine, DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide).

2. Experimental section

2.1. General

The chemicals used in this work are of analytical grade and were purchased from Jinan Henghua Sci. & Tec. Co., Ltd and were used without further purification. The FT-IR spectra were measured with KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a PerkinElmer FT-IR Spectrometer. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin–Elmer 240C analyzer. The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed in a SHIMADZU DTG-60 simultaneous DTA-TG apparatus instrument, under dynamic N2 atmosphere (20 mL/min) and heating rate of 10 °C/min from room temperature. Powder X-ray diffraction investigation on polycrystalline samples were carried out with a Bruker D8 advanced diffractometer equipped with a diffracted-beamed monochromator set for Cu-Kα (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation. The data were collected using a Ni-filtered Cu-target tube at room temperature in the 2θ range from 5° to 50° at an angular rate of 0.1 s/step, with a scan step width of 0.02°. Photoluminescent spectra were measured using a PerkinElmer LS55 Fluorescence Spectrometer.

2.2. Synthesis of {[Mg2L(μ2-H2O)(μ2-DMA)]·DMA}n (1)

A mixture of H4L (0.025 mmol, 10.2 mg), Mg(NO3)2˙6H2O (0.075 mmol, 19.2 mg), DMF (5 mL), DMA (2 mL) and H2O (1 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined bomb and heated at 90 °C for 3 days, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C/h. Colorless hexagonal crystals of 1 suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 72% yield. Elemental analysis calcd. for C30H30Mg2N2O11: C, 55.97; N, 4.35; H, 4.66%. Found: C, 56.12; N, 4.17; H, 4.82%. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 420 w, 509 w, 599 w, 724 w, 786 m, 859 w, 1020 w, 1252 w, 1351 m, 1431 s, 1602 vs, 3428 m.

2.3. Synthesis of [Ag4L(DMF)2]n (2)

A mixture of H4L (0.025 mmol, 10.2 mg), AgNO3 (0.075 mmol, 12.7 mg) and DMF (5 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined bomb and heated at 85 °C for 2 days, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C/h. Colorless diamond-like crystals of 2 suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 36% yield. Elemental analysis calcd. for C28H24Ag4N2O10: C, 34.29; N, 2.86; H, 2.45%. Found: C, 34.45; N, 2.78; H, 2.32%. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 491 w, 571 w, 652 w, 715w, 786 w, 1020 w, 1342 m, 1378 vs, 1557 s, 1655 w, 3401 s.

2.4. Crystal structure determination

X-ray diffraction data of the single crystals of 1–2 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD area detector diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Cell parameters were retrieved using SMART software and refined using SAINT [32] on all observed reflections. Data were collected using a narrow-frame method with scan widths of 0.30° in ω. The highly redundant data sets were reduced using SAINT and corrected for Lorentz and polarization effects. Absorption corrections were applied using SADABS [33] supplied by Bruker. Structures were solved by direct methods using the program SHELX-97 [34] program package. The positions of the metal atoms and their first coordination spheres were located from direct-method E maps; other non-hydrogen atoms were found using alternating difference Fourier syntheses and least-squared refinement cycles and, during the final cycles, were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a uniform value of Uiso. Crystallographic details for 1–2 have been summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles for 1–2 are listed in Tables S1–S2. CCDC reference numbers for 1–2 are 1415281 and 1415287, respectively.

3. Results and discussions

Both the compounds 1 and 2 were obtained under the solvothermal conditions and are stable in air. The experimental PXRD patterns of the compounds 1 and 2 are agreed with simulated patterns from their single-crystal structures, which confirm the phase purity of the compounds at room temperature (Fig. S1).

3.1. Crystal structure description of {[Mg2L(μ2-H2O)(μ2-DMA)]·DMA}n (1)

Single crystal X-ray crystallographic analysis reveals that the asymmetric unit of 1 contains three crystallographically independent Mg2+ ions (Mg2 and Mg3 lie on the special positions with site of fractional (SOF) of 0.5 in the asymmetric unit), one L4− ligand, one coordinated water molecule, one coordinated DMA molecule (DMA is disordered over two symmetrically related sites with 60%:40% split occupancies) and one free DMA molecule (Fig. 1a). The Mg1, Mg2 and Mg3 centers are all six-coordinated and have slight distorted octahedral coordination environments. The Mg1 ion is coordinated by four oxygen atoms (O2, O4i, O5ii and O7iii) from four L4− ligands, one oxygen atom (O10) from a water molecule and one oxygen atom (O11) from one DMA molecule. The Mg2 ion is coordinated by four oxygen atoms (O3i, O3iv, O8iii and O8v) from four L4− ligands and two oxygen atoms (O11 and O11vi) from two DMA molecules. The Mg3 ion is coordinated by four oxygen atoms (O1, O1vii, O6ii and O6viii) from four L4− ligands and two oxygen atoms (O10 and O10vii) from two water molecules. There exist metal–metal bonds between Mg1 and Mg2, Mg1 and Mg3 with the bond lengths of 3.657(1) and 3.652(1) Å, respectively. The M−O bond lengths range from 2.023(2) to 2.228(2) Å for 1. The L4− ligand bridges eight Mg ions through each carboxylate oxygen atom coordinating to a metal ion (Scheme 1a). The neighboring two Mg ions are tri-bridged by two carboxylate groups and one oxygen atom from the DMA or H2O to form a series of parallel aligned MgOC chains extending along the b axis (Fig. 1b). The MgOC chains are linked along the [301] and [−101] directions by para-terphenyl moieties of L4− ligands to lead to the three-dimensional framework (Fig. 1c).

3.2. Crystal structure description of [Ag4L(DMF)2]n (2)

The asymmetric unit of 2 contains four crystallographically independent Ag+ ions, two halves of the L4− ligands (the L4− ligands lie on the center of inversion), and two coordinated DMF molecules (Fig. 2a). The Ag1, Ag2 and Ag3 centers are all three-coordinated and have slight distorted T-type coordination environments. The Ag4 center is four-coordinated and have distorted tetrahedral coordination environment. The Ag1 ion is coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O1, O5i and O7ii) from three L4− ligands. The Ag2 ion is coordinated by two oxygen atoms (O2 and O6i) from two L4− ligands and one oxygen atom (O10) from one DMF molecule. The Ag3 ion is coordinated by three oxygen atoms (O2, O3i and O4iii) from three L4− ligands. The Ag4 ion is coordinated by two oxygen atoms (O5iv and O8) from two L4− ligands and two oxygen atoms (O9 and O9iv) from two DMF molecules. There exist metal–metal bonds between Ag1 and Ag2, Ag1 and Ag4, Ag1 and Ag1ii, Ag1 and Ag4ii, Ag3 and Ag3v, with the bond lengths of 2.828(1), 3.066(1), 3.131(1), 3.293(1) and 2.844(1) Å, respectively. The AgO bond lengths range from 2.147(3) to 2.468(4) Å, which are all comparable to those found in other Ag+ complexes [35], [36]. The L4− ligand bridges ten Ag ions with two oxygen atoms from two carboxylate groups in the terminal phenyl rings linking two Ag ions, respectively, and other six oxygen atoms linking six Ag ions through each carboxylate oxygen atom coordinating to a metal ion (Scheme 1b). Two Ag1, two Ag2 and two Ag4 ions are connected by the carboxylate groups and the AgAg bonds to form the hexanuclear Ag6 unit. Two Ag3 ions are connected by two carboxylate groups and the AgAg bonds to form the dinuclear Ag2 unit. The Ag6 units and the Ag2 units are connected alternatively by the carboxylate groups to form a serial of parallel aligned AgOC chains extending along the c axis (Fig. 2b). The AgOC chains are linked along the [010] and [−210] directions by para-terphenyl moieties of L4− ligands to lead to the three-dimensional framework (Fig. 2c).

3.3. Properties

Infrared spectra for H4L and 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. S2. The band at 1673 cm−1 is characteristic of CO stretching vibrations in free H4L ligand. However, the band disappears and two new bands appear. The bands are at 1431 cm−1 and 1602 cm−1 for 1, 1378 cm−1 and 1557 cm−1 for 2. The changes may be due to COO− in compounds 1 and 2, which is multi-electron π bond system with two special average C ≈ O evolved from the original CO and CO in COOH. The symmetrical stretching vibration (about 1400 cm−1) and antisymmetric stretching vibration (about 1650–1550 cm−1) of the two special average C ≈ O form two new bands.

As shown in Fig. 3, the solid-state photoluminescence properties of 1, 2 and free H4L ligand were studied at room temperature. The free H4L ligand emits blue-violet light with the narrow emission peak and the emission maximum at 400 nm upon excitation at the maximum excitation wavelength of 320 nm. The emission of the free ligand may be attributed to the π* → n or π* → π transitions [37], [38]. The compound 1 displays blue-violet light emission and has a similar emission spectrum curve to the free H4L ligand, with the emission maximum at 380 nm upon excitation at 320 nm, blue-shifted by about 20 nm when compared to the emission of H4L. Judged from the maximum excitation wavelength and spectra shape, the emission of compound 1 should be originated from the coordinated L4− ligands. The compound 2 is one d10 metal compound and exhibits a broad emission peak from 300 to 800 nm, with an emission maximum at 484 nm and some of the shoulder peaks upon excitation at 280 nm. Since the emission spectrum curve has a clear turning point at about 375 nm, there should be one emission shoulder peak centered at about 375 nm, blue-shifted by about 25 nm when compared to the emission of H4L, which should be originated from the coordinated L4− ligands. In 2, there exist AgAg interactions, and it has been reported that the AgAg interactions have an important influence on the energies of the ground and excited states of the silver coordination polymers and contribute to their photoluminescent properties [39], [40], [41], [42]. So the emission peaks centered at 484 and 531 nm (red-shifted by about 84 and 131 nm when compared to the emission of H4L, respectively) may arise from the synergistic effects of different interactions (such as AgAg interactions), as well as possible charge transfer between the Ag+ ions and the L4− ligands [43], [44], [45], [46], [47], [48].

Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed to verify the thermal stability of the two compounds (Fig. 4). The TGA curves of compound 1 reveals that 1 undergoes the first weight loss of 2.79% in the temperature range from room temperature to 130 °C, corresponding to the release of one water molecule (calcd. 2.80%) and the second weight loss of 27.21% from 130 °C to 480 °C corresponding to the loss of two DMA molecules (calcd. 27.05%). The decomposition continued with residue MgO (calcd. 12.4%) and amorphous carbon. The TGA curves of compound 2 shows that 2 undergoes a weight loss of 14.76% in the temperature range from room temperature to 320 °C, corresponding to the release of two DMF molecules (calcd. 14.90%). The decomposition continued with amorphous carbon and residue Ag (calcd. 44.04%).

4. Conclusion

In summary, we have synthesized and structurally characterized two CPs based on 1,1′:4′,1″-terphenyl-2′,4,4″,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4L). Both two compounds possess a series of parallel aligned MOC chains linked along two directions by para-terphenyl moieties of L4− ligands to lead to the three-dimensional frameworks. The photoluminescence properties of the compounds 1 and 2 have also been investigated. The emission of compound 1 should be originated from the coordinated L4− ligands. However, for the d10 metal compound, the emission of compound 2 may be the result of synergistic effects of the intraligand charge transfer (ILCT), charge transfer between the Ag+ ions and the L4− ligands (LMCT or MLCT) and the AgAg interactions.
