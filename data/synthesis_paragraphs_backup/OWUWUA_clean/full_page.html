Two heterotrimetallic organic frameworks constructed using a functionalized Schiff base ligand: syntheses, structures and visible photocatalytic activities for the degradation of chlorophenols†

Li Maa, Peng Dub, Jin Yanga, Ying-Ying Liu*a, Xiao-Li Liuc and Jian-Fang Ma*a
aKey Laboratory of Polyoxometalate Science, Department of Chemistry, Northeast Normal University, Changchun 130024, P. R. China. E-mail: liuyy147@nenu.edu.cn; majf247@yahoo.com; Fax: +86-431-85098620
bDepartment of Chemical Engineering, Yingkou Institute of Technology, Yingkou 115014, P. R. China
cQingtongxia City Jiahua Chemical Co. Ltd., Qingtongxia 751603, P. R. China

Received 25th July 2016 , Accepted 30th September 2016

First published on 17th October 2016

Two heterotrimetallic organic frameworks [(CH3)2NH2][Cd2Na(Fe-L)2(H2O)6]·4DMF·4H2O (1) and [ZnNa(Fe-L)(H2O)]·2DMF·2H2O (2), have been synthesized via the reaction of a novel tetracarboxyl-functionalized 1,2-ethanediamino-N,N′-bis(3-tertiarybutyl-5-((3,5-carboxybenzyl)oxy)salicylidene) (H6L) Schiff base ligand. In 1 and 2, the internal [N2O2] pockets of L6− anions are embedded by Fe(III) ions, while the external carboxylate groups are ligated by Zn(II)/Cd(II) and Na(I) ions. Both frameworks display fascinating 3D frameworks. The photodegradation properties of 1 and 2 towards 4-chlorophenol (4-CP) and 2,4-dichlorophenol (2,4-DCP) under visible light have been systematically investigated. Their magnetic behaviors have also been studied.

1. Introduction

Many research efforts have been aimed towards the design and synthesis of heterometallic frameworks because of their intrinsic motifs and unique catalysis behaviour.1–4 Compared with monometallic polymers, heterometallics contain two or more kinds of metals. Different metals can act as structural centers or in catalytic roles.5–7 In this category, a variety of heterometallics, such as Pd/Pb, Co/Cd and Co/Mn have exhibited effective catalytic performances in the Suzuki–Miyaura reaction, the hydrolytic resolution of epoxides and in Knoevenagel condensation reactions.8–10 The heterometallics based on Fe(III) ions can serve as excellent heterogeneous catalysts in various catalytic reactions, such as enzymatic catalysis, selective oxidation and asymmetric alkene epoxidation.11–14

Functionalized Schiff base ligands are usually used in the generation of heterometallics because they have multiple coordination sites and various coordination fashions.15–21 These ligands have internal [N2O2] sites and external functional groups, simultaneously. Various heterometallics have been reported based on this such as Mn(salen), Co(salen), Ni(salen) and Cr(salen).22–25 In this regard, Fe(salen) heterometallics have received a certain attention, and recent studies have highlighted some efficient photocatalysis phenomena in several Fe(III)-containing frameworks on the degradation of organic pollutants.26–30 However, research based on Fe(salen) heterometallics still remains rare because of their poor crystallinity.31

For quite some time, our lab has been committed to the syntheses of Fe(salen)-based heterometallic frameworks and analysis of their photocatalysis abilities on organic pollutants.28,29 As a kind of pollution in waste water, chlorophenols (CPs) are produced by insecticides and various industries. A lot of efforts have been made to solve the problems caused by CP pollutants. Photocatalytic degradation is one of the prospective techniques to solve this problem.32–39

Aiming at further exploring this field, we designed a novel tetracarboxyl-functionalized Schiff base ligand, 1,2-ethanediamino-N,N′-bis(3-tertiarybutyl-5-((3,5-carboxybenzyl)oxy)salicylidene) (denoted as H6L, Scheme 1). In H6L, the bridged oxygen and methylene groups can freely rotate. The internal [N2O2] sites and external carboxylic functional groups may suit different coordination requirements of metal ions in reactions.


Scheme 1 Organic ligand used in this study.

In this work, we report the assembly of two ternary-metallic Fe(III)/salen-derived frameworks, [(CH3)2NH2][Cd2Na(Fe-L)2(H2O)6]·4DMF·4H2O (1) and [ZnNa(Fe-L)(H2O)]·2DMF·2H2O (2) (DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide). Investigations into the photocatalytic degradation of 2-CP and 2,4-DCP were demonstrated. The frameworks’ magnetic properties have also been studied.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis of [(CH3)2NH2][Cd2Na(Fe-L)2(H2O)6]·4DMF·4H2O (1)

A mixture of CdCl2·2.5H2O (9.1 mg, 0.04 mmol), FeCl3·6H2O (5.4 mg, 0.02 mmol), NaCl (1.6 mg, 0.03 mmol), H6L (15.3 mg, 0.02 mmol), DMF (4 mL) and water (2 mL) was transferred into a Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 100 °C for 3 days. After being cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1, dark red rhomboid crystals were obtained via filtration in a 28% yield. Anal. calcd for C98H132Cd2Fe2NaN10O38 (Mr = 2417.91): C, 49.67; H, 5.51; N, 5.79. Found: C, 49.48; H, 5.56; N, 5.92. 2.2. Synthesis of [ZnNa(Fe-L)(H2O)]·2DMF·2H2O (2)

A mixture of ZnCl2 (5.5 mg, 0.04 mmol), FeCl3·6H2O (5.4 mg, 0.02 mmol), NaCl (1.6 mg, 0.03 mmol), H6L (15.3 mg, 0.02 mmol), DMF (3 mL) and water (1.5 mL) was transferred into a 10 mL glass vial and heated at 90 °C for 4 days. After being cooled to room temperature at a rate of 10 °C h−1, red rhomboid crystals were obtained via filtration in a 23% yield. Anal. calcd for C48H58ZnFeNaN4O17 (Mr = 1107.33): C, 52.06; H, 5.29; N, 5.06. Found: C, 52.39; H, 5.06; N, 5.22. 3. Results and discussion

3.1. Syntheses

In order to investigate the structures of the H6L ligand with Fe(III) and Zn(II)/Cd(II) atoms, we carried out numerous parallel experiments by adjusting conditions such as the type of solvent, ratio of reaction materials and temperature. Maximum yields and good quality crystals of 1 and 2 could only be obtained using the synthesis conditions as listed in the text. In our original experiments, a variety of single solvents (such as DMA, DMF, alcohol and methanol) were tested, but we only achieved unknown precipitates. Then, we carried out a series of attempts by using mixed solvents. Fortunately, single crystals of 1 and 2 could be obtained in DMF/water mixtures. We also employed a lot of effort in adjusting the ratios of Fe(III):Zn(II)/Cd(II):Na(I):H6L. However, other experiments gave small polycrystal or powder forms.

3.2. Description of the crystal structures

3.2.1. Crystal structure of [(CH3)2NH2][Cd2Na(Fe-L)2(H2O)6]·4DMF·4H2O (1). The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one Cd(II) ion, half a Na(I) ion, one Fe(III) salen unit (Fe-L), half a (CH3)2NH2+ cation, three coordinated water molecules, two free water molecules and two free DMF. The reagent DMF decomposed into the (CH3)2NH2+ cation, which acts as a negative charge to balance the framework. As shown in Fig. 1a, the octahedral coordination sphere of Na1 is completed by two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two distinct L6− anions and four water molecules. Cd1 is seven coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from three distinct L6− anions and one coordinated water molecule. The external carboxylate groups of each L6− anion attach to one Fe(III), one Na(I) atom and three Cd(II) atoms with bischelating coordination fashions. In this way, a porous 3D framework is generated (Fig. 1b). The free solvent-accessible volume of 1 is 27.5% of the unit-cell calculated by PLATON.
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environments of metal ions and the coordination mode of L6− anions. (b) View of the 3D porous framework of 1.
3.2.2. Crystal structure of [ZnNa(Fe-L)(H2O)]·2DMF·2H2O (2). The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one Zn(II) ion, one Na(I) ion, one Fe(III) salen unit (Fe-L), one coordinated water molecule, two free DMF and two free water molecules. As shown in Fig. 2a, the Fe(III) ion is embedded in the [N2O2] pocket of one L6− anion, and six coordinated by two oxygen atoms from another L6− anion. Na1 is tetra-coordinated by three oxygen atoms from three L6− anions and one coordinated water molecule. Zn1 is also in a tetrahedral coordination sphere, completed by oxygen atoms from three L6− anions and one water molecule. The external carboxylates of each L6− anion attach to one Fe(III) ion, three Na(I) ions and three Zn(II) ions by displaying bischelating and bisdentate coordination fashions. In this way, a 3D framework is engendered (Fig. 2b). The free solvent-accessible volume of 2 is 28.4% of the unit-cell volume.
Fig. 2 (a) The coordination environments of the metal ions and the coordination mode of L6− anions. (b) View of the 3D porous framework of 2.

Some analogues of the frameworks of 1 and 2 have been prepared based on the biscarboxyl-functionalized Schiff base 1,2-cyclohexanediamino-N,N′-bis(3-methyl-5-carboxysalicylidene) (H4L′). In frameworks [Zn2(Fe-L′)2(μ2-O)(H2O)2]·4DMF·4H2O,28 [Cd2(Fe-L′)2(μ2-O)(H2O)2]·2DMF·H2O28 and [BaNa(Fe-L′)2(μ2-OH)(H2O)]·DMF·2H2O,29 Fe(III) ions are also embedded in the [N2O2] pockets of the L′4− anions, but each of the (Fe-L′) units are bridged by μ2-O or μ2-OH anion to give (Fe-L′)2(μ2-O) or (Fe-L′)2(μ2-OH) dimers. Their structures are also 3D porous frameworks because of the interactions of the external carboxylate groups.

As a comparison, complexes synthesized via ordinary Schiff base ligands with Zn(II) or Cd(II) ions are generally monometallics and low dimensional.40,41 It is clear that internal [N2O2] sites and the external four carboxylate groups of H4L ligands play an important role in the metallic and structural diversity of frameworks 1 and 2.

3.3. Photocatalytic properties

The environmental problems caused by toxic and persistent organic pollutants have seriously affected the survival of organisms. Choosing an efficient and economic technology to eliminate these pollutants is one of the most active topics in environmental science.42–49 In this work, the degradation abilities of catalysts 1 and 2 to 4-CP and 2,4-DCP under visible-light irradiation were investigated in detail. The irradiated samples are analyzed by employing gas chromatography.

During the photodegradation process, tests at various pH values (3, 4 and 6) of 1 to 4-CP versus irradiation time were carried out. As shown in Fig. 3, the photocatalytic activities increased from 12.8% (without catalyst) to 25% (pH = 6), 48.1% (pH = 4) and 60.7% (pH = 3) with the decline of pH values after irradiating for 80 min, respectively. Obviously, the best degradation efficiency of 4-CP was at pH = 3. Under the optimized pH value, the degradation experiment of 2 was carried out. The photocatalytic activity of 2 to 4-CP resulted in a degradation efficiency (51.2%) which was a little lower than that of 1.


Fig. 3 The mass of 4-CP (a) without catalyst, and (b–d) with the addition of catalyst 1 at different pH values: (b) 6, (c) 4, and (d) 3. (e) The use of catalyst 2 at pH = 3. (f) Merged catalytic results of 1 for 4-CP at different pH values. (g) The removal of 4-CP without catalyst, and with catalyst 1 or 2 at pH = 3.

On the basis of these results, we made a further investigation into the photocatalytic degradation of dichlorophenol. As shown in Fig. 4, the photocatalytic activities of 1 to 2,4-DCP increase from 12.2% (without catalyst) to 34% (pH = 6), 38.8% (pH = 3) and 43% (pH = 4) after irradiating for 80 min. So we developed the degradation experiments of catalyst 2 at pH = 4. The degradation efficiency (57.4%) of 2 to 2,4-DCP is higher than that of 1.


Fig. 4 The mass of 2,4-DCP (a) without catalyst, and (b–d) with the addition of catalyst 1 at different pH values: (b) 6, (c) 3, and (d) 4. (e) The use of catalyst 2 at pH = 4. (f) Merged catalytic results of 1 for 2,4-DCP at different pH values. (g) The removal of 2,4-DCP without catalyst, and with catalyst 1 or 2 at pH = 4.

The photocatalytic degradation activities of 1 and 2 to 4-CP is slightly lower than the reported framework [BaNa(Fe-L)2(μ2-OH)(H2O)]·DMF·2H2O.29 The reason for this may lie in the flexibility of the –CH2– and –O– groups in the L6− Schiff base anion, which results in the shrinking of the flexible frameworks of 1 and 2 upon the degradations. Even so, as a pioneering study into the photocatalytic degradations of 2,4-DCP under visible-light irradiation, ternary metallic framework 2 shows a relatively high photocatalytic activity to 2,4-DCP.

Further photocatalytic tests with recycled catalyst 1 to 4-CP under the same conditions were carried out. The results show that 1 retains catalytic activity after being used three times (from 60.7% to 58.6% and 58.1%).

The crystal phase purity of fresh and used catalysts was verified via powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) studies (Fig. S2†). After photocatalysis at different pH values, the PXRD patterns are consistent with the calculated ones. The results demonstrate that the crystals can retain crystallinity after photocatalysis, and that the stabilities of the structures are excellent.

On the basis of the catalytic degradation results of 1, we found that 2,4-DCP is more stable than 4-CP. According to the literature information, this should be influenced by the number of Cl groups. The Cl group has electron-withdrawing features, and its resonance effect may also to some extent stabilize the aromatic ring. These cooperative effects may lead to the slower degradation rate of 2,4-DCP.50 As to the abnormal activity result of 2, the catalytic process may also be affected by some factors, such as the nature of the intermediates, the number of coordinated waters, or the pH values.29,51,52

Further, the experiment of 2,4-DCP was carried out in the presence of tert-butyl alcohol (denoted as TBA) under the optimal degradation efficiency conditions. As shown in Fig. 5, it can be seen that the presence of TBA greatly depressed the degradation activities of 2,4-DCP with compound 2 as catalyst (from 57.4% to 6.04%). TBA is a typical hydroxyl radical remover, which can capture ˙OH radicals in the reaction process.53,54 That is, the photodegradation process with catalyst 2 is predominately dependent on ˙OH radicals attacking the aromatic ring of 2,4-DCP.


Fig. 5 (a) The mass of 2,4-DCP during the decomposition reaction at pH = 4 with 2 as catalyst in the presence of TBA. (b) Comparison of photocatalytic degradation abilities at pH = 4 of 2 alone and with TBA.

Based on information in the literature, the photocatalytic activities originate from Fe(III) centers instead of Schiff base ligands. A possible photocatalytic reaction route is proposed in Scheme 2.52,55 Firstly, the central Fe(III) units of the heterometallic framework yielded [L-Fe(III)–OH] substances by interacting with water. In the presence of H2O2 (nucleophile addition reagent) and upon the irradiation with visible-light, [L-Fe(III)–OOH]* excited-state transitions formed. Then the O–O bond ruptured, bringing about the generation of [L-Fe(V)O] and ˙OH radicals. The ˙OH radicals immediately reacted with CPs to yield small degradation products.56


Scheme 2 The possible route for the generation of ˙OH radicals over catalysts with H2O2 as oxidant.

3.4. Magnetic property

For 1 and 2, the χmT values at 300 K are 6.24 and 5.18 cm3 K mol−1 (Fig. 6), which are higher than the 4.38 cm3 K mol−1 expected for Fe(III) ions with S = 5/2 and g = 2.00. Upon cooling, their χmT values first gradually and then rapidly decrease and reach to 2.73 and 1.34 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, respectively. This behavior indicates the overall antiferromagnetic coupling among the Fe(III) ions.57–59 The magnetic susceptibility data can be fitted by the Curie–Weiss law with C = 6.41 cm3 K mol−1, θ = −18.55 K for 1 above 20 K, and C = 6.41 cm3 K mol−1, θ = −55.71 K for 2 above 22 K.
Fig. 6 The temperature dependence plots of χm−1 and χmT for 1 and 2.

4. Conclusions

In this paper, we have successfully developed two heterotrimetallic 3D frameworks based on Fe(III)/Cd(II)/Na(I) and Fe(III)/Zn(II)/Na(I) metals with a novel tetracarboxyl-functionalized Schiff base. The photodegradation investigation of catalysts 1 and 2 to 4-CP and 2,4-DCP under visible light has been investigated in detail. The results indicate that 1 shows a higher activity to 4-CP at pH = 3, while 2 shows a higher the activity to 2,4-DCP at pH = 4. It also suggests that the flexibility of the frameworks may influence the photodegradation activity. Further application of heterotrimetallics in photocatalytic decomposition of organic pollutants is in progress.
