Syntheses, structures and properties of three-dimensional lanthanide frameworks constructed with a trigonal anti-prismatic lanthanide cluster†

Ning Yuan ab, Tianlu Sheng a, Chongbin Tian a, Shengmin Hu a, Ruibiao Fu a, Qilong Zhu ab, Chunhong Tan ab and Xintao Wu *a
aState Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, 350002, PR China. E-mail: wxt@fjirsm.ac.cn.; Fax: +86 591 83714946; Tel: + 86 591 83719238
bGraduate University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, 100049, PR China

Received 13th November 2010 , Accepted 1st April 2011

First published on 28th April 2011

Two three-dimensional lanthanide coordination polymers, [Ln9(HNA)12(O2H)]·6H2O (Ln = Gd (1), Eu (2); HNA = 2-hydroxylnicotinic anion), were obtained from the reactions of Ln2O3 with 2-mercaptonicotinic acid (MNA) under hydrothermal conditions, in which HNA was generated from in situsubstitution reactions of MNA. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that 1 and 2 are isomorphous and crystallize in trigonal space group Rc. The structure features an ideal trigonal anti-prismatic hexanuclear cluster and a unique [O2H]3−group encapsulated in the cluster center, which represents the first example involving the lanthanide complexes. The hexanuclear cluster further forms a three-dimensional cubic network by bridging six outer lanthanide ions. Compound 2 displays the characteristic EuIII emission bands upon excitation at 395 nm. The magnetic properties of compounds 1 and 2 have also been investigated.

Introduction

The past two-decade period has witnessed rapid progress regarding polynuclear lanthanide complexes, generally featuring fascinating polyhedral structures1 and showing potential applications as magnetic, catalytic and fluorescent materials.2 Due to the flexible coordination numbers and the versatile coordination modes, lanthanide metal ions by chelating with the widely alternative organic ligands are excellent candidates for the construction of new materials. However, the rational choice and assembly of the organic ligands and lanthanide metal ions to achieve a precise, predictable outcome is currently a challenging task,1,3 although a considerable number of organolanthanides have been synthesized with coordination to carboxylate ligands, amino acid ligands, beta-diketonate ligands, alkoxide ligands, cyclopentadienyl ligands and other ancillary ligands.1 In comparison to the well-established cluster chemistry of transition metals,4 the number of their counterparts involving lanthanide metals is still relatively limited yet,5 especially for the three-dimensional lanthanide–organic coordination polymers constructed with polynuclear cluster nodes (so called secondary building units, SBUs),6 although there exist a few discrete high-nuclearity lanthanide clusters3b,7 and a large number of lanthanide polymers whose metallic moieties show low-nuclearity polyhedra,1 which would limit their performances for applicable value.6c Thus, it is a challenge to synthesize multi-dimensional lanthanide complexes constructed with high-nuclearity cluster nodes. To date, lanthanide–organic polymers using hexanuclear building units are rarely reported,8 notwithstanding the isolated hexanuclear clusters involving lanthanide which have been well-developed.9 Further more, their configurations are dominated with octahedrons.8b,9b,10 By contrast, lanthanide clusters with trigonal anti-prismatic configurations are extremely rare.11 For some time, our group has made some progress in the studies of heteronuclear rare earth–transition metal compounds by utilizing amino acids as the ligands.12 Recently, we extended our interests to investigate pure rare earth metal complexes. Herein we report two complexes [Ln9(HNA)12(O2H)]·6H2O (Ln = Gd (1), Eu (2); HNA = 2-hydroxylnicotinic anion). HNA is generated from in situsubstitution reactions of MNA under hydrothermal conditions. The two three-dimensional complexes display primitive cubic net-like structures, featuring an ideal trigonal anti-prismatic hexanuclear cluster. The crystal structures and magnetic properties of 1 and 2 are discussed, and the fluorescent property of 2 is also reported.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All chemicals were of AR grade commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were carried out with Vario MICRO elemental analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded in the range of 4000∼400 cm−1 on a Perkin-Elemer Specture-one FT-IR instrument with KBr pellet. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were acquired on D/MXA-2500 diffractometer measurements using Cu Kα radiation in the ambient environment. The calculated patterns of complexes 1 and 2 were generated with PowerCell. The solid-state luminescence emission and excitation spectra were recorded on a FLS920 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The magnetic measurements for 1 and 2 were carried out on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL magnetometer.

Synthesis of complexes

[Gd9(HNA)12(O2H)]·6H2O (1). A mixture of Gd2O3 (0.25 mmol, 0.091 g), 2-mercaptonicotinic acid (0.25 mmol, 0.039 g) and 12 ml H2O was stirred in air for 30 min and then sealed in a 23 ml Teflon-lined autoclave. After being heated at 180 °C for 7 days, the mixture was cooled down slowly to room temperature over 72 h. Large, light yellow crystals were obtained in a yield of 31% based on Gd. Anal. Crystal structure determination

The program of Multi-scan was used for the empirical absorption corrections. The structure was solved by direct methods with SHELXTL program package.13 The non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters with the exception of oxygen atoms from free water molecules. Results and discussion

In situ reaction analysis

To our surprise, the initial reactant, 2-mercaptonicotinic acid, was transformed into deprotonated 2-hydroxylnicotinic acid in the final products (Scheme 1). The length of a carbon–sulfur bond is normally around 1.780 Å as reported in various previous works,14 while that value is approximately 1.296 Å (to be precise, 1.296(7)–1.297(7) Å for 1, and 1.285(8)–1.293(8) Å for 2) from the single-crystal X-ray analysis in our case, which is the suitable length for a carbon–oxygen bond.15 The formation of the C–O bond was also supported by the elemental analysis of complexes 1 and 2. The result suggests that the reaction involves the cleavage of a sp2carbon–sulfur bond and the formation of a carbon–oxygen bond via a substitution reaction.

Crystal structures

Single X-ray diffraction analyses reveal that 1 and 2 are isomorphous with the general formula [Ln9(HNA)12(O2H)]·6H2O (Ln = Gd (1), Eu (2); HNA = 2-hydroxylnicotinic anion) and crystallize in the trigonal space group Rc. Here only the crystal structure of complex 1 is taken as an example to be discussed in detail. Selected bond lengths and bond angles for complex 1 are listed in Table S1 (ESI†). The structure of complex 1 may be described as a three-dimensional cubic network constructed by the hexanuclear cluster cores and the bridging gadolinium ions along with six free water molecules.

There are two crystallographically dependent GdIII ions in the structure of complex 1. As shown in Fig. 1, GdIII (1) is attached with an approximate Cs symmetry to one oxygen atom, O7 of the [O2H]3−group (discussed below), and five oxygen atoms of carboxylate groups from four HNA ligands and two oxygen atoms from hydroxyl groups (Fig. 1a). On the other hand, the bridging GdIII (2) is ten-coordinated with regular C2 symmetry by six oxygen atoms of carboxylate groups from six ligands and four nitrogen atoms from four ligands (Fig. 1b).


Fig. 1 Coordinated environments of GdIII(1) (a) and GdIII(2) (b) in compound 1. All hydrogen atoms have been omitted for clarity. Symmetry codes: A) x − y + 1, x, −z; B) −x + y + 1, −x + 2, z; C) −x + 4/3, −x + y + 2/3, −z + 1/6; D) x − y + 1/3, −y + 5/3, −z + 1/6. Gray, C; blue, N; red, O; green, Gd.

As shown in Fig. 2, the core consists of six lanthanide atoms, twelve completely deprotonated ligands and one [O2H]3−group. It can be seen that the six gadolinium atoms are disposed in an ideal trigonal anti-prismatic arrangement (Fig. 3). The two parallel faces of the trigonal anti-prism, each of which is composed of three GdIII ions, are connected by six μ2-oxygen atoms (O1) of carboxylate groups. The distance between the two parallel layers is 3.8677(1) Å, and that between the neighboring GdIII ions from the different layers is 4.4581(4) Å. In each parallel layer, the three GdIII ions form an equilateral triangle with the length of 3.8402(5) Å, with each two GdIII ions in the edge being bridged by a carboxylate oxygen atom (O4).

Fig. 3 View of the [O2H]3−group in (a) the metal–oxygen framework of the hexanuclear cluster; (b) the trigonal anti-prism formed by hexanuclear cluster. Symmetry codes: A) x − y + 1, x, −z; B) −x + y + 1, −x + 2, z; C) −x + 4/3, −x + y + 2/3, −z + 1/6; D) −x + 2, −y + 2, −z; E) y, −x + y + 1, −z. Red, O; pink, H; green, Gd.

It is intriguing that the hexanuclear core encapsulates two μ3-endo-oxygen atoms lying along the three-fold axis (Fig. 3), with each connecting to three GdIII ions of the corresponding parallel layer of the anti-prism. This is rarely reported in previous works involving clusters constructed by lanthanides, since the μ3-oxygen linking metal atoms in clusters tend to adopt an exo-coordination mode.12a The μ3-endo-oxygen atoms may be reckoned to originate from the reactant lanthanide oxide in the reaction system. Besides, it should be noted that the distance between the two μ3-endo-oxygen atoms is 2.5248(87) Å, and the charge balance, together with this relatively short distance, suggests that there should be a resonant hydrogen atom between them (Fig. 3).16 As far as is known, this phenomenon has not been reported in clusters involving lanthanides, though it has been observed in related systems involving transition metals, such as iron,17manganese,18tungsten,19nickel,20copper21 and so on.

In the previous reports, the structures of the hexanuclear clusters involving lanthanides included octahedron,8b,9b,10 trigonal prism,22 macrocyclic cluster9a and so on,1 usually featuring an interstitial μ6- or μ4-oxygen23 or halogen22a located at the central site. However the number of constructions formed with trigonal anti-prism, which may be viewed as prolonged or shortened octahedron along the three-fold axis, was extremely limited.11 Hence, our current work supplements a rare example, and supplies the first case of six lanthanide atoms displaying in an ideal trigonal anti-prismatic arrangement.

For the ligands, there exist two types of coordination modes (Scheme 2), both of which adopt μ6-η1:2:2:1-coordination modes displaying five- and three-membered chelate rings. The bond distances of Gd–O are in the range of 2.311(4)–2.529(4) Å (2.321(5)–2.555(5) Å for 2·Eu), and those of Gd–N in the range of 2.567(7)–2.666(6) Å (2.590(7)–2.670(6) Å for 2·Eu).

Six outer GdIII ions act as linkers to connect each core via four chelating oxygen atoms which are transformed from sulfur atoms by an in situ reaction (Fig. 4b). The chelating surroundings of the linker GdIII ion is shown in Fig. 4a. Each hexanuclear node is connected through six linkers to its six neighboring nodes and the network topology might be described as a distorted primitive cubic network (Fig. 5).

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibility measurements of compounds 1 and 2 were performed with polycrystalline samples in the temperature range of 2–300 K, where χM (χM = magnetic susceptibility) is corrected molar magnetic susceptibility per [Ln9] (Ln = Gd, Eu) unit, and diamagnetic corrections of the constituent atoms were estimated from Pascal's tables.24 The results indicate that they show different magnetic properties though with the same structures.

The magnetic dependence of the magnetic susceptibility of 1 is displayed in Fig. 6 in the form of χMT and χM−1versus T curves. At room temperature, the χMT value is 72.47 cm3 K mol−1, which is slightly higher than the expected theoretical value of 70.92 cm3 K mol−1, calculated by nine isolated GdIII ions in the 8S7/2 ground state at 293 K on the basis of literature data.25 With the temperature lowered, the χMT value keeps constant approximately at 72 cm3 K mol−1 until 25 K, indicating basic paramagnetic property. Upon further cooling, the χMT value plunges dramatically and reaches 63.60 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, suggesting the presence of weak anti-ferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent GdIII ions mediated by bridging oxygen atoms. Due to the complexity of the structural unit, the Heisenberg model could not be used. Hence the magnitude of the interaction was estimated on the basis of the Curie–Weiss law, and the Weiss constant, θ, is calculated to be −0.46 K. The negative Weiss constant also indicates an overall anti-ferromagnetic interaction.26


Fig. 6 Plot of the temperature dependence of χMT and χM−1 for 1 (per [Gd9] unit).

The magnetic behavior of 2 is shown in Fig. 7, from which we can see that the χMT value decreases smoothly with the temperature cooling from 300 K to 2 K. The χMT value at room temperature is 13.30 cm3 K mol−1, slightly less than the expected theoretical value of 13.50 cm3 K mol−1 for nine EuIII ions based on the van Vleck equation allowing for population of the lower excited state of 7FJ (EuIII) at 293 K. Because of the depopulation of the Stark levels for a single EuIII ion, the χMT value descends continuously as the temperature is lowered.27 When the temperature is close to 0 K, the χMT value is predicted to reach zero as a result of the ground state of the EuIII ion, i.e., 7F0.28 The magnetic susceptibility above 150 K coincides with the Curie–Weiss law owing to the presence of thermally populated excited states.29

Fluorescent properties

Due to the excellent fluorescent properties of EuIII ions,30 the photoluminescent spectrum of compound 2 was measured in the solid state. Under UV excitation at room temperature, 2 emits intense red fluorescence. The excitation spectrum of 2 shows the absorption bands in the range from 200 nm to 550 nm (see Fig. S3, ESI†).

Upon excitation at 395 nm, the emission spectrum of 2 at room temperature exhibits the characteristic transition of 5D0 → 7FJ (J = 0–4) of EuIII ions via a ligand-to-metal energy mechanism (Fig. 8). The most intense emission at 618 nm is attributed to 5D0 → 7F2 transition induced by the electric dipole, suggesting that the interactions between the complex and its local environment are strong31 and that the ligand generated from the in situ reaction is favorable for the sensitization of red luminescence for EuIII ions under the experimental conditions used.32 The medium–strong emission at 590 nm and 700 nm are corresponding to the magnetic dipole induced 5D0 → 7F1 transition and the electric dipole induced 5D0 → 7F4 transition, respectively. The presence of the 5D0 → 7F0 transition at 579 nm indicates that the EuIII in 2 occupies sites with low symmetry and possess a non-centrosymmetric coordination environment.33 As is well known, the intense ratio of 5D0 → 7F2/5D0 → 7F1 was widely used as a measurement of the symmetry of the rare earth. For 2, the intense ratio of 5D0 → 7F2/5D0 → 7F1 is ca. 6.12, which testifies the low site symmetry of EuIII in 2 as well.


Fig. 8 Solid state emission spectrum of 2 (λex = 395 nm) at room temperature.

The emission spectrum of 2 exhibits plenty of small splittings, which is useful for confirming the local site symmetry of the lanthanide ions.34 As shown in Fig. 8, the splittings for the 5D0 → 7FJ transition are relatively clear with the exception of the 5D0 → 7F3 transition. Moreover, it is noted that the 5D0 → 7F0 transition of EuIII ions only arises in a limited number of site symmetries (only allowed for the following 10 site symmetries, Cs, C1, C2, C3, C4, C6, C2v, C3v, C4v, and C6v).35 According to the coordination number of the europium ions and the splittings, it can be concluded that the central ions could be Cs or C2 symmetry, which is in agreement with the single-crystal X-ray analysis (slightly distorted Cs symmetry for EuIII (1), and regular C2 for EuIII (2)).

Conclusions

Two three-dimensional lanthanide–organic coordination polymers have been synthesized and characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction along with powder XRD, IR, fluorescent and magnetic analyses. The structures show a three-dimensional cubic network, featuring a rare, ideal trigonal anti-prismatic hexanuclear cluster, and a [O2H]3−group encapsulated in the core. Compound 2 shows red luminescence with characteristic EuIII emission, and 1 and 2 possess different magnetic behaviors though with the same constructions.
