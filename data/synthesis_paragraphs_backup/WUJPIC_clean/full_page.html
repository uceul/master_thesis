Size-exclusive and coordination-induced selective dye adsorption in a nanotubular metal–organic framework†

Yi Han a, Shunan Sheng a, Fan Yang a, Yabo Xie a, Minjian Zhao a and Jian-Rong Li *ab
aBeijing Key Laboratory for Green Catalysis and Separation, Department of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, College of Environmental and Energy Engineering, Beijing University of Technology, Beijing 100124, P. R. China. E-mail: jrli@bjut.edu.cn; Fax: +86-10-6739-2332
bState Key Laboratory of Organic–Inorganic Composites, Beijing University of Chemical Technology, Beijing 100029, P. R. China

Received 5th February 2015 , Accepted 3rd May 2015

First published on 26th May 2015

A new metal–organic framework, {[(CH3)2NH2][Co2NaL2(CH3COO)2]·xS}n (BUT-51, H2L = 5-(pyridine-4-yl)isophthalic acid) with two types of nanotubular channels, was synthesized and used in dye adsorption. It was found that cationic dyes, including Methylene Blue (MB), Acridine Red (AR) and Acriflavine Hydrochloride (AH), can be rapidly adsorbed by BUT-51, but larger Methylene Violet (MV) and anionic Methyl Orange (MO), as well as neutral Solvent Yellow 2 (SY2), can hardly be adsorbed. These results imply that the adsorption behaviour of BUT-51 with the cationic dyes follows not only a charge- but also size- and shape-exclusive effect. Surprisingly, BUT-51 also preferentially adsorbs (or separates) AH from MB and AR, respectively, in their mixed acetone solution, probably due to the coordination between AH and Co2+ in its framework. Furthermore, AH uptake can change the porosity and framework stability of BUT-51 to give a robust partner.

1. Introduction

Porous solid materials are playing key roles in adsorption/separation in every aspect of relevant human activity.1–3 Over the last three decades, as an excellent type of crystalline porous material, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs)4–6 that are formed by connecting metal ions (clusters) with organic ligands via coordination bonds have gained increasing attention due to their well-tailored structures and associated applications ranging from gas storage/separation,7–12 ion exchange,13,14 catalysis,15–17 chemical sensing,18–21 and so on.22 As porous materials, the exploration of host–guest chemistry in MOFs based on guest inclusion or exchange is one of the most auspicious fields.23,24 Guest molecules in the pores of host MOFs are not “free”, usually the encapsulation involved in these kinds of interactions includes weak H-bonding or π–π stacking,25–28 and strong coordination with metal sites.29–31 In this regard, preferential adsorption or separation of a specific molecule could be driven by means of the strength of such host–guest interactions.

As a large amount of industrial byproducts, dyes including electrically neutral, positively, and negatively charged ones, are mostly immune towards light and oxidation, resulting in the difficulty to degrade and therefore serious pollution. Consequently, the removal of dyes before discharge into nature is of great imperative. Recently, charged MOFs with nano-sized pores have been applied in the field of selective adsorption and separation of dyes.32–39 Compared with other traditional techniques, such as chemical degradation and membrane filtration technologies,40 the method based on ion exchange adopting by MOFs possesses incomparable advantages in user-friendly control and low energy-cost. However, it still remains a challenge to separate like-charged dyes with a similar size by using MOFs or other porous materials. In this work, utilizing a tridentate pyridyl-carboxylic ligand, 5-(pyridine-4-yl)isophthalic acid (H2L), we synthesized a novel di-functional nanotubular anionic framework MOF with the formula of {[(CH3)2NH2][Co2NaL2(CH3COO)2]·xS}n (BUT-51, BUT = Beijing University of Technology, S represents unassigned free solvent molecules). It was found that this MOF can rapidly adsorb cationic dyes with a similar size, including Methylene Blue (MB), Acridine Red (AR), and Acriflavine Hydrochloride (AH) based on the cation exchange, but hardly adsorb larger Methylene Violet (MV) and anionic Methyl Orange (MO), as well as neutral Solvent Yellow 2 (SY2) from acetone solution. Surprisingly, BUT-51 also preferentially adsorbs AH from MB and AR, respectively, in their mixed acetone solution, probably due to the coordination between AH and Co2+ in its framework. It is much more interesting that the AH uptake can stabilize the original framework of BUT-51, and make AH@BUT-51 show permanent porosity in the dried state.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Materials and methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used as received without further purification. 5-(Pyridin-4-yl)isophthalic acid (H2L) was purchased from Jinan Henghua Sci. & Tec. Co., Ltd. IR spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu IR435 spectrometer as KBr pellets (4000–400 cm−1). Thermal analysis was performed on a Netzsch STA 449C thermal analyzer from 30 to 900 °C at a heating rate of 10 min−1 in the atmosphere. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded on a PANalytical X'Pert PRO diffractometer by using Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.541874 Å). UV-vis spectra were recorded with a Double Beam Hitachi U-2900 Spectrophotometer. Gas adsorption/desorption measurements were performed on an ASAP 2020 surface area analyzer with the extra pure quality N2 (99.999% purity).

2.2. Synthesis

{[(CH3)2NH2][Co2NaL2(CH3COO)2]·xS}n (BUT-51). A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (43.6 mg, 0.15 mmol), H2L (36.4 mg, 0.15 mmol), and NaOH (12 mg, 0.3 mmol) dissolved in DMA (10 mL) and MeOH (2.5 mL) was heated at 160 °C for 96 hours. After cooling the reaction system to RT at a rate of 5 °C h−1, purple block crystals of BUT-51 (22.3 mg, 17% yield based on H2L) were harvested, and then thoroughly washed with DMA. The as-synthesized material was determined to be insoluble in common organic solvents, such as DMSO, DMA, DMF, and acetone. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3399(m), 2967(w), 2257(m), 1619(s), 1573(s), 1445(w), 1358(s), 1296(w), 1221(w), 1109(w), 1076(w), 1026(w), 939(m), 840(w) 777(s), 723(s), and 640(s). The thermal stability and phase purity of the as-synthesized BUT-51 were checked by TGA (Fig. S2†) and PXRD (Fig. 2a), respectively.

2.3. Dye absorption, separation, and release

Dye absorption. Freshly prepared BUT-51 (∼10 mg) was first washed and solvent exchanged with acetone for three days, and then transferred into acetone solutions of Methylene Blue (MB), Acridine Red (AR), Acriflavine Hydrochloride (AH), Methylene Violet (MV), Methyl Orange (MO), Solvent Yellow 2 (SY2), C. I. Acid Yellow 1 (AY1), and C. I. Solvent Yellow 7 (SY7) with given concentrations in a vial, respectively. UV-vis spectra of the dye solution were used to record the dye adsorption ability of BUT-51 along with the soaking time.
Dye separation. Acetone exchanged BUT-51 (∼10 mg) was transferred into acetone solutions of equimolar MB and AH, and AR and AH mixtures in a vial, respectively. UV-vis spectra of the solution were then used to record the selective dye adsorption ability of BUT-51 along with the soaking time.
Dye release. The BUT-51 samples (∼10 mg) loaded with MB, AR, and AH were transferred into pure acetone or a saturated solution of NH4Cl in acetone in a vial, respectively. UV-vis spectra of the extraction solution were used to record the selective dye release ability of BUT-51 vs. soaking time.

2.4. Sample activation

The as-synthesized sample of BUT-51 was thoroughly washed with fresh DMA 3 times, and the extract was discarded. Fresh acetone was subsequently added, and the sample was allowed to stay in it for 24 h. This procedure was again repeated two times. After decanting the acetone extract, the sample was dried under a dynamic vacuum (<10−3 Torr) at room temperature for 1 hour. Before the adsorption measurement, the sample was further activated using the “outgas” function of the adsorption analyzer at 50 °C for 10 h. The activation procedure of AH@BUT-51 was similar to that of the newly synthesized BUT-51, except that it was washed thoroughly with fresh acetone and left in acetone for 3 days to remove any excess AH.

2.5. Single-crystal X-ray diffraction

The diffraction data of BUT-51 were collected on an Agilent Technologies SuperNova Single Crystal Diffractometer that was equipped with graphite-monochromatized Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 173 K. Empirical absorption corrections were applied to the intensities by using spherical harmonics, implemented in the SCALE3 ABSPACK scaling algorithm.41 The structure was solved by using the program SHELXS-97 and refined with the program SHELXL-97.42 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The hydrogen atoms of ligands were included in the structure-factor calculations at idealized positions by using a riding model and were refined isotropically. The diffraction data of BUT-51 were collected by using the “SQUEEZE” as implemented in PLATON to remove the contribution of disordered solvent molecules.43† Crystal data of BUT-51 are summarized in Table S1† and the selected bond lengths and angles in Table S2.†

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis, general characterization, structure, and pore nature of BUT-51

Purple crystals of BUT-51 were obtained by the solvothermal reaction of Co(NO3)2·6H2O, NaOH, and H2L in DMA/MeOH. The as-synthesized BUT-51 is stable in air, however, from the TG analysis, the decomposition of the framework structure starts at 275 °C accompanied by the escape of the guest solvent molecules (Fig. S2†). The phase purity of BUT-51 is confirmed by PXRD analyses, in which the experimental pattern is almost consistent with the simulated one (Fig. 2a). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction reveals that BUT-51 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group P42/m and has a three-dimensional (3D) nanotubular anionic framework structure with protonated [(CH3)2NH2]+ as counterions. In the structure, the crystallographically asymmetric unit contains 1/2Co2+, 1/4Na+, 1/2L2−, 1/2CH3COO−, and 1/4[(CH3)2NH2]+. The CH3COO− and [(CH3)2NH2]+ should be generated from the decomposition of DMA during the solvothermal reaction.44 The Co2+ center exhibits a five-coordinated distorted square-pyramidal geometry (τ = 0.172), binding with two O atoms from one CH3COO− ligand, two carboxylate O atoms, and one pyridyl N atom from three independent L2−. The Co–O bond lengths range from 2.036(5) to 2.379(5) Å. Na+ is octahedrally coordinated by two O atoms from two independent CH3COO− ligands and four carboxylate O atoms from distinct L2−. The carboxylate groups in each L2− adopting μ2–η2 linking mode connect two Co2+ and two Na+ atoms, respectively, while each CH3COO− ligand bridges one Co2+ and one Na+ in a μ2–η2:η1 linkage fashion (Fig. 1a).
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination environments of Co2+, Na+, L2−, and CH3COO− in BUT-51. (b) 3D structure of BUT-51 highlighting its tubular pores A and B. (c) Disordered [(CH3)2NH2]+ located in B pores of BUT-51. Color scheme: Co, purple; Na, pink; N, blue; C, cyan; O, red. H atoms were omitted for clarity.

Notably, BUT-51 features a large square nanotubular channel (namely pore A) as shown in Fig. 1b, which is composed of L2− ligands bridging Co2+ atoms with a pore diagonal of 14 Å and an edge distance of 10 Å. The adjacent nanotubes are linked by the Na–O bonds to generate a 3D anionic framework, resulting in another channel B with a pore diagonal of 6.5 Å (4.6 Å edge distance), in which disordered [(CH3)2NH2]+ cations accommodate (Fig. 1c). The solvent-accessible volume estimated by PLATON is 26.4% of the total volume, after removing all the guest solvent molecules.43 In order to confirm the permanent porosity of this MOF, BUT-51 was degassed under dynamic vacuum at 50 °C for 12 h after solvent exchange with acetone. Unfortunately dried BUT-51 showed no N2 adsorption, probably due to the framework collapse, which was confirmed by subsequent PXRD analysis (Fig. 2b).


Fig. 2 Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns of BUT-51 in different states.

3.2. Selective adsorption and release of dyes in BUT-51

Based on the large nanotubular channels in the framework of BUT-51, the investigation of guest capture in solution seems to be promising to explore the porosity and its potential application. We chose different dye molecules to evaluate the absorption ability of this MOF. Considering the pore size of A, five dyes with similar sizes: positively charged MB, AR, and AH, negatively charged MO, and neutral SY2 were first checked (Fig. S1†). The freshly prepared BUT-51 crystals were respectively immersed in acetone solutions of these dyes at room temperature. The adsorption abilities of BUT-51 toward these dyes were determined by UV-vis spectroscopy (Fig. 3). It was found that cationic dyes MB, AR, and AH could be efficiently absorbed over a period of time, and the purple crystals gradually became blue, claret-red, and red towards MB, AR, and AH, respectively (Fig. 4), whereas the adsorption of anionic and neutral dyes (i.e., MO and SY2) was not observed. This observed result suggests that BUT-51 can rapidly and selectively adsorb cationic dyes into pore A via cation exchange probably accompanied by the release of [(CH3)2NH2]+ from pore B, while anionic and neutral dyes were only staying in the solution. In addition, PXRD confirms that dye adsorption takes place with the retention of the framework structure of the MOF (Fig. 2a). Furthermore, the adsorption ability of BUT-51 toward another positive dye of MV was also checked. The result shows no distinct MV uptake. The difference in absorption between the four types of cationic dyes may result from the size and shape exclusion because MV is triangle-planar and relatively large compared with the other three, which are linear. These results suggest that the adsorption behavior of BUT-51 upon these dyes should be not only a charge exclusion effect but also a size and shape exclusion effect. In addition, another two smaller (compared to MO and SY2) negatively charged C. I. Acid Yellow 1 (AY1) and neutral C. I. Solvent Yellow 7 (SY7) were further tested. UV-vis results indicate that there are still no obvious uptakes toward the two dyes (Fig. S3†), suggesting that the cation exchange may be the driven-force and plays a dominant role in the dye adsorption of this MOF, even though the anionic and neutral dyes selected here seem to be able to accommodate pores A with regard to size.
Fig. 3 UV-vis spectra of acetone solutions of MB (a), AR (b), AH (c), MV (d), MO (e), and SY2 (f) in the presence of BUT-51 monitored with time to check the adsorption ability of this MOF towards these dyes.

Fig. 4 Photographs of tracing the dye-adsorption process through immersing the as-prepared crystals of BUT-51 in acetone solutions of different dyes.

On the other hand, the exploration of the reversibility of the cation exchange in BUT-51 is also interesting, because in practical application whether or not the adsorbent could be reused and recycled is greatly significant. Here, the release experiments were performed with the dyes loaded MB@BUT-51, AR@BUT-51 and AH@BUT-51 in pure acetone and a saturated acetone solution of NH4Cl, respectively. It was demonstrated that the MB and AR can hardly be released from MB@BUT-51 and AR@BUT-51 in fresh acetone, whereas they can be readily discharged in the presence of NH4Cl as the trigger (Fig. 5). This result demonstrates that the MB and AR adsorptions are accompanied by the cation exchange as proposed above. Surprisingly, no obvious AH release was observed in both pure acetone and a saturated acetone solution of NH4Cl (Fig. S4†). This might be attributed to such a fact that MB and AR in the pores A of BUT-51 bond with the anionic framework via weak host–guest interactions, such as H-bonding, while AH is tightly bonded by the coordination between the terminal group “–NH2” in it and the unsaturated Co2+ ions. The coordination of the –NH2 group could be reflected, to some extent by the difference of solid-state UV-vis spectra of AH@BUT-51 and BUT-51. It was found that the maximum absorption peak of AH@BUT-51 (552 nm) is significantly blue-shifted by 15 nm compared with that of BUT-51 (537 nm), demonstrating the change of the coordination environment of Co2+ in AH@BUT-51, due to the uptake of AH (Fig. S5†). However, the attempt to determine the exact position of the AH by single-crystal X-ray diffraction failed, due to the weak diffraction of the AH@BUT-51 crystals.


Fig. 5 The MB and AR release from MB@BUT-51 (a) and AR@BUT-51 (c) in an NH4Cl saturated solution of acetone monitored by UV-vis spectroscopy; and the release rate comparison of MB from MB@BUT-51 (b) and AR from AR@BUT-51 (d) in pure acetone and an NH4Cl saturated solution of acetone, respectively.

Furthermore, we also studied the selective adsorption and separation of AH from mixtures of AH & MB and AH & AR. AH, MB, and AR were selected, in order that their charge, size and shape effects could be excluded. We found that with increasing time AH could be gradually trapped within the pores of BUT-51, while MB and AR were denied (Fig. 6). As a result, it was demonstrated that AH can be separated from MB and AR in acetone solution. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example, a MOF can be applied to separate cationic dyes with a similar size and shape. From the above experiments, we can conclude that BUT-51 might be a good adsorbent for efficient and selective removal of cationic dyes.


Fig. 6 AH selective adsorption from its mixtures of MB (a) and AR (b) in acetone by BUT-51.

3.3. N2 adsorption in AH@BUT-51

As discussed above, BUT-51 is not so stable after removing guest molecules from its pores. This might be attributed to the unsaturated coordination number in Co2+ centers and the coordination modes adopted by the carboxylate groups in its structure. However, the release and separation experiments disclosed that the access of dye AH possibly endows the Co2+ saturated coordination number, thereby stabilizing the framework of BUT-51. This is indeed further confirmed by the TGA, PXRD, and gas adsorption experiments in this work. TG analysis indicates that AH@BUT-51 could be stable up to 280 °C after an initial weight loss of solvent molecules from 50 to 120 °C (Fig. S2†), and its intact framework was further confirmed by PXRD (Fig. 2b). Compared with the original BUT-51, the relatively high thermal stability of AH@BUT-51 may be due to the coordination between the terminal group “–NH2” in AH and the unsaturated Co2+ ions in its framework. According to the size of pores in this MOF, AH could only steadily accommodate in the pores A after adsorption through the cation exchange, whereas probably leave the pores B empty. Inspired by this speculation, we checked the gas adsorption of the fully loaded sample AH@BUT-51. Upon the activation at 50 °C for 12 h, AH@BUT-51 shows a type I isotherm for N2 adsorption/desorption, revealing the retention of its microporous nature. The N2 saturated uptake is calculated to be 160.4 cm3 g−1 at 77 K and 1 bar, and the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of AH@BUT-51 is 340 m2 g−1 (Fig. 7). Clearly, after N2 adsorption, AH@BUT-51 keeps its intact framework structure, which was further confirmed by the PXRD (Fig. 2b).
Fig. 7 N2 sorption isotherms of BUT-51 and AH@BUT-51 at 77 K.

The use of ionic MOFs in selective absorption and separation of dyes via the ion exchange has become a promising research topic. Recently, Bu and co-workers utilized a positive framework MOF, {[In3O(pba)3(bpdc)1.5](NO3)}n (pba = 4-(pyridine-4-yl)benzoate, bpdc = 4,4′-biphenyldicarboxylate) (ITC-4), to selectively adsorb OG (Orange G) from its mixture of MB (Methylene blue) and MTB (Methyl blue), respectively. This work demonstrated that ionic MOFs and their ion exchange properties may serve as the basis for MOF applications in ion chromatography.32 Another positive framework MOF, {[(Cu4Cl)(CPT)4·(H2O)4]·3NO3·5NMP·3.5H2O}n (HCPT = 4-(4-carboxyphenyl)-1,2,4-triazole, NMP = N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone), showed a selective uptake of MO from its mixture of MO.34 While two anionic MOFs, [(CH3)2NH2]6[M(H2O)6]3{M6(η6-TATAT)4(H2O)12}·xH2O (M = Co2+ or Ni2+; H6TATAT = 5,5′,5′′-(1,3,5-triazine-2,4,6-triyl)-tris(methylene)tris(azanediyl) triisophthalic acid), exhibited a selective adsorption of cationic dyes from their differently charged dye mixtures.35 It should be pointed out that in all the above examples, the achieved dye selective adsorption undergoes a size- or charge-selective ion exchange process. In this work, besides some observed selective adsorption of cationic dyes based on the size or charge exclusion effect, more importantly, we also realized the adsorptive separation of dyes with a similar size and the same charge in this MOF via a coordination-induced effect for the first time.

4. Conclusions

A 3D nanotubular MOF was synthesized and used in dye selective adsorption. It was found that this MOF could rapidly and selectively adsorb cationic dyes (MB, AR and AH) via a cation exchange process, following not only a charge exclusion effect but also a size and shape selective effect. Moreover, this MOF can efficiently separate cationic dyes with similar sizes by means of the comparative strength of host–guest coordination interactions. In addition, upon the dye adsorption, the original framework of BUT-51 can be reinforced to give a stable microporous partner, AH@BUT-51, with permanent porosity. These results open up the exploration of the new aspects towards unique properties and potential applications of MOFs.
