Inorganic Chemistry Communications

Volume 40, February 2014, Pages 51-54

A 3D Keggin-based framework containing AgI-ribbons and cycles by using rigid tetrazole-based ligand

Author links open overlay panelAixiangTianaXiaolingLinaNaSunbJunYingaJuwenZhangaNaLiaXiuliWanga
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.inoche.2013.11.032Get rights and content

Highlights

•

A novel POM-linked compound 1 has been synthesized with a rigid 5-phenyl-1H-tetrazole.

•

Compound 1 possesses a 3D Keggin-based framework containing AgI-ribbon and cycles.

•

The electrochemical properties of compound 1 have been investigated.

Abstract

A new Keggin-type polyoxometalate (POM)-based compound, [Ag9(ptz)5][PMoVI11MoVO40]·H2O (1) (ptzH = 5-phenyl-1H-tetrazole), was synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. Compound 1 exhibits a 2D layer containing AgI ribbons and hexa-nuclear AgI circles, which are further linked by AgI atoms and [PMoVI11MoVO40]4 − anions to construct a 3D framework. The electrochemical properties of compound 1 have been investigated.

The assembly of polyoxometalate (POM)-based organic–inorganic hybrid materials has attracted extensive attention in recent years, not only from intriguing architectures, but also from their actual and potential applications in the field of magnetism, gas storage, photochemical activity, catalytic activity and electrical chemistry [1]. At present, the introduction of different POMs into coordination polymers has become an appealing branch aiming to construct inorganic–organic frameworks with versatile topologies [2]. In this series, POMs usually play two roles: inorganic building blocks to link coordination polymers and inorganic template to induce the formation of 3D inorganic–organic frameworks [3]. Among numerous types of POMs, the Keggin POMs seem to construct 3D inorganic–organic frameworks with ease and many Keggin-based examples with abundant topologies have been reported [4]. Thus, using Keggin POMs as inorganic building blocks to build novel structures is a rational strategy.

Multinuclear clusters are a kind of important building subunits in the crystal engineering of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), because their multiple coordination sites are capable of forming high-connected and high dimensional MOFs [5]. Among the transition metals, silver is a good candidate for assembly of multinuclear clusters due to its flexible coordination modes and high affinity to O- and N-containing ligands [6]. It can provide various coordination modes, such as ‘seesaw’, ‘linear’, ‘square-pyramidal’, ‘T-type’, ‘trigonal–bipyramidal’ and ‘octahedral’ geometries [7]. So d10 AgI ion is often used as a significant metallic synthon in building multinuclear clusters, especially multinuclear AgI cycles and ribbons. Up to now, the compounds with combination of multinuclear AgI clusters and POMs are relatively scarce. Thus, under hydrothermal conditions the construction of POM-based compounds containing multinuclear silver clusters seems more challenging. Among these limited POM-based multinuclear AgI clusters, we found that most of these compounds utilize flexible ligands, such as 1,2-bis(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene [8], 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane [9], and N,N′-bis-pyridin-2-ylmethylene-hydrazine [10]. At present, our group has used 3/5-(pyridyl)tetrazolate as organic ligands and obtained some POM-based multinuclear AgI cycles [11]. In order to continue this attempt, in this work, we choose 5-phenyl-1H-tetrazole (ptzH) (Scheme S1) as organic moiety, owning a tetrazole group and a phenyl group. The tetrazole group has four successive N donors conducing to form multinuclear AgI clusters. Though the phenyl group owns no N donors, it has steric hindrance. This character can conduce to converge Ag ions and induce novel multinuclear AgI clusters, which has been confirmed by our previous work [12]. In this work, by using rigid ptz ligands and Keggin anions, we synthesized a new 3D POM-based compound, [Ag9(ptz)5][PMoVI11MoVO40]·H2O (1) (ptzH = 5-phenyl-1H-tetrazole), containing silver ribbons and six-nuclear cycles as expected.

Compound 1 was prepared under hydrothermal conditions from a mixture of H3PMo12O40·12H2O, AgNO3, ptzH and H2O at 170 °C for 3 days [13]. Single crystal X-ray structural analysis [14] (Table 1) reveals that compound 1 is constructed from one [PMoVI11MoVO40]4 − (abbreviated to PMo12) anion, nine AgI ions, five ptz ligands and one crystallization water molecule, as shown in Fig. 1. The valence sum calculations [15] show that one of twelve Mo atoms is in the + V oxidation state and Ag atoms are in the + I oxidation states. Furthermore, each ptzH ligand precursor has lost a proton to act as a ptz− anion (simplified as ptz).

In compound 1, there are nine crystallographically independent AgI ions, which exhibit four coordination modes: (i) the Ag6 and Ag7 ions are all three-coordinated in a slightly distorted T-type geometry. The Ag6 and Ag7 ions are coordinated by three N atoms (N4, N5 and N7 for Ag6, N6, N13 and N20 for Ag7) from three ptz ligands. (ii) The Ag1, Ag3, Ag4 and Ag9 ions all adopt four-coordination modes. The Ag1 adopts a “distorted seesaw” geometry coordinated by one N12 atom from one ptz ligand, one terminal O22 atom from one PMo12 anion and two O atoms from two water molecules. The Ag3 ion is coordinated by three N atoms (N3, N11 and N19) from three ptz and one Ag2 atom, while the Ag4 is coordinated by two N atoms (N9 and N16) from two ptz ligands and two C atoms (C20 and C21) from one ptz ligand. The Ag9 is in a distorted tetrahedron geometry by one N atom from one ptz ligand, one bridging O36 atom from one PMo12 anion and two O atoms from two water molecules. (iii) The Ag5 and Ag8 ions all adopt five-coordination modes in a distorted bipyramidal geometry. The Ag5 is coordinated by one C3 atom from one ptz ligand, one terminal O17 atom from one PMo12 anion and three N atoms (N2, N8 and N18) from three ptz ligands, while the Ag8 is coordinated by one terminal O38, one bridge O5 atom from the one PMo12 anion and three N atoms (N14, N15 and N17) from three ptz ligands. (iv) The Ag2 is six-coordinated by two N atoms (N1 and N3) from one ptz, one terminal O40 atom from one PMo12 anion, two C atoms (C41 and C39) from one ptz and one Ag3 atom (Fig. S1). The distances and angles around the Ag ions are 2.199(11)–2.444(12) Å for Ag–N, 2.43(2)–2.694(17) Å for Ag–C, 1.93(4)–2.468(10) Å for Ag–O, 96.6(4)–141.9(4)° for N–Ag–N and 82.3(5)–162.3(9)° for N–Ag–O, which are comparable with POM–Ag compounds, especially containing AgC bonds [16]. In compound 1, the PMo12 anion offers three terminal O atoms (O22, O36 and O40) and one bridging O5 atom to link four AgI ions. The coordination of the anion occurs in the same side of the anion sphere. The ptz ligand meets our expectation to use its all four N donors to connect Ag ions. Furthermore, the phenyl also contributes to link AgI ions through scarce AgC bonds. The bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S1.

In compound 1, one interesting structural feature rests on the multi-nuclear Ag-ribbon, which contains two kinds of tetra-nuclear AgI clusters. Two Ag5 and two Ag6 atoms are connected by four ptz ligands to form one tetra-nuclear AgI cluster, while two Ag7 and two Ag8 atoms are also linked by four ptz ligands to construct the other {Ag4} cluster. These two kinds of tetra-nuclear AgI clusters connect each other alternately and a 1D multi-nuclear AgI ribbon is constructed, as shown in Fig. 2. The tetrazole group of ptz devotes itself to convergence of AgI ions. The phenyl groups are at two sizes/sites of the AgI ribbon. The adjacent AgI ribbons are further fused by Ag3-ptz-Ag4 subunits, as shown in Fig. 3, and a 2D Ag-ptz layer is formed (Fig. S2). The Ag3 ions link the AgI ribbon through Ag3N3 and Ag3N9 bonds, while the Ag4 through Ag4N16, Ag4C20 and Ag4C21 bonds. Furthermore, the N9-containing ptz acts as bridges to link the Ag3 and Ag4 ions (Fig. S3). Thus, in compound 1 there exists a 2D layer. The Ag5 ion links N9-containing ptz through the Ag5C3 bond, which further strengthens the stability of this 2D layer.

In this 2D layer, there exists another structural feature of compound 1. The connection of N9-containing ptz for adjacent Ag ribbons induces the formation of hexa-nuclear circinate-like cycles (Fig. S4). The phenyl groups in this cycle are in an anticlockwise style. Moreover, there are plenty of π…π interactions between phenyl groups. In the POM-based multi-nuclear AgI clusters, the structure of 1 including both AgI ribbons and AgI cycles is scarce. The 2D layers are further linked by Ag2 ions to form a 3D channel-like Ag-ptz framework (Fig. S5). The PMo12 anion acting as a tetra-dentate inorganic ligand offers three terminal O atoms and one bridging O atom to embed the channel of the 3D Ag-ptz framework, as shown in Fig. 4.

The simulated and experimental PXRD patterns for compound 1 are presented in Fig. S6. The diffraction peaks of both simulated and experimental patterns match well, thus indicating the good phase purity of compound 1.

The IR spectrum of compound 1 is shown in Fig. S7. In the spectrum, characteristic bands at 1098, 962, 884 and 782 cm− 1 are attributed to ν(P–Oa), ν(Mo–Od), ν(Mo–Ob–Mo) and ν(Mo–Oc–Mo) [17], respectively. Bands in the region of 1377–1627 cm− 1 are characteristic peaks of the ptz ligand [12].

The TGA experiment was performed under N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C·min− 1 in the temperature range of 25–800 °C, as shown in Fig. S8. The TG curves of compound 1 show three weight loss steps: the first weight loss step below 300 °C corresponds to the loss of water molecules 0.55% (calc. 0.50%). The second and third weight loss steps can be ascribed to the loss of organic molecules 4.10% (calc. 4.01%) in the range of 300–800 °C.

The electrochemical behavior of compound 1 bulk-modified carbon paste electrode (1-CPE) is studied. The cyclic voltammograms for 1-CPE in 0.1 M H2SO4 + 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution at different scan rates are presented in Fig. 5 in the potential range from + 794 to − 140 mV. There exist three reversible redox peaks I–I′, II–II′, and III–III′ with the mean peak potentials E1/2 = (Epa + Epc)/2 at + 326(I–I′), + 168(II–II′), and − 66(III–III′) mV (scan rate: 100 mV·s− 1). The redox peaks I–I′, II–II′, and III–III′ correspond to three consecutive two-electron processes of the Mo centers [18]. The peak potentials change gradually following the scan rates from 40 to 500 mV·s− 1: the cathodic peak potentials shift to the negative direction and the corresponding anodic peak potentials shift to the positive direction with increasing scan rates. It can be seen from Fig. S9 that the peak currents are proportional to the scan rates, indicating that the redox process of 1-CPE is surface-controlled.

The electrocatalytic reduction of nitrite in 0.1 M H2SO4 + 0.5 M Na2SO4 aqueous solution was investigated at the 1-CPE, as shown in Fig. 6. It can be clearly seen that with addition of nitrite, all three reduction peak currents increase while the corresponding oxidation peak currents decrease, suggesting that the reduction of nitrite mediated by the reduced species of PMo12 polyanions in compound 1. These three reduced species all show good electrocatalytic activity toward the reduction of nitrite.

In summary, an interesting 3D POM-based compound has been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions through using rigid ptz ligand. In compound 1, two kinds of tetra-nuclear AgI clusters connect each other to form a multi-nuclear AgI ribbon, which are further fused by bridging N9-containing ptz ligands to construct a 2D layer. This linkage style of this 2D layer induces the formation of hexa-nuclear circinate-like cycles. These 2D layers are further fused by AgI ions and the PMo12 anions to build a 3D framework of 1. As expected, the ptz ligand has more merits for construction of POM-based compounds with novel metal ribbons and metal cycles. Other study on the construction of another metal aggregation by the ptz ligand is underway.
