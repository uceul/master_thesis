Abstract

A metal‐organic coordination polymer formulated as (H3O)2[Zn(BTEC)] (1), where BTEC = 1,2,4,5‐benzenetetracarboxylate, was synthesized by solvothermal reaction and characterized by elemental analyses, thermal stability analysis, fluorescence and IR spectroscopy, as well as single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction analysis. Compound 1 is a three‐dimensional network containing one‐dimensional rhombic channels, the sizes of the windows are approximately 8 Å at the widest and 4.5 Å at the narrowest spacing (excluding the van der Waals radius of atoms in the framework) running along the c axis. The study of the physical properties of 1 demonstrates that it exhibits intense blue fluorescence emission in solid state at room temperature.

Introduction

The rational engineering and preparation of metal‐organic coordination polymers with well‐regulated network structures has received considerable attention, mostly motivated by their intriguing structures and potential applications in the fields of catalysis, gas absorption, nonlinear optics, ion‐exchange, luminescence, and so on 1. In recent years, solvothermal techniques were found to be an effective route to obtain various metal‐organic coordination polymers 2. Solvothermal reactions provide a close system and higher pressure, which may be useful for crystallization. A brief look in the corresponding literature showed that aromatic carboxylic acids as one class of multi‐dentate ligands have been widely employed to construct novel coordination polymers because of their versatile coordination modes. Among these, 1,2,4,5‐benzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4BTEC) has attracted increasing interests. It has four carboxylic groups attached the aromatic ring. Some of its carboxylic groups may not lie in the phenyl ring plane upon complexation to metal ions because of structural constraints; thus, it could connect metal ions in different directions and can be considered as a good candidate for the construction of multidimensional metal‐organic coordination polymers 3. In addition, fluorescence properties have been widely used in various fields, such as medicine, molecular biology as well as daily life [4, 5]. We focus our attention on d10 metal complexes, which attracted our interest because not only of their unique structures but also of their fluorescent properties 6.

Keeping in mind the aforementioned points, our research attempts to design and synthesize multidimensional metal‐organic coordination polymers constructed from zinc and aromatic carboxylic acids with excellent photoactivity. Fortunately, we were able to isolate compound 1 under solvothermal conditions. It has a 3D architecture with ordered one‐dimensional rhombic channels along the c axis, constructed by [Zn4(BTEC)4]12– units. Herein, the synthesis, crystal structure, and photoluminescence properties of the compound are reported.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and Structure

Compared to traditional solution techniques such as evaporation, diffusion, and cooling, solvothermal reactions are relatively complex processes, and the final products under a given set of conditions are often unpredictable. Nevertheless, they were found to be an effective route to obtain various compounds 8. Compound 1 was successfully synthesized by choosing DMF as solvent at 110 °C.

After a lot of parallel experiments, we found that the highest yield could be 78 % with a reaction time of 2 days at a temperature of 110 °C. The final pH value is about 5.5. We also attempted to synthesize compound 1 from Zn(NO3)2. However, we did not obtain crystals but some precipitate, which demonstrates that the source of metal ion is also important for the successful preparation of compound 1.

A single‐crystal structural analysis shows that compound 1 is a unique three‐dimensional network, which accommodates guest water molecules. The formula is (H3O)2[Zn(BTEC)], which was determined by combining single‐crystal structure, elemental microanalysis, and thermogravimetric analyses. Considering the weak acidic synthetic condition, we presume that the two guest molecules are protonated to compensate charge balance. The coordinative environment around the metal center for 1 is presented in Figure 1a. The zinc(II) atom is connected to four oxygen atoms from four different BTEC ligands in a tetracoordinate environment and forming a slightly distorted tetrahedron. Zn–O bond lengths are 1.972(4) Å and 2.012(4) Å, respectively. Each carboxyl group adopts a monodentate mode coordinating to one zinc atom, therefore, the BTEC4– acts as a μ4‐bridging ligand, linking to four zinc(II) atoms. Then, the [Zn4(BTEC)4]12– unit is linked together through Zn–O bonds to generate a 3D structure with rhombic channels (Figure 1b). The dimensions of this channel are approximately 8 Å × 4.5 Å (excluding the van der Waals radius of atoms in the framework) running along the c axis. The effective free volume of 1 is calculated by PLATON analysis as 17.9 % of the crystal volume (314.9 Å3 out of the 1754.4 Å3 unit cell volumes).

IR Spectroscopy and Thermogravimetric Analysis

The infrared spectrum of 1 (Figure S2) shows the characteristic bands of the oxygen donor ligand and fully supports the presence of organic components. The absorptions for the antisymmetric stretching vibrations vas(–CO2–) of the carboxyl appears at 1609 cm–1 and symmetric stretching vibrationsvs(–CO2–) is observed at 1372 cm–1. The carboxyl frequencies stretches seem to be shifted to lower values compared with free H4BTEC, indicating changes in the vibrational status of the ligand upon coordination to the metal ion 9. No absorption peaks are observed between 1730 and 1690 cm–1, indicated that all the carboxyl groups are deprotonated 10. This is in agreement with the single‐crystal structure.

The thermal stability (under a continuous nitrogen flow) of compound 1 was investigated between ambient temperature and ca. 600 °C with the registered thermograms (Figure S3). The TGA curve exhibits two continuous weight loss stages below 481 °C, leading to a weight loss of 77.11 % (calcd. 76.97 %), which corresponds to the release of two water molecules and BTEC ligand (the decomposition of the sample). The final residue is zinc oxide.

Fluorescence Property

The solid‐state photoluminescent spectrum of compound 1 at room temperature is shown in Figure 2. It can be observed that the emission maxima at 436 nm upon excitation at 332 nm exhibits intense blue photoluminescence, whereas the main emission peak of free H4btec ligand is at 339 nm upon excitation at 309 nm, which is already known. According to previous reports, the blue fluorescence may be assigned as the emission of ligand‐to‐metal charge transfer (LMCT) 11. Similar emission bands were also observed in other metal–H4BTEC polymers. It is noteworthy that the blue photoluminescence from the colorless crystals under UV irradiation (365 nm) is so intensive that it can be observed macroscopically (Figure S4).

Conclusions

We successfully obtained a metal‐organic coordination polymer based on zinc under solvothermal conditions. The 3D architecture of compound 1 displays one‐dimensional rhombic channels and exhibits strongly blue fluorescent emission at room temperature, which makes it a potential candidate for photoactive material. In addition, the size of pore diameter is similar to the kinetics diameter of some small gas molecules. It thus may be used as an adsorbent or separation material. A relating study is currently under way.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods

All the reagents and solvent were purchased commercial sources and used as received without further purification. Elemental analyses (C, H, and N) were performed with a Perkin–Elmer 2400 CHN elemental analyzer. IR spectra were recorded in the range 400–4000 cm–1 with an Alpha Centaurt FT/IR spectrophotometer using KBr pellets. Powder X‐ray diffraction measurements were performed with a Rigaku D/MAX‐3 instrument with Cu‐Kα radiation in the angular range 2θ = 3–60° at 293 K. Thermal gravimetric (TG) analyses were performed with a Perkin–Elmer TGA7 instrument in flowing N2 with a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1. Excitation and emission spectrum of 1 was obtained with a SPEX FL‐2T2 spectrofluorometer equipped with a 450‐W xenon lamp as excitation source.

Preparation of (H3O)2[Zn(BTEC)] (1)

A mixture of ZnCl2 (0.0680g, 0.5 × 10–3mol), H4BTEC (0.1270 g, 0.5 × 10–3 mol) in DMF (10 ml) was sealed in a 23 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and heated at 110 °C for 2 days. Then, slow cooling to room temperature. After being washed with DMF several times, 1 was collected as colorless shuttle‐shaped crystals with a yield of about 78 % (based on Zn). Anal. calcd. for 1: C 33.96; H 2.28 %; found: C 33.87; H 2.39 %.

X‐ray Crystallography

Diffraction intensities for compound 1 were collected with a Rigaku R‐AXIS RAPID IP diffractometer with Mo‐Kα monochromatic radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 283 K. The linear absorption coefficients, scattering factors for the atoms, and the anomalous dispersion corrections were taken from the International Tables for X‐ray Crystallography. Empirical absorption corrections were applied. The structures were solved by the direct method and refined by the full‐matrix least‐squares method on F2 using the SHELXTL‐97 crystallographic software package 7. All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically.

The voids within the framework of 1 are occupied by protonated disordered water molecules. The number of disordered water molecules was determined according to elemental analyses and TG analysis owing to the complexities of the structure and the highest disordered.
