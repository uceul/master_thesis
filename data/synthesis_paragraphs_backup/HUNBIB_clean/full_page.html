1. Introduction

The use of hydrothermal techniques to the synthesis of novel inorganic materials with open architectures has grown steadily during the last decade or so [1]. This synthetic approach generally employs the use of an organic amine, which is accommodated within the cavities and channels of these open structures and, in many cases, can be removed by post-synthesis treatments such as calcination or chemical extraction (leaching). It is now clear that judicious choice of reaction conditions (such as gel composition, structure-directing agent, and temperature) is necessary for synthesizing new compounds with novel architectures.

Of the many framework solids that have been prepared and characterized, metal phosphates form an important class of materials. These materials are based on phosphate networks resulting in one of the constituents of the framework being a tetrahedral atom. Thus, a large number of phosphates of manganese [2], iron [3], [4], [5], cobalt [6], [7], [8], [9] and nickel [10] have been prepared and characterized. Recently, the continuous research by many workers has shown that it is also possible to prepare novel materials using arsenates as building units [11], [12], [13]. Framework materials containing dicarboxylate and metal ions have been made and characterized in recent years [14], [15], [16], [17], [18]. It is also established that oxalates readily substitute for phosphates in some of the open-framework structures forming organicâ€“inorganic hybrid structures. Thus, phosphate-oxalate solids with open architectures have been reported for V [19], Mn [20], Fe [21], [22], Co [23], Zn [24], Al [25], Ga [26], In [27] and Sn [28]. In these metal phosphate-oxalates, the metal phosphate layers are generally pillared by oxalate units [20], [22], and in some cases the oxalate units perform a dual role of connecting one-dimensional metal phosphate ladders as well as pillaring such composite layers [21], [23]. In the light of the above, it occurred to us that it might be possible to prepare other hybrid architectures involving arsenates as building units in place of phosphates. Our efforts in this direction were indeed successful and we have isolated the first iron arsenate-oxalate [C4N2H12]2[Fe4(HAsO4)6 (C2O4)2], I, possessing iron arsenate layers pillared by oxalate units. The framework architecture is similar to the iron phosphate oxalate [C10N4H28][Fe2(HPO4)3 (C2O4)]2, synthesized hydrothermally in the presence of 1,3-bis(3-aminopropyl)piperazine. In this paper the synthesis and structural characterization of I is presented.

2. Experimental

The iron arsenate oxalate I was synthesized hydrothermally starting from a mixture containing piperazine. In a typical synthesis, 0.099 g of iron oxalate was dissolved in 2 ml of water followed by the addition of 0.3826 g of As2O5 under continuous stirring. Finally 0.0478 g of piperazine was added and the mixture was homogenized for 30 min at room temperature. The final mixture with the composition, Fe(C2O4).2H2O:3As2O5:C4N2H10 :200H2O, was sealed in a 23-ml PTFE-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 150 Â°C for 48 h under autogeneous pressure. The initial pH of the mixture was about 2.0 and after the reaction the pH did not show any appreciable variation. The resulting product, a mixture of powder and pale greenish single crystals, was filtered under vacuum and dried at ambient temperature. The single crystals were separated easily from the mixture physically under a polarizing microscope. The same reaction mixture at 125 Â°C for 96 h yielded a single phasic pale greenish powder, which was identified as the title compound by powder X-ray diffraction (XRD), by comparison with the simulated XRD pattern from single crystal data. This sample was used for all other characterizations such as thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and magnetic measurements. EDAX analysis gave an Fe:As ratio of 1:1.5 which matches with the composition obtained from the single-crystal X-ray study.

3. Single crystal structure determination

A suitable single crystal of compound I (0.12Ã—0.16Ã—0.20 mm3) was carefully selected under a polarizing microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber. Crystal structure determination by X-ray diffraction was performed on a Siemens Smart-CCD diffractometer equipped with a normal focus, 2.4 kW sealed tube X-ray source (Mo KÎ± radiation, Î»=0.71073 Ã…) operating at 40 kV and 40 mA. Pertinent details for the structure determinations are presented in Table 1. An empirical absorption correction based on symmetry equivalent reflections was applied using the Sadabs program [29]. Other effects, such as absorption by glass fiber etc. were simultaneously corrected. The structure was solved and refined using Shelxtl-plus suite of program [30]. The solution readily established all the heavy atom positions (As and Fe) and facilitated the identification of most of the other fragments (O, C, N and H) from the difference Fourier maps. All the hydrogens atoms were initially located in the difference Fourier maps and for the final refinement the hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically and held in the riding mode. The last cycles of refinement included atomic positions for all the atoms and anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms and isotropic thermal parameters for all the hydrogen atoms. Full-matrix least-squares refinement against |F2| was carried out using the Shelxtl-plus [30] suite of programs. Details of the final refinements are given in Table 1. The final atomic coordinates, selected bond distances and angles for I are listed in Table 2, Table 3, Table 4.

4. Results and discussion

The asymmetric unit of I contains 58 non-hydrogen atoms as shown in Fig. 1, of which 46 atoms belong to the â€˜frameworkâ€™ and 12 atoms to the â€˜guestâ€™ species. The structure consists of neutral layers of formula [Fe4(HAsO4)6], which are cross-linked by two oxalate units forming the anionic framework. Charge neutrality is achieved by the incorporation of the doubly protonated piperazine molecule. There are two [C4N2H12]2+ molecules present per framework formula. The framework structure is built up from a network of FeO6, AsO4 and C2O4 moieties with each Fe atom bound to six oxygens, which are, in turn, bound to carbon and arsenic atoms forming the network.

There are six crystallographically independent arsenic and four iron atoms in the asymmetric unit. The iron atoms are octahedrally coordinated by their oxygen atom neighbors with Feî—¸O bond distances in the range 1.917(6)â€“2.168(5) Ã… (av. 2.014 Ã…). It may be noted that of the six oxygens bound to iron, four are associated with shorter distances (av. Feî—¸O=1.9665 Ã…), whilst the other two have longer distances (av.=2.1075 Ã…). These are, probably, oxygens linked to arsenic and carbon atoms, respectively. Similar short and long bond distances have been encountered before in phosphate-oxalates of iron [21], [22]. The Oî—¸Feî—¸O bond angels are in the range 77.9(2)â€“173.0(2)Â° (av. Oî—¸Feî—¸O=105.6Â°). The four iron atoms make four sets of Feî—¸Oî—¸As bonds to the six distinct As atom neighbors with a fairly wide spread of angles with an average Feî—¸Oî—¸As bond angle of 135.5Â°. The arsenic atoms make only three Asî—¸Oî—¸Fe linkages and possess one terminal linkage. The Asî—¸O bond distances are in the range 1.647(6)â€“1.743(6) Ã… (av. Asî—¸O=1.684 Ã…) and the Oî—¸Asî—¸O bond angles are in the range 102.3(3)â€“118.1(3)Â° (av. Oî—¸Asî—¸O=109.4Â°). The carbon atoms are connected to the iron atoms through regular Cî—¸O linkages with Cî—¸O distance in the range of 1.244(10)â€“1.283(9) Ã… (av. Cî—¸O=1.2596 Ã…) and an average Oî—¸Cî—¸O bond angle of 126.3Â°. Assuming the usual valences of Fe, As, C and O to be +3, +5, +4 and âˆ’2, respectively, the framework stoichiometry of [Fe4(AsO4)6(C2O4)2] creates a net framework charge of âˆ’10. The presence of two molecules of [C4N2H12] would account for +4 arising from the complete protonation of the amine. The excess negative charge of âˆ’6 is then needed to be balanced. Bond valence sum calculations [31] indicate that one of the oxygen atoms attached to each of the arsenic [O(4), O(8), O(12), O(16), O(20), O(24)] has a hydrogen atom attached to it making it a terminal î—¸OH group, which also corresponds well with the proton positions located in the difference Fourier maps. In addition to this, two other terminal oxygens (O(5) and O(13)) attached to As(2) and As(4) are found to be terminal double-bonded oxygen atoms. The various structural parameters observed in I are as expected and are listed in Table 3, Table 4.

The framework structure of I is made from a network of strictly alternating FeO6 and AsO4 polyhedral units forming neutral iron arsenate layers that are bridged by oxalate units. The inorganic layers, along the bc plane, can be considered to be formed by the linkages between two edge-shared ladders as shown in Fig. 2. Similar inorganic layers are commonly observed in many open-framework metal phosphates [1]. In the present case, however, the iron arsenate layers are further cross-linked by oxalate units that act as pillars between the layers forming the three-dimensional architecture (Fig. 3). It may be described that the oxalate units keep the inorganic layers apart and together. The other way to describe the structure of I is to consider 4-membered edge-shared ladders possessing a pendent arsenate group that are linked through the oxalate units as shown in Fig. 4. The pendent arsenate groups link up with another such ladder completing the framework architecture. The organic amine molecule, piperazine, occupies the 8-membered channels formed by the linkages between these units and interacts with the framework via the oxygens.

Thermogravimetric analysis carried out under flowing air (flow rate 50 ml minâˆ’1) in the temperature range 25â€“700 Â°C indicate one sharp mass loss followed by a broad tail. The sharp mass loss of 34.6% occurring around 350 Â°C may correspond to the loss of the amine molecules, oxalate and terminal î—¸OH group (calc. 21.4%). The broad tail with a mass loss of about 42% in the region 350â€“650 Â°C correspond to the loss of arsenic as As2O3 from the framework (calc. 41.2%). The total mass loss of 74.5% corresponds with the complete loss of amine, oxalate, hydroxyl and arsenic from the solid (calc. 73.3%). The calcined sample showed weakly diffracting XRD pattern with the majority of the lines corresponds to the crystalline phase maghamite, Fe2O3 (JCPDS:15â€“0615). A preliminary AC susceptibility measurement at 100 Hz was carried out to investigate the magnetic behavior of I. In Fig. 5, the variation of magnetic susceptibility as a function of temperature is presented. The studies indicate strong antiferromagnetic interactions with an ordering temperature of âˆ¼40 K.

The importance of hydrogen bonding in open-framework materials has been emphasized before and the current solid also possesses important interactions involving the amine molecules and the framework. In addition, strong intra-framework hydrogen bond interactions are also observed. The selected hydrogen bond interactions that are observed in I are listed in Table 5. 5. Conclusions

In summary, the synthesis and structural characterization of the first open-framework iron arsenate-oxalate has been accomplished. The present work illustrates that it is possible to prepare novel inorganicâ€“organic hybrid solids by careful modifications of the experimental parameters under hydrothermal conditions. It is likely that the arsenate-oxalates, of the type described herein, can also form with novel structures similar to the phosphate-oxalates already known in the literature. We are currently pursuing this lead further.
