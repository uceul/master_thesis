Abstract

Two rigid 1H-imidazol-4-yl containing ligands of 1, 3-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (L1) and 1, 4-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (L2) were used to react with Ni(II) and Cd(II) salts together with 1,3,5-tri(4-carboxyphenyl)benzene (H3BTB) under hydro- and solvo-thermal conditions, and four new complexes [Ni3(L1)4(BTB)2(C2H5OH)2(H2O)6]Â·3H2O (1), [Cd3(L1)2(BTB)2(H2O)]Â·DMFÂ·H2O (2), [Ni3(L2)3(BTB)2(H2O)6]Â·4H2O (3) and [Cd3(L2)3(BTB)2]Â·H3BTBÂ·H2O (4) (DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide) were achieved and structurally characterized. The complexes show structural diversity. 1 is an infinite one-dimensional (1D) chain, 3 and 4 are two-dimensional (2D) networks, which are further linked together by hydrogen bonds to give eventual three-dimensional (3D) architectures, while 2 is a 3D framework. Fluorescent sensing and selectively adsorption properties of the complexes were investigated.

1. Introduction

Recently, considerable attention has been paid to the study of metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) not only because of their fascinating architectures but also due to their potential applications in the fields of adsorption, recognition/sensing, heterogeneous catalysis, magnetism, photochemistry and so on. Although much progress has been achieved in this field over the past years, it is still a challenge for us to fabricate the desired MOFs with expected structures and properties, because there are varied factors that can affect the structure and property of MOFs [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9]. From the previously reported studies, it has been demonstrated that organic ligands are crucial in determining the formation of definite MOFs. In our group, we focus our attention on MOFs with imidazole-containing ligands and a series of 1H-imidazol-1-yl- and 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligands such as 1,3,5-tris(1-imidazolyl)benzene, 1,3-bis(1-imidazolyl)-5-(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene, 1,4-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene, 1,3-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene, 4,4,-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)biphenyl and 1,3,5-tri(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene have been designed and prepared. The results imply that such imidazole-containing ligands are versatile and MOFs with varied framework structures and interesting properties have been achieved [10], [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. In addition, mixed organic ligand strategy is a powerful method for fabrication of novel MOFs since additional ligands can increase structural diversity [17].

Herein we focused our attention on reactions of 1,3-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (L1), 1,4-di(1H-imidazol-4-yl)benzene (L2) together with multicarboxylate ligand 1,3,5-tri(4-carboxyphenyl)benzene (H3BTB) and Ni(II) and Cd(II) salts. In this work, four new coordination complexes [Ni3(L1)4(BTB)2(C2H5OH)2(H2O)6]Â·3H2O (1), [Cd3(L1)2(BTB)2(H2O)]Â·DMFÂ·H2O (2), [Ni3(L2)3(BTB)2 (H2O)6] Â·6H2O (3) and [Cd3(L2)3(BTB)2]Â·H3BTBÂ·3H2O (4) (DMF = N, N-dimethylformamide) were successfully synthesized. The compounds were characterized by X-ray crystallography, elemental and thermal analyses. Selectively adsorption, photoluminescence and sensing properties of the complexes were investigated.

3.6. Fluorescent sensing property

Recently, inorganic-organic hybrid complexes, especially those comprising d10 metal centers and aromatic-containing system, have been intensively investigated for attractive fluorescence properties and applications, such as chemical sensors and electroluminescent materials for displays [26], [27], [28]. Accordingly, the solid-state luminescent emissions of 2 and 4 as well as free L1 and L2 ligands were investigated at ambient temperature. Intense emissions were observed at 381 nm for the free L1 and 455 nm for L2 upon excitation at 340 nm. As shown in Fig. 6, emission bands were observed at 379 nm (Î»ex = 335 nm) for 2, 386 nm (Î»ex = 330 nm) for 4. Compared with the free L1 and L2 ligands, the blue-shifts of the emissions of 2 and 4 are considered to be caused by the coordination of the ligand to the metal centers.

It has been reported that the fluorescence is a powerful technique for studying the sensing property of MOFs. Accordingly, the fluorescence behavior of 2 immersing in different solvent was investigated. The results clearly show that the emission intensities of 2 depend on the solvent molecules (Fig. 7 and Fig. S2), however, only the presence of acetone can quench the fluorescence of 2 efficiently. The observed fluorescence quenching may be caused by the interactions between the framework and the acetone molecules, since such ligand based fluorescence can be quenched by energy transfer between the organic ligands and the acetone molecules [29], [30]. In addition, acetone has triplet state in solution, which may be related with the fluorescence quenching [31]. Furthermore, after the as-synthesized sample of 2 was soaked in acetone for 24 h, the fluorescence was completely quenched as verified by the luminescence in the solid state (Fig. 6). The PXRD pattern of 2 soaked in acetone for 24 h is identical to the one of as-synthesized sample, implying that the framework of 2 remains after the acetone treatment.

The quenching efficiency of 2 dispersed in CH3CN suspension with different amount of acetone was also investigated. As exhibited in Fig. 8, a gradual decrease of the emission intensity was observed upon the increasing addition of acetone. The fluorescence decrease was nearly proportional to the acetone concentration and the fluorescence intensity at 335 nm vs the volume ratio of acetone could be fitted by a first-order exponential decay (Fig. 9), indicating that the fluorescence quenching of 2 with acetone is diffusion controlled. To examine the luminescence quenching degree, the quenching coefficient was calculated by using the Stern-Volmer equation: I0/I = 1 + KSV[M] [32], in which Ksv is the quenching constant, [M] is the molar concentration of the analyte, I0 and I are the luminescence intensities before and after addition of the analyte, respectively. As shown in Fig. S3, a good linear Stern-Volmer relationship is observed in 2 for acetone sensing, implying that it is a dynamic quenching process for detecting acetone, the Ksv value is calculated to be 3.3146. Based on the Ksv values and the standard error (Ïƒ) for three repeated fluorescent measurements of blank solutions, the detection limit is estimated to be 0.122 vol% for 2. Thus, 2 can be considered as a potential candidate for selective sensing of acetone molecules, while no acetone sensing property was observed for 1, 3 and 4.

3.7. Selective adsorption property

From the crystal structural analysis, it was found that there are voids occupied by solvent molecules in 2. The porosity of activated 2 was further studied by adsorption measurements of N2 at 77 K and CO2 at 195 K (Fig. 10). Before gas-adsorption measurements, the sample 2 was soaked in acetone for 72 h, and the degassing process were performed at 105 Â°C for 10 h, it was found that the free solvent molecules in 2 were removed (Fig. S4) without destroying the framework (Fig. S5). As show in Fig. 7, the sorption curves of N2 at 77 K for 2 suggest only surface adsorption, however complex 2 exhibits typical type-I gas uptake isotherms with the CO2 adsorption abilities of 49.27 cm3 gâˆ’1, corresponding to 7.39 CO2 molecules per formula unit at 195 K and 1 atm. On the basis of the CO2 adsorption isotherm, the BET surface area and pore volume are estimated to be 136.71 m2 gâˆ’1 and 0.09 cm3 gâˆ’1, respectively [33], [34]. The results indicate that 2 might be promising materials for selective adsorptive separation of CO2 from industrial flue gas or the removal of CO2 from natural gas.

On the other hand, the porous framework of 2 encourages us to investigate their dye adsorption property. The desolvated sample 2â€² (10 mg) was added into aqueous solution (20 ml) of dyes at 25 Â°C under neutral condition [10 mg Lâˆ’1 for Methyl Orange (MO), Methylene Blue (MB) and Rhodamine B (RhB)]. The results show that MO concentration decreases to almost zero, implying the complete removal of MO from aqueous solution during 24 h (Fig. 11a and Table S3). In the case of MB, the blue solution color changes are limited, and the MB concentration decreased from 10 to 4.23 ppm after 24 h (Fig. 11b and Tables S4). In addition, the RhB concentration varied from 10 to 9.79 ppm in 24 h (Fig. 11c and Tables S5). The selective adsorption of MO over other dyes is probably attributed to the different size of the dye molecules (Scheme S2). The results show that 2 has selectively adsorption ability for removal of MO dye from polluted water [10]. However, 1, 3 and 4 have no such dye adsorption property.

3.8. SHG activity and ferroelectric properties

Second-order non-linear optical (NLO) materials have attracted more and more attention in fields such as laser frequency conversion and optical parameter oscillators. Among them, new NLO materials made from metal-organic coordination polymers have been a major point of focus in recent years because of their advantages over pure inorganic or organic compounds. It is well-known that only a non-centrosymmetric structure may have second-order NLO effects [35]. Therefore, it is believed that 2 with Cc space group has a NLO response, and a quasi-Kurtz second-harmonic-generation (SHG) measurement was carried out to evaluate their potential application as a second-order NLO material. The preliminary experimental results revealed that 2 exhibits modest powder SHG activity with a response of 0.3 times of that for urea, which indicates that the 2 can be used as a potential optical material.

Recently, a prevalent research has focused on developing ferroelectric materials based on metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) and have reported some such materials [36]. Complex 2 crystallizes in the acentric space group Cc, which belongs to the polar point group Cs, which falls in one of the 10 polar point groups (C1, Cs, C2, C2v, C4, C4v, C3, C3v, C6, C6v), required for ferroelectric materials. Therefore, the dielectrical hysteresis loop of 2 was measured at room temperature. Experimental result indicates that 2 displays typical ferroelectric behavior. Fig. S6 clearly shows that there is an electric hysteresis loop that is a typical ferroelectric feature with a remanent polarization (Pr) of ca. 0.065 Î¼C cmâˆ’2 and coercive field (Ec) of ca. 5.75 kV cmâˆ’1. The saturation spontaneous polarization (Ps) is ca. 0.161 Î¼C cmâˆ’2.

4. Conclusion

In conclusion, in this work four new MOFs [Ni3(L1)4(BTB)2(C2H5OH)2(H2O)6]Â·3H2O (1), [Cd3(L1)2(BTB)2 (H2O)]Â·DMFÂ·H2O (2), [Ni3(L2)3(BTB)2(H2O)6]Â·4H2O (3) and [Cd3(L2)3(BTB)2]Â·H3BTBÂ·3H2O (4) were successfully synthesized by using mixed organic ligands. Complexes 1â€“4 displayed diverse structures: 1 is a 1D chain, 3 and 4 are 2D networks, while 2 is a 3D framework. The Cd(II) frameworks 2 and 4 exhibit strong emissions, furthermore 2 showed selective dye adsorption properties, SHG activity and fluorescent sensing properties.
