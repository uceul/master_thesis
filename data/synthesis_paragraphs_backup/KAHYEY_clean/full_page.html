Volume 7, Issue 2, February 2005, Pages 221-226

A novel organically templated hybrid open-framework manganese phosphate–oxalate

Author links open overlay panelRanboYuaXianranXingaTakashiSaitobMasakiAzumabMikioTakanobDanWangcYunfaChencNobuhiroKumadadNobukazuKinomurad
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.solidstatesciences.2004.10.033Get rights and content

Abstract

A novel hybrid open-framework manganese phosphate–oxalate templated with an organic amine, [CH3NH2CH3][Mn2(OH2)(HPO4)(C2O4)1.5], has been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. The light pink compound crystallizes in a triclinic system of space group P1¯ (No. 2), with lattice parameters a=8.3014(1)Å, b=9.740(2)Å, c=7.744(2)Å, α=102.32(2)°, β=93.38(2)°, γ=75.90(1)°, V=572.7(2)Å3. In this structure, MnO6 octahedra, PO4 tetrahedra and C2O4 groups are linked to give rise to a three-dimensional open framework containing channels with 10-, 8- and 10-membered windows running along the a, b and c axis, respectively. The organic template diethylenetriamine lies disorderedly in the interspace of the channels along the c axis. The compound is an antiferromagnet with a TN=9K.

1. Introduction

Open-framework materials currently form a diverse and active field of research [1], [2], [3]. The initial work in this field produced aluminosilicate zeolites and aluminophosphates with channels and cages of various dimensionalities and sizes [4]. Incorporation of transition metals into such open frameworks [5] is of great attraction for potential applications [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12]. However, although a great number of transition metal phosphates have been prepared, to build up open-framework architectures is not so easy. Recently, it has been noticed that the use of polydentate ligands like the oxalate group leads to novel open frameworks [13], [14], [15]. In the past two years, phosphate–oxalates of Sn [16], [17], Al [18], [19], Ga [20], In [21] and Fe [22], [23], [24], [25] were synthesized. Moreover, three manganese phosphate–oxalates, Mn2(NH4)4(P6O18)(C2O4)⋅6H2O [26], [Mn4(PO4)2(C2O4)(H2O)2] and [H3N(CH2)3NH3][Mn2(HPO4)2(C2O4)(H2O)2] [27] were reported.

The effect that by using organic species, which are generally organic amines, the shape and size of the channels of open-framework materials can be controlled has been recognized for a long time. Use of a new organic amine usually leads to a novel inorganic framework. In this work, by using diethylenetriamine to direct the structure formation and also by using H2O2 to adjust the oxidation states of the starting materials, a novel inorganic–organic hybrid open-framework material has been obtained. Herein, the synthesis, structure, and some properties of this new manganese phosphate–oxalate [CH3NH2CH3][Mn2(OH2)(HPO4)(C2O4)1.5] are discussed.

2. Experimental

2.1. Synthesis

The title compound was synthesized hydrothermally from a starting mixture of MnCl2, H2O2, H2C2O4, H3PO4, H2N(CH2)2NH(CH2)2NH2 and H2O in a molar ratio of 1.0:3.0–9.0:1.0:10:1.0:400. In a typical synthesis procedure, 0.792 g of MnCl2⋅4H2O was dissolved in 30 ml of deionized water, to which 1 ml of H2O2 (40% aq.) was added under vigorous stirring. After 30 min of stirring, H3PO4 (85% aq.) and H2C2O4⋅2H2O were added to the solution successively, and then diethylenetriamine was added under continuous stirring. The reaction mixture was then sealed in a PTFE-lined stainless steel autoclave and heated at 130 °C for 48 h under autogenous pressure. By filtering the product and washing with distilled water and alcohol, we obtained a large quantity of light pink crystals with a very small amount of white powder mixed.

2.2. Single crystal X-ray diffraction

Crystal structure determination using X-ray diffraction (XRD) was performed on a Rigaku AFC-7R Four-circle diffractometer with graphite monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ=0.71069Å). A suitable single crystal of approximate dimensions of 0.20×0.20×0.05mm3 was mounted on a glass fiber. Of the 9899 collected reflections, 9453 were unique (Rint=0.093), and equivalent reflections were merged. The crystal structure was solved with heavy-atom Patterson methods [28] and expanded using Fourier techniques [29]. The nonhydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The final cycle of full-matrix least-squares refinement based on 7054 observed reflections (I>3.00σ(I)) and 182 variable parameters converged to unweighted and weighted agreement factors of R=0.055, Rw=0.080. All calculations were performed using the teXsan [30] crystallographic software package. Further details of the relevant crystal data collection and analysis are summarized in Table 1. The coordinates and temperature factors are given in Table 2, and selected bond lengths and angles are shown in Table 3, Table 4, respectively.

2.3. Powder X-ray diffraction

Powder XRD patterns were taken on a Mac Science MXP3 X-ray diffractometer, using Ni-filtered Cu-Kα radiation.

2.4. Infrared absorption spectroscopy

Infrared absorption spectra (IR) were recorded on a JASCO FT/IR-410 spectrometer using transparent KBr pellets: 1–2 mg of the sample was crushed and mixed with 300 mg of KBr.

2.5. Thermal analysis

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out using a Rigaku Thermoflex TAS 200 thermal analysis system at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 over a temperature range of 20–1000 °C.

2.6. Magnetic susceptibility measurement

Magnetic susceptibility was measured using a Quantum Design MPMS-XL SQUID magnetometer for a temperature range of 5–300 K at 1000 Oe.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Synthesis

The synthetic conditions and the crystallization products are summarized in Table 5. The experimental results indicated that for a given molar ratio of the starting materials, the product varied depending upon the kind of organic amine used, reaction temperature, and time. Generally, when simple organic amines such as ethylenediamine and 1,3-diaminopropan were used, a compound with the formula of Mn2(OH2)(PO4)(C2O4)0.5 was obtained. The title compound could be synthesized by using diethylenetriamine as the structure directing agent at 130 °C for 48 h. The use of H2O2 proved to be important. Otherwise, only an amorphous phase could be obtained. Although the single phase product could not be obtained, the crystals of the title compound could be easily separated through a sonication procedure, which were then served for further characterizations. Under higher reaction temperatures and/or longer reaction times needle-like crystals of Mn5(PO4)4(H2O)4 [31] were easily formed. Use of other organic amines always led to new crystalline phases, the structures of which will be analyzed when suitable single crystals are obtained.

3.2. Crystal structure description

The single crystal X-ray diffraction study of the title compound showed that the framework structure is made up of MnO6 octahedra, PO4 tetrahedra and oxalate groups. Through edge sharing, the Mn(1)O6 octahedra and the Mn(2)O6 octahedra form [Mn2O10] dimers. The phosphate groups are connected with these dimers at three vertices, with the fourth one being an OH group, to form one-dimensional chains along the a axis as shown in Fig. 1. These chains are further linked via oxalate groups to give rise to an open-framework structure with one-dimensional channels running along the a, b and c axes. Those along the c axis have a 10-membered window (six MnO6, two PO4 and two C2O4) as shown in Fig. 1. It has a pore opening of 6.74Å×8.02Å. The channel systems along the a axis and the b axis have a 10-membered window and an 8-membered window, respectively (Fig. 2). The structure analysis indicated that the organic diethylenetriamine template lay in the channel system along the c axis disorderedly (Fig. 1). The diethylenetriamine species are protonated to balance the framework's negative charge. The use of H2O2 as well as the uniqueness of the hybrid framework might be the reason for the organic template disorder.

The Mn(1)O bond lengths lie in the range from 2.075 to 2.254 Å (average, 2.187 Å), and the Mn(2)O bond lengths lie in the range from 2.030 to 2.308 Å (average, 2.189 Å). The MnO bond distances for the MnOP linkages are relatively short, while the longest bond distance Mn(1)O(8) = 2.254 Å is for the MnOH2 linkage. The OMnO bond angle becomes relatively small, as for [O(1)Mn(1)O(3)], [O(1)Mn(2)O(3)], [O(4)Mn(1)O(5)] and [O(7)Mn(2)O(8)], when the oxygen atoms are coordinated with carbon atoms of the oxalate group. The average PO bond distance and OPO angle are 1.536 Å and 109.44°, respectively, which are comparable to those found in the tin- and iron-phosphate-oxalates reported in literatures [23]. The longest PO(11) distance of 1.586(1) Å is considered to be for the POH linkage.

3.3. Elemental analysis and thermal analysis

Elemental analysis indicated that the C, H and N contents are 14.93, 2.86, and 3.58 wt%, respectively. This corresponds to a molar ratio of C:H:N = 4.86:11.18:1.00, which is in good agreement with the calculated ratio of C:H:N = 5:11:1 for the formula of the title compound.

Termogravimetric analysis of this compound was carried out in air from room temperature to 1000 °C. The observed total weight loss of 48.5% agreed well with the calculated value of 48.6% for the loss of H2O, the organic amine, and the C2O4 groups. The powder X-ray diffraction of the sample decomposed at 1000 °C suggested a very poor crystallization.

3.4. Infrared spectroscopy

The IR band at 1648 cm−1 could be assigned to the CO stretching vibrations of the C2O4 groups [32]. The presence of bands at 1252 and 1123 cm−1 are characteristic of the CO [32] stretching vibration for the oxalic acid. Following the framework vibration model for microporous aluminophosphates [33], [34], the absorption bands at 1077, 1009, 906 and 811 cm−1 are assigned to the stretching vibrations of the PO4 units, and the bands at 572 and 468 cm−1 to the bending vibrations of the PO4 groups. Additionally, the absorption bands for the organic template are also observed. The bands at 2928 and 2867 cm−1 are attributed to the CH stretching vibration, and the 1466 and 1382 cm−1 bands to the CH scissoring and bending vibrations [32]. The NH and OH vibrations are observed in the broad band at 3500 cm−1.

3.5. Magnetic susceptibility

The temperature dependences of the magnetic susceptibility (χ) and the reciprocal magnetic susceptibility (χ−1) are shown in Fig. 3. The compound is proved to be antiferromagnetic with a TN=9K. At temperatures between 30 and 300 K, the magnetic susceptibility followed the Curie–Weiss law with C=4.41emumol−1Oe and θ=−22.5K. The effective magnetic moment per manganese atom is μeff=5.94μB. This value agrees well with the magnetic moment (5.92μB) for Mn(II) in high spin state.

4. Conclusions

In summary, a novel hybrid open-framework manganese phosphate–oxalate with an organic amine template has been hydrothermally synthesized and structurally characterized. The employment of H2O2 proved to be an important factor for the crystallization. The compound shows antiferromagnetic properties.
