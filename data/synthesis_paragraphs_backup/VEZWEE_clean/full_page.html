Structures and magnetic properties of copper(II) and manganese(II) polymers derived from pseudohalides and a flexible zwitterionic dicarboxylate ligand†

Kun Wang , Xiu-Chun Yi , Xuan Wang , Xiu-Bing Li and En-Qing Gao *
Shanghai Key Laboratory of Green Chemistry and Chemical Processes, Department of Chemistry, East China Normal University, Shanghai 200062, P. R. China. E-mail: eqgao@chem.ecnu.edu.cn; Fax: +86-21-62233404; Tel: +86-21-62233404

Received 20th February 2013 , Accepted 30th March 2013

First published on 3rd April 2013

The flexible zwitterionic dicarboxylate ligand 1,4-bis(4-carboxylato-1-pyridinium)butane (bcpb) assumes different conformations to collaborate with pseudohalides in various coordination modes to produce coordination polymers in which distinct anionic motifs with mixed carboxylate and pseudohalide bridges are interlinked by the cationic butylenebis(pyridinium) tethers. The Cu(II) compound, [Cu2(bcpb)(N3)4]n·nH2O (1), is a 1D coordination polymer based on the defective dicubane-like [Cu4(μ3-1,1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-OCO)2] cluster. With Mn(II), four distinct 3D coordination polymers, [Mn4(bcpb)4(N3)(H2O)4]n(ClO4)7n·nCH3OH·3nH2O (2), [Mn2.5(bcpb)(N3)5(H2O)2]n (3), [Mn2(bcpb)(N3)4]n·nH2O (4), and [Mn2(bcpb)(NCO)4]n·nH2O (5), were characterized. 2 is the first Mn(II) compound with the rare μ4-1,1,3,3 azide bridge and exhibits an unusual 3D framework based on the [Mn4(μ4-1,1,3,3-N3)(μ-1,3-OCO)6] cluster. In 3, the unique undulated honeycomb-like [Mn2(μ-1,3-N3)3]n layers are interlinked into a 3D framework by disordered [Mn(μ-1,1-N3)4(μ-1,3-OCO)2] and [(Oaqua–H)2⋯OCO]2 moieties, and the bcpb ligands serve as additional interlayer linkers to lead to the rare self-catenated 66 net. 4 and 5 show 3-fold interpenetrated 3D frameworks based on the chains with (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) and (μ-N,N-NCO)2(μ-1,3-OCO) bridges, respectively. Magnetic studies indicated that 1 shows competing ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions. Compounds 2–5 all show antiferromagnetic coupling between Mn(II) ions, while 3 shows 3D ordering. Analyses of magneto-structural data suggest a general trend that the antiferromagnetic interaction through (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) or (μ-N,N-NCO)2(μ-1,3-OCO) increases with a decrease of the Mn⋯Mn distance.

Introduction

Molecular magnetic systems have attracted great attention for decades for revealing fundamental magnetic phenomena, unveiling magnetostructural correlations, and constructing new magnetic materials with potential applications.1–4 The most extensively studied systems are the metal coordination compounds in which paramagnetic metal ions are linked into finite-sized polynuclear motifs or ‘infinite’ coordination polymers by short bridging groups.5 Pseudohalides (especially azide) and carboxylate can bind two or more metal ions in various modes to mediate magnetic exchange of different nature and magnitude, and have been extensively used to obtain magnetic systems with multifarious structures and magnetic properties.6–12 The last decade has witnessed the rapid emergence of magnetic studies with mixed pseudohalide and carboxylate bridges.13–17 To facilitate the simultaneous bridging coordination of the two competing negative groups, we have been employing pyridinium-carboxylate zwitterions as carboxylate sources, in which the positive pyridinium group serves to compensate for the negative carboxylate group and hence to help the coordination of pseudohalides. This simple synthetic approach has successfully led to various magnetic motifs with the simultaneous bridges, including polynuclear clusters, infinite chains and layers, and even 3D frameworks.14,16,17

In continuation of these studies, here we report the structures and magnetic properties of five Cu(II) and Mn(II) coordination polymers derived from pseudohalides (azide or cyanate) and the zwitterionic dicarboxylate ligand 1,4-bis(4-carboxylato-1-pyridinium)butane (bcpb, Scheme 1).18 The ligand, with a long and flexible (CH2)4 tether between two pyridinium-4-carboxylate groups, can assume various conformations and coordination modes to collaborate with the pseudohalides in different bridging modes to generate diverse coordination networks. The compounds reported here include: 1, a 1D Cu(II) chain compound based on the defective dicubane-like [Cu4(μ3-1,1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-OCO)2] cluster; 2, an Mn4 cluster-based 3D framework representing the first Mn(II) species with the rare μ4-1,1,3,3-N3 bridge; 3, an unusual self-catenated 3D framework with mixed μ-1,3-N3, μ-1,1-N3 and μ-1,3-OCO bridges; 4, a 3D Mn(II) framework based on the [Mn(μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO)]n chain; and 5, an analogue of 4 with μ-N,N-NCO in the place of μ-1,1-N3. Compound 1 exhibits competing ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions, while 2–5 feature overall antiferromagnetic behaviours, with 3D magnetic ordering for 3. The magnetostructural correlations were discussed for the (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) and (μ-N,N-NCO)2(μ-1,3-OCO) bridges.

Experimental section

Physical measurements

FT-IR spectra were recorded in the range 500–4000 cm−1 using KBr pellets on a Nicolet Nexus 670 spectrophotometer. The phase purity of the bulk samples was verified by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements performed on a Rigaku Ultima IV diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα. Elemental analyses were determined on an Elementar vario EL III analyzer. Temperature-dependent magnetic measurements were performed on a Quantum Design MPMS-XL5 SQUID magnetometer. The experimental susceptibilities were corrected for the diamagnetism of the constituent atoms (Pascal's tables).

Synthesis

Reagents were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification, and the ligand in the dihydrobromide form, [H2bcpb]Br2, was prepared according to the literature.18

Results and discussion

Description of the structures

Compound 1. This compound is a 1D coordination polymer in which tetracopper(II) clusters are linked by zwitterionic bcpb ligands (Fig. 1). There are two unique Cu atoms, both adopting the square pyramidal (SP) geometry with four short basal bonds and a long apical bond. The basal bonds for both Cu centres involve three azide N atoms and a carboxylate O atom (Cu–N/O = 1.924(3)–2.070(4) Å). For Cu1, a fourth azide N atom resides at the apex (Cu1–N4A = 2.491(7) Å). The geometric index τ = (α − β)/60° (α and β are the two largest angles (α > β) around a five-coordinated metal centre)21 is 0.031, indicating small distortion towards trigonal bipyramidal. For Cu2, the pyramidal apex is furnished by a carboxylate O atom, for which the rather long bond distance (Cu2–O3C = 2.851(7) Å) indicates rather weak coordination. The index τ = 0.0028 for Cu2 suggests little distortion towards trigonal bipyramidal.
Fig. 1 (a) The tetranuclear cluster unit in compound 1 (thermal ellipsoids are drawn at 30% probability, and hydrogen atoms omitted for clarity). Symmetric codes: A. 1 − x, 2 − y, −z; B. −2 + x, −1 + y, −1 + z; C. 3 − x, 3 − y, 1 − z. (b) A 1D chain along the a direction.

The azide ions in 1 adopt three different coordination modes: μ3-1,1,1-N3 connecting two Cu1 and one Cu2 ions (Cu1–N4–Cu2 = 98.4(2)°, Cu1–N4–Cu1A = 95.4(2)°, Cu1A–N4–Cu2 = 102.3(1)°), μ-1,1-N3 connecting Cu1 and Cu2 (Cu1–N1–Cu2 = 103.2(2)°), and the non-bridging terminal mode binding Cu1 or Cu2. The carboxylate groups adopt two modes: μ-1,1-OCO binding Cu1 and Cu2 using a μ-O (Cu1–O3B–Cu2A = 93.2(1)°), and the terminal mode binding Cu2. The μ3-1,1,1-N3, μ-1,1-N3, and μ-1,1-OCO bridges collaborate to arrange four Cu(II) ions into a centrosymmetric tetranuclear parallelogram. The diagonal Cu1 and Cu1A sites are doubly linked by two μ3-1,1,1-N3 bridges with a distance of 3.384(8) Å, while the lateral sites are doubly linked by μ3-1,1,1-N3 and μ-1,1-N3 (or μ-1,1-OCO) with Cu1⋯Cu2 = 3.10(2) Å (or Cu1⋯Cu2A = 3.528(6) Å). The [Cu4N4O2] cluster may be described as a defective dicubane with two missing vertices. A similar, but all-azide motif, [Cu4N6], has been recognized in a 1D railroad-like Cu-azide complex.22

The flexible bcpb ligand in 1 assumes the TTT conformation (T = transoid) for the butylene linker between pyridinium rings and uses its two carboxylate groups to bind three metal ions (Scheme 2). They appear in pairs to serve as long double bridges between tetranuclear clusters, generating 1D coordination chains along the (211) direction. The shortest Cu⋯Cu distance spanned by the ligand is 17.610(8) Å.

Compound 2. This compound is a 3D coordination polymer with mixed azide and carboxylate bridges. The coordination moiety in the asymmetric unit looks like a frog (Fig. 2), with a tetranuclear Mn4(μ4-N3)(μ-OCO)6 cluster as the body and four bcpb ligands sticking out like legs. The cluster has an azide ion in the μ4-1,1,3,3 bridging mode, with Mn1–N1–Mn2 = 113.6(2)°, Mn3–N3–Mn4 = 110.1(2)° and Mn–N = 2.281(6)–2.343(6) Å. The two Mn–N–Mn moieties form a dihedral angle of 25.57(5)°, and each Mn–N–Mn moiety is quasi-coplanar with the linear azide bridge. Among the various coordination modes of azide, the μ4-1,1,3,3 mode is still very rare and has been found in only a few Cu(II) and Ni(II) compounds.23,24 To our knowledge, 2 is the first Mn(II) compound with this rare mode. In previous Cu(II) and Ni(II) compounds, the bridging mode is usually supported by coexisting multichelating or macrocyclic ligands that contain binucleating sites to seize metal ions. In 2, there are no chelating ligands, and the bridging mode (and the Mn4 cluster) is reinforced by six peripheral μ-1,3-carboxylate bridges. The short Mn–N–Mn linkage (Mn1⋯Mn2 = 3.878(2) Å or Mn3⋯Mn4 = 3.771(2) Å) is reinforced by two carboxylate bridges that adopt the syn–syn coordination mode with small M–O–C–O torsion angles (2–28°) and small M–O–C angles (126–146°), while the long Mn–N–N–N–Mn linkage (Mn2⋯Mn3 = 5.044(2) Å or Mn1⋯Mn4 = 4.972(2) Å) is reinforced by a single carboxylate bridge, which adopts the out-of-plane syn-skew mode with a large M–O–C–O torsion angle (94–100°) and large M–O–C angles (150–152°) to adapt to the long distance. All Mn(II) ions adopt the [NO5] octahedral geometry, with short Mn–O (2.083(5)–2.239(5) Å) and long Mn–N (2.281(6)–2.343(6) Å). Each Mn(II) has one N atom and three O atoms from the azide and carboxylate bridges supporting the Mn4 cluster. The remaining octahedral vertices are occupied by two water molecules (for Mn3 and Mn4) or by two O atoms (for Mn1 and Mn2) arising from additional carboxylate groups. These additional carboxylate groups serve as double μ-1,3-carboxylate bridges between Mn1 and Mn2 from different clusters, and thus connect the clusters into a zigzag-like 1D motif along the a direction (Fig. 3a). The inter-cluster carboxylate bridge adopts the skew–skew coordination mode with metal ions significantly displaced out of the O–C–O plane towards the opposite sides. The Mn⋯Mn distance (4.504(2) Å) spanned by the (μ-1,3-OCO)2 bridges is intermediate between those by (μ-1,3-N3)(μ-1,3-OCO) and (μ-1,1-N3)(μ-1,3-OCO)2.

The four independent bcpb ligands in 2 all serve as μ4 bridges but assume three different conformations (Scheme 2). The two ‘foreleg’ ligands (Fig. 2) adopt the bent TTG (G = gauche) conformation, and each uses one carboxylate to bind a Mn–N–N–N–Mn moiety in one Mn4 cluster and uses the other carboxylate to bind a Mn–N–Mn moiety in another cluster. The two “hind-leg” ligands adopt the quasi-linear TTT or TGT conformation, with one carboxylate binding a Mn–N–Mn moiety in one cluster and the other carboxylate serving as a bridge between another two clusters. As can be expected, the TTG conformation leads to shorter Mn⋯Mn distances (∼14.0 Å) than TTT or TGT (∼18.5 Å). All the bcpb ligands serve as linkers between different zigzag-like 1D motifs to generate a complicated 3D network (Fig. 3b). The network is positively charged and possesses irregular apertures and chambers in which the perchlorate counterions are enclosed.

To clearly describe the 3D network, topological analysis was performed. To better express the real topology, the C atom of the inter-cluster carboxylate bridge is regarded as a 3-coordinated node: it is connected to a third cluster through the butylenebis(pyridinium) spacer, besides the two clusters bonded directly by the carboxylate. Consequently, the Mn4 cluster can be represented by a 10-coordinated node: it is connected with six identical clusters through bcpb ligands and four 3-coordinated nodes defined above. The calculations using the TOPOS program25 suggest that the simplified net (Fig. 3c) represents a new topology with point symbol (4.5.6)2(46.518.619.72).

Compound 3. This compound shows a 3D coordination network completely different from that of 2. The structure has two independent metal sites, the fully occupied Mn1 site and the disordered and half occupied Mn2 site (Fig. 4). Mn1 is coordinated by three azide N atoms and a carboxylate O atom. The octahedral geometry is completed by statistically disordered water and azide ligands. The non-disordered azide ions serve as single μ-1,3 bridges between equivalent Mn1 atoms. Mn1 atoms related by the c-glide plane are connected by the N2–N3–N4 bridges to form zigzag chains along the c direction, with Mn⋯Mn = 6.131(2) Å, and the chains are connected by the centrosymmetric N5–N6–N5′ bridges (Mn⋯Mn = 6.359(2) Å) to generate an undulated layer (Fig. 5a). The 24-membered hexametallic Mn6(N3)6 macrocycles constituting the layer take the chair conformation, and the layer is an undulated variant of the 2D honeycomb net.

The Mn1-azide layers are packed in parallel with half-occupied Mn2 sites intercalating the interlayers. Taking into account the disordered environment of Mn1, there are many crystallographically possible connection modes between the layers. Considering that azide is more prone to bridge Mn(II) ions than water and that water tends to form hydrogen bonds, two chemically likely connection modes are proposed (Fig. 5b). (i) The coordinative mode involving the octahedral coordination of Mn2 with four μ-1,1-N3 and two μ-1,3-OCO groups. In this mode, an interlayer Mn2 ion and two Mn1 ions from different layers form a centrosymmetric trinuclear motif through the triple (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) bridges. The Mn–N/O distances (2.14–2.25 Å), the Mn–N–Mn angles (∼90°) and the Mn⋯Mn distance (3.16 Å) are comparable with the values reported for such mixed bridges.14 (ii) The hydrogen-bonding mode in the absence of Mn2. In this mode, Mn1 has two water ligands, and the neighbouring Mn1 centres from different layers are connected by the quadruple hydrogen bonding moieties [(Oaqua–H)2⋯OCO]2: each carboxylate group binding Mn1 of one layer uses the uncoordinated O atom to form two O–H⋯O bonds with two water molecules binding Mn1 of another layer (see the ESI† for parameters). Fig. 5b shows the two connection modes in an alternating way so that double O–H⋯N hydrogen bonds are formed between water and azide.

The two connection modes serve to associate the Mn1-azide layers into a 3D network (Fig. 5c). Furthermore, the bcpb ligand adopting the long TTT conformation (Scheme 2) threads through the chair-conformational mesh of a Mn1-azide layer to connect two next-nearest neighbouring layers. For topological analyses of the complicated 3D structure, a convenient simplification is to regard Mn1 sites as 5-connected nodes. The links include three intralayer μ-1,3-azide bridges and an interlayer long bcpb linker. The fifth link is either the Mn2-coordination motif or the [(Oaqua–H)2⋯OCO]2 hydrogen-bonding motif (the two motifs are disordered and crystallographically undistinguishable). TOPOS calculations25 suggest a topology with the simple point symbol 610. This topology has been theoretically derived (for details, see the output of the TOPOS program given in the ESI†) but not yet observed in real systems before. As can be seen from Fig. 5d, the net can be generated by systematically connecting some nodes of the very common 4-coordinated diamond net (the net of 3 in the absence of bcpb) with long links (bcpb linkers) threading through chair-shaped six-membered cycles. Obviously, the net features polythreading and self-catenation.

Compound 4. This compound displays a third type of 3D coordination network derived from Mn(II), bcpb and azide. There are three independent Mn(II) ions, all residing in the [N4O2] octahedral environments composed of four azide N atoms and two carboxylate O atoms (Fig. 6). Mn1 is located in a general position with the two O atoms in cis positions, while Mn2 and Mn3 are located at inversion centres and adopt the trans-octahedral geometry. For all Mn ions, the Mn–N distances (2.199(4)–2.407(3) Å) are longer than Mn–O (2.145(4)–2.170(4) Å). The metal ions alternate along the c direction in the Mn1–Mn2–Mn1–Mn3 sequence and neighbouring Mn ions are triply bridged by a syn–syn μ-1,3 carboxylate group and two μ-1,1 azide ions to generate an infinite chain of edge-sharing octahedrons (Fig. 7a). Associated with the cis-coordination around Mn1, the 1D arrangement of metal ions has a zigzag shape with Mn1 at the turning point. For the mixed triple bridges, the Mn–N–Mn angles (the Mn⋯Mn distances) are 91.9(2)° (3.304(7) Å) between Mn1 and Mn2, and 93.2(2)° (3.246(7) Å) between Mn1 and Mn3 (the Mn–N–Mn values are the averages for two independent azides).

The bcpb ligands in 4 reside at inversion centres, assume two different conformations (TTT and GTG, Scheme 2), and serve as interchain linkers. The GTG ligand is more zigzag and leads to a shorter Mn⋯Mn distance (17.06(6) Å) than the TTT ligand (19.51(5) Å). Through the ligands extending out in four divergent directions, each [Mn(N3)2(OCO)]n chain is linked to four others to produce a 3D framework (Fig. 7b, left). For topological analysis, we follow the approach of O'Keeffe and Yaghi for metal–organic frameworks with rod-like secondary building units (SBUs).26 With the carboxylate C atoms as the points of extension, the chain in 4 can be simplified into a 1D screwy SBU (Fig. 7a, right), and then the underlying net of the 3D framework becomes the 3-coordinated uninodal ths net (point symbol 103, Fig. 7b, right). To minimize the large ‘void’ volume in a single framework, three-fold interpenetration is adopted (Fig. 8). Although the chain with (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) triple bridges has been found in a few compounds,14a–f compound 4 is the first 3D framework based on such chains.

Compounds 2–4 are derived from the same metal ion and the same ligands, but different coordination modes or conformations of the azide and bcpb ligands have led to various mixed azide–carboxylate bridging networks with different topologies. Note that none of these Mn(II) structures are similar to those derived from the similar zwitterionic dicarboxylate ligand 1,3-bis(4-carboxylato-1-pyridinium)propane (bcpp).16a,f The ligands bcpb and bcpp differ only in the length of the alkane tether between pyridinium groups. The remarkable differences between their coordination structures reflect, on the one hand, the important role of the flexible organic ligands in determining the structure, and on the other hand, the great adaptability of azide and carboxylate to various bridging networks.

Compound 5. This compound displays a 3D framework (Fig. 9) similar to that of 4, although they crystallize in different crystal systems. There are two crystallographically independent Mn(II) ions in 5, both adopting the [N4O2] octahedral coordination consisting of four cyanate N atoms and two carboxylate O atoms. Mn1 resides on a crystallographic C2 axis and adopts the cis-octahedral geometry, while Mn2 is located in a centrosymmetric trans-octahedral environment. The bond distances and angles around Mn are similar to those in 4, and again, the Mn–O distances (2.164(1)–2.178(1) Å) are generally shorter than Mn–N (2.196(2)–2.254(2) Å). Neighbouring metal ions are triply bridged by a syn–syn carboxylate group and two cyanate ligands in the N-bridging mode to generate a [Mn(μ-N,N-NCO)2(μ-1,3-OCO)]n chain along the c direction (Fig. 9a), with a similar shape to that in 4. The Mn–N–Mn angles and the Mn⋯Mn distance are 92.34(7)° (av.) and 3.2275(3) Å, respectively.

Magnetic properties

Compound 1. The magnetic susceptibilities (χ) of compound 1 were measured with a crystalline sample under 1 kOe in the range of 2–300 K and are shown as χT and χ versus T plots in Fig. 10. The χT value per tetracopper cluster at 300 K is about 2.03 emu K mol−1, larger than the spin-only value (1.50 emu K mol−1 for g = 2.00) expected for four magnetically isolated Cu(II) ions. As the temperature is lowered, the χ value increases continuously, while χT increases slowly to reach 2.32 emu K mol−1 at 45 K, then decreases continuously to 1.33 emu K mol−1 at 2 K. The data above 130 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 1.90 emu K mol−1 and θ = 19.85 K. The above behaviours in the high temperature range suggest dominant ferromagnetic interactions between Cu(II) ions, and the decrease of χT at lower temperature is attributable to competing antiferromagnetic interactions.

There are three different bridging motifs between adjacent Cu(II) ions in the tetranuclear cluster of 1: Cu1(μ-1,1-N3)2Cu2, Cu1(μ-1,1-N3)2Cu1A, and Cu1(μ-1,1-N3)(μ-1,1-OCO)Cu2A. Accordingly, the superexchange scheme is drawn in Scheme 3.

The best fit of the experimental data of 1 led to J1 = 159.3 cm−1, J2 = −3.46 cm−1, J3 = −0.14 cm−1 and g = 2.17 with R = 7 × 10−5 (R is the residual factor defined as [Σ(χobsd − χcald)2]/(n − p), where n and p are the numbers of the data points and parameters, respectively).

The J parameters obtained are in good agreement with the structural data in the tetranuclear cluster. The bridges between SP Cu(II) ions have three different disposition modes, basal–basal, basal–apical and apical–apical. Since the magnetic orbital for SP Cu(II) with axial elongation is dx2−y2, which lies at the basal plane, the basal–basal bridges can efficiently induce magnetic exchange, while the basal–apical or apical–apical bridges cannot. In 1, for the diagonal pair Cu1⋯Cu1A, the μ3-1,1,1-N3 bridge is basal for Cu1 but apical for Cu1A (see Fig. 1), so the diagonal exchange should be weak, consistent with the small J2 value. For Cu2⋯Cu1A, both μ3-1,1,1-N3 and μ-1,1-OCO are basal–apical, so weak exchange is expected and can be related to the small J3 value. For Cu1⋯Cu2, both μ3-1,1,1-N3 and μ-1,1-N3 are basal–basal, favouring a strong interaction associated with the large J1 value.

The ferromagnetic interaction through the double basal–basal azide bridges is in good agreement with the previous observations for Cu(II) systems with such bridges.28 It has been demonstrated experimentally and theoretically that the interactions through “symmetric” double μ-1,1-azides are ferromagnetic for Cu–N–Cu angles smaller than ca. 104°.29 The known Cu(II) dimers with the bridges have Cu–N–Cu angles in the range 97.5–104.7° associated with J values in the wide range from 230 to 2.5 cm−1.28 In general, the magnitude of the interactions decreases with an increase of the Cu–N–Cu angle and the Cu–N bond distances. The distortion of the SP geometry towards trigonal bipyramidal may also reduce the interaction. For the basal–basal double bridges in 1, the Cu–N–Cu angles (av. 100.8°) and the Cu–N distances (av. 2.01 Å) are intermediate in the ranges for known compounds, and the distortions of both Cu1 and Cu2 are only slight. These factors collaborate to yield a relatively strong ferromagnetic interaction.

Compound 2. The magnetic susceptibilities under 1 kOe are shown in Fig. 11. The χT value per tetramanganese cluster at 300 K is about 14.64 emu K mol−1, smaller than the spin-only value (17.50 emu K mol−1 with g = 2.00) expected for four magnetically isolated Mn(II) ions. Upon cooling, the χT value decreases continuously to 0.306 emu K mol−1 at 2 K, whereas the χ value increases to a maximum at 18 K and then decreases. The data above 100 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 17.5 emu K mol−1 and θ = −56.0 K. The temperature-dependent behaviours clearly suggest overall antiferromagnetic interactions. The structural analysis revealed that the 3D framework of 2 is built of complex chains based on tetranuclear Mn(II) clusters. There are six independent pairs of bridged Mn(II) ions within the cluster, the bridging motifs falling into three types: (μ-OCO)2(μ-N) for Mn1⋯Mn2 and Mn3⋯Mn4, (μ-OCO)(cis-μ-NNN) for Mn2⋯Mn3 and Mn1⋯Mn4, and trans-μ-NNN for the diagonal pairs Mn1⋯Mn3 and Mn2⋯Mn4. Furthermore, Mn1 and Mn2 from different clusters are coupled through the (μ-OCO)2 double bridges. Although μ4-1,1,3,3 azide between Mn(II) ions has not been reported before, the above bridging motifs have been individually studied, all displaying antiferromagnetic interactions of similar magnitude (several cm−1). Seven or at least four J parameters (if the bridging motifs of the same type are assumed to have the same J value) are needed to fully describe the complex magnetic system. No appropriate model is applicable to such a multi-J infinite system, preventing us from quantitatively evaluating the magnetic interactions in 2.

Compound 3. The magnetic susceptibilities under 1 kOe are shown in Fig. 12. The χT value at 300 K is about 8.94 emu K mol−1, and as the temperature is lowered, the value decreases continuously to 0.179 emu K mol−1 at 2 K, whereas χ increases to a maximum at 28 K and then decreases. The data above 50 K follow the Curie–Weiss law with C = 11.0 emu K mol−1 and θ = −69.0 K. The C value is in good agreement with the theoretical spin-only value for 2.5 Mn(II) ions [C = 2.5Ng2β2S(S + 1)/3k = 10.9 emu K mol−1], supporting the disorder of Mn sites, and the negative θ value suggests antiferromagnetic interaction between Mn(II) ions. Due to the complication arising from the 3D bridging network and the disorder of Mn sites, it is impossible to quantitatively evaluate the multiple interactions. Previous studies have established that μ-1,3-N3 or the mixed (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) bridges always induce antiferromagnetic coupling between Mn(II) ions. In 3, these two types of antiferromagnetic couplers link Mn(II) ions into a 3D framework. The sharp maximum of the χ–T curve for 3 (for comparison, the maxima of 2 and 4–5 are round) may indicate antiferromagnetic ordering, the Neel temperature (TN) being somewhat lower than the temperature for χmax.30 The occurrence of magnetic ordering is confirmed by the λ-type anomaly in the d(χT)/dT curve (Fig. 12, inset). The d(χT)/dT data are a good approximation to magnetic specific heat and are called “Fisher specific heat”.31 An anomaly in Fisher specific heat is associated with a similar anomaly in real specific heat at the same temperature, so the temperature at which the d(χT)/dT anomaly occurs provides a reliable and simple determination of TN.30–32 For 3, we have TN = 26.0 K.

Compound 4. The magnetic susceptibilities (χ) of 4 are shown in Fig. 13. The χT value per Mn(II) at 300 K is about 3.80 emu K mol−1, lower than the spin-only value (4.38 emu K mol−1) expected for a magnetically isolated high-spin Mn(II) ion. Fig. 13 Temperature dependence of χ and χT for 4. The solid lines represent the best fits to the uniform-chain model (cyan) and the alternating-chain model (red) (see the text).

Two different classical-spin models have been utilized to fit the experimental magnetic data.

(1) The first model treats the system as a uniform chain in which the magnetic interactions between neighbouring metal ions are identical. Then the interaction (J) can be expressed by the spin Hamiltonian H = −J∑SiSi+1, and the thermal magnetic behaviours of 4 can be described by the following expression in the classical-spin approximation:33

 
χchain = [Ng2β2S(S + 1)/3kT][(1 + u)/(1 − u)](2)
where u is the Langevin function u = coth[JS(S + 1)/kT] − kT/[JS(S + 1)] with S = 5/2, and the other symbols have the usual meanings. Including the molar fraction (ρ) of the paramagnetic impurity as an additional parameter to account for the low temperature rise, the best fit of the experimental data of 4 to the above expression led to J = −5.12 cm−1, g = 2.00 and ρ = 0.0016 with R = 1.56 × 10−7.

(2) Considering that the chain in 4 actually contains two sets of triple bridges having different structural parameters and alternating in the AABB sequence, it is best described as a 1D Heisenberg chain with alternating J1–J1–J2–J2 interactions. The Hamiltonian is H = −J1Σ(S4i−2S4i−1 + S4i−1S4i) − J2Σ(S4iS4i+1 + S4i+1S4i+2), and the expression of χ for such a chain in the classical-spin approximation was deduced elsewhere as follows.16a

 
χchain = [Ng2β2S(S + 1)/(12kT)](A/B)(3)
with

A = 4 + 4u1 + 4u2 + 4u1u2 + 2u12 + 2u22 + 4u12u2 + 4u1u22 + 4u12u22

B = 1 − u12u22

The best fit led to J1 = −5.7 cm−1, J2 = −4.6 cm−1, ρ = 0.0015 and g = 2.00 with R = 1.53 × 10−7.

The fits using the above two models both led to good agreement. The average of J1 and J2 from the alternating-chain model is very close to the J value obtained from the uniform-chain model. Actually, the fit using the alternating-chain model is not very sensitive to the difference between J1 and J2, so the above values for J1 and J2 should be taken as rough approximations. Nevertheless, it is reasonable that the two sets of structurally different triple bridges lead to different interactions. A tentative assignment is to attribute the larger value of J1 = −5.7 cm−1 to the Mn1⋯Mn3 interaction and the smaller J2 value to the Mn1⋯Mn2 interaction (see below).

Our studies using pyridinium-based carboxylate zwitterions as carboxylate sources have fruitfully led to various systems with mixed azide and carboxylate bridges.14,16,17 The most frequently encountered mixed bridges in the systems are the triple motifs (μ-1,1-N3)(μ-1,3-OCO)2 and (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO). Both motifs have been found to always induce antiferromagnetic exchange in Mn(II) compounds. For (μ-N3)(μ-OCO)2, some studies have suggested that the Mn–N distance and the Mn–N–Mn angle of the azide pathway are the main factors determining the magnitude of the magnetic interaction:16 the larger the Mn–N distance or the Mn–N–Mn angle, the stronger the antiferromagnetic interaction. Conclusions

The bis(pyridiniumcarboxylate) ligand bcpb, which combines a high conformational flexibility and the zwitterionic nature, has been incorporated into metal–pseudohalide magnetic systems, generating various mixed pseudohalide–carboxylate bridged motifs with formal negative charge, including clusters, 1D chains and even a 3D framework. In compound 1, the [Cu4(μ3-1,1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,1-OCO)2(N3)4(OCO)2] clusters with defective dicubane-like cores are linked into 1D chains by butylenebis(pyridinium) tethers. The cluster shows competing ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions. With Mn(II) and azide, three different motifs are obtained. 2 is the first Mn(II) compound with the rare μ4-1,1,3,3 azide bridge. In this compound, the [Mn4(μ4-1,1,3,3-N3)(μ-1,3-OCO)6] clusters are interlinked into 1D chains through additional μ-1,3-OCO, and the chains are linked into a 3D framework by organic tethers. In 3, the undulated honeycomb-like [Mn2(μ-1,3-N3)2]n layers are interlinked into an unusual 3D framework by disordered [Mn(μ-1,1-N3)4(μ-1,3-OCO)2] moieties. 4 shows 3-fold interpenetrated 3D frameworks in which the chains with (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) triple bridges are interlinked by organic tethers. Compound 5 is similar to 4, with cyanate in place of azide. 2–5 all show antiferromagnetic coupling between Mn(II) ions, while 3 shows 3D ordering. Magneto-structural data suggest that the Mn⋯Mn antiferromagnetic exchange through (μ-1,1-N3)2(μ-1,3-OCO) and (μ-N,N-NCO)2(μ-1,3-OCO) increases with a decrease of the Mn⋯Mn distance.
