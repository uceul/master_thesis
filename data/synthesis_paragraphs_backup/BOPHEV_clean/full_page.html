Luminescent group 12 metal tetracarboxylate networks as probe for metal ions†

Hai-Yun Ren, Cai-Yun Han, Mei Qu and Xian-Ming Zhang*
School of Chemistry & Material Science, Shanxi Normal University, Linfen, Shanxi 041004, China. E-mail: zhangxm@dns.sxnu.edu.cn

Received 3rd June 2014 , Accepted 16th September 2014

First published on 16th September 2014

Hydrothermal in situ decomposition of DMF generated three [NH2(CH3)2]+ cation templated anionic zinc and cadmium terphenyl-3,2′′,5′′,3′-tetracarboxylate (tpta) frameworks. Compound 1 shows a kgd topological layer constructed by 6-connected paddlewheel-type [Zn2(CO2)4] SUBs and 3-connected tpta groups. Compound 2 has a 3D sra network constructed by the zigzag chains of metal centers and tpta groups. Compound 3 adopts a new (4,7)-connected topological network with four types of nodes. Compounds 1–3 show two blue phosphorescent emission bands attributed to ligand centered transitions perturbed by metal ions, which displays significant temperature-dependent behavior. The higher energy emissions are red-shifted and weakened with increasing temperature, while the lower energy emissions increase in intensity and finally become the main emission bands; the CIE coordinates for 1 are changed with decreasing temperature from blue to greenish blue. Interestingly, 1 could selectively detect traces of Ca2+ ions at ppm level in a pyridine suspension, which makes it more suitable to be a luminescent probe for sensing Ca+ ions.

Introduction

The post-synthetic modification of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) by the diffusion or exchange of atoms has recently attracted the current interest of material scientists because this can be a simple and versatile way to modify the physical environment of the pores and cavities within MOFs, which can be utilized for gas storage, molecular sensors, and nonlinear optical devices.1 For instance, a reversible switch of the inversion symmetry in a cobalt ferrocyanide framework was achieved by alkaline cation exchange, which can effectively control the second harmonic generation activity of compound K1.88Co[Fe(CN)6]C0.97 at room temperature.2 However, cation-exchange within anionic MOFs, in particular, has been shown to be a very easy way to modify the physical properties of the host framework.3 By cation exchange procedures, luminescent anionic MOFs have been proven to be successful in the detection of volatile organic compounds, small molecules, and ionic species. Synthetically, the rational design in the construction of anionic metal–carboxylate frameworks may borrow an organic template idea from traditional aluminosilicates.4 Using N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF) as a precursor of [NH2(CH3)2]+ templates, Sun et al. prepared anion luminescent europium–tetracarboxylates, which could selectively sense Fe3+ and Al3+ ions through fluorescence quenching and enhancement, respectively.5 Recently, transition-metal-based luminescent MOFs have been focused on d10 zinc and cadmium MOFs because these metal ions not only possess various coordination numbers and geometries, but they also exhibit superior luminescent properties when bound to functional ligands, which makes them particularly suitable for the development of optical devices and tunable luminescent sensors for chemical species.6 It should be noted that anionic zinc and cadmium tetracarboxylate frameworks with tunable luminescent properties for probe metal ions are still rare.

In the ongoing research of porous luminescent frameworks, multifunctional terphenyl-3,2′′,5′′,3′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4tpta) attracted our attention. First, H4tpta can be twisted by different degrees across the C–C single bonds, indicating it possesses both rigidity and flexibility. Second, it has a significant degree of conjugation, which often induces strong absorption and emission. Additionally, the electron-withdrawing carboxylic groups of H4tpta can enhance the probability of the intersystem crossing from the first excited singlet state to the triplet state to promote the emission of phosphorescence.7

In this paper, by in situ hydrolysis of DMF to generate [NH2(CH3)2]+ cations, we herein present the syntheses, structures, and luminescent properties of three anion open frameworks [Me2NH2]2[Zn(tpta)] (1), [Me2NH2]2[Cd(tpta)]·H2O (2), and [Me2NH2]3[Cd2.5(tpta)(H2O)(DMF)0.5]·3DMF (3), along with their optical response to the exchange of cationic guest molecules. The comparison of photoluminescent spectra for free H4tpta and 1–3 suggests that the coordination of tpta ligands to metal clusters could impose structural rigidity and slightly tune their electronic structure, leading to red shift and much longer lifetime for their emissions. The emission spectra of 1–3 all show strong temperature-dependence. From 4 K to 298 K, the centered emission is inversed between the higher energy band and the lower energy band in 1–3. Compound 1 in a pyridine suspension exhibits a high sensitivity for the detection of Ca2+, which shows that it may be efficient phosphorescence sensor.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All chemicals from commercial sources were analytically pure and used without further purification. Elemental analyses were performed on a Vario EL-II analyzer. FTIR spectra were recorded using KBr pellets in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum BX FT-IR spectrometer. Variable temperature powder X-ray diffraction (VT-PXRD) data were collected using a Bruker D8 advance diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. The purity of all compounds was confirmed by the comparison of experimental PXRD patterns with the simulated pattern derived from the X-ray single crystal data compound. Photoluminescence was performed on an Edinburgh FLS920 luminescence spectrometer. The luminescent quantum yields of 1–3 were measured using an absolute method8 using a calibrated integrating sphere. Low temperature luminescence study was performed on a Janis SHI-4s-1 closed cycle refrigerator, which provides convenient means for cooling the samples to temperatures of 4 K by a closed loop of helium gas. The thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in air atmosphere using Setaram LABSYS equipment at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1.

X-ray crystallography

X-ray single-crystal diffraction data for 1–3 were collected on a Bruker Smart APEX CCD diffractometer at 298(2) K using Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The program SAINT was used for the integration of the diffraction profiles, and the program SADABS was used for absorption correction. All the structures were solved by the direct method using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined by a full-matrix least-squares technique with SHELXL.9 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters. The hydrogen atoms of organic ligands were theoretically generated onto the specific carbon and isotropically refined with fixed thermal factors. Syntheses

[Me2NH2]2[Zn(tpta)] (1). A mixture of ZnCl2 (0.056 g, 0.40 mmol), H4tpta (0.042 g, 0.10 mmol), NH3·H2O (0.1 mL/5 M, 0.5 mmol), and DMF (3 mL) was stirred, then sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave at 150 °C for 6 days. After it was cooled to room temperature and subjected to filtration, yellow block crystals of 1 in a yield of 68.6% (based on H4tpta) were recovered. Anal. calcd (%) for C26H21ZnN2O8 (1): C, 56.28; H, 3.78; N, 5.05. Found: C, 55.82; H, 3.69; N, 4.95. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3454 (b), 2354(w), 1626(s), 1482(s), 1098(w), 843(w), 796(s).
[Me2NH2]2[Cd(tpta)]·H2O (2). A mixture of CdBr2 (0.104 g, 0.3 mmol), H4tpta (0.041 g, 0.1 mmol), NH3·H2O (0.1 mL/5 M, 0.5 mmol), and DMF (3 mL) was stirred, then sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave at 130 °C for 6 days. After it was cooled to room temperature and subjected to filtration, yellow block crystals of 2 in a yield of 56.7% (based on H4tpta) were recovered. Anal. calcd (%) for C26H28CdN2O9 (2): C, 49.97; H, 4.52; N, 4.48. Found: C, 50.11; H, 4.42; N, 4.73. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3424 (b), 2344(w), 1616(s), 1400(vs), 998(w), 832(w), 598(w).
[Me2NH2]3[Cd2.5(tpta)2(H2O)(DMF)0.5]·3DMF (3). A mixture of CdBr2 (0.138 g, 0.4 mmol), H4tpta (0.041 g, 0.1 mmol), NH3·H2O (0.1 mL/5 M, 0.5 mmol), and DMF (3 mL) was stirred, then sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave at 130 °C for 6 days. After it was cooled to room temperature and subjected to filtration, yellow block crystals of 3 in yield of 86.7% (based on H4tpta) were recovered. Anal. calcd (%) for C60H70.5Cd2.5N6.5O20.5 (3): C, 48.31; H, 4.76; N, 6.10. Found: C, 49.63; H, 4.62; N, 6.03. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3452 (b), 2352(w), 1570(s), 1368(vs), 826(w), 760(w), 588(w).

Preparation of suspensions

First, finely ground 2 mg samples of 1, 2 and 3 were immersed in different organic solvents (4 mL), subjected to ultrasonication for 1 h, and then sealed in 5 mL vials to age for three days to form stable suspensions before the luminescence study. Second, 1 was immersed in pyridine that contained equivalent amount of nitrate {M(NO3)x, M = Mg2+, Ca2+, Co2+, Cu+, Cu2+, Ag+, Pb2+, Bi3+, Nd3+, Dy3+, La3+, Ce3+, Er3+, Tb3+, Sm3+, or Y3+}, 2 and 3 were immersed in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene that contained equivalent amount of nitrate {M(NO3)x, M = Ca2+, Cu+, Cu2+, Ag+, Nd3+, Dy3+, Ce3+, Er3+, Tb3+, Sm3+, Y3+, Eu3+, La3+, Gd3+, or Bi3+}, and they were subjected to ultrasonication for 1 h, and then sealed in 5 mL vials at 85 °C for 3 days to perform cation-exchange for the luminescence studies. Third, 1 was immersed in pyridine that contained different concentrations of Ca(NO3)2, subjected to ultrasonication for 1 h, and then sealed in 5 mL vials at 85 °C for 3 days to perform cation-exchange for luminescence studies.

Results and discussion

Description of structures

Compound 1 crystallized in the triclinic space group P with an asymmetric unit consisting of one crystallographically independent Zn(II) ion, one tpta, and two [Me2NH2]+ cations, as shown in Fig. S1.† The dihedral angle between the two phenyls of tpta is 146.69°, which is in agreement with the semi-rigid nature of tpta. The Zn(1) resides in a distorted square pyramidal geometry ligated by four bis(monodentate) carboxylate oxygen atoms in the equatorial plane and one monodentate carboxylate oxygen atom at the axial position. The slightly distorted square pyramidal environment can be evidenced by the discrimination parameter (τ) value of 0.001, which is defined as |β − α|/60 by Addison et al.10 The carboxylate O(3)–C–O(4) is also deprotonated as a result of the formation of N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds with [Me2NH2]+ cations. The Zn–O distances in the equatorial plane are in the range of 2.028(2)–2.072(2) Å, and the axial shorter Zn(1)–O(1) distance is 1.971(2) Å. The coordination bond angles around Zn(II) vary from 86.37(8)° to 157.50(7)°. The ligand tpta adopts a μ5-coordination mode: two carboxylate groups in μ2–η1:η1-bridging modes are connected to two Zn(II) atoms with a Zn⋯Zn separation of 3.0268(5) Å, whereas other carboxylate groups are coordinated in a monodentate mode (Scheme 1A). In 1, Zn(1) atoms are bridged by four carboxylates to generate paddlewheel-type [Zn2(COO)4] SUBs, each of which is surrounded by six tpta groups. Moreover, each tpta can link with three SUBs to yield a 2D layer (Fig. S2†). Topological analysis revealed that the 2D layer exhibits a planar kgd net with a short Schläfli symbol [43]2[46.66.83], in which [Zn2(COO)4] SBUs act as 6-connected nodes and tpta groups act as the 3-connected nodes (Fig. S3†). The [Me2NH2]+ cations in situ generated by the decomposition of DMF molecules are filled between adjacent layers via N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds (Fig. 1).
Scheme 1 Schematic view of coordination modes of tpta ligands.

Fig. 1 View of packing two layers showing interlayer [Me2NH2]+ cations.

Compound 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit consists of one crystallographically independent Cd(II) ion, one tpta, two [Me2NH2]+ cations, and one water molecule, as shown in Fig. S4.† Compared with 1, the smaller torsion angle of the two phenyls of tpta is 134.3°. Cd(II) displays a distorted octahedral geometry, coordinated by eight carboxylate oxygen atoms from four tpta groups. The Cd(1)–O distances are from 2.236(4) to 2.448(4) Å in the equatorial plane and 2.277(4) to 2.370(5) Å in the axial positions. The O–Cd(1)–O bond angles are in the range of 54.08(15)° and 170.36(17)°. The tpta group shows μ4-mode (Scheme 1B). 2 has a 3D microporous sra topological net with a short Schläfli symbol [42.63.8], possessing two different four-connected nodes (Fig. S5†).11 The calculated void volume is 736 Å3 per unit cell, which is filled by water molecules and [Me2NH2]+ cations. The [Me2NH2]+ cations play an important role in charge-compensation and space-filling. As shown in Fig. 2, the channel window is closed, which suggests that the encapsulated [Me2NH2]+ cations could not be facilely exchanged.


Fig. 2 View of 3D framework of 2 along the a-axis direction showing the encapsulated [Me2NH2]+ cations..

Compound 3 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P, and the asymmetric unit consists of three Cd(II) ions, two tpta, and one [Me2NH2]+ cation. Charge balance and thermal gravimetric analyses indicate that there are non-located [Me2NH2]+ cations, which is normally observed in porous MOFs. As shown in Fig. S6,† the Cd(1) ions coordinated with seven oxygen atoms to form a distorted capped octahedron. The Cd(1)–O bond lengths vary in the large range of 2.196(8)–2.826(6) Å, and the corresponding O–Cd(1)–O ranges from 53.4(2)° to 167.4(4)°.12 The Cd(2) site adopts an octahedral coordination geometry ligated by four O atoms from four different tpta, one terminal water molecule, and one O atom from DMF. The Cd(2) localizes special positions with site occupancy of 0.5. The Cd(2)–O bond lengths are within the range of 2.070(15)–2.407(7) Å. The cis- and trans-O–Cd(2)–O angles are in the range of 81.9(2)°–98.4(3)° and 173.1(2)°–176.4(4)°, respectively. Cd(3) atom is occupied by six O atoms from four individual tpta in a highly distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd(3)–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.184(8)–2.401(7) Å. The cis- and trans-O–Cd(3)–O are in the range of 54.6(2)–111.7(3)° and 152.4(2)–165.4(3)°, respectively, indicating a large amount of distortion from the octahedral coordination geometry. Compared with 1 and 2, tpta ligands in 3 adopt one μ4- and three different μ6-coordination modes, as shown in C, D, E and F in Scheme 1. Two carboxylate groups adopt μ2–η1:η2–chelating-bidentate and μ2-η1:η1–syn–syn-bidentate modes to connect two shared-vertex Cd atoms with Cd⋯Cd distances of 3.9812(12) and 4.1153(14) Å. 3 adopts a new (4,7)-connected topological network with the Schläfli symbol [42.52.72][44.5.6][44.62][49.53.69]2. It is interesting to note that there are four types of nodes. As shown in Fig. S7,† vertex-sharing Cd(1)–Cd(2)–Cd(3) trimers act as 7-connected nodes, and the tpta ligands in Scheme 1C–E act as different 4-connected nodes, but the tpta ligands in Scheme 1F act as linkers. The calculated void volume is 971.3 Å3 per unit cell, namely 31.7%, which is filled by [Me2NH2]+ cations and DMF molecules. The organic cations [Me2NH2]+ for charge-compensation and space-filling are fixed via N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds in the channels and holes. The channel window is closed, which indicates that the encapsulated [Me2NH2]+ cations may not be easily exchanged (Fig. 3).

Photoluminescence and sensing

As shown in Fig. 5a, the room temperature photoluminescence spectra of 1–3 as well as free ligand H4tpta have been investigated in the solid state (Fig. 4). H4tpta show broad luminescence upon excitation at 367 nm, with a central peak at 435 nm and a low energy peak at 462 nm, which can be assigned to π* → n and π* → π transitions.13 The lifetime of the main emission band for H4tpta is 27.19 μs. Compared with free H4tpta, both the low and high energy peaks in 1–3 are red-shifted by about 4–11 nm (Table S6†). The more important observation is that the relative intensities of the low and high energy peaks are inversed; the low energy peaks become the main emission bands. The luminescent quantum yields for 1–3 were determined by an integrating sphere, and they were found to be 31.05%, 36.42%, and 39.81%, respectively. The lifetime of the main emission bands for 1–3 were increased to 44.70, 64.63, and 73.33 μs, respectively, suggesting some characteristics of phosphorescence. The emissions of 1–3 are very similar to the free ligand, which may be mainly ligand-centered electronic transitions perturbed by metal ions.14 In this case, the red-shifted phenomenon and increase of lifetime for 1–3 may be possibly attributed to the increased stability of ligands within 1–3. In addition, the coordination environment of metal ions could affect the orientation of organic ligands, which results in the difference in emissions of 1–3.

Fig. 5 (a) Emission spectra of 1 from 4 to 298 K with λex = 374 nm; (b) the CIE coordinates (marked by the circles) for 1 at various temperature.

The variable temperature luminescence spectra of 1–3 from 4 K to 298 K are shown in Fig. 5a. The luminescence maxima and intensities of 1–3 showed a temperature-dependence.15 At 4 K, the emission spectrum of 1 displays a higher energy peak centered at λmax = 420 nm, and a lower energy peak at about 467 nm (Fig. 5a). The peak at higher energy undergoes a red shift to 430 nm at 100 K, 439 nm at 200 K and 443 nm at 298 K (Table S6†), but reduces in intensity with increasing temperature.16 Although the maximum of the lower energy peak does not shift, but the intensity progressively increases from 4 K to 298 K. With increasing temperatures, the relative intensities of emission bands at the lower and higher energy are gradually inversed. In the range of 4–200 K, the emission is dominated by a higher energy band, and the lower energy band is extremely weak, while the luminescence is dominated by the lower energy one at room temperature. The inverse emission maxima observed herein suggests different mechanisms. Based on the Commission International de L'Eclairage (CIE) chromaticity diagram, the corresponding CIE coordinates were calculated and the values are (0.1428, 0.1398), (0.1376, 0.1729), (0.1349, 0.1854), and (0.1355, 0.1875) for 4 K, 100 K, 200 K and 298 K, respectively (Fig. 5b). With increasing temperature, the chromaticity coordinates apparently changed from blue to greenish blue. As shown in Fig. S8a and 9a,† the emission spectra of 2 and 3 display similar changes with increased temperature: the higher energy emission peaks red-shift accompanied with decreased intensity; the lower energy emission peaks significantly increase in intensity and finally become the main emission bands; the chromaticity coordinates changed from blue to greenish blue from 4 K to 298 K; which may be ascribed to a decrease of chromophore aggregation with the increasing temperature (Fig. S8b–9b†).14

As mentioned above, [Me2NH2]+ groups are located in the channels of the three anionic frameworks, which provided us an opportunity to study ion-exchange and its effect on luminescence.17 Taking into account the important role of solvents, several experiments were then performed in different solvents to determine an optimized solvent for cation-exchange. As shown in Fig. 6, the suspension of 1 in pyridine remarkably enhances the phosphorescence intensity of 1, which may be due to the extended π-system of tpta as a linker and a significantly increased degree of conjugation by hydrogen bonding between pyridine and 1. Its emission spectrum displays a central peak at 436 nm with additional peaks at a higher energy of 414 nm and a lower energy of 466 nm, which is slightly different from the maximum emission at 476 nm for free H4tpta in pyridine. The difference in their emissions is probably due to the reaction of the proton of H4tpta with the nitrogen atom of pyridine, changing the ratio of the acid and conjugate base of H4tpta (Fig. S10a†). In contrast, other solvents such as water, alcohols (methanol, ethanol, isopropanol, n-butanol, and n-hexanol), acetone, amides, CH2Cl2 and acetonitrile have a less effect on the luminescence of 1.


Fig. 6 Phosphorescence emissions of 1 in different solvents.

With the optimized solvent established, the phosphorescent enhancement effects of various metal ions on 1 in a pyridine suspension were investigated under excitation at 378 nm. After the cation exchange, as shown in Fig. 7, Ca2+ could remarkably increase luminescence intensity considerably more than other metal ions, which may result from more effective intramolecular energy transfer.18 As is well known, a highly selective probe for Ca2+ gives a positive response rather than phosphorescent quenching, which is usually preferred to promote sensitivity.


Fig. 7 Phosphorescence response of 1 to metal ions showing the selectivity for Ca2+ in pyridine.

To further explore the availability of 1 to be a highly selective probe for Ca2+, the phosphorescent spectra of 1 in different concentrations of Ca2+ were examined (Fig. 8). Firstly, the highest concentration probed for Ca2+ was up to 16 mM. When the concentration of Ca2+ was increased again, the phosphorescence intensity of 1 in a pyridine suspension had no significant spectral changes. Secondly, the lowest concentration that could be detected was 1.3 × 10−3 mM. A good linear correlation (R2 = 0.974) exists between the phosphorescence efficiency and concentration of Ca2+ over the range of 1.3 × 10−3 to 4 × 10−3 mM in Fig. 9.


Fig. 8 The PL emission of 1 in the presence of various concentrations of Ca2+ in pyridine.

Fig. 9 Plot of the enhanced efficiency of 1 dispersed in pyridine at different concentrations of Ca2+. Emission intensities were selected at 414 nm. Inset: linear relation between the enhanced efficiency and the concentrations of Ca2+ in the range of 0 to 4 × 10−5 M.

As shown in Fig. S11,† the suspension of 2 in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene displays stronger emission with a peak maxima at 440 nm with additional peaks at a higher energy of 414 nm and a lower energy of 468 nm compared to other solvents, which may be due to the fact that large spin–orbit coupling of halogen atoms induce a heavy-atom effect to increase the intersystem crossing.19 In addition to 2, the suspension of 3 in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene also shows the strongest emission with a small red-shift (Fig. S12†). Both are comparable with the emissions of H4tpta in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene (Fig. S10b†), which may be due to ligand-centered electronic transitions.

The phosphorescent enhancement effects of various metal ions on 2 and 3 in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene were then investigated under excitation at 380 nm. As shown in Fig. S13† and S14, after cation-exchange, most metal ions give positive response. However, compared with the suspension of 1 in pyridine, the suspensions of 2 and 3 in 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene exhibit poor selectivity. This phenomenon might be explained as follows: (i) [Me2NH2]+ cations were encapsulated in the closed channel window in 2 and 3, and thus could not be efficiently exchanged. (ii) 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene as the solvent disturbs the phosphorescence response of 2 and 3.

Thermal analyses

The thermogravimetric analyses of polycrystalline samples of 1–3 were performed in air at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The thermal stability of 1–3 was further assessed by VT-XRPD. As shown in Fig. S15–18,† the different coordination networks of 1–3 result in different thermal stabilities. For 1, as shown in the VT-XRPD (Fig. S16†), the main network of compound 1 may be stable up to 250 °C, which illustrates good thermal stability. The first weight loss of 59.2% in the temperature range of 260–520 °C is in agreement with the removal of [Me2NH2]+ cations and a partial tpta ligand, accompanied by the decomposition of the framework. The continuous loss arises upon further heating; a stable residue is not formed up to 1100 °C. For 2, the framework remains intact up to 250 °C in the VT-XRPD (Fig. S17†). However, as shown in Fig. S15, the weight loss begins at 180 °C. The continuous weight loss of 73.50% in the range of 190–580 °C corresponds to the removal of tpta ligand and [Me2NH2]+ cations, which is followed by the decomposition of the framework. The final residue of 19.8% (calcd 20.5%) for 2 is close to the percentage of CdO. For 3, with increased temperature, as shown in Fig. S18, 2θ values of the corresponding diffraction peaks are slightly lowered (∼0.14°) than those of the one simulated at 298 K, implying that the crystal structure varies a little bit at different temperatures. The TG analysis displays the first weight loss of 28.3% (calcd 27.5%) within the range of 40–360 °C corresponding to the removal of one water, three DMF molecules and three [Me2NH2]+ cations, which is followed by the decomposition of the framework. The final residue of 21.3% is in agreement with that for CdO (calcd 21.5%).

Conclusions

Hydrothermal in situ decomposition of DMF generated three [NH2(CH3)2]+ cation templated zinc and cadmium terphenyl-3,2′′,5′′,3′-tetracarboxylate anionic frameworks. With increased temperature from 4 K to 298 K, the emission spectra of 1–3 show temperature-dependence; emission maxima are gradually inversed from the higher energy band to the lower energy band. Correspondingly, CIE coordinates are changed from blue to greenish blue. Interestingly, the dispersed suspension of 1 in pyridine exhibits strong phosphorescence emission, which can selectively probe traces of Ca2+ at ppm level. The selective probe indicates that [Me2NH2]+ cations between the layers could be exhanged by Ca2+ ions, which have a significant effect on the phosphorescence intensity. The high selectivity and sensitivity of the phosphorescence response of 1 to metal ions shows that it could be used as an efficient phosphorescence sensor.
