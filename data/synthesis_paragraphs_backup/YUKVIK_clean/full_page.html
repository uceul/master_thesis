Abstract

We report the synthesis, crystal structures, and spectral, thermal, and magnetic properties of a family of metal–organic perovskite ABX3, [C(NH2)3][MII(HCOO)3], in which A=C(NH2)3 is guanidinium, B=M is a divalent metal ion (Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, or Zn), and X is the formate HCOO−. The compounds could be synthesized by either diffusion or hydrothermal methods from water or water‐rich solutions depending on the metal. The five members (Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, and Zn) are isostructural and crystallize in the orthorhombic space group Pnna, while the Cu member in Pna21. In the perovskite structures, the octahedrally coordinated metal ions are connected by the anti–anti formate bridges, thus forming the anionic NaCl‐type [M(HCOO)3]− frameworks, with the guanidinium in the nearly cubic cavities of the frameworks. The Jahn–Teller effect of Cu2+ results in a distorted anionic Cu–formate framework that can be regarded as Cu–formate chains through short basal CuO bonds linked by the long axial CuO bonds. These materials show higher thermal stability than other metal–organic perovskite series of [AmineH][M(HCOO)3] templated by the organic monoammonium cations (AmineH+) as a result of the stronger hydrogen bonding between guanidinium and the formate of the framework. A magnetic study revealed that the five magnetic members (except Zn) display spin‐canted antiferromagnetism, with a Néel temperature of 8.8 (Mn), 10.0 (Fe), 14.2 (Co), 34.2 (Ni), and 4.6 K (Cu). In addition to the general spin‐canted antiferromagnetism, the Fe compound shows two isothermal transformations (a spin‐flop and a spin‐flip to the paramagnetic phase) within 50 kOe. The Co member possesses quite a large canting angle. The Cu member is a magnetic system with low dimensional character and shows slow magnetic relaxation that probably results from the domain dynamics.

Introduction

In the past decade, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), the crystalline hybrid materials containing inorganic and organic components and possessing extended structures with coordination bonds, have become very attractive in the study of condensed matter.1–10 Because of their hybrid characteristics, these materials benefit from the characteristics of both inorganic and organic building blocks. Consequently, they can possess new and interesting properties or functionalities, which originate from this inorganic–organic combination.1 Although MOFs can have many other potentials and interesting properties,2 magnetism is one major area of interest with great potential.3 Especially if the paramagnetic metal sites are linked by short ligands (such as oxygen anion, cyanide, and oxalate) to form infinite arrays (1D, 2D, or 3D) in the frameworks, the structural basis for the significant magnetic interaction or coupling between the metal sites, the spin carriers, is then provided.1a One main advantage of magnetic MOFs lies in the possibility and opportunity to tune or to control the nature of the magnetic coupling within the materials.3–6 The magnetic coupling itself can be ferromagnetic (FO) or antiferromagnetic (AF), depending on the spin carriers and the details of the coupling path (through the short ligand) between them. The organization and topology of the magnetic couplings through the space could be further tuned or controlled if the starting building blocks, metal ions, short ligands, co‐ligands, templates, and so on, are carefully chosen and properly assembled into the solid state.3a For example, isolated clusters such as single‐molecule magnets (SMMs),4 1D magnetic chains like single‐chain magnets (SCM),5 and 2D magnetic layers,6 which often exhibit anisotropy of the coupling along different dimensionalities, could be archived by the employment of carefully selected coligands, endo or exo, in cooperation with the short ligands. Magnetic MOFs have also afforded materials with multiple functionalities7–9 such as conductivity and magnetism,7 magnetic and optical properties,8 and porosity and magnetism9 when the related functional building blocks or structural features are incorporated. These multifunctional materials currently attract considerable attention because they provide a good approach to the creation of novel materials with dual or multiple functionalities and a possible interplay between different functions. Besides, in the view of structural chemistry, the structures and topologies of MOFs are often analogous to known inorganic structures such as diamond, rutile, CsCl, and perovskite (or NaCl‐type).1a, 3c, 10

In this regard, we have recently investigated the magnetic metal–formate frameworks of 3d divalent metals systematically, by using cations of protonated amines as templates, and quite interesting results have been achieved.3a, 11–14 The formato anion HCOO− has been rarely used as a short ligand, in contrast to those such as cyanide, azide, oxalate, and even acetate, in the construction of magnetic MOFs.3–9 Some detailed research works were reported 30–40 years ago,15–21 typically for the isostructural dihydrate metal–formate series, M(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O (M=Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, and Cu).15–19 We are interested in the formato ligand, because 1) its small stereo effect is beneficial for the formation of extended structures thus MOFs; 2) it can act as a three‐ or single‐atom connector, thus providing significant magnetic coupling (FO or AF) between magnetic metal sites; and 3) it can adopt multiple bridging modes such as syn–syn, anti–anti, syn–syn/anti, and so on.3a, 11–14 These characteristics facilitate the formation of MOFs of different structures and consequently different magnetic properties. By employing protonated amine cations of different sizes and charges, several new series of metal–formate frameworks, the structures of which depend on the size, shape, and charge of the template cations, have been successfully obtained. Surprisingly, it was observed that the smallest ammonium NH4+ resulted in the chiral magnetic salts of [NH4][M(HCOO)3] from the achiral starting components, showing a framework of rarely observed 49⋅66 topology in which the octahedral metal sites are connected by anti–anti formato ions and the NH4+ cations located in the channels.11 The mid‐sized organic monoammonium cations (AmineH+=CH3NH3+, (CH3)2NH2+, CH3CH2NH3+, and (CH2)3NH2+), with 2–4 non‐hydrogen atoms, led to a series of metal–organic perovskites of [AmineH][M(HCOO)3] with NaCl‐type frameworks, [M(HCOO)3]−, of 412⋅63 topology.12 The metal–metal linkages also take place through anti–anti formates and the ammonium cations sit in the cubic cavities of the framework. The employment of more bulky AmineH+ cations such as (CH3CH2)3NH+, however, resulted in the porous magnet family of [M3(HCOO)6] with a diamond topology in which formate adopts a syn–syn/anti mode to link metal ions.13 We have very recently expanded our study into protonated diamines and polyamines,14 parallel to the study of metal phosphates and oxometalates, and have observed that, for example, the protonated N,N′‐dimethylethylenediamine (dmenH22+) has templated the formation of novel binodal metal–formate frameworks, [dmenH2][M2(HCOO)6] of (412⋅63)(49⋅66) topology, which is the first metal–organic framework analogous to the minerals niccolite (NiAs) or colquiriite (LiCaAlF6).14a For tri‐ or tetraamines, metal–formate frameworks of (412⋅63)(49⋅66)n (n=2, 3) topologies14b have been obtained. These achievements indicated the significant malleability and adaptability of metal–formate frameworks to conform to the templates. These materials displayed promising magnetism. For example, the porous [M3(HCOO)6] (M=Mn and Fe) showed guest‐dependent magnetism,13a–13d and the critical temperatures of ammonium‐templated Ni–formate frameworks can be as high as 30 K.11, 12b

In this work, we present the metal–organic perovskite series [C(NH2)3][M(HCOO)3], in which the guanidinium cation, C(NH2)3+, hereafter denoted Gua+, is the cationic template. Gua+ is a unique, outstanding hydrogen‐bonding cationic component and possesses a powerful structure‐directing capability in constructing serendipitous MOFs22 or predictable organic architectures.23 The six hydrogen atoms of Gua+ can be involved in the formation of three pairs of hydrogen bonding to O‐Z‐O entities (Z=B, C, S, Cr, Mo, etc).22 Some guanidinium salts such as guanidinium aluminum sulfate hexahydrate are known as ferroelectrics.24 The six compounds, [C(NH2)3][M(HCOO)3] (M=Mn (1 Mn), Fe (2 Fe), Co (3 Co), Ni (4 Ni), Cu (5 Cu), and Zn (6 Zn)), have been successfully synthesized and characterized. They possess a similar perovskite‐like structure, in which the octahedral metal ions are connected by anti–anti formato ligands to form the anionic NaCl‐type framework, and the Gua+ cations are located in the nearly cubic cavities of the framework. The magnetic members 1 Mn to 5 Cu display long‐range ordering (LRO) of spin‐canted AF (or weak ferromagnetism, WF) with the critical temperatures of 8.8 (1 Mn), 10.0 (2 Fe), 14.2 (3 Co), 34.2 (4 Ni), and 4.6 K (5 Cu). In addition, 2 Fe shows both spin‐flopped and spin‐flipped to paramagnetic phases within 50 kOe, 3 Co has a quite large canting angle, and an abnormal slow magnetic relaxation is observed for 5 Cu. Thermal and spectroscopic properties are also investigated.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis: As mentioned before, in the last few years, we have carried out a systematic investigation on the formation of 3d divalent metal–formate frameworks templated by cations of protonated amines.3a, 11–14 While the size, shape, and charge of the protonated amines were considered to be the key factors in controlling the structures of the final metal–formate frameworks, the use of a noncoordinating solvent such as methanol was another important factor to obtain these metal formates without coligands (such as coordinating water). We observed that when a small amount of water is present, even water absorbed from humid air during crystallization, the product is very easily contaminated by the well‐known metal–formate dihydrate, M(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O, even the dihydrate salts. In contrast to these observations, we found that for the present series with Gua+ the use of anhydrous methanol always resulted in uncharacterized precipitates, thus we turned to the synthetic procedure using mixed solvents, methanol–water, or just water only. It was surprising to see that the compounds (except 6 Zn, see the Experimental Section) could be successfully obtained from water or water‐rich media. This could be attributed to the strong hydrogen‐bonding interaction between the templating cation (Gua+) and the anionic metal–formate framework (see the later discussion on the structures), which resulted in the formation of the series of compounds in pure phase, as proved by powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD; Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). For the previously explored organic ammonium cations,12, 14 it is clear that the above hydrogen‐bonding interactions were not so strong because half or more of them are the weak CH⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen‐bonding interactions. It is expected, of course, in methanol solvent, that the compounds could be synthesized by carefully applied slow diffusion, as proved by the preparation of 6 Zn (see the Experimental Section) by means of slow diffusion using an H‐shaped tube. Compound 4 Ni was prepared by a hydrothermal reaction. We had applied more than ten conventional attempts to synthesize 4 Ni at room temperature by choosing different solvents, starting chemicals, and methods; the products were always green precipitates, or small green hemispherical particles, which were amorphous as the small hemisphere particle did not show any diffraction spots or lines on our KappaCCD diffractometer, even when using a very long exposure time. Why room temperature reactions did not work for preparing crystalline products is still not clear. We think the very low solubility of 4 Ni at room temperature, similar to that observed for the Ni members of other metal–formate series,11–14 could be the reason, and the hydrothermal reaction might provide the suitable conditions for crystal growth of 4 Ni. It is also noted that several metal–formate frameworks can be obtained under solvothermal conditions12b, 25 although they could be prepared through mild solution chemistry methods at ambient temperature by our group.11–14

Crystal structures: The six compounds possess NaCl‐type anionic metal–formate frameworks of [M(HCOO)3]− with Gua+ cations located in the nearly cubic cavities, or the structures are all perovskite‐like (Figures 1 and 2). It could be expected that Gua+, a cationic template with four non‐hydrogen atoms, could result in these kinds of metal–formate frameworks, which are similar to the previously reported—mainly by us[12]—compounds using protonated organic amines of 2–4 non‐hydrogen atoms. Although the compounds of this Gua+‐templated series emphasize the size effects of the template cations on the induced structures of the metal–formate framework, they add more members to the metal–organic perovskite family of metal formate. Only one divalent Fe compound has been reported, by Hagen et al., very recently.26

The five compounds, 1 Mn to 4 Ni, and 6 Zn, are isomorphs (Figure S1 in the Supporting Information), and they crystallized in the orthorhombic space group Pnna (Table 1). We selected 1 Mn as the representative structure for discussion. In the structure (Figure 1) the unique octahedral Mn2+ ion is located on the 2‐fold axis (x, 1/4, 1/4; Wyckoff site 4d). Each Mn2+ ion, as a six‐connected node, connects to six neighboring metal ions through six bridging formate ligands of anti–anti mode, thus forming the NaCl‐type anionic metal–formate framework with the topology of 412⋅63.10, 12, 27 The MnO6 octahedron is distorted, with MnO bond lengths of 2.161–2.190 Å, cis O‐Mn‐O angles of 81.65–106.12°, and trans O‐Mn‐O angles of 167.77–177.79° (Table 2). These structural parameters are comparable to those of the [AmineH][Mn(HCOO)3] series reported previously.12a The framework has approximately cubic cavities occupied by the template Gua+ cations (Figure 1a). The cavity has eight long edges of 6.219 Å (the MnOCOMn linkages along the ac plane) and 4 slightly shorter ones of 5.989 Å (the MnOCOMn linkages along the b direction), and these are the M⋅⋅⋅M distances through bridging HCOO− (Table 3). The cavity volume of 80 Å3, calculated by PLATON software,28 lies in the range of 57–97 Å3 observed for the [AmineH][Mn(HCOO)3] series.12a In the cavity, the planar Gua+ cation has its molecular plane nearly parallel to the two body‐diagonal lines but normal to the third one. One notable feature of the structure is that the Gua+ cation, as good a hydrogen‐bonding donor as expected, forms six conventional NH⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen bonds to the anionic metal–formate framework. The pattern of hydrogen bonding is typically observed for many guanidinium–carboxylato assemblies.22, 29 The hydrogen‐bonding geometries are N⋅⋅⋅O 2.95–2.99 Å, thus indicating different hydrogen‐bonding strengths, and NH⋅⋅⋅O angles of 160–169° (Table 3). The strong NH⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen‐bonding interactions provided higher thermal stability (see below) for the compounds of this series than other perovskite metal formates in which weak CH⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen bonding was involved between the template and framework, and allowed for the success in preparing these compounds from a water medium (see above).

When running through the series, lattice parameters, MO bond lengths, M⋅⋅⋅M distances, and N⋅⋅⋅O distances of NH⋅⋅⋅O hydrogen bonds decrease gradually, with 6 Zn close to 3 Co (Tables 2 and 3), and these are in good agreement with the ionic radii of the divalent metal ions30 and the fact that the radius of Zn2+ is almost the same as that of Co2+. With the cell volumes reduced from 924.7 Å3 for 1 Mn to 849.5 Å3 for 4 Ni, the cavity sizes decrease from 80 Å3 for 1 Mn to 66 Å3 for 4 Ni. An interesting observation is that the CO bond lengths of HCOO− increase slightly upon the shortening of MO bond lengths (Table 2), and this led to the shift of the IR spectroscopic absorption bands regarding COO groups through the series (see below and Table 4).

The Cu member, 5 Cu, possesses a similar structure to the other members but belongs to the different space group of Pna21 (Table 1 and Figure 2). Cu2+ is a Jahn–Teller ion, thus it displays 4+2 elongated octahedral coordination geometry, with four short equatorial CuO bond lengths of 1.953–2.003 Å and two long apical CuO bond lengths of 2.360 and 2.382 Å. It could be expected that the Jahn–Teller effect of Cu2+ resulted in the difference of crystal structure of 5 Cu from the other five isomorphic members in the series. If the long apical CuO bonds are involved, the Cu–formate framework is NaCl‐type. However, if the long CuO bonds are considered as the secondary bonds from the viewpoint of magnetism, the framework is composed of Cu–formate chains (Figure 2b and c) linked by these secondary CuO bonds, and within the chain, the square‐planar Cu ions are connected by anti–anti HCOO− and the neighboring CuO4 square planes are tilted with a dihedral angle of approximately 20°. The unique intrachain Cu⋅⋅⋅Cu distance is 5.675 Å, and the interchain ones are 6.199 and 6.219 Å (Table 3). The chains run along the c direction, and the adjacent chains have their CuO4 square planes nearly normal to each other (Figure 2c). One perovskite Cu formate, [CH3NH3][Cu(HCOO)3], was reported in 199031a and another one [(CH3)2NH2][Cu(HCOO)3] in 200631b as an impurity of a solvothermal reaction, but they did not receive much attention. We have obtained several perovskite Cu formates of [AmineH][Cu(HCOO)3] as well as some [AmineH][Zn(HCOO)3] compounds,12a analogously to the reported members of Mn, Co, and Ni, and these results will be reported shortly. The fact that 5 Cu belongs to the polar space group Pna21 might be of special interest for ferroelectricity or multiferroics in light of its magnetism, discussed below. In fact, a very recent communication32 reported the antiferroelectricity for the perovskite Zn formate of [(CH3)2NH2][Zn(HCOO)3], thereby indicating the opportunity to find multiferroics in these materials of ammonium metal formates.1a

Finally, it is noteworthy that due to the bending characteristics of anti–anti formato bridges in its coordination orientation, the neighboring MO6 coordination octahedra are tilted from one another. This structural feature, together with noncentrosymmetry between the magnetic M2+ ions bridged by HCOO−, allow the occurrence of Dzyaloshinsky–Moriya (DM) interaction or antisymmetric exchange33 to give rise to spin canting for 1 Mn to 5 Cu, the magnetic properties of will be discussed in detail below. The spin canting has been observed for many metal–formate frameworks reported up to now.11–21

Thermal properties and IR and UV/Vis spectra: The thermal stabilities of the six compounds were investigated by combined thermogravimetric analysis and differential scanning calorimetry (TGA/DSC) runs (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information). In air, the four compounds from 1 Mn to 4 Ni displayed one weight loss on their TGA traces, but their DSC traces showed that the thermal processes might include two or more steps that merged together. The thermal decomposition of Mn, Fe, and Co compounds started from a small endothermic step followed by one or two large exothermic ones, whereas for 4 Ni only one exothermic step was observed. The energy requirements/releases for the endo‐/exothermic processes were 130/630 (1Mn), 20/136 (2 Fe), 230/700 (3 Co), and not observed/850 kJmol−1 (4 Ni); the fact that 2 Fe had much smaller thermal effects than others might be due to the instrumental setting. The decomposition occurred at approximately 280, 250, 280, and 330 °C for 1 Mn, 2 Fe, 3 Co, and 4 Ni, respectively. The decomposition temperatures for the Mn, Co, and Ni members are significantly higher than those of the related compounds in other [AmineH][M(HCOO)3] series thermally decomposed below 230 °C, even under inert atmosphere,11, 12 but similar to those of the more robust, porous, diamond‐framework compounds of [M3(HCOO)6] (ca. 270 °C).13 The higher thermal stability of the compounds here obviously resulted from the strong hydrogen‐bonding interaction between the Gua+ and the metal–formate framework within the materials (see above). The experimental residues after pyrolysis were 31.4, 33.3, 32.5, and 29.0 % for 1 Mn, 2 Fe, 3 Co, and 4 Ni, respectively, which is in agreement with the calculated values of 31.6, 31.8, 32.6, and 29.4 % based on Mn2O3, Fe2O3, Co2O3, and NiO. The Cu and Zn members, 5 Cu and 6 Zn, mainly showed two distinguishable steps of thermal decomposition, which occurred at 170 and 340 °C for 5 Cu, and 230 and 650 °C for 6 Zn. For 5 Cu, the two steps were all exothermic (520 and 660 kJmol−1), but for 6 Zn the first one was endothermic (200 kJmol−1) and the second exothermic (490 kJmol−1). The intermediate residues were approximately 50 %, but identification of the intermediate phases will need more characterization, such as the study of the thermal evolution of powder X‐ray diffraction. The final residues were 29.5 % for 5 Cu and 31.3 % for 6 Zn, and they agree with the calculated values of 30.8 and 31.2 % based on CuO and ZnO.

The IR spectra of the six compounds are fairly similar, albeit with some slight differences from one another (Figure S3a in the Supporting Information). This is expected given the fact that the six structures are closely similar. The IR absorption bands and their assignments are given in Table 4, and they are characteristic for the NH2 groups of the Gua+ cation and the HCOO− group.11, 12a, 34, 35 Typically for 1 Mn, the double IR bands at 3366 and 3317 cm−1 of NH stretching modes (the band at 3167 cm−1 is another NH stretching mode34a), and those at 1693 and 1677 cm−1 for NH bending modes indicate that the NH2 groups are involved in the formation of hydrogen bonds of different strengths,34b and this agrees with observations made of the structures. The IR bands for HCOO− groups coincide with those previously reported,11, 12, 35 and show some regular shift of band positions running through the series. For example, the OCO symmetric stretching bands (2) showed the sequence of 1 Mn>2 Fe>3 Co≈6 Zn>4 Ni>5 Cu in wave numbers, whereas the OCO symmetric deformation bands (3) displayed the sequence of 1 Mn<2 Fe≈6 Zn<3 Co<4 Ni<5 Cu (one of the two bands). This could be attributed to the sequence of MO bonding strengths, that is, Mn<Fe<Co<Ni<Cu, with ZnO in between MnO and CoO within the series and the accompanied weakened CO bonds as revealed by slightly but gradually elongated CO bond lengths (Table 2). Finally, the observed split of the band of OCO symmetric deformation (829 and 804 cm−1) for 5 Cu is due to the fact that the Jahn–Teller Cu2+ ion resulted in two significantly different kinds of CuO bonds, apical and equatorial ones.

The plots of the temperature dependence of the magnetic moments (represented in the form χT versus T) of 1 Mn to 4 Ni, measured under applied magnetic fields of 100 Oe, are shown in Figure 3. The four compounds displayed mainly AF characteristics. In the high‐temperature region they behaved similarly. The χT values at 300 K of 4.33 (1 Mn), 3.44 (2 Fe), 3.19 (3 Co), and 1.24 cm3 K mol−1 (4 Ni) , respectively, were as expected for the divalent metal ions.37 Upon cooling from 300 to 50 K, the χT values decreased gradually. The susceptibility data in the high‐temperature region, in other words, data above 15 (1 Mn), 40 (2 Fe), and 50 K (3 Co and 4 Ni), fitted the Curie–Weiss law well (Figure S4 in the Supporting Information), thereby resulting in Curie [cm3 K mol−1]/Weiss [K] constants of: 4.53/−14.5 (1 Mn), 3.63/−16.9 (2 Fe), 3.76/−53.3 (3 Co), and 1.53/−71.7 (4 Ni). From the Curie constants, the derived Landé g factors of 2.047, 2.20, 2.837, and 2.477 for 1 Mn, 2 Fe, 3 Co, and 4 Ni, respectively, are normal for isotropic Mn2+ and anisotropic Fe2+, Co2+, and Ni2+.37 The significant negative Weiss temperatures correspond to AF exchange interactions between the nearest‐neighbor metal ions, and this is expected for an anti–anti mode of coordination by the formate bridges.11–21 In the low‐temperature region, the χT values of the four compounds went down to minima first, then rose up quickly to maxima, and finally went down again upon cooling, thus suggesting the presence of spontaneous magnetization due to the magnetic LRO. The sequence of the magnetic‐ordering temperature was 1 Mn<2 Fe<3 Co<4 Ni. There were different details in the temperature dependence of χT. Compound 1 Mn showed a minimum of 1.51 cm3 K mol−1 at 9 K and a maximum of 2.77 cm3 K mol−1 at 7.4 K, and finally to 1.02 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The transition at 9 K was very sharp. For 2 Fe, the minimum of 2.00 cm3 K mol−1 at 12 K, the maximum 5.38 cm3 K mol−1 at 8.5 K and 1.50 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K were observed. The transition at 12 K was less sharp. For 3 Co, the χT value went to a minimum of 1.45 cm3 K mol−1 around 22 K, then rose smoothly to a large maximum of 217.2 cm3 K mol−1 at 11.5 K, followed by a decrease to 44.3 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The large χT value of the maximum observed for 3 Co is quite unusual for Co–formate compounds.11a, 12b, 13e, 14, 15i, 17 The χT value of 4 Ni first reached a minimum of 0.48 cm3 K mol−1 at 35 K, then displayed a sharp rise to a maximum of 19.4 cm3 K mol−1 at 27 K, and decreased again to 1.80 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The behavior for 4 Ni is similar to that of 1 Mn. These differences are due to the different magnetic anisotropies of the metal ions. However, under an applied field of 10 kOe, the sharp rises after the minima were suppressed, and for 1 Mn and 2 Fe, only kinks in χT versus T plots were observed at 9 and 11 K, respectively (Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). This suppression by the high field is normal for weak ferromagnets. All these observations characterize the four compounds as weak ferromagnets, similar to the known metal–formate perovskites.12

To confirm the conclusion above for the four compounds, we further measured the zero‐field‐cooling (ZFC) and field‐cooling (FC) magnetizations under a low magnetic field (Figure 4), alternating current (ac) susceptibilities at different frequencies (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information), and isothermal magnetizations (Figure 5, Figure S7) around the phase transitions. The ZFC/FC data under the 10 Oe field (Figure 4) better define the occurrence of LRO of WF or spin‐canted AF. In addition, the different behaviors between ZFC and FC and their bifurcations give one a feel for the hardness of the magnets: soft for 1 Mn, hard for 2 Fe and 3 Co, and intermediate for 4 Ni. The critical Néel temperatures (TNs), determined by the positions of the negative peak on the derivative dM/dT (M=magnetization) of FC data, are 8.8 (1 Mn), 10.0 (2 Fe), 14.2 (3 Co), and 34.2 K (4 Ni). Whereas 1 Mn, 2 Fe, and 4 Ni showed small spontaneous magnetizations of tens of cm3 G mol−1, thereby suggesting small spin‐canting angles within these materials, 3 Co has quite large spontaneous magnetization up to 460 cm3 G mol−1, which is consistent with the large χT values previously described, and this indicated a large spin‐canting angle in 3 Co.

The temperature dependences of the ac susceptibilities obtained under zero direct current (dc) field (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information) for the four compounds display peaks in both the in‐phase (χ′) and out‐of‐phase (χ′′) responses. For 1 Mn, frequency‐independent peaks in χ′ and χ′′ were observed at 8.9 K (Figure S6a). They were sharp and more easily observed for a measurement at 10 Hz with fine steps of 0.025 K, and the peaks are located at 8.88(3) K. The peak of χ′ defines the Néel transition temperature. Compound 2 Fe exhibits more pronounced peaks at 10.0 K in both χ′ and χ′′, thereby coinciding with the TN obtained in ZFC/FC measurements. Although the peaks were frequency‐independent, their heights became lower for higher frequencies (Figure S6b), thereby suggesting the presence of domains. The χ′ and χ′′ peaks of 3 Co were around 14 K, and they were slightly frequency‐dependent (Figure S6c), but the onset is at the same temperature. For χ′ signals, the peak shifted from 13.9 to 14.1 K when the frequency varied from 0.32 to 1500 Hz; for χ′′ signals, the peak shifted from 13.0 to 13.7 K. The small peak shift is characterized by a small parameter of ϕ[38]=(ΔTP/TP)/Δ(log f)=0.006, in which TP was the peak temperature of χ′ and f the ac frequency. A possible reason for the frequency dependence is the movement of the domain wall for this weak ferromagnet with quite a large spontaneous magnetization.39 This behavior has seemingly not been observed in other Co–formate systems11a, 12b, 13e, 14, 15i, 17 but has been observed in a weak ferromagnet of a Co–azide salt40 that shows a large spin‐canting angle. Compound 4 Ni displayed weak ac responses, but the sharp and frequency‐independent peak at 34.3 K was clear for χ′ (Figure S6d). In χ′, two extra weak flat peaks at 18 and 13 K were observed; these need further investigation. Therefore, the ac studies confirmed the LRO of spin‐canted AF within these materials.

The isothermal magnetizations at 2.0 K for 1 Mn to 4 Ni (Figure 5) displayed hysteresis loops, albeit with linear dependence at high fields. The coercive fields (HCs) and remanent magnetization (MR) (Table 5) were indicative of weak ferromagnetism. Compounds 1 Mn and 2 Fe were soft, with HC/MR being 180 Oe/0.0068 Nβ (1 Mn) and 170 Oe/0.0098 Nβ (2 Fe), respectively. Compounds 3 Co and 4 Ni were quite hard, with HC and MR values of 1740 Oe and 0.33 Nβ, and 4200 Oe and 0.018 Nβ, respectively. These observations are expected because Co2+ and Ni2+ ions are more anisotropic than Mn2+ ions.37 Surprisingly, the Fe2+ compound is a soft magnet. From the remanent magnetizations and the g factors previously derived from Curie constants of susceptibility data under 100 Oe, the canting angles α, related to MR and saturated magnetizations (MS (units Nβ)=gS) by sin α=MR/MS, were estimated to be 0.076° (1 Mn), 0.26° (2 Fe), 4.5° (3 Co), and 0.42° (4 Ni), when S= 5/2, 2, 3/2, and 1 are assumed for 1 Mn, 2 Fe, 3 Co, and 4 Ni, respectively. That 3 Co displayed a quite large canting angle is an unusual observation. At 50 kOe the magnetizations were 1.56 (1 Mn), 1.59 (2 Fe), 0.65 (3 Co), and 0.12 Nβ (4 Ni), respectively, thus confirming the AF LRO again. The spin‐flop transition (AF‐SP) occurred for 1 Mn and 2 Fe. At 2 K, for 1 Mn the field for spin‐flop (HSP) was 7.1 kO, determined by the peak position of dM/dH, and 7.2 kOe by the measurement of the field dependence of the ac susceptibility with a frequency of 10 Hz (Figure S7a in the Supporting Information). For 2 Fe, the HSP was approximately 10 kOe, though not well defined (Figure S7b). Interestingly, a quite low critical field (HSP−P) of 29 kOe for the transition from flopped to the paramagnetic phase (SP‐P) was observed for 2 Fe, thus providing a rare example for the observation of AF‐SP and SP‐P in quite a low‐field region, and this merits further investigation. For 3 Co and 4 Ni, the spin‐flop transition did not occur below 50 kOe.

Several known magnetic metal–formate series have been discovered11–21 and their structures and magnetic properties reported for divalent 3d metal ions up to now. It is interesting to make a comparison with regards to magnetism and structural correlations. Those series with anti–anti formate as the unique linkages are listed in Table 6. For these compounds, metal ions are usually octahedral, but various structural types and topologies of the metal–formate frameworks have been observed. The isostructural dihydrate metal formate series, M(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O, a representative15–19 of metal formates studied in earlier days, possess a 3D framework structure consisting of layers of a (4,4) net of M(HCOO)2 linked by trans‐[M(H2O)4(HCOO)2] units, in which the metal–metal linkages are all through anti–anti formates. The materials predominantly exhibit long‐range spin‐canted AF ordering, with quite low TN values, and both the intralayer M⋅⋅⋅M coupling strengths through anti–anti formate show the trend of Mn<Fe<Co<Ni (Table 6). Most members of the series could be considered to be 2D magnetically ordered layers of a (4,4) net of M(HCOO)2, whereas the interlayer [M(H2O)4(HCOO)2] units remained paramagnetic, even below TN. The series of [M(HCOO)2L2], in which L is formamide20 or urea,21 possess structural 2D layers of a (4,4) net of M(HCOO)2 linked by interlayer hydrogen bonds through L, and show WF with quite a similar TN and M⋅⋅⋅M coupling to the M(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O series. As mentioned before, we have studied several spin‐canted AF or WF series of 3D anionic metal–formate frameworks templated by various ammonium cations, [NH4][M(HCOO)3] with 49⋅66 topology,11 metal–organic perovskites12 of [AmineH][M(HCOO)3] ([AmineH]+=monoammonium of 2–4 non‐hydrogen atoms, including Gua+ reported in this work) with 412⋅63 topology, and [dmenH2][M2(HCOO)6]2 ([dmenH2]2+=protonated N,N′‐dimethylethylenediamine) with (412⋅63)(49⋅66) topology.14a Although the framework structures indicate the malleability and adaptability of metal–formate frameworks in conforming to the templates, the metal–metal linkages are all anti–anti formates. With similar M⋅⋅⋅M couplings in strength, the TN values are in the same sequence (Mn<Fe<Co<Ni) and two times higher than those of the related 2D metal–formate frameworks, and this could be expected by the difference in dimensionality (3D versus 2D). The TN values for the 3D frameworks of the same kind of metal are quite similar, in other words, approximately 8, 10, 15, and 30 K for Mn, Fe, Co, and Ni members, though the topologies of the frameworks are different. We have observed that metal–formate frameworks of (412⋅63)(49⋅66)n (n=2, 3) topologies templated by protonated tri‐ or tetraamines show similar magnetism, and these will be reported later.14b It is also noteworthy that the Fe and Cu members are still few in number, and the studies thereof are in progress. Several other kinds of metal–formate frameworks were also reported, such as the porous diamond family13 of [M3(HCOO)6], listed also in Table 6 for the purpose of comparison, anhydrous Mn(HCOO)2 with a 3D‐framework,42 α‐Cu(HCOO)2 with a 3D‐framework, and β‐Cu(HCOO)2 with an infinite chain.43 The metal ions in these compounds are not simply linked by anti–anti formate, and therefore more complicated magnetic behaviors have been observed; a detailed discussion is beyond the scope of this paper. Several metal–formate frameworks with ditopic coligands, such as 4,4′‐bipyridine, possess similar metal–metal coupling strength through the anti–anti formate bridges.44

We now turn to the magnetism of 5 Cu. The Jahn–Teller Cu2+ ions afforded a anionic Cu–formate framework that consisted of CuOCHOCu chains linked by the long axial CuOformate bonds, whereas within the chain the adjacent Cu2+ ions are connected by bridging anti–anti HCOO− through the short, basal CuOformate bonds (Figure 2b and c, Table 2). It was therefore expected that the material will display low‐dimensional magnetism and this was indeed observed. As depicted in Figure 6, in an applied field of 2 kOe, the temperature evolution of the susceptibility displayed a broad maximum (0.00418 cm3 mol−1) around 45 K, a minimum (0.00353 cm3 mol−1) at 11 K, and further a steep increase below 6 K, to 0.0242 cm3 mol−1 at 2 K. It is clear that the broad maximum around 45 K corresponds to the strong intrachain AF coupling through the anti–anti HCOO− bridge within the Cu–formate chain, whereas the rise below 6 K indicated a 3D LRO of spin‐canted AF. At 300 K, the χT value was 0.466 cm3 K mol−1, as expected for the Cu2+ ion.37 The χT continuously decreased upon the lowering of the temperature to reach a minimum, 0.025 cm3 K mol−1 at 6 K, then increased to a maximum of 0.085 cm3 K mol−1 at 4 K and further went down to 0.048 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. Fitting the susceptibility data above 80 K to the Curie–Weiss law (Figure S8 in the Supporting Information) afforded the Curie and Weiss constants of 0.583 cm3 K mol−1 and −75.4 K, respectively. The Curie constant resulted in a g factor of 2.49, and the large negative Weiss temperature indicates AF coupling between the Cu2+ ions.

The material was subjected to further dc magnetic investigation in the low‐temperature region. ZFC/FC measurements (Figure 6, inset) in the 10 Oe field showed very small bifurcation, but the spontaneous magnetization is clearly visible, with the magnetization value of approximately 25 cm3 mol−1 around 2 K, and a critical temperature TN of 4.6 K, determined again by the negative peak position of the derivative dMFC/dT. The FC magnetizations under 50 and 100 Oe were similar to that under 10 Oe, with slightly higher magnetizations, and again a broad maximum around 45 K was observed for 100 Oe FC data that extended to 100 K (not shown here). At 2 K, the isothermal magnetization (Figure 7) below the 50 Oe field increased quickly to approximately 25 cm3 mol−1, then the increase slowed down, and after 4.0 kOe the magnetization showed nearly linear field dependence until 30 kOe, at which point a slight rise occurred. At 50 kOe the magnetization was 0.038 Nβ. A very narrow hysteresis loop was observed (Figure 7, inset), but when the field scanned from small positive (or negative) to small negative (positive) in 5 Oe steps, the magnetizations became small negative (positive) when the field was still small positive (negative). This could be due to an inaccuracy in the low‐field settings of the SQUID instrument. All these measurements confirmed the spin‐canted AF LRO within the material with small HC and MR.

The ac susceptibility measurements at several frequencies from 5.6 to 1488 Hz were carried out under zero dc field between 1.9 and 6.4 K and revealed interesting and complicated features of the relaxation behavior of 5 Cu (Figure 8 and Figures S9 and S10 in the Supporting Information). For all frequencies, both χ′ and χ′′ signals showed a sharp peak at 4.60(2) K, which is coincident with the occurrence of WF LRO at 4.6 K as observed by dc measurements, and the peaks of ac responses were lowered for higher frequencies. At 4.52(2) K, a weak peak appeared for low frequencies, and it became less significant for higher frequencies and almost unobservable above 560 Hz. This weak peak might indicate spin reorientation within the materials. More complicated, below 4.5 K, a broad frequency‐dependent peak was observed for ac responses starting from 32 Hz (Figure 8 a, χ′′ only). For both χ′ and χ′′ data, when the frequency increased, the peak became higher in strength and the peak position moved to higher temperatures; under the same frequency the peak positions of χ′ and χ′′ were very close, with the temperature difference less than 0.2 K. The parameter ϕ=(ΔTP/TP)/Δ(log f)=0.28 from the χ′ data was quite large. The plot of ln τ (τ=, where f is the ac frequency) versus 1/TP from the χ′′ data (Figure S9 in the Supporting Information) for the magnetic dynamics showed two straight segments with a crossover between them. The two straight segments could be fitted by the Arrhenius law of τ=τ0exp (Ea/kT) in which Ea is the activation energy,39, 48 and resulted in τ0=3.1(6)×10−9 s and Ea/k=46.2(8) K for high‐frequency data, and τ0=1.0(2)×10−5 s and Ea/k=16.3(5) K for low‐frequency data, respectively, thereby indicating at least two different thermally excited relaxation processes. The large ϕ value is due to the crossover of the two processes. In the isothermal Argrand plots (χ′′ versus χ′, or Cole–Cole plots, Figure S10 in the Supporting Information) derived from the χ′/χ′′ versus T data for the frequencies measured, an unusual phenomenon was observed. Above 4.6 K, the plots show irregular patterns and the χ′′/χ′ value quickly became very small (Figure S10a in the Supporting Information). Between 4.6 and 4.2 K, the χ′′ versus χ′ plots are inclined in a V shape (Figure S10b in the Supporting Information), with an upper low‐frequency (several to tens of Hz) branch and lower high‐frequency (tens to hundreds of Hz) one. The upper branch displays low frequencies on the right side, as usual in Cole–Cole plots,39, 48 but the lower branch has high frequencies on the right side and are thus abnormal. Upon further cooling (Figure S10c in the Supporting Information), the V‐shaped plots gradually became S‐shaped, with the high‐frequency end turned to the left side, and at 3.4 K, a loop upon frequency was formed. The plots gradually shrank into small noisy ones below 3 K, which corresponded to the very small ac responses (Figure S10d in the Supporting Information). Since 5 Cu showed WF LRO and it is a soft magnet, these complicated observations in ac susceptibilities should be due to the possible complicated domain structure and domain wall dynamics within the material.49 However, given the current available data, understanding the phenomenon is difficult, and it merits further investigation in the future.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we have successfully synthesized a series of metal–organic perovskites of six metal formates [C(NH2)3][M(HCOO)3] (M=divalent Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, and Zn) by employing guanidinium as a cationic template. This is the first reported complete perovskite metal–formate series for the late‐3d divalent metals from Mn to Zn. The structures possess NaCl‐type anionic metal–formate frameworks in which the octahedrally coordinated metal ions are bridged by the anti–anti formate ligands, and the guanidinium cations are located in the cubic cavities of the frameworks. The five members, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, and Zn are isomorphic, and within the space group of Pnna. In contrast, the Cu member is in a different, polar space group of Pna21, and thereby possesses a distorted anionic Cu–formate framework of Cu–formate chains through short basal CuO bonds further linked by the long axial CuO bonds, afforded by the Jahn–Teller effect of Cu2+. These compounds emphasize the template effect of protonated amines of medium size (containing two to four non‐hydrogen atoms), thus resulting in such metal–organic perovskites. The strong hydrogen‐bonding interaction between the guanidinium cation and the anionic framework allowed the synthesis of these materials from water‐rich reaction systems and afforded their higher thermal stability. The five magnetic members from Mn to Cu exhibit long‐range ordering of spin‐canted antiferromagnetism occurred at the Néel temperatures of 8.8 (Mn), 10.0 (Fe), 14.2 (Co), 34.2 (Ni), and 4.6 K (Cu), respectively, and this could be attributed to the Dzyaloshinsky–Moriya interaction or antisymmetric exchange mediated by the acentrosymmetric, three‐atom, anti–anti HCOO− bridge. In addition to the general feature of spin‐canted antiferromagnetism, the Fe member was found to show two isothermal transformations (a spin‐flop and a spin‐flip to the paramagnetic phase) within 50 kOe, the Co member possesses quite a large canting angle, and the Cu member is a magnetic system of low‐dimensional character and displays interesting slow magnetic relaxation that probably originates from the domain movement. Further work will involve 1) determining the magnetic structures, 2) investigating the mixed‐metal systems given the isostructural character within the series, and 3) exploring possible multiferroics within the Cu member.

Experimental Section

General methods: All starting materials were commercially available, reagent grade, and used without further purification. M(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O (M=Mn, Co) were prepared by dissolving the metal carbonate in dilute perchloric acid and drying.

CAUTION! Perchlorate compounds are potentially explosive. They should be prepared in small quantities and handled with care.

Synthesis of 1 Mn: Formic acid (0.40 g, 8.7 mmol) and [C(NH2)3]2CO3 (0.76 g, 4.2 mmol) were dissolved in water (20 mL). This solution was added by an aqueous solution (10 mL) containing Mn(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O (0.38 g, 1.0 mmol). The resulting mixed solution was kept undisturbed and allowed to evaporate slowly. Pale yellow block crystals of 1 Mn were harvested after two weeks, washed with ethanol, and dried in air. The yield was 26 % based on Mn(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Mn: C 19.21, H 3.63, N, 16.80; found: C 19.13, H 3.66, N 16.83.

Synthesis of 2 Fe: The compound was prepared by a slow diffusion method. A methanol–water (1:1 v/v) solution (1.5 mL) containing [C(NH2)3]2CO3 (0.088 g, 0.48 mmol) and formic acid (0.13 g, 2.8 mmol) was placed at the bottom of a glass tube. Then 0.5 mL of methanol and water (v/v, 1:1) was layered carefully, followed by a solution of Fe(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O (0.079 g, 0.20 mmol) in methanol–water (1:1 v/v, 2 mL) . The tube was sealed and kept undisturbed. Pale green block crystals of 2 Fe were harvested after two weeks in a yield of 82 % based on Fe(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Fe: C 19.14, H 3.61, N 16.74; found: C 19.06, H 3.75, N 16.81.

Synthesis of 3 Co: The compound was prepared in a similar way to that of 1 Mn, by mixing solutions of formic acid (0.23 g, 5 mmol) in methanol (10 mL), [C(NH2)3]2CO3 (0.38 g, 2.1 mmol) in water (2.5 mL), and Co(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O (0.18 g, 0.50 mmol) in water (5 mL). Pink block crystals were harvested after an overnight crystallization, with a yield of 25 % based on Co(ClO4)2⋅6 H2O. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Co: C 18.91, H 3.57, N 16.54; found: C 18.87, H 3.56, N 16.51.

Synthesis of 4 Ni: The compound could not be prepared in a crystalline form by means of conventional reactions at room temperature (see the Results and Discussion section), therefore a hydrothermal method was employed. A mixture of Ni(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O (0.19 g, 1.0 mmol), [C(NH2)3]Cl (0.12 g, 1.2 mmol), and NaHCOO⋅2 H2O (0.13 g, 1.2 mmol) in water (5 mL) in a small glass vessel was sealed in a Teflon‐lined stainless steel cell (25 mL), heated at 150 °C for 3 days under autogeneous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Green block crystals of 4 Ni were harvested in a yield of 19 % based on Ni(HCOO)2⋅2 H2O. Another batch synthesized by using double the amount of the starting chemicals but the same water volume produced microcrystals of 4 Ni in a higher yield of 69 %. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Ni: C 18.93, H 3.57, N 16.55; found: C 18.85, H 3.90, N 16.44.

Synthesis of 5 Cu: Compound 5 Cu was synthesized in a similar route as that used for 1 Mn and 3 Co, from a crystallization over four days by slow evaporation of an aqueous solution (8 mL) containing formic acid (0.23 g, 5.0 mmol), [C(NH2)3]2CO3 (0.38 g, 2.1 mmol), and Cu(NO3)2⋅6 H2O (0.12 g, 0.40 mmol). Blue block crystals were harvested in a yield of 59 % based on Cu(NO3)2⋅6 H2O. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Cu: C 18.57, H 3.51, N 16.24; found: C 18.53, H 3.58, N 16.30.

Synthesis of 6 Zn: The compound was prepared by a slow diffusion method in an H‐shaped glass tube. A solution of [C(NH2)3]2CO3 (0.30 g, 1.7 mmol) and formic acid (0.34 g, 7.4 mmol) in methanol (5 mL) was placed at the bottom of the left side, and a solution of Zn(NO3)2⋅6 H2O (0.16 g, 0.5 mmol) in methanol (5 mL) on the other side. Methanol (10 mL) was carefully layered upon both solutions and linked them to provide a diffusion pathway. Crystallization took two weeks and afforded colorless block crystals of 6 Zn in a yield of 42 % based on Zn(NO3)2⋅6 H2O. Elemental analysis calcd (%) for C4H9N3O6Zn: C 18.44, H 3.48, N 16.13; found: C 18.55, H 3.47, N 15.90.
