Three porous metal–organic frameworks based on an azobenzenetricarboxylate ligand: synthesis, structures, and magnetic properties†

Miao Meng , Di-Chang Zhong * and Tong-Bu Lu *
MOE Key Laboratory of Bioinorganic and Synthetic Chemistry/State Key Laboratory of Optoelectronic Materials and Technologies/School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, 510275, China. E-mail: zhong_dichang@yahoo.com.cn; Fax: +86-20-84112921; Tel: +86-20-84112921lutongbu@mail.sysu.edu.cn

Received 30th June 2011 , Accepted 18th August 2011

First published on 13th September 2011

Three porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) of {[Cd(HABTC)(H2O)2]·3H2O}n (1), {[Cd3(ABTC)2(H2O)9]·CH3OH·DMF·2H2O}n (2), and {[Mn3(ABTC)2(H2O)4]·9.5H2O}n (3) have been constructed using azobenzene-3,5,4′-tricarboxylic acid (H3ABTC) and Cd(NO3)2·4H2O/MnCl2·4H2O as building blocks. The results of single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis indicate that 1 is a 2D layer structure with 1D open channels. 2 is a rare 3D porous structure constructed from polycatenated 2D layers. 3 also shows a 3D porous structure with 1D carboxylate bridged Mn–(COO)–Mn chains. The result of magnetic susceptibility measurements indicates that the Mn(II) ions in 3 exhibit antiferromagnetic interactions.

Introduction

In the past decade, much attention has been drawn to the area of design and synthesis of porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), due to their intriguing structures and potential applications for gas separation and storage,1 heterogeneous catalysis,2 magnetism,3 luminescence tuning4 and so on. Among them, aromatic carboxylic ligands have been widely used as building blocks to construct MOFs, as carboxyl groups can be partially or completely deprotonated, and can coordinate with metal ions in multi-coordinated ways. To date, a large number of porous MOFs based on aromatic carboxylic ligands have been reported, which were discussed in several current reviews.5

Recently, azobenzenecarboxylic compounds such as azobenzene-dicarboxylic and azobenzene-tetracarboxylic acids (Scheme 1) were utilized to construct functional MOFs.6 For example, Yaghi et al. used 4,4′-azodibenzoic acid (4,4′-H2ADB) to react with Tb(NO3)3·5H2O to get an interpenetrating network of {Tb2(ADB)3[(CH3)2SO]4·16[(CH3)2SO]}n with large free volume.7 Chen and co-workers used 3,3′- and 4,4′-azodibenzoic acids as building blocks to assemble a series of coordination polymers with diverse structrues.8 Very recently, a series of 3D lanthanide porous MOFs with 4,4′-H2ADB have been reported, which exhibit interpenetrating structures with CaB6 and NaCl-type topologies.9 Qiu and co-workers used 3,3′,5,5′-azobenzenetetracarboxylic acid (H4ABTC) as a building block to construct three 3D microporous MOFs with NbO and PtS topologies, which show hydrogen storage and luminescent properties.103,5,4′-Azobenzenetricarboxylic acid (H3ABTC) is a ‘Y’-shaped carboxylic ligand (Scheme 1(c)), while its coordination chemistry has been rarely explored. To our knowledge, only two isostructural porous lanthanide MOFs (Dy(III) and Gd(III)) of H3ABTC have been reported,11 and no transition metal MOFs with H3ABTC have been reported so far. In this paper, we report three H3ABTC-based transition metal MOFs of {[Cd(HABTC)(H2O)2]·3H2O}n (1), {[Cd3(ABTC)2(H2O)9]·CH3OH·DMF·2H2O}n (2), and {[Mn3(ABTC)2(H2O)4]·9.5H2O}n (3). Their syntheses, structures, and magnetic properties have been presented.

Experimental section

Materials and methods

All the chemicals are commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses were determined using an Elementar Vario EL elemental analyzer. The IR spectra were recorded in the 4000–400 cm−1 region using KBr pellets and a Bruker EQUINOX 55 spectrometer. Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses data were collected on a Netzsch TG-209 instrument under nitrogen atmosphere in the temperature range of 20–800 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns were recorded on a D8 ADVANCE X-ray diffractometer. Magnetic susceptibility data were collected in the 2–300 K temperature range with a Quantum Design SQUID Magnetometer MPMS XL-7 and a field of 0.1 T. A correction was made for the diamagnetic contribution prior to data analysis.
{[Cd(HABTC)(H2O)2]·3H2O}n (1). A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.0308 g, 0.10 mmol), H3ABTC (0.0157 g, 0.05 mmol), NaOH aqueous solution (0.2 mL, 1 M) and water/methanol (10 mL, v:v = 1:1) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, and heated at 110 °C for 3 days. After the autoclave was cooled over a period of 18 h at a rate of 5 °C h−1, orange block-shaped crystals of 1 were collected, washed with distilled water, and dried in air. Yield: 16.0 mg, 31% based on Cd. Anal. calcd for C15H18CdN2O11: C, 35.00; H, 3.52; N, 5.44%. Found: C, 35.03; H, 3.50; N, 5.46%. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3433(vs), 1677(w), 1609(s), 1561(s), 1381(s), 1274(m), 1108(m), 697(s).
{[Cd3(ABTC)2(H2O)9]·CH3OH·DMF·2H2O}n (2). A solution of H3ABTC (0.0157 g, 0.05 mmol) in DMF (2 mL) was added to a solution of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.0308 g, 0.1 mmol) in methanol (2 mL). The mixture was stirred at room temperature for 15 min and then filtrated. The resulting solution was allowed to evaporate slowly at room temperature to give orange plate crystals after a few weeks. Yield: 10.1 mg, 24% based on Cd. Anal. calcd for C34H47Cd3N5O25: C, 32.33; H, 3.75; N, 5.55%. Found: C, 32.42; H, 3.80; N, 5.69%. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3429(vs), 1655(w), 1610(s), 1547(w), 1383(s), 1106(m), 731(w).
{[Mn3(ABTC)2(H2O)4]·9.5H2O}n (3). A mixture of MnCl2·4H2O (0.0396 g, 0.2 mmol), H3ABTC (0.0157 g, 0.05 mmol), NaOH aqueous solution (0.5 mL, 1 M) and distilled water (10 mL) was sealed in a 20 mL Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, and heated at 65 °C for two days. After the autoclave was cooled over a period of 7 h at a rate of 5 °C h−1, orange block-shaped crystals of 3 were collected, washed with distilled water, and dried in air. Yield: 13.3 mg, 20% based on Mn. Anal. calcd for C30H41N4O25.5Mn3: C, 34.97; H, 4.01; N, 5.44%. Found: C, 35.07; H, 4.02; N, 5.45%. IR data (KBr, cm−1): 3399(s), 1636(m), 1610(w), 1576(w), 1543(w), 1378(s), 1109(w), 790(w), 709(s).

X-Ray crystallography

Single-crystal data for 1 were collected at 110 K on a Bruker Smart 1000 CCD diffractometer, with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å), the empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program.12 Single-crystal data for 2 were collected at 123 K on an Oxford Gemini S Ultra diffractometer, with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å), the empirical absorption corrections were applied using spherical harmonics, implemented in the SCALE3 ABSPACK scaling algorithm.13 Single-crystal data for 3 were collected at 153 K on a Rigaku R-AXIS RAPID Imaging Plate diffractometer with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å), the numerical absorption corrections were applied using the program of ABSCOR.14 All the structures were solved using direct methods, which yielded the positions of all non-hydrogen atoms. These were refined first isotropically and then anisotropically. The DMF (C16, C17, N3, O12) and CH3OH (C18, O13, O13′) molecules in 2 are disordered, and were treated with an occupancy of 0.5. All of the disordered parts were restrained using DFIX, ISOR, and EADP instructions to make the displacement parameters more reasonable. All the hydrogen atoms of the ligand were placed in calculated positions with fixed isotropic thermal parameters and included in the structure factor calculations in the final stage of full-matrix least-squares refinement. The hydrogen atoms of water molecules in 1–3 and disordered DMF molecules in 2 were not assigned. All the calculations were performed using the SHELXTL system of computer programs.15 The crystallographic data are summarized in Table 1, and the selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S1†.

Results and discussion

Crystal structures

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one Cd(II), one HABTC2−, two coordinated and three lattice water molecules. As shown in Fig. 1a, Cd1 coordinates with five carboxylic O atoms (O1, O3A, O4A, O5C, O6C) from three individual HABTC2− ligands and two water molecules (O7, O8), forming a slightly distorted pentagonal bipyramid geometry. The O1, O3A, O4A, O5C and O6C atoms locate the equatorial plane, and O7, O8 occupy the axial positions. The HABTC2− ligand employs an asymmetric coordination mode (Scheme 2, mode I), in which one carboxylic group of HABTC2− is not deprotonated. Through the bridging of HABTC2−, Cd(II) ions are connected together to generate a 2D layer (Fig. 1b), in which each Cd(II) coordinates with three HABTC2−, and each HABTC2− connects with three Cd(II), resulting in a 3-connected uninodal honeycomb (hcb) topology layer, with the point symbol of {63} (Fig. 1c). The 2D layers are further linked together through interlayer hydrogen bonds (Fig. 1d) and π–π stacking interactions (Fig. 1e) to form a 3D porous structure of 1 (Fig. 1f), with the O7⋯O6 and O8⋯O3 hydrogen bonding distances of 2.702(4) and 2.682(9) Å, respectively, and the centroid⋯centroid distance of 3.585(3) Å. The pores, with the sizes of 7.5 × 6.9 Å (measured by the O7⋯O8A and C3⋯C4A distances), are filled with lattice H2O molecules. About 20.1% solvent-accessible volume is estimated by using PLATON program.16
Fig. 1 (a) The coordination environments of Cd(II) and HABTC2− in 1; (b) HABTC2− bridged 2D layer, and (c) its topological structure (the atoms with green and gray color represent Cd and HABTC2−, respectively); (d) hydrogen bonding and (e) π–π stacking interactions between adjacent layers; (f) the 3D porous structure of 1.

Scheme 2 The coordination modes of HABTC2−/ABTC3− in 1–3.

Compound 2 crystallizes in a chiral space groupC2, with a Flack parameter of 0.032(11). As shown in Fig. 2a, there are two crystallographically independent Cd(II) ions in 2. Both Cd1 and Cd2 are seven-coordinated with four oxygen atoms from two bidentate carboxylate groups of two individual ABTC3− and three water molecules. The coordination geometries of Cd1 and Cd2 are different (Fig. S1†), in which Cd1 shows a pentagonal bipyramid geometry, with O1, O2, O3A, O4A and O8 locating the equatorial plane, and O7 and O9 occupying the apical positions, while Cd2 exhibits a distorted capped triangular prism geometry, with O5, O6, O10, O11, O6A and O11A locating the six apical positions of the distorted triangular prism, and O5A occupying the capped position. The Cd1–O1, Cd1–O3 and Cd2–O5 distances (2.537(5)–2.770(4) Å) are longer than the other Cd–O distances (2.236(4)–2.315(5) Å), indicating the weak interactions between Cd1 and O1/O3, and Cd2 and O5.17


Fig. 2 (a) The coordination environments of Cd(II) and ABTC3− in 2; (b) the 2D layer structure, with a layer thickness of 3.32 nm (the coordinated H2O molecules are omitted for clarity); (c) the extension of 2D layer to 3D network through polycatenation between adjacent layers; (d) the net topology of 2, the atoms in blue and red represent the 3-connected ABTC3− anions.

In 2, ABTC3− serves as a 3-connected ligand, with its three carboxylate groups linking three Cd(II) ions (Scheme 2, mode II). Through the bridging of ABTC3−, Cd1 and Cd2 are connected together to generate a 2D layer along the ab plane (Fig. 2b). The thickness of the 2D layer is 3.32 nm (Fig. 2b), which allows each layer to further interlock with two adjacent identical layers, extending 2D layers to a 3D porous MOF along the c axis (Fig. 2c). The pores, with the sizes of 12.3 × 8.9 Å (Fig. S2†), are filled with CH3OH, DMF, and H2O molecules. About 22.4% solvent-accessible volume is estimated by using PLATON program.16 According to the simplifying regulation of topology, Cd1/Cd2 and ABTC3− can be considered as a linker and a 3-connected node, respectively. Therefore, the 2D layer of 2 is a 3-connected uninodal sheet with the point symbol of {82·10} (Fig. 2d). Up to now, only a few MOFs show a 3D framework constructed from polycatenated 2D layers.18

Compound 3 crystallizes in a non-centrosymmetric space groupCc, with a Flack parameter of −0.02(3). As shown in Fig. 3a, the asymmetric unit of 3 contains three Mn(II) ions. They show similar distorted O6 octahedral geometries, in which four O atoms come from four individual ABTC3− ligands, and the remaining two O atoms around Mn1/Mn2 are from one μ2–H2O bridge (O16) and one terminal water molecule (O14/O15), while those around Mn3 come from two terminal water molecules (O13 and O17). The Mn–O distances of 2.094(5)–2.323(5) Å are close to the literature values.19 Each ABTC3− connects six Mn(II) atoms (Scheme 2, mode III). Through the bridging of ABTC3−, Mn1, Mn2 and Mn3 are linked together to form a 3D porous MOF with 1D channels along the b axis. The pore sizes of the channels are 8.8 × 9.2 Å (Fig. 3b). The 1D channels are filled with lattice water molecules, and about 33.4% solvent-accessible volume is estimated by using PLATON program.16 There are 1D carboxylate bridged Mn–(COO)–Mn chains in 3 (Fig. 3c), and the distance of Mn1⋯Mn2 (3.846(3) Å) is shorter than the distances of Mn1⋯Mn3 (4.870(6) Å) and Mn2⋯Mn3 (5.290(5) Å).


Fig. 3 (a) The coordination environments of Mn(II) and ABTC3− in 3; (b) the 3D framework of 3 with 1D channels along the b axis; (c) the carboxylate bridged Mn–(COO)–Mn chains.

It is interesting to note that although all the three MOFs are constructed from the H3ABTC ligand, they show different structures. 1 is a 2D layer structure with 1D open channels, 2 is a 3D porous structure constructed through polycatenation of 2D layers, and 3 is a 3D porous structure constructed from Mn(II) and ABTC3−. The diverse structures of 1–3 can be ascribed to the different metal ions, and the different coordination modes of HABTC2−/ABTC3− induced by the different reaction conditions.

X-Ray powder diffraction and thermogravimetric analyses

XRD was used to check the purity of 1–3. As shown in Fig. 4, all the peaks displayed in the measured patterns for each compound closely match those in the simulated patterns generated from single-crystal diffraction data, indicating that the single phases of 1–3 were formed. The TG measurements for 1–3 were carried out from room temperature to 800 °C. As shown in Fig. 5, the TG curve of 1 displays a weight loss of 17.2% from room temperature to 90 °C, corresponding to the removal of three lattice water molecules and two coordination water molecules (calcd 17.5%). The desolvated compound is stable up to 350 °C, and then began to decompose upon further heating. For 2, the observed weight loss of 23.8% in the temperature range from room temperature to 380 °C is close to the losses of one CH3OH molecule, one DMF molecule, two lattice H2O and nine coordinated H2O molecules (calcd 24.0%). Upon further heating, the framework began to decompose. For 3, the TG curve shows a weight loss of 23.2% from room temperature to 150 °C, corresponding to the losses of nine and a half lattice water molecules, as well as four coordinated water molecules (calcd 23.6%). The desolvated 3 is stable up to 420 °C, and began to decompose after that temperature.

Magnetic properties

The magnetic susceptibility measurements for 3 were performed with a polycrystalline sample at an applied field of 1 kOe in the temperature range of 2–300 K. As shown in Fig. 6, the measured χMT value of 13.49 cm3 K mol−1 at 300 K is close to the expected value for three isolated high-spin Mn(II) ions (13.13 cm3 K mol−1). The χMT values slowly decrease with decreasing temperature to reach a value of 9.62 cm3 K mol−1 at 30 K, then sharply decrease to 1.77 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. This behavior suggests an antiferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent Mn(II) ions. Curve fit for 1/χMversus T plots obeys the Curie–Weiss law in the temperature range of 60–300 K, with a negative Weiss constant (θ) of −20.2 K and a Curie constant (C) of 14.36 cm3 K mol−1. The negative value of the Weiss constant further elucidates an antiferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent Mn(II) ions.
Fig. 6 Temperature dependence of χM and χMT for 3. Inset: plot of χM−1vs. T, the solid line is the best-fit curve.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully constructed three porous transition metal–organic frameworks of 1–3 by using H3ABTC as a building block. 1 shows a 2D layer structure with hcb topology. 2 is a rare 3D porous MOF constructed from polycatenated 2D layers. 3 is also a 3D porous MOF with 1D carboxylate bridged Mn–(COO)–Mn chains. The result of magnetic susceptibility measurements for 3 indicates an antiferromagnetic coupling between the adjacent Mn(II) ions.
