Introduction

The study of POMs, which have remarkable features of metal oxide surfaces and diversity in geometric topology, is developing rapidly. POM-based supramolecular assemblies thus become an intense area of contemporary research for potential applications in heterogeneous catalysis, molecular adsorption, ion exchange, as well as electrical, magnetic, photochemical areas and so on.1 Besides charge balancing, due to their controllable shape and size, POMs can act as anionic templates to build extended hybrid metalâorganic frameworks (MOFs) with O-donor organic ligands or so.2,3

By means of their multiple coordination requirements and oxophilicity, rare earth ions are of great use to link O-donor organic ligands to assemble high-connected 3D MOFs with potentially useful photochemical properties.4 Although some of the highly oxophilic rare earth ions can precipitate with O-donor organic ligands and POMs fast instead of crystallizing,5 recent elaboration has proven that the hydrothermal technique is an effective approach for the preparation of higher-dimensional hybrid materials.6 Therefore, this one-pot hydrothermal synthesis provides a way to solve the fast precipitation process.7

Prior to now, there have been some of the above-mentioned architectures reported and they display strong photoluminescent properties, but the photocatalytic degradation tests remain to be done.8 It is well known that POMs such as heteropolymolybdates possess photocatalytic activity.9 Many of them share similar general photo-chemical characteristics as the semiconductor photocatalysts, such as TiO2. For example, a variety of organic substrates can be oxidized photocatalytically by POMs under UV irradiation.10 Comparisons of the photo-oxidation of organics by UV-illuminated POMs and semiconductors have also been reported.9,10c,11 As a result, It is reasonable to assume that Ln(III)âorganic ligand has the same property (Ln3+ ions have the property of enhancing the photocatalytic activity of TiO2.28) to enhance the photocatalytic activity of POMs. So, the abovementioned architecturesâ photo-chemical characteristics need to be examined.

Here we report three Î±-Keggin heteropolymolybdates with pyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate based frameworks, which are formulated as [(C5H4NH)COOH]3[PMo12O40] 1, {[Sm(H2O)4(pdc)]3}{[Sm(H2O)3(pdc)]}[SiMo12O40]Â·3H2O 2 and {[La(H2O)4(pdc)]4} [PMo12O40]F 3. All three compounds comprise a ball-shaped Keggin type [XMo12O40]nâ counterion (n = 3, X = P; n = 4, X = Si, respectively) with different types of carboxylic ligands derived from H2pdc, and these cluster anions are isostructural. RhB photocatalytic degradation and 2D-IR COS tests are investigated for 1, 2 and 3 in order to explore properties like photocatalytic, magnetic sensitivity and POM's stability. As far as we know, it is the first time that the photocatalytic activity of the above-mentioned compounds have been examined, and 2D-IR COS is rarely used in discussing structure properties.

2D-IR COS analysis

2D-IR COS, proposed by Noda,21 is developed to analyse various types of spectral data often obtained under dynamic experiments like thermal or magnetic perturbation. This method is a practical and effective measure in the dynamic analysis.22 With 2D-IR COS, it is possible to clearly describe the molecular dynamic mechanism in certain conditions.23

The 2D-IR COS under temperature-induced changes of the three compounds are shown in Fig. 8â10. Obvious inductions appear from 700 to 1650 cmâ1, but auto-peaks from PâO/SiâO stretching vibrations around 1050â1080 cmâ1 are not obvious. In the synchronous spectra of 1, peaks around 700â900 cmâ1 are assigned to temperature-induced stretching vibrations of MoâOt and MoâÎ¼2-O.24 Spectra of 1 show the same pattern as 3, regardless of the different POM valence. But in 2, peak intensities of stretching vibrations of MoâOt are much stronger than that of MoâÎ¼2-O. It may mean that the [SiMo12O40] cluster is more stable than [PMo12O40], which was confirmed by McGarvey and Moffat.25 The peak at 1625 cmâ1 in 1 is assigned to the stretching vibrations of carboxylate groups and caused by free carboxylate groups (they also may be caused by the cooperative effect of carboxyl to water molecules).24 For 2, the intensity of the peak at 1615 cmâ1 is much stronger than the peak at 1575 cmâ1 (assigned to the stretching vibration of carboxylate groups). While in 3, the peaks at 1635 and 1575 cmâ1 have the same intensities. This difference may be caused by the different sizes of the four-connected nodes in 2 and 3. As mentioned previously, 3 has bigger nodes than 2, larger tetranuclear cyclic units avoid excessive intermolecular interactions and then lead to more stable structure (the Sm(4) in 2 only has 3 coordinated water molecules, and the crystal yield of 2 is much lower than 3. This instability in 2 may result from the strong H-bond interaction around Sm(4)). Besides, the lanthanide in 1 (which has a smaller radius than Sm(III)), because the nodes are too small, cannot be crystallized using similar synthesis methods to 2 and 3.

Conclusion

In this work, we have successfully assembled three Î±-Keggin heteropolymolybdates from the H2pdc ligand under hydrothermal conditions. These compounds comprise different frameworks and exhibit good photocatalytic activity. This photocatalytic activity, which is mainly due to the effect of POMs, is enhanced by Ln(III)âpdc metalâorganic frameworks. These zeolite-like GIS topology frameworks of compounds 2 and 3 are well retained below 400 Â°C, according to TGA and the thermal infrared spectrum analyses. Temperature-dependent 2D-IR COS analysis reveal relative structure properties of three compounds with different POMs or ligands, which have the guiding significance for further study and synthesis. And, their sensitivity under temperature/magnetism-induced disturbance indicates that they may be potential thermo/magneto sensitive materials. It is reasonable to believe that the present work is important so as to expand on the application of these kinds of materials in molecular adsorption and catalysis. These efforts are currently ongoing.
