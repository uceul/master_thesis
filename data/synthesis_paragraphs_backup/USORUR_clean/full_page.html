Four metal–organic frameworks based on a semirigid tripodal ligand and different secondary building units: structures and electrochemical performance†

Xiao-Ming Lin *ab, Ji-Liang Niu a, Dan-Ni Chen a, Yan-Na Lu a, Gang Zhang b and Yue-Peng Cai *a
aSchool of Chemistry and Environment, Guangzhou Key Laboratory of Materials for Energy Conversion and Storage, South China Normal University, 510006, PR China. E-mail: linxm@scnu.edu.cn; caiyp@scnu.edu.cn
bState Key Laboratory of Supramolecular Structure and Materials, College of Chemistry, Jilin University, Changchun, 130012, PR China

Received 6th May 2016 , Accepted 8th July 2016

First published on 25th July 2016

Four new metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), [Co3(TCPB)2(CH3OH)4]·4DMF (1), [Cd3(TCPB)2(H2O)4]·3DMF·19H2O (2), [Mn3(TCPB)2(H2O)2(DMF)2]·2DMF (3), and [Zn2(μ3-OH) (TCPB)(H2O)(DEF)]·3DEF (4) (H3TCPB = 1,3,5-tri(4-carboxyphenoxy)benzene), have been successfully synthesized and structurally characterized. Structural analyses show that complexes 1–3 present two-dimensional (3,6)-connected kgd net topology with the Schläfli symbol of (43)2(46·66·83) based on linear trinuclear M3(COO)6 (M = Co, Cd, and Mn) secondary building units (SBUs) and display distinct one-dimensional (1-D) channels. Complex 4 (Zn-TCPB) exhibits a rare three-dimensional twofold interpenetrated (3,6)-connected rutile topology with the Schläfli symbol of (42·610·83) (4·62)2 based on the tetranuclear Zn4(μ3-OH)2(COO)6 clusters, which shows three different types of 1-D opening channels. In addition, as an anode material, the complex 4 (Zn-TCPB) electrode exhibits an irreversible high capacity in the first discharge process and a reversible lithium storage capacity of up to about 455 mA h g−1 at 0.1 A g−1 after 100 cycles. This might provide a new method for finding a promising candidate for the electrode materials in lithium-ion batteries.

Introduction

In recent years, cluster-based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have witnessed an explosive growth in the field of solid-state functional materials because of their fascinating structural topologies1 and potential applications in gas storage and separation,2 catalysis,3 and luminescent materials.4 To date, a large number of cluster-based MOFs with rich physical or chemical properties have been obtained via the rationally designed metal cluster directional self-assembly approach,5 but it still remains a very difficult scientific challenge to explore and predict a preferred structure with a specific net topology because the final structures are frequently modulated by various factors, such as the temperature, pH, concentration and ratio of reactants, solvent, reaction time and template agents.6 Fortunately, crystal engineering principles have been used to construct structures from secondary building units (SBUs) as the “joints” and organic linkers as the “struts” of the underlying net, and this has been proved to be an effective and powerful synthetic strategy for the design of diverse new MOFs.7

It is noted that utilizing metal clusters as SBUs can increase the critical pore sizes and also serve as highly-connected nodes due to the metal clusters bearing different sizes and connectivity. For constructing appropriate SBUs, the most important factor is the selection of organic ligands. Among the numerous organic ligands, multifunctional carboxylic acid ligands which display multiple coordination sites could connect more than one metal to form prospective SBUs and sequentially expand the dimension of MOFs.8 For example, Yaghi has constructed a family of isoreticular MOFs with pcu net topology by a combination of various linear dicarboxylate organic linkers and octahedral [Zn4O(COO)6] clusters as SBUs.9 When 1,3,5-benzenetribenzoic acid (H3BTB) is combined with a 4-connected [Cu2(COO)4] paddle wheel as a SBU, then MOF-14, showing a 3,4-connected pto net topology, is obtained,10 while 4,4′,4′′-[benzene-1,3,5-triyl-tris(benzene-4,1-diyl)]tribenzoate (BBC) reacts with Zn(II) ions to generate MOF-200, having a (3,6)-connected qom net topology based on an octahedral 6-connected [Zn4O(CO2)6] SBU.11

We have been working on crystal engineering of porous frameworks connected by metal ions and organic linkers.12 In our recent work, we have reported two porous Zn-MOFs with a rare (3,6)-connected net topology of the MoS2–H structure based on a linear trinuclear [Zn3(COO)6] as SBU and a flexible H3TCPB = 1,3,5-tri(4-carboxyphenoxy)benzene ligand.13 Along with this work in the same field, herein, we obtained four new MOFs based on polynuclear metal cluster SBUs, namely, [Co3(TCPB)2(CH3OH)4]·4DMF (1), [Cd3(TCPB)2(H2O)4]·3DMF·5H2O (2), [Mn3(TCPB)2(H2O)2(DMF)2]·2DMF (3), and [Zn2(μ3-OH) (TCPB)(H2O)(DEF)]·3DEF (4). These complexes were characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, infrared (IR) spectroscopy, powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD), and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). Moreover, the electrochemical property of complex 4 was also studied.

Experimental

Materials and measurements

All the reagents and solvents employed were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. Infrared spectra were collected from KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a Nicolet/Nexus-670 FT-IR spectrometer. Elemental analyses were performed using a Perkin-Elmer 240 elemental analyzer. X-ray powder diffraction patterns were measured on a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer at 40 kV and 40 mA with a Cu target tube and a graphite monochromator. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed on a Netzsch Thermo Microbalance TG 209 F3 Tarsus from room temperature to 900 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under flowing nitrogen.

X-ray crystallographic studies

Suitable single crystals of these complexes were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to thin glass fibers. Structural measurements were collected on an Oxford Gemini S Ultra diffractometer equipped with CuKα radiation (λ = 1.54178 Å) at 150 K by using ϕ and ω scans.14 The reflections have been corrected by empirical absorption corrections. The structures were solved by the direct method and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 using SHELXL programs.15 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. In complex 2, free solvent molecules were disordered; therefore, the diffused electron densities resulting from these residual solvent molecules were removed from the data set using the SQUEEZE routing of PLATON and refined further using the data generated.16 The number of electrons in the voids is 629, which may be assigned to 3 DMF and 19 H2O guest solvent molecules. Due to the fact that all guest solvent molecules of complex 4 are located in the voids of the coordination frameworks, they display typical large thermal displacements; therefore, constrained refinements were applied by using DELU/SIMU to approximate to isotropic behaviours. Electrochemical measurement

For electrochemical testing, Zn-TCPB samples were dried for about 4 h at 120 °C in a vacuum oven. Electrodes were prepared by mixing crystalline Zn-TCPB (80 wt%) with Super P carbon black (10 wt%) and 10 wt% polyvinylidene fluoride binder in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidinone solvent to form a homogeneous slurry. Then, the slurry was spread onto a copper foil. The coated electrodes were dried in a vacuum oven at 70 °C for 10 h and then pressed. Electrochemical measurements were carried out using CR 2032 coin-type cells which were assembled in an argon-filled glovebox (Mbraun, Unilab, Germany) by stacking a porous polypropylene separator containing liquid electrolyte between the composite electrodes and a lithium-foil counter electrode. The electrolyte used was 1 M LiPF6 in a 50:50 (v/v) mixture of ethylene carbonate and dimethyl carbonate. Galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were performed by using the LAND CT2001A multichannel battery testing system at a constant current density of 100 mA g−1. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were carried out on a CHI660C Electrochemical Workstation at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s−1 in a potential range of 0–3.0 V vs. Li/Li+.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of [Co3(TCPB)2(CH3OH)4]·4DMF (1)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies revealed that 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic P21/c space group. The Co(1) atom, being located at an inversion centre, links two Co(2) atoms by sharing one edge of a coordination octahedron to form a linear trinuclear cluster Zn3(COO)6via four carboxylate groups in a syn–syn bridging bidentate mode and two caboxylate groups in a μ-carboxylate chelating and bridging bidentate mode. In the trinuclear secondary building unit (SBU), the centre Co(1) ion exhibits an octahedral coordination surrounded by six oxygen atoms from six bridging carboxylate groups. The two terminal Co(2) ions reside in a distorted octahedral coordination sphere, defined by four oxygen atoms from three carboxylate groups and two oxygen atoms from two ligated CH3OH molecules (Fig. 1a). On the other hand, each TCPB3− ligand links three SBUs using its carboxylate group in μ2-η1:η1 and μ2-η2:η1 coordination bridging modes to give rise to a 2-D layer structure (Fig. 1b).
Fig. 1 Crystal structure of 1. (a) Linear trinuclear cobalt clusters as a SBU. Symmetry code: i: −x, −y, 1 − z. (b) Polyhedral view of 2-D network. (c) Trinuclear cobalt cluster as a 6-connected node with a schematic connectivity to the centres of six neighboring ligands and the ligand TCPB3− as a 3-connected node. (d) View of the schematic 2-D layer showing the (3,6)-connected kgd net topology. (e) A space-filling view of the network showing two different types of 1-D channels along the crystallographic (e) a-axis and (f) c-axis, in which the solvent molecules are omitted for clarity.

A better insight into the nature of this structure can be achieved by the application of a topology approach; each TCPB3− ligand acts as an organic trinodal building block and the trinuclear cobalt cluster serves as hexatopic node geometry (Fig. 1c). The combination of the 3-connected node and the 6-connected node generates an infinite two-dimensional (2-D) layered framework with a binodal (3,6)-connected kagomé dual (kgd) net topology with the Schläfli symbol of (43)2(46·66·83) analyzed using the Topos4.0 program (Fig. 1d).17 These two 2-D layers are stacked in an eclipsed fashion and the stacking gives rise to two different types of one-dimensional (1-D) opening channels with an effective size of 8.7 × 5.3 Å2 and 5.4 × 2.7 Å2 along the crystallographic a- and c-axis, respectively (Fig. 1e and f). The skewed intersection of the two sets of 1-D channels lead to three-dimensional (3-D) solvent channels packed with lattice DMF molecules and PLATON analysis suggests that channels occupy 44.4% of the total volume [1610.4 Å3/3624.4 Å3].

Crystal structure of [Cd3(TCPB)2(H2O)4]·3DMF·19H2O (2)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction studies reveal that 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c and the structure also contains a linear trinuclear cadmium cluster at the crystallographic inversion centre with a Cd1–Cd2 distance of 3.599 Å and a Cd2–Cd1–Cd2 bond angle of 180°. As shown in Fig. 2a, Cd1 lies on an inversion centre and the metal Cd ions present similar coordination geometry to that in 1, while the two terminal coordinating methanol molecules are replaced by H2O molecules. The trinuclear cadmium clusters as SBUs are united together by six ligands to generate a 2-D layer structure (Fig. 2b). A better insight into the nature of this structure is presented here; the whole 2-D sheet can be seen as a (3,6)-connected kgd network by simplifying the ligand as a 3-connecting node and the trinuclear cadmium cluster as a 6-connecting node (Fig. 2c). An appealing structural feature in 2 is that the 2-D layers are stacked in the ABAB mode along the b-axis without interpenetration and the water solvent molecules are encapsulated between the adjacent layers along the c-axis, giving a 1-D channel with an effective pore size of 5.6 × 5.6 Å2 (Fig. 2d and e). The solvent-accessible volume is 2456.9 Å3 per unit cell, and the pore volume ratio is calculated to be 54.5% using the PLATON program.

(c) View of the (3,6)-connected kgd net topology with the Schläfli symbol of (43)2(46·66·83). (d) Stacking of 2-D layers in an ABAB mode along the b-axis. Crystal structure of [Mn3(TCPB)2(H2O)2(DMF)2]·2DMF (3)

Interestingly, evaporation of the solvent following a solvothermal reaction of Mn(NO3)2·4H2O and H3TCPB at 80 °C in DMF instead of Cd(NO3)2·6H2O generates complex 3. The structural determination of 3 shows that the crystal system and space group are obviously different from those of 2, with 3 crystallizing in the lower symmetric triclinic space group P. The Mn(1) atom, being located at an inversion centre, links two Mn(2) atoms via six TCPB3− ligands to form a Mn3(COO)6 linear trinuclear SBU with a Mn(1)–Mn(2) distance of 3.605(3) Å and a Mn2–Mn1–Mn2 bond angle of 180° (Fig. 3a). Similar to that in 1 and 2, each TCPB3− ligand links three SBUs using its carboxylate group in two μ2-η1:η1-bidentate bridging modes and one μ2-η2:η1 coordination mode to form an infinite two-dimensional network (Fig. 3b). The 2-D layer can be simplified to a (3,6)-connected kgd net topology with SBU–SBU distances of 9.77(5), 9.95(3) and 10.50(6) Å, respectively (Fig. 3c). The AA packing fashion also affords 1-D open channels running along the b-axis with effective dimensions of 2.7 × 1.8 Å2, obviously smaller than those of 1 and 2. PLATON analysis suggests that the channels occupy 34.0% of the crystal volume (322.1 Å3 of the 1701.6 Å3 unit cell volume) (Fig. 3d).
Fig. 3 Crystal structure of 3. (a) Linear trinuclear cluster Mn3(COO)6 as a SBU. Symmetry code: i: 2 − x, 2 − y, 1 − z. (b) Polyhedral view of 2-D network along the b-axis. (c) Schematic representation of the kgd network. (d) A space-filling view of the network showing 1-D channels along the b-axis. All hydrogen atoms are omitted for clarity.

Crystal structure of [Zn2(μ3-OH) (TCPB)(H2O)(DEF)]·3DEF (4)

Structural analysis reveals that 4 is a (3,6)-connected twofold interpenetrating rutile architecture. Compound 4 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with space group P21/c. As shown in Fig. 4a, Zn1 is six-coordinated with a distorted octahedral geometry involving two oxygen atoms from two carboxylate groups, two μ3-OH, and two coordinated solvent molecules. Zn2 is four-coordinated, showing a tetrahedral coordination environment with three oxygen atoms from three carboxylate groups and one μ3-OH. The connection of the centrosymmetric tetranuclear Zn cluster Zn4(μ3-OH)2(COO)6 serving as the hexatopic basic SBU and the ligand TCPB3− acting as a trinodal building block results in a binodal 3-D (3,6)-connected network topology, which can be described as a rutile net with the Schläfli symbol of (42·610·83) (4·62)2 (Fig. 4b).
Fig. 4 Crystal structure of 4. (a) Tetranuclear zinc cluster Zn4(μ3-OH)2(COO)6·solvent as a SBU. Symmetry code: i: −x, 1 − y, 2 − z. (b) A schematic drawing of the 3-D network showing a rare (3,6)-connected rutile net topology. (c) Schematic view of the twofold interpenetration rutile network. (d) A twofold interpenetration in the framework with a space-filling model. H atoms are omitted for clarity.

It is interesting to find that another identical rutile network exists in the single-crystal structure, and the two discrete networks are displaced from each other by a distance of 12.2 Å. Because of the spacious nature of the single network, it allows other rutile networks to intertwine with it solely through van der Waals interactions, thus resulting in a twofold interpenetrating rutile array (Fig. 4c and d). Moreover, it is noted that compound 4 has a 3-D aperture, 6.4 × 3.9 Å2 channels along the [100] direction and 3.5 × 2.1 Å2 and 8.3 × 11.4 Å2 channels along the [010] and [001] directions (Fig. S1†). The solvent cavity is 62.6% of the crystal volume (3370.2 Å3 of the 5385.9 Å3 unit cell volume) calculated by PLATON analysis, a value seldom observed for interpenetrating networks, which generally incorporate either no free or a very small solvent cavity.18

Effect of metals and different solvent

Based on the above description of the syntheses and crystal structures of 1–4 (Scheme 1), we could conclude that the solvents and metal ions have important effects on the features and overall structures of the four MOFs with respect to their construction, flexibility, coordination modes, etc.19 As described in the Experimental section, all the complexes were synthesized at 80 °C under solvothermal conditions with different metal ions and solvents (Co2+, DMF and CH3OH in 1; Cd2+, DMF in 2; Mn2+, DMF in 3, Zn2+, DEF and H2O in 4), except 3 was obtained by evaporation of solvent following a solvothermal reaction. Although the metal(1) in complexes 1–3 lies in an inversion centre with a ∠M2–M1–M2(i) angle of 180°, the terminal coordinated solvent molecules of the linear trinuclear M3(O2CR)6 are obviously different (CH3OH in 1; H2O in 2; DMF and H2O in 3, DEF and H2O in 4). However, complexes 1 to 3 possess similar secondary building units and features in a similar (3,6)-connected 2D network with kgd topology, but displaying different crystal systems and space groups, especially the distinct 3-D stacking structures. Complex 3 has lower point and lattice symmetry, resulting in smaller 1-D lattice channels. However, complex 4 has a 3-D twofold interpenetrated (3,6)-connected rutile topology. In 3, the formed 2D networks are stacked in a parallel fashion with a distance of 5.8 Å between two central phenyl rings from two adjacent layers, while the layers in both 1 and 2 are stacked in an offset fashion with a distance of 4.6 Å and 9.8 Å, respectively (Fig. S2†). The significant staggered packing of the network may be caused by the formation of a stable lattice via optimization of the packing interactions. Compared with the rigid ligands, flexible ligands are easy to bend and rotate freely and have a tendency to generate subtle structure diversifications due to the conformational freedom. When the ligand interacts with metal ions, the C3 symmetry of H3TCPB is reduced and undergoes distortion. The trigonal angles of the ligands are in the range of 112.7–132.7° in 1, 111.1–129.3° in 2, 61.6–168.3° in 3, and 115.5–122.3° in 4 (Table S2†). Herein, owing to the incorporation of flexible ether groups into the rigid benzene spacers, the semi-rigid TCPB3− ligand has a strong ability to manage the connectivity and topology of the coordination networks.

Thermogravimetric analysis and X-ray powder diffraction

To examine the thermal stability, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on polycrystalline samples under a N2 atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. As shown in Fig. 5, TGA of 1 shows the first weight loss at a temperature up to 200 °C amounting to 18.13%, which may be attributed to the loss of four DMF guest molecules (calcd 18.74%). The second weight loss (8.31%) is in the range of 200 to 400 °C, corresponding to the loss of four coordinated CH3OH molecules (calcd 7.96%). When the temperature is raised above 400 °C, an abrupt weight loss occurs, which suggests that the structure has decomposed. The TG curves of 2 in the range of 25–400 °C show a total loss of 22.3%, which corresponds to the loss of DMF and water molecules (calcd 22.6%). It is noted that there are 19 water solvent molecules in the single-crystal structure of 2 that is obtained at 150 K. The loss of water molecules is in agreement with the observation that the as-synthesized crystals of 2 are apt to lose the partial water molecules at ambient temperature as soon as they are isolated from the mother liquor, which is further confirmed by elemental analysis. The TG curves in the range of 25–400 °C show a total loss of 23.4% (for 3) and 39.3% (for 4), which corresponds to the loss of all solvent molecules (calcd 22.5% and 40.2%, respectively). Above 400 °C, an abrupt weight loss occurs, indicative of the decomposition of the whole framework. The final residuals after thermal decomposition of compounds 1–4 were presumed to be Co3O4 (found 15.1%, calcd 15.4%), CdO (found 21.9%, calcd 22.8%), Mn3O4 (found 15.2%, calcd 15.7%), and ZnO (found 17.0%, calcd 15.5%), respectively.

PXRD patterns for the bulk samples of 1–4 were also carried out at room temperature. For 1 to 4, most peak positions of simulated and experimental patterns are nearly identical to those of the as-synthesized samples, indicative of the phase purity of the bulk samples. The dissimilarities in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples (Fig. S3†).

Electrochemical performance for lithium storage

The electrochemical performance of Zn-TCPB for lithium storage as an anode material was investigated. Fig. 6a shows the galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles at 100 mA g−1 in the voltage range 0–3 V versus Li+/Li. The first discharge and charge specific capacities are ∼1180 and 784 mAh g−1, respectively. The large irreversible capacity can be attributed to the formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layer and electrolyte decomposition.20 The cycling performance of the Zn-TCPB electrodes is presented in Fig. 6b. The specific capacity of synthesized Zn-TCPB electrode becomes stable and reversible after the initial few cycles. The capacity can be up to ∼455 mA h g−1 at 0.1 A g−1 after 100 cycles, which is superior to those of most reported porous MOF materials.21 The coulombic efficiencies of the Zn-TCPB materials showed more than ∼95% after the initial few cycles. In addition, the prepared Zn-TCPB materials also showed a remarkable rate capability. At current densities of 100, 500, and 1000 mA g−1, the reversible capacities of the Zn-TCPB materials are ∼457, 322, and 239 mA h g−1, respectively (Fig. 6c). The capacity could recover back to ∼446 mA h g−1 at 100 mA g−1 after the rate performance test, demonstrating the excellent recyclability of the as-prepared materials. Fig. S4† shows a typical cyclic voltammogram of the Zn-TCPB electrode. There is one reduction peak of ∼0.82 V in the first cycle that is ascribed to the irreversible processes between the Zn-TCPB electrode material and the cointercalation of lithium ions into frameworks.22 From the second to the fifth cycle, there is no clear change, suggesting that the Zn-TCPB electrode is stable with reversible reactions during the following charge–discharge cycles.

Conclusions

Four new transition metal–organic frameworks based on linear trinuclear/tetranuclear secondary building units and a triangular flexible carboxylic ligand have been successfully obtained. Complexes 1 to 3 show a 2-D (3,6)-connected kgd net topology, while 4 exhibits a twofold interpenetrating 3-D framework with rutile topology. The prepared Zn-TCPB materials as anode electrodes show good electrochemical performance with high reversible specific capacity and remarkable rate capability.
