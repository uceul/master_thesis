Crystal structure, morphology and sorption behaviour of porous indium-tetracarboxylate framework materials†

Jinjie Qian *abc, Panpan Yu ab, Kongzhao Su c, Youqing Dong ab, Shaoming Huang *ab and Maochun Hong c
aCollege of Chemistry and Materials Engineering, Wenzhou University, Wenzhou, Zhejiang 325035, China. E-mail: smhuang@wzu.edu.cn
bZhejiang Key Laboratory of Carbon Materials, Wenzhou, Zhejiang 325035, China
cState Key Laboratory of Structure Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, China

Received 14th July 2015 , Accepted 1st October 2015

First published on 7th October 2015

In this report, two totally new indium-tetracarboxylate framework isomers based on the same starting tetracarboxylate ligand are presented, namely, [Me2NH2][In(BPTC)]·Guest (InOF-12) and [Hdabco][In(BPTC)]·Guest (InOF-13). Their corresponding crystal structures essentially originated from the guest templates and were then fully characterized and solved by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. Through low pressure gas capacity comparison, it is noted that desolvated InOF-12 exhibits a better performance and its H2 isotherm reaches a high uptake capacity value of 1.32 wt% at 77 K and 1.0 bar, and a high carbon dioxide uptake of 82.2 cm3 g−1 (3.67 mmol g−1) at 273 K and 1.0 bar, while InOF-13 has a lower value of 80.4 cm3 g−1 (3.59 mmol g−1) under the same conditions.

1. Introduction

Since the emergence of crystalline metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs), the extraordinary ability of MOFs to flexibly tune well-refined crystal structures and internal pore environments gives them a huge advantage over conventional solid state porous materials, including artificial zeolites1 and carbon nanotubes.2 Recently, with more and more MOF materials acting as excellent candidates in many practical applications, these inorganic–organic hybrid materials are extensively applied to natural gas separation and storage,3 heterogeneous catalysis,4 biochemical sensing,5 drug delivery6 and lithium-ion batteries.7

On the other hand, the structural stability of the MOF framework is the most important factor for further applications, because highly porous structures decompose after the removal of supporting guest solvents. Therefore, tremendous efforts have been dedicated to the design and synthesis of rigid water-/acid-/base-resistant MOFs, especially zirconium-based MOFs in recent years.8 Among those crystalline materials, indium–organic frameworks (InOFs) are one branch of stable MOF materials due to the high coordination number of the trivalent indium ion. For example, previously we reported one 3-dimensional water-stable structure (InOF-1) with chiral 41 In(OH)(COO)2 helix chains, which exhibits a selective sorption capacity towards carbon dioxide (CO2) over methane (CH4) and nitrogen (N2) at 273 K.9

Although it is a facile approach to obtain functional MOF structures with predicted coordination modes for inorganic building units using extended organic ligands,10 the procedure to prepare new and large ligands is time-consuming and usually gives low yields. Sometimes, the expected single-crystal frameworks are not even successfully acquired owing to the characteristics of the organic ligand.10a In contrast to the use of new ligands, many MOF chemists have long been trying to construct novel MOF structures using some common and reported ligands under different solvothermal conditions to resolve some harsh environment-related problems.11

Despite the fact that many InOF structures are obtained using In(III) ions and multi-carboxylate ligands,12 publications on the preparation of indium–organic frameworks constructed from the same starting carboxylate ligands are still rare. Except for NOTT-204 and InOF-1, we have subsequently synthesized another new negatively charged framework (InOF-2) with a unc-type topology from biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylic acid (H4BPTC), which goes through a distinct porosity transition from a reversible type-I N2 isotherm to an irreversible type-IV one with a hysteresis loop by simple thermal treatment.13 Now we present herein a comprehensive investigation into a total of five diverse indium-tetracarboxylate framework materials with different synthesis conditions (Scheme 1), and their morphology and gas sorption behaviour in the low pressure region were studied. These include two new InOF crystals, which are a pair of framework isomers and formulated as [Me2NH2][In(BPTC)]·Guest (InOF-12) and [Hdabco][In(BPTC)]·Guest (InOF-13) (Hdabco = single protonated 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]-octane).

2. Experimental section

2.1 Materials and methods

All reactions were carried out in 23 ml glass vials under autogenous pressure. All the reactants are of reagent-grade quality and used as commercially purchased without further purification. The power X-ray diffraction patterns (PXRD) were collected using a Rigaku MiniFlex 600 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.154 nm). Single component gas adsorption measurements were performed in an Accelerated Surface Area and Porosimetry 2020 System (ASAP 2020). Elemental analyses (for C, H, and N) were carried out on a German Elementary Vario EL III instrument. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were conducted on a NETZSCH Model STA 449C unit at a heating rate of 10 Kelvin per minute under flowing nitrogen atmosphere. Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) images were taken on a Phenom G2 instrument.

2.2 Synthesis of [Me2NH2][In(BPTC)]·4/3EtOH·3H2O·2/3DMA (InOF-12)

A mixture of InCl3 (0.10 mmol, 33 mg) and H4BPTC (0.10 mmol, 33 mg) in a DMA (3.0 ml, DMA = N,N′-dimethylacetamide) and EtOH (3.0 ml) mixed solvent system with an extra drop of nitric acid (0.1 ml; HNO3 65 wt%) was sealed in a glass vial, which was heated at 85 °C for 5 days, and then cooled down to room temperature. After being washed with fresh MeCN, colorless cubic crystals were obtained in ca. 60% yield based on the organic ligand. Elemental analysis calculated for InOF-12: C, 42.42%; H, 5.19%; N, 3.53%. Found: C, 41.40%; H, 5.27%; N, 3.52%. Finally, the phase purity of the sample was confirmed by PXRD (Fig. 3 and S6†).

2.3 Synthesis of [Hdabco][In(BPTC)]·3H2O·1/3DEF (InOF-13)

A mixture of In(NO3)3 (0.10 mmol, 33 mg), H4BPTC (0.10 mmol, 33 mg) and dabco (0.10 mmol, 11 mg) in DEF (5.0 ml, DEF = N,N′-diethylformamide) solvent with an additional HNO3 (0.1 ml) was sealed in a 23 ml glass vial, which was also heated at 85 °C for 5 days, and then cooled to room temperature. As for the charge equilibrium, the guest dabco molecules were protonated into charge-balancing Hdabco+ cations during the reaction process. After being washed with fresh MeCN and dried in air, colorless bulk crystals were obtained in ca. 40% product yield based on the H4BPTC ligand. Elemental analysis calculated for InOF-13: C, 44.28%; H, 4.50%; N, 5.09%. Found: C, 44.68%; H, 4.39%; N, 5.12%. The phase purity of the sample was also confirmed by PXRD (Fig. 3 and S6†).

2.4 Single crystal X-ray crystallography

The structure data of InOF-12 and InOF-13 were collected on a SuperNova (Dual, Cu at zero, Atlas) diffractometer equipped with a multi-layered mirror for Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) by using ω scan. The crystals were kept at 99.99(16) K during the whole data collection time. The structures were resolved by direct methods and refined on F2 by full-matrix least-squares methods with the program package SHELXTL-97. The crystallographic data and structure refinement parameters for these two crystals are listed in Table S1.† We have employed PLATON/SQUEEZE to calculate the contribution to the diffraction from the solvent region and thereby produced a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities because of the highly disordered guest molecules, which could not be modelled properly. The final formulae were calculated from the SQUEEZE results combined with the elemental analysis data and TGA data.

Crystallographic data for the structures reported in this paper have been deposited at the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre and allocated the deposition numbers (CCDC no.) 1039033 and 1039034 for InOF-12 and InOF-13, respectively.

2.5 Solvent-exchange and activation procedure

Prior to the single component gas sorption experiment, both InOF materials were MeCN-exchanged thoroughly, in which the acetonitrile solvent was refreshed daily, in order to remove the large formamide type molecules (DMA and DEF molecules). As a result, about 100 milligrams of MeCN-exchanged InOF-12 and InOF-13 were loaded into the sample basket within the ASAP 2020 System, and then both samples were degassed at 80 °C under dynamic vacuum for at least 10 hours to acquire their fully desolvated materials according to their corresponding TGA curves (Fig. S7†). The MeCN-exchanged and activated samples show excellent retention of their main frameworks.

2.6 Gas sorption measurement

By volumetric methods, the single component gas sorption isotherms (N2, CO2, CH4 and H2) for pressures in the range 0–1.1 bar were measured on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 surface area and pore size analyzer at 77 K, 87 K, 273 K and 293 K. All gases were of 99.998% purity or higher. The isotherms at 77 K and 87 K were measured in liquid nitrogen and argon baths, respectively, whereas the isotherms at 273 K and 295 K were measured using liquid circulators to maintain a constant temperature. The specific surface areas were calculated using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method as well as the Langmuir equation from the N2 adsorption data at 77 K. The DFT pore size distributions were also calculated from the N2 adsorption data at 77 K. The isosteric heat of H2, CO2 and CH4 was calculated by the application of the Clausius–Clapeyron equation.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Crystal description of InOF-12

Under solvothermal reaction conditions, colourless InOF-12 crystals, with the framework formula [Me2NH2][In(BPTC)], were successfully prepared by heating the mixture of InCl3 salt and H4BPTC ligand in a 1:1 molar ratio in DMA/EtOH/HNO3 solvent (3:3:0.1, v/v/v; HNO3 65 wt%) in a 23 ml vial at 85 °C. Afterwards, its final formula with guest solvent molecules was calculated after the SQUEEZE process using the combination of thermogravimetric analysis data and elemental analysis data. For more details, please refer to the ESI.†

It is revealed by single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis that InOF-12 crystallizes in the tetragonal space group P42/mmc (SG #. 131) with cell parameters a = b = 9.9843(6) Å, c = 18.2816(18) Å, and V = 1822.4(2) Å3 (refer to Table S1†). In the asymmetric unit, it is found that there are 1/8 In(III) ion, 1/8 BPTC4− ligand and 1/8 disordered charge-balancing Me2NH2+ cation after the removal of other disordered and/or isolated solvent molecules.

Fig. 1a and S1† show that the In1 atom lies at a site with 2m symmetry and that the centre of the BPTC ligand lies at a site with mmm symmetry. Structurally speaking, each In(III) centre is 8-coordinated to 4 pairs of carboxylate groups from 4 separate fully deprotonated BPTC4− ligands in a chelating mode to make up a typical [In(CO2)4] building block (Fig. 1a and S1†) where the In–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.217(4) to 2.465(4) Å, and which is further interconnected to others by BPTC4− linkers to construct an anionic indium–organic [In(BPTC)]− framework.


Fig. 1 (a) Coordination environment of In(III) centre and BPTC4− ligand; symmetry code: #1: −x, 1 − y, z #2: −x, y, −z #3: x, 1 − y, −z. (b) Topological representation of InOF-12. (c) Views of the 3-dimensional structures with 1-dimensional open channels along three different axes.

Careful observation of the InOF-12 structure tells us that it is a 3-dimensional framework with 3 kinds of channels going along the a, b, and c axes (Fig. 1c), whose window sizes are 9.20 × 10.13 Å2, 10.13 × 9.20 Å2, and 10.13 × 10.13 Å2, respectively (Fig. S3†). In view of the topology, the ligand BPTC4− acts as a 4-connected node, and the typical [In(CO2)4] moiety also acts as a 4-connected node (section S2,†Fig. 1c). Therefore, InOF-12 can be structurally simplified as a 3-dimensional 4,4-connected 2-nodal PtS-type network with the same topological type as previously reported NOTT-204 with a tetragonal system but a relatively higher symmetry (Fig. 1b). Subsequently, what should be emphasized here is that all disordered and unrefined Me2NH2+ counter cations accommodated within the large open channels are the by-products of the in situ decomposition of the starting DMA solvent molecules, which leads to the final charge equilibrium for InOF-12.

3.2 Crystal description of InOF-13

Under very similar solvothermal conditions, colourless brick-like single crystals of InOF-13, with the main framework having the chemical formula [Hdabco][In(BPTC)], were successfully synthesized by heating the mixture of In(NO3)3 salt, H4BPTC and dabco ligand in a 1:1:1 molar ratio in DEF/HNO3 solvent (5:0.1, v/v; HNO3 65 wt%) in a glass vial. Meanwhile, the phase purity of the bulky crystalline products was also confirmed by the following PXRD analysis.

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that InOF-13 crystallizes in the orthorhombic space group P21212 with cell parameters a = 19.72440(10) Å, b = 17.08140(10) Å, c = 9.95540(10), and V = 3354.18(4) (Table S1†). As shown in Fig. 2a, the asymmetric unit consists of one In(III) ion, two different half BPTC4− ligands (BPTC1 and BPTC2), and one refined and isolated Hdabco+ ligand. In this case, the asymmetric unit has an In1 atom and one Hdabco+ cation located in a general position and two half BPTC4− anions, each lying about independent 2-fold axes. Although these two types of BPTC4− ligands have different symmetry operations, we note that both BPTC4− ligands in this structure are fully deprotonated and their carboxylate groups are used to construct the typical [In(CO2)4] building blocks with the observed In–O bond lengths in the range of 2.178(4)–2.457(6) Å (Fig. S1†).


Fig. 2 (a) Coordination environment of In(III) cation and BPTC4− ligand in the asymmetric unit. (b) Topological representation of InOF-13 in the a-axis. (c) Views of the 3-dimensional structures with open channels in a, b, and c axes. (d) Observed C–H⋯O bonds.

Only viewed along the a-axis, there are 1-dimensional narrow channels with a window size of 6.98 × 13.07 Å2, while there are no channels observed running along the remaining b and c axes (Fig. 2c). Topological analysis reveals that InOF-13 is a 3-dimensional 4,4,4-connected 3-nodal nou-type network which is rarely reported in the InOF realm with a point symbol of {4.6^5}2{4^2.8^4}{6^4.8^2}, whose topological diagram is illustrated in Fig. 2b (section S2†).

In this 3-dimensional InOF-13 structure, we note that through very weak noncovalent interactions between the main framework and the single protonated dabco ligand, the large charge-balancing Hdabco+ cation is stabilized in the narrow channel with the observed shortest C–H⋯O distance of 2.446 Å, as shown in Fig. 2d. On the other hand, in Zhou's Perspective article titled “Isomerism in Metal–Organic Frameworks: “Framework Isomers””, the authors pointed out that MOF researchers used to squeeze solvent molecules from the main structure and primarily discuss the topology of the framework itself.15 In this context, for InOF-12 and InOF-13, we notice that these two crystal structures have a completely equal anionic framework formula of [In(BPTC)] with different counter cations, such as the Me2NH2+ cation and the Hdabco+ ligand, respectively. As a consequence, it is more logical for us to refer to these two InOF materials in this case as a pair of framework isomers, because the stoichiometry of the skeletal frameworks of both crystals is exactly equivalent. Thus, we tried to compare the sorption capacity of these two new InOFs from a reasonable point of view.

3.3 Morphology analysis of five InOF crystals

Among this series of InOF networks, we are delighted to see that these structurally sophisticated indium-tetracarboxylate frameworks are all constructed from the same starting H4BPTC ligand. Through a search in the latest CCDC database, the research presented here is one rare example of the design and synthesis of a total of five single crystal structures including NOTT-204,16 InOF-1 (ref. 9) and InOF-2.13Fig. 3 shows the representative scanning electron microscope photographs of NOTT-204 (a), InOF-1 (b), InOF-2 (c), InOF-12 (d) and InOF-13 (e) which we prepared for the subsequent gas sorption experiment. Obviously, it can be seen from the SEM images that all five crystals exhibit totally different morphologies, in which NOTT-204 is solvothermally obtained according to the previous literature and has a uniform octahedral morphology (Fig. 3a and S8a†); InOF-1 crystals have a rice-like shape (Fig. 3b and S8b†); and InOF-2 crystals are truncated cuboids (Fig. 3c and S8c†). As for the new InOF-12 and InOF-13 crystals, their appearances are substantially different from the morphologies of the above-mentioned crystals, which originated from their structural characteristics. InOF-12 has an interesting box-like shape (Fig. 3d and S8d†), while InOF-13 showcases a brick-like shape (Fig. 3e and S8e†). Moreover, the phase purity of the microcrystalline materials has been also confirmed by PXRD analysis.
Fig. 3 Scanning electron microscope photographs and the corresponding PXRD patterns: (a) octahedral NOTT-204 crystals; (b) rice-like InOF-1 crystals; (c) truncated cuboid InOF-2 crystals; (d) box-like InOF-12 crystals; and (e) brick-like InOF-13 crystals. Meanwhile, the PXRD patterns simulated from the crystallographic information file are shown in black; from the as-prepared sample in red; from the desolvated sample in blue.

As we all know, the growth of crystals can be substantially affected by various factors during the solvothermal process, including solvent, temperature, reaction time, template and others. In this case, together with previously reported NOTT-204, InOF-1 and InOF-2, these five material morphologies in nature originated from the crystal structures, in which the different dihedral angles of BPTC4− ligands are distinctly induced by the free rotation of the two phenyl rings apart from the above-mentioned factors (InOF-12 0.00°; InOF-13 2.81°; NOTT-204 19.98°; InOF-1 33.08°; InOF-13 33.08°; InOF-2 62.93°, Fig. S5†). This phenomenon is very similar to the case we have reported on InOF-3 and InOF-4, which are also a pair of framework isomers due to the free rotation of phenyl rings from the same starting biphenyl-3,3′-dicarboxylate ligand (InOF-3 14.40° and 29.33°; InOF-4 144.31°).17

3.4 Sorption behaviour analysis of InOF-12 and InOF-13

Theoretically speaking, as calculated by the PLATON package, the potential porosity of InOF-12 is up to 68.1% (free volume: 1240.4 Å3; unit cell volume: 1822.4 Å3), and the porosity of InOF-13 is 47.2% (free volume: 1581.7 Å3; unit cell volume: 3354.2 Å3) with a probe size value of 1.2 Å after the removal of all solvent molecules and cations in the pore structure. The calculated mass density values for InOF-12 and InOF-13 are 0.8037(1) g cm−3 and 1.0975(1) g cm−3, respectively. Consequently, the calculated pore volumes are found to be 0.847 cm3 g−1 and 0.430 cm3 g−1, respectively.

On the other hand, experimentally, prior to the single component sorption experiment, we used the above-mentioned solvent exchange approach to remove most of the guest solvents within the pores, and the fully desolvated samples of InOF-12 and InOF-13 have been obtained by degassing under dynamic vacuum. To examine the retention of the microporosity of both desolvated materials, we recorded the corresponding N2 isotherms at liquid nitrogen temperature (77 K). The N2 isotherm of the activated InOF-12 crystals exhibits an irreversible type-IV N2 isotherm (Fig. 4a), while the N2 isotherm of InOF-13 shows us a typical type-I isotherm (Fig. 4b), with a maximum uptake value of 260.4 cm3 g−1 and 187.2 cm3 g−1 at 77 K and 1.0 bar, respectively. As one typical microporous solid, the analysis of N2 isotherms gives BET and Langmuir surface areas and a cumulative pore volume for InOF-12 of 824.4 m2 g−1, 1062.1 m2 g−1 and 0.403 cm3 g−1 (nearly half of the experimentally calculated value), respectively. Similarly, the BET and Langmuir surface areas and pore volume for InOF-13 are 729.9 m2 g−1, 806.2 m2 g−1 and 0.289 cm3 g−1 (two-thirds of the calculated value), respectively. At the same time, the pore size distribution curves obtained by the DFT pore size method show that there is a wide distribution of micropores centered at 5.0–10.0 Å with the highest distribution at ~7.5 Å, whose values are well consistent with the size of the internal pore structures for both frameworks (Fig. 4a and b).


Fig. 4 Comparison of the gas adsorption isotherms of InOF-12 (in red) and InOF-13 (in blue): a) a type-IV N2 isotherm for desolvated InOF-12. b) A type-I N2 isotherm for desolvated InOF-13; ● adsorption, ○ desorption. The inset shows the plot of pore size distribution incremental pore volume (V) vs. pore width (d). c) H2 isotherms at the low pressure zone at 77 K and 87 K for both activated samples. d) The isosteric heats of H2 sorption; e) CO2 isotherms at the low pressure zone at 273 K, 283 K and 295 K. f) The isosteric heats of CO2 sorption.

As hydrogen storage is a fierce topic in the field of energy, we tried to measure the volumetric H2 uptake at 77 K and 87 K at the same time. In contrast, as illustrated in Fig. 4c, both H2 isotherms exhibit rapid kinetics and good reversibility without any hysteresis loop. All low-pressure H2 isotherms show very high uptake capacities, with a maximum value of 147.4 cm3 g−1 (1.32 wt%) at 77 K and 1.0 bar, and 102.3 cm3 g−1 (0.91 wt%) at 87 K and 1.0 bar for InOF-12, while the uptake capacity reaches up to 135.0 cm3 g−1 (1.21 wt%) at 77 K and 1.0 bar, and 111.6 cm3 g−1 (1.00 wt%) at 87 K and 1.0 bar for InOF-13, whose data are better than those reported for InOF-2 (1.14 wt%) (5.72 mmol g−1) under the same synthesis conditions.13 More importantly, the isosteric enthalpy (Qst) of hydrogen is simulated by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation, and the values for InOF-12 and InOF-13 are calculated to be 6.83 kJ mol−1 and 6.29 kJ mol−1 at zero coverage, respectively, then both curves decrease continuously with an increase in the surface coverage over the entire pressure range (Fig. 4d), close to those for well-known MOF materials, for example, InOF-8 (6.51 kJ mol−1), HKUST-1 (6.6 kJ mol−1) and FJI-2 (7.18 kJ mol−1).18 In these two cases, we assume that the high H2 uptake capacity is mainly decided by the large guest-accessible porosity after the solvent-exchange and activation process, while the big binding affinity might be attributed to the pores and/or narrow channels which seem to better interact with small H2 molecules. Meanwhile, similar to the isotherms for H2, the isotherms for CO2 at 273 K, 283 K and 295 K exhibit good reversibility. Very rapid adsorption can be observed at low pressure (InOF-12, 26.9 cm3 g−1; InOF-13, 31.1 cm3 g−1 at 0.2 bar and 273 K, Fig. 4e) and may indicate the presence of a suitably strong interaction between the linear CO2 molecules and the host frameworks.

All in all, we note that InOF-12 has a better performance in terms of sorption capacity, such as the calculated and accumulative pore volume, but reaches a high CO2 uptake of 82.2 cm3 g−1 (3.67 mmol g−1) at 273 K and 1.0 bar, while InOF-13 has a slightly lower value (80.4 cm3 g−1, 3.59 mmol g−1). Likewise, at 1.0 bar pressure, InOF-12 possesses a saturated CO2 uptake of 70.1 cm3 g−1 at 283 K (3.13 mmol g−1) and 46.6 cm3 g−1 (2.08 mmol g−1) at 295 K; on the other hand, InOF-13 has a saturated CO2 uptake of 75.2 cm3 g−1 (3.36 mmol g−1) at 283 K and 52.5 cm3 g−1 (2.34 mmol g−1) at 295 K (Fig. 4e, S11 and S15†). Although the density of adsorbed carbon dioxide in these two structures is unclear, it can be deduced that the volumes of CO2 adsorbed in InOF-12 and InOF-13 at 1.0 bar and 273 K are 0.157 cm3 g−1 and 0.153 cm3 g−1, respectively, by utilizing the known density of 1.032 g cm−3 of liquid CO2 at 253 K. Thus, these values correspond to coverages of 29.0% and 52.9% of the total experimentally calculated pore volume of 0.403 cm3 g−1 and 0.289 cm3 g−1, as mentioned above, respectively. Therefore, in this case, we tend to suppose that these relatively low values are theoretically acceptable in view of the high adsorption temperature during the experiment, which might be also induced by an incomplete removal of large solvents during the solvent exchange and activation procedure. Moreover, the initial affinities of InOF-12 and InOF-13 for CO2 are found to have fairly high values of 26.53 kJ mol−1 and 20.41 kJ mol−1 at zero coverage, respectively. These high Qst values indicate that there is some weak interaction, and similarly increase very slowly with an incessant loading below 6.0 mg g−1 (Fig. 4f).

4. Conclusions

In conclusion, the report presented here is a comprehensive investigation on the construction of indium-tetracarboxylate frameworks starting from the biphenyl-3,3′,5,5′-tetracarboxylate ligand. Through the 8-coordinated [In(CO2)4] moieties, we have successfully obtained one pair of new framework isomers, namely, InOF-12 and InOF-13. With different solvent systems, the dihedral angles of the BPTC4− ligand change. Moreover, together with the previously reported NOTT-204 (octahedral), InOF-1 (rice-like) and InOF-2 (truncated cuboid), new InOF-12 (box-like) and InOF-13 (brick-like) all show totally different morphologies, as displayed in SEM images. Through gas capacity comparison, we found that InOF-12 exhibits a better low pressure performance than InOF-13, and efforts to further explore new indium–organic framework materials are under way.
