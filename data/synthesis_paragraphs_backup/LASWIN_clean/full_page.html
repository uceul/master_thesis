Two pillared-layer metal–organic frameworks constructed with Co(II), 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate, and 4,4′-bipyridine: syntheses, crystal structures, and gas adsorption properties†

Ping Song , Bin Liu , Yaoqi Li , Junzhi Yang , Zheming Wang and Xingguo Li *
Beijing National Laboratory for Molecular Sciences (BNLMS), The State Key Laboratory of Rare Earth Materials Chemistry and Applications, College of Chemistry and Molecular Engineering, Peking University, Beijing, 100871, China. E-mail: xgli@pku.edu.cn; Fax: +86-10-62765930; Tel: +86-10-62765930

Received 18th May 2011 , Accepted 20th December 2011

First published on 27th January 2012

Two new metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) [Co2(btec)(bipy)2(DMF)]·DMF·3H2O (1) (btec = 1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate; bipy = 4,4′-bipyridine; DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide) and Co2(btec)(bipy)(DMF)2 (2) were synthesized with the same starting materials under different solvothermal conditions. The higher temperature favors the generation of compound 2, while the slightly lower temperature with the higher bipy ratio leads to the porous structure of compound 1. Their structures are determined by single-crystal X-ray diffractions. Compound 1 is a 3D pillared-layer framework with interconnected channels along two directions after the removal of free guest molecules. The BET (Brunauer–Emmett–Teller) surface area of 1 was calculated to be 596 m2 g−1 by nitrogen adsorption at 77 K. The hydrogen adsorption isotherm at 77 K shows an excess uptake of 1.1 wt% at 15 bar. The heat (Qst) of hydrogen adsorption was estimated to be 7.3 kJ mol−1 at zero coverage. In compound 2, the wave-like layers constructed with btec are further pillared by bipy, giving rise to the 3D framework with the 1D channels occupied by coordinated DMF molecules.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are extended structures constructed with metal ions and organic linkers, which demonstrate outstanding properties exceeding those of traditional porous materials. They have attracted great attention in the areas of gas adsorption, separation, catalysis, and other related applications.1 Many MOFs show remarkable capacity and specific selectivity in gaseous adsorption.2 They have exhibited several excellent properties, such as full reversibility, cyclic stability, and rapid kinetics.3 Also, the crystallinity of MOFs can provide insights into gas adsorption mechanisms which are often lacking in the porous materials without long-range order. Hydrogen is considered a promising energy source to replace fossil fuels. The lack of hydrogen storage systems to meet the practical requirements is a bottleneck for hydrogen economy. However, most MOFs could only exhibit remarkable hydrogen uptake under cryonic temperatures, due to the low adsorption heat of physisorption.4

MOF structures can be designed with various metal ions and organic ligands.5 Even the same starting materials may generate several structures with different coordination modes.6 The mixed-ligand systems with O- and N-donors introduce even more diversity to MOF structures and often lead to pillared-layer structures. The MOFs of this type possess more complex pore environments, which may give interesting gas adsorption properties.7 Although there have been plenty of MOFs reported, it is still a challenge to control the structures by altering the synthetic conditions, especially for the solvothermal reactions.8 Btec (1,2,4,5-benzenetetracarboxylate) is a multi-coordinating ligand with four carboxyl groups, which shows a number of coordination modes in the construction of MOF structures.9 The compact structures are generally predominant over the porous ones. Only a few 3D frameworks formed by btec demonstrate permanent porosity after activation.10

In this work, we synthesize two new pillared-layer MOFs [Co2(btec)(bipy)2(DMF)]·DMF·3H2O (1) (bipy = 4,4′-bipyridine; DMF = N,N′-dimethylformamide) and Co2(btec)(bipy)(DMF)2 (2) by altering the molar ratios of the starting materials and the temperatures. The influences of the synthetic conditions on structures are discussed. Compound 1 demonstrates doubly stacked layers, which are much thicker than those in typical pillared-layer MOFs.11 Compound 2 possesses wave-like layers and is also further pillared by bipy molecules. Compound 1 exhibits permanent porosity after the removal of free guest molecules. The nitrogen and hydrogen adsorption properties are investigated.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

Elemental analysis was performed on an Elementar Vario El analyzer. Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Magna-IR 750 spectrometer equipped with a Nic-Plan microscope for pure samples. Powder X-ray diffractions (PXRDs) were performed on a Rigaku D/Max2500 VB2 + PC with monochromatic Cu Kα radiation. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) were performed on a thermal analysis TGA-DSC-DTA (Q600 SDT) and a DSC 2010 differential scanning calorimeter, with a heating rate of 5 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. The nitrogen adsorption isotherms were measured with a COULTER SA 3100 apparatus at 77 K. Low-pressure hydrogen adsorption isotherms were measured by the volumetric method on a Micromeritics ASAP2020 instrument. High-pressure isotherms were recorded using an automated Sieverts' apparatus (PCT-Pro 2000 from Hy-Energy LLC). The as-synthesized samples were heated under dynamic vacuum before each gas adsorption test.

Synthesis

All reagents and solvents are from commercial suppliers without further purification.

[ Co 2 (btec)(bipy) 2 (DMF)]·DMF·3H 2 O (1). X-Ray crystallography and measurements

Crystallographic data for 1 and 2 were collected at 290 K on a Nonius KappaCCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated MoKα radiation (λ = 0.71073 K.).12 Empirical absorption corrections were applied using the Sortav program.13 The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2 using SHELX14 with anisotropic thermal parameters for all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms were added geometrically and refined using the riding model. The SQUEEZE option of PLATON was used to eliminate the contribution of disordered guest molecules to the reflection intensities. Results and discussion

Syntheses and infrared spectra

Both the compounds were synthesized with Co(NO3)2·6H2O, btec and bipy ligands in DMF. For compound 1, the feeding molar ratio for Co:btec:bipy is 2:1:3, while for compound 2, the ratio is 2:1:1. The synthetic temperature for compound 1 and 2 is 80 and 90 °C, respectively. The phase purity of 2 can be easily obtained. However, to obtain high purity of compound 1, the molar ratio and temperature should be carefully controlled to avoid the generation of 2 in the system. The stability of these two samples is investigated under ambient conditions. The red crystal of 1 turns into a pink powder gradually over a few weeks, while the purple crystal of 2 is very stable. This indicates a higher stability of compound 2 than that of 1. A higher temperature is favorable for the generation of the more stable structure 2. On the other hand, to get the porous structure of compound 1, the temperature should be lower and more bidentate bipy ligands are required.

In the IR spectrum of compound 1 (Fig. S2 in the ESI†), the broad band centered at 3178 cm−1 is attributed to the stretching vibrations of O–H, suggesting the presence of hydrogen bonding. After the sample evacuated at 120 °C for 2 hours, the strong peak is weakened and the adsorption peaks corresponding to N–H and C–H stretching are revealed, indicating the removal of free guest molecules. In the IR spectrum of compound 2 (Fig. S3 in the ESI†), the peaks between 3440 and 2820 cm−1 can be assigned to the stretching of N–H and C–H bonds. It is confirmed that there are no free water molecules in the as-synthesized sample. The antisymmetric and symmetric stretching vibrations of the carboxylate group are observed between 1647–1561 cm−1 and 1489–1362 cm−1. The differences between these peaks of the two compounds suggest different binding modes of the carboxylate groups. The IR spectra of 1 and 2 fully support the crystal structures solved by SXRD (single X-ray diffraction).

Structures

Compound 1 crystallizes in the space group P with two unique octahedrally coordinated Co2+ ions. Co1 is coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from three btec molecules and two nitrogen atoms from two bipy molecules (Fig. 1a). Co2 is coordinated by three carboxylate oxygen atoms from two btec molecules, one oxygen atom from the coordinated DMF, and two nitrogen atoms from two bipy molecules. The distance between the adjacent Co1 and Co2 atoms linked by carboxylate bridges is 4.8 Å. The Co–O bond distances are in the range of 2.060–2.119 Å, and the Co–N bond distances are in the range of 2.137–2.215 Å. Compound 1 is a 3D porous framework with a pillared-layer structure. Each btec molecule coordinates to Co2+ in the fashion as shown in Fig. 2a, and this connection leads to an infinite ladder chain. The adjacent chains are further linked by one type of bipy molecules (associated with N1 and N4) to form the 2D layers along the ac plane (Fig. 3a). The layers are further pillared by the other type of bipy molecules (associated with N2 and N3), leading to the 3D framework (Fig. 3b). The coordinated DMF molecules can be recognized as part of the layers. It is worth noting that the layers in 1 are actually doubly stacked and therefore are much thicker than those in typical pillared-layer frameworks constructed with linear-binary or planar-ternary carboxylates.15 The channels along the [100] direction are intercrossed by the ones along the [101] direction (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). The narrow windows of the two channels are 2.2 × 4.4 Å and 1.6 × 3.5 Å, respectively. The total potential solvent accessible void volume was calculated by the PLATON analysis to be 36.3% of the crystal volume i.e. 780.2 Å3 out of the 2148.9 Å3 per unit cell volume. There are free DMF and water molecules accommodated in the micropores.

Compound 2 crystallizes in the space groupP21/c. As shown in Fig. 1b, the unique Co2+ is in a distorted octahedral geometry and coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from two btec molecules, one oxygen atom from the coordinated DMF and one nitrogen atom from the bipy molecule. The Co–O bond distances are in the range of 2.051–2.249 Å. The Co–N bond distance is 2.089 Å. Each btec molecule coordinates with four Co2+ ions. The btec and bipy molecules lie about independent inversion centres in the structure, while the Co2+ ion and the coordinated DMF molecule are in general positions (Fig. 4). In contrast to the layers in 1, the layers in 2 are constructed without bipy molecules, extending in a wave-like manner along the bc plane (Co2+: serving as the hinge nodes; btec: as the rods). The bipy molecules further link the hinge nodes of adjacent layers to form the 3D structure. The 1D honeycomb-like channels running along the c axis are occupied by the coordinated DMF molecules.

The phase purity of the two compounds is confirmed by the PXRD experiments. As shown in Fig. 5 and S4 in the ESI†, the PXRD patterns of the two compounds agree well with the simulations based on the SXRD results. The PXRD pattern of sample 1 degassed at 120 °C is very similar to that of the as-synthesized sample, indicating that the framework maintains intact after evacuation. The weight loss without remarkable changes in PXRD patterns suggests a great possibility of permanent porosity. The calculated weight loss corresponds to one free DMF and three H2O molecules per formula unit, which is consistent with the results of elemental analyses.

Thermal study and nitrogen adsorption

TGA and DSC analyses were performed on compounds 1 and 2 under nitrogen flow in the temperature range of 30–600 °C. The TGA, DSC, and differential thermogravimetric (DTG) curves of compound 1 are shown in Fig.6. The TGA curve of 1 suggests that the loss of the guest molecules is followed by the decomposition of the framework without obvious platforms between the thermal steps. The positions of most peaks in the DTG curve agree with those in the DSC curve. The weight loss is 14.4 wt% before 160 °C in the TGA curve, corresponding to one DMF and three water molecules per formula unit. The first two peaks in the DSC curve are assigned to the loss of free water and DMF guest molecules, respectively. The weight loss at 210 °C is 23%, corresponding to further loss of the coordinated DMF molecules (the third peak in the DSC curve). Meanwhile, the framework starts to collapse. The large weight loss after 250 °C (corresponding to the last three peaks in the DSC curve) is attributed to the reactions during the framework decomposition. On the other hand, there is no remarkable weight loss in compound 2 before the framework starts to collapse at approximate 300 °C (Fig. S5 in the ESI†). The coordinated DMF molecules in these two compounds are difficult to remove without any framework collapse. This is probably due to the stronger interactions of the coordination bonding than the hydrogen bonding of free guest molecules.
Fig. 6 The TGA, DSC and DTG curves of compound 1.

The N2 adsorption property of compound 1 was studied at 77 K. In Fig. S6 in the ESI†, the isotherm shows type I adsorption behavior without significant hysteresis, which is typical of microporous materials.16 The BET (Brunauer–Emmett–Teller) surface area is calculated to be 596 m2 g−1. The pore volume of 0.344 cm3 g−1 estimated with the T-plot method relates to a porosity of 33.9%, which is close to 36.3% calculated based on the crystal structure of 1. The surface area decreased when the degassing temperature becomes higher than 120 °C. This temperature is lower than that indicated by the TGA curve. This is because the temperature is maintained for hours under dynamic vacuum before gas adsorption, while the temperature goes up at a constant rate under nitrogen flow in the thermal analyses. It can be concluded that the direct removal of the coordinated DMF molecules is accompanied by framework collapse.

Hydrogen adsorption

The excess and total H2 adsorption isotherms of compound 1 at 77 K are shown in Fig. 7a. The excess isotherm reaches the saturation around 15 bar with an excess capacity of 1.1 wt%. The total capacity was estimated to be approximately 1.3 wt% (13 g H2 L−1).17 The H2 isotherms under 0–1 bar were measured at 77 and 88 K (Fig. S7 in the ESI†). The heat (Qst) of H2 adsorption was calculated by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation (Fig. 7b), and the Qst at zero coverage is estimated to be 7.3 kJ mol−1. The value is similar to that of Ni2(BTEC)(bipy)3 (7.1 kJ mol−1) reported earlier.18 This is attributed to the similar chemical environment in the micropores. The coordination saturated metal ions in MOF structures are considered to have little effect on gas adsorption properties. To our knowledge, the initial Qst of compound 1 is higher than the average value for MOFs without exposed metal sites.19 This is because the size of the micropores in 1 is comparable to the kinetic diameter of H2 molecules (2.8 Å). Despite a relatively small pore volume, narrow micropores in MOFs favor the interaction with H2 by introducing multi-interaction sites.20
Fig. 7 (a) H2 isotherms for compound 1 at 77 K. The shaded symbols and open symbols represent adsorption and desorption, respectively. The red circles above represent the total hydrogen uptake under high pressure. (b) The heat of H2 adsorption in compound 1.

Conclusion

We have successfully synthesized two pillared-layer MOF structures [Co2(btec)(bipy)2(DMF)]·DMF·3H2O (1) and Co2(btec)(bipy)(DMF)2 (2) with the same starting materials. Compound 1 possesses doubly stacked layers and exhibits permanent porosity with interconnected channels along two directions after the removal of guest molecules. Compound 2 has wave-like layers, and the 1D channels along the c axis are occupied by coordinated DMF molecules. The relatively strong interactions with H2 of 1 are attributed to the narrow micropore structures. This work implies that for multi-dentate ligands a higher temperature favors the generation of compact and stable products, while a lower temperature with higher ratio of rigid linear ligands may lead to porous structures. Work in progress is to remove the coordinated DMF molecules in these two compounds by other methods such as solvent-exchange, and investigate their separation properties to gaseous mixtures.
