Synthesis, characterization and photoluminescent properties of Zn-based mono- and hetero-MOFs containing the R-isophthalate (R = methyl or tert-butyl) ligands†

Qi-Bing Bo *a, Hong-Yan Wang a and Da-Qi Wang b
aSchool of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, University of Jinan, Jinan 250022, China. E-mail: chm_boqb@ujn.edu.cn
bCollege of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Liaocheng University, Liaocheng 252059, China

Received (in Montpellier, France) 24th August 2012 , Accepted 15th October 2012

First published on 19th October 2012

Four novel Zn-based homo- and hetero-metal organic frameworks (MOFs), [Zn2(H2O)(mip)2]n (Zn-mip), [ZnMg(H2O)2(mip)2·3(H2O)]n (ZnMg-mip), [Zn3(OH)2(tbip)2]n (Zn-tbip) and [Zn2Ba(O)(H2O)2(tbip)2.5·(H2O)]n (ZnBa-tbip), have been hydrothermally synthesized by employing the single ligand 5-methyl isophthalic acid (H2mip) or 5-tert-butyl isophthalic acid (H2tbip). The structures of all the MOFs are determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction analyses and further characterized by elemental analyses, inductively coupled plasma (ICP) analysis, FT-IR spectroscopy, DTA/TG analyses and X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) techniques. The results indicate that all the homo- and hetero-MOFs exhibit the 3D open frameworks with the unique Zn-based cluster second building units (SBUs). The influence of the coordination geometries of the hetero-metal centers on the structures of Zn-based mono-MOFs is realized through replacing the five (or four)-coordinated Zn atoms with the alkaline-earth metal ions. Moreover, compared with the free ligands, the photoluminescent properties of the Zn-mip, ZnMg-mip, Zn-tbip and ZnBa-tbip are also studied in the solid state at room temperature. The different emission bands of the homo- and hetero-MOFs demonstrate that the substituent effect of alkaline-earth metal ions plays a key role in directing the related photoluminescent properties of the Zn-based MOFs.

Introduction

It is well known that structural controlling is a prerequisite for improving the ability to rationally design and synthesize functional MOFs (metal organic frameworks), and studies in this field have been focused on the design and construction of novel MOFs, as well as the relationships between their structures and properties.1 The selection and synthesis of the organic bridging ligands often have a significant effect on the construction of desirable frameworks, because changes in flexibility, length, and symmetry of organic ligands can result in MOFs containing diverse architectures and functions. Therefore, organic ligands are usually modified with different substituents, and as a result a lot of functional MOFs containing organic ligands or their derivatives have been reported recently.2 However, most of the studies are concerned with homo-MOFs, and relatively little attention has been paid to the influence of incorporating the heterometallic ions into the crystal lattice of the homo-MOFs on the structural diversification and photoluminescent properties, especially for the Zn-based MOFs. While the MOFs containing metal ions with a d10 configuration, such as Zn2+ ions, often show structural diversities and intense photoluminescent properties, which may be used for fluorescence-based OLED devices or fluorescent detection in some biological systems.3 We wonder that if parts of the Zn2+ ions in the Zn-based homo-MOFs are substituted by some other heterometallic ions, what could happen in the analogous reactions among the different substituted ions, Zn2+ and the organic bridging ligands? Therefore, alkaline-earth metal ions are carefully introduced into the synthetic systems containing the single R-isophthalate (R = methyl or tert-butyl) ligands and Zn2+ ions, which is based on the following considerations: firstly, doping the alkaline-earth metal ions can efficiently tune the photoluminescence properties of the Zn-based inorganic host materials (such as ZnO).4 Secondly, parts of the 3D MOFs containing the alkaline earth metal ions also exhibit fluorescence in the solid state at room temperature,5 but the introduction of alkaline-earth metal ions into Zn-based mono-MOFs to study the structural variation and the photoluminescent property has not been documented. Thirdly, the reports of Zn-based hetero-MOFs with the ligands H2mip and H2tbip or their derivatives are scarce,6 although many of the Zn-based homo-MOFs with the same ligands have been synthesized and their photoluminescence properties are reported.7–9

With the aim of further understanding the influence of the substituent on the structures of the MOFs and preparing new materials with photoluminescent properties, herein four novel homo- and hetero-MOFs with Zn-based cluster SBUs have been synthesized from two kinds of single bridging ligands H2mip and H2tbip. In comparison with two Zn-based homo-MOFs, the substitutions of Zn ions with alkaline-earth metal ions generate two novel hetero-MOFs with interesting structural motifs. The studies show that by changing the substituent on Zn ions, it is possible to control the structural diversification and photoluminescent properties of the Zn-based homo-MOFs. The differences in their structures and photoluminescent behaviors are found to result from the nature of the substituted heterometallic ions and the single R-isophthalate (R = –CH3 or –C(CH3)3) bridging ligands. Considering lots of the Zn-based homo-MOFs reported in the literature, the systematic substitution of heterometallic ions will provide further insights into the rational design and preparation of novel hetero-MOFs with the structural diversifications and good photoluminescent properties in future.

Experimental section

General remarks

All the syntheses are performed in poly(tetrafluoroethylene)-lined stainless steel autoclaves under autogenous pressure. Reagents are purchased commercially and are used without further purification. Elemental analyses (C and H) are performed on a Perkin–Elmer 2400 Series II CHNS/O elemental analyzer. Zn, Mg and Ba are determined by Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) analysis performed on a Perkin–Elmer Optima 3300DV spectrometer. FT-IR spectra are recorded in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Perkin–Elmer FTIR spectrometer using KBr pellets. TG-DTA analyses are performed on a Perkin–Elmer Diamond TG/DTA instrument in flowing air at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Solid-state emission and excitation spectra are carried out on an Edinburgh FLS920 phosphorimeter equipped with a continuous Xe-900 xenon lamp and an nF900 ns flash lamp. X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns of the samples are recorded on an X-ray diffractometer (Rigaku D/Max 2200PC) with a graphite monochromator and CuKα radiation at room temperature while the voltage and electric current are held at 40 kV and 20 mA. Crystal data of the compounds Zn-mip, ZnMg-mip, Zn-tbip and ZnBa-tbip are collected on an Xcalibur, Eos, Gemini diffractometer (MoKα radiation, λ = 0.71073 Å). Data reduction is accomplished by the CrysAlisPro (Oxford Diffraction Ltd., Version 1.171.33.55) program. The structures are solved by a direct method and refined by a full matrix least-squares technique based on F2 using SHELXL 97 program.10 All of the non-hydrogen atoms are refined anisotropically. The organic hydrogen atoms are generated geometrically, the aqua hydrogen atoms are located from difference maps and refined with isotropic temperature factors. CCDC-865282 (ZnMg-mip), CCDC-865283 (Zn-mip), CCDC-884314 (Zn-tbip) and CCDC-884317 (ZnBa-tbip). In compound ZnMg-mip, the asymmetric unit includes a large region of disordered solvent water molecules, which could not be modeled as discrete atomic sites. We employed PLATON/SQUEEZE to calculate the diffraction contribution of the solvent water molecules and, thereby, to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities. The SQUEEZE calculations showed a total solvent accessible area volume of 1337.2 Å3 in ZnMg-mip and the residual electron density amounted to 220 (electron) per unit cell, corresponding to nearly 3 molecules of H2O per asymmetric unit. As for compound ZnBa-tbip, the results of the PLATON/SQUEEZE show that the contributions of some 38 to 40 electrons were removed from the unit cell contents. This is consistent with the asymmetric unit containing one additional lattice water molecule, which has been treated as a diffuse contribution to the overall scattering without specific atom positions. Drawings of the molecule are performed with the program Diamond.11 Crystallographic data and structure refinement for the Zn-based MOFs containing the mip and tbip ligands are summarized in Table 1.

Results and discussion

Hydrothermal reaction of 5-methylisophthalic acid (or 5-tert-butyl isophthalic acid) with zinc and the alkaline-earth metal salts affords four colorless crystals formulated by single-crystal X-ray diffraction as [Zn2(H2O)(mip)2]n (Zn-mip), [ZnMg(H2O)2(mip)2·3(H2O)]n (ZnMg-mip), [Zn3(OH)2(tbip)2]n (Zn-tbip) and [Zn2Ba(O)(H2O)2(tbip)2.5·(H2O)]n (ZnBa-tbip). Where, mip and tbip denote the 5-methylisophthalate and 5-tert-butyl isophthalate dianions, respectively. The structures determined by X-ray crystallography show that all of the crystals have different structures, and all the 5-methylisophthalic acids (or 5-tert-butyl isophthalic acids) are deprotonated to coordinate with the corresponding mono-or hetero-metallic ions.

Structural description of [Zn2(H2O)(mip)2]n (Zn-mip)

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that Zn-mip crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c. Both Zn1 and Zn2 are in the tetrahedral geometries. Zn1 is four-coordinated to three different carboxyl groups from three mip ligands and one coordinated water molecule with the Zn–O distances falling into 1.9489(2)–1.9660(2) Å. The Zn2 dwelling at the tetrahedral site is coordinated to four mip ligands with the average Zn–O distance being 1.9414(2) Å. The Zn1⋯Zn2 distance bridged by the two carboxylate groups is 3.7014(4) Å. Furthermore, the mip ligands exhibit two kinds of coordination modes: one acts as a tetradentate bridging ligand to link four Zn atoms with carboxylate groups adopting the μ2-η1:η1-bis-monodentate mode (1b, ESI†), the other tridentate bridging ligand links three Zn atoms adopting μ1-η0:η1-monodentate modes and μ2-η1:η1-bis-monodentate modes (1a, ESI†). Two pairs of the symmetry-related zinc atoms [Zn1⋯Zn1D = 7.2200(2) Å, Zn2⋯Zn2B = 6.8961(2) Å], and ten carboxylate groups (O–C1–O, O–C1B–O, O–C1D–O, O–C8C–O, O–C8G–O, O–C10–O, O–C10B–O, O–C17A–O, O–C17E–O and O–C17F–O) constitute a 10-connected noncentrosymmetric tetranuclear metallic cluster SBU Zn4(COO)10 (Fig. 1a and S2a, ESI†). Each of the Zn-based cluster SBUs is fist extended through O–C1–O and O–C8–O carboxylate groups (labeled with mauve and green parts in Fig. 1a), to entrain a 2D bilayer structure with the left-and right-handed double helical chains in the bc plane (Fig. 1b). Then, the adjacent 2D bilayers are pillared by 1D zig-zag double chains originating from the coordination of another carboxylate groups (O–C10–O and O–C17–O, labeled with blue parts in Fig. 1c) with the Zn4 clusters. Finally, a complicated 3D MOF constructed from the noncentrosymmetric Zn4 cluster SBUs and mip ligands is formed as shown in Fig. 1d. It is noticeable that the 3D MOF contains the close-packed 1D open channel along the c direction, and the diameter of these channels is sufficiently small that they are highly stable without the free water molecules. Topologically, as shown in Fig. 2a, each mip ligand is linked to five Zn4 clusters to represent a 5-connected node, and each Zn4 cluster serves for a 10-connected node by combining ten mip ligands, the resulting structure of Zn-mip displays an equivalent 3D topology framework with a mixed binodal (5, 10)-connected net (Fig. 2b), and the point (Schlafli) symbol for the net being (431.614)(49.6)2.

Structural description of [ZnMg(H2O)2(mip)2·3(H2O)]n (ZnMg-mip)

Single-crystal structure analysis reveals that ZnMg-mip is a novel 3D hetero-MOF with the formula [ZnMg(H2O)2(mip)2·3(H2O)]n. Each Mg atom has a slightly distorted octahedral environment consisting of six oxygen atoms from four different mip ligands and two terminal water molecules. Each Zn atom is in a tetrahedral environment, coordinated by four O atoms from four different mip ligands. The Mg–O and Zn–O bond lengths are in the range of 1.9852(38)–2.0835(40) Å and 1.9303(41)–1.9557(31) Å, respectively. The Zn1⋯Mg1 distance bridged by the two carboxylate groups is 3.9719(15) Å. The subtle distinction between Zn1 and Mg1 originates from the coordination modes of the μ4-bridge linking mip ligands as shown in S1c (ESI†). And the mip ligands lie about mirror planes. Two symmetry-related zinc and magnesium atoms (Zn1 vs. Zn1B, Mg1 vs. Mg1C) are bridged by ten carboxylate groups (O–C1–O, O–C1B–O, O–C8A–O, O–C8D–O, O–C8E–O, O–C10–O, O–C10B–O, O–C10C–O, O–C16–O and O–C16B–O) from different mip ligands. As a result, a noncentrosymmetric linear array consisting of a tetranuclear heterometallic cluster unit Zn2Mg2(COO)10, can be regarded as a 10-connected SBU with Zn1⋯Zn1B and Mg1⋯Mg1C separations being 7.7670(5) and 7.1035(5) Å, respectively (Fig. 3a and ESI† 2b). Furthermore, the adjacent carboxylate groups (O–C10–O and O–C16–O in Fig. 3a, labeled with mauve and green parts, respectively) in the SBUs coordinate to the heterometallic clusters Zn2Mg2 forming one 2D layer structure with the zig-zag double chains in the bc plane (Fig. 4). Then, the other 2D double chain layer is also generated through the coordination between the carboxylate groups (O–C1–O and O–C8–O, labeled with blue parts in Fig. 3a) and the Zn2Mg2 clusters in the ac plane (Fig. 5). Finally, these two kinds of 2D layers are cross-linked along the c-axis into a 3D porous MOF featuring irregular channels impregnated by parts of the free and coordinated water molecules, as shown in Fig. 3b. By virtue of the PLATON analysis,12 approximately 25.5% of the crystal volume is occupied by free water molecules (1259.2 out of the 4945.6 Å3 in each cell unit). Topologically, as shown in Fig. 6a, three kinds of mip ligands are linked to four, five and six Zn2Mg2 clusters, respectively. And each Zn2Mg2 cluster serves for a 10-connected node by combination of the ten mip ligands. Therefore, the equivalent 3D topology framework for ZnMg-mip can be viewed as a quadrinodal (4, 5, 6, 10)-connected net with a Schlafli symbol of (411.64)(432.613)2(46)(49.6)2, as shown in Fig. 6b.

Structural description of [Zn3(OH)2(tbip)2]n (Zn-tbip)

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound Zn-tbip crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. The crystal structure of Zn-tbip contains three Zn atoms, two hydroxyl groups and two mip ligands in the independent crystallographic unit. Both Zn1 and Zn2 atoms connect four carboxylate oxygen donors from three different tbip anions and one hydroxyl group, simultaneously adopt a tetrahedral coordination mode with the average Zn–O distance being 1.9459(1) Å. While Zn3 has distorted trigonal bipyramidal geometry, and is five-coordinated by two oxygen atoms from two tbip ligands and three oxygen atoms from three hydroxyl groups with the average Zn–O distance being 2.0466(1) Å. Furthermore, the tbip ligands act as tetradentate bridging ligand to link four Zn atoms with carboxylate groups adopting the μ2-η1:η1-bis-monodentate mode, as shown in1d (ESI†). The closest distances of Zn1⋯Zn3, Zn1⋯Zn2 and Zn2⋯Zn3 are 3.7074(2), 6.8479(3) and 3.2773(2) Å, respectively. Six symmetry-related zinc atoms [Zn1⋯Zn1A = 5.9428(3), Zn2⋯Zn2D = 6.2840(3), Zn3⋯Zn3D = 3.2709(1) Å], together with five hydroxyl groups, can be viewed as a Zn-based hexanuclear cluster. The hexanuclear cluster and eleven carboxylate groups constitute an 11-connected noncentrosymmetric SBU Zn6O5(COO)11, as shown in Fig. 7a and 2c (ESI†). Pairs of Zn-based hexanuclear clusters are linked by O–C1–O and O–C5–O carboxylate groups (labeled with mauve and green parts in Fig. 7a) from tbip ligands to form a 2D bilayer as shown in Fig. 7b, which consists of the infinite chain with alternating arrangement of 4- and 8-membered rings built by zinc atoms, hydroxyl groups and O–C5–O carboxylate ones. The Zn⋯Zn separations are 3.2709(1) and 3.3982(2) Å within the 4-membered ring and the 8-membered ring, respectively. At the same time, the 2D bilayer is further pillared by a 1D ribbon-like double chain consisting of Zn atoms, hydroxyl groups, O–C13–O and O–C17–O carboxylate ones (Fig. 7c), into a 3D porous MOF (Fig. 7d). Considering both the tbip ligands and the Zn6O5 clusters as nodes, the equivalent 3D topology framework for Zn-tbip can be viewed as a trinodal (5, 6, 11)-connected net with a Schlafli symbol of (413.62)(443.612)(49.6), as shown in Fig. 8.

Structural description of [Zn2Ba(O)(H2O)2(tbip)2.5]n (ZnBa-tbip)

Compound ZnBa-tbip crystallizes in the monoclinic space group Cm and the asymmetric unit contains one barium atom, two and half of tbip ligands, two coordinated water molecules, as well as two zinc atoms shared with a bridging oxygen atom. As shown in 1e ESI,† the carboxylate groups in ZnBa-tbip adopt μ2-η1:η1-bis-monodentate coordinated modes. Both Zn1 and Zn2 atoms adopt the tetrahedral coordination modes. Zn1 atom dwelling at the tetrahedral site is coordinated to the two carboxylate oxygen atoms from two different tbip ligands, one oxygen atom from the coordinated water molecule and one bridging oxygen atom. And one of the tbip ligands lies about a mirror plane. The average bond length of Zn1–O is 1.9580(1) Å. While the Zn2 atom is coordinated by four O atoms from three different tbip ligands and one bridging O atom with the average Zn2–O bond length being 1.9813(3) Å. Both Zn1 and Zn2 atoms, together with one bridging oxygen atom O4, can be viewed as a Zn-based binuclear cluster, which further shares with the Ba1 atom and seven carboxylate groups (O–C10–O, O–C26A–O, O–C14A–O, O–C14B–O, O–C22–O, O–C22C–O and O–C1–O), resulting in the 7-connected noncentrosymmetric heterometallic SBU Zn2OBa(COO)7 with Zn1⋯Zn2, Zn1⋯Ba1 and Zn2⋯Ba1 separations being 3.4776(2), 3.7104(2) and 3.9472(2) Å, respectively (Fig. 9a and 2d, ESI†). Through corner sharing, each of the heterometallic cluster SBUs is further interlinked by the adjacent three ones, reticulating into a 3D hetero-MOF, as shown in Fig. 9d. Fig. 9b shows that each of the heterometallic clusters (Zn2Oba) is first connected by O–C26–O, O–C14–O, O–C10–O and O–C22–O carboxylate groups (labeled with mauve and green parts in Fig. 9a) into a 2D bilayer. The 2D bilayer structure is then pillared by O–C1–O carboxylate groups (Fig. 9c), leading to a 3D porous MOF finally. If the tbip ligands and the heterometallic clusters Zn2OBa are considered as individual linkers and connecting nodes, respectively, the equivalent 3D topology framework for ZnBa-tbip can be simplified to be a uninodal 7-connected ose topology with a Schlafli symbol of (36.48.57), as shown in Fig. 10.

Synthetic consideration and structural comparison

The basic concept of our synthetic aim is to study the influence of substituted hetero metallic ions on the structures and properties of Zn-based mono-MOFs with the R-isophthalate (R = methyl or tert-butyl) ligands. Considering the possible synergistic and competing effects, we selected alkaline-earth metal ions Mg2+ and Ba2+ as the secondary metal ions to make this investigation. Firstly, the corresponding Zn-based mono-MOFs are synthesized for the sake of comparison. Then, the magnesium or the barium ion is directly added to the reaction solution during the synthesis of the Zn-based mono-MOFs. By using the hydrothermal method, single crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction are obtained.

An overview of the structures of the four MOFs indicates that both the mono-MOFs and the corresponding substituted hetero-MOFs are synthesized under the similar conditions, but their final structures are completely different. In the case of Zn-mip, all the zinc atoms are four-coordinated and exhibit the normal tetrahedral coordination modes. When the magnesium nitrate is added to the reaction solution similar to the synthesis of Zn-mip, one of the two tetrahedral metal sites in the SBU of Zn-mip is occupied by the magnesium atom. As for Zn-tbip, besides the 4-coordinated tetrahedral geometry, the zinc atoms in SBUs also display the 5-coordinated trigonal bipyramidal geometries, accompanied by the bridging hydroxyl adsorption sites. The addition of barium ions to the synthesis system of Zn-tbip results in the substitution of the five-coordinated Zn atoms. It is known that both magnesium and barium atoms generally favor octahedral coordination modes, as a result, the additional coordinated water molecules are attached to the alkaline earth metal atoms in order to satisfy their demands for the high coordination number. Finally, two different structural hetero-MOFs ZnMg-mip and ZnBa-tbip with the normal bond length ranges13 (such as Zn–O, Mg–O and Ba–O) are generated due to the rearrangement of the framework.

As demonstrated by the comparisons of Zn-based mono- and hetero-MOFs, it is evident that the coordination geometries of the central metal ions have an effect on the formation of the final structures, and the influence of the coordination geometries of the hetero metal centers on the structures of Zn-based mono-MOFs is realized through replacing the five (or four)-coordinated Zn atoms with the alkaline-earth metal ions. On the other hand, it can also be concluded that if the tetrahedral and octahedral metal sites existed in the pre-formed Zn-based mono-MOF synchronously, the direct alkaline-earth metal substitutions would be originated from the octahedral metal site, resulting in an isostructural Zn-based hetero-MOF.

TG-DTA and X-ray powder diffraction analyses

In order to investigate the thermal stabilities and check the phase purity of the MOFs, TG-DTA and X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) analyses are performed. The TG-DTA curves of all the MOFs are similar, and consist of two steps of the weight losses accompanied by the endothermal or exothermal peaks. In the TG-DTA curve of Zn-mip, there is a weight loss of 3.16% within the temperature range of 270–320 °C, corresponding to the release of coordinated water molecules (calcd 3.56%), and a continuous weight loss starting at 400 °C could be assigned to the subsequent decomposition of the framework (S3, ESI†). For ZnMg-mip (S4, ESI†), a weight loss of 16.12% in the temperature range of 50–350 °C corresponds to the liberation of coordinated and lattice water molecules (calcd 16.79%). For Zn-tbip, a weight loss of 4.65% within the temperature range of 250–370 °C is assigned to the release of hydroxy groups (calcd 5.07%), and the decomposition of the residue can be observed at 410 °C (S5, ESI†). The TG-DTA curve of ZnBa-tbip displays an initial weight loss of 6.01% from 120 °C to 370 °C, suggesting that the loss of the coordinated and lattice water molecules (calcd 6.08%) and the framework of ZnBa-tbip can be stable up to 400 °C (S6, ESI†). Evidently, TG-DTA curves show that the measured weight loss values are all in good agreement with the calculated ones from the corresponding compositions. Furthermore, the measured TG-DTA curves also demonstrate clearly that the organic parts in all the MOFs are stable below 400 °C, which is unusually high for coordination polymers, indicating the good thermal stability. The simulated and experimental XRPD patterns of the mono- and hetero-MOFs show that all the peaks presented in the measured curves approximately match the simulated curves generated from single-crystal diffraction data (S7–S10, ESI†), which clearly confirms the phase purity of the as-prepared products. The differences in intensity may be due to the preferred orientation of the crystalline powder samples. Therefore, the XRPD patterns taken on the as-synthesized samples, together with the results of the elemental analysis of Zn-mip, ZnMg-mip, Zn-tbip and ZnBa-tbip, further confirm that the compositions of corresponding homo- and hetero-MOFs are reasonable.

Photoluminescent properties

Previous studies have shown that MOFs containing zinc ions exhibit intense photoluminescent properties.3 Therefore, the photoluminescent properties of two free ligands (H2mip and H2tbip) and four Zn-based homo- and hetero-MOFs are investigated in the solid state at room temperature. The emission spectra of all the MOFs are provided in Fig. 11. Their excitation spectra are also presented in S11–S14 (ESI†). Upon excitation at 315 nm, the free ligands H2mip and H2tbip display intense ultraviolet emissions at 357 and 345 nm, respectively, which could be attributed to the π* → π transition (S15, ESI†). Furthermore, the four homo- and hetero-MOFs display strong emission bands centered at 448 nm (λex = 340 nm) for Zn-mip, 479 nm (λex = 370 nm) for ZnMg-mip, 418 nm (λex = 320 nm) for Zn-tbip and 530 nm (λex = 320 nm) for ZnBa-tbip, respectively. It is evident that upon photoexcitation at 315–370 nm, the emission bands of the homo- and hetero-MOFs are all red-shifted compared to the corresponding free ligands, and emit strong luminescence in the range of 400–600 nm at room temperature. Compared with the emission peaks of H2mip and H2tbip, the red shifts of ZnMg-mip and ZnBa-tbip may result from the ligand bridging effect upon metal coordination, which can effectively modify the rigidity and conjugation, and then reduce the energy loss by radiationless thermal vibrations. It is evident that the free ligands exhibit no observable photoluminescence in the range of 400–600 nm, which eliminates ligand-centered (LC) and ligand-to-ligand charge transfer (LLCT) excited states. Therefore, taking the emission bands of two free organic ligands into consideration, the fluorescent emissions in the Zn-based MOFs may be proposed to originate from the coordination of mip or tbip to the metal atoms and tentatively assigned to ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT), which is similar to the reported literatures for the Zn-based MOFs.14 From the other hand, the different fluorescent emissions between Zn-mip and Zn-tbip may be caused by their structural difference because of the coexistence of bulky tert-butyl groups and the smaller methyl groups in dicarboxylate derivatives. At the same time, due to the nature of the substituted heterometallic ions and the different coordination environments around them, the emission bands of the substituted hetero-MOFs show prominent red-shifts compared to the corresponding primary mono-MOFs. This demonstrates that the substitutions between the alkaline earth metal ions and zinc ions have a key effect on adjusting the photoluminescent properties of the Zn-based homo-MOFs.

Conclusion

In summary, we have succeeded in employing 5-methyl isophthalic acid and 5-tert-butyl isophthalic acid as two kinds of single bridging ligands under hydrothermal synthesis conditions to design the structurally characterized four novel homo- and hetero-MOFs Zn-mip, ZnMg-mip, Zn-tbip and ZnBa-tbip in an attempt to investigate the influence of the heterometallic substituent on the structural diversification and photoluminescent properties of the Zn-based MOFs. For the Zn-based homo-MOFs, the substitution of Zn2+ with the alkaline-earth metal ion generates the hetero-MOF with interesting structures and different luminescent properties. The most intriguing structural feature is that all the MOFs crystallize in a 3D open framework constituted by the unique Zn-based cluster SBUs and corresponding single ligands. The alkaline-earth metal ions participate in the coordination of carboxylate ligands and play the joint role in the expansion of network structures. The solid state luminescent spectra of the Zn-based MOFs demonstrate that the alkaline earth metal ion substituents have significant influence on the photoluminescent properties of the Zn-based homo-MOFs. Furthermore, as far as we know, the systematic investigation of the incorporation of alkaline earth metal ions into the crystal lattice of the Zn-based homo-MOFs resulting in novel hetero-MOFs with structural diversification and photoluminescent properties has not been reported so far. Based on the fact that the structure and photoluminescence properties of the Zn-based homo-MOFs can be affected by the substituted alkaline-earth metal ions, a promising avenue for rational design of such Zn-based MOFs through a systematic substitution of heterometallic ions would open for further research on the photoluminescent materials with intriguing architectures.
