Wings waving: coordinating solvent-induced structural diversity of new Cu(II) flexible MOFs with crystal to crystal transformation and gas sorption capability†

Zhanfeng Ju and Daqiang Yuan*
State Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, 350002, Fujian, China. E-mail: ydq@fjirsm.ac.cn; Fax: +86 0591 83714946; Tel: +86 0591 83792525

Received 11th June 2013 , Accepted 12th July 2013

First published on 12th July 2013

A new semi-rigid 5-(bis(4-carboxybenzyl)amino)isophthalic acid (H4L) is designed and synthesized to obtain novel flexible metal organic frameworks (MOFs). The tetracarboxylate ligand (L4−) links to Cu(II) paddle-wheel second build units (SBUs) under different solvent condition to afford two MOFs, [Cu2(L)(S)2]n (S = DMF, 1 and H2O, 2). Single crystal X-ray diffraction structural analyses reveal that the coordinating solvent molecules are DMF and H2O in the SBUs for 1 and 2, respectively. The torsion angle between two methylene benzoate ring subunits of the ligand is 122.7° in 1 but 175.1° in 2. Three phenyl rings of the ligand are nonplanar and orient in different directions in 1 and 2. A reversible crystal to crystal transformation between 1 and 2 are investigated by exchanging the terminal ligated solvent molecule, in which the phenyl rings of the benzoate subunit act as “a pair of waving wings” accompanying the ligated solvent exchange. Time-dependent powder X-ray diffraction data confirms this reversible dynamic transformation. This hinge within the semi-rigid ligand is a built-in breathing mechanism and suggests a novel approach for general synthesis of breathing MOFs. A gas sorption study for 1 demonstrates 1 has the ability to selectively adsorb CO2 over CH4, H2 and N2.

Introduction

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) or porous coordination polymers (PCPs) have been of considerable interest over the past two decades due to their varieties of topology,1 designable synthetic strategies2 and wide range of potential applications such as gas storage2a,3 and separation,4 catalytic activity5 and sensing.6 Among these MOF materials, flexible or soft porous networks, as an intriguing subcategory, also called the third generation of porous coordination polymers or dynamic frameworks, have received much attention,7 These are defined as porous solids that possess both a highly ordered network and structural transformability. They are bistable or multistable crystalline materials with a long-range ordered structure, reversible transformability between the different crystalline phases, and permanent porosity.8 Thus, such materials often offer good opportunities for crystal to crystal transformation. Furthermore, flexible MOFs may display some additional attributes which are not observed in the conventional porous material, such as gate-type gas sorption properties, enhancement of drug storage/delivery capacity and alteration of guest properties deriving from their dynamic nature.8 Thus, developing synthetic approaches towards flexible MOFs would be attractive. Typically, a strategy for making a soft porous crystal is the incorporation of a flexible component into the porous framework which will create softness throughout the network.8 Meanwhile, MOFs are composed of organic linkers and metal SBUs; therefore the nature of the SBUs and the organic ligand play key roles in constructing flexible MOFs.

The best studied example of flexible coordination compounds is the MIL-53 series MIII(OH)(bdc) (MIII = Al, Cr, Fe, Ga, Sc, In; bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate). The MIL-53 series show a reversible structural change between a large pore form and a narrow pore form upon gas- or solvent-host interactions.9 For example, MIL-53(Cr) displays pore expansion and contraction triggered by the local re-orientation of the coordination bond, in which the pores actually shrink as the Cr–O(OH)–Cr angle changes from 128° to 124° and the cross-section of the diamond-shaped pore changes from 16.8 × 13.0 Å2 to 19.7 × 7.9 Å2.9b Another well-known compound is [Zn2(bdc)2(dabco)]n (dabco = 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]),10 where the flexible nature of the Zn(II) paddle-wheel SBUs results in the framework shrinking upon evacuating the compound.11 The [Zn2(bdc)2(dabco)]n shows some intrinsic framework flexibility due to the elastic paddlewheel SBUs. Meanwhile, DFT calculations also support the flexibility in Zn(II) paddle-wheel SBUs.12 However, preparing this kind of MOF has still been challenging, in part because the inorganic bricks that meet the requirements are quite rare.7g,13

Utilizing flexible or semi-rigid organic ligands to construct soft coordination polymers should be more convenient and controllable, because the organic ligands are easy to design and synthesise. More recently, the number of flexible frameworks based on semi-rigid polytopic organic ligands are increasing. Based on a tricarboxylic acid ligand containing a semi-rigid imine linkage between its phenyl-carboxylate moieties, inducing some (but not unlimited) rotational freedom to the phenyl rings in this organic molecule,14 the Nd(III) and Cd(II) flexible MOFs have been prepared, which exhibit single crystal to single crystal transformation properties. Jenkins et al. utilized a semi-rigid di-1,2,4-triazole linker to construct a flexible Cu(II) MOF with breathing behavior, which was due to the ligand itself and not the metal–ligand juncture.15 MOFs based on the semi-rigid octacarboxylic acid (5,5′-(2,2-bis((3,5-dicarboxyphe-noxy) methyl)propane-1,3-diyl)bis(oxy)diisophthalic acid have been reported by three different groups.16 Xu et al. have explored the solvent induced controllable synthesis, single crystal to single crystal transformation and encapsulation of Alq3 phenomenon of the Zn(II) MOFs based on the octatopic ligand. Despite the fact that the strategies of constructing flexible MOFs with flexible or semi-rigid organic ligands have received attraction, the case of the pre-designed semi-rigid ligands is still scarce. It would be attractive to synthesise new semi-rigid or flexible ligands and to construct novel soft MOFs materials.

In this article, we present two new Cu(II) soft MOFs utilizing a new semi-rigid tetracarboxylic acid, 5-(bis(4-carboxybenzyl)amino)isophthalic acid, denoted as H4L (Scheme 1). A solvent dependent reaction of H4L with Cu(II) salts under solvothermal reaction condition was performed and affords two new three-dimensional MOFs, [Cu2(L)(S)2]n (S = DMF, 1 and H2O, 2), in which the Cu(II) paddle-wheel SBUs act as inorganic nodes, which exhibit reversible crystal to crystal transformability. Single crystal X-ray characterization reveals that the structural changes are owing to the rotation of subunits in the ligand accompanied by terminal ligated solvent exchange in the SBUs, but not the change in the metal–ligand juncture.

Experimental

Materials and instrumentation

All the reagents and solvents were purchased from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were carried out on a German Elementary Vario EL III instrument. The FT-IR spectra were performed on a Nicolet Magna 750 FT-IR. The spectrometer using KBr pellets was set in the range of 4000–400 cm−1. 1H NMR spectra were obtained on a Burker AVANCE 400 (400 MHz) spectrometer. Thermogravimetric analyses were recorded on a NETZSCH STA 449C unit at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under nitrogen atmosphere. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were collected by a Rigaku DMAX2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54 Å). Simulation of the PXRD spectrum were carried out by the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury program.

Synthesis of H4L

A solution of KOH (3.93 g, 0.07 mol) in 50 mL H2O was dropwise added into 4-(chloromethyl) benzoic acid (5.12 g, 0.03 mol) in 100 mL H2O with stirring. After that, 5-aminoisophthalic acid (1.81 g, 0.01 mol) was slowly added to the reaction mixture, and then the mixture was refluxed under about 90 °C for 30 h. The reaction solution was cooled to room temperature, and then 6 M hydrochloric acid (HCl) aqueous solution was added to the remaining aqueous solution until the pH value was adjusted to approximately 2.5. The resulting precipitate was collected by filtration, washed with water, and dried in air to give a white solid of the crude product, which was esterified by thionyl chloride (SOCl2) in ethanol. The ethyl ester of the product was purified by column chromatography (silica gel, hexanes:ethyl acetate = 3:1), and then hydrolysed by KOH in H2O. Finally, the solution was re-acidified by HCl (6 M), and the white powdery product was filtered, washed by water and dried in air (yield: 35%, based on 5-aminoisophthalic acid). Synthesis of [Cu2(L)(DMF)2]n (1)

A mixture of Cu(NO3)2·3H2O (0.030 g, 0.12 mmol), H4L·H2O (0.010 g, 0.02 mmol), DMF (2 mL) and 3 drops HBF4 (40% in water) was sealed in a glass vial. The mixture was heated at 75 °C for 36 h, and then cooled to room temperature. Green crystals of 1 were obtained in a 39% yield based on H4L. X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray data of 1 and 2 were collected on a SuperNova, Dual, Cu at zero, Atlas diffractometer. The crystal was kept at 100(1) K during data collection. The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares on F2 with anisotropic displacement using the SHELXTL software package.17 The non-H atoms were treated anisotropically, whereas the aromatic and hydroxy- and alkyl-hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated, ideal positions and refined as riding on their respective carbon atoms. In 1 and 2, free solvent molecules were highly disordered, and the attempts to locate and refine the solvent peaks were unsuccessful. The diffused electron densities resulting from these residual solvent molecules were removed from the data set using the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON and refined further using the data generated.18 The contents of the solvent region are not represented in the unit cell contents in the crystal data. The details for data collection and refinement are included in the CIF file in the ESI.† Crystallographic data for the structures reported in this paper have been deposited in the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center with CCDC numbers: 943580 for 1 and 943581 for 2.

Gas sorption measurements

Gas sorption isotherm measurements were performed on an ASAP 2020M Surface Area and Pore Size Analyzer. An as-isolated sample of 1 was soaked in methanol for three days to remove the solvent molecules (during the exchange the methanol was refreshed six times). The sample was collected by decanting and treated with dichloromethane similarly to remove methanol solvates. After the removal of dichloromethane by decanting, the wet sample was activated by drying under a dynamic vacuum at room temperature overnight to obtain activated 1 (1-ac). Before the measurements, the 1-ac sample was dried again by using the “degas” function of the surface area analyzer for 10 h at 80 °C. UHP grade (99.999%) N2, Ar, H2, CO2, and CH4 were used for all measurements. The temperatures were maintained at 77 K (liquid nitrogen bath), 87 K (liquid argon bath), 273 K (ice-water bath), and 298 K (room temperature), respectively.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

H4L is synthesized by the reaction of 4-(chloromethyl) benzoic acid with 5-aminoisophthalic acid. In order to ensure the reaction is carried out completely, excess 4-(chloromethyl) benzoic acid is added to the reaction system, which leads to the impurity in the crude product obtained according to the synthesis route in Scheme 1. It is hard to obtain pure H4L by a recrystallization method. Thus, it is necessary to esterify the crude product and then purify it by column chromatography. The pure ethyl ester of H4L are hydrolysed and acidified at the end to give pure H4L.

The same synthesis is carried out in different solvents to give compounds 1 and 2. It is obvious that the solvent plays a key role in the synthesis. 1 is obtained in DMF while 2 is formed in the mixed solvent of DMF and CH3OH. Furthermore, in the synthesis of 2, additional HBF4 is necessary to be added to the reaction system after heating at 75 °C for 30 h, because the initial green microcrystal is not 2. For 1, when a similar synthesis strategy to 2 is adopted, only 1 can be obtained. This result reveals that additional HBF4 and mixed DMF–CH3OH solvent are essential in the synthesis of 2.

Crystal structures

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group C2/c, and the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one Cu(II), half of one L4− ligand, and one coordinating DMF, as shown in Fig. 1. The structure consists of a well-known paddle-wheel SBU in which the two Cu(II) have a distorted square pyramidal geometry. Each paddle-wheel SBU is coordinated by four carboxylates groups from four different L4− ligands and the two apical positions are occupied by oxygen atoms from two DMF molecules. For the same L4− ligand, four carboxylates of the ligand link to four different SBUs. Thus a three-dimensional MOF is formed.
Fig. 1 The coordination geometries of Cu(II) center in 1, H atoms are omitted for clarity. Symmetry codes: (a) −x, y, 0.5 − z; (b) 1 − x, 1 − y, 1 − z; (c) 0.5 − x, 1.5 − y, 1 − z; (d) 0.5 + x, −0.5 + y, z.

The structure of compound 2 is illustrated in Fig. 2. 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P and also contains the similar paddle-wheel SBUs in 1 but the difference is that the two apical positions are occupied by oxygen atoms from two H2O molecules. The asymmetric unit consists of one ligand, two Cu(II) and two coordinating H2O. The connection mode of the SBUs and the tetratopic carboxylate ligand in 2 is also similar with that of 1.


Fig. 2 The coordination geometries of Cu(II) center in compound 2, H atoms are omitted for clarity. Symmetry codes: (a) 2 − x, −1 − y, 1 − z; (b) 2 − x, −2 − y, 1 − z; (c) x, −1 + y, z; (d) −1 + x, y, z; (e) 3 − x, −y, −z; (f) 2 − x, −y, −z.

Comparing the structure of compound 1 with 2, the terminal coordinated solvents of the paddle-wheel SBUs are significantly different. DMF molecules occupy the two apical positions in 1 while H2O molecules instead of DMF occupy the two apical positions in 2. DMF and H2O molecules are quite different in shape and size, which induces the diverse orientation of the flexible ligand in 1 and 2. As shown in Fig. 3, the torsion angles between two methylene-benzoate rings of the ligand subunit are 122.7° in 1 and 175.1° in 2, respectively. Three phenyl rings of the ligand are nonplanar and orient in separate directions in 1 and 2. In 1, the dihedral angles between the phenyl ring plane of the benzoate subunit and the phenyl ring plane of the isophthalate fragment are both about 83.6°, and the dihedral angle between the two phenyl ring subunit plane of benzoate is 84.1°. In 2, the dihedral angles between the phenyl ring planes of the benzoate subunit and the phenyl ring plane of the isophthalate fragment are 75.2° and 80.1°, respectively, and the dihedral angle between the two phenyl ring subunit planes of benzoate is 76.3°. These discrepancies cause the two compounds to crystallize in different space groups and have varied packing features (Fig. 4).


Fig. 3 SBUs and the reversible transformation of the orientation of the ligands in compounds 1 and 2.

Fig. 4 A section of the 3D coordination frameworks of 1 and 2. a) View along the a-axis for 1; b) view along the b-axis for 1; c) view along a-axis for 2; d) view along b-axis for 2. H atoms are omitted for clarity.

Obviously, by inserting a methylene (–CH2–) unit among rigid phenyl rings, some rotational degrees of freedom are induced to the phenyl rings in the ligand, which provides softness to the ligand and makes the ligand more flexible. Thus, based on the inherent softness of this linker, the flexible MOFs are formed. In the most flexible MOF system, the breathing mechanism was activated by rotation around the O–O axis of the carboxylate, known as “kneecap”, which required an opposite rotation on the opposing side of the SBUs.7g,19 Jenkins et al. firstly moved the “kneecap” from the ligand–metal junction to the ligand itself,15 and then the synthesis and characterization of a Cu(II) MOF using a semirigid ligand, 4,4′-(1,4-(trans-2-butene)diyl)bis(1,2,4-triazole), were reported. However, the “kneecap” was entirely based on the ligand and not primarily at the ligand–metal junction. Unlike these cases, we find that the two phenyl ring of the benzoate subunit of the ligand in 1 and 2 (Fig. 3) act as “a pair of wings” and not as “kneecap”. By changing the coordinating solvents of the SBUs, the phenyl rings of the benzoate subunit rotate and display different orientations, which is just like a pair of “waving wings”.

Reversible crystal to crystal transformation

As mentioned in the introduction, soft coordination polymers often exhibit high potential for crystal to crystal transformation for the reason that they are bistable or multistable crystalline materials.8 The reversible crystal to crystal transfer capacity of compounds 1 and 2 are investigated. 1 and 2 are synthesized according to the above method and the phase purity of the samples are confirmed by experimental and simulated powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns, as illustrated in Fig. 5. When the fresh crystalline sample of 1 is exposed to air, the coordinating DMF molecules are gradually exchanged by H2O molecules in air, and therefore the crystalline sample of 1 transfers to the crystalline sample of 2. More interestingly, after putting the crystalline sample of 2 into DMF solvent, DMF molecules will replace the terminal ligated H2O molecules, and crystalline 2 can easily be turned to 1. Time-dependent PXRD pattern confirms this reversible dynamic transformation (Fig. 6).
Fig. 5 Experimental and simulated PXRD patterns of 1 and 2.

Fig. 6 Time-dependent PXRD patterns for the reversible transformation. (a)–(g) represent the patterns after exposing 1 to air for 20 min, 40 min, 60 min, 90 min, 2.5 h, 4 h and 15 h, respectively; (h) represent the patterns after putting 2 into DMF solvent for 24 h.

As we know, the physical or chemical nature of solvents, such as polarity, protic/aprotic behavior, van der Waals volume, and stoichiometric ratio of the mixed solvents can affect the structures of MOFs in two ways. Firstly, the solvent is absent from the final crystal lattice which primarily influences the crystal growth rate, crystalline morphology and lattice formation of distinct MOFs. Secondly, the solvent acts as a guest or as a coordinating ligand incorporated to the crystal lattice which determines the final network of the MOFs. Some solvent induced structural diversity or crystal to crystal transformation cases have been reported.14,15,16c,20 Du et al. systematically studied the compounds of 5-bromonicotinate and Cd(II) in different mixture solvents, and found crystalline diversity and solid-state conversions.20a Chen et al. investigated crystal to crystal sliding of a neutral laminated MOF which was observed by guest exchange, dehydration and reabsorption.20b The crystal to crystal conversion influenced by solvent exchange base on the semi-rigid ligands were also found.14,15,16c Among these cases, most of the structures only changed slightly accompanied by the crystal to crystal transformation. Moreover, some transformations are irreversible, which are confined in studies of breathing behavior of flexible MOFs. In our results, the structure changed obviously and the reversible crystal to crystal transformation was carried out facilely in mild conditions. This hinge within the semi-rigid ligand is a built-in breathing mechanism, which suggests a novel approach towards the general synthesis of breathing MOFs. It is of great significance in constructing porous soft coordination polymers with breathing behavior.

Thermogravimetric analysis

The thermal stability of compounds 1 and 2 are investigated by means of thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) in the temperature range of 25–1000 °C. The TGA curves for 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 7. 1 and 2 show similar thermal stability, and the lattice and coordinating solvents are lost gradually before 200 °C. When the temperature reaches about 300 °C, the ligands decompose rapidly. The TGA results are in accordance with the similar linkage of the ligand with the SBUs in 1 and 2, which are displayed in the structural analyses.

Gas sorption

The PLATON routine calculation (probe radius = 1.80 Å) shows the solvent accessible void fractions of compounds 1 and 2 in the desolved structure are 62.1% and 47.5%, respectively. To investigate the permanent porosity of these flexibly MOFs, we performed gas sorption experiments for N2, H2, CO2 and CH4. The freshly prepared crystals of 1 are fully activated to obtain 1-ac according to the procedure mentioned above. As shown in Fig. 8, the N2 sorption isotherm reveals that 1 exhibits typical type I sorption behavior, a characteristic of microporous materials, which is coincidental with the crystal structure. Derived from the N2 adsorption data, the Langmuir surface area of 1 is 510 m2 g−1, corresponding to a BET surface area of 469 m2 g−1 and a total pore volume of 0.18 cm3 g−1. The former is smaller than the calculated surface area of 2215 m2 g−1 based on the crystal structure using Poreblazer software.21 The significant difference reflects that the frameworks of 1 may shrink or collapse. Even so, 1-ac can still display some microporosity. However, the crystals of 2 show no porosity though many activation methods have been used to activate 2. The reason of unsuccessful activation of 2 may be due to the narrow pore size. The theoretical pore structure analysis shows that the pore in 2 is percolated in one dimension and the limiting diameter is only 3.54 Å which is too small to free the solvents during the activation.21
Fig. 8 N2 isotherms for 1 at 77 K.

Low-pressure H2 sorption isotherms at 77 K and 87 K were collected as well to evaluate its H2 adsorption performance. 1 can take up 100 cm3 g−1 of H2 at 77 K and 1 bar and 76 cm3 g−1 of H2 at 87 K (Fig. 9). To estimate the hydrogen affinity towards the framework of 1, the heats of adsorption (Qst) for H2 were calculated using the Clauius–Clapeyron equation through fitting the H2 adsorption data following the Langmuir–Freundilich equation.22 As shown in Fig. 10, at low coverage 1 has a heat of adsorption of 7.95 kJ mol−1, which is higher than that of MOFs which lack special sorption sites whose heats of adsorption are typically in range of the 4–5 kJ mol−1.23


Fig. 9 H2 isotherms under 77 and 87 K for 1.

Fig. 10 The heats of adsorption for 1.

Except for the N2 and H2 uptakes at low temperatures, CO2, CH4, N2 and H2 adsorption measurements of 1 have been carried out at 273 K (Fig. 11). At 273 K and 810 mmHg, 1 exhibits remarkable CO2 uptake capacity (67.8 cm3 g−1), and little gas uptake for CH4, N2 and H2 (20.4, 6.3 and 2.3 cm3 g−1). The results demonstrate that the guest-evacuated 1 has the ability to selectively adsorb CO2 over CH4 and N2. At 298 K and 810 mmHg, the uptakes of CO2 and CH4 sharply decrease to 47.5 and 13.1 cm3 g−1, while the uptakes of N2 and H2 could hardly be observed, which reflects the nature of physical adsorption.

Conclusions

In summary, a semi-rigid tetracarboxylic acid H4L has been designed and synthesized, in which the methylene making phenyl ring subunit rotates more freely. Based on this soft H4L and Cu(II) paddle-wheel SBUs, three-dimensional flexible MOFs are constructed, where solvents play key roles in the formation of the MOFs. X-ray structure analyses reveal that reversible crystal to crystal transformations are performed accompanied by terminal ligated solvent molecule exchange. The “free” phenyl ring subunit acts as a pair of waving wings and orients to distinct directions in the transformation. Furthermore, gas sorption investigations demonstrates the guest-evacuated compound 1 has the ability to selectively adsorb CO2 over CH4, H2 and N2. The breathing behavior is not detected in the MOFs, but this hinge within the semi-rigid ligand is a built-in breathing mechanism which suggests a novel approach for the general synthesis of breathing MOFs. These results are of great significance in constructing porous soft coordination polymers with breathing behaviors.
