A terbium(III) lanthanide–organic framework as a platform for a recyclable multi-responsive luminescent sensor†

Min Chen , Wen-Ming Xu , Jia-Yue Tian , Hui Cui , Jiao-Xing Zhang , Chun-Sen Liu * and Miao Du *
Henan Provincial Key Laboratory of Surface & Interface Science, Zhengzhou University of Light Industry, Zhengzhou 450002, China. E-mail: dumiao@zzuli.edu.cn; chunsenliu@zzuli.edu.cn; Tel: +86-371-86609669

Received 27th December 2016 , Accepted 19th January 2017

First published on 19th January 2017

A terbium(III) lanthanide–organic framework (534-MOF-Tb) with a green-emission signal was successfully obtained by the solvothermal reaction of Tb3+ ions with the organic ligand H3TBOT (2,4,6-tris[1-(3-carboxylphenoxy)ylmethyl]mesitylene). 534-MOF-Tb contains microporous quadrangle channels with accessible Lewis-base sites and coordinated water molecules, which are feasible to anchor and recognise multifarious analytes. It can serve as a recyclable multi-responsive sensing material for detecting Fe3+, MnO4−, Cr2O72−, and p-nitrotoluene (4-NT). Significantly, this is the first reported MOF-based sensor for detecting explosive 4-NT. Moreover, the mechanism of the selective luminescence quenching response for Fe3+, MnO4−, Cr2O72− or 4-NT can be mainly explained in terms of the competition between the absorption of the light source energy and the electronic interaction between the analyte and the TBOT ligand.

Introduction

In modern analytical chemistry, novel advanced materials for meeting analytical challenges such as improvements in sensitivity, selectivity, and detection limits have received great attention because of their potential analytical applications. As a class of hybrid porous materials, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are self-assembled from organic bridging ligands and metal ions/clusters with interesting properties for many potential applications. Until now, various MOFs for gas storage and separation,1 catalysis,2 magnetism,3 drug delivery,4 energy storage,5 sensors,6 and biological imaging7 have been synthesized and extensively studied. Typically, sensory MOF-based materials can interact with analytes and be accompanied by electrical,8 optical,9 or mechanical10 signal transduction. Therefore, luminescent MOF-based sensors have attracted intense interest due to their advantages in terms of definite fluorescence spectra, visible signals by the naked eye, high sensitivity and selectivity, and simplicity in operation. The luminescent MOFs reported so far displayed nice sensing for cations,11–17 anions,18–20 gases,21 pH,22 small organic molecules,23–25 and nitroaromatic explosives.26–31

The Fe3+, MnO4−, and Cr2O72− are three well-known important ions, in which Fe3+ plays a crucial role in biological processes such as oxygen metabolism, oxygen uptake, and electron transfer,15,32 while MnO4− and Cr2O72− are important oxidants in laboratory and industry.18 However, the massive abuse of these ions has brought serious environmental pollution. Therefore, sensory materials with selectivity and sensitivity for probing Fe3+, MnO4−, and Cr2O72− ions are urgently required. Currently, luminescent MOFs for responding to MnO4− and Cr2O72− anions are relatively rare,18 compared to the ones for sensing Fe3+ cation.15 Furthermore, most reported luminescent MOFs for sensing those ions are in nonaqueous solvents.18a,15d Thus, it remains a challenge to use MOFs as probes to detect ions in aqueous solutions, especially for a mixture of pollutant ions in water, which is more approximate to the realistic situation.

Nitroaromatic compounds (NACs) such as 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP), 2,4,6-trinitrotoluene (TNT), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), picric acid (PA), nitrobenzene (NB), 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH), and p-nitrotoluene (4-NT) have become serious pollution sources of groundwater, soils, and other security applications due to their explosivity and high toxicity.33 Among them, 4-NT is extensively used in chemical engineering and pharmaceutical industries. During its industrial application, the leakage of 4-NT can directly contaminate soil, aquatic biosystems and human health, resulting in skin irritation, respiratory diseases, methemoglobinemia, and even death. Up to now, luminescent MOFs sensing DNP,26 TNT,27 4-NP,28 2,4-DNPH,29 NB,30 and PA31 have been explored except for 4-NT. Thus, it is still a challenge to selectively detect 4-NT in the presence of other NACs.

Usually, the luminescence of MOFs stems from the organic linkers containing aromatic or conjugated π moieties,34 metal ions or clusters,35 or guests trapped in the cavities.36 Among the luminescent MOFs, lanthanide–organic frameworks (LnOFs) have attracted extensive attention due to their intense, long-lived, and line-like emission in the visible region.37 In addition, the emission intensities of LnOFs are associated with the ligand-to-metal energy transfer, coordination environments of lanthanide ions, and the interactions with guest species, thereby providing an effective platform for chemical sensing.37 Herein, we report the synthesis of a LnOF (534-MOF-Tb), namely [Tb(TBOT)(H2O)](H2O)4(DMF)(NMP)0.5 (DMF = N,N-dimethylformamide and NMP = N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone). It displays recyclable selective sensing of Fe3+, MnO4−, Cr2O72−, and 4-NT, which represents the first MOF that can be used to detect 4-NT. Meanwhile, the mechanisms for selective luminescence quenching responses towards different analytes are discussed in detail.

Experimental

Materials and methods

With the exception of the organic ligand H3TBOT (Scheme S1, ESI†) that was prepared according to a literature method,38 all of the starting reagents and solvents were obtained commercially and used as received. Elemental analysis (C, H, and N) was performed on a Vario EL III elemental analyzer. IR spectra were measured on a Bruker Tensor 27 OPUS FT-IR spectrometer (with KBr pellets) in the 4000–400 cm−1 range. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer Diamond SII thermal analyzer from room temperature to 800 °C, with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a nitrogen atmosphere. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were recorded on a Rigaku (model Ultima IV) diffractometer with a Rigaku D/teX ultrahigh-speed position sensitive detector and Cu-Kα X-rays (40 kV, 40 mA). The emission/excitation spectra of the samples were recorded on an Agilent Cary Eclipse fluorescence spectrophotometer at room temperature.

Synthesis of 534-MOF-Tb

A mixture of Tb(NO3)3·6H2O (22.7 mg, 0.05 mmol), H3TBOT (17.1 mg, 0.03 mmol), N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (6 mL), N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) (1 mL), and formic acid (0.4 mL) was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel, which was heated at 140 °C for 3 days in an oven. After cooling to room temperature, colorless prism single crystals were produced in 62% yield (17.5 mg, based on H3TBOT). IR (cm−1): 3394 (m, br), 1656 (s), 1551 (s), 1442 (s), 1408 (s), 1238 (m), 1134 (w), 1010 (m), 827 (w), 772 (m), 685 (w). Elemental analysis (calculated/experimental, %) for [Tb(TBOT)(H2O)](H2O)4(DMF)(NMP)0.5 (C38.5H48.5N1.5O15.5Tb): C, 49.23/49.46; H, 5.20/5.32; N: 2.24/2.30.

Luminescence sensing experiments

The powder sample of 534-MOF-Tb was dispersed in an aqueous solution (3 mL) of M(NO3)x (Mx+ = Li+, Na+, K+, Mg2+, Cu2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Hg2+, Cr3+, and Fe3+, 1 × 10−3 mol L−1) or KX (X = Br−, I−, NO3−, SO42−, MnO4−, and Cr2O72−, 1 × 10−3 mol L−1) at room temperature. Luminescence data of the solution were collected after 3 min.

Typical procedure for fluorescence titration studies

The powder sample of 534-MOF-Tb was dispersed in solution (3 mL) and subsequently transferred to a quartz cell of 1 cm width. Quenching tests were carried out by gradually adding the analyte (Fe3+, MnO4−, or Cr2O72− aqueous solution, or nitroaromatic DMF solution).

Recyclable luminescence experiments

The reproducibility of 534-MOF-Tb towards sensing Fe3+, MnO4−, Cr2O72−, or 4-NT was studied. After the first quenching experiment, the 534-MOF-Tb powder was recovered by centrifugation and washed with water (Fe3+, MnO4−, and Cr2O72−) or DMF (4-NT). The recovered solid was collected and subsequently used in the successive quenching experiments.

Crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction data for 534-MOF-Tb were collected on a SuperNova Oxford Diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54184 Å). Multi-scan absorption corrections were performed with the CrysAlisPro program.39 Empirical absorption corrections were carried out by using spherical harmonics, implemented in the SCALE3 ABSPACK scaling algorithm. The structure was solved by using direct methods and all non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically by using the full-matrix least-squares method with the SHELXTL crystallographic software package.40 All hydrogen atoms were generated geometrically and refined isotropically as riding. Attempts to locate and model the highly disordered solvent molecules in the pores were unsuccessful. Therefore, the SQUEEZE routine, a part of the PLATON software package,41 was used to calculate the disorder area and remove the diffraction contribution to afford a set of solvent free diffraction intensities. Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structure of 534-MOF-Tb

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis indicates that 534-MOF-Tb crystallizes in the monoclinic C2/c space group (see Table S1, ESI†). The asymmetric unit of 534-MOF-Tb contains one Tb3+ ion, one TBOT, and one coordinated water ligand (Fig. 1a). The coordination geometry of the Tb3+ ion can be described as a distorted bicapped trigonal prism (Fig. S1, ESI†) furnished by seven carboxylate oxygen atoms from seven different TBOT ligands and one oxygen atom of the water ligand (Fig. 1a). The bond lengths and angles around the Tb3+ ion are listed in Table S2 (ESI†). Two carboxylate groups of the TBOT ligand adopt μ2-η1:η1 coordination modes and the third one connects two Tb3+ ions through the μ2-η2:η1 coordination mode (Fig. S2, ESI†). As for the flexibility of the TBOT ligand, the three dihedral angles between the terminal benzene groups and the central benzene ring are 79.6, 89.8, and 60.0°, respectively. A pair of edge-sharing polyhedra of Tb3+ ions constructs the dinuclear cluster, [Tb2(COO)4(H2O)2], with a Tb⋯Tb distance of 4.02 Å. These clusters are connected by carboxylate groups to result in an infinite 1-D rod-shaped inorganic SBU along the b-axis (Fig. 1b, left). The adjacent rod-shaped SBUs are further linked by TBOT ligands to form a 3-D framework with quadrangle channels (Fig. 1b, right). The size of the channel is roughly 5.0 × 5.0 Å2 (considering the van der Waals radii of atoms), with overall free voids of 2500.4 Å3 (30.8% of 8118.2 Å3 per unit cell volume) calculated by using PLATON (with a 1.8 Å probe radius).41 Clearly, the dinuclear cluster in 534-MOF-Tb can be considered as the isolated node and connects four TBOT ligands via the coordination linkage of two pairs of carboxylate groups. As a consequence, each dinuclear motif is defined as a 4-connected node. On the other hand, each TBOT ligand links eight dinuclear motifs and serves as the 8-connected node. On the basis of the above simplification principle, the resulting structure of 534-MOF-Tb is an exceptional 3-D binodal (4,8)-connected network with a (44·62)2(48·617·83) point symbol (Fig. 1c) calculated by using the TOPOS program.42 Alternatively, the topological approach proposed by Yaghi et al.43 can be applied to analyze such frameworks with infinite rod-shaped SBUs. According to this concept, the 1-D rod-shaped SBU is simplified by linking the carboxylate C atoms and forms a twisted zigzag ladder (Fig. 1d, left), while each TBOT ligand connects three rods. Thus, the 3-D net of 534-MOF-Tb exhibits a simple (3,4)-connected topological network based on 3-connected TBOT ligands and 4-connected 1-D rod-like SBUs with a point symbol of (42·6·8·92)(42·62·82)2(6·8·9) (Fig. 1d, right).42

Photoluminescence properties of H3TBOT and 534-MOF-Tb

The emission spectra of H3TBOT and 534-MOF-Tb in the solid state were investigated at room temperature. The phase purity for bulk materials of 534-MOF-Tb was confirmed by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD, Fig. S3a, ESI†). The free H3TBOT exhibits an emission band at 421 nm (λex = 300 nm), which is probably derived from the π–π* or n–π* transitions (Fig. S4a, ESI†).30b,31b 534-MOF-Tb shows characteristic emission bands of Tb3+ ions (Fig. S4b, ESI†). Upon excitation at 300 nm, the four major peaks of 534-MOF-Tb are centered at 490, 544, 585, and 621 nm, which can be attributed to the f–f 5D4 → 7FJ (J = 6, 5, 4, 3) of Tb3+ transitions, respectively. The most intense emission is centered at 544 nm and corresponds to the 5D4 → 7F5 transition. The above sharp, colour specific emissions of 534-MOF-Tb benefit from the coordination between metals and organic ligands, which can be defined as the ‘antenna effect’.37 However, the persistent presence of ligand-originated emission in the luminescence spectra of 534-MOF-Tb signifies that the efficiency of energy transfer from the TBOT ligand to the Tb3+ ions is not thorough.

Selective sensing of cations

The intense green luminescence of 534-MOF-Tb promotes us to investigate its ability to sense common metal cations. The finely ground samples of 534-MOF-Tb were dispersed in the aqueous solutions of M(NO3)x (1.0 × 10−3 mol L−1; M = Li+, Na+, K+, Mg2+, Cu2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Hg2+, Cr3+, and Fe3+, respectively). The corresponding luminescence curves still show four characteristic emission peaks (Fig. 2a) of Tb3+ ions, and only the highest emission peaks at 544 nm were monitored (Fig. 2b). The results reveal that various metal cations display markedly different effects on the luminescence of 534-MOF-Tb. The luminescence intensity of the sample in Mg2+ ions slightly enhances. In contrast, the rest of the metal cations weaken the luminescence intensities in different degrees. Interestingly, the sample dispersed in Fe3+ ions exhibits a drastic quenching behaviour, which leads to a dark solution under UV-light (Fig. 2a inset). The different quenching effects between Fe3+ and other metal cations indicate that 534-MOF-Tb can be considered as a promising luminescent probe for Fe3+ ions. Furthermore, anti-interference experiments were performed to validate the high selectivity of 534-MOF-Tb for detection of Fe3+ ions. Once Fe3+ ions are introduced into an aqueous solution of 534-MOF-Tb and the other metal cations, the luminescence is significantly quenched (Fig. S5, ESI†). The result reveals that the interference from the other metal cations (Li+, Na+, K+, Mg2+, Cu2+, Co2+, Cd2+, Ni2+, Hg2+, and Cr3+) can be neglected and further confirms the selectivity of 534-MOF-Tb for Fe3+ detection. To explore the detection limit of 534-MOF-Tb as a Fe3+ probe, concentration-dependent studies were carried out in the presence of Fe3+ ions with different concentrations. As depicted in Fig. 2c, the luminescence intensity gradually decreases as the Fe3+ contents increase. When the Fe3+ content is 800 ppm, the quenching efficiency could reach 87.8% (Fig. S6, ESI†). Detailed analysis further reveals that there exists a good linear correlation (R2 = 0.996) between the quenching efficiency and the amount of Fe3+ in the low concentration range of 0–0.1 mmol L−1 (Fig. S7, ESI†). From the slope and standard error of the fitting line, the detection limit is calculated to be 0.13 mmol L−1 according to 3σ/k (σ: standard error; k: slope),44 which is a clear indication of high sensing sensitivity of 534-MOF-Tb towards Fe3+. Quantitatively, the quenching constant (Ksv) calculated from the Stern–Volmer equation, (I0/I) = Ksv[M] + 1, can be used to explain the quenching efficiency. Here, I0 and I are the luminescence intensities of the 534-MOF-Tb suspension without and with addition of Fe3+, respectively; [M] is the molar concentration of Fe3+. As demonstrated in the Stern–Volmer plot of 534-MOF-Tb at low concentrations (Fig. S8b, ESI†), the Ksv is 5.51 × 103 M−1, which is comparable to those of previously reported MOF-based sensors (Table S3, ESI†). Considering the cost of material synthesis, the regenerable performance plays an important role in luminescent probes. Nevertheless, a fast and simple luminescence regeneration method is the important precondition. Fortunately, the luminescence intensity of 534-MOF-Tb is easily recovered by washing with water several times. Significantly, the recyclable sample can be regenerated and reused for 5 cycles and the quenching efficiencies of five cycles maintain high efficiency levels (above 99.5%) (Fig. 2d).

In previous studies, the luminescence quenching caused by metal cations may be due to four approaches: (1) the collapse of the crystal structure;15a (2) the ion exchange between central metal cations of MOFs and the targeted cations;15b (3) the interaction between metal cations and organic ligands;15c and (4) competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between the host framework and analytes.15d,e Herein, the possible sensing mechanism for the luminescence quenching by Fe3+ cations has been further investigated. Firstly, the PXRD pattern of the recyclable sample after five runs is not very well consistent with the simulated one (Fig. S9, ESI†), which indicates that the luminescence quenching may be partly caused by partial collapse of the framework. Secondly, the fast and simple regeneration method shows that luminescence quenching does not result from the ion exchange. According to the UV-Vis absorption spectra (Fig. S10, ESI†), only the Fe3+ aqueous solution exhibits a strong absorption band from 270 to 320 nm, whereas the solutions of the other metal ions have no significant absorption band in this range. Thus, competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between 534-MOF-Tb and Fe3+ ions is responsible for the luminescence quenching.15d,e

Selective sensing of anions

Simultaneously, various anions were selected to carry out the anion-sensing function of 534-MOF-Tb by similar research methods for metal cations. As shown in Fig. 3a and b, Cr2O72− and MnO4− give significant luminescence quenching effects (Fig. 3b inset), while the other anions have only slight effects on the luminescence intensities. Further, the anti-interference sensing ability towards Cr2O72− or MnO4− was further ascertained using competing experiments (Fig. S11, ESI†). The equivalent samples of 534-MOF-Tb were dispersed in mixed anionic aqueous solutions without or with Cr2O72− or MnO4−. The luminescence response of 534-MOF-Tb is related to only the existence of Cr2O72− or MnO4−, indicating that the quenching effect of Cr2O72− or MnO4− is not influenced by the other coexisting anions. The sensitivity for Cr2O72− or MnO4− is investigated using concentration-dependent experiments. The reduction of emissive intensity is in correspondence with a gradual increase of the Cr2O72− or MnO4− content. The result shows that the Cr2O72− or MnO4− content for almost complete quenching is 500 (99.1%) or 150 ppm (99.7%) (Fig. 3c, d and Fig. S12, ESI†). As shown in Fig. S13 (ESI†), the quenching efficiency linearly increases with the Cr2O72− or MnO4− content in the low concentration and the detection limit of 534-MOF-Tb for Cr2O72− or MnO4− is 0.14 or 0.34 mmol L−1 calculated by 3σ/k (σ: standard error; k: slope).44 The low detection limit indicates a high quenching efficiency constant of Cr2O72− or MnO4−. Further, the quenching constant (Ksv) for Cr2O72− (Fig. S14, ESI†) or MnO4− (Fig. S15, ESI†) is found to be 1.37 × 104 M−1 or 6.63 × 104 M−1, as verified by linear fitting of the Stern–Volmer plot in a low concentration range. Notably, there are rare MOFs which exhibit luminescence quenching for Cr2O72− or MnO4− (Table S3, ESI†). In addition, the samples of 534-MOF-Tb dispersed in the aqueous solution of Cr2O72− and MnO4− anions can be readily regenerated by washing with water in several times. The recyclable experiments for Cr2O72− and MnO4− reveal that the quenching efficiencies in cycles 1–5 remain basically unchanged and are close to 100%, displaying high recyclability and stability in the detection application (Fig. 3e and f). Similar to the situation for Fe3+, the PXRD patterns of the recyclable samples soaked in Cr2O72− and MnO4− changed after five runs (Fig. S9, ESI†). To explore the luminescence response mechanisms, UV-Vis absorption spectra of aqueous solutions of all anions tested were obtained (Fig. S16, ESI†). As shown in Fig. S16 (ESI†), only the overlaps of the absorption range between Cr2O72−/MnO4− and the excitation of 534-MOF-Tb was found, proving that the competitive adsorption of excitation wavelength energy between 534-MOF-Tb and Cr2O72−/MnO4− anions is responsible for the luminescence quenching.18a,b

Selective sensing of nitroaromatic compounds

The ability of 534-MOF-Tb to detect NACs by luminescence quenching was investigated by continuously adding NB, 2,6-DNT (2,6-dinitrotoluene), 1,3-DNB (1,3-dinitrobenzene), PA, and 4-NT to the suspension of 534-MOF-Tb. The quenching behaviors of the luminescence spectra of 534-MOF-Tb upon addition of various concentrations of NB, 4-NT, 1,3-DNB, 2,6-DNT, and PA are shown in Fig. 4a–e, respectively. All five NACs lead to luminescence quenching and the luminescence intensities of the solutions are largely dependent on the concentrations of the NACs. Upon increasing the concentration of the NACs, the luminescence intensities decrease at different levels. When the concentration of NB/2,6-DNT/1,3-DNB/PA/4-NT increases to 2800/1200/900/450/200 ppm, the quenching efficiency could reach 91.9/97.7/97.9/98.9/98.4% (Fig. S17, ESI†). As shown in Fig. 4f, 4-NT exhibits the highest quenching efficiency (98.4%) among the five NACs (NB: 48.1%, 2,6-DNT: 56.3%, 1,3-DNB: 74.85%, PA: 73.11%) in the same concentration solutions of 200 ppm. Thus, 534-MOF-Tb can distinguish 4-NT from the other four NACs, and is the first reported MOF-based sensor for detecting explosive 4-NT. For further confirming the selectivity of 4-NT, anti-interference experiments were performed (Fig. S18, ESI†). As depicted in Fig. S18a–d (ESI†), the luminescence intensities rapidly decrease when adding 4-NT to the solutions of different single NAC and equivalent 534-MOF-Tb. Further detailed analysis shows that the detection limit for 4-NT is 0.32 mmol L−1 according to 3σ/k (σ: standard error; k: slope)44 (Fig. S19, ESI†) and the Ksv value (quenching constant based on the linear Stern–Volmer plot, Fig. S20, ESI†) is 7.90 × 104 M−1. The recyclable performance of 534-MOF-Tb in 4-NT was also investigated. Importantly, 534-MOF-Tb can be easily regenerated by washing with DMF. The framework still remains intact after 5 cycles (Fig. S9, ESI†) and the quenching efficiency of each cycle maintains above 99.5% (Fig. 4g). To the best of our knowledge, 534-MOF-Tb is the first MOF that can be used for the fast and recyclable fluorescent probe for 4-NT.

As for the quenching mechanism of 534-MOF-Tb towards 4-NT, we speculate that it is attributed to competition between the absorption of the light source energy and the electronic interaction between 4-NT and the TBOT ligand.27a,31c,45 Firstly, the luminescence quenching does not stem from the collapse of the framework because of the structural integrity of the recyclable sample confirmed using the PXRD pattern (Fig. S9, ESI†). Secondly, the luminescence of 534-MOF-Tb results from the antenna effect in which the TBOT ligand adsorbs the energy and, by vibronic coupling between TBOT and Tb3+, transfers energy to Tb3+, leading to the luminescence of Tb3+. As shown in the UV-Vis absorption spectra of the five NACs (Fig. S21, ESI†), 4-NT has the highest absorbance and ε (molar extinction efficient) in comparison to the other NACs under excitation at 300 nm. Thus, 4-NT filters the most light adsorbed by TBOT ligands and decreases the probability of energy transfer from TBOT to Tb3+, which results in the strongest luminescence quenching of Tb3+.

Conclusions

In summary, a green-emission signal microporous terbium(III) MOF-based sensor was obtained via a solvothermal reaction. The luminescent material serves as a recyclable multi-responsive luminescent sensor, capable of detecting Fe3+, MnO4−, Cr2O72−, as well as 4-NT, which is the first reported MOF-based sensor for detecting explosive 4-NT. This work demonstrates that luminescent MOFs can be rationally designed to exhibit excellent luminescence-sensing properties for the detection of different analytes.
