Abstract

A new family of resorcin[4]arene‐based metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), namely, [Eu(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2]⋅3 H2O (1), [Tb(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2] 3 H2O (2), [Cd4(L)2(DMF)4(H2O)2] 3 H2O (3) and [Zn3(HL)2(H2O)2] 2 DMF⋅7 H2O (4), have been constructed from a new resorcin[4]arene‐functionalized tetracarboxylic acid (H4L=2,8,14,20‐tetra‐ethyl‐6,12,18,24‐tetra‐methoxy‐4,10,16,22‐tetra‐carboxy‐methoxy‐calix[4]arene). Isostructural 1 and 2 exhibit charming 1D motifs built with the cup‐like HL3− anions and rare earth cations. Compounds 3 and 4 show a unique sandwich‐based 2D layer and a fascinating 3D framework, respectively. Remarkably, compounds 1 and 2 display intensive red and green emissions triggered by the efficient antenna effect of organic ligands under UV light. More importantly, systematic luminescence studies demonstrate that Ln‐MOFs 1 and 2, as efficient multifunctional fluorescent materials, show highly selective and sensitive sensing of Fe3+, polyoxometalates (POMs), and acetone, which represents a rare example of a sensor for quantitatively detecting three different types of analytes. This is also an exceedingly rare example of Fe3+ and POMs detection in aqueous solutions employing resorcin[4]arene‐based luminescent Ln‐MOFs. Furthermore, the possible mechanism of the sensing properties is deduced.

Introduction

It is of great importance to identify and monitor the heavy metals, anions, and small organic solvents directly detected by the eyes through the development of advanced materials.1, 2 To date, various spectroscopic and analytical methods have been used for the detection of these species,3, 4 but low sensitivity, high cost, and high levels of interference by other analytes involving these methods have restricted their wide utilization.5 In this regard, luminescence quenching‐based detection using luminescent materials has attracted much attention owing to the selectivity, sensitivity, convenience, and short response time.6, 7 Among luminescent materials, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) displaying interesting luminescence properties, inherent or triggered by interactions with guest species, have been given considerable attention because they are useful for the development of sensing technologies among other applications.8, 9 However, up to now, only very limited luminescent MOFs‐based sensors have been discovered that are capable of quantitative detection.

As a new class of functional materials, MOFs, are comprised of infinite networks constructed by the assembly of metal cations with appropriate organic linkers.10 Apart from a large number of diverse topologies with aesthetic beauty,11 extensive efforts on MOFs have led to some important applications in the areas of gas adsorption, catalysis, drug delivery, magnetism, sensors, luminescent probes, and so on.12–17 Among these applications, there is a particular increasing trend in using luminescent MOFs to probe guest species and thus develop luminescent MOFs as promising sensors.

Among the fruitful production of luminescent MOFs, lanthanide‐based MOFs (Ln‐MOFs) as fluorescent sensors are more attractive candidates since Ln‐MOFs, especially Eu‐ and Tb‐MOFs, have exceptional luminescence features with sharp and characteristic emissions.18 The unique luminescent mechanism of Ln‐MOFs is associated with the ligand‐to‐metal energy transfer (LMET), and their emission properties are very sensitive to their structural characteristics, coordination environments, and their interactions with guest species.19 Therefore, during the construction of Ln‐MOFs, organic ligands can serve as bridging linkers for the structures and also function as antennae for the Ln3+ ions.20 In this regard, polycarboxylate‐based ligands are a good selection because Ln3+ ions are hard acceptors, making coordination with the hard polycarboxylate oxygen atoms more favorable.21 Particularly, aromatic polycarboxylate groups are excellent fluorescent chromophores, resulting in the intense luminescence of lanthanide.22 This prompted us to conduct a continuous investigation on the newly functionalized resorcin[4]arenes‐based MOFs.2d

In this work, we designed a new resorcin[4]arene‐functionalized tetracarboxylic acid: 2,8,14,20‐tetra‐ethyl‐6,12,18,24‐tetra‐methoxy‐4,10,16,22‐tetra‐carboxy‐methoxy‐calix[4]arene (H4L) ligand (Scheme 1). By utilizing the new resorcin[4]arene‐functionalized tetracarboxylic acid, a new series of MOFs, [Eu(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2] 3 H2O (1), [Tb(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2] 3 H2O (2), [Cd4(L)2(DMF)4(H2O)2] 3 H2O (3) and [Zn3(HL)2(H2O)2] 2 DMF⋅7 H2O (4), have been prepared under hydrothermal conditions. Most strikingly, systematic luminescence studies indicate that the MOFs, especially Ln‐MOFs 1 and 2, could be efficient multifunctional luminescent materials for highly selective and sensitive sensing of metal cations, anions and small organic molecules, especially for Fe3+, polyoxometalates (POMs), and acetone. The luminescent sensing mechanism was studied in detail as well.

Results and Discussion

Structural descriptions of [Eu(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2]⋅3 H2O (1) and [Tb(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2]⋅3 H2O (2)

Single‐crystal X‐ray analysis reveals that compounds [Eu(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2] 3 H2O (1) and [Tb(HL)(DMF)(H2O)2] 3 H2O (2) are isostructural and crystallize in the same triclinic space group P, so only the structure of 1 will be described in detail. As shown in Figure 1 a, the asymmetric unit of 1 consists of one EuIII cation, one HL3− anion, one terminally coordinated DMF molecule, two coordinated water molecules, and three lattice water molecules. Each EuIII cation is nine‐coordinated by six carboxylate oxygen atoms from three HL3− anions (Eu1‐O2=2.417(3), Eu1‐O7#1=2.458(4), Eu1‐O8#1=2.484(4), Eu1‐O3#2=2.492(3), Eu1‐O4#2=2.469(4)), three oxygen atoms from one terminally DMF molecule (Eu1‐O17=2.368(4)), and two waters (Eu1‐O1W=2.414(4), Eu1‐O2W=2.412(3)), forming a distorted tricapped trigonal prism. The EuO bond lengths vary from 2.368 to 2.747 Å, which are comparable to the ones found in the reported Eu‐MOFs.7 It is noticeable that each HL3− anion links three EuIII cations using three deprotonated carboxylates each in bis‐chelating coordination mode (Figure 1 b). In this way, adjacent EuIII cations are linked by the HL3− anions into a charming cup‐based 1D chain structure (Figure 1 c and 1d). For 1 and 2, the free volumes after removal of the solvent molecules are calculated by PLATON analysis to be 13.8 and 12.3 %, respectively.23

Structural description of [Cd4(L)2(DMF)4(H2O)2]⋅3 H2O (3)

Compound 3 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As illustrated in Figure 2 a, the asymmetric unit of 3 contains two CdII cations, two coordinated DMF molecules, one L4− anion, one coordinated water molecule, and one and a half lattice water molecules. Although the two CdII cations exhibit the same distorted octahedral geometries, their coordination environments are greatly different. Cd1 is bonded to six carboxylate oxygen atoms from five different L4− anions, whereas Cd2 is surrounded by two carboxylate oxygen atoms and one ether oxygen atom from two L4− anions, and three oxygen atoms from one water and two DMF molecules. As depicted in Figure 2 b, each L4− anion bridges eight CdII cations with four carboxylates and one O group. In this fashion, adjacent CdII cations are linked by the L4− anions to give a unique sandwich‐based layer structure (Figure 2 c and 2d). Notably, in the 2D structure four CdII cations are held together by six carboxylates of L4− anions to afford a tetranuclear CdII cluster. The free volume after removal of the solvent molecules is calculated by PLATON analysis to be 25.0 %.23

Structural description of [Zn3(HL)2(H2O)2]⋅2 DMF⋅7 H2O (4)

During the refinement of 4, the SQUEEZE function in PLATON was applied because of the highly disordered solvents.23 Thermogravimetric analysis and elemental analysis suggest the presence of two DMF molecules and nine water molecules (the Supporting Information, Figure S1). Compound 4 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P. As shown in Figure 3 a, the asymmetric unit of 4 is composed of three ZnII cations, two HL3− anions, two free DMF molecules, two coordinated water molecules, and seven lattice water molecules. The two ZnII cations show different coordination spheres: Zn1 is ligated by five carboxylate oxygen atoms from four HL3− anions in a distorted square‐pyramidal geometry, whereas Zn2 is six‐coordinated by four carboxylate oxygen atoms from four HL3− anions and two oxygen atoms from two waters, showing a distorted octahedral geometry. Each HL3− anion links six ZnII cations by four carboxylate groups to generate a fascinating 3D framework structure (Figure 3 b and 3 c). Notably, in the 3D framework structure three ZnII cations are hold together by four carboxylates of HL3− anions to yield a trinuclear ZnII cluster. A PLATON calculation for 4 suggests the free solvent‐accessible volume is approximately 27.5 % of the unit‐cell volume.

Luminescent properties

The luminescent spectra of the as‐synthesized samples 1, 2 and 4 are measured at room temperature. The free H4L exhibits an emission band at 473 nm (λex=375 nm), which is probably derived from the π*→π or π*→n transitions (Figure 4 a).24 The main emission peak of 4 is centered at 441 nm (λex=375 nm) and blueshifted by 32 nm in respect to H4L (Figure 4). Compounds 1 and 2 show characteristic emission bands of the corresponding Ln3+, whereas the emission from the free H4L is not observable. The absence of the free H4L emission in 1, 2, and 4 implies energy transfer from the L4− anions to the metal cations. When Eu‐based 1 is excited at 318 nm, characteristic emission bands appear at 579, 593, 617, 649, and 700 nm, corresponding to the transitions from 5D0 state to 7FJ (J=0–4) levels, respectively (Figure 4 b).25 Among these transitions, the strongest 5D0→7F2 transition (electric dipole transition) is extremely sensitive to chemical bonds in the vicinity of EuIII ion, resulting in bright‐red luminescence of 1. Moreover, the intensity ratio between the 5D0→7F2 transition and the 5D0→7F1 one (magnetic dipole transition) is widely employed as a measurement of the coordination state and the site symmetry of the EuIII cation. In 1, the intensity ratio of I(5D0→7F2)/I(5D0→7F1) is about 3.8, indicating that the symmetry of the coordination environment of EuIII ion is low, which corresponds well to the result of single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction.26

The emission spectrum of the TbIII‐containing 2 shows an intense, characteristic transition spectrum of TbIII cations upon excitation at 301 nm. As illustrated in Figure 4 c, the emission peaks at 489, 543, 584, and 623 nm correspond to the 5D4→7FJ (J=6, 5, 4, 3) transitions of the TbIII cations. The 5D4→7F6 and 5D4→7F5 emissions arise from an electric dipole transition and a magnetic dipole transition, respectively.27 The emission spectrum is dominated by the 5D4→7F5 transition, which is more intense than the others, leading to a strong green emission output for 2.

Sensing of Fe3+ cations

Currently, considerable efforts have been devoted to the detection of metal cations using the luminescent MOFs. Among the metal cations, the Fe3+ cation is one of the most necessary elements involved in biochemical processes in humans.28 An overload or deficiency of Fe3+ lead to various physiological disorders. For example, lack of Fe3+ can cause anemia, skin ailments, and insomnia, whereas when serum Fe3+ is beyond normal range it will induce certain damage of organs like the liver and kidneys.29 Therefore, the identification and quantification of Fe3+ is of great importance.

In light of the excellent luminescence and good water stability of 1, 2, and 4, we examined their potential application for detecting metal cations in water. In this study, the as‐synthesized 1, 2, and 4 were simply treated by aqueous solution of 0.01 mol L−1 AgNO3 or MClx (M=Na+, K+, Ca2+, Cd2+, Co2+, Cu2+, Mn2+, Ni2+, Zn2+ or Fe3+) to give the metal cation‐loaded 1M, 2M, and 4M solids. Among the chosen metal cations, the K+, Na+, Fe3+, Mn2+, Ca2+, and Cu2+ are abundant in cells, the Zn2+, Co2+, Ni2+, and Ag+ are trace in living organisms, whereas Cd2+ prevalent is highly toxic in the environment. As illustrated in Figure 5, the luminescent emissions of the metal cation‐loaded 1@M, 2@M, and 4@M reveal that their luminescent intensities are significantly dependent on the loaded metal cations. For 1@M, the luminescent intensities at 617 nm are decreased when loaded metal ions are involved, especially Fe3+ with the most significant quenching effect. The unusual selective quenching of Fe3+ prompted us to further evaluate the sensitivity of 1 to trace amounts of Fe3+ in water. Therefore, the concentration‐dependent studies were carried out in the presence of Fe3+ with different concentrations. As shown in Figure 6, the fluorescent intensities of 1 decline sharply with the increase of Fe3+ concentrations from 0 to 5×10−3 mol L−1. Notably, the luminescent intensity of Fe3+ loaded 1@Fe almost disappeared at a Fe3+ content of 5 μM (the Supporting Information, Figure S2). Quantitatively, this quenching effect can be rationalized by the Stern–Volmer Equation I0/I=1+KSV[M], in which the values I0 and I are the luminescent intensities of 1 without and with addition of Fe3+, respectively, KSV is the quenching constant, and [M] is the concentration of Fe3+.30 On the basis of the quenching experimental data, the linear correlation coefficient (R) in the KSV curve of 1 with addition of Fe3+ is 0.98137 (Figure 6 a), suggesting that the quenching effect of Fe3+ on the fluorescence of 1 fits well the Stern–Volmer model. The KSV value is calculated to be 1.519×103, which indicates a strong quenching effect on the luminescence of 1.

The metal ion‐loaded 2@M also exhibits a fluorescence performance similar to that of 1@M. To further study the quenching effect of trace amounts of Fe3+ in water, luminescent performance of 2 with various concentrations of loaded Fe3+ from 0 to 5×10−3 mol L−1 was conducted (the Supporting Information, Figure S3). As illustrated in Figure 6 b, the luminescent intensities of 2@Fe decrease sharply with the increasing concentrations of Fe3+. The KSV value is 4.479×103 calculated from the Stern–Volmer Equation, showing a strong quenching effect on the fluorescence of 2. Moreover, a good linear relationship is achieved with a coefficient of 0.99548. Notably, the limit of Fe3+ detection is determined as 50 μM, which is comparable to or better than some previously reported fluorescence sensors for Fe3+.18b In addition, the low detection limit is also suitable to the requirements for intracellular sensing and imaging of Fe3+.

For 4, the loaded metal ions show markedly different effects on the luminescent performance. The addition with Ag+ and K+ enhanced the luminescent intensities of 4. In contrast, the luminescent intensities are depressed when Zn2+, Na+, Ni2+, or Fe3+ are involved, especially Fe3+ with the most significant quenching effect, is more than 5.0 times weaker than that of the original. The rest of the tested metal cations (Cd2+, Co2+, Ca2+, Mn2+, Cu2+) did not cause any significant change to the intensities of the luminescence. For a better understanding of the fluorescence response of 4 to Fe3+, concentration‐dependent luminescent measurements were performed. As depicted in Figure 6 c, the emission intensities of 4 decreased with the increase of Fe3+ concentrations from 0 to 0.01 mol L−1. The KSV value of 381.85 deduced from the Stern–Volmer Equation is much smaller than those of 1 and 2 toward Fe3+ cations (see Table 1). The results demonstrate that the luminescent quenching effects of 1 and 2 are much more sensitive to trace amounts of Fe3+ ions than 4.

The above luminescent studies indicate that 1, 2, and 4 could selectively sense the exoteric Fe3+ through the luminescent quenching. It can be inferred that the decrease of emission intensity may account for little effective intramolecular energy transfer from the L ligand to metal ions with the addition of Fe3+.18b Moreover, the PXRD patterns of the Fe3+ loaded 1@Fe, 2@Fe, and 4@Fe were measured, which are in good agreement with the as‐synthesized ones, demonstrating that the basic frameworks remain unchanged after loaded Fe3+ (the Supporting Information, Figures S4–S6).

The luminescent quenching results demonstrate that the energy‐transfer process become little effective with the addition of Fe3+. To deeply identify the luminescent responses induced by the addition of metal ions, the UV/Vis spectra of the metal ion solutions are measured. As shown in Figure S7 (the Supporting Information), the solution of Fe3+ exhibits a strong absorption band from 250 to 400 nm, whereas the remaining metal ion solutions have no significant absorption band in this range. In addition, the strong absorption bands of 1, 2, and 4 are located in the range of 250–350 nm (the Supporting Information, Figure S8), which are completely overlapped by the absorption band of Fe3+ ion solution. This is possibly responsible for the observed luminescent quenching, which effectively suppresses transfer of the excitation energy to metal ions, leading to a sharp decrease in the luminescent intensities or even quenching.27

Sensing of POMs anions

POMs, as a subset of metal oxides, represent a tremendous range of inorganic clusters with an unmatched range of physical and chemical properties.31 Thus far, a number of applications in catalysis, magnetism, molecular electronics, medicine, and materials science have been found for POMs because of their wide range of sizes, composition, redox chemistry, and charge distribution, et al.32–36 As a result, the qualitatively and quantitatively chemical analyses of the POMs should be performed accurately for these applications. Generally, spectrophotometric and titrimetric methods are employed for the chemical analyses of POMs.37 Recently, electronic tongue (ET) multisensory and catalytic adsorptive stripping voltammetry have also been developed to identify and quantify POMs.38 However, to date reports are rare on the utilization of luminescent Ln‐based MOFs as a platform for sensing POMs in aqueous solution. In this study, various POM anions were chosen to perform the POMs‐sensing function by immersing 1, 2, and 4 in different aqueous solutions of POMs (10−2 M), including H4[SiW12O40]⋅26 H2O, H3[PW12O40]⋅12 H2O, H3[PMo12O40]⋅13H2O, K6[P2W18O62]⋅15 H2O, K6[P2Mo18O62], Na9[PW9O34]⋅7 H2O, K14[NaP5W30O110]⋅15 H2O, (NH4)6[Mo7O24]⋅4 H2O and K[SiW11CoO40H3]⋅3 H2O (Figure 7).

To understand the ability of Ln‐MOFs 1 and 2 for detection of POMs in water, luminescent experiments were carefully conducted. Typically, compounds 1 and 2 were immerged into water and different POMs aqueous solutions, and their emission spectra were achieved when excited at 318 and 301 nm, respectively. As shown in Figure 8, their emission spectra show well‐resolved luminescence of the f–f transitions of Eu3+ and Tb3+ ions, which are attributable to the 5D0→7FJ (J=0–4) and 5D4→7FJ (J=6–3) transitions, respectively. However, the luminescent intensities of 1 and 2 decreased sharply in aqueous solutions of the POMs. In particular, the luminescent intensities of the 5D4→7F5 transition for the Eu3+ ion and 5D0→7F2 transition for the Tb3+ ion almost disappeared, indicating that 1 and 2 are sensitive to POMs and can be considered as potential fluorescent probes for POMs in aqueous solution.

To further investigate the sensitive sensing properties for POMs, the emission spectra of 1 and 2 in different concentrations of [PW12O40]3− were studied carefully. As illustrated in Figure 9, the emission intensities of 1 and 2 were sharply depressed with the increase of [PW12O40]3− concentrations. In particular, when the concentration of [PW12O40]3− reached to 1 mM, the emission intensities are almost completely quenched with quenching efficiencies of 93.75 and 99.92 % for 1 and 2, respectively (the Supporting Information, Figure S9). The result indicates that Ln‐MOFs 1 and 2 are extremely sensitive towards POMs, making it an excellent candidate for the luminescent detection of POMs in aqueous solutions.

Moreover, luminescent sensing property of the luminescent d10 metal‐based 4 toward POMs in aqueous solutions was also studied. As demonstrated in Figure 8 c, compound 4 shows selective luminescent sensing of POMs anions. The luminescent intensities of 4 in aqueous solutions of [PW12O40]3−, [PW9O34]9−, and [Mo7O24]6− are almost the same as that in water, demonstrating that there are no obvious responses to the luminescence of 4. However, the luminescent intensities of 4 significantly decreased in the aqueous solutions of [PMo12O40]3−, [P2W18O62]6−, and [P2Mo18O62]6−. Nevertheless, the [SiW12O40]4− and [SiW11CoO40H3]− only exhibit moderate quenching effects on the luminescent intensities of 4. The results suggest that 4 can selectively detect POMs in aqueous solutions.

It is well established that the luminescent mechanism of Ln‐MOFs is based on antenna sensitization effects. The organic ligands around the lanthanide centers firstly absorbed light, and then energy was transferred to the lanthanide centers through the organic ligands.39 Finally, fluorescence arose from the Ln centers. Thus, it can be inferred that the luminescent quenching effect of Ln‐MOFs 1 and 2 in POMs aqueous solution probably originates from the homogeneous‐phase photoinduced electron and energy‐transfer processes between POMs aqueous solutions and the lattices of 1 and 2.40 Usually, under UV irradiation POMs exhibit fast and reversible multi‐electron redox transformations. On the other hand, the structures of 1 and 2 demonstrate that it is impossible to efficiently adsorb the water‐soluble POMs. Thus, the reversible and fast multi‐electron redox processes is probably from the interfaces between the POMs aqueous solutions and solid‐state lattices of 1 or 2. Typically, when 1 and 2 were soaked into aqueous POMs solution, the photoelectrons is capable of partly transfer to POMs, thus decreasing the energy‐transfer efficiency from the organic ligands to the Eu3+ and Tb3+ centers. Thus, the decayed luminescence was observed upon increasing the concentrations of POMs gradually.

Sensing of small organic molecules

Organic pollutants, such as small organic molecules, are increasingly concerned owing to their environmental hazards.41 In this regard, luminescent MOFs, as a promising class of sensing materials, have been investigated to identity small organic pollutants, in view of the obvious luminescent signals induced by the small organic molecules. To evaluate the potential of 1 and 2 for the sensing of small organic molecules, their luminescent behaviors in different solvent suspensions were studied carefully. Typically, the finely ground samples of 1, 2, and 4 (3 mg) were dispersed in different solvents (3 mL), including DMF, methanol, ethanol, acetone, acetonitrile, isopropanol and butyl alcohol, respectively. Then, the suspensions were ultrasonicated for 30 min for fluorescence studies.

As demonstrated in Figure 10, the luminescent intensities of 1 are highly dependent on the solvents, especially in the case of acetone, which exhibited a great quenching effect. As a result, the acetone content in the suspension was gradually increased to monitor the emissive response. As illustrated in Figure 11, the luminescent intensities of the suspension sharply decreased with the addition of acetone. Particularly, the emissions almost disappeared at a content of 25 vol % for acetone. Clearly, the decreasing trend of the luminescent intensities is significant to detect the presence of small amounts of acetone in solution.

Similarly, the luminescent intensities of 2 in the suspension also heavily depend on the solvent types, particularly in the presence of acetone, which exhibits clearly quenching effect. Consequently, the quenching effect of acetone solvent was further examined in detail. The emissive response of the dispersed suspension was identified, when the content of acetone was gradually increased. It can be found that the luminescent intensities significantly decrease with the increasing content of acetone in the suspension. Remarkably, the emissions almost disappeared at a content of 5.0 vol % for acetone (Figure 11 b). The result indicates that 2 is much more sensitive to the acetone than 1, and can be used to detect trace amounts of acetone in solution.

The above luminescent quenching study indicates that the effect of the guest solvents on LMET definitely acts as a significant role because the efficiency of LMET is crucial for the emission of rare earth.42 For 1 and 2, the lattice guest molecules are replaced by acetone gradually, and the luminescent intensities of their suspensions are quenched. The result demonstrates that the LMET efficiency is influenced by the interactions of organic ligands with acetone solvents in the lattices and thus results in luminescent quenching response.18b On the other hand, acetone exhibits a strong UV absorption band from 250 to 350 nm, while no significant absorption bands are observed for other solvents in this range. As illustrated in Figure S10 (the Supporting Information), the UV absorption bands of H4L, 1 and 2 are almost mantled by the wide absorption of acetone. Therefore, competition exists for the absorption of the light source energy between MOFs and acetone molecules from 250 to 310 nm. It was suggested that the absorbed energy by the L ligands is partially or completely transferred to the acetone molecules, leading to a decrease in the luminescent intensity or quenching.43

Conclusion

A new family of MOFs with fascinating 1D‐, 2D‐, and 3D‐motifs have been successfully prepared by employing the new resorcin[4]arene‐functionalized tetracarboxylic acid. Systematic investigation of the luminescent emissions demonstrates that the frameworks are excellent candidates as potential multifunctional luminescent probes for selective and sensitive detection of metal cations, anions and small organic molecules through fluorescence quenching. More significantly, the MOFs perform as rare examples of highly selective and sensitive fluorescence probe for Fe3+ ions and POMs anions in aqueous solutions. Further study and speculation of the mechanism exhibit that the mainly luminescent responses to metal cations and solvents are triggered by the interaction of substrate (Fe3+ or acetone) with organic ligands, which affect the LMET efficiency. The sensing of POMs likely originates from the homogeneous‐phase photoinduced electron and energy‐transfer processes between POMs aqueous solutions and the lattices of the MOFs. In addition, the present work represents a rare example of a sensor for quantitatively detecting three different types of analytes, and will probably be useful under conditions that are more realistic in the future. The related studies are currently under way.

Boron trifluoride etherate (11.36 g, 80 mmol) was added dropwise to the solution and the mixture was stirred at room temperature overnight. Then the reaction mixture was washed using water (100 mL), and the organic layer was dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate.
