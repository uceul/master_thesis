The role of solvents in framework dimensionality and their effect on band gap energy†

K. S. Asha , P. R. Kavyasree , Anu George and Sukhendu Mandal *
School of Chemistry, Indian Institute of Science Education and Research Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, 695016 India. E-mail: sukhendu@iisertvm.ac.in; Web: http://iisertvm.ac.in/~sukhendum Fax: (+91) 471-2597-438

Received 6th June 2014 , Accepted 1st November 2014

First published on 5th November 2014

The crystal growth rate and morphology can be modulated by tuning the ratio of the solvent in mixed solvents during synthesis. We present here a solvothermal method to synthesize a Cd–FDC based metal–organic framework with different morphologies by tuning the ratio of water in the DMF–water mixed solvent system without adding any other additives. With the increasing water volume ratio a series of crystals with different morphologies were synthesized. Among these we have isolated two single crystal structures, [Cd3(FDC)3(DMF)4(H2O)], 1 and [DMA]2[Cd3(FDC)4]·2H2O, 2. Compound 1 was synthesized from DMF with small amounts of water while 2 was formed from 25 vol% of water in the DMF–water mixed solvent. Compound 1 contains trimer clusters as building units, which are linked by the furan dicarboxylate ligand to form a two-dimensional structure. Compound 2 also contains trimer clusters which are linked to each other to form a one-dimensional chain with the Cd–O–Cd linkage. This one-dimensional chain in turn is connected by the furan dicarboxylate ligand to form a three-dimensional structure. All these structures are characterized by SEM, XRD, TGA and IR. We have measured the band gap energy and measurements show that the values are decreasing from 1 to 2. The lower band gap energy of 2 may be due to the presence of infinite Cd–O–Cd linkages which split the states of the conduction band and reduces the band gap energy.

Introduction

Enormous efforts have been made toward the synthesis of metal organic frameworks (MOFs) due to their potential application in catalysis, gas/solvent storage and separation, magnetism, luminescence, etc.1–22 The controlled synthesis of the MOF for target materials is still a great challenge, as several factors are associated with its synthesis. It has been proved that the solvent,23 pH,24 counter anion,25 concentration26 and temperature27 are the factors which determine the overall structure of the compound, thus, control over solvothermally obtained products is very difficult. For synthesizing target materials it is very important to understand the role of the above said factors. Temperature is one of the important factors, which governs the overall structure of the materials. Systematic studies on the effect of temperature on the dimensionality of MOFs are very rare.27 Cheetham et al.27e and Natarajan et al.27g,h have shown how thermal desolvation and subsequent changes in the coordination environment lead to different dimensionality. Solvent effect is an important parameter for influencing the formation of MOFs.23 Due to their different physical and chemical properties, different solvents influence the crystal growth rate and the final morphology. The solvents also play an important role via solute–solvent interactions towards the synthesis of the hierarchy of structures. The effect of solvents on the structural dimensionality will provide a new route to synthesize target materials but has not been studied systematically.23g

Design of band gap energy tunable materials is enormously important in the field of nanoscience and nanotechnology due to their possible application as optoelectronic materials.28,29 Generally MOF structures possess a band structure due to the extended network and exhibit a definite band gap energy.30 Surprisingly, the tunability of the band gap energy of MOFs has not been explored as widely as applications like gas sorption, catalysis, and magnetism.1–22 MOFs afford the opportunity for variable architecture as well as control over dimensionality via a judicious choice of metals and organic linkers. This variable architecture and dimensionality offers the feasibility of chemically tuning the bulk electronic properties of the MOFs by changing the metals and linkers.31 Recently Stroppa et al. have done extensive theoretical calculations on electric properties of multiferroic perovskite like MOFs.32 To the best of our knowledge very few MOFs have been reported where the band gap energy has been measured extensively, which can enhance their performance as semiconductors.33

Theoretical calculations of the MOF structure also predict that the valance band (VB) localized on the organic ligand and the metal or metal oxide cluster contributing towards the conduction band (CB).33b Recently it has been shown that conjugation and functionalization of linkers have a pronounced effect towards the band gap energy.33 However, the band gap engineering for MOFs is still unexplored. Hence, there remains a demand to understand the effect of several factors towards the band gap energy of MOF materials.

Herein we report two new MOFs synthesized solvothermally using Cd(II), H2FDC (H2FDC = 2,5-furandicarboxylic acid) (1:1), at 100 °C with the DMF–H2O mixture as the solvent. [Cd3(FDC)3(DMF)4(H2O)], 1, is a two-dimensional structure, and [DMA]2[Cd3(FDC)4]·2H2O, 2, is a three-dimensional structure. Compound 1 contains a corner shared trimer as the building unit while in compound 2, a corner- and edge-shared trimer is the building unit. Band gap energy measurements show that the band gap decreases from compound 1 to compound 2. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first example where systematic tuning of dimensionality and their effects on the band gap energy is achieved via a solvent tuning strategy.

Results and discussion

Synthetic aspects

A series of crystals with different sizes and morphologies were obtained by varying the ratio of DMF–water mixed solvents. Fig. 1 shows SEM images of the samples synthesized with different volume ratios of water in DMF–water solvents. When we used pure DMF with small amounts of water as a solvent, the product obtained was rod-like crystals suitable for single crystal X-ray diffraction (Fig. 1A). On adding water to DMF (water ratio = 25 vol%), the resulting samples became block-type and were also good for single crystal X-ray diffraction (Fig. 1B). When the amount of water (water ratio = 50 vol%) was increased a plate-like crystal aggregate was formed (Fig. 1C), whose quality was not good for single crystal X-ray diffraction. When the water ratio was further increased (75 vol%) crystalline powder samples were formed (no specific morphology) (Fig. 1D). When 100 percent water was used as the solvent, no product was formed. PXRD patterns for all the compounds are shown in Fig. S1.†

Mechanism for morphological change

The rate of nucleation and crystal growth can be controlled by tuning the rate of deprotonation of organic linkers or adding capping agents to inhibit the coordination interaction between metal ions and organic linkers, resulting in crystals with different morphologies. In the present case, we have tuned the water ratio in the DMF–water mixture; as both water and DMF are polar solvents and hard to form an emulsion, we inferred that this change in the morphology of crystals may be related to the degree of deprotonation of H2FDC in the mixed solvent system since no capping agents were added. DMF has a stronger ability to speed up the deprotonation of H2FDC than water, based on the fact that DMF molecules are more favourable to form weak coordination with hydrogen atoms. When less amounts of water were used, it could reduce the association effects between H2FDC molecules by interleaving the water molecules in them. However, more water in the mixed solvent will in turn inhibit the deprotonation of the organic linker.

On the basis of synthesis conditions and crystals formed, a mechanism was proposed for the formation of Cd-FDC crystals with different morphologies. When DMF with small amounts of water was used as the solvent, the deprotonation of H2FDC was fast and formed rod-like crystal aggregates (Fig. 1A). When a small amount of water (25 vol%) was introduced into the solution as a mixed solvent, it reduced the association effects between H2FDC molecules, resulting in accelerated rates of deprotonation and nucleation, therefore decreasing the crystal size (Fig. 1B). At the same time, water promoted crystal growth in one specific direction, so the crystal became a thick block-type (Fig. 1B). On the other hand, the length of the crystal was reduced due to the dispersive action of water. Too much of water inhibited the deprotonation of H2FDC and reduced its solubility in the reaction mixtures, which decreased the amount of deprotonated H2FDC ions available for coordination and slowed down nucleation rates, which led to increased crystal size and different morphologies (Fig. 1C and 1D).

Structural description

Single crystal X-ray analysis reveals that the compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic system with the space group P21/n (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit contains three Cd ions, three molecules of FDC ligands, four molecules of dimethyl formamide (DMF) and one water molecule (Fig. S2†). The Cd(1) ion adopts a distorted octahedral geometry with three oxygen atoms from DMF, one from water and two from carboxylate moieties. Cd(2) and Cd(3) ions adopt a distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometry. The Cd(2) ion is heptacoordinated with six carboxylate oxygen atoms and one oxygen atom from DMF. While the Cd(3) ion is heptacoordinated with seven carboxylate oxygen atoms. The Cd–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.214 (6)–2.575(5) Å (Av. 2.236 Å) (Table S2†). The Cd ions are connected through oxygen atoms to form the trinuclear cluster (Fig. 2a). These Cd polyhedral units are linked through corners (Fig. 2b). This trimeric cluster unit with six FDC ligands forms a hexa-connected Cd3(RCOO)6 node (Fig. S3†). Each trinuclear cluster is connected to four such clusters through six FDC ligands and forming the two-dimensional structure in the ab plane (Fig. 2c). The coordinated water and dimethylformamide molecules occupy the interlayer spaces and interact with the framework through O–H⋯O and C–H⋯O hydrogen-bond interactions (Table S3†). Topological analysis using TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with 4,9-c net with stoichiometry (4-c)(9-c)2; 2-nodal net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {310·423·52·6}2{34·42} (td10 = 619) (see ESI†).34
Fig. 2 (a) Trimeric cluster present in 1, (b) corner-shared Cd polyhedral units, (c) trimeric clusters are linked with FDC ligands to form the two-dimensional structure in the ab plane.

Compound 2 was formed using 25 vol% of water in the DMF–H2O mixture. The asymmetric unit of 2 contains one and half Cd ions, two molecules of FDC, one molecule of the dimethyl ammonium ion and one molecule of lattice water (Fig. S4†). Both Cd ions are hexacoordinated with carboxylate oxygen atoms and adopt a distorted octahedral geometry. The Cd(1) ion is connected to Cd(2) ions through oxygen atoms to form the trinuclear cluster. In this trimeric cluster the Cd(1) octahedral unit is connected to Cd(2) octahedra through a corner whereas two Cd(2) octahedral units are connected through an edge (Fig. 3a). These trimeric clusters are linked to form the infinite Cd–O–Cd chain (Fig. 3b). The Cd–O bond lengths are in the range of 2.185–2.519 Å (Av. 2.318 Å) (Table S4†). This trimeric cluster unit with eleven FDC ligands form an eleven connected Cd3(RCOO)11 node (Fig. S5†). The one-dimensional Cd–O–Cd chains are arranged in AAA fashion and are linked by the FDC ligand to form the layered structure along the bc plane (Fig. 3c). This two-dimensional layer is pillared by another type of FDC ligand to form the three-dimensional structure in the ab plane (Fig. 3d). A three-dimensional structure contains two different types of channels, one is 6-membered and another is 4-membered. The 6-membered ring channel is more hydrophilic in nature where oxygen atoms of the furan ring are pointed towards the channel whereas the carbon backbone of the furan ring pointed towards the 4-membered ring channel. The hydrophilic channel contains the dimethyl ammonium cation (Fig. S6†). The furan rings are separated by 3.48 Å, which clearly indicates the favourable π–π interactions in the structure (Fig. S7†). Topological analysis using TOPOS software identified that the structure has a new topological network with 11,12-c net with stoichiometry (11-c)2(12-c); 2-nodal net, which can be presented as a Schläfli symbol {315·433·57}2{318·436·511·6} (td10 = 4160) (see ESI†).34


Fig. 3 (a) Trimeric cluster in 2, (b) trimers are linked to form the infinite Cd–O–Cd chains, (c) One-dimensional chains are linked by the FDC ligand to form the 2D layer along the bc plane, (d) 2D layers are pillared by the FDC ligand to form the 3D structure along the ab plane.

Band gap energy

We have collected the diffuse reflectance UV-Vis spectra of the powder samples of 1 and 2 in order to measure the band gap energy (Ebg), which was confirmed as the intersection point between the energy axis and the line extrapolated from the linear portion of the absorption edge in a plot of the Kubelka–Munk function vs. energy. As shown in Fig. 4, the optical absorption associated with Ebg can be assessed at 3.58 eV and 2.75 eV for 1 and 2, respectively. It was reported that this kind of material exhibits flat bands, which indicates an indirect band gap in the material.28,35 To understand these variations in the band gap energy, we have performed electronic structure studies on the MOFs using Density Functional Theory (DFT). The density of states for all compounds of 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 5a and S9.† The calculated band gap energy of 1 is 3.26 eV which is slightly lower than the experiment, while for 2 it is 3.19 eV, which is higher than the experiment. The projected density of states on compounds 1 and 2 are quite similar and that the top of the valence band (HOMO) is composed of a furan motif (Fig. 5b) and the bottom of the conduction band (LUMO) is localized over the furan and carboxylate moieties (Fig. 5c). The furan motif is unchanged in compounds 1 and 2 (Fig. S8†), so the top of the valence band (HOMO) position would be more or less same. The bottom of the conduction band (LUMO) is different in compounds 1 and 2 due to the different coordination of Cd ions with carboxylate oxygen atoms and dimensionality (1 is two-dimensional and 2 is three-dimensional, respectively). Single crystal X-ray structural analysis reveals that compound 1 contains three Cd ions (one adopts distorted octahedral and other two adopt distorted pentagonal bipyramidal geometry) and these ions are linked through a corner to form the trimer cluster (Fig. 2a), while in the case of 2, two Cd ions adopt a distorted octahedral geometry. These Cd ions form a trimer where octahedral units are linked through both a corner and an edge (Fig. 3a). These trimers are linked to form the one-infinite Cd–O–Cd chain structure (Fig. 3b). In the case of 2, the interactions between the Cd ions are more (trimers are linked in 2 while trimers are isolated in 1) which splits the states of the conduction band (CB) and reduce the band gap energy.28a,35c To understand the role of metal coordination on the band gap energy we have activated the samples and measured the band gap energy of the activated samples. On activation compound 1 losses coordinated DMF and water molecules while in the case of compound 2 lattice water and the extra framework cation were removed. Loss of coordinated solvents leads to change in the metal coordination site whereas loss of extra framework cations and solvent molecules cannot affect the metal coordination site. Activated 1 (abbreviated as 1′) lost the structural integrity due to loss of coordinated solvent molecules while activated 2 (2′) retains the structural rigidity as evidence by PXRD patterns (Fig. S10†). The measured band gap energy also reflects the same as the PXRD pattern. For 1′ the band gap energy reduced to 2.40 eV which is much lesser than 1 (Ebg = 3.58 eV) (Fig. S11†) while 2′ (Ebg = 2.68 eV) has the band gap energy close to 2 (Ebg = 2.75 eV) (Fig. S12†) (Table 1).

Luminescent properties

The room temperature photoluminescence properties of compounds 1 and 2 have been investigated in the solid state and compared with the free ligand (Fig. S13†). The excitation wavelength of 300 nm was set from the absorption maxima determined from the UV-Vis spectral data. The emission spectra of the free FDC ligand show maxima at 370 nm. The emission spectra of as-made 1 and 2 display strong emission bands at 378 nm and 399 nm, respectively. Red shifts of 8 nm and 29 nm were observed in the emission spectra upon coordination with the free FDC ligand in the as-made framework of 1 and 2, respectively. The emission spectra are more red shifted in 2 and this is due to strong π-conjugation of the ligands in the structure.

Experimental section

Materials

All chemicals were of reagent-grade quality obtained from Sigma-Aldrich and used as received without further purification. The elemental analysis (C, H and N) was carried out on the vario MICRO cube elemental analyzer. The FT-IR spectrum was recorded using KBr pellets in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 on a SHIMADZU FT-IR spectrometer. The TGA was performed on a SDT Q600 (Shimadzu) analyser in flowing nitrogen with a heating rate of 10 °C per minute. Powder X-ray diffraction data were collected using an X'pert PRO (PANalytics) powder diffractometer equipped with Cu Kα radiation (1.540598 Å).

Synthesis

Compound 1. The compound 1 was synthesized using Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.0616 gm, 0.2 mmol) and 2,5-furandicarboxylic acid (0.0312 g, 0.2 mmol) in 10 mL N,N′-dimethylformamide (DMF) in a glass vial. Tiny drops of water were added to this. The mixture was sonicated for 5 minutes and the reaction mixture was heated at 100 °C for 2 days. Then it was cooled to room temperature. The colourless rod-like crystals were formed. The crystals were washed with DMF several times to remove the crystalized ligand. Yield was 80% based on the metal.
Compound 2. The compound 2 was synthesized using Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.0616 gm, 0.2 mmol) in water (2.5 mL) and 2,5-furandicarboxylic acid (0.0312 g, 0.2 mmol) in DMF (7.5 mL) in a glass vial. The mixture was sonicated for 5 minutes and the reaction mixture was heated at 100 °C for 2 days. Then it was cooled to room temperature. The colourless block-type crystals were formed. Crystals were washed several times to remove the crystalized ligand. Yield was 75% based on the metal.
Single crystal X-ray diffraction. For single crystal XRD measurement, suitable crystals for both the compounds were carefully selected under a polarizing microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber. The single crystal diffraction data were collected on a Bruker AXS Smart Apex CCD diffractometer at 298 K. The X-ray generator was operated at 50 kV and 35 mA using Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) radiation. Data were collected with a ω scan width of 0.3°. A total of 606 frames were collected in three different settings of φ (0°, 90°, 180°) keeping the sample-to-detector distance fixed at 6.03 cm and the detector position (2θ) fixed at −25°.

The data were reduced using SAINTPLUS36 and an empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.37 The crystal structure was determined by direct methods using SHELXS97 and refined using SHELXL97 present in the SHELXTL V6.1438 package. All hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions during the final step of the refinement process. For the final refinement, the hydrogen atoms were placed in geometrically ideal positions and refined using the riding mode. The last cycles of the refinement included atomic positions, anisotropic thermal parameters for all the non-hydrogen atoms, and isotropic thermal parameters for all the hydrogen atoms. Full-matrix-least-squares structure refinement against F2 was carried out using the WINGX39 package of programs. The crystallographic parameters for compounds 1 and 2 are provided in Table S1.† CCDC 1007066–1007067 contain the crystallographic data for 1 and 2, respectively.

Characterization

Initial characterization was carried out by elemental analysis, powder X-ray diffraction (XRD), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), and IR spectroscopic studies. Elemental analysis of the crystals was carried out using a CHNS analyzer. Elemental analysis calculated (%) for 1: C 32.46, H 3.26, N 5.04; found: C 32.42, H 3.23, N 5.01 and 2: C 31.08, H 2.61, N 2.58; found: C 31.05, H 2.58, N 2.57.

The powder XRD pattern was recorded on crushed single crystals in the 2θ range 5–50°. The XRD pattern indicated that the product was a new material; the pattern was entirely consistent with the simulated XRD patterns generated based on the structures determined using single-crystal XRD (Fig. S14†). The TGA for as-synthesized materials showed an initial weight-loss step at 25–200 °C, which is due to the loss of coordinated DMF and water molecules for 1 and lattice water molecules for 2 (Fig. S15†). Compound 1 was stable up to 380 °C, whereas 2 was stable up to 400 °C. The second weight loss after 380 °C for 1 and 400 °C for 2, is due to loss of the furan dicarboxylate ligand. IR spectroscopic studies were carried out in the range 400–4000 cm−1 using the KBr pellet method (Perkin-Elmer, SPECTRUM 1000). The IR spectrum exhibited typical peaks corresponding to the lattice and coordinated water molecules, dimethyl formamide, dimethyl ammonium cation and carboxylate moieties in their respective bands (Table S5 and Fig. S16†).

Activation of the compound. The compound was activated under high vacuum to remove the guest solvent molecules from the structure. For this the obtained crystals were ground well into fine powder and the powder was activated under vacuum using the Schlenk line at 150 °C overnight. TGA data of the activated samples show that the activated compounds are completely desolvated (Fig. S17†).

Optical band-gap measurements

The optical absorption spectra of 1, 1′, 2 and 2′ were recorded on a Shimadzu UV/Vis/NIR spectrophotometer. The room temperature diffused reflectance spectra were measured using BaSO4 as a reference material and converted to a Kubelka–Munk function, from which the band gap of the compound was estimated.40,41 This two-flux model, which considers only diffused light, is used to determine the absorption coefficient from a surface that both scatters and absorbs incident radiation. Using the above equation, the band gap of a material can be obtained by extrapolating to zero with the linear fit to a plot of (κhν)1/nvs. hν.

Conclusions

We have demonstrated an interesting method to synthesize Cd-based Metal–Organic Framework (MOF) crystals with different morphologies by altering the ratio of water in DMF–water mixed solvents. Here we have explored the effects of water on controlling the nucleation and the crystal growth process in detail. A small amount of water is necessary for the deprotonation of the organic linker H2FDC in a DMF solution. However, a critical amount of water is crucial, as excess water in turn will inhibit its deprotonation and its solubility. Thus we have controlled the deportation rate of the organic linker, which influenced the nucleation rate and crystal morphology, by tuning the water ratio in the mixed solvent. From a rod-like single crystal to a block-like single crystal to plate-like crystalline materials and to crystalline powder materials, all were formed by tuning the water ratio. During these processes we have isolated two single crystal structures [Cd3(FDC)3(DMF)4(H2O)], 1 and [DMA]2[Cd3(FDC)4]·2H2O, 2. Both the structures contain trimeric clusters as building units and in the case of 1, trimers are linked to form a layered structure while in the case of 2, trimers are linked to form the infinite Cd–O–Cd chains which in turn are connected to form the three-dimensional structure. Band gap energy measurements show that compound 2 (Ebg = 2.75 eV) has a lower band gap energy than 1 (Ebg = 3.58 eV). Theoretical calculations show that HOMO is localized on the furan motif and LUMO is composed of furan and carboxylate moieties. The difference in metal coordination, bonding and dimensionality can affect the bottom of the conduction band (LUMO) which could be responsible for the variation of the band gap energy in 1 and 2, respectively. The band gap energy of the activated compounds show that metal coordination has a pronounced effect towards the band gap energy as the band gap energy of 1 (Ebg = 3.58 eV) reduced to 2.40 eV (activated 1′) due to loss of coordinated solvent molecules. So using the solvent tuning method one can synthesize materials with variable band gap energy, which can be modified by activation to create materials with low band gap energy. These low band gap energy materials can find applications in photocatalysis, water splitting and solar energy conversion.
