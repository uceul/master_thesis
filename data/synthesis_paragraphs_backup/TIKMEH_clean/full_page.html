Two cadmium(II) coordination polymers constructed by carboxylate and pyridine mixed ligands: synthesis, structure and luminescent properties†

Xiaoping Zhang, Jingmin Zhou, Wei Shi*, Zhenjie Zhang and Peng Cheng*
Department of Chemistry, Key Laboratory of Advanced Energy Materials Chemistry (MOE), Nankai University, Tianjin 300071, PR China. E-mail: shiwei@nankai.edu.cn; pcheng@nankai.edu.cn; Fax: +86 22 23502458

Received 9th June 2013, Accepted 5th September 2013

First published on 6th September 2013

Two new cadmium(II) coordination polymers (CPs), {[Cd(BTG)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5glycol·H2O}n (1) and {[Cd6(BTB)4(bpy)4.5(glycol)]·9H2O}n (2) (H2BTG = 1,2,3-benzene tricarboxylic acid 2-(2-hydroxy-ethyl) ester, an ester of 1,2,3-benzenetricarboxylic acid and glycol; H3BTB = 1,2,3-benzenetricarboxylic acid), have been synthesized with the same reactants but different reaction conditions. CP 1 possesses an interesting stair-shaped 2D (4,4) network that stacks in a parallel fashion with a dihedral angle of 80.599(5)°. CP 2 exhibits a complicated 3D structure which can be simplified into a 3,10-connected network with an unreported Schläfli symbol of {32·4}{34·421·513·67}. The luminescent properties of CPs 1 and 2 are studied.

Introduction

In recent years, the design and construction of coordination polymers have received great attention for their intriguing topological structures and physical properties.1,2 The diversity of the structures is due to the coordination preferences of the metal ions and the binding character of the organic ligands.3 The architectural assembly of coordination polymers is significantly affected by various kinds of external physical or chemical factors, such as temperature, pH, ratio of reactants and solvent.4 Among these factors, temperature is one of the fundamental keys. A typical example is Co(II) succinates, which possess six different structures with reaction temperatures in the range of 75–200 °C at intervals of 25 °C.5 The pH value is another significant factor. For instance, multi-carboxylic acids have different deprotonation types under different pH values, leading to ligands with plentiful coordination modes.6 In addition, the concentrations and ratio of reactants can have a direct influence on the process of crystal growth.7 It would be of great importance for scientists to study the influence of reaction conditions on the final architecture structures and the rational design and prediction of coordination polymers is still a great challenge.

Containing N- or O-donors, pyridine and carboxylate derivatives have been hot types of organic ligands for CPs.8,9 Compared with the usually used carboxylic acids, such as 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid and 1,3,5-benzenetricarboxylic acid, 1,2,3-benzenetricarboxylic acid (H3BTB) has been paid less attention. Among the numerous benzene-carboxylic acids, H3BTB has special characteristics. Firstly, H3BTB possesses three adjacent carboxylic groups that could be deprotonated to H2BTB−, HBTB2– and BTB3–, and form plentiful linking modes from monodentate to multidentate.10,11 Secondly, the three neighboring carboxyl groups can rotate to satisfy the space requirement and can extend the structure by both short bridges via the carboxylates and long bridges via the benzene ring.12,13 However, Cd(II) CPs with H3BTB are less documented.14 In reported examples, [Cd3(BTB)2(L)(H2O)2]3·2.5H2O (L = tetrakis-(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)methane) is a chiral 3D α-Po structure based on trinuclear Cd(II) clusters;14a [Cd(H2BTB)2(H2O)4]·2H2O is a 2D hydrogen-bonded complex,14c and [Cd5(BTB)2(HBTB)2(bpy)3(H2O)2]n shows a 3D coordination framework that is constructed from 2D metal–carboxylate layers pillared by rod-like bpy spacers.14b,d On the other hand, an assistant ligand such as 4,4′-bipyridine (bpy) can help to deprotonate the carboxylate and serve as linear linkers to extend the structure into higher dimensions. Our group has reported systematic work in the field.15 In this contribution, we chose H3BTB as the major ligand and bpy as the assistant ligand. Two novel CPs, {[Cd(BTG)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5glycol·H2O}n (1), and {[Cd6(BTB)4(bpy)4.5(glycol)]·9H2O}n (2) were obtained via tuning of the reaction conditions. CP 1 possesses a stair-shaped 2D (4,4) network which was obtained under reflux and evaporation of the filtrate for two months. The BTG2– ligand in CP 1 was generated in situ via the esterification reaction of 1,2,3-benzenetricarboxylic acid and glycol. CP 2 was synthesized under solvothermal conditions. The complicated 3D structure of CP 2 can be simplified into an unreported 3,10-connected binodal net with a Schläfli symbol of {32·4}{34·421·513·67}.

Experimental section

General methods and materials

All the reagents and solvents were commercially available and used without further purification. Elemental analyses for C, H, and N were carried out on a Perkin-Elmer analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction measurements were recorded on a D/Max-2500 X-ray diffractometer using Cu–Kα radiation. FT-IR spectra were measured with a Bruker Tensor 27 spectrometer on KBr disks. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a Delta Series TA-SDTQ600 analyzer in a nitrogen atmosphere from room temperature to 700 °C (10 °C min−1) using aluminum crucibles. The fluorescent spectra were measured on a Varian Cary Eclipse Fluorescence spectrophotometer.
Preparation of {[Cd(BTG)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5glycol·H2O}n (1). A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.102 g, 0.33 mmol), H3BTB (0.062 g, 0.25 mmol), bpy (0.039 g, 0.25 mmol), LiOH·H2O (0.021 g, 0.50 mmol), 8 mL of water and 8 mL of glycol was refluxed for 2 h. After filtration, colorless crystals were obtained by slow evaporation of the filtrate for two months. Element analysis (%) found (calcd.) for 1: C 45.01 (44.95), H 3.96 (3.94), N 4.71 (4.76); Yield: 34%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3428 br, 1600 br, 1450 m, 1379 m, 1203 w, 1068 s, 814 vs, 678 m, 530 w.
Preparation of {[Cd6(BTB)4(bpy)4.5(glycol)]·9H2O}n (2). A mixture of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.102 g, 0.33 mmol), H3BTB (0.062 g, 0.25 mmol), bpy (0.039 g, 0.25 mmol), LiOH·H2O (0.021 g, 0.50 mmol), 3 mL of water and 3 mL of glycol was put into a 25 mL Teflon-lined Parr, heated to 160 °C for 3 days and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C h−1. Colorless crystals were obtained. Elemental analysis (%) found (calcd.) for 2: C 41.11 (41.04), H 3.02 (2.95), N 5.08 (5.19); Yield: 44%. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3396 br, 1560 vs, 1460 s, 1380 vs, 1222 m, 1066 m, 941 w, 813 m, 772 m, 718 m, 631 m.

X-ray crystallography

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements of CPs 1 and 2 were recorded on an Oxford Supernova diffractometer with graphite monochromatic Mo–Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structures were solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares techniques on F2 using the SHELXS-97 and SHELXS-97 programs.16 Anisotropic thermal parameters were assigned to all non-hydrogen atoms. The hydrogen atoms were placed in idealized positions or located in the difference Fourier map. The disordered water molecules in 2 were subjected to geometric restraints during the refinements. The formulas were identified by combining single-crystal structure, element analysis and thermogravimetric analysis. Results and discussion

Synthesis of CPs 1 and 2

A reflux reaction of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with H3BTB and bpy in 8 mL of water and 8 mL of glycol gave CP 1, in which the BTG2− ligand was generated in situ as the ester of BTB3− and glycol. CP 1 shows a two-dimensional stair-like network. Meanwhile, a solvothermal reaction of Cd(NO3)2·4H2O with H3BTB and bpy in 3 mL of water and 3 mL of glycol at 160 °C afforded CP 2, exhibting a three-dimensional structure (Scheme 1). The difference between 1 and 2 is due to the different bridging modes of the ligand and coordination environment of the Cd(II) ions, which will be discussed below.
Scheme 1 The synthetic route of CPs 1 and 2.

Crystal structures of {[Cd(BTG)(bpy)(H2O)]·0.5glycol·H2O}n (1)

X-ray crystallography determination reveals that CP 1 crystallized in the monoclinic space group P21/c. The asymmetric unit of 1 comprises one Cd(II) ion, one bpy ligand, one BTG2− ion, one coordinated water molecule, one free water molecule and half a glycol molecule. The Cd(II) ion is seven-coordinated with two nitrogen atoms from two bpy ligands, four carboxylic oxygen atoms from two μ1-η2:η0 carboxylates of two BTG2− and one water molecule, forming a distorted pentagonal bipyramid coordination geometry. The Cd–O and Cd–N bonds are in the range of 2.292(6)–2.521(6) and 2.319(6)–2.397(6) Å, respectively. There is only one type of BTG2− ligand, which adopts a type A coordination mode (Scheme 2). Two adjacent Cd(II) ions are connected by a BTG2− ligand to afford a 1D chain along the crystallographic c direction (Fig. 1b). The chains are further interlinked by the bpy ligand to form stair-shaped 2D (4,4) networks that stack in a parallel fashion with a dihedral angle of 80.599(5)°. For each BTG2− ligand, one carboxyl oxygen atom (O5) does not participate in the coordination. It forms O–H⋯OC hydrogen bonds with the glycol molecules, coordinated and uncoordinated water molecules. All of these hydrogen bonding interactions give the 3D supramolecular network (Fig. S1, ESI†) and the details of the hydrogen bonding are given in Table S1 in the ESI.† In our previous work, a Mn(II) coordination polymer {Mn3(tda)2(H2O)6}·6H2O]n (H3tda = 1H-1,2,3-triazole-4,5-dicarboxylic acid) also posseses a 2D stair-like network with a dihedral angle of exactly 90°.17 To the best of our knowledge, this particular structure is rather rare among coordination polymers.18

Crystal structures of {[Cd6(BTB)4(bpy)4.5(glycol)]·9H2O}n (2)

CP 2 crystallizes in the triclinic space group P, possessing a 3D structure. The asymmetric unit contains six crystallographically unique Cd(II) ions, four BTB3− ligands, four and a half bpy ligands, one glycol molecule and nine free water molecules. As shown in Fig. 2a and b, Cd1 is seven-coordinated to form a distorted geometry, completed by six carboxylic oxygen atoms (O5, O6, O7, O8, O21A and O22A) from three different μ1-η2:η0 carboxylates of three independent BTB3− ligands and one nitrogen atom (N1) from one bpy ligand. All the other cadmium atoms (Cd2, Cd3, Cd4, Cd5 and Cd6) possess similar coordination geometry and are six-coordinated to form a distorted octahedral geometry. Cd2 coordinates with four carboxylic oxygen atoms (O3A, O5A, O18 and O23A) from three μ2-η1:η1 carboxylates and one μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate of three BTB3− ligands and two nitrogen atoms (N6A and N9) from two bpy ligands. The coordination sphere of Cd3 is analogous to Cd2, defined by four carboxylic oxygen atoms (O10, O11, O13 and O20) from three BTB3− ligands and two nitrogen atoms (N4 and N5) from two bpy ligands. Cd4 is also analogous to Cd2 and coordinates with four carboxylic oxygen atoms (O17, O1G, O4A and O24A) from three independent BTB3− ligands and two nitrogen atoms (N7 and N2A) from two bpy ligands. However, different from Cd2, the octahedral geometry of Cd5 is finished by five carboxylic oxygen atoms (O11, O12, O15, O16 and O2A) from three different μ1-η2:η0, μ2-η2:η1 and μ2-η1:η1 carboxylates of three independent BTB3− ligands and one nitrogen atom (N3A) from one bpy ligand. Cd6 coordinates with one oxygen atom (O27) from one glycol molecule, four carboxylic oxygen atoms (O14, O19, O8A and O9A) from three μ2-η1:η1 carboxylates and one μ2-η2:η1 carboxylate of three BTB3− ligands and one nitrogen atom (N8A) from one bpy ligand. The Cd–O and Cd–N bonds are in the range of 2.182(8)–2.611(7) and 2.268(8)–2.335(8) Å, respectively. There are three different types of BTB3− ligands (Fig. 2b) in 2, which adopt type B, C and D coordination modes (Scheme 2).

(b) The coordination geometries of the CdII ions and the coordination environment of the BTB3− ligands in 2. (c) The hexa-nuclear SBU. As shown in Fig. 3a, the hexa-nuclear subunits were linked together by BTB3− ligands, forming a 1D chain along the crystallographic c direction. The 1D chains are connected via two kinds of BTB3− ligands with coordination modes C and D (Scheme 2) to form 2D nets (Fig. 3b), which are further interlinked by bpy ligands to generate a 3D structure (Fig. 3c). The 3D framework shows 1D channels along the [100] direction with dimensions of ca. 7.3 Å × 8.1 Å, occupied by water molecules. PLATON analysis reveals that the framework of 2 is composed of voids of 1197.0 Å3 which represent 25.7% per unit cell volume with the removal of water molecules.


Fig. 3 (a) The 1D chain of 2 constructed by SBUs through BTB3− ligands along the c axis; (b) the 2D net linked with Cd(II) ions and BTB3− ligands along the c axis in 2; (c) representations of the porous 3D structure along the a axis in 2.

From above structure analysis, it is obvious that each hexa-nuclear SBU is connected with nine bpy ligands and seven BTB3− (Fig. 2b). Therefore, the SBU can be viewed as a 10-connected node with all the bpy and BTB3− ligands of coordination modes B and C (Scheme 2) simplified as linkers, while each BTB3− ligand of coordination mode D (Scheme 2) serves as a 3-connected node linked with three SBUs. As shown in Fig. 4, each hexa-nuclear SBU center was connected with the other seven SBUs and three 3-connected BTB3− nodes. Such a structure can be characterized by a point (Schläfli) symbol of {32·4}{34·421·513·67}, which is a 3D dinodal (3,10)-net. This 3,10-connected dinodal net has not been discovered as far as we know. Compared with other (3,10)-networks,19 this novel topology may be ascribed to the complicated coordination modes of the neighboring tricarboxylic ligands with the hexa-nuclear SBUs.


Fig. 4 Schematic representation of the (3,10)-connected {32·4}{34·421·513·67} framework of 2 (green: 10-connected SBU nodes; purple: 3-connected BTB ligands).

PXRD patterns and thermal stability analysis

PXRD experiments have been carried out on 1 and 2 in order to establish their crystalline phase purity. The PXRD patterns of bulk samples of 1 and 2 are consistent with the simulated patterns in terms of the single crystal data, suggesting the presence of mainly one crystalline phase in the corresponding samples of 1 and 2 (Fig. S2–S3, ESI†).

The thermal stabilities of CPs 1 and 2 were studied by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) in a N2 atmosphere from 25 to 700 °C at a rate of 10 °C min−1 (Fig. S4, ESI†). The curve of CP 1 exhibits a continuous weight loss of 9.7% up to 150 °C, in agreement with the calculated value (8.3%) of the lattice water and glycol molecules. No obvious thermal stable stage is observed and the compound decomposes subsequently. CP 2 displays two main steps of weight loss. Firstly, a weight loss of 9.1% from 50 to 120 °C was observed and could correspond to the loss of the free water molecules and coordinated glycol (theoretical 9.2%). The second step of weight loss occurs at ca. 300 °C due to the decomposition of the organic ligands.

Luminescent properties

Luminescent CPs are of great interest because of their various applications in chemical sensors, photochemistry and electroluminescent displays.20 Owing to the excellent luminescent properties of compounds with d10 metal centers and organic ligands,21 the photoluminescence of CPs 1 and 2 in the solid state have been investigated at room temperature. CP 1 shows one emission broad band at 475 nm with excitation at 347 nm; CP 2 displays one emission band at 396 nm (λex = 300 nm) (Fig. 5).
Fig. 5 The solid-state luminescent spectra of 1 and 2.

The photoluminescent spectra of H3BTB and bpy were measured (Fig. S5, ESI†). Emission peaks at 330 nm (λex = 280 nm) for bpy and 437 nm (λex = 340 nm) for the H3BTB ligand were monitored, respectively.

These emissions of CPs 1 and 2 are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal transfer (LMCT) in nature, since the CdII ions are difficult to oxidize or reduce due to their d10 configuration.22 Thus, they may be assigned to intraligand (n–π* or π–π*) emission. In comparison to that of the free ligand, the emission maxima of 1 and 2 are significantly shifted. Thus, the shifts of the emission bands may be attributed to both the deprotonated effect of H3BTB and the coordination interactions of the organic ligands to the Cd(II) ions.

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized two new Cd(II) CPs with mixed ligands in the presence of the same reactants and solvents under different reaction conditions. Low temperature reaction of the reactants gave an interesting stair-shaped 2D (4, 4) network. Increasing the reaction temperature resulted in an unusual 3,10-connected binodal net with a Schläfli symbol of {32·4}{34·421·513·67}. The different structural features of CPs 1 and 2 are ascribed to the coordination modes of the neighboring tricarboxylic and bpy ligands with the Cd(II) center. The result reveals that reaction conditions play an important role in the crystallization process and further influence both the final structures and the properties.
