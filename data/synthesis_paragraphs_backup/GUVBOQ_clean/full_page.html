Experimental and theoretical investigation of a stable zinc-based metal–organic framework for CO2 removal from syngas†

Ruiqin Zhong *a, Jia Liu b, Xing Huang a, Xiaofeng Yu a, Changyu Sun a, Guangjin Chen a and Ruqiang Zou *b
aState Key Laboratory of Heavy Oil Processing, China University of Petroleum, Beijing 102249, China. E-mail: rzhong@cup.edu.cn
bBeijing Key Laboratory for Theory and Technology of Advanced Battery Materials, College of Engineering, Peking University, Beijing 100871, China. E-mail: rzou@pku.edu.cn

Received 6th July 2015 , Accepted 3rd September 2015

First published on 4th September 2015

A stable Zn-based metal–organic framework has been synthesized and applied for CO2 removal from syngas composed of CO and H2 for the first time. Further study using the combination of Monte Carlo simulation and ideal adsorbed solution theory method demonstrates the CO2 adsorption behavior and separation performance which have been further confirmed by breakthrough experiments.

In recent years, CO2 capture technologies have become more and more significant and have been continually developed to reduce the amount of greenhouse gas that contributes to global climate change. Besides electricity generation and combustion of fossil fuels in transportation, industry is one of the main sources of CO2 emission, as reported by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.1 Syngas, a fuel gas mixture consisting primarily of H2 and CO, is one of the most important reagents utilized in refineries, chemical processes and power generation.2 The removal of CO2 from syngas is not only essential in the fight against climate change,3 but also greatly beneficial to the promotion of energy efficiency and conservation in many chemical reactions.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) as a relatively new family of crystalline and porous materials are constructed by metal–ligand coordination bonds and/or weak intermolecular interactions.4 Thanks to their intriguing properties, such as ultra-high surface area, well-ordered pore structures and modifiable chemical functionality, MOFs have shown improved performance for adsorption-based CO2 separation,5 especially for binary mixtures of CO2/H2,6 CH4/CO2,7 and CH4/H2,8 in comparison with many conventional industrial adsorbents like zeolites and active carbons.9 Mg2(dobdc), known as MOF-74, exhibits the highest CO2 uptake of 5.3 mmol g−1 (18.9 wt%) at 0.15 bar and 313 K compared with any solid adsorbent under these conditions.10 The high potential application of a MOF for syngas purification has been proposed by Jiang's group who reported that a charged soc-MOF for syngas purification could reach a selectivity of 600 at 298 K and 300 kPa using atomistic simulations.11 It should be noted that the syngas was designated as H2, and the syngas purification only referred to the separation of H2 from a CO2/H2 mixture.

However, the real application of MOFs in industry also lies in their structural stability in the presence of water vapour or moist air which is almost unavoidable and cannot be neglected in industrial environments.12 For example, IRMOFs,13 one of the archetypical families of MOFs featuring oxide-centered Zn4O tetrahedral clusters and widely studied as candidates for gas separation and catalysis, will experience framework collapse at a very low water content of 6 wt% at room temperature.14 It is also a great pity for Mg-MOF-74 that a significant decrease in CO2 adsorption was observed after exposure to water.15 Therefore, more efforts for a better understanding of the critical contributors to water sensitivity and more investigations on the new generation of MOFs with both target function and good water stability are significantly needed for elevating MOFs to the applied level.

Herein, we report a novel microporous Zn-based MOF, [Zn9(OH)2L6](H3O)2(H2O)6 (1), where H3L = 1,3,5-tri(3-hydroxyl-4-carboxyl)phenylbenzene, synthesized by the reaction of H3L with zinc nitrate and triethylene diamine in water under hydrothermal reaction conditions.‡As a matter of fact, the material synthesized in water could be expected to possess good water stability high potential application in humid industrial environments, which has also been verified in this work. In order to investigate its performance for CO2 removal from syngas which is primarily composed of CO and H2 in most cases, a combination of Monte Carlo (MC) simulation and Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST) method, and breakthrough experiments were applied to study the selectivity of CO2 from a syngas/CO2 mixture. To our knowledge, this is the first report about CO2 capture for binary syngas purification using a MOF.

X-Ray crystallographic analysis indicates that 1 crystallizes in the cubic space group P4132, consisting of an anionic [Zn9(OH)2L6]2− framework and protonated water. The host framework is composed of two crystallographically independent Zn(II) coordinated to the L ligand and hydroxyl ion. The Zn(1) atom adopts a tetrahedral coordination mode with one hydroxyl ion and three carboxyl oxygen from a separate L ligand, in which the bond length of Zn–O(OH) (2.024(3) Å) is longer than that of Zn–O(COO) (1.940–1.964 Å), indicating much stronger coordination interactions of the Zn(II) ion to the carboxyl group in this tetrahedron. The Zn(2) atom is located in a crystallographic 2-fold axis along the c direction with a half occupancy in an asymmetric unit, and coordinates to six carboxyl oxygen atoms in a typical octahedral configuration. The Zn–O(COO) bonds fall into the range of 2.047 to 2.094 Å, which show slightly weaker coordination bonding interactions than those in the tetrahedral configuration. The two crystallographically independent zinc atoms self-assemble into a linear trinuclear Zn3 molecular building block (MBB) with a Zn–Zn separation of 3.562(2) Å. This MBB is bound together by six carboxyl groups in a μ2 fashion from separate L ligands (Fig. 1a), which is a typical MBB in many Zn-based MOFs.16 The L ligand adopts a uniform μ2 coordination mode to bridge six Zn(II) ions, and the hydroxyl groups do not participate in the coordination. Interestingly, three adjacent Zn3 MBBs are further symmetrically linked together by μ3 hydroxyl (–OH) ions, resulting in a three dimensional [Zn9(COO)18(OH)2]2− anion network (Fig. 1d and e), in which the hydroxyl oxygen atom is located in a crystallographic 3-fold axis along the b direction. As shown in Fig. 1e, 1 assembles into a porous trigonal bipyramid-like polyhedron formed by six intertwined L ligands linked by metal clusters with a pore diameter of around 1 nm, in which the pores are inconsecutive and separated by ligands (Fig. S5, ESI†). These trigonal bipyramids are interweaved together to form a 3D network (Fig. 1f).§

To better understand such a complicated structure, the topology of the anion framework of 1 was studied by using Topos 4.0 software.17 The network topology is based on 8-connected Zn3 MBBs and two types of 3-connected nodes from the hydroxyl ion and L ligand. The structure has a (3,8)-connected (3,8-c) net with the stoichiometry (3-c)8(8-c)3 (Fig. 2). Therefore, 1 self-assembles into a 3D net with the point symbol {43·69·84·1012}3{43}6{63}2. To the best of our knowledge, this is a new (3,8)-connected topology based on the search from the RCSR database (http://rcsr.net/nets).


Fig. 2 View of (a) Zn3 MBBs as 8-connected node, hydroxyl (–OH) as 3-connected node, and L ligand as 3-connected node, and (b) 3D topological net of 1.

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on crystalline 1 and the TGA curve shows a significant decline before 100 °C, indicating the loss of water molecules that occupy the vacancies in the lattice (Fig. S7, ESI†). After a subsequent platform up to 270 °C, the framework began to collapse according to the sharp decline in the TGA curve.

Furthermore, the framework stability of 1 was studied and characterized by powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD). As shown in the PXRD patterns (Fig. S8, ESI†), the peak position of 1 before and after being activated at 100 °C for 5 h is in good agreement with that of the simulated pattern, indicating that 1 retains its framework integrity after the removal of the guest molecules. When the activated sample was soaked in water for 7 days, and even distilled in boiling water for 12 hours, the original crystalline phase of 1 still remained, indicating that 1 exhibits both thermodynamic and kinetic stability in the presence of water.12,18 One reason for its good water stability is probably the six-coordinated zinc atom in the center of the trinuclear MBB that is relatively robust and renders it unfavourable for an irreversible hydrolysis reaction to occur. Moreover, it is worthwhile to note that the coordination mode of the four-coordinated zinc atom is usually unstable and is easy to break down through the competitive coordination of water. However, in the four-coordinated zinc unit of 1, besides the coordination to three oxygen atoms from –COO groups in a μ2 mode, there is a μ3 hydroxyl (–OH) anion coordinated to the zinc atom through a strong coordination bond which is more competitive than the oxygen atom of water. It is also worth noting that the pKa of the coordination atom on the ligand can be used as a first-order approximation of the metal–ligand bond strength, based on the Lewis acid–base coordination chemistry in MOFs. It was first noted by Long et al. and used as a strategy in the synthesis of a series of highly stable Co-, Zn-, Ni-, and Cu-based MOFs containing a pyrazolate ligand (pKa 19.8).19 Meanwhile in 1, the pKa of the coordinated oxygen atom of the –COO group on the ligand is only 2.02, less than that of the oxygen atom of the –COO group on the ligand of MOF-177 (3.46), according to pKa calculation using Advanced Chemistry Development, Inc. (ACD/Labs) software V6.0.20a Therefore, the water stability of 1 may be mainly due to the stable coordination modes of zinc atoms in the MBB.

The solvent-accessible volume in 1 is 5122.7 Å3 which constitutes as high as 29.1% volume per unit cell calculated by the PLATON routine,20b indicating its porous nature. The permanent porosity of 1 was confirmed by N2 adsorption measurement with the largest uptake amount of 156.0 cm3 g−1. The N2 sorption isotherm of activated 1 exhibits type-I sorption behaviour (Fig. S9†) according to the IUPAC classification.21 The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 1 is 439.2 m2 g−1. Furthermore, the adsorption behaviour of 1 for pure CO2, CO and H2 was investigated by both MC simulation and gas sorption measurement. The CO2 adsorption field was calculated by the Monte Carlo method and the details have been described in the ESI.†22 For clarification, only Zn–O clusters are shown in Fig. 3a. CO2 molecules were distributed regularly in three regions. First, CO2 molecules are found around the O–H(ZnO) group and H3O+ in which H atoms present a positive charge and induce the quadrupole moment of CO2. The strong interaction between positive H and CO2 results in a high uptake of CO2. Second, CO2 molecules are found around O–H groups in the L ligand which have the same effect on CO2. The electron pair of sp2 hybridized O(O–H) have a special effect on CO2. Third, CO2 molecules are found around the space surrounded by the ligands which produce a strong potential trap for CO2 molecules. Compared with CO2, CO is distributed mainly in the space surrounded by the ligands (Fig. S10†), demonstrating a different adsorption mechanism. CO with a poor quadrupole moment does not have a special effect on the charged H atom. Moreover, the polarizability of CO is less than that of CO2 because of the strong C–O sigma bond and feedback π bond. Both reasons result in less uptake of CO than that of CO2. Due to the weak intermolecular force, H2 scatters in the pore of 1 without an obvious feature. The adsorption amount of CO2 reaches 51.0 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and 1 atm, which is much higher than those of CO (3.8 cm3 g−1) and H2 (0.5 cm3 g−1) under the same conditions. It should be noted that the simulated uptakes of CO2 and CO by the MC method in 1 are slightly higher than the experimental results, which are probably attributed to either some loss of active sites of 1 like protonated water molecules during activation or crystal defects (Fig. 3b). The isosteric heat of CO2 calculated by MC simulation is ca. 27 kJ mol−1, which is in agreement with the experimental results (Fig. S11 and S12†). The –OH group and protonated water contribute to have a better affinity for CO2 molecules, compared with CO and H2 molecules, considering that the polarizability (2.63 × 10−40 J−1 C2 m2) and the quadrupole moment (−14.3 × 10−40 C m2) of CO2 are much higher than those of CO (1.98 × 10−40 J−1 C2 m2, −6.7 × 10−40 C m2) and H2 (0.819 × 10−40 J−1 C2 m2, 2.12 × 10−40 C m2).23


Fig. 3 (a) CO2 density distribution at 278 K under 1 atm; (b) comparison of experimental (symbols) and simulation (lines) sorption isotherms for CO2 (black), CO (red) and H2 (blue) at 273 K.

The significant difference of uptake amounts makes 1 promising for application in CO2 capture from syngas. The adsorption selectivities of CO2 over CO at 273 K were calculated by MC simulation and the IAST method. The details have been described in the ESI.† The volumetric ratio of the gas mixtures for calculation is CO2:CO:H2 = 1:1:1. The selectivities of CO2/CO and CO2/H2 in 1 calculated by the IAST method are 27.5 and 858.3 under 1 atm and 273 K, which fit well with the calculated results of 36.1 and 865.8 by MC simulation, respectively.

To better evaluate the potential of 1 in the separation of CO2 from a syngas/CO2 mixture in dynamic conditions, vapour-phase breakthrough experiments were carried out for a ternary gas mixture composed of 35.04 v% H2, 19.96 v% CO2, and 10.02 v% CO balanced with helium. All of the breakthrough curves were recorded by a home-made instrument illustrated in Fig. S4 (ESI†). Fig. 4 shows the breakthrough curves obtained at 273 K and at a total mixture gas pressure of 0.1 MPa. The experimental details can be seen in the ESI.† A clear difference in breakthrough time between CO2 and syngas was observed. After an initial period during which all three components were fully absorbed, H2 was first eluted, followed by CO. The concentration of syngas at the outlet exceeds the inlet concentration because the absorbed CO2 molecules displace some of the adsorbed syngas molecules. Finally CO2 started to be eluted and the concentration of all components gradually reached the feed concentration level, indicating that the adsorption bed was saturated. As expected, for the CO2/syngas mixture, CO2 is the more adsorbed component when it is present. CO2 might have the strongest interaction with the electric field of the framework of 1 due to its high polarizability and great quadrupole moment. The following order of the adsorption capability of 1 can be derived from Fig. 4: CO2 > CO > H2, which is in fair agreement with the order of their pure component equilibrium. The same phenomenon was also observed in the breakthrough curves of the gas mixture with the same volumetric ratio at 1 MPa (Fig. S13, ESI†).


Fig. 4 Breakthrough curves of 1 at 273 K and at a total mixture gas pressure of 0.1 MPa.

The separation efficiency for the ternary mixture can be simplified and compared in terms of sorption selectivity, S, defined as S = (ni/nj)/(Ci/Cj), where component i is the more adsorbed, and n and C represent the amount adsorbed and the concentration of components, respectively. The calculated SCO2/H2 is 49.2 and SCO2/CO is 5.1, indicating that 1 exhibits a higher selectivity for CO2, which makes it a promising candidate for CO2/syngas separation.

Conclusions

In summary, we have succeeded in preparing a water-stable Zn-based metal–organic framework with a (3,8)-connected topology and applied it for CO2 removal from binary syngas for the first time. The water stability of 1 may be mainly due to the stable coordination modes of zinc atoms in the MBB. The real gas separation for CO2 from syngas carried out by the breakthrough experiments has verified the potential application for syngas purification which has been primarily suggested by simulation and IAST prediction. Moreover, the high water stability of the material may pave the way for practical usage of syngas purification. Future research will focus on the design and synthesis of water-stable frameworks with higher surface areas and greater capacities for CO2 storage.

‡ Synthesis and activation of 1: Zn(NO3)·6H2O (0.045 g, 0.15 mmol), H3L (0.024 g, 0.05 mmol), triethylene diamine (0.033 g, 0.15 mmol) and 15 mL of H2O were added into a 20 mL scintillation vial. The mixture was sonicated at room temperature until clear and then transferred to a 25 mL Teflon-lined steel autoclave. The autoclave was sealed, gradually heated to 140 °C within 12 h and then kept at this temperature for 72 h. After slowly cooling down to room temperature within 48 h, colorless column crystals were obtained. The crystals were filtered out and washed with distilled water 3 times. The solvent within the pores of the resulting solid was removed under dynamic vacuum at 100 °C for 5 h. Yield: 31% (based on H3L). IR (cm–1): 3423br, 1628m, 1581s, 1508m, 1414s, 1363m, 1257m, 1196m, 1163w, 958w, 831w, 785w. w = weak, s = strong, m = medium, br = broad. Anal. calcd. for C162H110O64Zn9: C, 53.03; H, 3.02%. § The crystal data were collected on a Bruker APEXII CCD Detector single-crystal X-ray diffractometer at room temperature with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The structures were solved by direct methods using the program SHELXS of the SHELXTL package and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods with SHELXL.24 The Zn atoms in each complex were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were located in successive difference Fourier syntheses, which were refined with anisotropic thermal parameters on F2. The hydrogen atoms of the ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms and refined isotropically with fixed thermal factors. The solvent molecules in the structure were randomly dispersed, and thus their positions were impossible to refine using conventional discrete-atom models.
