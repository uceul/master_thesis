Abstract

The MnII‐based porous metal‐organic framework, [Mn3(btca)2(HCOO)(μ3‐OH)(H2O)2]·2DMF (1) (H2btca = benzotriazole‐5‐carboxylate acid), was prepared by the solvothermal reaction of Mn(CH3COO)2·4H2O and H2btca, which was characterized by infrared spectroscopy, thermogravimetric analyses, and X‐ray crystallographic study. 1 exhibits 3D framework with 1D rectangle channels constructed by the strip‐shaped chains containing [Mn5(μ3‐OH)2(btca)4]– pentaclusters subunits. Furthermore, the magnetic measures show that 1 exhibits weak ferromagnetic behavior at low temperature.

Introduction

Over the past decade, metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) have emerged as one of the most promising classes of microporous materials, particularly in view of their potential applications.1-8 The design and construction of functional MOFs with additional physical properties, such as magnetism, is a challenge because it is difficult to obtain simultaneously larger pore sizes and relatively strong magnetic interactions.9-13 Magnetic exchange requires relatively short exchange pathways between two adjacent central metal atoms. However, the MOFs usually depend on the use of relatively long ligands, which increases the distance of adjacent central metal atoms to some extent. These magnetic MOFs have usually been focused on two synthetic strategies: (1) using magnetic clusters as central metal atoms connected by organic linkers to form a porous framework;14-16 (2) using an inorganic anion ligand, such as N3– and SCN–, to link the adjacent central metal atoms to build porous skeleton.17, 18 In addition, some hydroxide‐bridged porous coordination polymers also show interesting magnetic properties.19, 20

Up to now, multidentate N‐donor or O‐donor ligands, especially imidazolate, triazolate, polycarboxylates, and their derivatives have attracted much more attention for constructing a series of MOFs.21-30 Much efforts have also been focused on syntheses of novel functional materials based on the aromatic organic ligands containing carboxylic and nitrogen donor groups, such as 2‐(pyridin‐4‐yl)‐1H‐imidazole‐4,5‐dicarboxylic acid (H3PIDC),31, 32 4,5‐imidazoledicarboxylic acid (H3imdc),33, 34 1,2,3‐triazole‐4,5‐dicarboxylic acid (H3tzdc),35, 36 and benzimidazole‐5,6‐dicarboxylic acid (Hbidc).37, 38 These multifunctional ligands possess versatile coordination modes and easily coordinate with metal ions to form multidimensional complexes. Furthermore, the special orientation and cooperative coordination between N‐heterocyclic and carboxyl groups may obtain intriguing structures and properties. Nevertheless, 3D homometallic systems containing metal hydroxide strip‐shaped chain remain poorly reported. Benzotriazole‐5‐carboxylate acid contains 1,2,3‐benzenetriazole and carboxyl group. It has been proved that 1,2,3‐benzenetriazole is an ideal building block for forming metal clusters and carboxyl in favor of articulation of the clusters into an extended three‐dimensional framework.39, 40 In this article, we report a porous homometallic MOF, namely [Mn3(btca)2(HCOO)(μ3‐OH)(H2O)2]·2DMF (1) (H2btca = benzotriazole‐5‐carboxylate acid), with 1D porous channels constructed by {Mn–O–Mn}n chains and btac2– linkers. In addition, the stabilities and magnetic properties of this compound were also characterized.

Experimental Section

Materials and Methods: All reagents and solvents for synthesis were commercially available and used directly without further purifications. Infrared spectra were recorded with a Perkin‐Elmer FT‐IR spectrophotometer in the 4000–400 cm–1 ranges as KBr pellets. The elemental analyses of C, H, and N were measured with an Elementar Vario EL III microanalyzer. Power X‐ray diffraction patterns were taken with a Rigaku MiniFlex‐II X‐ray diffractometer with a Cu‐Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). Thermal stability studies were performed with a NETSCHZ STA‐449C thermoanalyzer with a heating rate of 10 K·min–1 in a nitrogen atmosphere in the range of 30–800 °C. Magnetic susceptibility data was collected over the temperature range of 2–300 K in magnetic field of 1000 Oe with a MPMS (SQUID)‐XL magnetometer. The gas sorption studies were performed with a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 adsorption equipment.

Synthesis of [Mn3(btca)2(HCOO)(μ3‐OH)(H2O)2]·2DMF (1): A mixture of Mn(Ac)2·4H2O (0.20 mmol, 49.0 mg), H2btac (0.1 mmol, 16.3 mg), N,N′‐dimethylformamide (4 mL), and acetonitrile (1 mL) was stirred for 10 min in air, then transferred and sealed in a 23‐mL Teflon‐lined autoclave, which was heated in an oven to 120 °C for 72 h. The reaction mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 K·h–1, and the light yellow crystals were obtained about 28 % yield (10.2 mg based on H2btac). C21H26Mn3N8O11 (731.3): calcd. C 34.46; H 3.56; N 15.31 %; found: C, 34.37; H, 3.53; N, 15.27 %. IR (KBr): ν̃ = 3377 (s), 3262 (s), 3071 (w), 2945 (w), 2867 (w), 1659 (s), 1585 (s), 1550 (s), 1403 (s), 1290 (s), 1169 (m), 1097 (m), 899 (m), 794 (s), 758 (m), 691 (m) cm–1.

Crystal Structure Determination: Suitable single crystal of 1 was mounted on glass fibers for the X‐ray measurement. Diffraction data were collected with a Rigaku‐AFC7 equipped with a Rigaku Saturn CCD area‐detector system. The measurement was made by using graphite monochromatic Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). The frame data was integrated, and absorption correction was calculated using the Rigaku CrystalClear program package. All calculations were performed with the SHELXTL‐97 program package,41 and the structure was solved by direct methods and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares fitting on F2. All non‐hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and the hydrogen atoms of the organic ligands were generated theoretically onto the specific atoms. Crystallographic data and other pertinent information for 1 are summarized in Table 1. Selected bond lengths are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).

Results and Discussion

Solvothermal reaction of Mn(CH3COO)2 and H2btca in DMF and MeCN mixed solvent at 120 °C for 3 d afforded light yellow block crystals of 1. The structure of 1 was characterized by single‐crystal X‐ray diffraction, elemental analysis, thermogravimetric analysis, and IR spectra. The purity of the crystalline materials generated was investigated by X‐ray diffraction on powder samples. The latter study reveals that almost only one phase was observed for 1 because of a rather good fit between its simulated and observed patterns.

Single crystal X‐ray diffraction study reveals that 1 crystallized in the monoclinic P21/c space group, exhibits an interesting 3D porous metal‐organic framework constructed by the strip‐shaped chains containing [Mn5(μ3‐OH)2(btca)4]– pentaclusters subunit and btac2– bridges. In the asymmetric unit of 1, there are two independent and two half independent MnII ions, two btac2– ligands, one μ3‐OH group, two coordinated water molecules, two free DMF molecules, and one coordinated formate anion from the decomposition of DMF at 120 °C (Scheme S1, Supporting Information).42

As shown in Figure 1, the Mn(1) atom is six‐coordinate by two nitrogen atoms from two different btac2– ligands and four oxygen atoms from two different btac2– ligands and two individual μ3‐OH groups to form a distorted octahedral arrangement. The Mn(2) atom also six‐coordinate and surrounded by two nitrogen atoms from two different btac2– ligands and four oxygen atoms from one btac2– ligand, two water molecules and one μ3‐OH group. Unlike Mn(1) and Mn(2), Mn(3) locates at the special position and adopts the trigonal bipyramidal coordination arrangement with two nitrogen atoms from two different btac2– ligands, three oxygen atoms from one btac2– ligand, one formate anion, and one μ3‐OH group. Mn(4) atom locates in a distorted octahedral coordination arrangement and is ligated by two nitrogen atoms from two individual btac2– ligands and four oxygen atoms from two different btac2– ligands, and two formate anions (Figure 1). All Mn–O and Mn–N bond lengths fall in the normal range of 2.097–2.325 Å.43 The btac2– ligand has only one type bridging mode: adopt μ5 bridging mode through three btac2– nitrogen atoms and μ2,η2‐carboxylate group (Scheme 1).

The prominent structural feature of 1 is the presence of extended {Mn–O–Mn}n chain in the framework (Figure 2a). In {Mn–O–Mn}n chain, Mn(1), Mn(2), and Mn(3) are linked each other through three –N–N– bridges and a μ3‐OH bridge, forming a triangular clusters [Mn3(μ3‐OH)]4+. Two triangular clusters are linked together by sharing Mn(1) atom to form [Mn5(μ3‐OH)2(btca)4]– pentacluster subunits, which are further connected through Mn(4) atoms to give a strip‐shaped chain along the a axis direction with the Mn···Mn distances ranging from 3.529 to 3.606 Å. Furthermore, the extended strip‐shaped chains are connected by the btca2– oxygen atoms to generate a 3D metal‐organic framework (Figure 2c). To the best of our knowledge, such framework based on metal strip‐shaped chain is extremely scarce.44 The solvent‐accessible volume of the unit cell is estimated by PLATON program45 to be 1130.6 Å3, which accommodates lattice solvent molecules, and is approximately 38.7 % of the unit‐cell volume (2919.9 Å3). The 1D pore exhibits a rectangle channel with a size of 7.2 × 3.7 Å (defined by the side length of rectangle, Figure 2b). The permanent porosity of 1 was established by N2 adsorption at 77 K. The freshly prepared samples of 1 were first activated through solvent exchange with methanol 10 times in 2 d, followed by the thermal/vacuum activation at 353 K to generate activated materials. As shown in Figure S8 (Supporting Information, the N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K shows that 1 displays typical type I sorption behavior, with BET surface areas of 346.8 m2·g–1.

The thermal stability of 1 was investigated by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Figure S2, Supporting Information) and powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern. The TGA curve of 1 shows an initial weight loss of 19.17 % from 30 to 200 °C corresponding to the removal of two DMF molecules (calcd. 19.98 %), and then the framework loses weight from 200 to 350 °C (obsd. 10.86 %, calcd. 10.94 %) revealing evacuation of two coordinated water molecules and one coordinated formate anion per formula unit. Finally, the framework collapses rapidly, with the decomposition of the btca2– ligand upon heating subsequently. The PXRD analysis shows that 1 retains its crystallinity after being heated up to 150 and 250 °C under vacuum, respectively, because the PXRD patterns are similar to the simulated pattern of 1 (Figure 3), which suggests that 1 has good thermal stability.

The magnetic measurements were carried out using a crystalline sample, whose phase purity was confirmed by powder X‐ray diffraction. The temperature‐dependent magnetic susceptibility of 1 was investigated in the range of 2.0–300 K with a 1000 Oe applied field (Figure 4). The χmT value of 1 at 300 K is 9.96 cm3·K·mol–1, which is appreciably lower than the spin‐only value of 13.12 cm3·K·mol–1 magnetically three isolated high‐spin MnII ions (S = 5/2, g = 2.0), indicating the presence of a dominant antiferromagnetic interaction. It is noteworthy that the χmT values of 1 continuously decrease upon cooling and reach to the minimum value (3.81 cm3·K·mol–1) at 11 K and then abruptly increase to 7.43 cm3·K·mol–1, which exhibits weak ferromagnetic behavior at low temperature for 1. The temperature dependence of the reciprocal molar susceptibility above 100 K follows the Curie‐Weiss law with C = 11.47 cm3·K·mol–1, θ = –48.09 K (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The relatively big negative Weiss constant indicates dominating antiferromagnetic interactions between MnII ions.

Conclusions

By employment of benzotriazole‐5‐carboxylate acid to assemble with the Mn2+ ions, a porous framework with strip‐shaped Mn–O chain and 1D channel is presented herein. Such an unusual structural assembly leads to a stable framework and exhibits good thermal stability. Simultaneously, the magnetic measures show that 1 exhibits weak ferromagnetic behavior at low temperature.
