In situ template generation via N-alkylation in the syntheses of open-framework zinc phosphites and phosphate†

Guoming Wang *a, Jinhua Li a, Xiao Zhang a, Pei Wang a, BeiBei Pang a, Zonghua Wang a, Yingxia Wang *b, Jianhua Lin b and Chunyang Pan c

bBeijing National Laboratory for Molecular Sciences, College of Chemistry and Molecular Engineering, Peking University, Beijing 100871, P. R. China. E-mail: wangyx@pku.edu.cn

In methanol–aqueous solutions, three novel structure-directing agents (SDAs) were generated in situ and presented. These in situ reactions feature N-methylation transformations between CH3OH solvent and organic cyclic aliphatic and aromatic amines, i.e. 2-methylpiperazine, 1,4-diazabicyclo[2,2,2]octane (dabco) and 3-(aminomethyl)pyridine. Distinct from conventional Eschweiler–Clarke methylation containing excess formic acid and formaldehyde, such a direct methylation transformation from methanol molecules is unique. In the presence of new SDAs, three organically templated zinc phosphites/phosphates, [pmpip][Zn3(HPO3)4] I (pmpip = N,N,N′,N′-tetramethyl-2-methylpiperazinium), [dmdabco][Zn3(HPO3)4] II (dmdabco = N,N′-dimethyl-1,4-diazabicyclo[2,2,2]octane) and [Hmampy][Zn2(HPO4)3] III (mampy = N-methyl-3-(aminomethyl)pyridine), have been prepared and structurally characterized by elemental analysis, IR, thermogravimetric analysis, powder and single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Compound I exhibits a 2D layer with eight-membered apertures, compounds II and III possess a 3D interrupted framework with intersecting 8-, 10- and 12-ring channels (for II), and 8-, 10-ring channels (for III), respectively.

Introduction

Microporous materials have attracted considerable interest due to their rich structural chemistry and potential applications in catalysis, absorption, ion-exchange and separation, etc.1 Since the pioneering work reporting the crystalline aluminophosphate molecular sieves in 1982,2 a great deal of attention has been paid to the synthesis of non-aluminosilicate-based zeolitic materials.3 The metal phosphate family has proved a particularly rich source of new materials, and a large number of such solids have been prepared and characterized with diverse and fascinating structural features. Such examples include several metal phosphates with helical channels and chiral frameworks,4 and a few cases possessing extra-large channels, such as aluminophosphate VPI-5 and gallium phosphates MIL-n (n = 31, 46 and 50) with 18-membered ring (18R) channels,5 aluminophosphate JDF-20 and gallium phosphate cloverite with 20R channels,6 zinc phosphate ND-1, nickel phosphates VSB-1, VSB-5 and gallium phosphate NTHU-1 with 24R channels.7 Recently, the replacement of tetrahedral phosphate anion groups PO43− by pseudopyramidal phosphite units HPO32− has resulted in a new class of metal phosphites with great success.8 Compared to the four-connected {PO4} unit, the presence of three-connected {HPO3} groups can reduce the M–O–P connectivity and generate more “open” interrupted architectures with larger pore sizes and lower framework densities. Prominent examples include Zn3(HPO3)4Zn(H2O) and [Ni(C6N2H14)2][Zn4(H2O)(HPO3)5] with intersecting 16R channels,9 CoHPO-CJ2 with 18R channels,10 TJPU-3 with 20R channels,11 Cr-NKU-24, ZnHPO-CJ1, [HR]2[Zn3(HPO3)4] (R = CHA, CHPA) and SCU-24 with 24R channels,12 bimetallic phosphite NTHU-5 with 26R channels,13 as well as the very recently reported NTHU-13 family with 28-, 40-, 48-, 56-, 64- and 72R channels.14

In general, open-framework metal phosphates/phosphites are synthesized by hydrothermal or solvothermal methods in the presence of organic amines acting as templates or SDAs. Thus, the judicious selection of appropriate SDAs is crucial for the construction of microporous materials with specific structures and properties. Yu and co-workers, for example, reported the syntheses of three open-framework zinc phosphites with desired extra-large 24-ring channels by utilizing computationally-assisted organic amines.15 Though some understanding has been achieved of the influence and roles of SDAs, reliable prediction of the final framework structure is still a challenging task when a new organic template is used. A review of the literature shows that most templates contained within the crystallizing porous framework are of direct use, and are conventionally introduced as a charge-compensating and space-filling constituent. In a very few cases, new organic amine molecules were unexpectedly produced from a simple decomposition or rearrangement of the initial amine precursor.16 Therefore, it would be highly desirable to generate new SDAs in situ from those most prolific and easily available amine precursors. With this in mind, we seek to use three different cyclic organic amines as the precursors for the in situ-template synthesis of new open-framework metal phosphates/phosphites in methanol and aqueous media. As expected, our efforts were indeed successful. We report here three unique one-pot solvothermal preparations involving in situ N-methylation transformations of cyclic organic amine precursors, resulting in the syntheses of three new zinc phosphate and phosphites with varying dimensionality: 2D [pmpip][Zn3(HPO3)4] I, 3D [dmdabco][Zn3(HPO3)4] II and [Hmampy][Zn2(HPO4)3] III. It is worth noting that the corresponding SDAs were generated in situ by direct methylation transformations between organic amine precursors and CH3OH solvent.

Experimental

Materials and instrumentation

Reagents were purchased commercially and used without further purification. The CHN analyses were carried out on an Elemental Vario EL III CHNOS elemental analyzer. Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were obtained using a Philips X'Pert-MPD diffractometer with Cu-Kα1 radiation (λ = 1.54076 Å). Infrared spectra were obtained from sample powder pelletized with KBr on an ABB Bomen MB 102 series FTIR spectrophotometer over a range 400–4000 cm−1.

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA 851e analyzer under air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 from 40 to 900 °C.

Preparation of [pmpip][Zn3(HPO3)4] (I). After cooling to room temperature, the resulting product, containing colorless rod-like crystals of I, was recovered by filtration, washed with distilled water, and dried in air (77.4% yield based on zinc). Crystallographic data collection and refinement

Suitable colorless single crystals of I (rod, dimensions 0.12 × 0.10 × 0.08 mm3), II (rod, dimensions 0.16 × 0.12 × 0.10 mm3) and III (rod, dimensions 0.10 × 0.06 × 0.04 mm3) were carefully selected under an optical microscope and glued to a thin glass fiber with epoxy resin. Crystal structure determination by X-ray diffraction was performed using a Siemens SMART CCD diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα (λ = 0.71073 Å) in the ω and φ scanning modes at room temperature. An empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.17 The structures were both solved by direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares methods on F2 with the SHELXL-97 program package.18 The zinc and phosphorus atoms were first located, whereas the carbon, nitrogen and oxygen atoms were found in the successive Fourier difference maps. The C(3) atom in I was found to be disordered over two positions with an equal occupancy. The hydrogen atoms residing on the phosphorus were located by Fourier maps and the remaining hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically and refined in a riding model. All of the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The crystallographic data and structure refinements for I–III are given in Table 1. Selected bond distances and hydrogen bonds are given in Tables 2 and 3. CCDC contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper with the deposition numbers CCDC 927967, 927968 and 940009 for I–III, respectively.

Results and discussion

Synthesis

In the process of preparing the title compounds I–III, three cyclic organic amines 2-methylpiperazine, 1,4-diazabicyclo[2,2,2]octane (dabco) and 3-(aminomethyl)pyridine were initially selected and introduced, but N,N,N′,N′-tetramethyl-2-methylpiperazinium (pmpip2+), N,N′-dimethyl-1,4-diazabicyclo[2,2,2]octane (dmdabco2+) and N-methyl-3-(aminomethyl)pyridinium (Hmampy2+) were found to act as the true SDAs. Obviously, such pmpip2+, dmdabco2+ and Hmampy2+ originated in situ respectively from direct N-methylation transformations between those corresponding amine precursors and CH3OH solvent in acidic solutions (Scheme 1). This is quite different from the typical Eschweiler–Clarke methylation,19 in which the primary (or secondary) amine is methylated by excess formic acid and formaldehyde. Although a large number of organic amines have been successfully introduced into the inorganic network in the form of nonprotonated linkers, monoprotonated and diprotonated countercations, reports on new SDAs generated in situ have still been rare to date. The present in situ N-alkylation reactions between such cyclic aliphatic/aromatic amines and methanol solvent, to the best of our knowledge, were first observed in the open-framework metal phosphate/phosphite system.
Scheme 1 In situ N-alkylation reactions between 2-methylpiperazine and CH3OH (top), dabco and CH3OH (middle), and 3-(aminomethyl)pyridine and CH3OH (bottom) under solvothermal conditions, showing the formation of three new SDAs dmdabco2+, pmpip2+ and Hmampy2+.
Structure description of [pmpip][Zn3(HPO3)4] (I). The asymmetric unit contains 18 non-hydrogen atoms, of which two zinc atoms and three phosphorus atoms are crystallographically independent (Fig. 1a). Atoms Zn(2) and P(2) are located in the general positions, while Zn(1), P(1) and P(3) atoms each occupy a special position with a site multiplicity of 0.5, respectively. Both the zinc atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms with Zn–O bond lengths in the range of 1.917(4) Å–1.952(4) Å and O–Zn–O angles lying between 99.7(2)° and 115.1(3)°. The phosphorus atoms each makes three P–O–Zn linkages with adjacent Zn atoms, with the fourth vertex occupied by a terminal hydrogen atom. The P–O distances are in the range 1.495(4)–1.516(5) Å (av. 1.501 Å), and the O–P–O bond angles are in the range 110.5(2)–115.8(3)° (av. 113.3°).
Fig. 1 (a) View of the coordination of the zinc and phosphorus atoms in I, showing the atom-labeling scheme and 50% thermal ellipsoids. (b) Views of the 6 × 1 building block. (c) Polyhedral view of the inorganic layer along the [100] direction showing the 12-ring channels. (d) Polyhedral view of the packing of the sheets along the [001] direction. Color code: ZnO4 tetrahedra, green; HPO3 pseudopyramids, purple.

The structure of I consists of ZnO4 and HPO3 units connected through their vertices, giving rise to a layered architecture. Interestingly, the inorganic layers can be conceptually considered to be built up from a secondary building unit (SBU) denoted as 6 × 1, featuring a six-membered ring capped by one HPO3 pseudopyramid (Fig. 1b). Each 6 × 1 building block is linked to four neighboring units to form a two-dimensional network with eight-membered rings (Fig. 1c). It should be noted that similar layered structures have also been observed in H2tmdp·Zn3(HPO3)4 (tmdp = 4,4′-trimethylenedipiperidine) and (H2bpp)2·Zn6(HPO3)8 [bpp = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)-propane].20 However, the arrangement of the layered structures in three compounds is quite different. In compound I, the adjacent inorganic sheets are stacked in an –AAAA– sequence (Fig. 1d), whereas distinct –ABAB– and –ABCD– stacking modes have been found in compounds H2tmdp·Zn3(HPO3)4 and (H2bpp)2·Zn6(HPO3)8, respectively. The pmpip2+ cations occupy the interlayer spaces, compensating the negative charges and simultaneously interacting with the inorganic framework through C–H⋯O hydrogen-bonding interactions (Table 3). A void space analysis performed by the use of the program PLATON indicates that these extra framework species occupy 46.6% of the unit cell volume.21

Structure description of [dmdabco][Zn3(HPO3)4] (II). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that compound II possesses a three-dimensional framework. The asymmetric unit contains 29 crystallographically independent non-hydrogen atoms, of which 19 atoms belong to the host framework and the remaining 10 atoms belong to the guest species (Fig. 2a). All the zinc atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms, and each makes four links with adjacent phosphorus atoms via Zn–O–P linkages. The four unique phosphorus atoms each share three oxygen atoms with adjacent zinc atoms, with the fourth vertex occupied by a terminal hydrogen atom. The Zn–O bond distances are in the range of 1.909(3)–1.957(2) Å, and the P–O bond lengths vary from 1.494(3) to 1.520(3) Å, in agreement with those of other open-framework zinc phosphites.
Fig. 2 (a) View of the coordination of the zinc and phosphorus atoms in II, showing the atom-labeling scheme and 50% thermal ellipsoids. (b) Polyhedral view of the single inorganic layer of II with 4-, 6-, 8- and 12-ring apertures along the [010] direction. Color code: ZnO4 tetrahedra, green; HPO3 pseudopyramids, purple. (c,d) Polyhedral view of the structure along the [010] direction showing the stacking of layers, and the zigzag 12-ring channels. (e,f) Polyhedral view of a single layer along the [001] direction with 10-ring windows, and the stacking of the sheets.

The framework structure of II is built up from strictly alternating ZnO4 tetrahedra and HPO3 pseudopyramids, which are linked through their vertices, giving rise to a three-dimensional architecture. Fig. 2b shows the single inorganic layer viewed down the [010] direction, containing four-, six-, eight-, and twelve-membered apertures. The 8-ring window, composed of 4ZnO4 tetrahedra and 4HPO3 pseudo pyramids, has a pore size of ca. 4.8 × 9.4 Å; the 12-ring window was defined by six ZnO4 tetrahedra and six HPO3 pseudopyramids, with a free diameter size of ca. 6.1 × 13.6 Å. Such inorganic layers are held together in –ABAB– stacking sequence along the [010] direction to generate a complex 3D open-framework (Fig. 2c). Therefore, the elliptical 12-ring channels are not straight along the [010] direction (Fig. 2d). They propagate in a zigzag way and are simultaneously intersected by the 6- and 8-ring channels along the [100] direction and the 10-ring channels along the [001] direction. Compared with other reported metal phosphites with 12-ring channels intersected by multidirectional 8-ring channels,22 the occurrence of 10-MR channels in II is unique and, to the best of our knowledge, firstly observed. As shown in Fig. 2e, the 10-ring window was defined by 5ZnO4 tetrahedra and 5HPO3 pseudopyramids, with a free diameter size of ca. 6.8 × 9.2 Å. Interestingly, such 10-ring channels also run a zigzag way along the [001] direction (Fig. 2f). The framework density of this structure measured by the number of polyhedra per 1000 Å3 is 14.0, which is comparable to those of compounds with 12-ring channels, such as FJ-15 (13.4), [CH3CH2NH3]2·[Zn3(HPO3)4] (14.2) and [H2N(CH3)2]2·[Zn3(HPO3)4] (14.8).22a,e

The guest dmdabco2+ cations are found perfectly arranged in the middle region of the 10-ring channels and, as the counterion, balance the overall negative charges of the three-dimensional architecture. Extensive C–H⋯O type hydrogen bond interactions have been observed in II (Table 3). A void space analysis using the program PLATON indicates that these extra-framework organic cations occupy 39.5% of the unit cell volume.

Structure description of [Hmampy][Zn2(HPO4)3] (III). Unlike the structure of I and II, the framework structure of III is built up from strictly alternating tetrahedral ZnO4 and PO3(OH) units. The asymmetric unit contains 26 non-hydrogen atoms, of which 17 atoms belong to the framework and 9 atoms belong to the guest species. There are two crystallographically independent zinc and three phosphorus atoms (Fig. 3a). Both zinc atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms with Zn–O bond lengths in the range of 1.903(2) Å–1.952(4) Å (av. 1.924 Å). The O–Zn–O angles are in the range of 101.2(1)–115.1(2)° (av. 109.4°). Of the three phosphorus atoms, P(1) and P(3) make three P–O–Zn linkages with one P–O terminal bond, while P(2) only makes two P–O–Zn linkages and have two P–O terminal bonds. The P–O distances are in the range 1.503(2)–1.585(2) Å (av. 1.528 Å), and the O–P–O bond angles are in the range 103.8(2)–114.5(2)° (av. 109.4°). These geometrical parameters are in good agreement with those observed in other open-framework zinc phosphates. Assuming the usual valence of Zn, P, O to be +2, +5, and −2, respectively, the stoichiometry of Zn2(PO4)3 creates a net charge of −5. Bond valence sum values23 indicate that the P(1)–O(1), P(2)–O(7) and P(3)–O(12) with distances of 1.595(2), 1.585(2) and 1.579(2) Å are terminal –OH groups, respectively. This assignment is also in agreement with the proton positions observed near the oxygen atoms in the difference Fourier map. The second terminal P–O linkage, in the case of P(2) with a P–O(6) distance of 1.521(2) Å, is a PO unit. The excess negative charge of −2 can be balanced by one molecule of monoprotonated N-methyl-3-(aminomethyl)pyridinium (Hmampy2+).

(b) The three-dimensional structure of III is constructed from two types of 4.82 layers and cross-linking PO4 nodes. (c–f) Views of the above 4.82 layers, each of which is constructed from two types of helical chains respectively. (g, h) Polyhedral view of the structure with regular 8- and 10-ring channels along the [100] and [001] directions.

The inorganic framework of III is exclusively made up of ZnO4 and PO3(OH) tetrahedral units, linked through their vertexes forming a 3D architecture. Interestingly, the 3D structure of III can be considered to be constructed from two types of regularly alternating zinc phosphate layers, which are cross-linked by PO4 nodes, as shown in Fig. 3b. The linkages between Zn(2)O4 and P(3)O3(OH) tetrahedral groups give rise to an infinite 2D zinc phosphate layer (layer-I, Fig. 3c); the linkages between Zn(1)O4 and P(1)O3(OH) tetrahedral groups form another 2D zinc phosphate layer (layer-II, Fig. 3e). Of particular interest is the apparent presence of polyhedral 4-rings and 8-rings in both sheets; thus the topology can be described in terms of a 4.82 network (i.e., each nodal Zn or P atom participates in one 4-ring and two 8-rings). The shape and pore sizes of eight-ring windows in each sheet, however, are quite different because the 8-rings may be subject to various degrees of distortion. The atom-to-atom dimensions of 8-ring apertures in layer-I and layer-II are approximately 6.1 × 6.4 Å and 4.8 × 9.4 Å, respectively. Two types of helices with opposite chirality are involved in the construction of such 4.82 layers. As shown in Fig. 3d, 1D left-handed and right-handed infinite helical chains, coexisting in the structure with a ratio of 50:50 and consisting of –Zn(2)–O(9)–P(3)–O(10)– and –Zn(2)–O(10)–P(3)–O(9)–, couple with each other by sharing O(11) atoms to generate the above zinc phosphate layer-I. In the construction of the above layer-II, the left-handed and right-handed infinite helical chains, consisting of –Zn(1)–O(2)–P(1)–O(3)– and –Zn(1)–O(3)–P(1)–O(2)–, couple with each other by sharing O(4) atoms (Fig. 3f). It should be noted that similar undulated sheet structures containing left- and right-handed helical chains are particularly rare and, to the best of our knowledge, have only been observed in zinc phosphite (C5H6N2)Zn(HPO3).24 The two types of 4.82 layers are further cross-linked together by two-connected P(2)O4 units to produce the three-dimensional framework with multidirectional 8-membered channels along the [100] and [010] directions, and a 10-membered channel along the [001] direction (Fig. 3g and h).

The presence of hydroxyl groups interrupts the framework of III and usually results in a relatively “open” structure. The framework density of this structure measured by the number of polyhedra per 1000 Å3 is 12.5, which possesses a degree of openness comparable to that observed in many aluminosilicate zeolites and aluminophosphates. The guest Hmampy2+ cations reside in the channels and interact with the framework through extensive N–H⋯O hydrogen bonds. The important hydrogen bond interactions in III are listed in Table 3. A void space analysis using the program PLATON indicates that these extra-framework organic cations occupy 40.2% of the unit cell volume.

Thermogravimetric analysis

Thermogravimetric (TG) analyses of the powder samples of I–III were performed in a flow of air with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. As shown in Fig. 4, the structures of compounds I–III remain stable up to 296, 312 and 308 °C, respectively. On further heating, compounds I and II undergo similar two-step weight-loss processes in which the organic amines in each structure started to decompose. The observed weight losses of 23.9% (for I, the temperature region 296–608 °C) and 21.1% (for II, the temperature region 312–586 °C) were in good agreement with the calculated values of 23.3% for I and 21.6% for II. For compound III, however, a three-step weight loss of 29.52% up to 798 °C was observed, assigned to the removal of organic molecules and the partial dehydration process (calculated 29.30% for one organic amine and two water molecules per formula unit).

Conclusions

Three new templates were generated in situ and involved in the syntheses of open-framework phosphate and phosphite materials, [pmpip][Zn3(HPO3)4] I, [dmdabco][Zn3(HPO3)4] II and [Hmampy][Zn2(HPO4)3] III. Compounds I and II are both constructed from alternating ZnO4 tetrahedra and HPO32− pseudopyramids, giving rise to a 2D layer structure with 8-ring apertures and a 3D macroanionic framework with intersecting 8-, 10- and 12-ring channels. For III, the structure is built up from tetrahedral ZnO4 and PO3(OH) units to generate an interrupted 3D framework with 8- and 10-ring channels. It is noteworthy that the templating agents, pmpip2+ in I, dmdabco2+ in II and Hmampy2+ in III, were derived from direct N-methylation reactions between CH3OH solvent and the corresponding organic amine precursors, i.e. 2-methylpiperazine, dabco and 3-(aminomethyl)pyridine. Evidently, such a methylation transformation is distinct from the conventional Eschweiler–Clarke methylation in which an excess of formic acid and formaldehyde was required. The present work may offer an effective synthetic methodology to design and generate new templates in situ from prolific and easily available amine precursors. Related work is still going on including using other organic amine precursors under similar methanol–aqueous media, as well as the possible N-alkylation transformations when other alcohol solvents instead of methanol (e.g. ethanol, propanol, isopropanol etc.) were involved.
