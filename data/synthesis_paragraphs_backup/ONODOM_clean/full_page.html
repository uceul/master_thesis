Two novel nickel(II) and cobalt(II) metal–organic frameworks based on a rigid aromatic multicarboxylate ligand: syntheses, structural characterization and magnetic properties†

Tuo-Ping Hu ab, Zhi-Jia Xue b, Bao-Hua Zheng b, Xiao-Qing Wang b, Xue-Na Hao b and You Song *a
aState Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing University, Xianlin Road, Nanjing 210023, PR China. E-mail: yousong@nju.edu.cn
bNorth University of China, Taiyuan, PR China. E-mail: hutuopingsx@126.com

Received 26th March 2016 , Accepted 30th May 2016

First published on 30th May 2016

Two novel metal–organic frameworks, formulated as [Ni3(qptc)2(H2O)3]·3.5H2O·3Me2NH (1) and [Co3(qptc)2(H2O)4]·5H2O (2) (H4qptc = 1,1′:4′,1′′:4′′,1′′′-quaterphenyl-2,4,2′′′,4′′′-tetracarboxylic acid), have been hydrothermally constructed based on a rigid aromatic multicarboxylate ligand. The single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis shows that complex 1 crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with space group P and possesses a 4,8-connected 3D open framework including trinuclear nickel Ni3 SBUs, which belongs to an scu topology with the point symbols (416·612) and (44·62). Complex 2 belongs to the monoclinic crystal system with space group C2/c and exhibits a 3D framework containing Co3 SBUs, which is a flu topology with the point symbols (412·612·84) and (46). Furthermore, the variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of complexes 1 and 2 have been investigated. Both complexes show weak ferromagnetic coupling between metal ions estimated by using a linear trinuclear model for 1 in the range of 2–300 K and the PHI program for 2 in the range of 20–300 K taking the orbital contribution into consideration.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs)1 are a fascinating class of crystalline hybrid materials based on the connection of metal centers or clusters and organic linkers. During the past decades, the rational design and synthesis of MOFs have been extensively studied not only for their attractive topological structures, but also for their applications in the fields of gas storage,2 selective guest sorption,3 catalysis,4 drug delivery,5 photochemical sensors,6 molecular magnetism7 and sensing for the detection of gases.8 Meanwhile, many MOFs display magnetic properties, due to their transition metal ion secondary building units (SBUs), which have been attracting much attention9 because of their potential applications in magnetic molecular sensing. It is a well-known fact that the construction of the multinuclear cluster of paramagnetic metal centers plays a key role in defining the magnetic behavior of a complex.10 SBUs are often isolated with low metal nuclearity, but higher nuclearity SBUs are relatively rare.11

Often rigid aromatic multicarboxylate ligands are utilized in synthetic tuning of the structural topology and pore sizes to suit particular applications,12 which possess complicated geometric constructions and specific functional characteristics because of the carboxylate groups adopting abundant coordination modes; collective long-range ferromagnetism arises from the coupling between these localized spin states via delocalized π electrons in conjugated aromatic linkers and such a fascinating magnetic behavior is analogous to that observed in dilute magnetic semiconductors.13 We have recently reported two novel MOFs: Mn-MOF1 and Co-MOF4 with the dinuclear SBUs [Mn2(COO)2] and [Co2(COO)2], respectively.14

In continuation of our interest in the development of MOFs with higher nuclearity SBUs, we further explored their design and syntheses using a rigid aromatic multicarboxylate ligand (1,1′:4′,1′′:4′′,1′′′-quaterphenyl-2,4,2′′′,4′′′-tetracarboxylic acid, H4qptc). In H4qptc, the carboxylate groups can be partially or fully deprotonated, which can coordinate with metal ions in multiple coordination modes to construct MOFs with attractive topologies and properties. Meanwhile, the phenyl rings can twist slightly to adapt to different coordination modes as a result of the steric-hindrance effect. In this study, we report the syntheses, crystal structures and magnetic properties of two novel MOFs [Ni3(qptc)2(H2O)3]·3.5H2O·3Me2NH (1) and [Co3(qptc)2(H2O)4]·5H2O (2).

Experimental section

The ligand H4qptc was purchased from Jinan Henhua Sci. & Tec. Co. Ltd, and the other materials and solvents were purchased and used without further purification. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiments were performed on a ZCT-A analyzer at the range of 22–700 °C with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under a N2 atmosphere. Elemental analyses (C, H, N) were carried out on a Vario MACRO cube elemental analyzer. IR spectra were collected on an FTIR-8400S spectrometer within the 4000–400 cm−1 region. Powder XRD data were obtained on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 PC diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5406 Å). Variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility study data were collected on a Quantum Design SQUID MPMS XL-7 instrument under an applied magnetic field of 1000 Oe at the range of 2–300 K.

Syntheses of complexes

Synthesis of [Ni3(qptc)2(H2O)3]·3.5H2O·3Me2NH (1)

A mixture of Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (0.015 mmol, 5.3 mg), H4qptc (0.0050 mmol, 2.4 mg) and DMF/cyclohexanol/H2O (1.0 mL, v/v/v, 1/4/5) was added into a hard glass tube, pumped into a near-vacuum, and heated at 130 °C for 2 days, then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C h−1. Green crystals of 1 were obtained by filtration, cleaning, and drying in air. Yield: 28%. Anal. calcd for C62H62Ni3O22.5N3: C, 53.71; H, 4.47. Found: C, 56.58; H, 2.91. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3418 (m), 2352 (m), 1592 (vs), 1384 (vs), 1175 (w), 1114 (w), 1004 (m), 918 (m), 832 (s), 783 (s), 747 (s), 673 (s), 649 (w), 551 (w), 502 (m), 416 (w).

Synthesis of [Co3(qptc)2(H2O)4]·5H2O (2)

A mixture of Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.020 mmol, 7.0 mg), H4qptc (0.0050 mmol, 2.4 mg), DMF/cyclohexanol/H2O (1.0 mL, v/v/v, 1/4/5) and 1 drop of 0.50 mol L−1 NH3 (aq) was added into a hard glass tube, pumped into a near-vacuum, and heated at 130 °C for 2 days, then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 2 °C h−1. Pink crystals of 2 were collected by filtration, washed with EtOH and dried in air. Yield: 23%. Anal. calcd for C56H46Co3O25: C, 51.86; H, 3.55. Found: C, 51.92; H, 3.52. IR (KBr, cm−1): 3430 (w), 3160 (m), 2352 (vs), 1629 (w), 1555 (w), 1396 (s), 1175 (w), 1102 (w), 1004 (w), 918 (w), 820 (w), 783 (m), 747 (m), 685 (m), 563 (w), 489 (w).

X-ray crystallography

The X-ray intensity data for 1 and 2 were collected on a Bruker Apex II CCD diffractometer with Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293 K. The empirical absorption correction was handled by the multi-scan method, and the data reduction was corrected for Lorentz, polarization and absorption effects. Direct methods structure solution and full-matrix least-squares on F2 were applied via SHELXL-2014.15 The highest possible space group was chosen. Non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically, and hydrogen atoms were refined as riding atoms with isotropic displacement parameters 1.2–1.5 times of the Ueq of the attached atoms. Some free solvent molecules in complex 1 are highly disordered, and no satisfactory disorder model could be achieved. The PLATON/SQUEEZE routine was used to remove scattering from the disordered solvent molecules.16 Their crystallographic data collection and refinement parameters are shown in Table 1, and the selected bond distances and angles are displayed in Table S1.† Their crystallographic data have been deposited with the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Center (CCDC: 1479948 for 1, 1479949 for 2).

Results and discussion

Description of the crystal structures

X-ray single-crystal diffraction analysis reveals that complex 1 possesses a 3D open framework including trinuclear nickel SBUs and crystallizes in the triclinic crystal system with space group P. Its asymmetric unit contains three nickel ions, two qptc4− ligands, three coordinated H2O molecules, three and a half H2O molecules, and three free Me2NH molecules that are obtained by decomposition of DMF. Fig. 1a shows the coordination mode of qptc4− and the coordination environments of the three Ni(II) atoms. Each ligand links to six H4qptc ligands, and the three Ni ions (Ni1, Ni2, Ni3) are coordinated by four carboxyl groups and two bridging H2O molecules into a trinuclear hourglass SBU, where the Ni1–Ni3 and Ni2–Ni3 distances are 3.373(6) Å and 3.141(8) Å, respectively. The three Ni ions are all six-coordinated and display slightly distorted octahedral geometries, but their coordination environments are different. The Ni1(II) centre is coordinated by five oxygen atoms from four qptc4− ligands and one oxygen atom from one coordinated water molecule, and the Ni2 and Ni3 ions are coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four qptc4− ligands and two oxygen atoms from different coordinated water molecules. The Ni–O covalent bond distances range between 2.008(6) and 2.156(6) Å. Each ligand connects four trinuclear SBUs. Here, each carboxylate group of the H4qptc ligand is deprotonated, and the ligand exhibits two different coordination modes: μ5 = η1:η0:η1:η1:η0:η1:η0:η2 and μ7 = η1:η1:η0:η1:η1:η1:η1:η1.
Fig. 1 (a) Coordination environment of Ni(II) ions in 1 and the linkage mode of qptc4−. (b) Projection view of the 3D framework along the c axis. (c) Ball-and-stick view of the 2D layer along the a axis (symmetry codes: (i) 3 − X, 3 − Y, −Z; (ii) 3 − X, 2 − Y; (iii) 1 − X, 2 − Y, 1 − Z; (iv) 1 − X, 1 − Y, 1 − Z; (viii) 1 + X, 1 + Y, +Z).

The trinuclear SBUs are jointed by the qptc4− organic linkers into a 3D open framework. Compared with the classical ligand H4BPTC17 (1,1′-biphenyl-2,2′,6,6′-tetracarboxylic acid), H4qptc possesses a longer linker that can enhance the pore volumes of MOFs. As shown in Fig. 1b and c, the trinuclear SBUs are concatenated through benzene rings to form a 2D plane, which are further linked by two benzene rings into a 3D open framework. It is clearly shown that the two incorporated benzene rings enhance the pore size along the c axis. As shown in Fig. 1c, the twisted structure displays a 2D plane along the a axis. Thus, complex 1 was constructed from 2D planes and the linker with two benzene rings. To further study the framework of 1, its multidimensional structure can be simplified into a node-and-linker reference net topology by using the free computer program TOPOs.18 In complex 1, each trinuclear SBU links to eight ligands, which can be taken as an 8-connected node. In the asymmetric unit, two ligands with different coordination environments can be simplified into 4-connected nodes. Thus, 1 can be seen as a 4,8-connected network with the point symbols (416·612) and (44·62), which belongs to an scu topology (Fig. 2).


Fig. 2 Schematic representations of a simplified 3D network of 1 with an scu topology containing 8-connected nodes and 4-connected nodes.

The crystal structure of complex 2 was confirmed by a single-crystal X-ray diffraction study. 2 crystallizes in the monoclinic crystal system with space group C2/c and is also a 3D framework. Its asymmetric unit includes Co1 in a general position, a half of Co2 on a twofold axis, one qptc4− ligand in a general position, two coordinated H2O molecules (O1W bonded to Co1 and O2W linking Co1 and Co2), and some free water molecules (a half of O3W on a twofold axis, unit-occupancy O4W and two half-occupancy water molecules O5W and O6W in general positions). As shown in Fig. 3, three Co ions are connected by four carboxylate groups of different qptc4− ligands and two bridging H2O molecules to form a trinuclear hourglass SBU, which is similar to that in complex 1. Co1 and Co2 are both six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from different qptc4− ligands and two coordinated H2O molecules into slightly distorted octahedral geometries with different coordination environments (Fig. 3a). The average Co–O distances are 2.099(3) Å and 2.102(3) Å for Co1 and Co2, respectively. Fig. 3b exhibits a 2D plane of complex 2 along the a axis. The qptc4− ion connects six Co ions of four SBUs, in which two SBUs provide two Co ions each and the others provide one Co ion each. The qptc4− ions coordinating to two Co ions of SBUs link the SBUs to form zigzag chains, which are further extended into 2D planes by the carboxylate groups of the qptc4− ligands. As shown in Fig. 3c, the 2D planes are connected by coordinated H2O molecules to form a 3D framework. During the reaction, all the carboxylate groups of the H4qptc ligand are deprotonated, and the ligand adopts a μ6 = η1:η1:η0:η1:η0:η1:η1:η1 coordination mode.


Fig. 3 (a) Coordination environment of Co(II) ions in 2 and the linkage mode of qptc4−. (b) Ball-and-stick view of the 2D layer with zigzag chains along the a axis. (c) Projection view of the 3D framework along the b axis (symmetry codes: (ii) 1 − X, 1 − Y, 1 − Z; (iv) 1 − X, +Y, 3/2 − Z; (vii) −1/2 + X, 3/2 − Y, −1/2 + Z).

Here, each hourglass SBU connects eight qptc4− ligands, and each qptc4− ligand links four trinuclear hourglass SBUs. Thus, the SBU and the ligand can be taken as an 8-connected node and a 4-connected node, respectively. The structure can be analyzed using TOPOs software,18 which is a 4,8-connected network with the point symbols (412·612·84) and (46) and belongs to a flu topology (Fig. 4).


Fig. 4 Stick views of a simplified 3D network of 2 with a flu topology containing 8-connected nodes and 4-connected nodes.

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) characterization and thermogravimetric analyses (TGA)

The PXRD of the MOFs 1 and 2 has been investigated in the solid state at room temperature (Fig. 5). The peak positions simulated from the single-crystal X-ray data are in good agreement with those from experimental data, confirming the good phase purity of the MOFs.
Fig. 5 PXRD patterns of the series complexes. Black: simulated from the X-ray single-crystal data; red: observed for the as-synthesized solids.

Their thermal behaviors were studied under a dry nitrogen atmosphere and the TG curves are shown in Fig. 6. Complex 1 shows a weight loss of 18.2% from 63 °C to 378 °C due to the loss of 3 coordinated and 3.5 lattice H2O molecules, and 3 free Me2NH molecules (calcd: 16.8%), followed by a rapid weight loss indicating the decomposition of complex 1 and the formation of NiO as the main residue. The TG curve of 2 displays a weight loss of 12.3% from 45 °C to 360 °C corresponding to the loss of lattice and coordinated H2O molecules (calcd: 12.5%). Then, complex 2 starts to decompose.


Fig. 6 TG curves of complexes 1 and 2.

Magnetic properties

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibilities of complexes 1 and 2 were measured over the temperature range of 2–300 K with a 1000 Oe external field and are shown as χMT and χM−1versus T plots in Fig. 7 and 8.
Fig. 7 Temperature dependence of χMT vs. T and χM−1vs. T for 1 at 1000 Oe. The red solid line represents the best fit curves.

Fig. 8 Plot of χMT vs. T for 2 in an applied field of 1 KOe from 2 to 300 K.

For 1, the χMT value at 300 K is 3.79 cm3 K mol−1, which is higher than the spin-only value of 3.00 cm3 K mol−1 for three isolated Ni(II) ions (S = 2/2, g = 2.0). Upon lowering the temperature, the value of χMT almost remains in a quasi-plateau from 300 K to 77 K, and then exhibits an abrupt increase to a maximum of 7.98 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K. The increase in χMT could be due to ferromagnetic interactions between Ni(II) ions (Fig. 7). The best fit gives J = 1.72 cm−1, g = 2.27 and zj′ = 0.015 cm−1 with an agreement factor R = 8.93 × 10−3, where R = ∑[((χM)obsd. − (χM)calcd.)2/∑(χM)obsd.2].

The plot of χM−1vs. T obeys the Curie–Weiss law [χM = C/(T − θ)] with C (Curie constant) = 3.79 cm3 K mol−1 and θ (Weiss constant) = 3.74 K. The positive θ value further confirms the ferromagnetic interaction between Ni(II) ions, which is in agreement with a positive J value as fitted above. The hysteresis loop measured at 2.0 K shows an analogously sigmoidal shape (Fig. S2†) with the coercive field and the remnant magnetization equal to zero, indicating no three-dimensional magnetic ordering above 2.0 K. However, the maximum magnetization of 6.35 NμB at 5 T corresponds to the ground state ST = 3, clearly indicating the ferromagnetic coupling between Ni(II) ions within the SBUs.

For 2 (Fig. 8), the room-temperature χMT value of 10.70 cm3 K mol−1 is much larger than the spin-only value (5.63 cm3 K mol−1) expected for three isolated Co(II) ions with a spin value of 3/2 and a Landé g value of 2.0, indicating that a significant orbital contribution is involved at higher temperature.20 Upon cooling from 300 K, the χMT gradually decreases to a minimum value of 6.69 cm3 K mol−1 at 20 K, which is mainly due to the thermal depopulation of the excited levels ascribed to the splitting of 4T2g ground states under crystal field effects and spin–orbit coupling, then rises rapidly to reach a maximum value of 7.04 cm3 K mol−1 at 13 K, which suggests ferromagnetism, and finally drops again to 3.70 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, which possibly results from antiferromagnetic interactions between SBUs and/or saturation effects. This changing trend of χMT following T is common for Co(II) ions.14,21

According to the structure containing linear trinuclear cluster, the temperature dependence of magnetic susceptibility for complex 2 was fitted using the same equation (eqn (1)) by the PHI program.22 The best fit gives J = 0.31 cm−1, g = 2.38 and zj′ = −0.92 cm−1 with spin–orbit coupling constant λ = −193.5 cm−1 and orbital quenching constant α = −0.92 cm−1 (−0.46 K). The fitted curve well matches the experimental data at the temperature range of 20–300 K and the value of λ is in good agreement with the range for Co(II) ions in an octahedral field.23

The χM−1versus T curve above 48 K follows the Curie–Weiss law with C (Curie constant) = 11.58 cm3 K mol−1 and θ (Weiss constant) = −24.32 K. The negative θ and initial decrease of χMT versus T are attributed to the thermal depopulation of the Kramer levels of Co(II) ions14 and the Weiss constant is much larger than that for three non-interacting Co ions (ca. −20 K for one Co ion),24 indicating the ferromagnetic coupling between spins within SBUs. In addition, the χMT of 2 becomes field-dependent at low temperature (Fig. S3†). The shape of the χMT vs. T plots with the maximum value at lower temperature is typical of ferromagnetic behaviour attributed to ferromagnetic interactions.25 The variable-field magnetization shows an unsaturated value of 6.25 NμB (Fig. S4†), which is much larger than that of one Co(II) ion corresponding to the net spins of antiferromagnetic coupling between metal ions, providing evidence of ferromagnetic coupling.

Conclusions

In summary, two novel transition metal–organic frameworks based on a rigid aromatic tetracarboxylate ligand have been synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Complex 1 possesses a 4,8-connected 3D open framework including trinuclear nickel SBUs, which belongs to an scu topology with the point symbols (416·612) and (44·62). 2 is a 4,8-connected 3D network with Schläfli symbols of (412·612·84) and (46). Furthermore, the study of magnetic properties shows that the weak ferromagnetic coupling between metal ions dominates the magnetic properties of both complexes.
