Fabrication of morphology-preserved microporous carbon from a zeolitic-like porous coordination polymer†

Bihang Zhou‡ a, Yinying Hua‡ a, Fujun Cheng a, Jingui Duan *ab, Lingyun Chen *c and Wanqin Jin ab
aState Key Laboratory of Materials-Oriented Chemical Engineering, College of Chemical Engineering, Nanjing Tech University, Nanjing, 210009, China. E-mail: duanjingui@njtech.edu.cn
bJiangsu National Synergetic Innovation Center for Advanced Materials, Nanjing Tech University, Nanjing, 210009, China
cSchool of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Chongqing University, Chongqing, 400044, China. E-mail: lychen@cqu.edu.cn

Received 23rd November 2016 , Accepted 22nd December 2016

First published on 26th December 2016

A new zeolitic-like microporous coordination polymer (PCP), [Zn2(L)·2H2O]·guest (named NTU-17), with crb (BCT) topology was designed and prepared. Interestingly and unusually, it is the first time that such rod-shaped NTU-17 crystals could be converted to morphology-preserved carbon rods with exclusive micropores and a large surface area by using a facile method of direct thermal transformation. Gas adsorption and selectivity showed that these PCP dependent carbon rods are suitable solid adsorbents for CH4 purification.

1. Introduction

With a specific pore system and good water/chemical stability, carbon materials have received considerable interest because of their wide applications in gas sorption/separation,1 heterogeneous catalysis,2 oxygen reduction reactions,3 supercapacitors,4 Li-ion batteries5 and so on. Many attempts, such as chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and thermal decomposition (TD) of non-porous precursors of wood, coal, or organic material have been used to fabricate carbon materials with unique advantages in pore creation.6 Meanwhile, a template-approach allows fine control over mother structures and hence the properties of their derived carbons.7 Among which, porous carbon has gained great progress in recent years. However, the synthesis of morphology-preserved carbon materials with exclusive micropore and a large surface area remains a challenge, due to the lower crystallinity, poor design ability and unregulated pore arrangement of general carbons.8

Due to the high crystallinity, tunable pore nature and promising properties, porous coordination polymers (PCPs) have received a great deal of attention in gas storage/separation, catalysis and conductivity.9 Considering the larger carbon content of the corresponding organic ligands and high thermal stability, thermal conversion of PCPs was believed to be a simple and rational pathway for obtaining carbons with dependent pores.10 Firstly, with pre-arranged distribution of organic units and serving as self-sacrificial and porous templates, PCPs could afford carbon materials with persistent atomic arrangements, and hence reproducible and controllable properties. Secondly, due to their low solubility and non-volatility, increased total yield of carbon products would be expected. Thirdly, for obtaining varied components of derived carbons, some small organics (such as furfuryl alcohol or imidazole) could be filled into the pores of PCPs as a second carbon source. Therefore, some porous carbons have been prepared by direct carbonization of PCPs without any additional precursors recently, however, the original morphology of PCP crystals would be partial or completely destroyed during the thermal transformation process.10b,11 Thus, preparation of morphology-preserved carbon materials by direct thermal transformation of PCPs, particularly in a self-templated fashion, is a great challenge. This is because morphology-preserved thermal transformation will provide not only the relationship between the template and pore properties of the generated products, but also the rational methods for tuning pore systems of carbon materials.

We are interested in developing functional inorganic–organic hybrid materials with finely controlled pores for gas storage/separation.9k,12 Herein, a new rod-shaped and zeolitic-like PCP with two kinds of micropores, [Zn2(L)·2H2O]·guest (NTU-17), was constructed from a well-designed organic linker (1,3,5-tris(3-carboxy-4-hydroxylphenyl)benzene (H6L)) and zinc acetate. As self-sacrificial porous templates, rod-shaped NTU-17 crystals were converted to morphology-preserved carbon materials by direct thermal transformation. Interestingly and unusually, all PCP dependent carbon rods possess exclusive micropores, centred at about 0.5–0.8 nm, similar to that of pore diameters of NTU-17. In addition, gas adsorption and selectivity showed that those carbon rods exhibited high potential for CH4 purification from mixtures of CO2/CH4 and C2 hydrocarbons/CH4 at room temperature.

2. Experimental

2.1 General procedures and materials

All the reagents and solvents were commercially available and used as received. The elemental analysis was carried out with a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. Thermal analyses were performed on a Universal V3.9A TA Instruments with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1 under flowing nitrogen. The powder X-ray diffraction pattern (PXRD) measurements were carried out on a Bruker AXS D8 Advance 40 kV, 40 mA for CuKα (θ = 1.5418 Å) with a scan rate of 0.2 deg s−1 at room temperature. Simulated powder patterns from single-crystal X-ray diffraction data were generated using Mercury 1.4.2 software. Raman spectra were collected using a Horiba Jobin-Yvon HR800 Raman spectrometer.

2.2 Synthesis of the organic building block

1,3,5-Tris(3-carboxy-4-hydroxylphenyl)benzene (H6L) was synthesized according to the previous work.13

2.3 Synthesis of [Zn2(L)·2H2O]·guest (named NTU-17)

Zn(OAc)2·2H2O (22 mg) and H6L (8 mg) were mixed with 2 ml of 1,4-dioxane/H2O (4:1) in a glass container and tightly capped with a Teflon vial and heated at 100 °C for two days. After cooling to room temperature, colourless and rod shaped crystals were obtained. Yield: ∼70% (based on the ligand). The sample was subjected to solvent evacuation at 150 °C, for 10 h and 10−3 Torr, giving rise to the anhydrous sample.

2.4 Syntheses of microporous carbons

NTU-17 crystals of about 220 mg were placed in a ceramic crucible (1 cm × 1 cm × 10 cm). The powder was distributed over the bottom of the crucible so that the temperatures could be uniformly applied to all crystals. The furnace was heated under N2 from room temperature to 200 °C for 2 h, then, the temperature was maintained for another 1 h. After that, the furnace was heated to 600 °C, 650 °C, and 700 °C with a heating rate of 2 °C min−1, respectively. The furnace was maintained at the corresponding temperature for 4 h, then, the samples were cooled in the furnace to room temperature in 6 h. The obtained black powder was immersed in HCl under stirring for 24 h to remove zinc species (final pH = 1). The black precipitates were then harvested by centrifugation. The washing process was repeated 5 times using distilled water and 5 times using EtOH. Subsequently, black precipitates were dried under vacuum for 2 h at 60 °C. Finally, ∼30 to 40 mg of the product was obtained. The carbon materials were named C-600, C-650 and C-700, according to the applied calcination temperatures.

2.5 Single crystal X-ray studies

The single crystal X-ray diffraction measurement was performed at 173 K with a Rigaku AFC10 diffractometer with a Rigaku Saturn Kappa CCD system equipped with a MicroMax-007 HF/VariMax rotating-anode X-ray generator with confocal monochromated MoKα radiation. Data were processed using Crystal Clear TM-SM (Version 1.4.0). The structure was solved by direct methods and refined using the full-matrix least squares technique using the SHELXTL package.14 Non-hydrogen atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parameters during the final cycle. Organic hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions with isotropic displacement parameters set to 1.2Ueq of the attached atom. The unit cell includes a large region of disordered solvent molecules, which could not be modelled as discrete atomic sites. We employed PLATON/SQUEEZE15 to calculate the diffraction contribution of the solvent molecules and, thereby, to produce a set of solvent-free diffraction intensities; the structure was then refined again using the data generated. The crystal data are reported in Table S1.†

2.7 Binary mixture adsorption

IAST was used to predict binary mixture adsorption from the experimental pure-gas isotherms. In order to perform the integrations required by IAST, the single-component isotherms should be fitted by a suitable model. There is no restriction on the choice of the model to fit the adsorption isotherm, but data over the pressure range under study should be fitted very precisely. Several isotherm models were tested to fit the experimentally pure isotherms for CO2, C2H4, C2H6 and CH4 in C-600, C-650 and C-700, and the dual-site Langmuir–Freundlich equation was found to best fit the experimental data:
 
(3)
Here, P is the pressure of the bulk gas at equilibrium with the adsorbed phase (kPa), q is the adsorbed amount per mass of adsorbent (mol kg−1), qm1 and qm2 are the saturation capacities of sites 1 and 2 (mol kg−1), b1 and b2 are the affinity coefficients of sites 1 and 2 (kPa−1), and n1 and n2 represent the deviations from an ideal homogeneous surface. The dual-site Langmuir–Freundlich equation fits the single-component isotherms extremely well. The R2 values for all the fitted isotherms were over 0.99997. Hence, the fitted isotherm parameters were applied to perform the necessary integrations in IAST.

3. Results and discussion

3.1 Single-crystal structural analysis

The solvothermal reaction of zinc(II) acetate with H6L in DMF/dioxane/H2O afforded rod-shaped crystals with a high yield. Single crystal X-ray diffraction revealed that [Zn2(L4−)·(H2O)2]·guest (named NTU-17) crystallizes in the space group P21/c with a = 7.3800(15) Å, b = 28.290 Å and c = 22.470(5) Å (Table S1†). The asymmetric unit of NTU-17 includes two Zn(II) ions, one partially deprotonated ligand, and two coordinated water molecules. The coordination geometry of Zn1 is a slightly distorted hexahedron, which was completed by an oxygen atom from a phenolic hydroxyl group, three oxygen atoms from three different carboxylate groups, and one coordinated water molecule. The coordination geometry of Zn2 is the same as that of Zn1. Therefore, the oxygen atom (O1) from the phenolic hydroxyl group plays a role as a bridge for connecting those two Zn atoms, making them as a dimer cluster. Meanwhile, an infinite metal–oxygen chain was created by the linkage of those dimers from carboxylate groups, which were further connected by ligands to form a highly porous 3D framework with two kinds of 1-D channels. Their window apertures are around 6–12 Å (Fig. 1a–e). Therefore, composed of 4-connected Zn-dimer nodes and 4-connected linkers, NTU-17 possesses a crb (BCT) zeolitic topology (Fig. S3†), similar to that of ZIF-64.16 The calculated total accessible volume of desolvated NTU-17 reached ca. 57.8%.15b Additionally, the powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern showed that the peak positions of the as-synthesized phase are the same as the simulated data, indicating high phase purity (Fig. 1f). Thermogravimetric analysis showed that NTU-17 starts to decompose around 380 °C (Fig. S4†). In addition, the rod configuration of NTU-17 crystals (width: 10–12 μm, length: 30–50 μm) was well observed by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Fig. S8†).

3.2 Synthesis and characterization of carbon rods

Carbonization of NTU-17 crystals at 600 °C, 650 °C and 700 °C in N2 yielded carbon rods (named C-600, C-650 and C-700), which were further washed using HCl under strong stirring. SEM results in Fig. 2 showed that morphology-preserved thermal transformations, with original dimensions of 10–12 μm (width) and 30–50 μm (length), were observed. With an increased reaction temperature from 600 °C to 700 °C, morphology of generated carbons is almost the same. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analyses of those carbon rods confirmed the removal of Zn species from the carbon matrix, and elemental analysis revealed very low N content (Fig. S5–S7 and Table S2†). In addition, EDS results indicated that the percentage of Zn present in all three carbon rods (washed using HCl) is almost negligible (less than 1%). However, it may work as the metal site for improved gas separation.17 PXRD analyses of those carbons showed only two broad peaks at 25° and 44° that were assigned to the carbon (002) and (101) diffractions, respectively (Fig. 1e). In order to further evaluate the quality of carbon materials, we performed Raman analyses. In general, the relative ratios of the G bands to the D bands (IG/ID) illustrate the crystallization degree of graphitic carbon. The appearance of the D peak is associated with the presence of a disordered carbon structure. On the basis of the Raman spectra shown in Fig. 1h, the relative intensity (IG/ID) ratios of the D (∼1350 cm−1) and G bands (∼1590 cm−1) for C-600, C-650 and C-700 are 1.21, 1.17 and 1.06, respectively, higher than that of the reported IG/ID value (0.99 to 1.04) for nanoporous carbon, derived from Al-based PCP.11a Furthermore, the gradually decreased value of IG/ID of those three carbons indicates increased defects, as well as a decreased crystallization degree of graphitic carbon. Fig. 3 shows transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of the products at different magnification. As shown in the low magnification TEM images in Fig. 3a, d and g, all the products display rod-likes structures with a diameter of several micrometers. And from high magnification TEM images in Fig. 3c, f and i, there are nanometer-sized pores in the obtained carbon materials, and the disordered carbon domains suggest relatively low degree of graphitization, revealing low concentration of parallel single layers in the obtained carbon rods, which is in agreement with the corresponding XRD patterns and Raman spectra results mentioned above.18

3.3 Permanent porosity

The specific surface area and pore size distribution of all carbon rods were comparatively characterized by means of N2 (77 K) adsorption isotherms. As presented in Fig. S10,†NTU-17 did not show any gas uptake. However, all carbon rods exhibit complete reversible type-I isotherm (Fig. 1i), and they steeply increase at low relative pressure (P/P0 < 0.1), suggesting the micropore characteristic. BET surface areas are 610 m2 g−1, 499 m2 g−1 and 474 m2 g−1 for C-600, C-650 and C-700, reflecting the importance of carbonization temperature for pore creation. Interestingly and unusually, shown in the inset of Fig. 1i, pore size distributions of those series carbons are in a very narrow range (mainly centering at 0.5 to 0.8 nm). Keeping the IG/ID values in mind, varied pore size distributions are strongly related to the crystallization degree of graphitic carbons (Fig. 4). In addition, their pore systems were further confirmed by the observation from TEM. Therefore, despite the absence of precise carbon structure in atom level, we believed that the generated pore systems in those carbon rods have strong relationships with well-defined ligands in NTU-17.

3.4 Adsorption heats and selective gas adsorption

Unique and narrow pore properties suggest that C-600–700 might be promising candidates for gas separation. Single-component adsorption isotherms for CH4, CO2, C2H4 and C2H6 were measured to 100 kPa at 273 K. As shown in Fig. 5a, c and e, C2H4 and C2H6 adsorption isotherms in all those three carbon rods display quick capture before 20 kPa, however, the uptake of CH4 increases slowly following this pressure. Thus, the differences in uptake at P = 20 kPa indicate that all of them are potential candidates for purification of CH4 from CO2 and C2 hydrocarbon involved mixtures. Interestingly, even though CO2 has a large quadruple moment, all of them prefer to capture C2 hydrocarbons, which is similar to previous performance of PCPs with open metal sites.12a

IAST predicted selectivity of C2H6/CH4, C2H4/CH4 and CO2/CH4 in C-600, C-650 and C-700 at 273 K (black line) and 298 K (blue line) (b, d and f).

We also collected gas isotherms at higher temperatures (283 and 298 K) (Fig. S11–S13†) and used these data to calculate the isosteric heats of adsorption (Qst) for C-600, C-650 and C-700 by a virial method. They exhibit binding affinity for CO2 around 30 kJ mol−1 (Fig. S14† and Table 1) at zero coverage, which is significantly higher than that of some porous carbons (C1000: 20.7 kJ mol−1) and commercially activated carbons (16–25 kJ mol−1)18,19 and approaches to that of some PCPs with functional sites (USTA-16: 32 kJ mol−1 and NJU-Bai3: 36.5 kJ mol−1).20 The heats of adsorption of C2 hydrocarbons are increased to 34–36 kJ mol−1, decreasing slightly to 31–33 kJ mol−1 at higher coverage. In contrast with the lower adsorption heat of CH4 (21.5 kJ mol−1), those results could account for the high adsorption selectivity for CO2/CH4 and C2 hydrocarbons/CH4.

Ideal adsorbed solution theory (IAST) is a precise method used to describe gas-mixture adsorption in representative porous materials and it was employed to predict multicomponent adsorption behaviours from the experimental single-component gas isotherms.22 The predicted adsorption selectivity for equimolar CO2 and C2 relative to CH4 mixtures in those three carbons as a function of bulk pressure is presented in Fig. 5b, d and f. As expected, the selectivity of C2H6 relative to CH4 of C-600 has an unprecedented value (ca. 150) around low coverage at 273 K. This value is two and more times higher than that of C-650 (ca. 75) and C-700 (ca. 66), indicating the important functions of the smaller micropore in C-600. In contrast, gas selectivity of C2H6/CH4 in C-600 is superior to those of many important PCPs with open metal sites (Mg-MOF-74 (14), La(BTB) (22), ZJU-48a (6) and HKUST-1 (11)),12b,23 as well as some mesoporous carbons (NAHA-1 (45) and NAC-600 (40))24 and COFs (88),25 under similar conditions. Further, inherent C2H4/CH4, CO2/CH4 selectivities for C-600, C-650 and C-700 derived principally from their adsorption heat for C2H4 and CO2 at moderate loading. Therefore, combining good selectivities of C2H4/CH4 and CO2/CH4, those carbon rods can be used as a suitable solid absorbent for CH4 purification.

4. Conclusions

In summary, we have synthesized a new zeolitic-like PCP containing two kinds of 1-D channels. Through a facile and effective strategy of direct carbonization, the PCP dependent and morphology-preserved carbon rods with exclusive micropores and good surface area were firstly fabricated. Gas adsorptions and IAST simulations showed that these carbon rods demonstrate good gas uptake, as well as good selectivities for CH4 purification at room temperature. Therefore, combining with high physicochemical stability, these carbon materials are very promising media for clean energy storage and separation. Thus, this work provides not only NTU-17 crystal dependent (morphology and pore) carbon rods, but also a new method for creating porous carbons with an easily tunable and template dependent pore system.
