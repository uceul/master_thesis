A microporous luminescent europium metal–organic framework for nitro explosive sensing†

bCollege of Chemistry and Materials Engineering, Wenzhou University, Wenzhou, 325035, China

A microporous metal–organic framework (MOF) Eu3(MFDA)4(NO3)(DMF)3 (1, H2MFDA = 9,9-dimethylfluorene-2,7-dicarboxylic acid) has been solvothermally synthesized and structurally characterized by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. 1 is a three-dimensional coordination polymer with pcu type rod-packing structure, through which 1D rhombic channels penetrate. The solvent-free form 1a could be obtained by direct heating of 1. Both 1 and 1a exhibit high intensity red light emissions with high quantum yields and long luminescence lifetimes when excited at 336 nm at ambient temperature. The potential of 1a for nitro explosive sensing is studied through luminescence quenching experiments, which show that 1a is a potential luminescent sensory material for nitro explosives.

Introduction

Nitro compounds including nitroaromatics and nitroalkanes are not only well-known explosives but also notorious environmental pollutants, for example, nitrobenzene (NB), 4-nitrotoluene (4-NT), 2,4-dinitrotoluene (2,4-DNT) and trinitrotoluene (TNT) were involved in the black list of priority pollutants in China, USA and the European Union, etc. Therefore, the reliable and accurate detection of nitro compounds has great significance to homeland security and environmental protection.1 The fluorescence quenching chemosensing technology has been applied to the detection of nitro compounds due to high sensitivity, quick response and convenience. Its interaction mechanism is the donor–acceptor electron-transfer between the electron-deficient nitro compounds and electron-rich fluorophores.2 The fluorene molecule and its derivatives are excellent electron donors,3 and have been extensively incorporated into fluorescence conjugated polymers, which demonstrated high sensing performance for nitro explosives due to the “molecular wire” effect.4,5

The metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) as one class of emerging multifunctional porous materials have shown promising applications in the areas of gas storage and separation,6 luminescence,7 magnetism8 and catalysis.9 The cavities or channels in their structures make MOFs competitive candidates for chemosensors. Firstly, the analytes are possibly accumulated in the cavities or channels, therefore a lower detection limit could be obtained. Secondly, the shape and size of the cavities or channels should result in the selective recognition for different analytes. However, only until 2009 were MOFs realized for the sensing of nitro explosives for the first time by Li et al.10 This pioneering work and subsequent works11 have displayed the great promise of MOFs for the sensing and detection of nitro explosives.

Herein, we report the synthesis and structure of a microporous red light emitting MOF Eu3(MFDA)4(NO3)(DMF)3 (1, H2MFDA = 9,9-dimethylfluorene-2,7-dicarboxylic acid), and investigate the ability of its solvent-free form 1a for nitro explosive sensing through luminescence quenching experiments.

Experimental

Materials

All reagents and solvents were used as received from commercial suppliers without further purification. All the nitro explosives and other aromatic compounds used in the fluorescence experiments were purchased and used as received. H2MFDA was synthesized according to the literature.12

Instrumentation

Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. Infrared spectra were recorded on a Vector22 Bruker Spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. The powder XRD patterns were recorded on a Shimadzu XD-3A X-ray diffractometer. TGA (thermal gravimetric analysis) was collected on a Shimadzu DTG-60 under 15 mL min−1 nitrogen gas flow. The fluorescence quantum yield in powder form was measured using quinine sulfate as reference and with an integration sphere. Time-resolved fluorescence measurements were performed using a Life-Spec-ps fluorescence lifetime analytical spectrometer (Edinburgh Instruments).

Photoluminescence measurements

The photoluminescence properties of 1a were investigated in the solid state and in various solvents as 1a–solvent emulsions at room temperature. The 1a–solvent emulsions were prepared by introducing 3 mg of 1a powder into 3.00 mL solvent and ultrasonic agitation for 30 min before testing. Photoluminescent spectra were measured using a RF-5301PC spectrofluorophotometer.

Theoretical calculations

The G03W software package S113 was used to carry out a DFT geometry optimization using the B3LYP functional with 6-31G(d) basis sets.

Synthesis of 1

A mixture of H2MFDA (0.1 mmol, 28.2 mg), Eu(NO3)3·6H2O (0.1 mmol, 44.7 mg), DMF (5 mL), CH3CH2OH (2 mL) and H2O (1 mL) was sealed in a 15 mL Teflon-lined bomb and heated at 80 °C for 3 days. The reaction mixture was slowly cooled to room temperature. Colorless rod-like crystals of 1 suitable for X-ray diffraction analysis were isolated in 72% yield. Elemental analysis (%) calcd for C77H69N4O22Eu3: C, 49.77; H, 3.74; N, 3.02%. Found: C, 50.06; H, 3.57; N, 3.19%.

X-ray crystallography

X-ray diffraction data of complex 1 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex CCD area detector diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at room temperature. Absorption corrections were applied using SADABS.14 The structure was solved by direct methods using the SHELX-97 program package.15 The positions of the metal atoms and their first coordination spheres were located from direct-method E maps; other non-hydrogen atoms were found using alternating difference Fourier syntheses and least-squared refinement cycles and, during the final cycles, were refined anisotropically. Hydrogen atoms were placed in calculated positions and refined as riding atoms with a uniform value of Uiso. Lanthanide ion Eu(2) in 1 is disordered over two symmetrically related sites with 50%:50% split occupancies. Crystallographic details for 1 have been summarized in Table 1. CCDC reference number for 1 is 888697.

Results and discussion

Crystal structure

The asymmetric unit of 1 contains one and a half europium ions, two MFDA ligands, half a nitrate ion, and one and a half coordinated DMF molecules (Fig. 1). The Eu(1) ion is seven-coordinated by six oxygen atoms from six MFDA ligands and one oxygen atom from a terminal DMF molecule. The Eu(2) ion is also seven-coordinated by four oxygen atoms from four MFDA ligands, two oxygen atoms from a bidentate chelated nitrate ion and one oxygen atom from a terminal DMF molecule. The Eu–O bond lengths in 1 are comparable to those reported for other Eu–O donor complexes.16–18 The MFDA ligand acts as a μ4-bridge to link four Eu ions with each carboxylate oxygen atom coordinating to a Eu ion, respectively. The Eu ions are connected by the carboxylate groups from MFDA ligands to produce the 1D Eu–O–C rod-shaped secondary building units (SBUs) extending along the [101] direction. Two Eu–O–C rods are linked by the 9,9-dimethylfluorene units of the MFDA ligands forming a ladder, in which two kinds of different oriented 9,9-dimethylfluorene units with a dihedral angle of 75.1(1)° between the fluorene units are repeatedly and alternatively arranged to act as the ladder rungs, and Eu–O–C rods as ladder posts. Each Eu–O–C rod is linked to another four Eu–O–C rods in the (1) and (11) crystal planes to generate a three-dimensional pcu type rod-packing structure with rhombic channels in the [101] direction having 9.3 × 5.1 Å2 dimensions in which terminal DMF molecules reside as coordinated guests (Fig. 2).19 The solvent-accessible space for the desolvated 1 is 1183.7 Å3 per unit cell or 28.8% of the total volume, calculated using the PLATON routine.20

Thermal properties

TGA data indicate that 1 releases its terminal DMF molecules and nitrate ions in the temperature range of 150 to 260 °C with weight loss of 14.9% (calc. 15.1%). The solvent-free phase Eu3(MFDA)4(NO3) 1a could be obtained by direct heating of 1 at 180 °C for 16 h and retains the identical framework structure of 1, which is confirmed by TGA (Fig. S1†) and PXRD (Fig. 3).

Photoluminescent properties

The excitation spectrum of 1a monitored under the characteristic emission (615 nm) of the Eu(III) ion exhibits a broad band with a maximum at around 336 nm and two sharp lines at 394 and 465 nm (Fig. S2†). The emission spectrum of 1a in the solid state or dispersed in DMF solution excited at 336 nm exhibits the luminescence peaks at 581, 592, 615 with shoulder peak at 620, 654 and 703 nm, which could be attributed to the characteristic transitions of the Eu3+ ion: 5D0→7FJ (J = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4), respectively (Fig. 7 and S2†). The strong blue light emission in free H2MFDA21 completely disappears and the ligands effectively transfer energy to Eu(III) ions with quantum yields of 62.3% for 1, and 22.7% for 1a. The decay dynamics of the emission peak at 615 nm for 1 and 1a is well fitted by a biexponential curve, suggesting two distinct emission centers, which is consistent with the two independent Eu(III) lattice sites in the structure. Release of the terminal DMF molecules from the as-synthesized 1 increase the luminescence lifetimes from 391 and 647 to 431 and 870 μs (Fig. S3†).

Detection for nitro compounds

During examining the potential of 1a for the sensing of small molecule solvents, we find that its luminescence was completely quenched when 1a was dispersed in NB as the emulsion (Fig. S4†). So, the luminescence responses of 1a to various nitro explosives were ascertained by dispersing 1a in 0.5 mM nitro compound solution in DMF (Fig. 4). All ten nitro compounds can weaken the photoluminescent intensity of 1a to a varying degree. The order of quenching efficiency is 3,4-DNT > 1,2-DNB > 2-NT ≈ 4-NT > 2,4-DNT ≈ 2,6-DNT > 1,3-DNB > NM ≈ NB > DMNB. The most effective quencher is 3,4-DNT with QP (quenching percentage = (I0 − I)/I0 × 100%, I0 and I are luminescence intensity of 1a before and after exposure to the nitro compound) of 45.3%, and the less effective one is DMNB. Unlike the nitro compounds, an opposite effect was observed for toluene, which enhanced the luminescence emission intensity. Therefore it is speculated that the donor–acceptor electron-transfer is the dominant mechanism of luminescence attenuation and enhancement. For 1a, the luminescence emission could be affected by the interactions of the analytes with ligands as antenna or Eu(III) ions. In order to further elucidate the mechanism, the luminescence lifetimes of 1a were measured and remain the same in different concentrations of 1,2-DNB (Fig. 5), suggesting that there are no interactions between analytes and Eu3+ and changes in emission intensity are related to the interactions between the analytes and ligands MFDA. Indeed, we find that the lowest unoccupied molecule orbital (LUMO) of H2MFDA has a higher energy than those of six nitroaromatic compounds selected for theoretical calculations (Fig. 6). However, the electron affinities as evaluated from their LUMOs energies follow the order 1,2-DNB > 1,3-DNB > 2,4-DNT > 3,4-DNT > NB > 2-NT, which is not fully in accordance with their quenching efficiencies, because in principle the higher the electron affinity is, the more effective the quenching behavior will be. This inconsistency could be explained by their absorption for the excited light source energy, with stronger absorption leading to greater luminescence quenching efficiencies owing to the quenchers decreasing the absorption of light by MFDA ligands.11c As exhibited in the UV-Vis absorption spectra of six nitroaromatic compounds (Fig. S5†), the molar absorption coefficients ε at 336 nm are in the same order with the quenching efficiencies, with the highest ε of 1020 dm3 mol−1 cm−1 for 3,4-DNT.

The photoluminescence titration upon addition of different concentrations of 3,4-DNT, 1,2-DNB and 2,4-DNT to 1a in DMF all showed that the emission intensity progressively decreased with the increase in the concentrations of the quenchers (Fig. 7). The luminescence titration results can be treated with the Stern–Volmer equation, I0/I = 1 + Ksv[Q], where Ksv is the quenching constant, and Q is the quencher.22 The linear Stern–Volmer relationships are all observed with Ksv of 4.59 × 103 M−1 for 3,4-DNT, 1.98 × 103 M−1 for 1,2-DNB and 1.31 × 103 M−1 for 2,4-DNT, indicating that either a static or dynamic quenching process is dominant.

Conclusions

In conclusion, a high intensity red light emitting Eu-MOF Eu3(MFDA)4(NO3)(DMF)3 possessing pcu type rod-packing 3D structure penetrated through by 1D rhombic channels is successfully synthesized under solvothermal conditions. Its solvent-free phase maintaining the identical framework structure shows efficient luminescence quenching phenomena for nitro compounds, especially nitroaromatics and it is a potential luminescent sensory material for nitro explosives. The luminescence response results from the cooperative effect of the donor–acceptor electron-transfer and the competition of absorption for the excited light source energy between quenchers and ligands MFDA.
