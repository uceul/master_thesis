A unique 3D metal–organic framework based on a 12-connected pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster exhibiting proton conduction†

Yu Shen a, Xu-Feng Yang a, Hai-Bin Zhu *a, Yue Zhao *b and Wei-Si Li c
aSchool of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Southeast University, Nanjing 211189, China. E-mail: zhuhaibin@seu.edu.cn
bCoordination Chemistry Institute, State Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing National Laboratory of Microstructures, Nanjing University, Nanjing 210093, China. E-mail: zhaoyue@nju.edu.cn

Received 4th July 2015 , Accepted 14th July 2015

First published on 17th July 2015

Solvothermal reaction of a TCA (TCA = 4,4′,4′′-tricarboxytriphenylamine) ligand with Cd(NO3)2 yielded a unique 3D 12-connected metal–organic framework of 1, which can be simplified as a (3,3,12)-connected 3-nodal net with a Schläfli symbol of {416·636·814}{42·6}2{43}2 based on a 12-connected node of a pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster and a three-connected organic linker of TCA. Compound 1 exhibits both photoluminescence and proton conductivity. The luminescence properties of 1 originate from the triphenylamine chromophore of the TCA ligand. The proton conductivity of 1 is supposed to be relevant to the structure attributes of 1. On the one hand, the curved narrow channels lined with a hydrophilic pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster in 1 can improve the water affinity of framework and facilitate the water absorption under humid conditions. On the other hand, the coordinated water molecules within the pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster can produce a mobile H+ proton due to coordination activation. At 80 °C and 85% RH (relative humidity), compound 1 shows the proton conductivity of 1.45 × 10−6 S cm−1.

Introduction

The design and construction of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) continue to attract immense interest because of their fascinating architectures and promising applications as functional materials in various fields,1 which typically consist of metal ions or clusters (inorganic nodes) and polytopic organic ligands (organic linkers) held together through coordination bonds. Compared to the discrete metal ions, the use of polynuclear metal clusters as secondary building units (SBU) is apparently superior in construction of highly connected MOFs owing to their inherent large surface areas together with more coordination sites. Although a number of three-dimensional (3D) MOFs with tetra-, penta-, hexa-, even octanuclear clusters have been reported in the literature reports that are largely built up by reacting Cd(II)/Zn(II) sources with aromatic polycarboxylate linkers,2 examples of high-connection such as nine-, ten- and especially twelve-connected MOFs are yet scarce which essentially depends on the suitable choice or subtle combination of cluster-formation groups and bridging connectors.3 Parallel to the intriguing structure and topology of MOFs, exploring new MOF-based function/multifunction is another fundamental research topic in this realm despite the fact that they have already found wide applications. For example, the development of MOFs as proton conductors has gained considerable attention in recent years considering that their potential application in clean energy technologies such as PEMFC (Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cells).4

4,4′,4′′-Tricarboxytriphenylamine (TCA) is a multifunctional aromatic tricarboxylate ligand that features a triphenylamine core. It might be simply employed as an organic building unit for constructing topologically new frameworks5 or luminescent MOFs as chemical sensors and photoactive catalysis by using its intrinsic triphenylamine emitter,6 even for enhancing hydrogen storage capacity of MOFs endowed by its redox-active characteristics.7 In this work, we choose this versatile organic molecule to construct a highly-connected 3D MOF, [Cd5(TCA)2(H2O)2]·8DMA·16H2O (1), with a pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster as a twelve-connected node,8 which exhibits both luminescence and proton conductive properties.

Results and discussion

Solvothermal reaction of Cd(NO3)2 with TCA in the DMA/EtOH/H2O (volume ratio = 2/1/1) mixed solvent resulted in yellow block crystals, [Cd5(TCA)2(H2O)2]·8DMA·16H2O (1). The formula of 1 was determined based on single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis, elemental analysis and thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA). The TGA curve of 1 exhibits a gradual and continuous weight loss in the range between 50 °C and 386 °C. The weight loss of 10.87% in the range between 50 °C and 281 °C corresponds to the removal of all guest water and coordinated water molecules (calcd 11.09%), and the weight loss of 23.50% from 282 °C to 386 °C is ascribed to the removal of all guest DMA solvent molecules (calcd 23.86%). Above 386 °C, a dramatic weight loss happens due to the decomposition of TCA ligands, leading to the formation of CdO as the residue (obsd 21.54%, calcd 21.98%) (Fig. S1 in the ESI†). The phase purity of the as-synthesized samples of 1 was confirmed by good agreement of the diffraction peaks between the experimental and simulated PXRD patterns (Fig. S2†).

Compound 1 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, which represents a unique 3D metal–organic framework with the SBU of a pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster. In the asymmetric unit of 1 (Fig. 1), there exists three crystallographically independent Cd(II) atoms (Cd1, Cd2 and Cd3). The Cd1 atom is six-coordinated by six carboxylate O atoms from six TCA ligands (Cd1–O, 2.198(6)–2.292(6) Å), adopting an octahedral coordination geometry. The six-coordinate Cd2 atom has a distorted octahedral coordination geometry completed by six carboxylate O atoms from five TCA ligands (Cd2–O, 2.197(7)–2.644(9) Å). The Cd3 atom also shows a distorted octahedral geometry that was bound by five carboxylate O atoms from four TCA ligands and one water O atom (Cd3–O, 2.271(8)–2.712(9) Å). In particular, Cd1, Cd2 and Cd3 atoms are united together through carboxylate groups of TCA ligands to give a central symmetric pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster (Cd⋯Cd separation, 3.5845(9)–9.4082(9) Å), of which two pairs of symmetry-related Cd2 and Cd3 atoms form a parallelogram with the Cd1 atom at its symmetric center (Fig. 2a). Within the cluster, the Cd1 octahedron is bridged to two symmetry-related Cd2 octahedra in a corner-sharing manner whilst Cd2 and Cd3 octahedra are adhered to each other in the way of edge-sharing (Fig. 2a). Notably, each cluster is linked to twelve nearest neighbors through twelve TCA ligands so the pentanuclear Cd cluster acts as a twelve-connected node (Fig. 2b). In compound 1, there are two types of TCA ligands that differ in coordination modes of three carboxylate groups although they both work as a three-connected linker: one (denoted by N1 atom) exhibits μ2–η1:η2; μ2–η1:η2; μ3–η1:η2 modes, and the other (denoted by N2 atom) displays μ2–η1:η1; μ1–η1:η1; μ2–η1:η1 modes (Fig. S3†). However, the central nitrogen atoms (N1 and N2) in these two types of TCA ligands both show sp2 hybridization as evidenced by C–N–C average angles of approximately 120° (C–N1–C av. 119.9°; C–N2–C av. 119.0°) and unusually short N–C bond lengths (N1–C av. 1.42 Å; N2–C av. 1.44 Å). From the topological point of view, the whole framework can be simplified as a (3,3,12)-connected 3-nodal net with a Schläfli symbol of {416·636·814}{42·6}2{43}2 if taking the pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster and TCA ligand as the 12-connected and 3-connected nodes, respectively (Fig. 3). To the best of our knowledge, compound 1 in the current case constitutes one of the few examples of twelve-connected MOFs based on SBU of a polynuclear cluster.3a–i

As anticipated, compound 1 displays photophysical properties characteristic of the triphenylamine chromophore.6a In particular, an intense UV-Vis absorption at 350 nm appears due to π–π* transition of the triphenylamine moiety, and a strong and broad photoluminescence centered at about 456 nm is observed that is ascribed to the triphenylamine emitter (Fig. S4†).

Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis discloses that the 3D MOF of 1 possesses curved narrow channels (Fig. S5†), and the total solvent accessible volume is about 33.5% calculated by using the PLATON program.9 Nonetheless, the N2 sorption test at 77 K revealed a Type-III sorption behavior indicating very weak interaction between the N2 molecule and the host framework of 1 that is unable to provide the exact channel information of 1 (Fig. S6†). Despite that, the following two structure elements of 1 encourage us to further investigate its potential proton conductive properties. Firstly, the narrow channels of 1 lined with a hydrophilic pentanuclear cluster might improve the water affinity of its framework, which can facilitate water molecule absorption under humid conditions and enhance the water-mediated proton conduction.10 Secondly, the coordinated water molecule within the pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster might produce mobile H+ protons due to coordination activation.11 The proton conductivity of 1 was therefore evaluated by the AC impedance method using a compacted pellet of the powered crystalline sample. At 30 °C, compound 1 shows a poor proton conductivity of ∼7.41 × 10−10 S cm−1 under 50% RH (RH = relative humidity) conditions, estimated from the Nyquist plot (Fig. S7†). However, the proton conductivity of 1 can reach 6.88 × 10−8 S cm−1 when measured at 30 °C and 85% RH. The humidity dependence of proton conductivity confirms the critical role of water as a proton carrier, which is presumably absorbed into the curved narrow channels of 1 by strong hydrogen bonding with a carboxylate bound metal cluster. The temperature dependence of the proton conductivity of 1 is also examined. In all cases the Nyquist plots (Z′′ vs. Z′) consist of a circular arc that represents the pellet impedance, followed by a low frequency tail representing the electrode–pellet interface (Fig. S8†). Fig. 4 depicts the Arrhenius plot of the proton conductivity of 1 in the temperature range of 30–80 °C under 85% RH conditions, and the proton conductivity of 1 is improved to 1.45 × 10−6 S cm−1 at 80 °C with 85% RH, which is almost 21 times larger than that at 30 °C with 85% RH. The lnσT increases almost linearly with temperature, and the corresponding activation energy (Ea) of conductivity was estimated to be 0.74 eV from the equation: σT = σ0exp(−Ea/KBT) (where σ is the ionic conductivity, σ0 is the preexponential factor, KB is the Boltzmann constant, and T is the temperature). The Ea value implies that the proton conduction in 1 possibly takes place via the Vehicle mechanism (0.5–0.9 eV).12 The free water molecules accept the protons from the coordinated water molecules to form H3O+, which moves with the aid of a moving “Vehicle” along the opposite direction to the unladen water vehicles.13


Fig. 4 Arrhenius-type plot of 1 at a temperature range of 30–80 °C (inset: a Nyquist plot of 1 at 80 °C and 85% RH.)

In summary, compound 1 is a multifunctional metal–organic framework that displays both photoluminescence and proton conduction. Although there are a large number of examples regarding multifunctional MOFs that combine two or more physical/chemical properties within the same material,14 less attention has been paid to integrate the proton conduction with the other functions in this area as far as we know.15

Experimental

Materials and methods

All solvents and reagents of analytical grade were used as received without prior purification. The TCA ligand was synthesized by a literature method.16 IR spectra were recorded on a Thermo Scientific Nicolet 5700 FT-IR spectrophotometer with KBr pellets in the 400–4000 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a CHN-O-Rapid analyzer and an Elementar Vario MICRO analyzer. The UV-Vis spectra were recorded on a Shimadzu UV-2450 spectrophotometer and fluorescence emission spectra were recorded on a Horiba FluoroMax 4 spectrometer. For AC conductivity measurements, the powdered crystalline samples were compressed to 5 mm in thickness and 13 mm in diameter under 12–14 MPa pressure. The two flat surfaces of the pellet were coated with a thin layer of silver paste and then sandwiched by a brass electrode (9 mm in diameter). AC impedance spectroscopy measurement was conducted on a CHI604E (Chenghua, Shanghai) electrochemical impedance analyzer over a frequency range from 1 Hz to 0.1 MHz with an input voltage amplitude of 200 mV. Samples were placed in a temperature-humidity controlled chamber (GDS-50A, Hasuc, Shanghai). The bulk conductivity (σ) of the pellet was estimated from the Nyquist plot by arc extrapolation to the Z′ axis on the low frequency side.

Preparation of compound [Cd5(TCA)2(H2O)2]·8DMA·16H2O (1)

A mixture of H3TCA (0.05 mol), Cd(NO3)2·4H2O (0.25 mmol)) and HBF4 (40% aqueous, 2 drops) in the DMA/EtOH/H2O mixed solvent (DMA 2 mL; EtOH 1 mL; H2O 1 mL) was fully stirred at room temperature for 30 min, sealed in a 25 mL Teflon-lined autoclave and heated at 100 °C for 40 h. The autoclave was slowly cooled (5 °C h−1) to ambient temperature. The resultant yellow crystals were isolated, and dried in air (67.8% yield based on H3TCA). X-ray crystallography

The crystallographic data of 1 were collected on a Bruker Smart Apex II CCD area-detector diffractometer with graphite-monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 298(2) K using the ω-scan technique. The diffraction data were integrated using the SAINT program,17 which was also used for the intensity corrections for the Lorentz and polarization effects. A semi-empirical absorption correction was applied using the SADABS program.18 The structure of 1 was solved by direct methods and all the non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by the full-matrix least-squares technique using the SHELXL-97 crystallographic software package.19 All the hydrogen atoms except for those with coordinated H2O were generated geometrically and refined isotropically using the riding model. The guest DMA and H2O solvent molecules of 1 are highly disordered, and attempts to locate and refine the solvent peaks were unsuccessful. Contributions to scattering due to these solvent molecules were removed using the SQUEEZE routine of PLATON structures and were then refined again using the data generated.20 The details of the crystal parameters, data collection and refinements for the complexes are summarized in Table 1. Conclusions

Solvothermal reaction of TCA with Cd(NO3)2 resulted in a unique 3D metal–organic framework of 1 which is constructed by a 12-connected pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster and a 3-connected TCA ligand. Compound 1 represents a rare example of MOFs that combine both luminescence and proton conductivity. The luminescence properties of 1 are ascribed to the embedded triphenylamine chromophore in the TCA ligand, and the proton conductivity is closely related to the structure elements of 1. On the one hand, the curved narrow channel in 1 lined with a hydrophilic pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster can facilitate water absorption under humid conditions to set up a water-mediated proton transporting network. On the other hand, the coordinated water with a pentanuclear Cd(II) cluster might produce mobile H+ protons due to coordination activation.
