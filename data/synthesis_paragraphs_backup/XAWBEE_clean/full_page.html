Microporous and Mesoporous Materials

Volume 149, Issue 1, 1 February 2012, Pages 95-100

Synthesis and characterization of a new chiral open-framework indium phosphite with intertwined host and guest helices

Author links open overlay panelLiangliangHuangabTianyouSongaYongFanaLipingWangaCaixiaJiaLiangShanaLiWanga
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.micromeso.2011.08.027Get rights and content

Abstract

A new chiral open-framework indium phosphite In3(HPO3)6(H3DETA)·2H2O 1 has been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions using achiral diethylenetriamine (DETA) as template and characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray diffraction, IR spectroscopy, TGA, ICP–AES and elemental analyses. Single crystal X-ray diffraction analysis reveals that the 3D inorganic framework is built up by alternation of InO6 octahedra and HPO3 pseudo-pyramids. Interestingly, the right-handed inorganic helical ribbons are presented in the 3D open-framework while fascinating hydrogen-bonded helices are self-assembled under hydrothermal conditions between organic template molecules and H2O molecules. It is worth noting that the guest hydrogen-bonded helical chain and the host helical ribbon of inorganic framework show the same chirality and intertwine with each other along the b axis. Further understanding of the hydrogen bonds between guest molecules and inorganic framework demonstrates that the flexible DETA molecule may conduce to the formation of the chiral framework. Crystal data: In3(HPO3)6·(H3DETA)·2H2O, Monoclinic, P21 (No. 4), a = 9.2713(19) Å, b = 14.453(3) Å, c = 9.996(2) Å, β = 116.16(3)°, V = 1202.2(4) Å3, and Z = 2, Flack parameter: 0.0(4).

1. Introduction

The rational design and synthesis of chiral materials are topics of current interest and of great challenge in materials science, not only because of their intriguing varieties of architectures and topologies but also owing to their promising applications in enantioselective catalysis and separation [1], [2], [3]. Chirality is commonly observed in nature and life, however, its occurrence in inorganic framework materials is particularly rare. An effective and well established strategy to synthesize inorganic framework materials with the characteristics of chirality is enantioselective synthesis [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10]. For example, gallophosphate [d-Co(en)3]·[H3Ga2P4O16] (en = 1,2-diaminoethane) [5], aluminophosphate [d-Co(en)3]Al3P4O16·3H2O and zincophosphate [d-Co(en)3][Zn3(H0.5PO4)2·(HPO4)2] employing chiral metal complexes [6], [8], (R-C5H14N2)2[Ga4(C2O4)(H2PO4)2(PO4)4]·2H2O and [(1S,2S)-H2DACH][Ga2(1S,2S-DACH)(HPO4)·(PO4)2] templated by chiral organic amine [9], [10]. Recently, a few chiral inorganic framework materials have also been isolated in the absence of chiral precursors through the spontaneous resolution process [11], [12], [13], [14], [15], [16], such as a chiral vanadium phosphate [(CH3)2NH2]K4[V10O10(H2O)2(OH)4(PO4)7]·4H2O with chiral interpenetrating double helices using achiral dimethylamine as structure-directing agent and a chiral open-framework zincphosphate [H3DETA] [Zn4(PO4)3(HPO4)]·H2O (DETA = diethylenetriamine) possessing interpenetrating 8-membered helical channels templated by achiral organic amine diethylenetriamine [12], [13].

As an important class of inorganic framework materials, metal phosphites exhibit rich compositional and structural diversity, because the less available coordination centers and low average charge per oxygen on the phosphite group, compared to the phosphate group, can reduce the connection of M–O–P to generate more open interrupted frameworks or lead to interesting frameworks with unusual metal: phosphorus ratio [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [22]. However, up to now, the occurrence of chiral structure in metal phosphites is still rare, only a few transition metal phosphites with chiral feature are reported [23], [24], [25], [26], such as the first chiral zincophosphite C4N2O3H8·ZnHPO3 templated by a chiral amino acid [23], chiral open-framework cobalt phosphite [C6N2H14][Co(HPO3)2] with helical channels and zinc phosphite (C6N2H13)[Zn3(C6N2H12)(HPO3)3(H2PO3)] containing single right-handed helical chains by using achiral organic amine 1,4-diazabicyclo[2.2.2]octane as the template [24], [25]. To the best of our knowledge, there is still no report of main group metal phosphite with chiral framework.

We are interested in the design and synthesis of IIIA group metal phosphites with novel structures using the different organic templates [27], [28], [29], [30], [31]. A study of the literature of inorganic framework materials indicates that diethylenetriamine (DETA) not only is a good organic ligand coordinated with metal atom to generate extra-large pore structure, such as 24-MR gallium phosphate [32], but also is a versatile template to construct appealing architectures, such as chiral framework [11], [13], [15]. Thus we choose DETA as template to synthesize new indium phosphite. Finally, a novel 3D chiral open-framework indium phosphite In3(HPO3)6(H3DETA)·2H2O 1 is synthesized under hydrothermal conditions. Compound 1 possesses special intertwined host helical ribbons and guest helical chains. It is noted that compound 1 is the first main group metal phosphite with chiral open-framework structure. Herein, we describe the synthesis, crystal structure and characterization of compound 1. Further understanding of the hydrogen bonds between guest molecules and inorganic framework demonstrates that the flexible DETA molecule may play an important role in the formation of the chiral framework.

2. Experimental section

2.1. Synthesis

All reagents were of analytical grade. Compound 1 was hydrothermally prepared from a starting mixture containing indium trichloride (InCl3), phosphorous acid (H3PO3), diethylenetriamine (DETA) and hydrofluoric acid (HF). Indium trichloride was obtained hydrothermally by direct reaction of In2O3 with HCl at 160 °C for 3 days under autogenous pressure. Typically, 0.08 g InCl3 and 1.0 g H3PO3 was dispersed into a mixture of 8 ml water, followed by addition of 0.26 ml DETA and 0.4 ml HF (50%) with stirring. The mixture was further stirred for one hour at room temperature and heated at 160 °C for 7 days in a 18 ml Teflon-lined stainless steel autoclave (filled up to 48% volume capacity), then slowly cooling down to the ambient temperature. Colorless block crystals of compound 1 were collected by filtration, washed with distilled water and air-dried. The yield of product was 57% in weight based on indium.

Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) data were obtained using SHIMADAZU XRD-6000 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å), with the step size and the count time of 0.02° and 4 s, respectively. FT-IR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet Impact 410 spectrometer between 400 and 4000 cm−1 using the KBr pellet method. ICP–AES (inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectroscope) analysis was performed on a Perkin Elmer Optima 3300DV ICP instrument. The elemental analysis was conducted on a Perkin Elmer 2400 elemental analyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was conducted on a Perkin–Elmer TGA 7 thermogravimetric analyzer with a heating rate of 10 °C·min−1 range from room temperature to 950 °C under nitrogen gas.

2.2. Crystal structure determination

Suitable single crystal of 1 (0.10 × 0.10 × 0.10 mm3) was selected for single-crystal X-ray diffraction analysis. The intensity data were collected on a Rigaku RAXIS-RAPID IP diffractometer using graphite-monochromated Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Data processing was accomplished with the SAINT processing program. The structure was solved by direct method with the SHELXTL 97 software package [33], [34]. All the indium and phosphorus atoms were first located and then the oxygen, carbon, nitrogen were found subsequently in difference Fourier maps. The hydrogen atoms of the P–H groups and water molecules were initially located from difference Fourier maps, and the remaining hydrogen atoms were placed geometrically for the final refinement. All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically. The crystallographic data of 1 is given in Table 1. Selected bond lengths and angles are listed in Table S1.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterizations

The XRD pattern of compound 1 is in agreement with the simulated one on the basis of single-crystal structure analysis, indicating the phase purity of the as-synthesized samples (Fig. S1). The difference in reflection intensities between the simulated and experimental patterns was due to the variation in crystal orientation for the powder sample.

The ICP and elemental analyses for compound 1 give the contents of In as 35.23 wt.%, P as 18.79 wt.%, C as 5.23 wt.%, H as 2.37 wt.%, N as 4.62 wt.%. These are in agreement with those calculated values of 35.66, 19.23, 4.97, 2.71 and 4.35 wt.% of In, P, C, H and N, respectively, on the basis of the empirical formula given by single crystal structure analysis.

The IR spectrum of compound 1 clearly shows the stretching vibrations of C–H and N–H bonds locate at 2931, 3026 cm−1, and the bands at around 1547, 1486 and 1418 cm−1 are associated with the N–H and C–H bending vibrations, which are in agreement with the participation of organic template. Absorptions at 3447, 3373 and 1632 cm−1 are due to the stretching and bending vibrations of O–H bonds, respectively. The broad bands at ca. 985–1183 cm−1 are associated with the stretching vibrations of P–O bonds. The strong band at 2396 cm−1 indicates the existence of P–H bond in the crystal structure. In conclusion, the IR results show clearly the vibrations from phosphite group, organic molecule and water molecule.

Thermogravimetric analysis of compound 1 was carried out under nitrogen gas with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The TG curve is shown in Fig. 1, which exhibits three stages of weight loss in a total of 12.58%. The first weight loss of 3.72% at 170–260 °C corresponds to the removal of lattice water molecules (calcd. 3.71%). The continuous two weight loss between 350 and 930 °C is attributed to the decomposition of DETA molecules. However, the observed weight loss (8.86%) was lower than the expected (10.99%). The lower reduction is due to the retention of carbon in the solid residue (black in color). The same phenomenon is observed in gallium phosphite system [27]. Powder XRD measurement indicates that the as-made product changes to amorphous after the decomposition of the organic template.

3.2. Structural description

As shown in Fig. 2, the asymmetric unit of compound 1 contains 36 non-hydrogen atoms, of which 27 belong to the framework and 9 belong to the guest species. There are three crystallographically distinct indium and six phosphorus atoms. All In atoms adopt six coordination geometry and are linked to the adjacent P atoms via bridging oxygen atoms. The In–O bond lengths are in range of 2.083–2.190 Å. All P atoms adopt pseudo-pyramidal coordination geometry, each bonds to three oxygen atoms and one hydrogen atom. The existence P–H bonds is also confirmed by the characteristic band of phosphite anions [ν(H–P), 2396 cm−1] in the IR spectrum. The framework stoichiometry of [In3(HPO3)6] creates a net charge of −3, which is balanced by one triprotonated DETA cation. There are also two free water molecules in the asymmetric unit.

The 3D open-framework structure of compound 1 is constructed by alternation of InO6 octahedra and HPO3 pseudo pyramids as shown in Fig. 3a. It is helpful to view the framework of compound 1 as being built up from a second building unit (SBU), enneamer, composed of three InO6 octahedra and six HPO3 pseudo pyramids (Fig. 3c). Interestingly, the SBU can be understood as two different [3.3.3] propellane-like motifs sharing a common In(2) atom. Similar SBU, enneamer, has been observed in indium phosphate [35]. The enneamers connect with each other by vertex-sharing oxygen atoms from HPO3 groups to form a 4,8-net sheet (Fig. 3b), and then the adjacent 4,8-net sheets are linked with each other via vertex-sharing oxygen atoms in an ABAB stacking sequence to form a 3D open-framework along the b axis. It is noted that the 3D framework crystallizes in a space group of P21 with a two fold screw axis related to the adjacent layers, so that there are no 8-membered ring (MR) straight tunnels shown in the ac plane. The triprotonated flexible DETA cations and free water molecules occupy the flexural 8-MR channels along the [0 1 0] direction.

Alternatively, in order to further comprehend the chiral character of the open-framework, a detailed description of the structural fashion of compound 1 is shown in Fig. 4. The InO6 octahedra and HPO3 pseudo pyramids through common vertices lead to ribbons, which are arranged around 21 screw axes to form helices along the crystallographic b axis. The spiral ribbon is built up with 4-MRs of alternant InO6 octahedra and HPO3 pseudo pyramids with a pitch of 14.453 Å. Two 4-MRs are connected to form an [In3P3] SBU, and then the SBUs link via vertex-sharing oxygen atoms to form a right-handed helical ribbon which is similar to that of zincophosphate [36]. The difference between them is that in zincophosphate, the 4-MRs are linked into the infinite strings through the Zn tetrahedra. As far as we know, the helical ribbon of compound 1 is first reported in indium phosphate and phosphite. The adjacent helical ribbons connect with each other via vertex-sharing oxygen atoms to form a layer containing 10-MR windows (Fig. 4a). Finally, the neighboring layers are interconnected by HPO3 pseudo pyramids (HP(3)O3, HP(5)O3, HP(6)O3) to form an infinite 3D open-framework (see Fig. 4b).

A study of previous literature demonstrates that hydrogen bonds play a significant role in determining stereospecificity between the template and the host framework in open-framework metal phosphate/phosphite [37], [38], and the flexible DETA molecule may conduce to the formation of the chiral framework structures [11], [13], [15]. However, in some chiral framework structures templated by DETA, the DETA molecule is disordered and can not be exactly located in structures [11], [15], therefore, the H-bonds existing between inorganic framework and guest molecule or between guest molecules can not be analyzed. In crystal structure of compound 1, the DETA molecule is clearly observed, so the detailed H-bonding interactions can be summarized as listed in Table 2. Extensive H-bonding interactions exist between oxygen atoms from inorganic framework and nitrogen atoms from the triprotonated DETA cations and the average N…O distance is 2.644(2) Å. Because DETA molecules have a steric effect and might impose an extra force to the [In3P3] SBU through strong H-bonding interactions, these lead to a highly distorted SBU as shown in Fig. 4a. Such a highly distorted SBU is the basic unit forming the helical ribbon and 3D chiral framework in the structure of compound 1. Thus, it is believed that this H-bonding interaction between DETA molecules and inorganic framework play a key role in the formation of 3D chiral framework structure. Strong H-bonding interactions also exist between oxygen atom (O1 W) from free water molecules and nitrogen atoms from the triprotonated DETA cations, where the O atom acts as the H-bonding acceptor [N(3)–H(3C)…O(1 W) = 2.802(7) Å, N(1)–H (1A)…O(1 W) = 2.894(7) Å]. Interestingly, the organic amine cations interact with water molecules via H-bonds to form a hydrogen-bonded helical chain, which follows the two fold screw axis with a pitch of 14.4530 Å along the crystallographic b axis. As far as we know, such distinctive H-bonded helical chain is quite rare in metal phosphate/phosphite [39]. Moreover, the helical chain of guest hydrogen-bonded systems is exclusively right-handed and is consistent with the right-handed helical ribbon of host inorganic framework. These two helices are further intertwined with each other along the b axis. It is worth noting that the intertwined left-handed host and guest helices are found in its enantiomer (as shown in Fig. 5). The other free water molecule decorates the H-bonded helical chain via H-bonding interaction between the nitrogen atoms from the triprotonated DETA cations and O(2 W) atom [N(1) − H(1C)...O(2 W) = 2.788(7) Å]. Likewise, there are also H-bonding interactions between water molecules and oxygen atoms of inorganic framework and the average O…O separation is 2.899(9) Å. The existence of H-bonding interactions can be confirmed by the IR spectrum, because the formation of H-bond often affects the position and intensity of absorption peaks, and usually causes the vibration frequency shift to low wave number. In order to better understand the hydrogen bonding, the characteristic peaks of free water molecule, DETA molecule and compound 1 are assigned and compared in Table 3. As listed in Table 3, the stretching vibrations of N–H bonds of free DETA molecule locate at 3350 and 3281 cm−1, while in compound 1 the stretching vibrations of N–H bonds at 3026 cm−1. The stretching and bending vibrations of O–H bonds of free water molecule locate at 3200–3500 and 1660 cm−1, respectively, whereas in compound 1 the stretching and bending vibrations of O–H bonds occur at 3447, 3373 and 1632 cm−1. These results show that compared to free water and DETA molecules, the vibration frequency of some bands such as N–H and O–H bands in compound 1 is shifted to lower wave number. It further confirms existence of H-bonding interactions between inorganic framework and guest molecule or between guest molecules in compound 1.

The fascinating chiral structural features of the known zeolites and related inorganic open-framework materials include helical chains, helical ribbons, helical channels and chiral network [4]. Moreover, one framework can show more than one chiral features and one chiral feature can contain various composition. The notable example is CoAl-CJ40, which contains 10-MR helical channels and double helical chains [40]. Another important examples is [H3DETA]·[Zn4(PO4)3(HPO4)]·H2O which possesses the interestingly intersecting helical channels. Though using the same achiral organic amine as template, and crystallizing in the same space group, but [H3DETA]·[Zn4(PO4)3(HPO4)]·H2O and compound 1 show the different structure features. Moreover, to our knowledge, most of chiral open-framework metal phosphites present helicity of inorganic framework and only a zincophosphite [Zn(C8H8N2)(HPO3)] contains hydrogen-bonded helix [23], [24], [25], [26]. The hydrogen-bonded helix of [Zn(C8H8N2)(HPO3)] is distinctly different to that of compound 1. In 1D chiral structure of [Zn(C8H8N2)(HPO3)], the organic ligands interact with zincophosphite chains via H-bonds forming two kinds of the hydrogen-bonded helical chains which are exclusively right-handed, whereas in compound 1 the right-handed hydrogen-bonded helical chains are formed via the H-bonds interactions only existing between guest molecules. So this type of the intertwined host and guest helices in the framework of compound 1 is first reported in metal phosphite. Optical rotation measurement shows that the products of 1 is not optically active, therefore the bulk products should be a 50:50 mixture of enantiomers exhibiting a racemic character.

By calculating from single crystal structure with PLATON, the framework of compound 1 possesses 387.7 Å3 (32.3% per unit cell volume) void volume after removal of guest molecules. To study porous properties, adsorption study with N2 gas was carried out with the activated 1. The adsorption isotherm of N2 (Fig. S2) shows that activated 1 does not take up N2 at 77 K, indicating that the kinetic diameter (3.6 Å) of N2 molecule is larger than the window dimension of the channels of compound 1. This behavior has already been described in other microporous materials with pore sizes of less than 10 Å [41], [42].

4. Conclusions

Employing achiral diethylenetriamine as template, a new chiral open-framework indium phosphite In3(HPO3)6(H3DETA)·2H2O 1 has been synthesized under hydrothermal conditions, which is the first chiral open-framework main group metal phosphite. It is noted that right-handed helical ribbons are presented in the inorganic framework while guest right-handed hydrogen-bond helical chains are self-assembled between organic template molecules and free water molecules. Moreover, the helix of the guest molecules is consistent with the helix of inorganic framework, both of which are intertwined. This work has further demonstrated that the flexible DETA molecule may conduce to the formation of the chiral framework. Further understanding of the interaction between organic guest molecules and the inorganic framework in the determination of the chiral structures will be helpful in the rational design and synthesis of chiral inorganic open-framework materials.
