    Abstract

    Two novel three-dimensional 3d-4f heterometallic-organic frameworks {[TbZn(BPDC)2(μ2-H2O)Cl(H2O)3]·5H2O·0.5DMA}n (1) and {[TbCo(BPDC)2(μ2-H2O)Cl(H2O)3]·8H2O·0.5DMA}n (2) have been synthesized and structurally characterized. They are isostructural and exhibit irregular one-dimensional channels with a large potential pore volume. Importantly, both of them demonstrate excellent catalytic activity for the chemical fixation of CO2 into cyclic carbonates under mild conditions. Moreover, the catalytic recycling of compound 1 as a representative example was explored; it can be easily separated and reused for at least four times without significant reduction in catalytic ability.

     Special Issue

    Published as part of a Crystal Growth and Designvirtual special issue on Crystal Engineering of Nanoporous Materials for Gas Storage and Separation

    Synopsis

    Two three-dimensional 3d-4f heterometallic metal organic frameworks demonstrate excellent catalytic ability for CO2 conversion into cyclic carbonates with high yield, and compound 1 can be used as a recoverable heterogeneous catalyst with high activity.

    Carbon dioxide (CO2), as the major greenhouse gas, has been considered to cause great environmental concerns, such as ocean acidity and melting of glaciers.(1, 2) The CO2 emission mainly originates from burning of fossil fuels and accumulates in the atmosphere at an alarming pace.(3) It is imperative to explore CO2 capture and sequestration (CCS) technologies to decrease CO2 from emission sources.(4-6) To date, extensive efforts have been made to obtain new materials for advanced CCS technologies.(7-9) Additionally, as a renewable carbon feedstock, CO2 can be utilized to transform into high value chemicals.(10-16) Particularly, the cyclic carbonates formed by the coupling of CO2 with epoxides have attracted significant attention owing to its 100% atom economic nature.(17, 18) Besides, cyclic carbonates possess potential extensive application as electrolytes in lithium-ion batteries, precursors for polymeric materials, intermediates of fine chemicals, organic solvents, and so on.(19-21) Impressively, various catalytic systems have been developed for CO2 fixation into cyclic carbonates, including homogeneous catalysts and heterogeneous catalysts, such as ionic liquids,(22, 23) alkali metal salts,(24) metal oxides,(25) and functional polymers.(26, 27) Thus, it is more favorable to develop materials which can efficiently capture CO2 and simultaneously catalyze CO2 conversion.
    Emerging as a new class of functional materials, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs),(28-33) which feature tailorable structures, exceptional properties,(34-41) have been proposed to be promising materials to meet the dual challenge of CO2 capture and conversion for the following reasons: (a) the porous MOFs can significantly absorb CO2,(42-45) (b) the well ordered porous structures can enrich substrates and facilitate the accessibility of substrates to active sites, (c) the metal ions can serve as Lewis acid sites to efficiently activate epoxide,(46-49) (d) stable MOFs, as heterogeneous catalysts, retain excellent catalytic performance after being successively reused. Thus, it is of vital importance to develop MOF-based catalysts for efficient CO2 capture and conversion.(50-53)
    In this contribution, two 3d-4f heterometallic MOFs {[TbZn(BPDC)2(μ2-H2O)Cl(H2O)3]·5H2O·0.5DMA}n (1) and {[TbCo(BPDC)2(μ2-H2O)Cl(H2O)3]·8H2O·0.5DMA}n (2) were obtained based on 5,5′-dicarboxylate-2,2′-dipyridine (H2BPDC) and structurally characterized. They are isostructural and exhibit one-dimensional (1D) open channels with a large potential pore volume. Furthermore, both of them exhibit excellent catalytic performance for CO2 conversion into cyclic carbonates in the absence of solvent under mild conditions, and compound 1 can be used as a recoverable heterogeneous catalyst with high activity.

    Synthesis of {[TbZn(BPDC)2(μ2-H2O)Cl(H2O)3]·5H2O·0.5DMA}n (1)

    A mixture of Tb(ClO4)3·6H2O (28.5 mg, 0.05 mmol), H2BPDC (24.4 mg, 0.1 mmol), ZnCl2 (6.8 mg, 0.05 mmol), H2O (1 mL), and DMA (4 mL) were added into a 8 mL glass vial.     Catalytic Performance Evaluation

    In each individual reaction, 2 mmol of epoxide substrate and 20 mg of compound 1 catalyst (1 mol % based on Tb metal center) together with 32 mg of tetrabutylammonium bromide (TBAB, 5 mol %) were added into a 10 mL reaction tube. The tube was purged with CO2 at 1 atm under a solvent free environment and then was stirred (600 rpm) for 12 h to complete the reaction. After the catalytic reaction, the catalyst was separated by centrifugation, and the product yields were determined by 1H NMR. To explore the recyclability, the catalyst was isolated from the reaction solution, washed with CH2Cl2 three times, dried at ambient temperature, and then reused in the next run of the reaction.

    Structure

    The X-ray single-crystal diffraction analysis reveals that 1 and 2 are isostructural. They both crystallize in the monoclinic system with space group C2/c. Thus, compound 1 as a representative example is selected to describe the crystal structures in detail. The asymmetric unit in 1 consists of one Tb3+ ion, one Zn2+ion, one μ2-H2O molecule, and two BPDC2– anions. Eight-coordinated Tb3+ has an triangular dodecahedron geometry, which is achieved by four carboxylic oxygen atoms, two water molecules, and two μ2-H2O molecules (Figure 1). The framework is composed of two distinct building blocks [Tb2(μ2-H2O)2(COO)8(H2O)4] (abbreviated as [Tb2] unit) and [Zn(BPDC)2(H2O)Cl] (abbreviated as [Zn(BPDC)2] unit) (Figure S2). Two Tb3+ ions are bridged into a binuclear [Tb2] unit by two μ2-H2O molecules. Four nitrogen atoms from two BPDC2– ions, one water molecule, and one chloride ion complete the six-coordinated environment of Zn2+ anions (Figure 1a), forming a [Zn(BPDC)2(H2O)Cl] motif, which connects the [Tb2] unit into a three-dimensional (3D) neutral framework (Figure 1b). The framework possesses 1D irregular channels along the c direction with a diameter of about 6 Å (Figure 1b). Additionally, the potential pore volume of 1 is as high as 46%, calculated by the PLATON program.
    In order to simplify the complicated framework, each [Tb2] unit is considered as an 8-connected node which links with eight [Zn(BPDC)2] units, and each [Zn(BPDC)2] unit is regarded as a 4-connected node which bridges four [Tb2] units. Thus, the framework can be simplified into a (4,8)-connected topological structure with topology symbol of flu(54) (Figure 1c, Figure S2). Thermogravimetric (TGA) analysis and elemental microanalyses (EA) indicate that five water molecules and half of one N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA) molecule are in the asymmetric unit.

    Powder X-ray Diffraction Analyses (PXRD) and Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA)

    PXRD analyses were carried out at ambient conditions. The PXRD patterns of as-synthesized samples conform with the simulated ones from the single-crystal data suggesting the high phase purity of the sample (Figure S3). As shown in the Figure S4, the TGA curve of compound 1 exhibits two steps of weight loss. The first weight loss of 20.3% between 36 and 190 °C corresponds to the loss of five free water molecules, half of one free DMA molecule, and three coordinated water molecules (calcd: 19.9%). Subsequently, the TGA curve mainly stays in a plateau, indicating the high thermal stability of the framework 1. Then, the second weight loss above 350 °C stems from the decomposition of the 3D framework. As to compound 2, the TGA curve also exhibits two steps of weight loss. The first weight loss of 24.4% between 46 and 220 °C corresponds to the loss of eight free water molecules, half of one free DMA molecule and three coordinated water molecules (calcd: 24.2%). Subsequently, the TGA curve mainly stays in a plateau, indicating the high thermal stability of the framework 2. Then, the second weight loss above 350 °C stems from the decomposition of the 3D framework.

    Gas Adsorption

    Inspired by its large void volume, the gas adsorption property of 1 as a representative example was studied. Before the measurement, about 100 mg crystals of compound 1 was immersed in CH2Cl2 for 6 days during which the supernatant was exchanged once every 8 h. Then the crystals were outgassed under a dynamic vacuum at 25 °C for 12 h to afford 1a. At 298 K, the CO2 adsorption property was studied in a relative pressure ranging from 10–3 to 1 atm. As shown in Figure S5, the adsorption measurement shows a linear increase with a maximum adsorption CO2 capacity of 25 cm3/g at point P/P0 = 1.0, which is relatively low compared to other MOFs materials.(5) It is interesting that the desorption isotherm of 1a does not retrace the adsorption one, showing a large hysteresis and an abrupt drop at point P/P0 = 0.1. It is very difficult to desorb all of the included CO2, possibly suggesting the strong chemisorption interaction occurs between CO2 and the framework 1a.(45, 55)

    Catalytic Properties

    The moderate CO2 uptake properties of compound 1 motivated us to investigate its heterogeneous catalytic ability for cycloaddition of CO2 with epoxides to generate cyclic carbonates. The styrene oxide was chosen as the model substrate to explore the optimized reaction condition. During our investigations of various reports, we chose relatively mild conditions in the presence of 2.5 mol % TBAB to study the catalytic ability of compound 1, and the results were summarized in Table 1. It can be seen that the yield gradually increased when the temperature was increased, but the yield was only 77% at 70 °C (Table 1, entries 1–3). In order to avoid much energy consume from higher temperature, the TBAB amount was increased to 5 mol % (Table 1, entries 4–6). The results revealed that the optimized condition should be 20 mg of compound 1 and 5 mol % TBAB at 70 °C (Table 1, entry 6). Under the optimized conditions, the conversion reached over 99%. Comparably, the yield was less than 1% when TBAB was absent in the system, and the yield was only 57% when compound 1 was absent in the system under the employed reaction conditions (Table 1, entries 7 and 8). Thus, both compound 1 and TBAB performed indispensable roles in the reaction. It is proposed that metal ions serve as Lewis acid sites to efficiently activate epoxide, and TBAB is employed as a cocatalyst in the experiment in the previous reports.(50, 51) The synergistic effect of compound 1 and TBAB is the main reason for the high catalytic activity of the catalyst system under mild conditions.

The oxyphilic and highly Lewis acidic nature make rare metals potentially good catalysts for the activation of epoxides and the subsequent coupling with CO2.(56, 57) Furthermore, one isostructural MOF (compound 2) was synthesized and investigated for its catalytic ability in the catalytic system.

In order to avoid much energy consumption from higher temperature, the TBAB amount was increased to 5 mol % (Table 2, entries 2–5). Thus, the excellent catalytic performance can be ascribed to significant synergistic effects between the Tb centers and transition metal (Zn, Co) centers.

    To test its recyclability, taking the CO2 cycloaddition with styrene epoxide as an example, catalyst 1 (as a representative example) was successively reused for four runs without any significant decrease in the catalytic efficiency (Figure 2). Additionally, the structure integrity of compound 1 after catalysis was proved by TGA analysis (Figure S6). Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) studies revealed that only trace amounts of leakage from the robust framework occurred, demonstrating its high recoverable performance (Table 4). Overall, the high efficiency and good recyclability make compound 1 appealing among heterogeneous catalysts for chemical fixation of CO2 into cyclic carbonates.

    On the basis of some previous reports,(51) a tentative mechanism was proposed for the cycloaddition of CO2 and epoxides into cyclic carbonates catalyzed by the strong Lewis acid based catalyst 1 (as a representative example), as illustrated in Figure 3.
    First, the framework enriched CO2 and substrates. Then the reaction was initiated by binding the epoxide with Lewis acid metal sites through the oxygen atom of epoxide to activate the epoxy ring. Thus, the C–O bond of the epoxide is weakened, partly due to electron transfer from oxygen atom to metal center. Subsequently, the Br– from TBAB attacked the less-hindered carbon atom of the coordinated epoxide to open the epoxide ring, due to its lower steric effect and higher positive charge. Then the carbon atom of CO2 interacted with the oxygen anion of the opened epoxide ring and positively charged carbon of the epoxide interacted with one oxygen atom from CO2, forming an alkylcarbonate anion. Finally, the corresponding cyclic carbonate was obtained through the intramolecular ring closure. It could be deduced that the synergistic effect of the Lewis acid metal sites and the moderate uptake of CO2 promote the cycloaddition reaction, leading to high catalytic activity of compound 1 for CO2 conversion into cyclic carbonates.

    In summary, two microporous 3d-4f heterometallic MOFs were obtained under solvothermal conditions. They are isostructural and feature a large potential pore volume. Furthermore, both of them exhibit excellent performance as catalysts for chemical fixation of CO2 into cyclic carbonates under mild conditions. Significantly, compound 1 could be easily separated and retained its high catalytic activity after at least four runs of recyclings.
