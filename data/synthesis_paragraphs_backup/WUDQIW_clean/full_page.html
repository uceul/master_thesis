Interconversion of two new nickel(II) coordination polymers with different topologies: synthesis, structure and magnetic properties†

Bo-Wen Hu , Jiong-Peng Zhao , Qian Yang , Fu-Chen Liu and Xian-He Bu *
Department of Chemistry, Nankai University, Tianjin, 300071, China. E-mail: buxh@nankai.edu.cn; Fax: +86-22-23502458

Received 15th May 2009 , Accepted 29th June 2009

First published on 3rd August 2009

A new three-dimensional (3D) weak ferromagnet with cationic (10,3)-a topology, [Ni(L)Cl·2H2O]n (1) (L = 1,2,4-triazol-1-yl-acetate), was obtained by solvothermal reaction. In different solvent media, another 2D complex [NiL2·2H2O]n (2) with 44-sql topology was obtained and structurally characterized. Furthermore, the interconversion between complexes 1 and 2 was observed.

Introduction

In recent decades, the construction of functional coordination polymers has attracted intense attention in materials chemistry because of their potential applications such as gas storage, ion exchange, catalysis and magnetic materials.1–3 In this context, some progress has been achieved in the combination of metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) and magnetic materials by molecular assembly.4 One of the main challenges of this field is to investigate structural modulation of such frameworks which can guide the overall properties of the materials. The factors that affect such materials include bridging ligands, metal ions, templates and synthesis conditions.5–7 Hydrothermal and solvothermal methods are well used to obtain intriguing structural frameworks with desired properties because the high temperature and high pressure of these methods may lead to the formation of coordination polymers with novel topologies and specific properties.8Solvent is one of the important factors affecting the formation of complexes, but in the studies of structure transformation of coordination polymers,9,10 the solvent effect in the interconversion between two-dimensional (2D) and 3D coordination polymers under hydrothermal conditions has been seldom reported.

On the other hand, investigations on magneto-structural correlations are always the most important part in the studies of magnetic materials.11 The selection of bridging ligands is very important since it has a great effect on the magnetic MOF structures and properties. The building blocks with multi-functional organic carboxylates can coordinate to metal ions in different ways and result in metal complexes with interesting magnetic properties.12,13 Thus 1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl-acetic acid (HL) with rich coordination modes containing both carboxylate and azole moieties, and provision of efficient pathways of magnetic exchange has been chosen to construct new magnetic materials.

Herein, we report a new 3D complex, [Ni(L)Cl·2H2O]n (1), synthesized by solvothermal conditions and a new 2D complex, [NiL2·2H2O]n (2), obtained from hydrothermal conditions. The interconversion between complexes 1 and 2 was observed. Complex 1 with cationic (10,3)-a (srs) network topology exhibits spin-canted antiferromagnetism.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All the reagents for synthesis were obtained commercially and used as received. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C analyzer. Ligand HL was synthesized by our reported procedure.14 The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) patterns were recorded on a Rigaku D/Max-2500 diffractometer at 40 kV, 100 mA for a Cu-target tube and a graphite monochromator. Simulation of the XRPD spectra was carried out by the single-crystal data and diffraction-crystal module of the Mercury (Hg) program available free of charge via the Internet at http://www.iucr.org. Magnetic data were collected using crushed crystal samples on a Quantum Design MPMS XL-7 SQUID magnetometer. The experimental susceptibilities were corrected for the diamagnetism of the constituent atoms by using Pascal's constants.

Synthesis of complexes 1 and 2

[Ni(L)·Cl·H2O]n (1). The single crystals of complex 1 were prepared by solvothermal reaction: a mixture of NiCl2·6H2O (2 mmol) and sodium 1,2,4-triazolylacetate (Na+L−) (4 mmol) in 15 mL EtOH:H2O (v:v = 1:1) was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), heated at 140 °C for 2 days under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Blue block crystals of 1 were harvested in ca. ∼25% yield based on NiII ions. Anal. calcd for C4H8NiN3O4Cl: C, 18.75; H, 3.15; N, 16.40. Found: C, 18.43; H, 3.41; N, 16.18%.
[NiL2·H2O]n (2). 2 was obtained by a similar hydrothermal method as described for 1 except for using H2O instead of 1:1 EtOH:H2O. Blue block crystals of 2 were harvested in ca. ∼30% yield based on NiII ions. Anal. calcd for C8H12NiN6O6: C, 27.70; H, 3.49; N, 24.22. Found: C, 27.54; H, 3.48; N, 23.66%.

Interconversion between complexes 1 and 2

As shown in Scheme 1, a mixture of complex 2 and 4 eq. of NiCl2·6H2O in 15 mL EtOH:H2O (v:v = 1:1) was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL), heated at 140 °C for 2 days under autogenous pressure, and then cooled to room temperature. Complex 1 was obtained from 2. Then complex 1 could transform back to 2 by using 140 °C hydrothermal conditions without any other addition. Single crystals from the interconversion were too small to be analysed by single-crystal X-ray diffraction, so XRPD experiments were used instead.

X-Ray data collection and structure determinations

Single-crystal X-ray diffraction measurements for 1 and 2 were carried out on a SCX-mini diffractometer (Rigaku) equipped with a graphite crystal monochromator situated in the incident beam. The determinations of unit cell parameters and data collections were performed with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) at 293(2) K and unit cell dimensions were obtained with least-squares refinements. The program SAINT (Bruker, SAINT Software Reference Manual, Madison, Wisconsin, 1998) was used for integration of the diffraction profiles and semi-empirical absorption corrections were applied using the SADABS program (Bruker, SADABS: Program for Empirical Absorption Correction of Area Detector Data, Madison, Wisconsin, 1998). The structure was solved by direct methods using the SHELXS program of the SHELXTL package and refined with SHELXL (G. M. Sheldrick, SHELXTL Version 6.1. Program for Solution and Refinement of Crystal Structures, University of Göttingen, Germany, 1998). The final refinement was performed by full matrix least-squares methods with anisotropic thermal parameters for non-hydrogen atoms on F2. Results and discussion

Synthesis

Solvent is one of the most important factors for the synthesis of coordination polymers.9,10 In our synthesis, we found that EtOH:H2O (v:v = 1:1) was the most suitable medium for the synthesis of complex 1 and the interconversion from complex 2 to 1, while the preparation of complex 2 and the interconversion from complex 1 to 2 could only be carried out in H2O (Scheme 1). We also tried to use other solvents such as pure ethanol, methanol and mixed solvents of ethanol, methanol, acetonitrile and acetone with different ratios to obtain complexes 1 and 2, and for observing the interconversion between the two complexes; unfortunately, our efforts were not successful. Thus two distinct frameworks with (10,3)-a and 44-sql topologies were obtained from different solvent media. In the interconversion process from 2 to 1, 4 eq. of NiCl2 was added in the solvothermal reaction, since chloride anions may also be the key point to form complex 1 probably due to charge balance. Reversibly, under the 140 °C hydrothermal conditions, the 3D srs framework of 1 was destroyed and then the 2D structure of 2 was reformed.

Structure descriptions

[Ni(L)·Cl·H2O]n (1). Single-crystal X-ray diffraction of 1 reveals that it crystallizes in the orthorhombic chiral space groupP212121 and has a 3D framework structure. In the crystallographic asymmetric unit there exists one NiII ion, one L ligand, two coordinated water molecules and one chlorine ion (Fig. 1a). The NiII ion lies in a general position and is coordinated by two O and two N atoms from three L ligands and two water molecules, giving an octahedral coordination environment. The octahedra show rhombic distortion, with two short Ni–O distances [Ni–O2W = 2.0549(15), Ni–O2A = 2.0587(13) Å] and two long Ni–N distances [Ni–N2 = 2.1027(15), Ni–N3B = 2.0928(14) Å], the axis of the octahedron is O1–Ni–O1W [Ni–O1W = 2.0752(16), Ni–O1 = 2.0687(13) Å] and the angle of O1–Ni–O1W is 177.46(6)° (Table 2). This distortion is an important feature for the analysis of the canting magnetic characteristic (vide infra). On the other hand, the angle between the two rhombuses of the octahedra from two adjacent NiII ions is 30.3°. Around one NiII ion, one L ligand chelates the NiII ion in a κN,O-mode, while another one binds NiII in a monodentate mode via one carboxylate O atom, and the third binds via one 4-N atom. Thus each L adopts a µ3-κN,O:κO′:κN″ coordination mode to bridge three NiII ions: these firstly form a 1D zig-zag chain through µ-2-4-triazolate, and then form an unusual non-interpenetrated chiral 3D framework by the bidentate coordination of carboxylate O atoms bridging adjacent chains. Chlorine ions are located in the cavities of the cationic srs framework within hydrogen bonding distance to the oxygen atom of a nearby water molecule, and result in a charge-balanced complex (Fig. 1b and Table 3).

Topologically, each L ligand provides three donor sites and connects three NiII ions, so each L and NiII ion can be defined as a three-connected node at a ratio of 1:1, which generated a new (10,3)-a (srs) network (Fig. 1c). The (10,3) nets are notably interesting because many of them hold helical channels or voids, and in some cases they are chiral. Most reported srs have been neutral until now: the main topologic network in 1 is positive. Thus, this is a new example of a metal-organic coordination polymer adopting a cationic non-interpenetrated chiral (10,3)-a (srs) topological network.

[NiL2·H2O]n (2). 2 crystallizes in orthorhombic symmetry with space groupP21/c. X-Ray single-crystal diffraction analyses reveal that 2 displays an infinite rhombic grid structure consisting of NiII ions and bridging L ligands. As shown in Fig. 2a, NiII is six coordinated by two nitrogen atoms of the imidazole, two oxygen atoms of carboxylate from four L ligands and two oxygen atoms from water molecules. The ligand L acts as a bridge in such a way that N of the imidazole ring coordinates to one NiII ion and one O of carboxylate bridges to a second one. All the bond lengths around NiII ions are within the normal range (Table 2). The NiII ions are interconnected by four L ligands to form a 2D layer (Fig. 2b) with 44-sql topology (Fig. 2c). The adjacent 2D layers are parallel resulting in a 3D structure.
Fig. 2 (a) The asymmetric unit of 2. NiII ions are shown as dark green, C as light green, O as red, and N as blue; (b) the 2D network of 2, viewed from the c axis; (c) schematic view of the 2D 44-sql network of 2.

NiII ions in complexes 1 and 2 are all hexacoordinated, in a distorted octahedral fashion in 1 and a more regular octahedron in 2. The ligands provide three donor sites and connect three NiII ions in 1, and provide two donor sites in 2. Furthermore, the coordinated water molecules occupy different positions in the octahedral arrangement. In 1, two water molecules are at axial and equatorial coordination sites, but in 2, two water molecules are opposite at the equatorial coordination sites. Complex 1 is in a chiral space group with a Flack parameter of 0.015(10), however the circular dichroism (CD) spectrum of 1 does not show an obvious signal.

XRPD results

In order to confirm the phase purity of the bulk materials, X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) experiments were carried out for complexes 1 and 2. The experimental and computer-simulated patterns of 1 and 2 are shown in Fig. 3. Although the experimental patterns have a few un-indexed diffraction lines and some are slightly broadened in comparison with those simulated from the single-crystal models, it still can be considered that the bulk synthesized materials and the as-grown crystals are homogeneous for complexes 1 and 2.

Magnetic properties of complex 1

Magnetic susceptibility data were collected for crushed crystalline samples of 1. The temperature dependence of the magnetic susceptibility was measured at 2 kOe in the 2–300 K temperature range for 1. The χmT vs. T plot is shown in Fig. 4. The value of χmT per NiII at 300 K is 1.38 cm3 mol−1 K. On cooling the χmT value decreases gradually until it reaches a minimum value at 8 K, which clearly indicates antiferromagnetic coupling between the metal ions in the lattice. There are two kinds of magnetic exchange pathways, Ni–Ni by carboxylate and Ni–Ni by µ-2-4-triazolate, and both have to be antiferromagnetic (AF). With continued cooling, the χmT value increases instantly indicating a magnetic phase transition. The field dependence of the magnetization, M(H), at 2 K of 1 (Fig. 4 inset) shows an instant increase of M/Nβ at very low field, and then a sigmoidal curve of the reduced magnetization, without achieving saturation. This shape is clearly indicative of a hysteresis plot (Fig. 5). This plot indicates long-range magnetic ordering in the 3D network of 1. New proofs have corroborated this magnetic ordering. In the low-temperature region, zero-field-cooled and field-cooled measurements (ZFC/FC, Fig. 6) indicate the 3D long-range antiferromagnetic ordering with weak ferromagnetism, and the Néel temperature is 7 K. Furthermore the ac magnetic susceptibility data showed an out-of-phase signal at 7 K indicating the weak ferromagnetic state in this material (Fig. 7).

Because of the Dzyaloshinski-Moriya interaction, the coupling energy is minimized when two spins are perpendicular to each other, which cants the spins. It is well known that if ∠M–X–M is not 180° and there is only one bridging X atom between M centers, the occurrence of spin canting is always observed. In some AF three-atom bridged system, canting also often happened, for the same symmetry reason.15 In this srs structure, there are two types of three-atom bridges according to the theory above, and the angle of the tilted equatorial planes of the octahedra from the two adjacent NiII ions is around 30° as mentioned above, which would be propitious to antisymmetric exchange. From the structural point of view, complex 1 has a swinging distorted octahedron, which may lead to the greater antisymmetric exchange causing weak ferromagnetism at low temperature. Hence, the tilted equatorial planes around the adjacent metal ions resulting in antisymmetric exchange have the dominant effect on the antiferromagnetic coupling with spin canting for complex 1.

Conclusion

Two new 1,2,4-triazolylacetate-based nickel(II) coordination polymers 1 and 2 have been synthesized, one of which adopts the rare cationic (10,3)-a (srs) topology while the other one exhibits a 2D 44-sql network. The interconversion between 1 and 2 was observed. Complex 1 displayed unexpected canted antiferromagnetism. This work may provide potential applications of triazole-based ligands in constructing magnetic coordination polymers, and solvent-dependent structural diversification and transformation may offer new information on the design and crystal engineering of such magnetic materials.
