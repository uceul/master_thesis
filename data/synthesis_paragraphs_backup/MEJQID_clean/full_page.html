Guest selectivity of a porous tetrahedral imidazolate framework material during self-assembly†

Fei Wang , Hui Yang , Yao Kang and Jian Zhang *
State Key Laboratory of Structural Chemistry, Fujian Institute of Research on the Structure of Matter, The Chinese Academy of Sciences, Fuzhou, Fujian 350002, P. R. China. E-mail: zhj@fjirsm.ac.cn

Received 10th July 2012 , Accepted 6th August 2012

First published on 7th August 2012

Presented here is the unusual guest selectivity observed in the self-assembly process of a porous tetrahedral imidazolate framework material [Zn(ad)(int)] (TIF-1, ad = adeninate, int = isonictinate). Four distinct solvent molecules, such as N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), ethyleneurea (e-urea), N-methy1-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA), can be trapped in the same in situ generated TIF-1 framework, respectively, which are identified by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Complicated mixed solvent systems are investigated to understand the guest selectivity of TIF-1. The results reveal TIF-1 has strict guest selectivity following the sequence DMF > e-urea > NMP > DMA. Moreover, the activated TIF-1 after methanol-exchange exhibits high H2 and CO2 adsorption capacity and remarkable selectivity of CO2 over N2.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are a new class of network solids that have great potential in specific applications like gas separation, storage and purification.1 The recent upsurge in MOFs reflects their application in the storage and separation of gases such as hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide,2 as well as organic compounds such as aromatic hydrocarbons.3 At present it is a challenge for chemists to predict and control the structures of MOFs, owing to the complicated influential factors during the assembly process, such as the organic ligands, the nature of the metal ions and the experimental conditions like solvent, metal-to-ligand ratio, pH and the reaction temperature.4 Among these factors, the effect of solvent plays dominant role on determining the structures of MOFs.5

Some organic reagents, such as N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF), ethyleneurea (e-urea), N-methy1-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMA), are important solvents and widely used for the construction of functional MOFs.5d,6 Except the solvent role, these molecules can also be trapped into porous frameworks and act as guests. Of particular interest is the guest selectivity of a given MOF in mixed organic solvents, which is closely associated with interesting host–guest chemistry. However, rarely is effort focused on such host–guest selectivity during the self-assembly process of a MOF, because a distinct solvent system often leads to the generation of different MOFs. Although mixed organic solvents are commonly employed for the synthesis of MOFs,1 the guest selectivity of a given MOF toward different solvent molecules remains rarely explored.

In this work, we report unusual guest selectivity during the self-assembly process of a tetrahedral imidazolate framework [Zn(ad)(int)] (TIF-1, ad = adeninate, int = isonictinate) . It is extraordinary that the crystallization of TIF-1 is independent on the use of what kind of solvents and it can be successfully generated from distinct mixed solvent systems. The framework of TIF-1 is ready to trap different solvent guests into its free pores. Thus, a series of solvent guest encapsulated host–guest compounds TIF-1·guest (guest = DMF, e-urea, NMP, DMA, see Scheme 1) are successfully synthesized and structurally characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction. Moreover, TIF-1 has strict guest selectivity following the sequence DMF > e-urea > NMP > DMA. It is noteworthy that the activated TIF-1 after methanol-exchange exhibits high H2 and CO2 adsorption capacity and remarkable selectivity of CO2 over N2.

Experimental

Materials and general methods

All chemicals were obtained from commercial sources and used without further purification. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were measured on a Vario MICRO E III elemental analyzer. The IR spectra (KBr pellets) were recorded on a Magna 750 FT-IR spectrophotometer. Powder X-ray diffraction data were recorded on a Rigaku MultiFlex diffractometer with a scan speed of 5° min−1. Thermal stability studies were carried out on a NETSCHZ STA-449C thermoanalyzer under N2 (30–800 °C range) at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. Fluorescence spectra were measured with a HORIBA Jobin-Yvon FluoroMax-4 spectrometer. The gases adsorption isotherms were measured by using ASAP-2020 volumetric adsorption equipment.

Results and discussion

The structural details of TIF-1 have been reported previously by us,7 and it features a three-dimensional tetrahedral framework from the cooperative assembly of the μ2-bridging ad and int ligands with the Zn2+ ions. In this work, we focus on its guest selectivity toward different solvent molecules, because its large rhombic channels with dimension of 8.0 × 7.0 Å can be filled with a series of structurally ordered solvent molecules (Fig. 1a–d). The relatively high quality and resolution of the diffraction data allow for unambiguous assignment of the framework conformation and the arrangement of the guest molecules within the cavities.
Fig. 1 TIF-1 with different guests (a, DMF; b, e-urea; c, DMA; and d, NMP).

Single-crystal structural analysis indicates that TIF-1 does not show significant guest-independent cell volume variation (Table 1). Only 4.9% cell variation (from 1682.98(7) to 1765.39(14) Å3) is observed between different guest-loaded TIF-1 frameworks. That means that guests have no profound effect on this host framework. Upon close inspection of the structures, we found that there are intramolecular hydrogen bonds between the –NH2 groups of the ad ligands and the carboxyl O atoms of the int ligands in the framework, which may be useful to strengthen stability of the host framework.

The most interesting feature of this work is the guest selectivity during the self-assembly process of TIF-1 (Table 2). First of all, for the synthesis of TIF-1·DMF, many other solvents, such as water, methanol, and other amide solvents, can be added into this synthesis system. Whether other solvents exist or not, TIF-1·DMF is always the only product proved by X-ray single-crystal data of 3 randomly selected crystals and element analysis. These results suggest that TIF-1 prefers to trap DMF molecules over others during self-assembly process.

For TIF-1·e-urea, it is always the only product whatever the use of e-urea, or mixed e-urea–methanol, or e-urea–DMA, or e-urea–NMP, or e-urea–DMA–NMP solvents. The results were also demonstrated by X-ray single-crystal data of 3 randomly selected crystals and element analysis. It indicates that e-urea molecules become the second choice of TIF-1 without the presence of DMF.

To get TIF-1·NMP, simple synthesis in NMP solvent without other solvents was failed. However, in mixed NMP/dmurea/methanol or NMP/DMA/dmurea/methanol solvents, TIF-1·NMP was successfully obtained as the only product, which was also proved by X-ray single-crystal data of 3 randomly selected crystals and element analysis. Thus, it looks that NMP molecules are easier to be selected by TIF-1 than DMA and dmurea.

Similarly, attempts to obtain TIF-1·DMA in DMA solvent without other solvents were failed, but it is synthesized in mixed DMA/dmurea/methanol or DMA/urea/methanol solvents.

Other simple of mixed solvents, such as formamide, 1,3-dimethyl-2-imidazolidinone (DMI), 1,3-dimethylurea (dmurea), and tetrahydro-1,3-dimethyl-2(1H)pyrimidine (DMPU), have also been investigated. Unfortunately, no positive results were obtained. All experimental results were listed in Table 2.

According to the above results, we may deduce the following guest selectivity of TIF-1 toward different solvent molecules: DMF > e-urea > NMP > DMA. To further understand such interesting phenomena, detailed analysis of host–guest interactions and size effect of the solvent molecules are performed.

In the structure of TIF-1·DMF, there is no obvious hydrogen bonding interaction between the host framework and DMF molecules, so the host–guest interaction is very weak. For TIF-1·e-urea, obvious N–H⋯N hydrogen bonding interaction between the e-urea guest and the ad ligand is observed. Such strong host–guest interactions are also reflected in its thermogravimetric behaviour, where the e-urea guests are difficult to remove (Fig. S5†). For TIF-1·DMA, only non-classic C–H⋯O hydrogen bonds between the DMA guest and the int ligand exists. For TIF-1·NMP, the host–guest interactions include complicated non-classic C–H⋯O and C–H⋯N hydrogen bonds. It is clear that the host–guest interactions in all four structures may not be the main effect on guest selectivity.

The sizes of these four solvent molecules clearly follow the sequence: DMF < DMA < e-urea < NMP, which is also not consistent with the sequence of guest selectivity. From the PXRD patterns, the main peaks between 2θ values of 8–20° slightly shift to low 2θ values with the increasing size of guest from DMF to DMA to NMP (Fig. 2), corresponding to the expansion of pore size. It is abnormal that the main peaks of TIF-1·e-urea shift to high 2θ values, and the cell volume of TIF-1·e-urea is slightly smaller than that of TIF-1·DMF (Ve-urea = 1682.98(7) Å3 < VDMF = 1721.12(18) Å3). We speculated that the size of DMF is just perfect for the formation of regular and size-suitable channels in TIF-1. For DMA and NMP, the increased sizes of the guests should expand the channels, which need more energy for TIF-1. That is also why other bigger molecules, such as DMI and DMPU, are unable to be trapped. But e-urea is truly special because it has strong host–guest interactions with TIF-1, so that the framework of TIF-1·e-urea further shrinks a little.


Fig. 2 The powder XRD patterns of TIF-1 with different guests: simulated (a) and experimental (b) TIF-1·DMF; simulated (c) and experimental (d) TIF-1·e-urea; simulated (e) and experimental (f) TIF-1·DMA; simulated (g) and experimental (h) TIF-1·NMP.

In our former report, the gas sorption properties of activated TIF-1 at high temperature (230 °C) have also been investigated.7 In that case, the crystalline sample tends to be amorphous after the departure of the guest molecules. Here we try to activate the sample of TIF-1·DMF under mild conditions and further study its gas sorption properties. Before gas sorption measurements, the as-synthesized TIF-1·DMF was dipped into dry methanol and heated at 60 °C for 1 week and full solvent-exchange was achieved. Following vacuum evacuation at 80 °C for 6 hours, a hollow crystalline phase TIF-1 was successfully obtained. The permanent porosity of the methanol activated TIF-1 was established by N2 sorption experiments at 77 K, which showed typical type-I sorption isotherm (Fig. 3a and b). The Langmuir and BET surface areas were 944 m2 g−1 and 681 m2 g−1, respectively. A single data point at relative pressure 0.980 gives a pore volume of 0.337 cm3 g−1 by the Horvath–Kawazoe equation. These values are obviously higher than those obtained from high temperature activated TIF-1 sample.


Fig. 3 The sorption isotherms of TIF-1: the N2 adsorption (a) and desorption (b) at 77 K; the H2 adsorption (c) and desorption (d) at 77 K; the H2 adsorption (e) and desorption (f) at 87 K; the CO2 adsorption at 273 K (g) and 298 K (h); the N2 adsorption at 273 K (i) and 298 K (j).

The adsorption isotherms of CO2 for TIF-1 were also measured up to 1 bar at 273 K and 298 K, respectively (Fig. 3g and h). The CO2 uptake values were 107.7 cm3 g−1 (4.81 mmol g−1) at 273 K and 86.7 cm3 g−1 (3.87 mmol g−1) at 298 K. The CO2 uptake values of TIF-1 are higher than those of many famous porous materials at 273 and 298 K, respectively, such as ZIFs and BIFs.2 Virial analysis of the CO2 adsorption isotherms revealed that the enthalpy of CO2 adsorption for TIF-1 at zero surface coverage is 31.0 kJ mol−1 (Fig. S8†). In comparison, N2 was hardly adsorbed at all (only 6.2 cm3 g−1 at 273 K and 5.5 cm3 g−1 at 298 K, Fig. 3i and j). The Henry's constant was used to estimate the adsorption selectivity of TIF-1 for CO2/N2, which has been shown to be valid for calculating the gas selectivity of MOFs.8TIF-1 showed an excellent CO2/N2 adsorption selectivity of 90 at 273 K and 60 at 298 K, respectively. These selectivity values are the particularly well reported to date for MOF materials.2f

Low-pressure H2 adsorption isotherms for TIF-1 were also measured up to 1 bar at 77 K and 87 K, respectively (Fig. 3c–f). The H2 uptake values were 1.58 wt% (177 cm3 g−1) at 77 K and 1.16 wt% (130 cm3 g−1) at 87 K, which is much higher than those of ZIF-11 (1.4 wt%) and ZIF-8 (1.3 wt%). The isosteric heat of adsorption (Qst) of TIF-1 was also calculated. At zero coverage, the enthalpy of H2 adsorption for TIF-1 is 7.7 kJ mol−1 (Fig. S11†). This value of the isosteric heat of adsorption is moderate compared with many famous porous materials, such as ZIF-8, MIL-100, PCN-10, HKUST-1, MOF-5, IRMOF-1 and IRMOF-8.9 What is more, although having the lower Langmuir apparent surface areas (ALang) and measured pore volumes (Vp), the fraction of the pore volume filled by liquid H2 (ρH2 = 0.0708 g cm−3) at 1 bar and 77 K for TIF-1 is much larger than those of many famous porous MOFs, such as IRMOFs, HKUST-1, and MOF-74, and compares well with our recent result.10

The fluorescent properties of the as-synthesized compounds were studied in the solid state at room temperature (Fig. 4). Upon ultraviolet (UV) excitation at 350 nm, TIF-1·guests display blue emissions with maximum at ca. 440–460 nm for different guests. The strongest peak of the TIF-1 with different guests are as follows: λTIF-1·DMF = 442 nm, λTIF-1·DMA = 426 nm, λTIF-1·E-urea = 459 nm and λTIF-1·NMP = 454 nm. The fluorescence properties of the activated samples of TIF-1 after degas were also studied and the maximum emission peak was 448 nm. Compared with the TIF-1, the slight red-shift (TIF-1·e-urea and TIF-1·NMP) and blue-shift (TIF-1·DMF and TIF-1·DMA) of the maximum emission peaks for different guests were observed. In comparison, the free HAd ligand displays an emission centered at 424 nm under excitation at 320 nm. Therefore, it is reasonable to attribute the main emissions in TIF-1 to the π* → n transitions of the ligand.11 The red-shift of organic linker within this MOF is attributed to the formation of the framework structure.

Conclusions

In conclusion, we presented here unusual guest selectivity observed in the self-assembly process of a porous framework TIF-1. The results reveal that TIF-1 has strict guest selectivity following the sequence DMF > e-urea > NMP > DMA. Both guest size effect and host–guest interactions contribute to such guest selectivity. Moreover, the gas sorption properties of TIF-1 have been further studied, and the results show that TIF-1 has high H2 and CO2 adsorption capacity and remarkable selectivity of CO2 over N2.
