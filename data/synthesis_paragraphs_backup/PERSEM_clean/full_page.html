Abstract

A new heterometallic uranyl zinc carboxylate, (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O, has been hydrothermally prepared (150 °C, 24 h) by using 1,2,3,4,5,6‐benzenehexacarboxylic acid (mellitic acid) as organic linker in order to form a three‐dimensional network. Four of the six carboxylate groups of the mellitate ligand interact with mononuclear uranyl or zinc cations, which are eightfold (hexagonal bipyramid, UO8) or sixfold [octahedron, ZnO2(H2O)4] coordinated, respectively. The remaining free carboxylate arms of the mellitate species preferentially interact through hydrogen bonds with water molecules trapped within the framework. Thermogravimetric and X‐ray thermodiffraction (up to 800 °C) analyses and in situ infrared spectroscopy (up to 210 °C) indicated that both free and bound water species are evacuated from the structure in one step between 80 and 170 °C, followed by its transformation into an unknown, anhydrous, poorly crystalline phase [UO2Zn(mel)] up to 320 °C. After the formation of an amorphous phase, the re‐crystallization of oxides α‐ZnU3O10 and ZnO was observed from 460 °C. The fluorescence spectrum of the as‐synthesized uranyl zinc mellitate shows the six bands that are typical for vibronic couplings of the [O=U=O]2+ moiety.

Abstract

A mixed zinc–uranyl compound was hydrothermally synthesized by using mellitic acid, an aromatic hexacarboxylic acid. Its crystal structure is a 3D coordination polymer of uranyl‐centered and zinc‐centered mononuclear units linked to each other via mellitate ligands. The dehydration process was investigated by in situ infrared spectroscopy.

Introduction

Exploration of the chemistry of uranyl carboxylates over the past years has revealed a rich variety of multidentate organic molecules that interact with the metallic centers in different ways to generate original extended hybrid networks.1 The uranyl moiety also adopts different coordination environments because the specific occurrence of the linear double bonds [O=U=O]2+ may induce square, pentagonal, or hexagonal bipyramidal geometries.2 The resulting uranyl–organic frameworks (UOF) exhibit various dimensionalities from molecular assembly of discrete building blocks, to chain‐like or layer‐like networks, or three‐dimensional structures. The uranyl oxide groups are relatively inert, except in rare cases of cation–cation interactions (U=O–U bond),3 and this fact favors the formation of low‐dimensional networks, because bonding occurs via the planar oxo or hydroxo groups from the equatorial plane around the uranyl cation. However, the flexibility or the use of polytopic ligands may also favor the formation of 3D frameworks.1c,4 Aromatic polycarboxylates4a,5 have been successfully used for the formation of such hybrid compounds, but association with hetero elements has not been well investigated in these chemical systems. A few structures of mixed uranyl copper1b,6 and uranyl zinc carboxylates,7 and more recently uranyl lanthanide8 carboxylates, have been reported, for instance.

In this context, we initiated a study dedicated to the preparation of heterometallic coordination polymers incorporating both uranyl moieties and transition metals. In particular, no pure carboxylate complexes with uranyl and zinc cations have been reported in the literature, since only multifunctional organic ligands as mixed O‐ and N‐donors, or phosphonate or arsonate groups, have been used for the formation of such heterometallic compounds.7,9 We previously described a mixed uranyl lanthanide coordination polymer8e synthesized with mellitic acid (1,2,3,4,5,6‐benzenehexacarboxylic acid, hereafter H6mel). This particular molecule was found to be formed in solution after the dissolution of uranium carbide in concentrated nitric acid.10

This paper deals with the hydrothermal synthesis of a first illustration of a mixed zinc–uranyl carboxylate, (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O, obtained with the mellitate ligand and its structural characterization by means of XRD single‐crystal analysis. The crystal structure of this compound reveals free water species encapsulated within the framework and water species bonded to zinc cations. The thermal behavior of this polymeric complex has been followed by thermogravimetric and X‐ray thermodiffraction (up to 800 °C) analyses, and in situ infrared spectroscopy (up to 210 °C).

Results and Discussion

The structure of (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O consists of mononuclear units of uranyl and zinc centers, at positions 1e (  0) and 1g (0  ), respectively (Figure 1). The unique uranium atom is eightfold‐coordinated, with a typical hexagonal bipyramidal environment defined by two axial short uranyl bonds [U=O11U 1.761(2) Å] and six carboxyl oxygen atoms in the equatorial plane [U–O 2.438(2)–2.518(2) Å]. The unique zinc atom is octahedrally coordinated by two carboxyl oxygen atoms [Zn–O52 2.085(2) Å] located in trans positions and four terminal aquo species [Zn–O1W 2.098(2) Å and Zn–O2W 2.109(2) Å]. The two metallic cations are linked to each other through one carboxylate arm of the mellitate ligand, which adopts a heterometallic bidentate bridging mode. The structure can be viewed in terms of the connection of uranyl‐centered hexagonal bipyramids UO8 linked to each other via the mellitate molecules to generate layers (Figure 2) in plane (a,b). Two of the carboxylate functions chelate the uranyl cations while two others act as bidentate bridges between uranyl and zinc cations. The remaining carboxylate arms are not bonded to metals and occur as the protonated form. As expected, these C–OH bonds are longer [C4–O41 1.297(3) Å] than the C–O bonds connected to the metallic centers [C–O 1.250(3)–1.256(3) Å]. A specific signature of the protonated carboxylic acid function is clearly observed by infrared spectroscopy (Supporting Information, Figure S4). In fact, in topological terms, the mellitate acts as a tetradentate linker toward uranyl ions, in a way similar to the tetracarboxylate pyromellitate molecule (the non‐bonded carboxylates of mellitate are replaced by hydrogen atoms in the pyromellitate). The trans arrangement of chelating and monodentate carboxylate arms around uranyl ions is very similar to that of the layered uranyl pyromellitate intercalated by ammonium groups and free water molecules in (NH4)6[(UO2)3(btec)3]·12H2O (btec = 1,2,4,5‐benzenetetracarboxylate).4a In the mixed uranyl zinc mellitate, the structure is three‐dimensional, as cohesion is ensured by the addition of zinc cations, which connect the uranyl mellitate sheets (Figure 3). The uranyl mellitate layers are stacked along the c axis. Between the zinc atoms, free water molecules are encapsulated in the intercalating space. These aquo species have strong hydrogen bond interactions with the free carboxylic acid.

The fluorescence spectrum (Supporting Information, Figure S5) of (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O consists of the typical well‐defined bands, corresponding to the electronic transitions S11→S00 and S11→S0v (v = 0–4) for hexavalent uranium.11 The most intense signal is located at 504.8 nm, the expected green fluorescence. The spectrum is blueshifted in comparison with the fluorescence spectrum of uranyl nitrate hexahydrate (maximum at 508.0 nm). This observation was previously reported for other pure uranyl coordination polymers.4a,12 In the uranyl pyromellitate series,4a blueshifted fluorescence spectra were observed for hexagonal bipyramidal coordination of the uranium center, whereas the spectra were redshifted for the pentagonal bipyramidal coordination. In the present mixed zinc mellitate, the uranyl cation adopts a hexagonal bipyramidal environment, and the blueshifted spectrum could be a signature of such a coordination for uranyl species in carboxylate‐based UOF compounds. The average energy splittings of the S11→S0v transitions were 810 cm–1 and are related to the symmetric vibrations of the double uranyl bond O=U=O. These values are in good agreement with those reported in literature.13

The thermogravimetric curve of (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O shows two weight‐loss steps (Supporting Information, Figure S3). The first event is assigned to the departure of free and coordinated water (–6H2O), associated with a weight loss of 13.2 % (calcd.: 13.8 %) between 80 and 170 °C. It is followed by a plateau up to 300 °C, which may correspond to the anhydrous form of as‐synthesized compound with the hypothetical chemical formula UO2Zn(mel). The second weight loss starts from 300 °C and corresponds to the evacuation of the organic part. The remaining weight value is constant between 400 and 800 °C, and it is 48.3 % of the original value. It fits well with the theoretical value (47.0 %) based on a stoichiometric mixture of ⅓ZnU3O10 + ⅔ZnO.

The thermodiffractogram (Figure 4) indicated the rapid disappearance of the Bragg peaks of the mixed uranyl zinc mellitate, when the sample was heated up to 80 °C. It showed the progressive thermal transformation into a new set of Bragg peaks, which are visible up to 320 °C. This new phase is quite poorly crystalline and its identification was not possible because the diffraction peaks were quite broad. Subsequently the sample transforms into an amorphous phase, and re‐crystallization is observed from 460 °C, with the formation of oxides α‐ZnU3O10 (JCPDS card no. 44‐0914) and ZnO (JCPDS card no. 89‐1397).

The dehydration of (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O was followed by in situ infrared spectroscopy. At room temperature (Supporting Information, Figure S4), the hydration state of the phase was confirmed by infrared spectroscopy, which showed a very broad absorption band in the range 3650–3500 cm–1, corresponding to the stretching vibrations of water molecules and their intermolecular hydrogen bonds. Unfortunately, one cannot distinguish water molecules linked to zinc from those trapped within the small cavities of the framework. The non‐coordinating carboxylate function of the mellitate ligand is characterized by a resonance at 1684 cm–1 whereas the coordinated carboxylate groups are located at 1618, 1572, and 1541 cm–1 for the asymmetric stretching vibrations [νasym(COO)] and at 1352, 1277, and 1255 cm–1 for the symmetric ones [νsym(COO)].14 Furthermore, the corresponding deformation vibrations of the attached carboxylate functions could be assigned to the two bands situated at 911 and 894 cm–1. In the range 1700–1200 cm–1, the remaining bands centered at 1458 and 1432 cm–1 are attributed to skeletal deformations of the ligand. Among the numerous bands characterizing metal–oxygen vibrations at lower wavenumbers, we assigned the peaks at 940 cm–1 to the asymmetric stretching of the uranyl bond [νasym(U=O)]. Indeed, the uranyl bond length calculated from the asymmetric vibration by using the empirical relation defined by Bartlett and Cooney15 [dcalcd.(U=O) = 1.756 Å] is in good agreement with that found by using XRD data [dobsd.(U=O) = 1.762(1) Å].

During the heating process (Figure 5), a clear modification was observed at 124 °C corresponding to the solid dehydration. In the range 3800–2800 cm–1 at this temperature a significant intensity decrease and the disappearance of the bands assigned to water species were observed. This rapid process did not show any differentiation between water molecules linked to zinc and those remaining free, and it did not allow distinction between these types of aquo species. This transformation also implies various changes related to the mellitate ligand. The band associated with the non‐coordinated carboxylate function shifts to 1710 cm–1, which denotes a modification of the remaining free C=O bond due to the loss of intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions with water molecules. At the same time, the bands characterizing the coordinated carboxylate arms exhibit a new group of resonances (1600, 1517, 1285, and 1258 cm–1) and a broadening of existing ones. This change would be correlated to the formation of an additional metal–oxygen bond in the anhydrous phase UO2Zn(mel). In fact, the remaining free carboxylate arms could be deprotonated and a new set of Zn–O–C bonds could be formed in order to satisfy the coordination environment around this metal following the departure of the terminal water molecules. Such a topotactic transition has been recently described during the dehydration of a mixed uranyl copper pyromellitate.6d The vibrations assigned to the benzene ring are also perturbed by this thermal process and display a slight shift to lower wavenumbers. Although this solid does not contain water molecules linked to uranium, the departure of water would lead to a distortion of the uranium environment, highlighted by a first‐order‐type shift of the νasym(U=O) band up to 954 cm–1 (instead of 940 cm–1), reflecting the shortening of the uranyl bond. In the hydrated form, the uranyl oxo group does interact with the non‐bonded carboxylic acid function (C–OH) with a U=O···HO–C distance of 2.857(3) Å. The shortening of the uranyl bond should be related to the displacement of the carboxylate arm of the mellitate and its distancing toward the uranyl oxo group, rather than the evacuation of the trapped water molecules.

Conclusions

This contribution describes the synthesis and crystal structure of a mixed uranyl zinc mellitate involving only O‐donor carboxylate anions. The three‐dimensional framework is built up from mononuclear uranyl centers (UO8, hexagonal bipyramidal motif) and zinc centers [ZnO2(H2O)4, octahedral motif] linked to each other through the carboxylate arms of the organic ligands. The structure can be viewed as a stack of layers containing uranyl moieties and mellitate anions linked to each other via the zinc cations. The hexavalent uranium cations have a strong tendency to favor the formation of low‐dimensional assemblies, through the preferential condensation of oxo groups from the equatorial plane. The three‐dimensional structure is obtained from the association of a heteroelement, zinc, which acts as a pillar between the uranyl–organic sheets. This structural feature has also been observed in a series of mixed uranyl lanthanide carboxylates, with layered uranyl–organic entities connected through the 4f metals.8f

Water molecules are also encapsulated within the small cavities of the hybrid network, between the uranyl–organic layers and zinc centers. Strong hydrogen bond interactions are observed between the free aquo species and the remaining free carboxylic acid functions of the mellitate ligand. Upon heating, the evacuation of the water molecules (free and bonded to zinc) occurs in one step, without any difference between the two types of aquo species. A second anhydrous compound with chemical formula UO2Zn(mel) is then observed up to 320 °C. After its decomposition due to the degradation of the organic part, oxides α‐ZnU3O10 and ZnO crystallize.

Experimental Section

Synthesis

Caution! Uranyl nitrate, UO2(NO3)2·6H2O, is radioactive and chemically toxic, and suitable care and protection for handling such substances should be used.

The compound (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O was hydrothermally synthesized under autogenous pressure in Teflon‐lined Parr‐type autoclaves from a mixture of uranyl nitrate hexahydrate [UO2(NO3)2·6H2O, Merck, 99 %], mellitic acid (1,2,3,4,5,6‐benzenehexacarboxylic acid or H6mel, Aldrich, 99 %), powdered metallic zinc (Merck, granular extra pure, particle size 3–8 mm), and deionized water. The starting reactants were commercially available and were used without any further purification.

The mixture of UO2(NO3)2·6H2O (250 mg, 0.5 mmol), metallic zinc (40 mg, 0.62 mmol), mellitic acid (220 mg, 0.64 mmol), and H2O (5 mL, 277 mmol) was placed in a Parr bomb and then heated statically at 150 °C for 24 h. The pH of the solution was 0.6 before the reaction and 1.1 at the end of the reaction, which results in the formation of a pure phase (see XRD powder pattern, Supporting Information, Figure S2). The resulting yellow product was filtered off, washed with water, and dried at room temperature. The title compound was examined by scanning electron microscope (Hitachi S‐3400N) and had large crystals of 100–200 μm in size (Supporting Information, Figure S1).

Characterization by X‐ray Diffraction: Crystals were selected under a polarizing optical microscope and glued onto a glass fiber for a single‐crystal XRD experiment. X‐ray intensity data were collected with a Bruker X8‐APEX2 CCD area‐detector diffractometer using Mo‐Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) with an optical fiber as collimator. Several sets of narrow data frames (20 s per frame) were collected at different values of θ for two initial values of φ and two initial values of ω, using 0.3° increments of φ or ω. Data reduction was accomplished with SAINT V7.53a.16 The substantial redundancy in data allowed a semiempirical absorption correction (SADABS V2.1017) to be applied, on the basis of multiple measurements of equivalent reflections. The structure was solved by direct methods, developed by successive difference Fourier syntheses, and refined by full‐matrix least‐squares on all F2 data by using the SHELX18 program suite with the WINGX19 interface. Hydrogen atoms of the benzene ring were included in calculated positions and allowed to ride on their parent atoms. The final refinements included anisotropic thermal parameters of all non‐hydrogen atoms and were performed with the SHELX program on the basis of F2. The crystal data are given in Table 1. CCDC‐907993 contains the supplementary crystallographic data for this paper. These data can be obtained free of charge from The Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre viawww.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/data_request/cif.

Fluorescence Spectroscopy: The fluorescence spectrum of the powdered compound (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O was measured at room temperature between 440 and 620 nm with a SAFAS FLX‐Xenius spectrometer equipped with a xenon lamp. The fluorescence spectrum of uranyl dinitrate hexahydrate, UO2(NO3)2·6H2O, is also presented for comparison.

Thermal Analysis: The thermogravimetric experiments were carried out with a thermoanalyzer TGA 92 SETARAM in air with a heating rate of 5 °C min–1 from room temperature up to 800 °C. X‐ray thermodiffractometry was performed under 5 L h–1 air flow in an Anton Paar HTK1200N with a D8 Advance Bruker diffractometer (θ–θ mode, Cu‐Kα radiation) equipped with a Vantec1 linear position‐sensitive detector. Each powder pattern was recorded in the range 5–60° (2θ), at intervals of 20 °C up to 800 °C, with a 0.5 s step–1 scan, corresponding to an approximate duration of 30 min. The temperature ramps between two patterns were 0.08 °C s–1 up to 800 °C.

Infrared Spectroscopy: Infrared spectra of (UO2)Zn(H2O)4(H2mel)·2H2O were measured between 4000 and 400 cm–1 with a Perkin–Elmer Spectrum TwoTM spectrometer equipped with a diamond attenuated total reflectance (ATR) accessory (see Supporting Information). The dehydration process was characterized by in situ IR spectroscopy in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min–1 from room temperature to 210 °C. During this period, 195 spectra were recorded in the range 4000–400 cm–1, with 4 cm–1 resolution, with a Perkin–Elmer Spectrum2 spectrometer equipped with a Pike Specialir Galdi ATR accessory.
