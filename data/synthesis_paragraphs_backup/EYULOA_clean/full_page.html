A new 8-connected porous coordination polymer: crystal structure and selective adsorption properties†

Hua-Hong Zou *, Yan-Ping He , Liu-Cheng Gui and Fu-Pei Liang *
Key Laboratory for the Chemistry and Molecular Engineering of Medicinal Resources (Ministry of Education of China), School of Chemistry & Chemical Engineering of Guangxi Normal University, Guilin, 541004, PR China. E-mail: gxnuchem@gmail.com; fliang@mailbox.gxnu.edu.cn

Received 19th January 2011 , Accepted 11th March 2011

First published on 30th March 2011

A new coordination polymer associated with cobalt ion, [Co3(Tda)3(bpp)2]·2DMF (1, Tda = thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid, bpp = 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane, DMF = dimethylformamide), was prepared under solvothermal conditions, and characterized by the single-crystal X-ray diffraction, element analysis, and IR spectra. Each Tda is coordinated to four CoII ions with µ4-κO:κO′:κO″,κO‴-µ2 coordination mode and each bpp ligand is ligated to two CoII ions with anti–anti bridging mode in 1. Each cobalt(II) trimer is connected to eight adjacent Co3 units via four Tda and four bpp ligands reduced to a uninodal 8-connected topology. Sorption studies conducted on this permanently microporous material imply that it can separate carbon dioxide from nitrogen and methane with high selectivity.

Introduction

Metal–organic framework (MOF) coordination polymers have attracted tremendous attention because of their intriguing molecular topologies and their potentially useful ion-exchange, adsorption, catalytic, fluorescence, and magnetic properties.1,2 The design of microporous frameworks is mainly dependent on the selection of suitable organic ligands and inorganic secondary building units (SBUs).3 On one hand, the utilization of metal clusters as secondary building units has proved to be a feasible method to construct unusual MOFs with intriguing architectures and promising properties.4–6 On the other hand, judicious choice of the organic ligand is another key strategy. Such as, to make porous frameworks by the self-assembly process flexible ligands are much more sensitive to other factors than the rigid ligands, which arises from their easily possible conformational changes, and for the flexible ligands it is most likely that changes of the guest will generate frameworks with a different pore size, shape and even connectivity.7

We found that the thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid molecule possesses many interesting characteristics: various coordination modes (Scheme S1†),8 lone electrons,9 and similarly aromatic,10 unique physical and chemical activities and so on.11 In order to study the extension of the thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid ligand, we chose 1,3-bis(4-pyridyl)propane (bpp) as our assistant ligand to synthesize a complex because of its rich structural configuration, which can adopt various conformations (Scheme S2†) when it acts as a bridging ligand.12,13 In relation with the above considerations, we designed and efficiently synthesized a new trinuclear Co(II)-based microporous MOF and analysed the eight-connected 42068 topology structure. The complex exhibits selective gas sorption properties for CO2 over N2 and CH4 at 273 K and 298 K, respectively; suggesting its potential application in gas separation processes. The magnetic properties also have been discussed.

Experimental section

Materials and physical measurements

All chemicals were commercially available and used as received without further purification. IR spectra were recorded in the range 4000–400 cm−1 on a Perkin-Elmer Spectrum One FT/IR spectrometer using a KBr pellet. Elemental analysis (C, H, N, and S) was performed on a Perkin-Elmer 2400II CHNS elemental analyzer. The TG analysis was performed on Pyris Diamond TG/DTA. Detailed ac and dc magnetic data were collected on a Quantum Design MPMS SQUID-XL-5 magnetometer using the crushed single-crystal sample. Diamagnetic corrections were made with Pascal's constants.14XRD data of complex 1 were collected on a Rigaku D/Max-3B diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å). N2 and CO2 adsorption/desorption isotherms under low pressures were obtained by using a Quantachrome Autosorb 1C at 77 K. For the high-pressure gas adsorption, a Bel Japan Inc. Belsorp-HP was used. Prior to gas adsorption, all the samples were degassed for 8 h at 473 K under vacuum (10−3 Torr). It is pertinent to mention here that in the high-pressure measurements the adsorption amount varies to some extent for repeated runs. Thus we have repeated the high-pressure adsorption several times to obtain the most authentic result.

Synthesis

[Co3(Tda)3(bpp)2]·2DMF (1). Preparation of [Co3(Tda)3(bpp)2]·2DMF—hydrothermal reaction: Co(NO3)2·6H2O (0.30 mmol), H2tda (0.20 mmol), bpp (0.20 mmol), and DMF (15 mL) were stirred for 30 min in air, then transferred and sealed in a 23 mL Teflon-reactor, which is heated at 120 °C for three days and cooled to room temperature. Purple prism crystals were obtained in ca. 61% yield after washing by water and dried in air. Elemental analysis (%) calc.: C, 48.83; H, 3.93; N, 6.83 and S, 7.82. Found: C, 48.48; H, 4.12; N, 6.92; and S, 7.67%. Results and discussion

Crystal structures

The molecular structure was determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction. Each crystallographic independent unit 1 consists of a trinuclear cobalt species, in which 1.5 crystallographically independent CoII atoms exhibit different coordination spheres, although both are six-coordinate. As shown in Fig. 1a, the Co1 atom is coordinated by six carboxylic oxygen atoms from six Tda ligands (Co–O = 2.369(5)–2.161(4) Å) to furnish a distorted octahedral geometry. The other CoII center (Co2/Co2A) is ligated with four carboxylic oxygen atoms (Co–O = 2.047(6)–2.235(5) Å) from three Tda ligands and two nitrogen atoms from two bpp ligands (Co–N = 2.112(6)–2.164(7) Å) to furnish a distorted CoO4N2 octahedron. The octahedrally coordinated Co1 is connected to two adjacent Co2 centers in a corner-sharing mode to form a [Co3(µ3-O)2]6+ core viaµ2-carboxylate atoms with a nonbonding Co⋯Co distance of 3.583(2) Å. Obviously, we can see that each Tda is coordinated to four CoII ions with µ4-κO:κO′:κO″,κO‴-µ2 coordination mode (Scheme S1h†) and each bpp ligand is ligated to two CoII ions with anti–anti bridging mode (Scheme S2†). Each bpp bridges two cobalt ions together vianitrogen atoms to form a one-dimensional alternating chain along the a direction (Fig. 1b), the Co–Co distance is 12.053 Å. In each bpp ligand, the dihedral angle between two pyridine planes is 39.2°. Trinuclear cobalt units are bridged by six carboxylate groups to form a similar linear arrangement, which are further connected by aromatic Tda and bpp linkers and expand in crystallographic a- and c-axes to from a 3D framework with open channels with dimensions of approximately 9.45 × 10.83 Å2 along the crystallographic b axis (Fig. 2). What is particularly noteworthy is the channel in the framework with the pore volume ratio of 30.3% excluding guest DMF molecules as calculated by PLATON.16 The free spaces in the framework are occupied by the structurally disordered DMF guest molecules, equal to two DMF guest molecules per Co3(Tda)3(bpp)2 unit.
Fig. 1 (a) Co(II) coordination environment of 1 and (b) 1D alternatively chain by bpp-bridged along the a direction of 1.

Fig. 2 3D microporous structure of 1.

To fully understand the network structure of 1, the topological approach is applied to simplify such a 3D coordination framework. Apparently, the CoII trimers could be regarded as the secondary building units (SBUs) in the construction of this network. In fact, these trimers can be linked by either the shorter Tda ligands or the longer bpp ligands to form a new 3D uninodal framework topology with trinuclear cobalt units as nodes, in which each Tda or bpp serves as the 2-connected spacer to bridge two adjacent cobalt(II) trimers and each cobalt(II) trimer is connected to eight adjacent Co3 units via four Tda (red rods in Fig. 3) and four bpp (green rods in Fig. 3) ligands with the Schläfli symbol of 42068. As a consequence, this framework can be reduced to a uninodal 8-connected topology.17 It is very similar to the complex [Cd3(bdc)3(L)2(H2O)2] (bdc = 1,4-benzenedicarboxylate, L = 1,4-bis(1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)butane) reported by Wang et al.,17 in which a cross-linked five-fold interpenetrated diamond-like substructure, based on trinuclear cadmium clusters as eight-connected nodes, is an unusual example of a self-penetrating coordination framework displaying an unprecedented eight-connected 42068 topology.


Fig. 3 Schematic view of the topology structure of 1, in which each 8-connected Co3 SBU (blue ball) is extended by four bpp (green rods) and four Tda (red rods) ligands.

IR spectra analysis

The IR spectrum of thiophene-2,5-dicarboxylic acid exhibits broad absorption bands in the region 3000–2800 cm−1, characteristic of associated groups, and at 1628, 1433 and 1030 cm−1. In the IR spectra of compound 1, an insignificant shift in the frequency of vibrations of the thiophene ring is revealed. Bands characteristic of H2tda are retained.

In the IR spectrum of 1 (Fig. S1†), characteristic bands of carboxylic groups occur at 1551 cm−1 for asymmetric stretching and at 1406 cm−1 for the symmetric stretching. These values are shown in Fig. S1†. The comparatively low Δ value (145 cm−1, Δ = νasCOO− − νsCOO−) possibly indicates that Tda is bidentate in 1.18 The strong IR absorption of H2tda at 1662 cm−1, attributed to the characteristic bands of the acid carbonyl groups disappeared in the IR spectra of compound 1, also indicating the complete deprotonation of the H2tda ligand upon reaction with metal ions.18 The IR spectrum of compound 1 is consistent with its single crystal X-ray analysis.

Thermogravimetric analysis and the powder X-ray diffraction (XRD)

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out to examine the thermal stability of the MOFs of 1 (Fig. S2†). The crushed single-crystal sample was heated up to 850 °C in N2 at a heating rate of 10 °C min−1. The DTG and TG curves for 1 show that the first weight loss of 11.5% between 25 and 275 °C corresponds to the loss of solvent DMF (calc. 11.9%). The residue is stable up to 300 °C, where after pyrolysis of bpp and Tda occurs and ends at 615 °C (weight loss: 73.1%; calc. 73.7%). The final residual weight of 17.8% (calc. 18.3%) corresponds to that of CoO. The above thermal behaviors may attribute to the structural features and the TGA results basically agree with the formula of 1.

The thermal behavior of 1 was also investigated by X-ray diffraction in vacuum. The bulk samples of 1 for XRD characterization can be obtained by several runs of synthesis. The XRD patterns of 1 measured at 298 K were in good agreement with their corresponding simulated patterns, which indicated the phase purities of these samples. Fig. 4 shows the XRD patterns of the as-synthesized sample of 1 in the range of 25–400 °C. It is clear that the diffraction profiles below 300 °C are almost the same, indicating that the microporous framework is stable at this temperature and the crystal lattice remains intact after removal of the guest DMF molecules. The XRD pattern recorded at 320 °C exhibits a noticeable decrease in intensity, it means that the decomposition process starts at ca. 310 °C. It can be concluded that 1 has excellent thermal stability, which may be attributed to the presence of the bpp-bridged flexible chains that are further interconnected by Tda spacers into the robust layers.

Sorption properties

In the as-synthesized form, the channels are filled with disordered DMF molecules. The solvent accessible volume is 1707.1 Å3 per unit cell volume and the pore volume ratio is 30.3% as calculated with the PLATON program. Gas adsorption isotherms of 1 were measured by using nitrogen, hydrogen, carbon dioxide, and methane. The kinetic diameters of N2, H2, CO2, and CH4 are 3.64, 2.8, 3.3, and 3.8 Å, respectively. At 77 K, the permanent porosity of 1 is confirmed by its N2 sorption isotherm with the typical Type-III gas sorption behavior (Fig. 5a) and a dinitrogen uptake of approximately 15.5 cm3 g−1 (STP) at P/P0 = 1.0, which shows a typical external adsorption behavior.19 Most of the reported gas sorption selectivities in MOFs are related to the size-exclusion effect.21 In principle, guests with kinetic diameters smaller than the pore windows can diffuse through the window and reach any point of the pore in the framework.20 Although the kinetic diameter of N2 is much smaller than that of the effective pore window of 1·2DMF. N2 sorption at 77 K was not observed, which is not due to the lower kinetic energy of N2.20 A similar behavior was recently reported in Kitagawa's microporous Ni-MOF.21 However, at 273 K, sorption studies show that 1 can take up a moderate amount of carbon dioxide at 1 atm [37.81 cm3 g−1 (STP) or 7.37 wt%], exhibiting highly selective sorption of carbon dioxide over nitrogen.
Fig. 5 (a) CO2 and N2 sorption isotherms at 273 K; N2 sorption isotherm at 77 K; P/P0 is the ratio of gas pressure (P) to saturation pressure (P0), with P0 = 740 Torr and (b) high-pressure CO2, CH4 at 298 K and H2 at 77 K sorption isotherms.

High-pressure sorption studies show that the compound can take up a small amount of H2 10.5 cm3 g−1 at 23 atm and 77 K (Fig. 5b). It needs to be noted that, at a higher temperature of 298 K, the pore apertures are enlarged under certain pressures; thus, the compound absorbs a significant amount of CO2 [57.2 cm3 g−1 (STP) or 11.09 wt%] and a small amount of CH4 [14.5 cm3 g−1 (STP) or 1.02 wt%] (Fig. 5b). Its carbon dioxide uptake is about four times higher than its methane uptake at 298 K, highlighting its potential application for the CO2/CH4 separation. Such aperture expansion might be attributed to the framework dynamics in which higher temperature and pressure will facilitate the entrance of guest molecules through the pore channels.22 The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller surface area of 1 is calculated to be about 99.8 m2 g−1 based on the carbon dioxide adsorption isotherm. The sorption isotherms show typical hysteretic behaviors with respect to carbon dioxide and methane.

Magnetic properties

The variable-temperature magnetic susceptibility of 1 was measured in a temperature range from 300 to 2 K under 1 kOe applied field. As shown in Fig. 6a, upon lowering of the temperature, the χmT values decrease gradually from 9.51 cm3 K mol−1 (corresponding to per Co3 unit, which is larger than the spin-only (g = 2) values of 5.6 cm3 K mol−1 expected for noninteracting CoII3 species) at 300 K to a minimum of 5.15 cm3 K mol−1 at 2 K, thus indicating that a predominantly antiferromagnetic interaction exists in 1. Spin–orbit coupling and zero-field splitting may contribute to this decay.23 The fit of the χm−1versusT curve in the temperature range of 50–300 K gives a Weiss temperature θ = −27.7 K and a Curie constant C = 10.4 cm3 K mol−1 (Fig. S3†), which agrees well with that expected for high-spin cobalt(II).24 The large negative value of θ and the decrease of χmT indicate the existence of antiferromagnetic exchange interactions. The trinuclear cobalt units are interlinked by long Tda and bpp bridges. Thus, the magnetic behavior can be interpreted as being due to the coupling in each antiferromagnetic trinuclear cobalt unit. Furthermore, the antiferromagnetic nature of 1 is further supported by the Mvs.H plot at 2 K (Fig. 6b). The magnetization is 6.35 µB per Co3 formula unit at 5 T, which is smaller than the saturated value of three Co(II) ions. The magnetization vs. field at a sweeping rate of 1 mT s−1 at 2 K does not show any hysteresis. The magnetic hysteresis loops at 2 K are given in Fig. S4†. The remnant magnetization and coercive field (Hc) of 1 are extremely small (585 emu mol−1 and 12 Oe, respectively), resembling those of other CoII compounds.25 The soft magnet type hysteresis loop must be associated with the anisotropic electronic configuration of CoII ions. No detectable hysteresis and long range ordering was observed (Fig. S5†), which is consistent with the overall antiferromagnetic interactions between Co(II) ions.
Fig. 6 (a) χm, χmTvs.T curve for 1 recorded under a 0.1 T applied field and (b) Field dependence of the magnetization at 2 K.

Conclusion

In summary, we have described here the hydrothermal synthesis and structure of a new CoII coordination polymer in which the Tda2− ligand exhibits an unprecedented asymmetric tetradentate coordination mode µ4-κO:κO′:κO″,κO‴-µ2. The compound is a new example of eight-connected 42068 topology structure and porous MOFs with rationally designed micropores for selective gas sorption. It is worth noting that such microporous materials are of special interest for their potential applications for the separation and purification of gas molecules. The overall magnetic behavior of this compound is antiferromagnetic in trinuclear cobalt units.
