Two solvent-dependent zinc(II) supramolecular isomers: structure analysis, reversible and nonreversible crystal-to-crystal transformation, highly selective CO2 gas adsorption, and photoluminescence behaviors†

Bo Liu , Ling-Yan Pang , Lei Hou , Yao-Yu Wang *, Yue Zhang and Qi-Zhen Shi
Key Laboratory of Synthetic and Natural Functional Molecule Chemistry of the Ministry of Education, Shaanxi Key Laboratory of Physico-Inorganic Chemistry, College of Chemistry & Materials Science, Northwest University, Xi'an, 710069, P. R. China. E-mail: wyaoyu@nwu.edu.cn; Tel: +86 29 88302604

Received 24th May 2012 , Accepted 3rd July 2012

First published on 4th July 2012

Two new zinc(II) microporous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·3H2O (1) and [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·(CH3OH)0.5·(H2O)0.5 (2) constructed from the same initial materials 3-(2',5'-dicarboxylphenyl)pyridine (H2dcpy), 1,2-di(4-pyridyl)ethylene (bpe) and Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, exhibiting varying architectures from a 2-fold parallel interpenetrated structure to a 4-fold parallel interpenetrated framework were synthesized by changing the reaction solvents. Complexes 1 and 2 are supramolecular isomeric and both show 2-nodal (3,4)-connected networks, which represent rare dmc and coe topology styles, respectively. Interestingly, 1 presents reversible crystal structural transformation upon dehydration and rehydration, whereas 2 transforms into a new crystal phase 2a after desolvated. When dipping 2a into a DMF/CH3OH/H2O mixed solvent, another new crystal phase 2b can be formed, moreover, 2a and 2b could convert to each other through desolvation and solvation. In particular, the dehydrated 1a shows a high selective gas sorption for H2 over N2 at 77 K and high selectivity for CO2 over N2 at 293 K. The photoluminescence behaviors of 1 and 2 are also discussed.

Introduction

The studies on the synthesis and design of metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been extensively developed in recent years for their crystallographic diversity1 and potential applications such as magnetism,2 gas separation,3 molecular adsorption,4 and heterogeneous catalysis.5 However, the rational synthesis and controlled design of MOFs with a unique structure and function are influenced by many factors, such as the metal-to-ligand molar ratio, the nature of organic ligands, the coordination geometry of metal ions, the presence of solvent molecules, reaction temperature, counter ion, and pH value of the solution.6 A variant of one of the foregoing factors may lead to a drastic change in the dimensionality and topology, even for a fixed chemical composition, structural diversity of coordination polymers is a common phenomenon.7 Therefore, it is a great challenge to synthesize the predicted products at this stage. Supramolecular isomerism, the existence of more than one superstructure for a given set of components, is of particular importance because the superstructure plays an essential role in determining the properties of crystalline materials,8 and also may be helpful for finding the influencing factors on crystal growth,9 which has attracted great academic interest in supramolecular chemistry and crystal engineering.10 Nevertheless, the formation energy differences between the isomers are low, and the rational realization of a supramolecular isomerism system, even the simplest one, is still extremely difficult for synthetic chemists in crystal engineering of molecular solids to date.11 Up to now, many supramolecular isomers of coordination polymers have been reported, and most of them are obtained by changing the solvent or temperature of the reaction system.12 So the rational control of reaction solvent and temperature is a judicious strategy for constructing novel supramolecular isomerism in coordination polymers.

Furthermore, the effect of solvents is vital in the synthesis of porous materials,13 thus, a major concern in synthesis of porous MOFs is a judicious choice of reaction solvents. Interpenetration in MOFs has been considered as an undesirable feature because of a decrease in the available void space. However, recent reports demonstrated that some interpenetrated MOFs showed a high gas-uptake capability attributed to an increase in internal surface area, and it is also a good way to improve the thermal stability of the porous MOFs.14 Moreover, interpenetration is an effective way to restrict the pore sizes of MOFs for targeted gas separation,15 making them good potential candidates for selective gas adsorption and separation, and has been applied successfully in several examples.16 Based on the aforementioned considerations, we successfully obtained two interpenetrated 3D isomeric microporous MOFs (1 and 2) based on the same reactants through simply changing the reaction solvents. The crystal structures, topological analyses, crystal-to-crystal transformation and luminescent properties for both 1 and 2 have been well investigated. More interestingly, gas adsorption studies with the dehydrated solid of 1 (1a) exhibit a remarkable selective gas sorption for H2 over N2 and high selectivity for CO2 over N2.

Experimental section

Materials and general methods

All solvents and starting materials for synthesis were purchased commercially and were used as received. Infrared spectra were obtained in KBr discs on a Nicolet Avatar 360 FTIR spectrometer in the range of 4000–400 cm−1 region. Elemental analyses of C, H and N were determined with a Perkin-Elmer 2400C Elemental Analyzer. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were carried out in nitrogen stream using a Seiko Extar 6000 TG/DTA equipment with heating rate of 5 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) data were recorded on a Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray powder diffractometer (CuK, 1.5418 Å), and the Variable-temperature PXRD patterns were recorded with a Rigaku D/MAX-3C diffractometer. All the gas sorption isotherms were measured by using a ASAP 2020M adsorption equipment.

Synthesis of [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·3H2O (1)

A mixture of 3-(2',5'-dicarboxylphenyl)pyridine (0.025 g, 0.1 mmol), Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (0.059 g, 0.2 mmol) and 1,2-di(4-pyridyl) ethylene (0.018 g, 0.1 mmol) in water (10 mL) was placed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel vessel (25 mL) and heated at 120 °C for 72 h, and then cooled to room temperature at a rate of 5 °C min−1. The resulting colorless block crystals of 1 were isolated by washing with H2O, and dried in air. The yield was 87% (based on H2dcpy). Synthesis of [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·(CH3OH)0.5·(H2O)0.5 (2)

The procedure was similar to the preparation of 1, except that the water (10 mL) was replaced by DMF (6 mL), CH3OH (2 mL) and H2O (2 mL) as the solvent (yield: 84%). X-Ray data collection and structure determinations

Single crystals of both complexes 1 and 2 with appropriate dimensions were chosen under an optical microscope and mounted on a glass fiber for data collection. The diffraction data for all complexes were collected at 296(2) K with a Bruker-AXS SMART CCD area detector diffractometer using ω rotation scans with a scan width of 0.3° and Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å). Absorption corrections were carried out utilizing SADABS routine.17 The structure was solved by the direct methods and refined by full-matrix least-squares refinements based on F2.18 All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically with the hydrogen atoms added to their geometrically ideal positions and refined isotropically except that the hydrogen atoms on the coordinated water molecules were located from the electron density and then refined using a riding model. However, the hydrogen atoms of the guest solvates can't be located from the difference Fourier maps. Results and discussion

Synthesis and crystal structure

Hydrothermal or solvothermal reactions of Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, 3-(2',5'-dicarboxylphenyl)pyridine (H2dcpy) and 1,2-di(4-pyridyl) ethylene (bpe) in a 2:1:1 molar ratio in different reaction solvent systems at 120 °C for 72 h yielded two crystalline products, [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·3H2O (1) and [Zn(dcpy)(bpe)0.5]·(CH3OH)0.5·(H2O)0.5 (2), respectively (Scheme 1). Both of them exhibited a high synthetic yield and no further purification was necessary. In addition, complexes 1 and 2 were characterized by single crystal X-ray diffraction, powder X-ray and TG analyses.
Scheme 1 Synthetic procedures of the title complexes 1 and 2.

Single crystal X-ray crystallographic studies reveals that 1 and 2 are supramolecular isomeric frameworks with different solvent molecules, and crystallize in the monoclinic space group P21/c and P2/c, respectively. Both of the complexes have the same asymmetric unit which contains one Zn2+ ion, one dcpy2− and half bpe linker except the guest molecules (Fig. 1a and b). Each Zn2+ ion in 1/2 is four-coordinated with two oxygen atoms from two carboxyl groups of two individual dcpy2−, and two nitrogen atoms from one individual dcpy2− and one bpe linker, displaying a slightly distorted tetrahedral geometry. The Zn⋯O and Zn⋯N bond lengths of 1 and 2 are almost the same which are comparable to the related MOFs19 [Zn⋯O = 1.928 and 1.942 Å, Zn⋯N = 2.005 and 2.061 Å for 1; Zn⋯O = 1.942 and 1.996 Å, Zn⋯N = 2.041 and 2.072 Å for 2]. The coordination modes between the Zn2+ ions, dcpy2− and bpe linker in 1/2 are the same, and each dcpy2− ion bridges three Zn2+ ions by its two carboxylate ends and one pyridine nitrogen atom, and each bpe ligand links two Zn2+ ions by its two pyridine nitrogen atoms. In 1, each dcpy2− bonds to three Zn2+ ions to induce a wavelike 2D layer, and adjacent layers are connected by the bpe ligands via Zn–N bonds to form a 3D framework (Fig. S1†). While in the structure of 2, each dcpy2− ion also connects with three Zn2+ ions, however, do not form a 2D layer, but instead display a 1D zigzag chain, and each chain is connected by bpe ligands via Zn–N bonds resulting in a 3D framework (Fig. S2†). The reason for the structural discrepancies between 1 and 2 is that the coupled connected nodes representing the dcpy2− and bpe ligands have different orientations.


Fig. 1 View of the coordination environment of Zn(II) in 1 (a) and 2 (b); the 3D 2- and 4-fold parallel interpenetrated networks of 1 (c) and 2 (d).

In order to minimize the hollow cavities and stabilize the frameworks of 1 and 2, the potential voids formed via one 3D net combines with other identical ones, leading to the formation of a 2-fold parallel interpenetrated structure in 1 and a 4-fold parallel interpenetrated structure in 2. The 3D framework undergoes 2- and 4-fold interpenetration ensuing an analogous irregular quadrilateral channel having dimension of 3.9 × 3.3 Å2 for 1 and 3.9 × 3.4 Å2 for 2, inducing the solvent accessible voids to 19.0% and 16.9%, respectively (Fig. 1c and d). The void is occupied by three water molecules per formula unit in 1, and half CH3OH and half water molecules per formula unit in 2, which were confirmed by TG and elemental analyses for crystal samples.

According to the simplifying regulation of topology, as depicted in Fig. 2 and Fig. S3†, dcpy2− and Zn2+ ions can be regarded as 3- and 4-connected nodes, respectively, while bpe ligands can be considered as connectors forming a 2-nodal (3,4)-connected network. The analysis of the vertex symbols and coordination sequence of 1/2 revealed that the topology of 1 is related to a rare dmc net with the point symbol of (4.82)(4.85) and 2 represents a rare coe topology with the point symbol of (42·6)(42·6 × 102·12).20 So far, only limited examples of dmc and coe nets have been reported.20,21


Fig. 2 Schematic representations of simplified 3D networks: (a) view of the dmc topology of 1 along the a-axes, and the coe topology of 2 (b) along the c-axes.

Framework stability and crystal-to-crystal transformations

Thermal gravimetric analysis shows that 1 loses 11.8% up to 120 °C and can be stable up to 350 °C which corresponds to the loss of three lattice water molecules per formula unit (calc. 11.9%) (Fig. S4†). The TGA curve of 2 shows a weight loss of 6.1% below 260 °C, which is ascribed to the release of CH3OH and lattice water molecules (calc. 5.9%). The PXRD patterns of the bulk 1 and 2 indicated a good agreement with the simulated patterns based on X-ray crystallography (Fig. 3).
Fig. 3 PXRD patterns in different states, (1): (a) simulated from X-ray single crystal structure of 1, (b) as-synthesized, (c) dehydrated samples 1a and rehydrated samples (d); (2): simulated from X-ray single crystal structure of 2, (b) as-synthesized, (c) desolvated samples 2a, (d) resolvated samples 2b, (e) desolvated samples of 2b, (f) resolvated samples of 2a.

The openness of the pores makes it possible to remove the solvent molecules from both 1 and 2. When crystals of 1 and 2 were heated at 120 and 200 °C under vacuum for 8 h, respectively, the guest-free phases of 1a and 2a were obtained which were verified by TGA on the desolvated 1 and 2. The PXRD patterns indicate that 1a has the same framework with 1, whereas the PXRD pattern of 2a shows very different from the simulated pattern of 2. The results demonstrate that the framework of 1 can remain stable, while the structure of 2 has been changed after removing the solvent molecules. Unfortunately, the single crystal data of 1a and 2a could not be collected due to the weak diffraction. The dehydration phases could be reversed by exposing the dehydrated crystal sample 1a to the atmosphere for 48 h. However, the crystal sample 2a didn't occur any change by exposure to the atmosphere for one week which was directly used for PXRD measurements. Interestingly, a new crystal phase 2b is obtained when putting the sample 2a immersion into the initial reaction solvent (DMF, 6 mL; CH3OH, 2 mL; and H2O, 2 mL) for 48 h. The TG curve of 2b shows some solvent molecules are contained in the structure of 2b. Moreover, 2a can be recovered from 2b by heating the sample 2b at 140 °C under vacuum for 8 h, and the transformation can be testified by TGA and PXRD. Variable-temperature PXRD experiments were also performed for both 1 and 2 (Fig. S5†). For 1, it is clear that the diffraction profiles in the range of 25–350 °C are almost the same, indicating that the framework is stable below 350 °C and the crystal lattice remains intact after removal of the water molecules. However, for 2, the XRPD patterns at higher temperatures are different from those of the as-isolated sample, indicating a structural change upon guest water removal.

Adsorption properties

To evaluate the porosity of 1a and 2a, gas sorption studies were conducted. Adsorption isotherms of several gases were measured at different temperatures. Gas-sorption experiments of 2a were carried out for N2 and CO2 at 77 and 195 K, respectively, showing that the sample 2a only possess surface absorption, indicating that the dehydrated 2 becomes nonporous after removal of guest molecules under vacuum at 200 °C (Fig. S6†). In contrast, 1a possess high stability and structural rigidity. The N2 and H2 sorption isotherms were performed at 77 K, exhibiting very interesting selective adsorption of H2 over N2. As shown in Fig. 4a, the maximum N2 and H2 uptakes at 760 Torr for 1a are 13.9 and 88.2 cm3 g−1, respectively, however, do not exhibit the classically reversible type-I isotherms but instead display obvious hysteresis for both N2 and H2 desorption. The hysteresis can result from the hindered escape due to structural breathing of the framework during the adsorption-desorption process.22 The hydrogen gas adsorption isotherm is vertical at the low pressure region exhibiting the strong interactions between relatively small pores and H2 molecules (2.8 Å),23 and such a steeper hydrogen gas adsorption isotherm is rarely observed in the previous reports.24 In contrast to N2 adsorption, the amount of uptake for H2 is about 6.3 times higher than that of N2, underlining the potential for selective separation of H2 over N2,25 which may be applied in hydrogen separation of the H2/N2 exhaust in ammonia synthesis industries.
Fig. 4 Gas sorption isotherms for 1a: (a) H2 and N2 77 K; (b) CO2 195 K; (c) N2 293 K, CO2 273 and 293 K.

Microporous MOFs are currently attracting great attentions toward their application as CO2 separation and capture materials from flue gas owing to their well-defined chemical structures and facilely porous modification.26 To further explore the reliable pore characteristic of 1a, CO2 adsorption was measured at 195 K and shows comparatively high adsorption amount of CO2 at 760 Torr (77.5 cm3 g−1 at STP, 15.9 wt%) corresponding to 1.3 CO2 molecule per formula unit (Fig. 4b). The value of CO2 uptake is moderately high and comparable with recently reported zeolitic imidazolate frameworks (ZIF-68–ZIF-82) which have larger pore sizes 7.1 to 15.9 Å,27 indicating that the small pore is beneficial for enhancing the adsorption amount. Similar to H2 adsorption, it is worth noting that the CO2 gas adsorption isotherm is also vertical at the low pressure region, suggesting the strong adsorbent–adsorbate interactions between the framework and CO2. This phenomenon encourages us to further explore its potential properties on CO2/N2 gas separation under ambient conditions. The adsorption isotherms of CO2 and N2 for 1a at 293 K were measured (Fig. 4c). The CO2 uptake value of 48.1 cm3 g−1 can be reached at 760 Torr, whereas N2 can hardly be adsorbed at all (0.8 cm3 g−1), which indicates the obvious gas adsorption selectivity of CO2 over N2. Calculating the ratios of adsorption amounts by employing weight percentage, the material of 1a shows an excellent ideal CO2/N2 adsorption selectivity of 94 at STP. To our knowledge, MOFs with such high CO2 selectivity over N2 is rarely reported.28 It should be noted that the ratio of CO2/N2 adsorption amounts of 1a is up to 115 at 0.16 atm which is a typical partial pressure of CO2 in industrial flue gas and the CO2 loading is 22.2 cm3 g−1 (43.6 mg g−1). The ratios of CO2/N2 is comparable or even superior to that of many MOFs with highly polar functional sites in the pores.29

The significant CO2 sorption selectivity in 1a may be attributed to the smaller kinetic diameters of CO2 than that of N2 (CO2, 3.30 Å; N2, 3.64 Å). The selective sorption of CO2 rather than N2 can also be attributed to the significant quadrupole–quadrupole interactions.30 In the structure of 1a, all the carboxyl groups of dcpy2− adopt a monodentate binding manner, that is, only one O atom of each carboxyl group takes part in coordinating with metal centers, while another O atom of each carboxyl group is located within the wall of the porous surface. The numerous O donors and pyridine groups of bpe on the pore walls can interact effectively with quadrupole molecules, which can induce strong interactions between the framework and CO2 molecules because of its much larger quadrupole moment (−1.4 × 10−39 C m2) than N2 (−4.7 × 10−40 C m2). To further investigate the affinity of 1a for CO2, the isosteric heat of CO2 adsorption (Qst) was calculated according to the Clausius–Clapeyron equation from the sorption isotherms performed at 273 and 293 K. Qst for CO2 is around 33.6–35.5 kJ mol−1 in the adsorption region from 5 cm3 g−1 to 45 cm3 g−1 (Fig. S7†), and this value is comparable with that of some previous MOFs containing amine groups or open unsaturated metal sites that have high Qst values for CO2 (32–47 kJ mol−1).31

Fluorescence properties

Photoluminescent properties of complexes 1 and 2 were investigated in the solid state at room temperature. As shown in Fig. 5, upon the irradiation of ultraviolet light at 300 nm, complexes 1 and 2 show fluorescent emissions with a maximum at 376 and 402 nm, respectively. Since the Zn2+ ion is difficult to oxidize or to reduce owing to its d10 configuration, the emissions are neither metal-to-ligand charge transfer (MLCT) nor ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) in nature.32 According to the close emission energy, they are tentatively attributed to π*→n and π*→π transitions of the intraligands. And that, the difference of the emission peaks intensity for 1 and 2, mainly derives from the differences of the coordination mode of the H2dcpy ligand, the coordination environments of the metal ions, the difference of solvent molecules in the structures, and the rigidity of solid state crystal packing.

Conclusions

In conclusion, two interpenetrated 3D isomeric microporous MOFs based on the same reactants controlled by solvent systems have been successfully prepared. Both complexes 1 and 2 are (3,4)-connected networks with rare topologies of dmc and coe nets, respectively. Interestingly, 1 and 2 exhibit reversible and nonreversible crystal-to-crystal transformation upon removal and inclusion of guest molecules. More importantly, the dehydrated phase 1a shows a remarkable selective gas sorption for H2 over N2 and high selectivity for CO2 over N2, indicating its potential applications in gas separation. In addition, our case is an important example showing quite different behaviors based on MOF isomerism.
