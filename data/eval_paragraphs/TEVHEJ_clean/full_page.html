A polyhedron-based metal–organic framework with a reo-e net†

Guojian Ren , Shuxia Liu *, Feng Wei , Fengji Ma , Qun Tang and Shujun Li
Key Lab of Polyoxometalate Science, Department of Chemistry, Northeast Normal University, Changchun 130024, P.R. China. E-mail: liusx@nenu.edu.cn

Received 24th May 2012 , Accepted 5th August 2012

First published on 6th August 2012

A polyhedron-based metal–organic framework has been designed and constructed with a reo-e net, which is constructed from trinuclear nickel clusters and mixed ligands (copolymerization pattern). It comprises three kinds of polyhedra, which are the hexahedron, cuboctahedron and rhombicuboctahedron.

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) as an intriguing class of hybrid materials are built by assembling metal centres and organic linkers.1 Some of the MOFs can be viewed structurally as interlinked polyhedra, which can be assembled through metal ions or metal clusters with certain bending organic ligands.2 Coordination polyhedra are commonly found as either discrete molecules (metal–organic polyhedra) or structural building units in MOFs.3 Due to the definite interval and limited open window, polyhedron-based MOFs may lead to higher porosity and stability.4 During the development of polyhedron-based MOFs, different angular ligands are usually introduced, the octahedron as a pioneer is initially constructed by a paddle-wheel unit and isophthalic acid.5 Recently, various 5-sub isophthalic ligands have been applied in the MOFs construction with different symmetries (C2v and C3v).6 On increasing the length of the ligands, the reduced porosity imposed by interpenetration is almost unavoidable.7 Another strategy for the construction of polyhedron-based MOFs is the application of a two-step or multi-step procedure, which may first construct initial polyhedra and then build MOFs.8 Therefore, the adoption of simple ligands and a one-pot procedure are the most challenging issues facing researchers. The application of mixed ligands is a promising area during the MOFs construction. Two simple ligands with metal ions or metal clusters may build novel MOFs, which can be called copolymerization. It has been observed that two simple ligands with different angles could form fancy MOF structures.9

It is interesting to contemplate whether copolymerization patterns will dominate.10 To investigate this question the synthesis of porous MOFs based on two organic linkers of different topologies was performed. Recently, an alternative has emerged: coordination copolymerization of distinct topological linkers can drive new modes assembly.11 This discovery suggests that the utilization of coordination copolymerization is a method to develop novel porous solids with exceptional levels of functionality simultaneously avoiding the expense and time associated with exploring new organic linkers by leveraging existing ones. An approach based on a ligand-to-axial technique12 combining the two previous common techniques, axial-to-axial and ligand-to-ligand, is introduced, which contains two or more functional groups, and may construct higher dimensional MOFs.

A variety of MOFs based on trinuclear clusters and linear double carboxylic acid ligands have been widely explored.13 As far as we know, six double carboxylic acid ligands have been adopted to adjust the coordinate geometry of the trinuclear clusters (Fig. 1a). When additional linkages are added to such 6-connected nets by replacing the solvent sites of the trinuclear clusters, the resulting nets would have topologies somewhat different from typical MOFs with simple channel-type pores. In general, there are three solvent sites in a trinuclear cluster, as well as six double carboxylic ligand sites. It is easy to predict that the substitute mode is multiplicate when a multi-ligand is introduced in the system. First, if a pyridylcarboxylate ligand is involved in the system, six carboxylic acids may partially or entirely be replaced.14 Second, they can also substitute the carboxylic acid site as well as the solvent sites.15 Third, three solvent sites can also be partly or entirely replaced by the pyridylcarboxylate ligand. Furthermore, the suppositions have been confirmed in this work. When nicotinic acid and terephthalic acid are synchronously added into the same reaction system, two of the terminal molecules in the trinuclear cluster are replaced successfully (Fig. 1b).


Fig. 1 (a) The 6-connected trinuclear cluster. (b) Two of the terminal molecules in the trinuclear cluster are replaced.

The nicotinic acid is introduced in the reaction system, because it was essentially wanted for the construction of a molecule cage which is similar to cuboctahedra due to this having the same bend angle with isophthalic acid. And the terephthalic acid is supposed to build the framework into a 3D net. However, the case is that the terephthalic acid (BDC) ligands are used in building the molecule cage and framework, and the framework is supported by nicotinic acid (na) ligands.

A polyhedron-based MOF was prepared from the reaction of BDC (0.160 g, 1 mmol), na (0.120 g, 1 mmol) and Ni(NO3)2·6H2O (0.300 g, 1 mmol) in a DMF (12 mL) solution. This solution was sealed in a Teflon-lined stainless steel digestion bomb (15 mL) and heated to 140 °C, and this temperature was maintained for 48 h. It was then cooled to room temperature at 10 °C min−1 to give green block crystals of 1, which was formulated as (Ni3O)2Ni(H2O)2(BDC)6(na)4 (37% yield, based on Ni).‡ The purity of 1 was confirmed by similarities between simulated and experimental X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) as shown in Fig. S3.†

1 crystallizes in cubic space group Pm, and the asymmetric unit consists of three crystallographic Ni ions and four half BDC and an na ligand (Fig. S7†). 1 reveals a crystal structure consisting of cuboctahedra (24 functionalized BDC ligands connected by 12 trinuclear nickel clusters, Ni3(O2CR)6ON2) connected to six other cuboctahedra through BDC, which is not common for BDC ligands. Each inorganic unit is a trinuclear cluster and has the expected geometry. The Ni1 is coordinated by four oxygen atoms of four carboxylates, one central μ3 oxygen atom and one nitrogen atom of pyridine. The Ni2 is six-coordinated by four oxygen atoms of four carboxylates, one central μ3 oxygen atom and one water molecule. All carboxyl groups of the BDC coordinate in a bis-monodentate fashion to two nickel atoms to form the Ni3(O2CR)6ON2 units, which combine together to form the finite cuboctahedron. It is noted that the carboxyl groups of the na ligand link one Ni3 ion in a monodentate fashion. The formation of the oxo trimer with the BDC ligands results in a cuboctahedron, ignoring the nicotinic acid ligand, and linked to 6 neighboring cuboctahedra through BDC.

The cuboctahedra employ a simple cubic packing, which results in forming two other polyhedra of the interval. So, three types of polyhedra are presented in the framework. The first type is a hexahedron cage (Fig. 2a), which is formed by eight trinuclear nickel clusters occupying the vertices and six BDC ligands occupying six edges of the hexahedron cages, and has square windows of 10 × 10 Å. The second type is the cuboctahedral cage (Fig. 2b), in which the trinuclear clusters occupy the 12 vertices of a cuboctahedron, with 24 BDC ligands spanning all 24 edges. The triangle sides are 10 Å, and the square faces are also 10 Å in dimension. Finally, surrounded by six hexahedra and six cuboctahedra, a rhombicuboctahedron (Fig. 2c) is constructed including two kinds of faces (square and triangle). 24 vertices of that are occupied by trinuclear clusters, and 48 BDC are supported as edges. It has been observed that the diameters of the cuboctahedron and rhombicuboctahedron are 17 Å and 29 Å respectively (including van der Waals (vdw) radii), which are comparable with some of the zeolites and molecular sieves.


Fig. 2 Three kinds of polyhedra in 1 (a: hexahedron; b: cuboctahedron; c: rhombicuboctahedron). (d) The view of the tiling with the net of reo-e.

Considering the trinuclear clusters as six-connecting nodes (ignoring the na ligands), the overall structure of 1 topologically possesses a novel 6-connected uninodal net with the point (Schläfli) symbol [32·47·56] and the vertex symbol [3.3.4.4.4.4.4.4.4.6.6.8.8] calculated with TOPOS software.16 If three kinds of polyhedra are regarded as three kinds of tiling, the framework can be viewed as a reo-e net (Fig. 2d).17 Compared with other 6-connected nets, such as acs,18 which is common in the system of BDC and trinuclear clusters, the addition of na is quite important in increasing the kinds of tiling. It is very attractive to explore new topologies, and find fancy channels having different types of cages.

The free volume in fully desolvated 1 is about 61% as determined by PLATON,19 and the calculated density is 0.732 g cm−3. The 3D channel is depicted as the Connolly surface (Connolly radius 1.40 Å) of the framework in Fig. S9.† Two kinds of 3D channel with the square windows have been observed. The thermal stability of fresh samples of 1 has been investigated. TGA studies revealed that 1 is thermally stable up to 300 °C. Compound 1 is insoluble in DMF and common organic solvents such as benzene, cyclohexane, and ethanol. In view of the structural porosity of 1, N2 and H2 adsorption were measured. However, 1 showed no adsorption for N2 and H2 at 77 K. The crystals were soaked in acetone before the out-gassed process, and different pre-treatment temperatures were also undertaken. But we failed to get an ideal adsorption isotherm.

In summary, a polyhedron-based MOF (1) has been constructed through a solvent thermal method. Compound 1 comprises three kinds of polyhedra, and possesses a 3D channel. Two terminal molecules of the trinuclear cluster have been successfully replaced by na ligands, which exploit a new way in designing and synthesizing trinuclear cluster-based MOFs.
