A metal–organic framework as a “turn on” fluorescent sensor for aluminum ions†

Mei-Hui Yu a, Tong-Liang Hu *ab and Xian-He Bu *abc
aSchool of Materials Science and Engineering, National Institute for Advanced Materials, Tianjin Key Laboratory of Metal and Molecule-Based Material Chemistry, Nankai University, Tianjin 300350, China. E-mail: tlhu@nankai.edu.cn; buxh@nankai.edu.cn
bCollaborative Innovation Center of Chemical Science and Engineering (Tianjin), Tianjin 300071, China
cState Key Laboratory of Elemento-Organic Chemistry, Nankai University, Tianjin 300071, China

First published on 29th November 2016

Aluminum ions are harmful to human health and can damage the central nervous system leading to brain cell injury in excessive amounts. Hence, sensing and detection of Al3+ ions are important and require a method with high sensitivity and high selectivity. Here, a metal–organic framework {[Zn2(O-BTC)(4,4′-BPY)0.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)1.5·(DMA)0.5}n (NUM-2) was constructed by ligands 2-hydroxy-benzene-l,3,5-tricarboxylic acid (HO-H3BTC) and 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-BPY) based upon chain-shaped secondary building units (SBUs). NUM-2 itself exhibits extremely weak fluorescence emission, however, the luminescence intensity of NUM-2 is enhanced obviously when there are Al3+ ions in ethanol solutions, and the detection limit calculated using 3σ/k could reach 0.10 ppm, indicating that NUM-2 could be used as an efficient turn-on-based fluorescent sensor with high sensitivity and high selectivity for Al3+ ions.

Introduction

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) have been of wide interest and explored as a class of multifunctional materials due to their unique properties and potential applications in catalysis,1 gas storage and capture,2 drug delivery,3 sensor devices, etc.4 In recent years, there has been an increasing trend in using MOFs as fluorescent sensors to probe guest species, such as metal ions,5 small organic molecules,6 nitroaromatic compounds (NACs)7 and so on, for the protection of the environment and human health. Among various detection methods, fluorometric methods have received considerable attention because of their easy manipulation and high sensitivity, and the diverse MOFs display interesting luminescence properties for guest species. These advantages are the reason that MOFs are widely used in the field of luminescence sensing and detecting. Until now, MOFs as fluorescent sensors usually detect an analyte by means of fluorescence quenching (turn-off), fluorescence enhancement (turn-on) or fluorescence shift.8 For these fluorescence detection methods by utilizing MOF materials, fluorescence quenching (turn-off) is the most common, while fluorescence enhancement (turn-on) and fluorescence shift are less reported. However, “turn-on” has always had higher sensitivity and is easier to be detected than the other two methods, so the synthesis of more materials as luminescence turn-on-based sensors is extremely rewarding.

As is well-known, aluminum content in the Earth's crust is second only to oxygen and silicon, so is in third place, and it is the most abundant metal element in the crust.9 Based on this, aluminum is widely used in many aspects of daily life. However, Al3+ ions are harmful to human health and can damage the central nervous system in excessive amounts.10 According to the assessment of the World Health Organization, the daily intake of aluminum ions is 0–0.6 mg kg−1 (kg is in reference to the person's weight). In general, aluminum is not a trace element necessary for the human body, and aluminum ions slowly accumulate in the body; when it is present in high concentration in the human body, many diseases can be caused by aluminum toxicity,11 for example Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease.12 In our daily lives, it is necessary to prevent the absorption of aluminum, therefore sensing and detection of Al3+ ions are important and require a method with high sensitivity and high selectivity. Recently, some organic molecules as luminescent sensors for detection of Al3+ ions have been reported,13 and even a few MOFs as fluorescent sensors for detecting Al3+ ions have also been reported.14–16 Furthermore, MOFs detecting Al3+ ions are always by means of fluorescence quenching,15 but only a very limited number of MOFs can be used as a “turn on” fluorescent sensor for Al3+ ions.16 Thus, synthesis of MOFs as “turn on” fluorescent sensors to detect Al3+ ions is necessary.

Herein, we report a new metal–organic framework {[Zn2(O-BTC)(4,4′-BPY)0.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)1.5·(DMA)0.5}n (NUM-2), which is constructed by ligands 2-hydroxy-benzene-1,3,5-tricarboxylic acid (HO-H3BTC) and 4,4′-bipyridine (4,4′-BPY) forming a three-dimensional (3D) framework. 4,4′-BPY acts like a “guest molecule”, locating in the framework built by the Zn2+ ions and HO-H3BTC ligands. NUM-2 itself exhibits an extremely weak fluorescence emission, however, the luminescence intensity of the MOF is enhanced obviously when there are Al3+ ions in C2H5OH solutions, indicating that NUM-2 could be used as an efficient turn-on-based fluorescent sensor for Al3+ ions.

Experimental

Materials and methods

The ligand (HO-H3BTC) was synthesized according to the literature procedure17 with some modification and perfections, and other reagents were obtained from commercial suppliers and used without further purification. 1H NMR spectroscopy was conducted on a Mercury Vx-300 NMR spectrometer. The mass spectrum (MS) was measured by using an Agilent 6520 Q-TOF LC/MS. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a Rigaku standard TG-DTA analyzer between room temperature and 700 °C under an air atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 °C min−1, using an empty Pt crucible as a reference. Elemental analysis (C, H, and N) was performed on a vario EL cube elemental analyzer. IR spectrum was measured in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 on a Tensor 27 OPUS FT-IR spectrometer using KBr pellets (Bruker, German). Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) was conducted on a Rigaku Miniflex 600. UV/Vis absorption spectra were measured with a Hitachi U-3010 UV-Vis spectrophotometer (Hitachi, Japan). All fluorescence measurements were performed on a Hitachi F-4500 fluorescence spectrophotometer, and all metal ions used for fluorescence detection were provided by their nitrates. X-ray photoelectron spectra (XPS) were recorded with an Axis Ultra DLD spectrometer.

Synthesis of {[Zn2(O-BTC)(4,4′-BPY)0.5(H2O)3]·(H2O)1.5·(DMA)0.5}n (NUM-2)

Zn(NO3)2·6H2O (30 mg, 0.1 mmol), HO-H3BTC (11 mg, 0.05 mmol), and 4,4′-bipyridine (7.8 mg, 0.05 mmol) were dissolved in 4 mL of mixed solvent of DMA and H2O (1:1, v/v) in a 10 mL glass vial. After the mixture was stirred for 10 minutes, the vial was sealed and placed in a 95 °C oven for 3 days. Colorless block crystals were obtained after cooling to room temperature. The yield was about 70% based on the metal. IR spectrum (cm−1): 3306m, 1608s, 1541s, 1359s, 1278s, 1100w, 1013w, 832m, 789m, 702m. Elem. anal.: Calcd for NUM-2: C, 34.56; H, 3.51; N, 3.78. Found: C, 34.66; H, 3.72; N, 3.80.

Crystal structure determination

The diffraction data was collected on a Rigaku SCX-mini diffractometer at 293(2) K with Mo-Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the scan mode. The structure was solved by a direct method using SHELXTL and refined by full-matrix least squares on F2 using SHELX-97.18 Metal atoms in NUM-2 were located from the E-maps and other non-hydrogen atoms were subsequently located by successive difference Fourier synthesis and refined anisotropically. The guest molecules in NUM-2 are disordered and could not be modeled properly, so the disordered solvent molecules were removed by SQUEEZE in PLATON19 and the results were appended in the CIF files. Detailed crystallographic data are summarized in Table S1† and selected bond lengths and angles are given in Tables S2 and S3.† Full crystallographic data for NUM-2 have been deposited with the CCDC (1479165).

Results and discussion

Crystal structure of NUM-2

The X-ray single-crystal structure reveals that NUM-2 crystallizes in the monoclinic space group P21/c, and the asymmetric unit comprises two Zn2+ ions, one O-BTC4− ligand, one 4,4′-BPY ligand and three coordinated H2O molecules. The two crystallographically independent Zn2+ ions reveal different coordinated models. As shown in Fig. S4,† the coordination number of Zn1 is five, which are five oxygen atoms from the carboxyl groups and hydroxyl group of the O-BTC4− ligands, and two Zn1 ions constitute a binuclear building block. However, the coordination number of Zn2 is six and it has a distorted octahedral geometry, which is constructed from two oxygen atoms from the carboxyl groups, three coordinated H2O molecules and one nitrogen atom from the 4,4′-BPY ligand. As shown in Fig. 1, the binuclear and mononuclear building blocks constitute chain-shaped SBUs via the carboxyl groups, which are linked through the O-BTC4− ligands forming a 3D framework (Fig. S5a†). Meanwhile 4,4′-BPY acts like a “guest molecule” positioned in the 3D framework by coordination bonds. The 3D framework can be simplified as a 3-nodal (3,4)-connected topology with Point (Schlafli) symbol {6·75}2{62·8}2{74·82}, in which the 4-connected binuclear units and 3-connected mononuclear units were bridged by 4-connected O-BTC4− ligands (Fig. S5b†). Some uncoordinated guest molecules were confirmed by elemental analysis and TGA (Fig. S7†).
Fig. 1 The chain SBU and the three-dimensional (3D) framework of NUM-2 (Zn in turquoise, C in light orange, N in blue, and O in red; all H atoms were omitted for clarity).

Luminescence behaviors and sensing properties

The luminescence properties of NUM-2 in the solid state were investigated at room temperature, and are shown in Fig. S8.†NUM-2 exhibits an extremely weak emission at λmax = 377 nm at room temperature upon excitation at 324 nm. Compared with the free ligands of 4,4′-BPY (emission at λmax = 360 nm, Fig. S9†) and HO-H3BTC (emission at λmax = 440 nm, Fig. S9†), the emission of NUM-2 reveals only a 17 nm shift for 4,4′-BPY, so the emission may be caused by the intraligand π* → n or π* → π electronic transition of 4,4′-BPY. In addition, the fluorescence emission of NUM-2 in C2H5OH solution was measured at room temperature, which is similar to the emission in the solid state (Fig. S8†).

To explore whether NUM-2 can detect cations as a highly selective chemosensor, the as-synthesized samples (3 mg) were ground and suspended in C2H5OH solutions (3 mL) containing various metal ions (1 × 10−3 mol L−1) (Li+, Ni2+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Mg2+, Co2+, Ag+, Ca2+, Cr3+ and Al3+). As shown in Fig. 2, the luminescence intensities of the resultant suspensions with different metal ions were recorded, which exhibit that only the luminescence intensity of the suspension including Al3+ ions was obviously enhanced by about 7 times. While the suspensions containing other kinds of metal ions displayed a negligible influence on the emission intensity. Hence, NUM-2 can act as a promising chemical sensor of Al3+ by means of fluorescence enhancement (turn-on). Considering the high selectivity of NUM-2 to the Al3+ ions, competition experiments were performed in the presence of 1 equiv. of other metal ions, which reveal that the luminescence intensity of Al3+ ions remains almost unchanged (Fig. 3). These results demonstrate that NUM-2 is not only a highly selective sensor for Al3+ ions, but also that its fluorescence sensing is not intefered with by coexisting metal ions during detection.


Fig. 2 Luminescence intensity of NUM-2 upon the addition of different metal ions in the C2H5OH solution (1 × 10−3 mol L−1) at room temperature, λex = 324 nm.

Fig. 3 The influence of interfering ions on the luminescence intensity of Al3+ at room temperature, λex = 324 nm.

In order to investigate the detection limit of NUM-2 as a chemical sensor for detecting Al3+ ions, the fluorescence response was measured by gradually increasing the Al3+ ion concentration from 1.0 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 M. As shown in Fig. 4, the luminescence intensity is gradually enhanced as the Al3+ concentration increases. In addition, the detection limit calculated using 3σ/k (k: the slope, σ: the standard deviation) is about 0.10 ppm (Fig. S10†), which is lower than that of some other MOF sensors for Al3+ ions.15 Moreover, the time-dependent fluorescence response was monitored. As shown in Fig. 5, the luminescence intensities of the suspension with a Al3+ ion concentration of 1.0 × 10−3 were continuously recorded after a certain period of time. After 20 s, the luminescence intensity had a certain degree of enhancement, and at 420 s, the luminescence intensity reaches the maximum value. Thus, NUM-2 exhibits a high detection sensitivity towards Al3+ ions.


Fig. 4 Liquid luminescence spectra of NUM-2 as a result of different concentrations of Al3+ (1.0 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 mol L−1) at room temperature, λex = 324 nm.

Fig. 5 Time-dependent emission spectra of the suspension after adding Al3+ (1 × 10−3 mol L−1) at room temperature, λex = 324 nm.

MOFs as fluorescent sensors have been widely studied in recent years, but there are no definite mechanisms to date. An explanation of the possible mechanism that results in luminescence intensity enhancement for Al3+ ions is attempted. The PXRD patterns (Fig. S11†) reveal that when the sample is placed in a C2H5OH solution containing an Al3+ ion environment, the main framework is maintained. In addition, XPS was measured after fluorescence measurements (the sample was washed with C2H5OH), which shows that there exist Al3+ ions in the framework (Fig. S12†). For further exploration, UV–vis spectroscopy of NUM-2 in C2H5OH solution containing Al3+ ions was conducted (Fig. S13†). Following the gradual increase of the Al3+ ion concentration, a new absorbance appears located in the range of 305–355 nm, which probably indicates that new types of coordination environments are formed. Besides this, according to the analysis of the structural features, we found that between the two 4,4′-BPY ligands, the distance of the pyridine rings reaches 10.997 Å (Fig. S6†). Based on the analysis above, the mechanism can be established, such that paralleled pyridine rings and Al3+ ions form a cation-π model which promotes the transition of electrons to increase the intensity of fluorescence emission.20 Finally, the luminescence intensity is enhanced when NUM-2 is dispersed in C2H5OH solution containing Al3+ ions.

Conclusions

In conclusion, a new three-dimensional (3D) metal–organic framework (NUM-2) has been rationally constructed based on chain-shaped SBUs, and it can be simplified as a 3-nodal (3,4)-connected topology with Point (Schlafli) symbol {6·75}2{62·8}2{74·82}. NUM-2 exhibits high sensitivity and high selectivity for Al3+ ions, and the detection limit calculated using 3σ/k could reach 0.10 ppm. So, NUM-2 could be considered as a promising material as a “turn on” fluorescent sensor for aluminum ions.
