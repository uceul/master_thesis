Metal–organic frameworks with six- and four-fold interpenetration and their photoluminescence and adsorption property†

Jing Xu , Zheng-Shuai Bai , Man-Sheng Chen , Zhi Su , Shui-Sheng Chen and Wei-Yin Sun *
Coordination Chemistry Institute, State Key Laboratory of Coordination Chemistry, School of Chemistry and Chemical Engineering, Nanjing National Laboratory of Microstructures, Nanjing University, Nanjing, 210093, China. E-mail: sunwy@nju.edu.cn; Fax: +86-25-83314502

Received 9th March 2009 , Accepted 30th July 2009

First published on 17th August 2009

Three-dimensional interpenetrated metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) {[ZnL2]·4H2O}n (1) and {[Cu(HL)2(H2O)2]Cl2}n (2) were synthesized by reactions of the corresponding metal salt and 1-(imidazol-1-yl)-4-(imidazol-4-yl)benzene (HL) with and without addition of sodium hydroxide, respectively. Complex 1 is a 6-fold interpenetrated porous 66dia framework filled with water molecules, in which the metal atom has distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry and the ligand was deprotonated. While complex 2 with octahedral Cu(II) is a 4-fold interpenetrated dia framework with chloride as counteranions and no deprotonation of the ligand occurred. In addition, the stability, photoluminescence and the adsorption properties of the complexes were studied.

Introduction

Much attention has been paid to the design and assembly of porous coordination polymers in recent years not only because of their intriguing structures, but also due to their possible applications in various areas such as gas adsorption, catalysis, separation, ion exchange and so on.1 The recent results based on the investigation of adsorption properties of interpenetrated porous metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) proved that the increased adsorption capacity of such MOFs makes them new candidates for adsorption materials.2 It is known that the origin of interpenetration can be ascribed to the presence of large free voids in a single framework and it has been demonstrated that interpenetration does not prevent the possibility of obtaining open porous frameworks,3 however, the factors that control the resulted degree of interpenetration remain largely unknown and/or unproven.4

On the other hand, it is known that a self-assembly process is greatly affected by factors such as the nature of the ligand, metal center, reaction medium, template, metal-to-ligand ratio, pH value, and counterions.5 Therefore, further investigation is required for understanding the assembly process. In addition, suitable guests/templates can not only transform a higher interpenetrated coordination polymer to lower interpenetrated ones but also affect the dynamics of the framework.6

Compared to the well-developed coordination chemistry of transition and rare earth metal complexes with 1H-imidazol-1-yl-containing organic ligands,7 for example 1,4-bis(1-imidazolyl)benzene (bib), 1-(1-imidazolyl)-4-(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)benzene (iimb), 1,3,5-tris(1-imidazolyl)benzene (tib), 1,3,5-tris(imidazol-1-yl)methylbenzene (timb) (Scheme 1), the ones with 1H-imidazol-4-yl substituted ligands have not been well studied up to now.8 Obviously, there will be great difference between the coordination chemistries of 1H-imidazol-1-yl- and 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligands, since the former ones can only act as neutral ligands and their metal complexes must have an anionic part to balance the positive charge of the metal ions and the counteranions may have great influence on the structures as well as the properties of the complexes,9 however the latter can serve not only as neutral ligands but also as anionic ones in the case of the 1H-imidazol-4-yl groups deprotonated to generate an imidazolate anion. Therefore, the coordination mode of the 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligand is feasible, affected by the reaction pH, and as a result MOFs with varied structure and topology can be expected.


Scheme 1 Different kinds of imidzaole-containing ligands bib, iibm, tib, timb and HL.

Following this approach and also as an extension of our work, we are carrying out the research on the coordination chemistry of 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligand. A compound with two different substituted imidazole groups, 1-(imidazol-1-yl)-4-(imidazol-4-yl)benzene (HL) (Scheme 1), was designed and prepared. Herein, we report Zn(II) and Cu(II) three-dimensional (3D) coordination frameworks with the HL ligand, namely, {[ZnL2]·4H2O}n (1) and {[Cu(HL)2(H2O)2]Cl2}n (2). It is interesting that they have a different degree of interpenetration. The stabilities, luminescent and adsorption properties of the complexes are also investigated.

Experimental

Materials and methods

All commercially available solvents and starting materials were used as received without further purification. The ligand HL was synthesized by a reaction of imidazole and 1-(1H-imidazol-4-yl)-4-bromobenzene following the procedures reported for the preparation of tib.7bFT-IR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Vector22 FT-IR spectrophotometer using KBr disks. Elemental analyses for C, H and N were performed on a Perkin-Elmer 240C elemental analyzer. The luminescent spectra of the solid samples were recorded at room temperature on an Aminco Bowman Series 2 spectrophotometer with a xenon arc lamp as the light source. In the measurements of the emission and excitation spectra, the pass width was 5.0 nm. All measurements were carried out under the same conditions. Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were performed using a TGA V5.1A Dupont 2100 instrument heating from room temperature to 750 °C under N2 with a heating rate of 20 °C min−1. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation (1.5418 Å), and the X-ray tube was operated at 35 kV and 20 mA. The data were collected in the 2θ range of 5.00–60.00° with a step size of 0.02°. Nitrogen sorption experiments were carried out on a Micrometrics ASAP2020 M + C volumetric gas sorption instrument. Prior to the N2 isotherm measurements at 77 K, the samples were outgassed under high vacuum (less than 10−5 Torr) at 140 °C for 20 h, and the sample was weighed before and after the degassing procedure to confirm complete evacuation of the solvents.
Synthesis of {[ZnL2]·4H2O}n (1). After the mixture was cooled to the room temperature, colorless block crystals of complex 1 were collected with a yield of 44%. Anal. Calcd. for C24H26N8O4Zn (1): C, 51.86, H, 4.71, N, 20.16%. Synthesis of {[Cu(HL)2(H2O)2]Cl2}n (2). After the reaction mixture was cooled down to room temperature, green block crystals of 2 were collected with a yield of 49%. Anal. Calcd. for C24H24Cl2CuN8O2 (2): C, 48.78, H, 4.09, N, 18.96%. Crystal structure determination

Crystallographic data of 1 and 2 were collected at 20 °C on a Bruker SMART CCD system equipped with monochromated Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 Å) using the ω–φ scan technique. The data integration and empirical absorption corrections were carried out by SAINT programs.10 The structures were solved by direct methods using SHELXS 97.11a All non-hydrogen atoms were refined anisotropically on F2 by full-matrix least-square techniques (SHELXL 97).11b All hydrogen atoms except for those of solvent water molecules and C5, C6 atoms in 1 were found from different Fourier map directly and refined isotropically, while the ones of C5 and C6 in 1 were generated geometrically and refined isotropically using the riding model. The C and N atoms of the imidazole ring on 3- and 4-positions in 1 and 2 are disordered and treated by performing half-occupancies of 50:50%. The solvent water molecules in 1 are disordered into two positions with site occupancies of 0.64(2) and 0.36(2), respectively. Results and discussion

Preparation of the complexes

The reported studies on coordination complexes involving 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligands have shown that the 1H-imidazol-4-yl group can have a different coordination fashion when it is deprotonated or protonated under different pH values, in other words, the reaction pH may have a great impact on the formation and structure of the complexes with 1H-imidazol-4-yl-containing ligands.12 Our tactic is to prepare porous frameworks with a different structure and/or topology upon controlling the deprotonation of the 1H-imidazol-4-yl group of the HL ligand under different pH values in the assembly reaction. Following this approach, reactions of HL with Zn(II) as well as Cu(II) salts were carried out with and without addition of NaOH, respectively, and complexes 1 and 2 were isolated. However, under the experimental conditions no single crystals were obtained from the reactions of HL and Zn(II) salts without addition of NaOH as well as from the reactions of HL and Cu(II) salts with addition of NaOH.

Crystal structure description of {[ZnL2]·4H2O}n (1)

In the synthesis of 1, although different kinds of bases such as NaOH, NaHCO3, pyridine and triethylamine were used to deprotonate the HL, only the addition of NaOH was successful leading to the formation of suitable single crystals of 1. The result of X-ray diffraction analysis revealed that complex 1 crystallizes in a tetragonal form with space group of P42/n and the asymmetric unit has a quarter molecule of [ZnL2]·4H2O, namely a Zn(II) atom sitting on a special position with a quarter of occupancy, half of the deprotonated L− ligand, and one solvent water molecule. As shown in Fig. 1a, each Zn(II) atom is coordinated by four nitrogen atoms from four individual L− ligands with distorted tetrahedral coordination geometry. The Zn–N distance of 1.984(3) Å and N–Zn–N bond angles of 107.81(9), 112.86(18)° are typical of the tetrahedral Zn-Nim coordinations.7b,7e Each L− ligand in turn acts as a linear ligand to link two Zn(II) atoms with a Zn–Zn separation of 13.60 Å. It is noteworthy that only one of the two nitrogen atoms of the deprotonated imidazol-4-yl group coordinates with the Zn(II) atom. The connections of the tetrahedral node of the central Zn(II) and linear linker of the L− ligand make 1 a 3D framework with large rectangle channels (20.11 × 20.90 Å) as illustrated in Fig. 1b, each Zn(II) atom acts as a 4-connecting node, and the topology can be described as dia net with the short and long Schläfli symbols of 66 and (62.62.62.62.62.62), respectively.13

(b) Left: a single dia unit cage of 1. Right: the space filling mode of a single 3D dia net (plum: C; blue: N; sky blue: Zn). (c) Left: topological representation of the 6-fold interpenetrating network of 1. Right: a view of the interpenetrating nets approximately down the [0, 0, 1] direction.

There are large voids in the single net of 1 as mentioned above, and according to the famous Aristotle's observation that “nature abhors a vacuum”, interpenetration inevitably occurred. After carefully inspecting the structure of 1, we found that there are six individual 3D dia nets interpenetrating due to the large void in the single net (Fig. S2). The interpenetration mode of the six independent nets in 1 is the so-called ‘normal’ type for diamondoid frames,14 which belongs to class Ia with the interpenetration vector equal to the c axis (6.22 Å). A view of the interpenetrating nets approximately down the [0, 0, 1] direction is shown in Fig. 1c. The adamantanoid cages are strongly compressed in the interpenetration direction. It is noteworthy that there are still voids although the 6-fold interpenetration happened. The effective free volume of 1 calculated by PLATON analysis is 19.5% (237.4 Å3 per unit) filled by water molecules.15

Crystal structure description of {[Cu(HL)2(H2O)2]Cl2}n (2)

When no NaOH was added to the reaction solution, the HL acts as a natural ligand without deprotonation and complex 2 was isolated. Complex 2 crystallizes in orthorhombic fashion with space groupPban and the asymmetric unit also has a quarter molecule of [Cu(HL)2(H2O)2]Cl2 which is the same as that of 1, however, there are coordinated water molecules and chloride counteranions in 2. As depicted in Fig. 2a, the Cu(II) center is coordinated by four nitrogen atoms from four different HL ligands with the Cu–N distance of 2.014(4) Å and two oxygen atoms of water molecules with Cu–O distance of 2.455(6) Å. Therefore, the coordination geometry of the Cu(II) center can be regarded as an elongated octahedron with N4O2 binding set in which the equatorial plane is composed of four imidazole nitrogen atoms and two apical positions are occupied by two water molecules. The HL ligand also acts as a linear building block to link two Cu(II) atoms with a Cu–Cu distance of 13.65 Å. The connections of the central square-planar Cu(II) (omitting two terminal water molecules) and linear linker of the HL ligand also make 2 a 3D dia net with large channels (22.70 × 20.90 Å) (Fig. 2b). The structure of complex 2 consists of cationic diamondoid frameworks, four independent dia nets are interpenetrated within the whole structure, related by a translation vector corresponding to the crystallographic c axis (11.10 Å), as shown in Fig. 2c, Complex 2 also belongs to class Ia, which means all independent nets are related by a single vector.14 It is noticeable that there are uncoordinated chloride counteranions filling in the void of the interpenetrated framework.

(b) Left: a single dia unit cage of 2. Right: the space filling mode of a single 3D dia net (plum: C; blue: N; sky blue: Cu). In complexes 1 and 2, each metal atom is coordinated by four ligands in tetrahedral and square-planar arrangements, respectively. On the other hand, each ligand connects two metal atoms in the same way in 1 and 2, in spite of the deprotonated L− in 1 and non-deprotonated HL in 2. Thus the deprotonated species L− has a negative charge and no additional counteranions are needed in 1, while in the case of 2, the deprotonation of the HL ligand did not occur and Cl− acts as a counteranion in the resulting complex. Therefore, the different degree of interpenetration observed in 1 and 2 as well as the different species filling in the vacancy of the frameworks (water molecules in 1 and chloride in 2) are caused by the different coordination geometry of the central metal atom and different reaction conditions.

Luminescent property

Inorganic–organic hybrid coordination complexes, especially with d10 metal centers, have been investigated for fluorescence properties owing to their potential applications as luminescent materials.16 The photoluminescence properties of complex 1 and the HL ligand were investigated in the solid state at room temperature, as depicted in Fig. 3. It can be seen that 1 exhibits a strong fluorescent emission at 367 nm upon excitation at 344 nm. The luminescence behavior of 1 is such that its high-dimensional rigid polymeric structure leads to significant enhancement of fluorescence intensity as compared to the free ligand. The peak at 367 nm for complex 1 exhibits a blue-shift with respect to the free HL ligand (a broad photoluminescence emission band at ca. 410 nm upon excitation at 358 nm). It is probably due to the π*–π transitions changing into the π*–n transitions after forming the coordination polymer.17 The enhanced luminescence efficiency is probably due to the HL ligand coordinated to Zn(II) ions leading to a decrease in the nonradiative decay of intraligand excited states.18 No luminescence was observed for the Cu(II) complex 2 under the experimental conditions.

Stability and PXRD Studies

In order to make nitrogen adsorption measurements, the stability information about the porous structure is required and the results of TGA analysis not only show the thermal stability of MOFs but also turn out to be decisive data for ensuring an optimized temperature of activation in order to remove the guest molecules. For complex 1, the TGA data revealed a steady weight loss between 25 and 112 °C, which corresponds to the loss of solvent water molecules (calcd 12.95%, found 12.24%). No weight loss occurred until approximately 260 °C, when the whole structure started to decompose (Fig. S3a†). After heating of 1 at 140 °C for 24 h in vacuum, the guest water molecule was removed. The PXRD pattern (Fig. S3b) shows that the framework of 1 is retained except minor differences in the intensities and widths of the peaks. The result indicates that complex 1 has permanent porosity in the absence of solvent molecules.

Complex 2 is stable up until 130 °C, and the weight loss of 6.45% (calcd 6.09%) from 130 to 225 °C is attributed to dehydration of the coordinated water molecules, then weight loss occurs in consecutive steps and does not stop until heating up to 750 °C (Fig. S4†). The result indicates that complex 2 is not stable at a certain temperature range and collapses easily in the absence of coordinated water molecules, so is not suitable for testing gas sorption.

Gas sorption property

As discussed above, framework 1 is stable even up to 260 °C. Before gas adsorption tests, the activation for removing the guest molecules from the pores by heating in vacuum is a required pre-treatment and the as-synthesized crystal samples were heated at an optimized temperature at 140 °C for 20 h. The N2 adsorption isotherm for 1 is shown in Fig. 4, approximately 0.99 mmol of N2 per gram of solvent-free sample was adsorbed at 1 atm, the isotherm shows hysteresis between adsorption/desorption curve down to the lowest attainable pressure. On the basis of the IUPAC classification of sorption isotherms,19 the sorption isotherm for complex 1 is classified into the type H3. This sorption behavior is mainly characteristic of the microporous structure.20
Fig. 4 N2 adsorption isotherm (77 K) of 1 activated at 140 °C (black and red curves represent adsorption and desorption, respectively).

Conclusions

On the basis of a rationally designed rigid imidazol-1-yl- and imidazol-4-yl-containing ligand HL, two novel 3D coordination polymers have been synthesized from hydrothermal reactions with and without an addition of sodium hydroxide. Both 1 and 2 have 66dia topology, but complex 1 with a tetrahedral metal center was obtained as 6-fold interpenetrated coordination framework and water molecules filled in the vacancy, while complex 2 with an octahedral metal center displays 4-fold interpenetration with Cl− anions as counteranions and filling in the vacancy. Complex 1 has high stability and showed nitrogen adsorption property. 1 also exhibited intense and blue shifted photoluminescence compared to that of free HL ligand.
